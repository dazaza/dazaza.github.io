Hong Kong Company FAQs

Q: I want to establish a limited company in Hong Kong, what are the requirements?

A: At least one shareholder over 18 years old is required. Mainland citizens or overseas individuals with a passport or ID card are eligible. A registered address and a statutory secretary for the limited company (usually provided by us) are also needed.

Q: How do I go about setting up a Hong Kong company?

A: You just need to fill out a power of attorney form. The form includes three main aspects:

First, you need to decide on the Chinese and English names of your company.

Second, you need to determine the amount of registered capital. The minimum registered capital is HKD 10,000 (usually divided into 10,000 shares), which does not need to be fully paid up but can be increased according to actual needs.

Third, you need to submit the personal data and ID copy of a shareholder. If there are more than two shareholders, not all shareholders have to become directors, but there must be at least one director. After we receive the above three aspects of information from you, we will prepare complete legal documents for your signature the next day. After signing the documents, it usually takes about 10 working days to complete.

Q: How long does it take to set up a new company? How long does it take if I buy an existing shell company? Can I change the name after purchasing an existing company? How long does it take to change the name?

A: Setting up a new Hong Kong company takes 10 working days from the date of signing all legal documents;

Purchasing an existing company takes 1-2 working days, and can be expedited to be completed on the same day;

Setting up a branch company takes 3 working days; After purchasing the company, you can change the company's name, and changing the name requires 15 working days.

Q: What are the differences between shareholders and directors?

A: Shareholders are investors in a joint-stock company, while directors are elected by shareholders and are responsible for managing the company. The chairman of the board is the head of the board of directors. Of course, more often than not, shareholders are also directors.

Q: What are the differences between choosing a company name in Hong Kong compared to mainland China?

A: Naming a company in Hong Kong is relatively free. Some companies that are difficult to register in mainland China can be registered in Hong Kong as long as the name is not duplicated. Regardless of the size of the registered capital, the Hong Kong government allows company names to include words such as international, group, holding, industry, investment, college, association, center, foundation, and institute. The company name can also be prefixed with a preferred location, such as France, USA, Italy, Japan, Hong Kong, Shanghai, etc., or even use your own name and hometown name before the company name, fully reflecting personalization.

Q: Are there any restrictions on the business scope of a Hong Kong limited company?

A: Hong Kong is one of the freest commercial and trading ports in the world, and in principle, there are no major restrictions on the business scope. Companies can engage in any type of business, such as finance and investment, healthcare, shipping, import and export trade, real estate, construction, decoration, information networks, clothing textiles, tourism, cultural publishing, etc. Many small and medium-sized enterprise owners do not explicitly state their business scope so that they are not restricted to a certain area.

Q: Where does the registered capital of a Hong Kong company appear? Does the registered capital need to be fully paid up?

A: The minimum registered capital for a Hong Kong limited liability company is HKD 10,000, which does not need to be fully paid up (no capital verification required). There is no "registered capital" column on the company certificate; the registered capital only appears in the company's articles of association. If the client wants to prove their registered capital, showing the articles of association is sufficient proof. We can also have our accountant issue a letter of creditworthiness (CERTIFIED TRUE COPIES).

Q: What services are included in setting up a Hong Kong limited company?

A: The services included in setting up a Hong Kong limited company are:

1. Government certificate fees (company registration certificate, business registration certificate)

2. Printed materials (a set of 18 articles of association, 1 stock book, 1 shareholder register, 1 set of application forms)

3. Seal fees (1 signature seal, 1 steel stamp, 1 amendment stamp)

4. One year of registered address fees

5. One year of statutory secretary service fees for the limited company

6. Miscellaneous fees (document fees, courier fees, travel expenses)

7. Accountant professional service fees

Q: After confirming the establishment of an offshore company, how should payment be made?

A: Payment method: You can pay in cash, transfer, wire transfer, check, or promissory note to our designated Hong Kong or mainland account. Cash payment locations include Hong Kong, Los Angeles, Kuala Lumpur, Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Chengdu, Yiwu, etc. Overseas individuals can remit funds to the Hong Kong account; mainland China can remit funds to the Shenzhen account. Account details are available upon request.

Payment method: After the client fills out the power of attorney, 70% of the fees must be prepaid, and the remaining 30% is paid when the company is established. The full amount of the stamp duty on registered capital (1/1000 of registered capital) must be prepaid. The payment method for agents and clients is the same.

Q: After establishing a Hong Kong company, what annual fees are required?

A: There are two types of annual fees: annual review and annual report, and accounting and auditing. 1. The fees for annual review by the tax department and the annual report to the Companies Registry are mandatory annual fees. Prices may adjust according to government changes and typically fluctuate between HKD 3,000-5,000. Even if the company has no business activities, these fees must still be paid. 2. Regarding accounting and auditing, if the company starts operations and has business transactions, it must conduct accounting and auditing. If the company has no business transactions, it does not need to conduct accounting and auditing. However, tax reporting is mandatory.

Q: After establishing a Hong Kong company, who is responsible for annual accounting and auditing? If you handle the accounting for us, what documents are required? What are the specific charges? How long does it take?

A: Accounting can be done by the company itself or entrusted to a Hong Kong accounting firm. However, auditing must be entrusted to an accountant or auditor. If we handle it for you, you must submit all records of business transactions: ledgers, invoices, receipts, payment vouchers, bank statements, accounts receivable, and accounts payable. Generally speaking, organizing the accounts takes about a month, and auditing takes 1-2 months.

Q: What are the differences between the statutory secretary services and business secretary services you provide?

A: According to government regulations, a limited company must have a statutory secretary, who can be a local natural person or legal entity in Hong Kong. Generally speaking, we can arrange a statutory secretary for you. The statutory secretary is a formal secretary required by the Hong Kong government, facilitating government contact and handling official documents.

Business secretary services are tailored business practical services based on customer needs, ranging from answering calls, relaying messages, collecting and sending mail, to letters of credit, logistics services, etc., covering everything. The advantage of business secretarial services is that they can build your corporate image, equivalent to having your own employees and office in Hong Kong, with a complete set of company resources. This makes business operations smooth and efficient without missing any deals.

Q: Our company does not have an office or staff in Hong Kong. After establishing a Hong Kong company, how should we manage and operate it?

A: Our business secretary service is specifically designed for this purpose. For more details, please refer to the specifics of the business secretary service.

Q: How is taxation handled for Hong Kong enterprises?

A: Hong Kong is a low-tax free port with few tax items. The only tax businesses must pay annually is profits tax, which is calculated based on the company's income, i.e., 17.5% of net profit. For example, if a company has an annual turnover of HKD 10 million, gross profit of HKD 2 million, and net profit of HKD 1 million, the profits tax the company owes to the government would be 1 million x 17.5% = HKD 175,000. If the company does not make a profit, it does not need to pay taxes.

Q: Are goods entering or leaving Hong Kong taxed?

A: Except for tobacco and alcohol, goods generally entering or leaving Hong Kong are not taxed. However, all import and export goods must be declared through customs. Customs fees are charged at 0.5% (for goods valued up to HKD 470,000) and 0.3% (for goods valued over HKD 470,000).

Q: Are there any taxes to pay when setting up a company?

A: Yes. When setting up a Hong Kong company, you must pay the stamp duty on registered capital. The stamp duty on registered capital is charged at 1/1000 of the registered capital. For example, if the registered capital is HKD 1 million, the tax payable would be HKD 1,000, which is paid once to the government during registration and does not need to be paid again later.

Q: Which department produces invoices and receipts for Hong Kong companies?

A: Invoices and receipts for Hong Kong companies are not uniformly printed by the tax authorities but are printed and issued by the company's board of directors based on the nature of the company's business. Invoices, receipts, and other documents become effective once stamped with the company seal and signed by the responsible person.

Q: If the purpose of establishing a Hong Kong company is to apply for a Sino-foreign joint venture or wholly foreign-owned enterprise on the mainland, can all shareholders of the Hong Kong company be mainland residents?

A: Generally speaking, a Hong Kong company can be entirely composed of mainland shareholders. However, if applying for a Sino-foreign joint venture or wholly foreign-owned enterprise, the Ministry of Foreign Trade and Economic Cooperation usually invites at least one overseas shareholder to sign the documents. Therefore, arranging for an overseas shareholder to join the company is necessary.

Q: Can our newly established Hong Kong company directly apply for a Sino-foreign joint venture or wholly foreign-owned enterprise on the mainland?

A: Generally speaking, foreign investors coming to China must have been established for more than one year. Specific situations depend on local policies.

Q: If registering a company in Hong Kong, can the company account be set up in a foreign bank within the country, such as a branch of a Hong Kong bank in Shenzhen?

A: Yes. After your company is established, the directors can open an offshore account at a foreign bank within the country using their mainland ID cards. However, a prerequisite is that the directors must personally visit the bank to open the account.

Q: How are various daily tasks of Hong Kong companies, such as tax filing, resolved? Can we entrust you with these tasks? What are the annual fees?

A: In terms of Hong Kong taxation, taxes are filed once a year. Regarding charging, it depends on your company's actual situation. If you choose our business secretary service, tax filing is free of additional charges. However, accounting and auditing require payment, and the fees depend on the turnover and the size of the invoices. If the company has no business transactions, there will be no related fees.

Q: How is the tax rate for Hong Kong companies calculated? How is the specific tax filing time determined?

A: Hong Kong's tax rate is calculated based on 17.5% of actual profits. Hong Kong companies usually use March 31st each year as the fiscal year-end, and you can file taxes up to one year later.

Q: What does the "Nature of Business" section marked as "CORP" and the "Legal Status" section marked as "BODY CORPORATE" mean on the Business Registration Certificate?

A: In the "Nature of Business" section, marking "CORP" means there is no limitation on a specific business, meaning any business allowed under the Companies Ordinance can be conducted. Of course, in this section, you can also fill in specific business scopes like electronics, chemicals, or trade. As for "BODY CORPORATE," it refers to a corporate entity, representing the legal status.

Q: If you need to rent an office, how should it be arranged?

A: Based on different customer requirements, we offer company custody, desk rental, office rental, and building suite rental. Just let us know your needs, and we will immediately find suitable office space for you.

Q: Can background information of Hong Kong limited companies be queried online?

A: Currently, it is not possible. Anyone with questions about a company can visit the Hong Kong Companies Registry in Wanchai for inquiries.

Q: After establishing a publishing house, how can books be legally published?

A: After establishing a publishing house, you have obtained the qualifications to handle book publication matters. However, there are two tasks you still need to complete: apply for an International Standard Book Number (ISBN) or International Standard Serial Number (ISSN). Before publishing, you must also apply for publication registration. After completing these two tasks, you can legally publish books. If you wish to distribute in mainland China, you can apply to the relevant authorities with the qualifications of a Hong Kong publishing house.

Q: What do ISBN and ISSN mean? What are the differences between ISBN and ISSN?

A: ISBN stands for International Standard Book Number, and ISSN stands for International Standard Serial Number. Having these two numbers indicates that our publications have reached international standards and are registered with the ISBN and ISSN international organizations. These numbers can be used to query the background information of the publication at any time. The fundamental difference between ISBN and ISSN lies in the target audience. The former is issued to book publishers, while the latter is issued to magazine and journal publishers.

Q: If business is not good, and the company applies for suspension, how much must be paid annually?

A: A Hong Kong company can apply for temporary cessation at any time. Apart from paying the annual report fee to the Companies Registry and the annual review fee to the Tax Department, no other fees are required. If the market improves, the company can resume operations at any time, saving many costs.

Q: Can my Hong Kong company be dissolved?

A: Yes. If your company is no longer in use and you do not cancel it, the Hong Kong government will assume that your company continues to exist and engages in illegal operations. The government will impose cumulative fines on companies engaging in illegal operations according to Hong Kong law, and in severe cases, the board of directors may face imprisonment. Since dissolving a company also incurs costs, we recommend selling the company as the best option.
