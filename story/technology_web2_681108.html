Business Week: Windows 8 Leads PCs Into the Remote Deletion Era

by xue94fwsh on 2012-02-28 11:14:27

The following is the full text of the article: Google quickly responded and activated the little-known "delete switch," entering 250,000 Android smartphones affected by the infection and forcibly deleting the malicious code. "Seeing this situation makes you feel incredible," said Jittomaki, who had developed several dice simulation applications. The delete switch is a standard feature in most smartphones, tablets, and e-readers. Google, Apple, and Amazon all have the ability to enter devices, delete illegal content or code edited without user permission. This is an effective way to quickly stop threats, but it also leaves behind privacy and security risks.

Jittomaki, who first discovered the outbreak of Android malware, was one of the supporters. He believed that it was the right thing for Google to delete malware without user permission. "Are there any other options?" he said, "Just watch these apps be installed on 200,000 users' phones? It's a necessary measure."

The lead-in: The latest issue of the U.S. BusinessWeek print edition published an article stating that as a remote deletion tool, the "delete switch" has been popular in mobile devices for many years. With the launch of Windows 8, this function will also be introduced into the PC market. However, due to privacy and security risks, this tool has also faced some doubts.

As the Windows 8 operating system is expected to be released later this year, millions of desktops and laptops will also see the introduction of the delete switch for the first time. Microsoft has not publicly announced the reason for adding this function to Windows 8, only vaguely warning that it may be forced to use this function for legal or security reasons. In order to better control software download behavior, Microsoft will equip Windows 8 with an app store and issued corresponding usage terms last December. After studying the terms, the American IT website Computerworld discovered the delete switch function, which was widely reprinted by the media. Like other competitors, Windows smartphones have included this function for several years, but Todd Biggs, Director of Product Management at Microsoft Windows Phone Marketplace, stated that remotely deleting software is "the last resort, rarely used."

Google Android Engineering Vice President Hiroshi Lockheimer said that the company would use the delete switch against harmful content in cases of severe impact. Todd Biggs from Microsoft also revealed that the company would only use this function to address "technical issues and content issues." Apple and Amazon have not yet commented on this matter.

Microsoft refused to answer questions related to the Windows 8 delete switch, only stating that this function would only be used to delete or modify applications downloaded through the new app store. Any software loaded via USB drives, DVDs, or directly from the Internet remains out of Microsoft's control. However, the delete switch can indeed help Microsoft prevent large-scale malware infections. "For most users, being able to remotely delete applications is a good thing," said Charlie Miller, researcher at security company Accuvant.

Negative effects: Last year, when Finnish software developer Janne Kytomaki was looking for applications on Google Android Market, he found something strange: dozens of best-selling applications were suddenly incorrectly labeled under the same publisher. It was as if Stephen King's name was erased from his book covers and replaced with an unknown writer. Kytomaki realized that this might be caused by a rapidly spreading malware, and he published his findings online.

Most of the time during his career, Kevin Mhaffey, co-founder of American smartphone security software developer Lookout, had mixed feelings about the emergence of the delete switch. "This remote deletion tool largely responds to the mistakes of the PC era," he said, "Whether it overcorrects, history will judge. This can be used correctly, but as an industry, we must proceed with caution. It's easy to imagine some adverse development trends."

Due to the reluctance of tech companies to clearly define policies for using the delete switch, concerns arose that such functions might be abused. Civil rights and free speech supporters worried that tech companies might delete software or data under government pressure or for political reasons. "Someone has absolute control over my hard drive, but this is not the way I want or approve of," said Eric Goldman, Director of the High-Tech Law Institute at Santa Clara University School of Law, "If they use it properly, it can make my life better. I don't know if they can use it properly, in fact, I don't even know when they will exercise this right."

The development history of the delete switch in smartphones and e-readers shows that for companies using this function, it can be a double-edged sword. In 2009, Amazon entered users' Kindle e-readers and deleted George Orwell's "1984" and "Animal Farm" sold by publishers without necessary authorization. This incident subsequently caused strong dissatisfaction among users, and Amazon CEO Jeff Bezos publicly admitted that this action was "stupid, reckless, and completely inconsistent with our philosophy."
