Eighty percent of fans support Owen going to the World Cup, but the biggest enemy of the Golden Boy is not Capello.

by qinglai888 on 2009-12-12 11:52:51

Eight out of ten fans support Owen going to the World Cup. The biggest enemy of the golden boy is not Capello. Is Owen's biggest enemy Sir Alex?

Reported by Xiang Tong: "Next Monday is Owen's 30th birthday? Goodness, I suddenly feel like an uncle!" This was the exclamation of a British journalist. The dashing figure of the young man chasing the wind seems like yesterday, and today Owen is about to reach the age of thirty, which can't help but make people sigh at how time flies. Time has changed Owen in many ways, but no matter how he grows a beard, it's hard for anyone to get used to putting the title of "veteran" on him. Is Owen old? Finished? Half a year ago, many people would have given a definite answer. However, after seeing Owen's hat-trick in the Champions League match this Tuesday, you might reconsider your judgment.

When talking about his birthday on December 14th, Owen joked: "I'd rather you say I'm more experienced than older! My twenties are almost over, and I need to enjoy it!" How to enjoy it? When Owen left the field after the game against Wolfsburg, proudly holding the ball under his shirt as if with a "big belly," that was undoubtedly the happiest Owen. Scoring goals is the theme of Owen's career. The "almost-thirty" him may not say it outright, but everyone knows how much he longs to participate in the World Cup for the fourth time and leave more important goals in South Africa.

Super Substitution, Super Efficiency

Reasons to take Owen to the World Cup? Goals. Owen always reminds outsiders that in his one year at Real Madrid, his goal-scoring rate per minute was the highest in La Liga. And in this season's club matches, if calculated per minute, Owen's goal-scoring rate is still very impressive. Among the eight English forwards in the candidate World Cup lineup, his super-efficient rate of scoring one goal every 112 minutes is only behind Defoe, surpassing national team favorites like Rooney and Heskey.

Capello may not plan to include Owen in the starting lineup, but that's fine; being a super substitute, he is equally qualified. Owen's playing time in the Premier League this season has been limited, and he has only scored twice, but both goals were achieved as a substitute. Recalling that last-minute decisive goal against Manchester City, wouldn't Capello want to replicate this moment in South Africa?

Capello may still not trust Owen's health and stamina. But it should be noted that since coming to Manchester United, Owen is no longer the glass man he once was—there are injuries, but they're minor ones... On the other hand, how many players can stay healthy and play through an entire season without interruption? As for stamina, you just need to look at Owen's sprinting 40 yards with energy and speed during stoppage time in the Wolfsburg match to score that beautiful goal, and there shouldn't be too much doubt.

In post-match interviews, Owen refused to comment on his national team prospects, "I don't want to talk too much about myself, I prefer to talk about the team." But his Manchester United teammate Carrick strongly supported him, "The World Cup list isn't decided by me, but you just need to recall some of the important goals Owen has scored to form your own judgment. I don't think his performance in this Champions League match proves anything we didn't already know—he has always been seizing opportunities and scoring goals throughout his career, important goals."

Veteran Beardsley also supports Owen, "Given Owen's achievements, he will always be on my list of 23 players for the World Cup. Owen's skill is that he rarely misses opportunities—even during his time at Newcastle, it was proven again in the Wolfsburg match. In fact, the third goal wasn't even an opportunity, but he created that opportunity himself. I don't think Owen's health will be a problem; he's not a glass man, he just had some strange injuries during his time at Newcastle. I'm not saying I would definitely make Owen the starter, but taking him to South Africa is not a problem."

Former caretaker coach of the England team Peter Taylor said: "Taking him as a super substitute would be worthwhile. But I think it's either him or Defoe; taking both is unlikely."

Public opinion is also tilting towards Owen. In the online public opinion survey conducted by The Guardian, 80% of fans believe Owen should go to the World Cup. And in a poll involving over 30,000 participants by The Telegraph, the number of fans selecting Owen into England's World Cup starting lineup increased significantly this week, rising from 12% to 22%. In this poll, Rooney naturally won the favor of 96% of the fans, and among the choices for Rooney's partner, 43% of the fans still prefer Defoe, making Owen the third choice. Of course, the fans' wishes won't influence Capello, after all, Heskey, who only has the support of 13% of the public opinion, is still considered the preferred wingman for Rooney...

The odds set by betting companies are the result of meticulous analysis, and their values are naturally more rational. Ladbrokes has adjusted Owen's odds of participating in the World Cup to 2.25, while Bet365's assessment is more conservative at 4.0. These odds have improved greatly compared to before, but obviously, their judgment remains: not being selected is more likely than being selected. You should know that currently, seven forwards have better odds of being selected than Owen.

Three Enemies Hindering Owen's Fourth Dream

In a sense, Owen's biggest enemy in participating in the World Cup is not someone else, but his club manager Sir Alex Ferguson. Many media outlets, including The Daily Mail, insist that if Owen cannot play more games for Manchester United, his World Cup dream is still hopeless. Out of Manchester United's 25 games so far this season, Owen has started 8; in 15 league games, he has started only 3 times. Such an appearance rate provides the most convenient excuse for Capello, who clearly stated he only considers club starters.

Owen's direct competitor is generally considered to be Tottenham Hotspur forward Defoe. Capello mentioned that he hopes to bring only four forwards to South Africa, and the ideal combination would be two big names and two smaller ones. If this is true, then Heskey and Crouch are not competing with Owen on the same line; Rooney's core position is unshakable, and only Defoe is Owen's target.

In the past, people could say Defoe's ability to seize opportunities is not as good as Owen's, but for Tottenham's forward who has scored 12 goals in 13 league games this season, this statement is clearly outdated. In terms of other aspects, Defoe's footwork technique, shooting power, and long-range shooting ability are all superior to Owen's to varying degrees, while Owen has much more experience in major tournaments. His awareness and positioning are unmatched by Defoe, and he doesn't fall into offside traps as easily as Defoe does. However, with Defoe's fiery form this season, not taking him to the World Cup seems unreasonable. Former England manager Graham Taylor said: "Now, I would still choose Defoe. He is Tottenham's main striker, leading the Premier League goalscorer list, these conditions are enough..."

Meanwhile, The Times has a very unique view that Owen's real competitor is actually Walcott. Walcott can play as both a winger and a forward, and after his hat-trick against Croatia, his place in the World Cup squad should be secure. But like Owen, he is often troubled by injuries, having played only four times for England after his stunning performance against Zagreb, and only starting once in the Premier League this season for Arsenal. If Walcott's physical condition ultimately fails to meet the standards for major tournaments, and Capello decides to add a fifth forward, Owen will have a chance.

Of course, Owen's real obstacle is still Capello, the iron-willed coach who holds the final decision-making power. In veteran Casciarino's view, Capello has already made up his mind about the selections. Capello's England team no longer relies heavily on individual stars as in the past; the Italian selects players based solely on his tactical needs. In Capello's eyes, Rooney and Heskey are the first choices, and he believes Owen does not harmonize well with Rooney. Owen is only suitable to partner with Heskey, which makes Owen's chances extremely slim because such a combination is almost impossible in South Africa.

Moreover, Capello does not admire opportunistic forwards like Owen. One of Capello's famous sayings is: "A forward who only scores goals is not enough." Although veteran Shearer once retorted: "Do you expect Owen to save penalties?" In Capello's philosophy, forwards indeed need ball control, shielding, passing, and even defensive abilities. Supporters of Owen would say that a forward who scores goals is the ultimate truth; but Capello would argue that in Owen's 89 national team appearances, he has scored in only 32 matches, leaving his contribution in the remaining 57 matches quite limited.

Veteran Lineker also admitted that it is difficult for Owen to go to the World Cup, but he insists that Capello should not easily abandon him, "If Capello were to decide the list now, Michael would probably not be selected. Michael needs to play more games for Manchester United, and if he can do that and maintain a stable goal-scoring rate, then what you have before you is a player who has been tested at the highest level."
