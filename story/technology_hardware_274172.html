Although considered one of the eight major cuisines of Chinese cuisine, it's actually not easy to find authentic Huizhou cuisine in Shanghai where various dishes are readily available. This long-established popular restaurant finally makes up for this small regret; another new discovery is finding a good noodle shop that caters to the common people.

One evening, I went to a noodle shop with an interesting name—Heniu. At first glance, it seemed like a high-end Japanese restaurant, with spacious premises, gray walls, large black wooden tables, and several Japanese customers. It was essentially a simplified version of Xindu Li, yet surprisingly turned out to be a common people’s noodle shop. The menu was as simple as the environment, with just over ten dishes or noodles listed on a palm-sized paper. The signature noodle is called Heniu Noodle, which is actually Taiwanese braised beef noodle. Reasonably priced, the portion wasn't large, but the beef brisket used was excellent, tenderly stewed. The texture was incredibly smooth and flavorful, especially the parts with tendon. The noodles were handmade in a rough style, with tomatoes and vegetables added for extra flavor. "Slurp slurp," finishing the noodles and soup brought great satisfaction.

Recently, a friend introduced me to a Nepalese-themed bar located in a residential community. Calling it a garden restaurant-bar might not be entirely appropriate, as the so-called garden is merely a residents' fitness area, not a true garden. Nevertheless, once inside, there is a bit of Nepalese flair. The decorations and tableware have clearly been carefully selected, with soothing Buddhist chants creating a peaceful atmosphere. On the recommendation of the staff, I ordered a platter of assorted vegetable samosas, a dish found in many Southeast Asian restaurants. Here, however, it was not outstanding; the curry flavor was not strong, the various chopped vegetables inside were mushy and lacked flavor, the outer skin was not crispy, and the accompanying dipping sauce was unremarkable. The meat-scented eggplant, however, was quite good, served in a tin bowl resembling an eggplant hot pot. The eggplant absorbed the flavors of chicken and curry, with a slightly sweet taste that left a pleasant aftertaste.

I ordered the famous Huangshan Double Stones. The "double stones" refer to stone chicken and stone ear, produced in the cool streams and caves at an altitude of over 1,000 meters in Mount Huang and on rocky cliffs, a must-try for national banquet Huizhou cuisine. Indeed, the taste was fresh and clear, with tender and smooth meat, and also had nourishing effects. The Huangshan free-range mother chicken soup was also one of the store's specialties, using chickens raised in the pine forests of Mount Huang. These chickens have small heads and claws, feeding on tender grass, pine pollen, and caterpillars. Stewed slowly over a low flame, a pot of soup is served to the table, showing golden oil, with a fragrant chicken aroma. A bite reveals fine-textured meat, and the soup tastes even better. Although it looks greasy, it is actually light and fresh in taste. I also ordered a large rabbit leg from Dabie Mountain. Though not big, the meat was tender and smooth, roasted with a sweet flavor, distinct from the taste of grilled rabbit legs in Sichuan restaurants.

As one of the eight major cuisines, it was my first time tasting authentic Huizhou cuisine, at Hui Zhen Yuan. Flipping through the menu, I was full of curiosity, as all the dishes were very distinctive. I picked some of the most eye-catching dishes to try. They say Huizhou cuisine is "an art of fire and seasoning," focusing on elaborate cooking techniques such as roasting, stewing, smoking, and steaming. So, I ordered "Huofen Wanbei Donkey Meat." A large golden copper basin was served, with golden corn pancakes lining the edges, slightly sticky. The donkey meat was fresh and tender, not greasy, with a rich and delicious flavor. There were also dried bamboo shoots and bean curd sticks underneath, absorbing the essence of the donkey meat and the soup, making them very delicious. Another major feature of Huizhou cuisine is its expertise in cooking mountain delicacies and wild game, due to its location in the southern Anhui mountains, allowing for the use of many local specialties in dishes.
