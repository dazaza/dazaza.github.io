In sharp contrast to China, iOS still commands half of the market in the US.

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 18:18:43

Due to market environment, operator strategies and other reasons, the mobile phone markets in China and the United States differ greatly. This difference is not only reflected in the billing modes for calls and data traffic but also in the terminal price subsidies. To a large extent, this has led to significant differences in market structure.

According to Nielsen's recently released research data, 11% of mobile phone users in the U.S. replaced their devices in Q3 2013, and four-fifths of these users chose smartphones. Smartphones accounted for 64.7% of the overall market. What differs from China is the platform choice; although Android still ranks first, its share is only 52%, while iOS in second place is as high as 41%. That is to say, in the key U.S. market, the iPhone, with fewer models, has an output volume that is almost on par with numerous Android devices and does not fall behind.

Specifically, in terms of terminal manufacturers, Apple's lead stands out clearly, with a stable 41% of the shipment volume ranking it first. The share of Samsung, the leader in Android, is only 26.3%, HTC is at 8.5%, and the rest are single-digit figures. Nokia's Windows Phone devices account for just 1.3%, slightly higher than Huawei.

We often see numbers where Android holds around 80% of the share and iOS less than 20%, but in the U.S. market, iOS "stands alone" and is comparable to Android. In the Chinese market, the trend fits more with "Android dominating," leaving iOS in a marginal position. Whether in product variety, selection freedom, or price, Android completely surpasses iOS.

According to Strategy Analytics' investigation into the Chinese market in Q2 2013, Apple iPhone sales were merely 3.4 million units, holding a market share of 4.3%. Canalys believes it may even be surpassed by the newcomer Xiaomi. Android occupied over 90% of the market share in the quarter, with Samsung leading the pack followed by a host of domestic brands, showcasing Android's absolute dominance. The two largest markets show significant differences in market structure.

This not only stems from the high recognition of the iPhone among American users but is also related to the strong subsidies provided by operators. Due to the high price of the iPhone when purchased outright, consumers can obtain one for free through subsidized contract plans. In the Chinese market, however, most users buy phones outright, which hinders many people from purchasing iPhones. Moreover, due to the proliferation of local Android phone brands, offering diverse options with excellent cost-performance ratios, a large number of users are attracted.

Imagine if the level of operator subsidies in China reached that of the U.S., then Apple's market share in China would be much larger than it is today. This likely contributes significantly to the structural differences between the Chinese and U.S. markets. Price is an important factor in users' product choices. As evident from Nokia's Lumia series, despite the impressive high-end products, the lowest-end Lumia 520 accounts for nearly a quarter of its shipments.
