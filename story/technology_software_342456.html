How much will the appreciation of the RMB affect the losses of export enterprises?

by jkldas52 on 2010-04-08 19:49:16

There is a popular notion that the gross profit margin on the majority of China’s exported products is very low, and that an appreciation of the RMB by a few percentage points would lead to losses for many exporting enterprises. This idea sounds reasonable at first glance, but in fact does not entirely correspond with reality: between 2005-08, the RMB appreciated by about 5% per year, for an overall appreciation of more than 20%, yet there was no large-scale occurrence of export enterprises reporting losses. Moreover, this idea commits a basic error in economics, for the following reasons. An appreciating RMB increases export enterprises’ costs when measured in US dollars. If only one enterprise experiences increased costs, and that enterprise operates in a competitive industry where profit margins are already thin, then it is likely that the increase in costs will cause the enterprise to report a loss. However, RMB appreciation affects all exporting enterprises; when everyone’s costs rise, export prices denominated in US dollars also rise, rather than costs rising while prices remain unchanged. The extent of the price increase depends on the elasticity of foreign demand for Chinese products. If this elasticity is relatively low, then (when measured in US dollars) the extent of the price increase will be larger, while the impact on the quantity of exports will be smaller. Ultimately, this results in a greater reduction in the welfare of foreign consumers. Of course, China’s export volume will fall when measured in RMB, but when measured in US dollars, the decline will be more limited, or might even increase.

The elasticity of demand is primarily influenced by two factors. First, the degree to which the exports of other developing countries—such as India and Cambodia—can substitute for Chinese exports. In competing with these countries, China does not rely solely on cheap labor costs, but on a combination of factors, such as relatively better infrastructure, political stability, and a wider range of complementary goods. If we were to judge based solely on labor costs, wouldn’t we have already lost our competitive edge? It is not easy to replace China—a major exporting country—in terms of both its market share and status in the global export market. Additionally, over the past few years, the currencies of most countries, including developing nations like India, have appreciated relative to the US dollar, and the extent of RMB appreciation has been less. Furthermore, the increase in production costs (denominated in US dollars) due to RMB appreciation will force us to strengthen other advantages, such as improving investment environments (both hard and soft), enhancing government efficiency, and producing higher-level complementary goods.

The second determinant of demand elasticity lies in whether the inputs used in China’s exported goods are imported. Additionally, RMB appreciation can help reduce inflationary pressures, thereby alleviating the pressure of rising overall costs for export enterprises. Of course, RMB appreciation will bring difficulties to industries with weaker competitiveness. However, if an enterprise’s profitability is built on undervaluation of the currency, then from a holistic perspective, the exit of such enterprises should be considered a good thing.
