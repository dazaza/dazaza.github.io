87-year-old woman takes care of her blind younger uncle for 15 years

by wjwf8179 on 2012-02-28 12:09:56

"I don't call her sister-in-law, I call her mother. Without her care, I couldn't have lived this long." On February 9th, in an old house in the small village of Yinzhu Ridge, 49 kilometers away from the urban area of Fen Yi County, 81-year-old Yang Genya opened his heart to reporters. The elderly man never married, and 14 years ago he suffered the misfortune of losing his eyesight. His sister-in-law Huang Guinü is his only support for life and a source of spiritual strength.

Huang Guinü is now 87 years old. Her life has been full of twists and turns: she was once a child bride, and at the age of 51 remarried into the poor Yang family in Yinzhu Ridge Village, where her mother-in-law was already 80 years old, and her husband had only two brothers. The unmarried younger brother lived with the elder brother and sister-in-law. Despite living in poverty, with her husband's love and protection, Huang Guinü felt content. She took care of her sick mother-in-law while shouldering all household chores.

25 years ago, her husband suddenly fell ill and passed away. After arranging her husband's funeral, Huang Guinü hesitated: "Now that my husband is gone, is this still my home?" But remembering her husband's last words before he passed, "You must keep this family together and take care of your unmarried younger brother," Huang Guinü made up her mind not to leave this family! She planted rice, raised pigs and chickens, cut firewood on the mountain, and kept the household as organized as before. Seeing that her sister-in-law’s heart was still in the family, Yang Genya became reassured. He quit smoking and drinking, and during the off-season of farming, he worked odd jobs outside to supplement the household income, bringing vitality and energy back to the home.

Huang Guinü has no biological children, only one adopted daughter, and her Yang surname husband left behind no children. When her adopted daughter, who had already started her own family, saw that her mother was still in the Yang family and heard some gossip, she could not accept it. She advised Huang Guinü to leave the Yang family and live with her. However, Huang Guinü smiled faintly and said, "I've already promised the Yang brothers that I would not leave here. If I go, this family will truly fall apart."

In 1997, when Uncle Yang Genya, in his early 60s, was cutting bamboo in the mountains, he accidentally got a piece of bamboo stuck in his eye. Huang Guinü used all her savings to send her younger brother-in-law to the county hospital for treatment for more than a week, but it did not restore his sight. Back home, Yang Genya alternated between silence and outbursts. At that time, Huang Guinü was already 73 years old, yet she quietly fed him medicine, cooked for him, and washed his clothes. Worried that Yang Genya might feel confined staying indoors for too long, Huang Guinü often took him out for walks.

One day in 2008, as the weather grew colder, the then 83-year-old Huang Guinü climbed up to the attic to find warm winter clothes for her younger brother-in-law and accidentally fell from upstairs. Although she received medical treatment, she still has difficulty walking today. Now, Huang Guinü needs to use a cane to walk, and household chores like cooking and washing are becoming increasingly strenuous for her. But she told the reporter, "As long as I can still move, I won’t bother the government or neighbors unless necessary." In this dimly lit old house, although there isn't much furniture, the cabinets and stoves are spotlessly clean.

The elderly couple's main source of livelihood now relies on low-income assistance and subsidies for senior citizens. Their situation has also garnered attention and help from neighbors and all sectors of society. Neighbors frequently bring them daily necessities such as firewood, rice, oil, and salt. Fen Yi No. 3 Primary School, the County Federation of Industry and Commerce, and other units often visit them. The village sends comfort items and money during festivals, ensuring that the uncle and sister-in-law have a worry-free life.

By Deng Ai Yong, reporter of our newspaper.
