Familiar with the diagnosis of adolescent epilepsy

by xing790 on 2012-03-03 10:48:36

Experts from Guangzhou Plumeria Hospital state that epilepsy (epilepsy) is a chronic disease caused by sudden abnormal discharges of neurons in the brain, leading to temporary dysfunction of the brain. An epileptic seizure (epileptic seizure), on the other hand, refers to the clinical phenomenon caused by abnormal and excessive hyper-synchronous discharges of neurons in the brain. Its characteristics include sudden and transient symptoms, which vary widely depending on the location of the abnormally discharging neurons in the brain. These symptoms can involve motor, sensory, or autonomic nerves, with or without changes in consciousness or alertness.

Epilepsy is a very complex condition, and diagnosing epilepsy in adolescents requires great caution and high standards. Now, epilepsy experts will analyze relevant diagnostic content for everyone's reference.

(I) The principle of diagnosing epilepsy in adolescents is first to determine whether it is epilepsy.

1. Medical history

A detailed and accurate medical history is the main basis for diagnosis. When the patient cannot describe the episode process, it is necessary to carefully inquire about the entire episode from witnesses. This includes the environment at the time, the duration of the episode, the posture, complexion, and voice during the episode, as well as whether there was any limb convulsion and its approximate order. Understanding whether there was loss of consciousness during the episode is crucial for diagnosing generalized tonic-clonic seizures, with indirect evidence being tongue biting or urinary incontinence.

2. Electroencephalogram (EEG) examination

This greatly aids in diagnosis, especially when combined with various stimulation methods such as over-breathing and flashing light stimuli, as well as special electrodes like sphenoidal electrodes. Abnormalities are found in approximately 80% of patients. However, the standard for diagnosing epilepsy still needs to be considered in conjunction with clinical phenomena.

(II) The principle of diagnosing epilepsy in adolescents is to determine the type of epilepsy.

The main basis is the clinical manifestations of the patient, distinguishing between partial and generalized seizures first, and then further determining the type of seizure based on EEG results.

(III) The principle of diagnosing epilepsy in adolescents is to determine the cause of epilepsy.

1. Differentiation: Idiopathic versus symptomatic epilepsy, mainly through comprehensive consideration of idiopathic epilepsy evidence, such as family history, age of onset, seizure phenomena, EEG records, and physical signs. For patients who develop epilepsy in middle age or older, even if physical examinations and EEGs show no abnormalities, symptomatic epilepsy cannot be completely ruled out, and other auxiliary tests may be necessary if required.

2. Differentiation: Brain and systemic diseases. For symptomatic epilepsy, metabolic diseases need to be excluded first, such as hypoglycemia, hypocalcemia, and aminoaciduria.

3. Exploration: Nature of brain diseases. If localized neurological localization signs and papilledema are found during physical examination, further head CT/MRI, cerebral angiography, and other tests should be conducted to clarify the cause.

Epileptic seizures often occur suddenly, without warning, and are long-term, so patients are prone to accidents during seizures, posing risks to life safety, such as bites, suffocation, car accidents, and safety issues during childbirth for pregnant women.
