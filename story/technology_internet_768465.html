LinkedIn's Alternative Corporate Activity: Bring Your Parents to Work

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:35:30

LinkedIn held an event last Thursday at the Empire State Building in New York called "Bring Your Parents to Work Day," inviting 118 parents of 68 employees. During the event, the company's CEO, Jeff Weiner, addressed the audience while his own parents sat among the attendees.

According to LinkedIn, the purpose of this event was to bridge the generational gap between employees and their parents at the workplace. Weiner stated that big data and social media have created a whole new category of jobs, but one-third of parents do not fully understand what their children do.

This activity was not originally initiated by LinkedIn; tech giant Google hosted a similar "Bring Your Parents to Work" event last year. Google encouraged other companies, including financial services provider Deutsche Bank and real estate website Trulia, to also organize such events.

In many ways, the day was set aside to celebrate the employees' parents. The walls of the venue were adorned with blue and white balloons, and M83's "Midnight City" played through the speakers as employees gathered with their parents, taking photos by the windows. Company executives gave speeches during the event, and lunch was provided for both employees and their parents. In the afternoon, while employees worked, their parents were welcome to stay and accompany them.

Weiner reminisced in his opening remarks about his parents witnessing the company's IPO at the New York Stock Exchange and some of the phrases his father used to say before bedtime.

Weiner recalled: "He might have said 'Where there's a will, there's a way,' which I heard frequently until I was about 10 or 12 years old. After that, I never heard it again. That phrase has become a favorite saying of parents, much like 'Eat your vegetables.'"

Most of the employees' parents came from New York and New Jersey, with some traveling from Washington D.C., Massachusetts, and Connecticut. Many of the parents seemed genuinely thrilled to be part of the event.

One employee's father mentioned receiving text messages from colleagues asking about the day's activities. He joked, "I'm planning to brag over breakfast tomorrow."

The event seemed like a strange inversion of the traditional "Bring Your Kids to Work Day," reinforcing the stereotype that millennials are overly dependent on their parents. There is a growing trend where young people start businesses later, leave home later, and are more financially dependent on their parents.

A 27-year-old advertising manager at LinkedIn said she feels strange if she doesn't speak to her parents every day.

Hector Hernandez, a 31-year-old senior relationship manager at LinkedIn, commented, "Parents will definitely critique our work, but my perspective aligns with LinkedIn's - the goal of this event is to foster mutual understanding and show parents that the work we do is different from what they did."
