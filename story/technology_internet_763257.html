Ubuntu Edge: An Attempt at a New Model for Smartphone Production and Sales

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 17:53:26

The most powerful project on the crowdfunding site Indiegogo has emerged, and it may开创e a brand new model for the production and sales of smartphones.

This project comes from Canonical. They plan to launch a smartphone called Edge with an Ubuntu mobile operating system. However, due to a lack of funds, they initiated a fundraising campaign on Indiegogo.

The amount of funding requested by Canonical is astonishing, as high as $32 million, far exceeding the historical records of Indiegogo and Kickstarter. This is equivalent to raising $1 million per day on average, which is almost an impossible task to complete.

Fortunately, Ubuntu fans have provided strong support. Despite the minimum support amount being as high as $675 to obtain the phone, the Ubuntu Edge project received $3 million in sponsorships within the first 8 hours, and after 3 days, it broke through the $6 million mark. If this momentum continues, it is very likely that the goal will be achieved.

The popularity of the Ubuntu Edge owes much to its product specifications. The device claims to feature the fastest processor available today, with 4GB RAM, 128GB ROM, and can be flashed with both the Ubuntu mobile system and Android. The screen is 4.5 inches with a 720p resolution, and it includes LTE, Bluetooth 4.0, and NFC. In terms of the current market environment, these are top-tier specifications, though the product won't be available until May of next year.

The Ubuntu Edge inadvertently set a precedent, allowing people to pay for creativity upfront, collecting payments before manufacturing the phones. No mobile phone company has ever been able to get consumers to pay for a product 10 months in advance at such a high price of $675. They don't need to conduct surveys or analyses to please consumers, desperately pursue differentiation, or engage in large-scale marketing campaigns, only to worry anxiously about whether the market will accept the product after spending huge amounts of money on its release.

In a previous article, it was mentioned that the greatest advantage of crowdfunding is providing a low-cost opportunity to test ideas. If the sponsorship amount is insufficient, it indicates poor market acceptance, allowing the project to be halted early. Once the fundraising is completed, users are already locked in, and all that's left is to produce the product. Compared to other manufacturers, Ubuntu Edge's backing comes from consumers who have already paid, making it secure.

Crowdfunding might just be the first step; they are also exploring new ways to manufacture phones. When Canonical founder Mark Shuttleworth recently answered questions from netizens on Reddit, he mentioned an idea:

"The first version of Edge is just to prove the support for creativity through crowdfunding. If successful, I would consider holding such an event once a year, where the next product specifications are decided by votes from the previous sponsors."

This is another manufacturer using internet thinking to make phones, and they are asking for payment upfront before production. The fan culture of Ubuntu plays a significant role in this process.

However, the Ubuntu Edge is not without risks. Although the threshold for smartphones has decreased, producing a good phone still requires rich experience in hardware and a high degree of integration between software and hardware. Pebble experienced delays in shipping, and more complex smartphones are unlikely to be immune, especially in a smartphone market where configurations are updated almost every six months.
