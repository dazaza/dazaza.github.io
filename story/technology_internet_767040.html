Popcap and Apple jointly delete negative reviews of Plants vs Zombies 2, improving the game's rating

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:23:10

After the official Chinese version of "Plants vs. Zombies 2" was launched in China, it quickly became number one on the App Store's free chart. However, within just a few days, due to its aggressive monetization tactics, user dissatisfaction led to the game's rating dropping from five stars to two stars. But now, the rating for "Plants vs. Zombies 2" on the App Store has rebounded to four and a half stars.

At the recently held Game Developers Conference · China (GDC China 2013), Duncan, the game design director at PopCap Shanghai, told a reporter from the IT Times that the negative reviews (low ratings) at the time were orchestrated by competitors, and later Apple helped PopCap remove some of these negative reviews. He also believes that "games should be designed to be difficult."

Apple helps PopCap delete bad reviews

On September 18th, when the reporter logged into the App Store, they saw that currently, most of the ratings for "Plants vs. Zombies 2" are five stars, followed by one star, with the overall rating reaching four and a half stars. Many players expressed confusion about this situation.

Duncan stated that after the Chinese version was launched, PopCap noticed these negative reviews, "A large part of them were orchestrated by competitors. Apple worked with us to identify which reviews were fake, and then helped us delete these negative reviews."

An industry insider told the reporter that Apple indeed has the ability to do this. Anyone who has an objection to a review can report it, and then Apple will decide whether or not to delete it. On the App Store, there is a "Report Concerns" option to the right of each review, where you can choose reasons such as "This review contains offensive language," "This review is not a review or is too off-topic," or "I disagree with this review," and provide detailed explanations below for Apple to handle.

"For key manufacturers at the top of Apple's rankings, Apple will definitely give special attention. So it's no surprise that PopCap asked Apple for help in deleting reviews." The above person analyzed.

The deletion rights are also written into the "Terms and Conditions": "iTunes reserves the right, at any time according to its sole discretion, without notice or liability, to not post or publish any material, and to delete or edit any material." The above person believes that this is Apple's rule, "Some negative, pornographic, gambling-related, or drug-related content should certainly be deleted."

Data shows that Apple has previously deleted negative reviews for some applications on the App Store. In July 2012, a fault in the App Store caused many iOS apps to crash frequently, leading to numerous users posting negative reviews. Some developers asked Apple to delete the negative user reviews caused by this issue, and subsequently all related negative reviews were deleted.

In-app purchase model is for anti-piracy

Facing criticism from users, PopCap made concessions: two weeks after the release of "Plants vs. Zombies 2," on August 19, PopCap updated the game, reducing the number of stars required to unlock certain plants, offering a 30% discount on all gold-buying plants, among other changes. However, users were not satisfied, believing that such changes were "changing the soup but not the medicine."

"Games should be designed to be difficult." Duncan used the first part of "Plants vs. Zombies" as an example. He said that after the first part was released, PopCap received feedback from many Chinese users saying that "the game is too simple," so they later created "Plants vs. Zombies: Great Wall Edition," setting three times the number of zombies.

However, Duncan seems unaware that "Great Wall Edition" has been criticized by many gamers as "a money-making tool tailored for some uninformed and choice-limited low-end users." When players saw that "Plants vs. Zombies 2" showed no improvement, they were dumbfounded. The second edition follows the same in-app purchase philosophy as the Great Wall Edition: "The yuanbao in the Great Wall Edition, the diamonds in the second edition, under the intentional difficulty settings and multiple uses of currency, these special currencies are very easily consumed entirely, turning cash into sunk costs more easily, exacerbating the consumption process." Users' resentment towards the "Great Wall Edition" directly transferred to "Plants vs. Zombies 2."

Currently, "Plants vs. Zombies 2" has an Australian launch edition, a Western countries official edition, and a Chinese official edition. The file sizes of the first two are less than 50MB, while the Chinese version is 222MB. Duncan said that the Chinese version is the only one he has played, and he has completed it many times, "In the office, we keep playing, so our game level is relatively high."

Sun Jinchao, co-founder of Haoteng Jiakexin, the developer of the domestic game "Crazy Guess Picture," doesn't understand: "Why is the Chinese version intentionally designed to be much harder compared to the overseas version? This inconsistency is strange, and shouldn't be done this way."

Regarding this issue, James Gwertzman, currently the head of PopCap's strategic development department and former leader of the Shanghai team for five years, might have explained this problem in his earlier GDC sharing session on the success experience of "Plants vs. Zombies: Great Wall Edition." He mentioned that the rampant piracy and jailbreaking issues in the Chinese market have caused huge losses for game companies, so PopCap used localized elements and in-app purchases to solve part of the problem, resulting in a sharp increase in revenue.

"China is our largest market," Duncan said.
