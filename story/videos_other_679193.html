One of the accessories. A man's "three treasures" are ties, watches, and belts, and a well-matched belt often expresses a man's taste and refinement most directly.

The basic styles of men's clothing do not change much each year, so relatively free-to-match belts have become tools to showcase individuality and improve the upper body figure. Besides traditional suit belts, narrow belts have become increasingly popular in recent years. However, trends are not suitable for everyone. Men with different physiques should not blindly follow trends but should wisely choose styles that suit them. Overweight men should not choose overly narrow belts, or they may appear even fatter. Slim men should choose relatively narrower belts, which can increase the visual width of the upper body and add weight to the physique. Some trendy small belts are only suitable for fashionable occasions or parties, and should not be worn in the workplace, otherwise, it will make you look unprofessional.

Many men have plenty of belts in their wardrobes, but when wearing formal suits, they don't know how to match them. In fact, matching belts is not difficult. Every stylish man should at least have a solid navy blue or Burgundy red belt for daytime use, and a silk-woven or solid black belt for formal evening banquets to replace bow ties. Because belts always play a dominant role and are the most eye-catching part of the outfit. Generally speaking, attention should first be focused on matching the belt with the suit jacket. From a more refined perspective, the color of the jacket should serve as the base color for the belt. Stylists remind that similar patterns should not be paired together. For example, a plaid suit should not be matched with a plaid belt. If you wear a subtle plaid suit, pairing it with a plain color, striped, or patterned belt looks great.

Once the belt and jacket are properly matched, choosing a shirt becomes easy. Usually, the shirt color should match one of the secondary colors of the belt. Patterns-wise, generally speaking, the pattern on the belt should be more prominent than the shirt's. Sometimes, both shirts and belts with very distinct patterns can be chosen. However, the shirt's pattern should never overshadow the belt's. Stylists point out that simple-colored shirts can be paired with complex-patterned belts. More elaborate shirts are better suited with simple and single-color belts. Lively patterns like riding, flowers, musical instruments, or animal illustrations on belts can be paired with casual sports shirts. Color-wise, milky white shirts should be paired with gray-striped belts; white shirts with dark printed pattern belts; light blue shirts with light gold grid belts; light blue shirts should be combined with light blue printed pattern belts.

"One of the most classic pairings between belts and shirts is white or light blue shirts paired with solid color or brightly patterned belts. This timeless combination suits any occasion."

Additionally, stylists point out that the best material for belts is silk. Its bright yet not glaring colors allow its use almost unrestricted by time or occasion. Polyester mixed with silk materials, due to their stiffness compared to silk belts, still maintain the luxurious feel of silk belts, and are cheaper and more durable. On the other hand, rayon belts generally look glamorous when new, but after multiple uses, they easily deform and can be discarded after some time.
