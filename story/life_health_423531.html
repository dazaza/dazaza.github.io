Pregnant women need calcium during pregnancy and postpartum

Introduction: Solved problem, collected, reprinted to QQ space, rebroadcasted to Tencent Weibo. Pregnant women and those in the lactation period need calcium supplementation! Calcium deficiency is relatively common among pregnant women and lactating mothers who are in a special period. However, with the popularization of knowledge about quality birth, people are more familiar with the symptoms of calcium deficiency during this period. When they feel loose teeth; weakness in the limbs, frequent cramps, numbness; lower back and back pain, joint pain, rheumatic pain; dizziness, and suffer from anemia, pre-eclampsia syndrome, edema, and insufficient milk secretion, it should be diagnosed as calcium deficiency.

Calcium deficiency needs to be replenished in time. Relying solely on food for supplementation is not comprehensive or timely, which is also why some people still experience calcium deficiency even when consuming calcium-rich foods regularly. Once the body no longer lacks calcium and there are no symptoms, attention to diet alone will suffice.

For pregnant women taking calcium supplements, combining dietary and medicinal supplementation yields the best results. In terms of dietary calcium supplementation, it's advisable to consume more peanuts, spinach, soybeans, fish, seaweed, bone soup, walnuts, shrimp, and algae.

When selecting calcium supplements, choose those that are easily absorbed, have a good taste, and contain vitamin D.

Postpartum, it’s important not to overwork, eat well, especially paying attention to calcium and vitamin supplementation, which is very crucial during the "confinement" period. Whether it’s vaginal delivery or cesarean section, most mothers experience physical weakness after childbirth. Adequate rest and sleep are necessary for the mother's physical and mental adjustment and recovery. Starting from the second day after normal vaginal delivery or about a week after a cesarean section, the mother gradually adapts to the changes and regains some strength. At this point, in addition to daily indoor activities, outdoor activity levels can gradually increase, including doing some postpartum exercises as active physical training. This not only promotes the recovery of gastrointestinal function and prevents constipation but also avoids one of the consequences of poor confinement practices—obesity.

However, note that heavy physical labor should not be performed too early postpartum to avoid vaginal prolapse and uterine prolapse later on.

Postpartum exercises can effectively restore the tension and function of the pelvic floor muscles, abdominal muscles, and waist muscles, playing an important role in preventing postpartum urinary incontinence, bladder and rectal prolapse, and uterine prolapse.

Another key to avoiding "confinement illnesses" is calcium supplementation! Calcium deficiency during pregnancy and lactation not only affects the baby but is also the root cause of many "confinement illnesses" (back pain, joint pain, heel pain, etc.) and osteoporosis after menopause. Therefore, breastfeeding mothers should have a daily calcium intake of no less than 1500 milligrams. Postpartum, a high-protein diet increases the amount of calcium excreted by the kidneys, so calcium supplementation is even more important.
