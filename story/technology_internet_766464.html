In-depth analysis: various improvements of the iPhone 5S camera

by anonymous on 2013-09-15 08:02:10

One of the major improvements in the iPhone 5S is its new camera system. The sensor has been enlarged, increasing the size of individual pixels and expanding the aperture, all of which allow the camera to capture more light. It is equipped with two LED flashes that can adjust flash intensity and color temperature, providing ideal lighting for subjects.

Although there are no hands-on reviews yet, before reading any reviews, we can conduct an in-depth analysis of the camera changes based on the sample photos provided by Apple for the iPhone 5S, as well as our experiences at the launch event.

At some point, cameras became one of the main distinctions in smartphone specifications. You often see manufacturers emphasizing the cameras in their smartphones, with Nokia leading in this area. HTC also introduced a super pixel camera, and even Moto X sought to excel through filters, adopting Clear Pixel imaging technology.

Starting from the iPhone 3GS and iPhone 4, Apple has shown its emphasis on mobile phone cameras through its design and specifications, customizing the ISP starting with the iPhone 4S, and incorporating a custom Sony CMOS image sensor in the iPhone 5. According to statistics, the iPhone camera ranks third in usage on Flickr, proving its importance to users.

The evolution and direction of smartphone cameras are unusual in the photography world. As a photographer, I am very interested in studying which device offers the best camera experience, and the iPhone is often my second choice.

There are a few points about the changes in the iPhone 5S camera that have caught my interest. To increase pixel size, either you use a sensor of equal size but increase the number of pixels, or maintain the same number of pixels but reduce the size of the sensor and optical components. Apple chose a third path similar to HTC's strategy. Apple increased the pixel size to 1.5μm while maintaining the number of pixels, so it was necessary to enlarge the sensor.

Seeing "bigger pixels = better picture" displayed on the screen during the Apple event, and seeing Apple and HTC taking unconventional routes, I feel optimistic about the future of the iPhone camera. Increasing pixel size is indeed better, but it is more challenging for the industry to improve photo quality, low-light sensitivity, and signal-to-noise ratio. Don't forget that I'm talking about pixel pitch, so the increase from 1.4μm to 1.5μm actually means a 14.8% increase in pixel area. A quick calculation shows that Apple changed from a relatively standard 1/3.2" CMOS sensor size to approximately 1/3", the same as the HTC One. Increasing the sensor size really makes a big difference in terms of low-light sensitivity, noise, and dynamic range.

Details about the iPhone 5S camera elements are still unclear, but since Apple could have Sony customize the CMOS for the iPhone 5, it wouldn't be difficult for Sony to customize another sensor for the iPhone 5S.

Meanwhile, the optical design has been improved to increase the amount of light captured. The aperture for the iPhone 4S and iPhone 5 was F/2.4, changing to F/2.2 for the iPhone 5S. I had hoped for an F/2.0 aperture like Nokia and HTC, but F/2.2 might be a reasonable choice to avoid stray light issues common in other cameras. The iPhone 5 had stray light problems, causing purple hues in photos. This was an issue with the F/2.4 aperture, mistakenly thought to be caused by the sapphire glass around the iPhone 5 camera, though that layer of glass is colorless. We will have to wait until customers get their hands on the actual devices to know if the F/2.2 aperture on the iPhone 5S will have the same problem.

There is a slight change in focal length, increasing from 4.10mm on the iPhone 5 to 4.12mm on the iPhone 5S. Differences in sensor size and crop factor also result in different equivalent focal lengths between the iPhone 5 and 5S: 33mm for the iPhone 5 and 29.7mm for the iPhone 5S, compared to 35mm for the iPhone 4S.

To reduce the z-height of the camera module, many smartphone manufacturers tend to shorten the focal length, mostly hovering around 28mm or lower, promoting wide-angle focal lengths as a selling point, which is quite amusing. Apple has consistently maintained a 30mm focal length, and this time it must be admitted that the iPhone 5S does indeed have a wider field of view than the iPhone 5. Theoretically, the standard focal length of 35mm is the most ideal.

So far, the most obvious and frequently discussed new change in the iPhone 5S imaging system is the dual True Tone flashes, truly consisting of two LED flashes with different color temperatures. The spectrum of LEDs is relatively narrow, so it can lead to strange color rendering (just like the low color rendering index of LED bulbs or CCFL tubes). Moreover, white LED flashes compared to xenon flashes have a blue cold tone. The solution is to add another color temperature, mixing the two flashes to achieve an ideal color temperature that matches the shooting scene. In fact, I saw NVIDIA adopt two-color-temperature LED flashes in their Tegra 4 tablets at this year's Mobile World Congress, and other hardware manufacturers were also discussing this feature; however, Apple was the first to bring it to market.

After the event, I tried the dual flashes with smart software that calculates the exact flash intensity needed to match the color temperature of the shooting scene, then takes photos with the appropriate color temperature. The resulting photos indeed showed improvement in color rendering and color temperature.

Finally, there are improvements in the ISP and video recording. The ISP in the iPhone 5S has seen significant improvements, but SoC ISP is an industry secret, so we cannot know exactly what new features it contains. Apple claims that the new ISP improves automatic white balance, automatic exposure, new local tone mapping, new autofocus matrix metering, and automatically selects the clearest image. There is also a burst mode added, capable of taking 10 shots per second.

In terms of video recording, the iPhone 5S supports 120 frames per second recording and includes a "slow motion" option that can record 720p120 video. In this mode, the recorded video appears normal, but during playback, there are two buttons in the UI allowing you to play back the 120FPS video at 300FPS, making the speed one-quarter of the original video speed. However, it is unclear whether the original video is retained on the 5S itself, hopefully it is.

The iPhone 5S has eliminated OIS (Optical Image Stabilization), which I did not expect to be a selling point for the 5S, so it’s not surprising that Apple removed it. Nokia, HTC, and LG all use it. The lack of OIS is not the end of the world for the iPhone, as Apple has chosen automatic image stabilization, combining the clear parts of multiple images into one clear image. This technology is helpful under good lighting conditions but cannot fundamentally solve the blur problem in low light.

It is currently unclear whether the 2x2 pixel binning is still applied to the 5S, but 720p120 video recording requires higher sensitivity and frame rate, so it would be strange if it were removed.

From the sample photos provided on Apple's official website, none of the photos exceed ISO 100, in fact, most are at ISO 32, reducing noise and exposure time. However, Apple did not provide enough indoor low-light photos, making it hard to judge whether the flash has improved.

Overall, I am very pleased with the numerous improvements in the 5S camera system. It is also encouraging to see the trend towards larger pixels in future smartphone cameras, as both Apple and HTC are moving in this direction. Additionally, the dual LED flash is another improvement for low-light shooting, and high-frame-rate video recording still has the potential to achieve 1080P60. I am happy that Apple finally resolved the display ratio issue when previewing pictures.

The iPhone event will be held on September 10th.
