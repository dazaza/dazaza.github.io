● Plot Summary

The movie begins with the Battle of Changbanpo. After defeating Liu Bei, Cao Cao considers Eastern Wu a threat to his quest for dominion over all of China. Furthermore, after Lady Qiao refuses Cao Cao's advances and attempts suicide, Cao Cao becomes enraged and decides to attack Eastern Wu.

Sun Quan sends Lu Su to meet with Liu Bei under the pretense of paying respects to the late Liu Biao to discuss an alliance against Cao Cao. After agreeing to join forces with Eastern Wu, Liu Bei sends Zhuge Liang to Eastern Wu.

Sun Shangxiang, Sun Quan's sister, and Lu Su use reverse psychology to strengthen Sun Quan's resolve to resist Cao Cao and recall Zhou Yu to lead the resistance.

Ultimately, the two armies meet at Chi Bi. Using Huang Gai's feigned defection strategy, Cao Cao suffers a great defeat and retreats along the Huarong Trail back to Jiangling.

● Historical Background

The Battle of Red Cliffs was one of the most famous battles in Chinese history where the weaker side emerged victorious. In 208 AD (the thirteenth year of Jian'an during Emperor Xian of Han's reign), Cao Cao led a combined land and naval force, boasting over a million troops, launching a campaign in Jingzhou before moving against Sun Quan.

In July, Cao Cao personally led more than ten thousand troops southward into Jingzhou, described by Wang Fuzhi in "Readings on Comprehensive Mirror to Aid in Government" as "riding on the momentum of defeating Yuan Shao to subdue Jingzhou and Wu". He aimed to eliminate Liu Biao and Sun Quan of Eastern Wu to unify the land. As Cao Cao rapidly approached Wan and Ye, Liu Biao fell ill and passed away in August. In September, Liu Biao's second son, Liu Cong, surrendered without a fight in Xinye (now part of Henan Province).

Jingzhou's navy, consisting of thousands of warships, fell entirely into Cao Cao's hands, significantly enhancing his naval capabilities. Liu Bei, who had been stationed in Fancheng (modern-day Xiangyang in Hubei Province) under Liu Biao's command, retreated south upon hearing the news.

At that time, Jiangling (modern-day Jiangling in Hubei Province) stored large quantities of grain, weapons, and other supplies belonging to Liu Biao. Therefore, Liu Bei retreated from Fancheng towards Jiangling while ordering Guan Yu to lead the navy down the Han River to rendezvous there. In September, Cao Cao personally led five thousand elite cavalry, including his personal bodyguards known as the Tiger-Leopard Cavalry, pursuing Liu Bei. They defeated Liu Bei's forces at Changbanpo in Dangyang (northeastern Dangyang in modern-day Hubei Province) and occupied Jiangling.

After reaching Jiangling, Cao Cao immediately took measures to settle the local officials and civilians, issuing an order stating "To the officials and civilians of Jingzhou, we offer you a new beginning."

Despite Jia Xu's advice to first utilize Jingzhou's resources, rest his troops, and stabilize the newly acquired territory, Cao Cao ignored the suggestion. His military deployment was roughly as follows: Zhao Yan was appointed Governor of Zhangling (county seat located southeast of modern-day Zaoyang in Hubei Province) and Commander-in-Chief of the seven armies led by Yu Jin, Zhang He, Zhu Ling, Li Dian, Zhang Liao, and Feng Kai, unifying their commands for operations between the Han and Huai rivers. For Jingzhou, Cao Cao stationed Cao Ren and Xiahou Dun in Jiangling, Cao Hong in Xiangyang, and sent another combined land and naval force from Xiangyang southward along the Han River towards Xiakou. Cao Cao personally led part of his forces, including newly recruited soldiers from Jingzhou, down the Yangtze River to attack Eastern Wu.

When Cao Cao moved south, Sun Quan dispatched Lu Su to Jingzhou, ostensibly to pay respects to Liu Biao but also to persuade Liu Bei to unite against Cao Cao. Lu Su met Liu Bei in Dangyang, after which Liu Bei followed his advice and retreated southeast towards the Han River, joining Guan Yu's navy coming down the Han River. They then joined forces with Liu Qi, the eldest son of Liu Biao and Governor of Jiangxia, commanding over ten thousand troops, retreating to Xiakou (modern-day Hankou in Wuhan, Hubei Province) to plan a joint operation with Sun Quan against Cao Cao. Liu Bei sent Zhuge Liang to accompany Lu Su to Eastern Wu to devise plans for resisting Cao Cao.

Upon arriving at Chaishan (modern-day southwest Jiuxi County in Jiangxi Province), Sun Quan had already received a threatening letter from Cao Cao stating: "I am now training a navy of eight hundred thousand men, ready to hunt with your general in Wu." Unwilling to surrender "all the lands of Wu and its hundred thousand troops" to Cao Cao's control, Sun Quan was concerned whether the Sun-Liu coalition could match Cao Cao's forces. Within Sun Quan's ranks, there was intense debate between the pro-war faction represented by Lu Su and the pro-peace faction led by Zhang Zhao. Zhuge Liang analyzed the strengths and weaknesses of both sides for Sun Quan: "Although the army of Yuzhou (Liu Bei) suffered defeat at Changban, there are still ten thousand elite warriors returning, plus Guan Yu's navy and Liu Qi's ten thousand or more soldiers from Jiangxia." He pointed out three disadvantages for Cao Cao: (1) His troops were fatigued from long campaigns; (2) Northerners were unfamiliar with naval warfare; (3) The people of Jingzhou had not yet fully submitted to Cao Cao. Zhuge Liang concluded that if Sun and Liu united, they would surely achieve victory.

Lu Su then employed reverse psychology, persuading Sun Quan while simultaneously advising him to immediately recall Zhou Yu from Poyang (modern-day northeast Boyang County in Jiangxi Province). Zhou Yu supported the views of Zhuge Liang and Lu Su, analyzing the weaknesses of Cao Cao's forces: First, Cao Cao's troops were exhausted; Second, "Now it is bitterly cold, and the horses lack fodder"; Third, Ma Chao and Han Sui remained in the western passes, posing a rear threat to Cao Cao. He further analyzed the actual strength of Cao Cao's forces, pointing out that only fifteen or sixteen thousand troops came from Central Plains, while the newly surrendered seventy or eighty thousand troops from Liu Biao were not loyal to Cao Cao.

With increased confidence in the success of the Sun-Liu coalition against Cao Cao, Sun Quan appointed Zhou Yu and Cheng Pu as Left and Right Grand Marshals, and Lu Su as Military Advisor Captain. Zhou Yu led thirty thousand elite troops up the river to Xiakou, meeting Liu Bei's army of over twenty thousand troops to jointly resist Cao Cao.

Defeat at Wulin: Zhou Yu's army met with Liu Bei's forces at Fan Kou. Then they sailed upstream, reaching Chibi where they encountered Cao Cao's forces. Cao Cao's newly reorganized and newly attached Jingzhou navy lacked combat effectiveness and suffered from an epidemic, leading to an initial disadvantage. Cao Cao had no choice but to "withdraw his troops to the north bank of the river", mooring his ships on the northern side of Wulin. Zhou Yu docked his ships on the southern bank at Chibi, facing each other.

Northern troops were unaccustomed to life aboard ships, so Cao Cao ordered iron chains to be used to connect the ships together to reduce the effects of wind and waves. Zhou Yu's subordinate Huang Gai suggested using fire tactics to defeat Cao Cao's forces: "Now the enemy outnumbers us, making it difficult to sustain a prolonged battle. However, observing Cao Cao's fleet with ships connected bow to stern, they can be burned and driven away." Zhou Yu accepted Huang Gai's suggestion and decided to let Huang Gai pretend to surrender to approach Cao Cao's ships. Huang Gai prepared ten warships filled with firewood and oil, disguised with curtains, flying flags prearranged with Cao Cao.

It was deep winter, usually windy from the north, but according to meteorological patterns, after several cold days, there are often warmer days with winds possibly shifting to the east or south. It is said that on the day of Jiazi in the eleventh month (equivalent to December 7, 208 AD), the sky was clear and warm, with southerly winds rising in the evening and becoming strong by midnight. Huang Gai set out with the prepared ships, sailing downwind towards Cao Cao's ships. Upon reaching the middle of the river, Huang Gai lit torches, ordering his soldiers to shout loudly: "We surrender!" The Cao Cao troops were completely unprepared, "All craning their necks to watch, pointing and saying Gai has surrendered." When about two li away from Cao Cao's forces, Huang Gai ordered the firewood ignited, setting fires simultaneously. The flames were fierce and the wind strong, the ships rushing forward like arrows, burning all the northern ships and spreading to the camps on shore. In no time, "Smoke and flames filled the sky, with countless men and horses burned or drowned."

The main force of Sun Quan's navy on the southern bank took the opportunity to advance while drumming, and Liu Bei also advanced from Suishan towards Wulin. The allied forces crossed the Yangtze River, decisively defeating Cao Cao's forces. Realizing the situation was beyond saving, Cao Cao immediately set fire to the remaining ships and retreated. Zhou Yu and Liu Bei's forces pursued both by land and water, with Cao Cao retreating along the Huarong Trail (north of modern-day Jianli County in Hubei Province) towards Jiangling. Due to the epidemic and starvation, Cao Cao's forces lost more than half. Fortunately, he was rescued by Zhang Liao and Xu Chu, narrowly escaping danger. Upon reaching the walls of Jiangling, fearing instability in the rear, Cao Cao returned to the north, leaving Cao Ren and Xu Huang to defend Jiangling and Man Chong stationed in Dangyang.

Consequences: Before the Battle of Red Cliffs, Cao Cao held significant advantages: First, Cao Cao "held the emperor and gave orders to the feudal lords," making it morally difficult for the feudal lords to compete; Second, Cao Cao's victorious army moved south, naturally bolstering their morale; Third, Cao Cao's forces outnumbered those of Sun Quan and Liu Bei by several times. Given Cao Cao's numerous advantages, the achievements of the Sun-Liu coalition were even more remarkable. This battle showcased the strengths of Sun-Liu forces in naval warfare and cleverly implemented fire attacks, considered a decisive and famous example of winning with fewer numbers in Chinese history. After the Battle of Red Cliffs, Cao Cao retreated to the north and never again had the opportunity to invade Jingzhou on such a large scale. The Cao Cao group also lost the possibility of unifying the country in the short term.

Zhou Yu and Cao Ren faced each other across the river. Zhou Yu sent Gan Ning to seize Yiling (modern-day Yichang in Hubei Province). Cao Ren led his troops to besiege Gan Ning. Zhou Yu then led reinforcements, decisively defeating Cao Ren's forces. Liu Bei returned to Xiakou, planning to move up the Han River to flank Cao Ren's rear. The following year, Cao Ren was forced to retreat. Sun Quan continued to ally with Liu Bei to resist Cao Cao. Liu Bei took advantage of the situation to gain control of most of Jingzhou, including the four commanderies of Wuling (commandery seat in modern-day Changde, Hunan Province), Changsha, Guiyang (commandery seat in modern-day Chenzhou, Hunan Province), and Lingling (both in modern-day Hunan Province). Soon after, he captured Liu Zhang's Yi Province. Sun Quan controlled the Jiangdong region, forming the tripartite division of power among Wei, Shu, and Wu.
