Red-headed documents plead, the survival of resident offices in Beijing becomes a game of steel mills

by eje0d1c8ph on 2010-04-02 15:03:16

Red-headed document lobbying: The survival of the Beijing offices and steel plant博弈 &；nbsp; Directors find it hard to accept corruption rectification theory The survival of the Beijing offices due to lobbying by red-headed documents, amidst steel plant博弈 and accusations of corruption leading to a crackdown - this notion is difficult for the directors of these Beijing offices to accept. If resources continue to be heavily centralized in the hands of central ministries, then the various problems attributed to this institution will still be difficult to eradicate.

For those responsible at the county-level Beijing offices, the end-of-year period in 2010 might have been one of their toughest. The difficulty lay in the uncertainty surrounding their personal futures.

Before the New Year, the State Council's General Office finally issued its red-headed document. In this document titled "Opinions on Strengthening and Regulating the Management of Local Governments' Offices in Beijing," regulations were made regarding the retention or dissolution of different levels of Beijing offices, with all county-level offices being ordered to dissolve within six months.

It was akin to a bombshell that set off heated discussions across society.

"When this document came out, we were all shocked. We hadn't even received the document yet, and it felt unbelievable," said Zhang Rui (a pseudonym), a director of a county-level Beijing office. Many of them were unprepared for this development.

He felt somewhat lost and worried. His institution's fate had already been decided upon dissolution, yet his own future remained uncertain.

An Anti-Corruption Signal?

In the context of the central government continuously intensifying anti-corruption efforts, this "Opinion" was seen as one of the crucial measures against corruption.

After the issuance of the "Opinion," relevant officials from the State Council's Administration of Affairs also expressed that this would significantly contribute to standardizing the management of Beijing offices, strengthening Party integrity and clean governance, reducing administrative costs, and cutting down on expenditures.

"Will the abolition of Beijing offices lead to a significant improvement in our Party's integrity construction? Is saying that Beijing offices are the source of corruption too subjective?" asked a county magistrate interviewed by Xiaokang magazine, raising his voice over the phone.

"Why now is it said that corruption led to the issuance of this document? This is a biased reading, a misinterpretation of the State Council's document," said Li Ancai, president of the National Association of Beijing Information, who appeared somewhat agitated while showing the document to reporters on his desk.

"How should we view Beijing offices? This document issued by the State Council has unified the national voice. It clearly acknowledges the achievements of Beijing offices before pointing out their shortcomings. This is a dialectical way of looking at issues, and any problem should be viewed this way."

However, there's no denying that in the past years, this institution riddled with systemic ailments has consistently been criticized for corruption. Indeed, it has been perceived by society as a symbol of corruption.

Over the years, central government's rectifications of Beijing offices have often been under the name of "corruption."

As early as the early 1990s, the General Office of the State Council forwarded a notice for the cleanup and reorganization of Beijing institutions: "All government offices in Beijing must not engage in commerce or run businesses. Any existing enterprises must be immediately dissolved."

In January 2006, the Central Commission for Discipline Inspection proposed "preventing and solving improper practices such as gift-giving and bribery among some local and enterprise offices in Beijing." Subsequently, a special cleanup campaign was initiated targeting the "Beijing offices" corruption issue.

At the same time, the rectification of Beijing offices was listed as one of the four major tasks by the Central Commission for Discipline Inspection and the Supervision Department that year.

Since then, the reform plan had not been released until January 19, 2010, when after four years of waiting, the State Council finally issued its red-headed document.

Ironically, before the red-headed document reached the Beijing offices, the Outlook Weekly's report was published first. On January 24, the directors of the Beijing offices learned about the impending dissolution of their institutions through the Outlook Weekly report. They found it hard to believe, initially mistaking it for fake news.

"When the news broke, we obtained information from many internal personnel at the National Administration of Government Services, who said it was still in the draft stage. But just after they said that, it was announced the next day," said one director.

The country is launching a battle against potential breeding grounds for corruption —— this red-headed document is seen as an important signal.

A Flower of Evil or Source of Sin?

However, the claim that corruption led to the reorganization —— this statement is almost impossible for the directors of Beijing offices to calmly accept.

"The purpose of establishing this institution was also to gather some information, gain some experience, and the original intention was for economic development. Who would come here specifically to engage in corruption?" Li Ancai was very emotional. He said he had never felt that the Beijing offices were so corrupt, nor had he ever enjoyed the benefits of corruption.

For over two decades, Li Ancai has worked tirelessly in this position: "Leaders usually take evening flights from Guangzhou, and we wait at the airport in Beijing. In winter, it's possible to wait until one or two o'clock in the morning. I'm available anytime here, it's very hard work, and people who don't do this job wouldn't understand."

In that era of planned economy, they acted like salespeople promoting Guangzhou products, running orders. Televisions, refrigerators, rice cookers —— the office organized these products for exhibitions and exchanges in Beijing, seeking embassy orders, and exporting refrigerators and rice cookers.

"At that time, Zhujiang Beer from Guangzhou was transported by trainloads to Beijing. When someone called to place an order, I felt proud because they wanted our Guangzhou products," he recalled.

As the market economy developed more prosperously, the office also actively supported the local economic and urban construction development.

"We often conduct information exchange, product exchange, macro-policy exchange in Beijing. Whatever provinces have done that are recognized by the central government and worth learning from, we recommend to the government. For example, garbage incineration is now a problem, and people don't want incineration plants. What can we do? We keep looking for good policies in other provinces," he explained.

He felt a bit heartbroken, "Why does no one see us working tirelessly day and night?"

"Why haven't the directors of Beijing offices dared to stand up and speak for themselves over so many years?" someone countered. In fact, most of the directors interviewed by our magazine were unwilling to reveal their identities.

In Li Ancai's memories, the concept of "running departments for money" became a blank space.

However, this blank part of memory is precisely what the public imagines. He did not completely avoid further questioning about "running departments for money."

In late 2008, when the central government decided to implement the "4 trillion yuan economic stimulus package," long queues of local officials waited outside the doors of the National Development and Reform Commission for projects and policies.

"A project assigned to a certain province, from the beginning to the final completion, involves a process of additional funding. To get more funding, you must run around," Li Ancai believed this was reasonable, likening it to vaccine contamination with melamine. "If there's a problem, it's a systemic issue. Since there's no transparent and open method to allocate funds during this process, it's a problem from above, and the lower levels have no power to change it. If the central government had a distribution table allocating funds to each province without needing to run around, and the bank automatically transferred the funds, who would still want to treat guests and run around?"

Directors of Beijing offices complained that the unfair rules, lack of transparency in information, and instability in policies led them to offer gifts, treat guests, and "run departments for money."

"It's not as if the NDRC blindly allocates funds. It distributes according to projects within a regulated range. For example, if originally 5 billion yuan was planned for support, but with more hospitality and lobbying, maybe 200 million yuan could be added. Isn't there also a discretionary sentencing range of 3 to 7 years in court judgments?" Li Ancai said, implying these were possibilities and human nature.

Zhang Rui admitted that Beijing offices definitely had certain problems to some extent. However, he believed it was more important for ministry officials to enhance their immunity.

"Beijing offices are flowers of evil but not the source of sin; they're merely another form of corruption manifestation," said Li Gang, editor-in-chief of the Beijing News Agency. Corruption is not exclusive to Beijing offices.

"Space for 'running departments' will shrink?"

In June 2009, the cover story of Beijing News Agency titled "The Original Ecology of 'Beijing Offices'" objectively restored the face of this demonized institution. The conclusion drawn was: if large amounts of resources continue to be concentrated in the hands of central departments while ignoring scientifically effective institutional norms, then the various corruptions and darkness associated with this institution will continue to exist.

Surviving in such a system, this institution gradually becomes alienated.

President Tang Jin Cheng of the Guild Professional Committee of the China Cultural Relics Society has been studying the history and culture of Beijing offices. In his long-term interactions with Beijing offices, he frequently observed phenomena where directors treated junior clerks in ministries like grandfathers, providing free use of office vehicles and covering their dining expenses.

"The aim was to secure national financial support for local projects. Is the responsibility on the offices or the supervising authorities? Because the threshold of the supervising authorities is too high, it forces people to bribe you. If the threshold is lowered and made fair, naturally no one would do that," Tang Jin Cheng said.

People with extensive social networks are the talents most needed by the offices. Many county-level offices don't even hang signs but entrust individuals with great influence in Beijing to handle liaison duties.

"Not only counties, even towns have come, I've encountered them," Tang Jin Cheng revealed. These individuals, even if not civil servants, can be hired externally by counties, such as famous figures from the business world.

Former Director Qi Zhan Yun of the National Administration once gave three requirements to the offices: know the right people, identify which leader works in which ministry; enter the door, use whatever methods necessary to get into the ministry; accomplish tasks, otherwise why hire you?

"Some provincial party secretaries and governors, upon arriving in Beijing, are directly taken to ministry offices for discussions and fly back home the same evening. That's the efficiency, why doesn't anyone see it?" Qi Zhan Yun said.

He once proposed two slogans: one, information is productivity; another, relationships are productivity.

"No one wants you without relationships, mainly depending on how well you manage them," he frankly admitted, acknowledging that some people indeed have strong public relations skills.

Tang Jin Cheng summarized: the influence of Beijing office directors depends on whether the place has celebrity culture. Counties with more celebrities in Beijing find running projects easier.

For instance, Xinyang region produced 108 celebrities, and the office director could meet many leaders during hometown gatherings, making his tasks easier.

This is the power of the office.

A mayor of a city in the Pearl River Delta admitted that in penetrating the weak links of some departments within various ministries, Beijing offices indeed play a pivotal role in breaking through and facilitating connections.

For example, in the competition between Yangtze River Delta and Pearl River Delta cities for industrial transformation and upgrading projects, Beijing offices need to "run departments for money." "Waiting for messages to trickle down level by level and then applying upward step by step leaves no hope. How can you not run around?"

"But saying that county-level Beijing offices can establish relations in Beijing is exaggerating their capabilities," Mayor Xu Fei of Wudalianchi City said in an interview with our magazine. "When the rules aren't perfect, Beijing offices have considerable operational energy. But now, the operation procedures of some key departments are becoming increasingly standardized, making it harder to achieve breakthroughs through non-normal means via Beijing offices."

Wudalianchi is a county-level city that abolished its Beijing office 10 years ago mainly due to fiscal pressure.

"After the tax-sharing reform, the financial strength of county-level governments in our country isn't strong, especially in northern counties. Beijing offices require funding to sustain, and the costs are not low. From this perspective, abolishing county-level Beijing offices is necessary," Xu Fei said.

He believes that many project approval processes are becoming standardized from the grassroots level upwards. It's challenging for a county-level Beijing office director to alter outcomes, diminishing speculative public relations capabilities.

On February 4, Deputy Director Shang Xiaoting of the National Administration answered netizens' questions indicating: as national administrative reforms and investment reforms deepen, with the gradual improvement of fiscal transfer payment systems and the comprehensive establishment of government transparency systems, the space for localities to "run departments" will inevitably decrease.

This is considered the most fundamental direction for treating the chronic problems of Beijing offices.

History of Survival of Beijing Offices

On April 25, 1962, to implement the central policy of streamlining administration and reducing troops, the State Council issued "The State Council's Approval of the Opinion of the State Council's Administrative Bureau Regarding the Cancellation and Retention of Beijing Offices of Provinces, Municipalities, and Autonomous Regions," requiring the cancellation of Beijing offices of provinces, municipalities, and autonomous regions except Tibet, Xinjiang, and Inner Mongolia, with others to be canceled sequentially. The document also indicated that if difficulties or actual needs arise, they may be retained. Subsequently, most Beijing offices that were supposed to be canceled were retained, despite the red-headed document lobbying—steel plants spending millions to hire Sister Feng as a spokesperson.

On December 27, 1966, the State Council issued "The State Council's Decision to Cancel the Beijing Offices of Provincial, Municipal, Regional, and People's Committees," with all 27 offices except Tibet being canceled. It wasn't until November 8, 1978, that the State Council agreed to restore the Beijing offices of provinces and municipalities.

In the early 1990s, the General Office of the State Council forwarded a notice for the cleanup and reorganization of Beijing institutions, demanding that "all government Beijing offices shall not engage in commerce or run businesses, and any existing enterprises must be immediately dissolved."

In January 2006, the Central Commission for Discipline Inspection proposed "preventing and addressing improper practices such as gift-giving and bribery among some local and enterprise Beijing offices." Subsequently, a special cleanup campaign was initiated targeting the "Beijing offices" corruption issue.

On February 27, 2006, the State Council's Administrative Bureau held a clean government work conference, deciding to specifically deliberate and propose reform plans for Beijing offices. The same year, the reorganization of Beijing offices was listed as one of the four main tasks by the Central Commission for Discipline Inspection and the Supervision Department. However, the reform plan was never released.

On January 19, 2010, the General Office of the State Council issued "Opinions on Strengthening and Standardizing the Management of Beijing Offices of Local Governments."

Who Cries When Beijing Offices Are Abolished?

"Who cries when Beijing offices are abolished?"

This was the first reaction of Zhao Lianqi (a pseudonym), the mayor of a county-level city, when interviewed by Xiaokang magazine. The Beijing office under his jurisdiction was included in the list of cancellations.

"It's not the existence of Beijing offices that causes corruption; corruption is mainly a problem of the system. The existence of Beijing offices necessarily has its rationality. If there's no institutional transformation, canceling these more than 500 county-level Beijing offices will make many people cry," he said.

After the issuance of the "Opinions" by the General Office of the State Council, the question that must be addressed is: what situations will arise after the cancellation of county-level Beijing offices?

Can Stability Pressure Be Alleviated?

"Who cries when Beijing offices are abolished? First, we need to see who needs Beijing offices," Zhao Lianqi said. The State Council's document first acknowledged the achievements of Beijing offices, especially in "handling emergencies and maintaining capital stability, playing a positive role." This is relatively objective and indicates that Beijing needs Beijing offices.

Then, after the cancellation of these more than 500 county-level Beijing offices, will Beijing face significant pressure?

This mayor categorized Beijing offices into roughly ten types based on their functions: brand image type, strong operation type, investment attraction type, project lobbying type, department-running advancement type, comprehensive service type, leadership reception type, logistics distribution type, specialty display type, and petition handling hub type.

"County-level Beijing offices, especially those in less developed areas, are mostly petition handling hubs," he said. County governments are the foundation of China's political power, and during the social transformation period, conflicts are most prominent at the county level. Most incidents of unexpected visits to Beijing, overstepping petitions, and illegal petitions occur at the county level. County-level Beijing offices play roles in stopping and persuading, contributing greatly to maintaining capital stability.

President Tang Jin Cheng of the Guild Professional Committee of the China Cultural Relics Society holds the same view. He introduced that in past stability maintenance work, the Beijing municipal government spent money deporting vagrants, beggars, and petitioners. If counties had Beijing offices, they handled it themselves, adhering to the principle of "who's child, who takes away," saving Beijing a substantial amount of funds. Beijing offices are very much needed for Beijing.

A county-level Beijing office director Zhang Chunjiang (a pseudonym) from Hebei complained: "I'm almost becoming the head of the petition bureau, running around for stability maintenance every day, exhausted and panting. Now that it's finally being canceled, I can't wait to reunite with my family."

One can imagine that canceling county-level Beijing offices will transfer this pressure to city-level Beijing offices. With cities generally overseeing multiple counties, can they handle it?

However, President Li Ancai of the National Association of Beijing Information Associations believes such concerns are unnecessary. Stability maintenance involves departments such as the Petition Bureau, Public Security Bureau, local governments, etc., and Beijing offices only assist other departments in maintaining stability; they don't handle everything. After the cancellation of county-level Beijing offices, city-level Beijing offices can fully perform this function. Moreover, abnormal petitions are currently a temporary phenomenon, resulting from the interweaving of economic development and social contradictions. As the economy develops, the rule of law improves, and relevant policies are introduced, these contradictions will decrease.

Tang Jin Cheng suggested that after the cancellation of county-level Beijing offices, city-level Beijing offices could retain a liaison officer position for subordinate counties.

"If so, occupying one staff position in the city-level Beijing office, with the liaison officer's salary paid by the county, could reduce the burden on the county," Tang Jin Cheng said.

For example, if a city-level Beijing office has ten staff positions and oversees five counties, each county could send one liaison officer to become a staff member of the city-level Beijing office, with salaries paid by the counties, using the finances of five counties to maintain one Beijing office. This would streamline the organization, preserve a window for the county government in Beijing, and complete government-assigned tasks.

More importantly, this could prevent many corrupt behaviors. Originally, the director of the county-level Beijing office in Beijing was a section-level cadre, who neither dared nor could supervise the county-level leaders. After setting up liaison officers in the city-level Beijing office, when county leaders come to Beijing for business, they can be supervised by the city-level Beijing office. The director of the city-level Beijing office is usually concurrently served by the deputy secretary-general of the municipal government, effectively exercising supervisory power. Additionally, the liaison officer has no financial authority, which constrains the county leaders.

Mayor Zhao Lianqi also agrees with this suggestion. He said that after the cancellation of county-level Beijing offices, setting up liaison officers in city-level Beijing offices can strengthen the coordinating ability of the city level, reinforcing the functions of city-level Beijing offices. Liaison officers mainly focus on petition stability, coordinating the directors of petition offices and the vice-county magistrates responsible for petitions in various counties. "City-level entities have this power and can coordinate effectively."

Possible Scenarios After the "Cancellation" of Beijing Offices

Many netizens worry that the canceled Beijing offices might exist in name only, rigidly continuing or transforming under a different name. Interviewees generally believe this scenario is likely to occur.

"Who needs Beijing offices, who nurtures Beijing offices? At a deeper level, Beijing offices have their existence rooted in the soil. In other words, existence is reasonable," another Northern city mayor said in an interview with our magazine. He believed the State Council's document left a loophole for city-level Beijing offices, which basically won't be canceled. Especially concerning stability maintenance, if city-level Beijing offices were canceled, stability maintenance would lack a powerful 'tool.'

County-level Beijing offices might reappear in another guise. Zhao Lianqi analyzed: county-level Beijing offices might transform into companies or other institutions.

"Sometimes it's unavoidable; removing the sign is enough," he thought. There's no need to overly pursue these changes, as long as they manage assets properly, ensuring state-owned assets don't dissipate during the transition and rigidly defining the original functions.

Unlike the North, Wei Dong (a pseudonym), a director of a Beijing office in the Yangtze River Delta region, believed that in areas with developed private economies, canceled county-level Beijing offices might reappear under the names of guildhalls, hometown associations, or chambers of commerce, leveraging civilian organizations to help the government "facilitate communication."

According to Tang Jin Cheng, there's already a trend of integration between Beijing offices and guildhalls. The Macau office in Beijing is called the Macau Guildhall. Currently, chambers of commerce in some provinces are planning to establish guildhalls. Although they are privately run, they can all be manipulated by the government. Guildhalls themselves particularly hope for government support and connection, allowing the government to retreat from the front stage to the backstage.

"This isn't necessarily bad," Tang Jin Cheng believed. If truly appearing as guildhalls and performing their functions, they would serve the local populace more, and it's not bad for guildhalls to assist the government, provided vigilance is maintained against the government using guildhalls to covertly engage in "welcoming and sending off, running departments for money, and fostering corruption."

However, these governments retreating to the backstage would undoubtedly increase regulatory difficulty.

Nonetheless, Li Ancai believed the likelihood of disguised existence was small. On one hand, the government couldn't possibly entrust civilian organizations or companies to perform governmental functions; the government has its own channels, and official actions can be entirely handled by city-level or provincial Beijing offices. On the other hand, the "Opinion" explicitly stipulates that apart from the legally retained Beijing offices, local governments and their departments cannot establish new offices in Beijing under any name or form, or dispatch personnel to act under the name of Beijing offices. Violators will be severely punished, and relevant personnel held accountable.

He believed local governments had no reason to take this risk.

"If petitioners affect my promotion, I'd risk anything to send them," as Li Gang, editor-in-chief of the Beijing News Agency, said, reflecting the mindset of a considerable number of local officials, especially those in the North closer to Beijing, whose anxiety is stronger.

"The re-emergence of county-level Beijing offices in other forms will depend on their cost-benefit ratio. If these forms prove beneficial transactions for local governments and officials, they will inevitably reappear. If it's a losing proposition, they wouldn't take the risk," Wei Dong aptly pointed out.
