Additionally, Captain Feng of Xiangtan Bus Company stated that besides reminding citizens to be vigilant against pickpockets, they also frequently communicate "coded language" for preventing thieves with bus drivers. For example, there was once a case where a bus driver deliberately let a passenger being pickpocketed swipe the card again. The passenger couldn't understand initially, but eventually expressed gratitude to the driver. These coded languages are not unique to any specific city but offer good guidance for some bus drivers. Summarizing these points roughly includes the following:

1. Casual style: When encountering a thief, the driver usually uses seemingly casual words to warn passengers. For instance, seeing a thief reaching into a passenger's pocket, a driver might suddenly shout at the passenger: "Hey, everyone, take care of your belongings, don't let them fall!"

2. Appropriate reminder style: Some thieves block the driver's view upon boarding, while their accomplices surround passengers preparing to act. A driver might pat the relevant passenger on the shoulder and say, "Young man, move inside quickly and hold onto your stuff."

3. Conversation style: Some drivers notice a passenger being targeted by a thief and actively greet this passenger, asking where they're getting off and advising them to move inside. This seemingly extra conversation actually helps the passenger escape danger.

3. Watch actions: When a pickpocket is about to act, they often tightly stick to the target's body, using others or accomplices as cover, or using their own arms, bags, clothes, newspapers, etc., to obscure the target's vision.

4. Listen to language: Pickpockets often use coded language or jargon for easy communication. They call pocket-picking "back shell" or "find time"; they refer to each other as "craftsmen" or "pliers"; boarding a vehicle to steal is called "charging the car" or "finding money on the car"; upper garment pockets are referred to as "skylight" or "upper pocket"; lower garment pockets as "platform (pocket)"; trouser pockets as "hook pocket"; victims or owners are called "sheep (kid)" or "dull rabbit (kid)," etc.
