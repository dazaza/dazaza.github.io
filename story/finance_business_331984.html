Yangtze River Delta rushes to build IoT regional demonstration centers, possibly falling into the trap of land grabbing and money making

by logistics on 2010-03-08 15:04:58

"Our efforts last year were just a warm-up; this year we are ready to take the field." This statement, made by Yang Weize, the Party Secretary of Wuxi, has been widely circulated in Wuxi’s political circles and is considered an important signal that Wuxi will make a full-scale push for the Internet of Things (IoT) industry this year.

However, on this competitive stage of IoT, an increasing number of regional competitors are "ready to take the field," and fierce competition appears inevitable.

On March 2nd, Shanghai officially announced its collaboration with the Shanghai Institute of Micro-Nano Technology of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (CAS), establishing the "Shanghai IoT" center in Jiading District, located in the western suburbs. Just before, on February 23rd, China Mobile formally signed a cooperation agreement with the Suzhou municipal government for the "Perceiving China" application center—Suzhou project, hoping to turn Suzhou into a regional application center for IoT.

"After Premier Wen Jiabao delivered a speech targeting IoT in August last year, local governments contacting us for cooperation have been overwhelming," said Xing Tao, a doctoral supervisor at the Shanghai Institute of Microsystem and Information Technology (SIMIT) of CAS and deputy secretary-general of the National Standardization Working Group on Sensor Networks. Cities across the country eager for transformation are actively formulating their own development plans for IoT.

In this round of development, local governments are "footing the bill" to promote industrial development through leading demonstration applications, which has become the mainstay of the domestic IoT industry's current ecosystem.

Rushing to Build Regional Demonstration Centers for IoT

On March 2nd, Shanghai officially announced the establishment of the "Shanghai IoT" center in Jiading District, located in its western suburbs. Some insiders regarded this as a belated "make-up class."

Previously, due to the presence of the SIMIT in Shanghai, the city once held a very evident advantage in this emerging field. For example, Jiading District, where the center was established, already had a good foundation for IoT application demonstrations and obvious advantages in infrastructure construction such as "Wireless Urban Areas."

However, many years ago, CAS established a center in Jiaxing, directly taking away part of the R&D and manufacturing businesses related to IoT. Afterward, under the leadership of Liu Haitao, the SIMIT also set up the Wuxi IoT Industry Research Institute in Wuxi, causing subtle changes in the situation.

In August last year, when Premier Wen Jiabao inspected Wuxi, he proposed building Wuxi into the "Perceiving China" center, making Wuxi the hottest spot for the national IoT industry almost instantly.

Subsequently, China Mobile, China Telecom, and China Unicom (600050, Stock Bar) successively signed cooperation framework agreements with the Wuxi government. Beijing University of Posts and Telecommunications and other universities also signed agreements with the Wuxi government for integrated cooperation in politics, academia, industry, and application, further solidifying Wuxi's early-mover advantage in the IoT industry.

Moreover, Wuxi was approved at the end of last year to become the "National Sensornet Innovation Demonstration Zone (China Sensing Information Center)," known as the "IoT Pilot Base" externally, gaining the advantage of recognition.

This made Shanghai, which had once led, somewhat restless.

Recently, Shanghai has listed IoT industry development as one of the key focuses for the next step in information industry development. After the official launch of the Jiading project, Shanghai hopes to "create the most competitive IoT R&D center, engineering center, application center, and core technology industrialization hub domestically with international influence, forming a differentiated development pattern compared to other regions' IoT R&D plans in the country."

It is understood that the Shanghai IoT center being built in Jiading will rely on the Shanghai Institute of Microsystem and Information Technology of CAS, implementing the construction of the IoT center R&D base, developing key technologies for IoT industrialization ("positioning" technology) and related critical technologies of the industry, and leading the formulation of national standards.

In addition, the project will advance the construction of IoT industry R&D and application demonstration platforms, basically completing public R&D service platforms for IoT industrialization, IoT operation service platforms, IoT data control centers, IoT information acquisition network platforms, and other common technical support platforms. It will also utilize the infrastructure advantages of Jiading District's "Wireless Urban Area" to complete 3-4 demonstration projects first in Shanghai.

Currently, the first phase of the Jiading project has entered substantial implementation, with preliminary design completed. The total construction area reaches 170,000 square meters, and the total investment amounts to 800 million yuan.

"Without doubt, the Jiading project in Shanghai and the Wuxi project will definitely have some overlaps and competitions," Xing Tao stated. Currently, among several regional cooperation projects in the SIMIT, each has its own focus. Shanghai mainly serves as a basic R&D center, while the Jiaxing project leans more toward the manufacturing sector, and Wuxi is positioned as a base for transforming laboratory technology into practical applications.

However, the launch of the Jiading project in Shanghai shares some functions similar or even overlapping with Wuxi, which makes some officials in Wuxi nervous.

Meanwhile, the entry of another regional strong city, Suzhou, has intensified the competition for IoT development in the Yangtze River Delta region.

On February 23rd, China Mobile officially signed the "Perceiving China" application center—Suzhou project cooperation agreement with the Suzhou municipal government.

According to the agreement, both parties will use the construction of the "Perceiving China" application center—Suzhou project as a carrier, supported by TD and IoT technologies, and centered around the "Wireless City," establishing China Mobile Suzhou Urban IoT Application Center in Suzhou. They will focus on the development and promotion of applications integrating TD and IoT technologies, gradually turning Suzhou into the urban IoT application center of "Perceiving China." Simultaneously, both parties will extensively conduct various forms of application demonstration project cooperation in the informatization construction process of areas such as government transparency, urban management, people's livelihood projects, industries, agriculture, services, etc., including environmental monitoring, intelligent transportation, digital families, digital tourism, digital campuses, digital healthcare, citizen all-in-one cards, etc.

Who Will Invest in IoT?

"So far, we haven't seen a single penny from the central government allocated for IoT industry development," Xing Tao said. Although the development of the IoT industry received high attention from various ministries after last year, currently, six ministries are involved in the implementation phase, and coordination is proceeding rather slowly.

Another factor that cannot be ignored is that due to the start of the domestic 4 trillion yuan investment plan, central fiscal funds remain tight, which has, to a certain extent, delayed the disbursement of IoT support funds.

"Our institute still owes funds from 2008, and we even need to finance our activities ourselves," Xing Tao mentioned. A draft development plan for China's IoT industry was submitted to the central government before the Spring Festival and is expected to be approved and implemented this year. The industry hopes that central support can be issued and actively implemented as soon as possible.

Currently, the obstacles for IoT moving from laboratories to practical applications mainly include missing technology in some links, industry standards yet to be formulated, and persistently high costs.

This means investing in IoT applications still carries significant risks, so few enterprises venture into this field. Under these circumstances, local governments promoting IoT applications first has become an industry consensus. Local governments, including those in Shanghai, Suzhou, Wuxi, and Jiaxing, have all indicated they will allocate considerable funds to support the development of the IoT industry to seize the initiative in this sector.

Although more cities hope to develop IoT, the institutions mastering IoT technology in China are concentrated in a few organizations like CAS and China Mobile. This makes the current IoT development fever easily evolve into a new land grab movement.

"Everyone coming to Wuxi wants to carry out application demonstrations, but in the end, it's all about getting projects—in short, it's about making money from Wuxi," a municipal leader in Wuxi pointed out with concern during a certain meeting.

Previously, in cooperation agreements signed between local governments and various research institutions and operators, local governments often provided the money and land. While local governments hope to achieve mutual benefits in this process, the systemic risks brought by high-tech industries also give them headaches.
