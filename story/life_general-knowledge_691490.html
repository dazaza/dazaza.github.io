How to prevent side effects of drugs?

There is a saying "All medicines are one third toxic", a simple sentence that truly reveals the nature of drugs. Yes, drugs are something that "can be either toxic or medicinal", which means that any drug must inherently contain toxins.

Hearing this might scare you, but this is the essence of drugs. In other words, if a substance cannot be toxic, it cannot be called a "drug".

The phrase "All medicines are one third toxic" refers to the dual nature of drugs, generally known as "effect" and "side effect". For example, when a cold causes fever, we take antipyretics, but these drugs can place quite a heavy burden on the gastrointestinal tract and liver. In this case, we can say that the antipyretic reducing body temperature is an "effect", while the burden on the gastrointestinal tract and liver is a "side effect". The efficacy of drugs involves both "effect" and "side effect" existing simultaneously.

When taking medication, one should not only think about the "effect" but also consider the "side effect". Otherwise, the worst-case scenario may occur: the medicine prescribed for treatment ends up harming the body instead.

To prevent regret and suffering caused by side effects, the most important thing is to maintain good communication with the doctor when getting a prescription. Accidents caused by side effects may stem from various complex reasons such as insufficient professional knowledge of some doctors, inadequate drug instructions, or lack of patient awareness.

When we see a doctor and they are about to prescribe medication, we should first ask clearly why we need to take this medicine, what side effects may occur after taking it, and whether our body can tolerate it. We should also listen carefully to the doctor's instructions.

In recent years, the practice of "informed consent (doctors have the responsibility to inform patients about their condition and treatment methods)" has been fully implemented. Therefore, regardless of any questions the patient may have, the doctor must answer truthfully. If you encounter a doctor who does not explain in detail or believes there is no need to tell the patient too much, I suggest it would be better to go to another hospital.

However, the responsibility of "informing" is not just on the doctor; the patient themselves should also clearly state their own condition to the doctor so that the doctor understands your physical constitution and medical history. Some side effect incidents often occur due to insufficient information provided by the patient.

For example, if a patient tells the doctor "I seem to have a fever recently," the doctor actually cannot determine whether the fever started just a few days ago or a month ago. Therefore, it is better to specify "Since last weekend" or "Since last autumn" etc., so that the doctor can immediately understand your condition.

If you are allergic to certain types of drugs or are taking medications for other diseases at the same time, please make sure to inform the doctor beforehand. Sometimes mixing several drugs can easily lead to accidents, so this point must not be overlooked.
