Life is priceless, and this is what the integrated safety system aims to protect. It can protect workers, equipment, environment, and surrounding organisms from the hazardous environments of factories. Only with sufficient education and training as a foundation can intelligent design and technology truly avoid human factors—insufficient training and judgment errors—that cause accidents.

Safety sensors demarcate restricted entry zones. Safety sensing technology can detect intrusions into these areas and send signals to corresponding logic units, delaying or significantly slowing down potentially dangerous (hydraulic, pneumatic, electronic) actions. Additionally, safety sensors are designed as fail-safe types, meaning that if there’s an issue with measurement or signal transmission, the connected logic unit still ensures a safe outcome.

Safety sensors may also include redundant units so that if one unit fails, another takes over immediately, avoiding production losses due to downtime. Redundancy does not necessarily mean system safety, just as safety automation units do not imply that their integrated systems are safe. Using safety integrity levels for grading (SIL4 being the safest) in safety automation systems can enhance security, but certification increases costs; the way they are integrated into a design can vary greatly. Standards, codes, and best practices that can be applied, along with using risk assessment procedures to reduce hazards to acceptable levels (it's impossible to have zero risk), will be discussed in other articles.

Sensitivity and Cost Issues

Safety sensor or switch products can include automated safety barriers, safety touch panels, electronic safety sensors, emergency stoppers, two-hand buttons, safety control devices, laser scanners, light curtains, light pads, photoelectric devices (single or multi-beam light), and two-hand safety controllers.

John Drinkard, engineering vice president at Omron Scientific Technologies Inc., said that trends in the development of safety sensors include: higher integration, such as integrating photodiodes and amplifiers onto the same chip; higher sensitivity of sensing units; more efficient photodiodes (LEDs); lower prices; more product varieties and suppliers; better functional safety and reliability design.

Drinkard said: "The difference between safety products and non-safety products lies in: when an abnormality occurs, components of safety products fail safely, while non-safety products bypass safety functions and attempt improper operations. The design of safety products is to detect these conditions and respond safely, cutting off command output." Moreover, the design of safety devices can be changed according to specific applications to achieve better results, such as light curtains with silencing or blanking characteristics, controlled by dip switches or computers, greatly simplifying the setup process. Drinkard added: "Whenever we develop new safety sensing technologies, we consider whether it enhances existing products."

3D Safety Zones

With the development of faster, lower power consumption, easier-to-use, and more economical technologies, new inventions in existing safety sensing technologies continue to emerge. A technical developer from a major safety automation company said: machine vision technology in the safety field is the next step in the development of safety technology, he expects further developments in COMS imaging technology.

Following this is SafetyEye, released by Pilz Automation Safety LP in June last year, which Pilz calls the world's first 3D safety camera system for control and monitoring. This vision-based three-dimensional safety system can perform sensing and monitoring tasks and control potentially dangerous processes, which is simpler than using a network of two-dimensional sensors with light curtains and other means. Pilz says that the SafetyEye 3D system provides higher safety and faster diagnostic speeds through detection zones that are easy to set up on a computer, suitable for quick troubleshooting and obstacle-free situations.

Israel Alguindigue, automotive market manager for Sick's safety system distribution, said: "As safety sensor technology matures, devices are adding self-diagnostic functions that provide health and status information about the device, mostly providing information about the entire device. This information can be used by device users and builders to design better maintenance plans than after-the-fact maintenance, maximizing equipment availability."
