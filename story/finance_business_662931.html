(2) Beta-adrenergic receptor blockers

1. Characteristics of antihypertensive effects

The mechanism of antihypertensive action can be summarized in the following aspects: (1) Reducing cardiac output, the body produces an adaptive response, peripheral vascular resistance decreases, and blood pressure drops; (2) Blocking central beta receptors, reducing the transmission of sympathetic nerve fibers; (3) Blocking presynaptic membrane beta receptors, reducing NE release; (4) Inhibiting renin release; (5) Enhancing the vasodilatory effects of atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) and prostacyclin (PGI2); (6) Pressure sensor repair, etc. The impact on lipid metabolism: Non-selective or selective beta blockers without intrinsic sympathomimetic activity (ISA) have the same effect on lipids, increasing triglycerides (TG) and very low-density lipoprotein (VLDL), and decreasing high-density lipoprotein (HDL), with no effect on normal cholesterol levels. Beta blockers with ISA do not affect lipid metabolism when used long-term.

2. Pharmacokinetics

Based on elimination pathways or oil-water partition coefficients, they are divided into two groups: lipophilic and hydrophilic, which differ significantly in pharmacokinetics. Lipophilic drugs are easily absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract and reach various organs in the body, easily cross the blood-brain barrier, and easily produce central nervous system side effects. Most are metabolized and eliminated by the liver, and accumulation and toxicity may occur when liver function is impaired. Hydrophilic beta blockers are not easily permeable through cell membranes and are poorly absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract, thus having lower central nervous system side effects. They are mainly excreted by the kidneys, and severe renal dysfunction patients are prone to accumulation and toxicity. Therefore, in patients with liver or kidney insufficiency, the dosage should be reduced.

3. Status in antihypertensive treatment and medication precautions

(1) According to JNC-7 in the United States, beta-blockers are strong indications for hypertension combined with heart failure, coronary heart disease after myocardial infarction, and high-risk factors. Studies suggest that beta-blockers should not be used as first-line therapy for other hypertensive patients except those with myocardial infarction or symptomatic heart disease.

(2) Beta-blockers are irreplaceable for secondary prevention after myocardial infarction and other coronary artery diseases, chronic congestive heart failure, and patients with increased sympathetic activity.

(3) In clinical medication, selective beta-adrenergic receptor blockers with intrinsic sympathomimetic activity and lipophilic beta-adrenergic receptor blockers are preferred.

(4) The choice of antihypertensive drugs should follow individualized principles, and overemphasizing first-choice drugs is inappropriate. The current trust in beta-blockers cannot change their position in combination therapy.

(3) Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors

1. Antihypertensive mechanisms and pharmacological characteristics

The basic mechanism of action of ACE inhibitors (ACEIs) is to reduce the production of angiotensin II (AngII) and retain the activity of bradykinin. AngII generated by ACE is a powerful factor in constricting blood vessels. It directly constricts vascular smooth muscle and also acts on the central and peripheral sympathetic nervous systems, promoting the release of endothelin and other vasoactive substances, increasing vascular tension. AngII can also promote aldosterone release, increasing blood volume and raising blood pressure. It also has a role in promoting cardiovascular hypertrophy and proliferation. ACEIs block the generation of AngII and the above-mentioned effects, exerting antihypertensive effects. Simultaneously, they help alleviate heart failure and cardiac and vascular remodeling. In hemodynamic effects, ACEIs dilate arteries and veins, thereby reducing systemic peripheral vascular resistance and lowering blood pressure. On the other hand, they reduce aldosterone release, thus reducing water and sodium retention and blood volume, enhancing their antihypertensive effects. ACEIs dilate major cerebral and coronary vessels, increase vascular compliance, reduce cerebrovascular resistance, and increase cerebral blood flow. They dilate the efferent arterioles of the kidneys, reduce glomerular filtration pressure, increase renal blood flow, and do not affect or slightly increase glomerular filtration rate. ACEIs can reverse pathological remodeling of the myocardium or vessel walls caused by hypertension or other reasons, protect vascular endothelial cells, and have anti-atherosclerosis effects. They can reverse endothelial cell dysfunction caused by hypertension, heart failure, arteriosclerosis, and hyperlipidemia, restore endothelium-dependent vasodilation, dilate the efferent arterioles of the kidneys, and reduce glomerular pressure, thereby protecting renal function, especially for renal damage caused by glomerular hypertension.

2. Pharmacokinetics

Due to differences in chemical structure, the drug metabolism kinetics in the body vary, resulting in differences among various ACEIs. These differences include prodrugs or active drugs, and variations in absorption, bioavailability, half-life, metabolic and excretion pathways, and percentage binding to plasma proteins. The time to reach peak concentration reflects the onset time of the drug's efficacy, which varies among different drugs. There are no significant differences in therapeutic efficacy among these drugs, but differences exist in their chemical structures containing thiols, carboxyl groups, or phosphate groups. For example, captopril is an organic acid containing an SH group, absorbed quickly in the digestive tract, with higher bioavailability, and itself is an active drug, hence having the shortest time to reach peak concentration and fastest onset. Some ACEIs are excreted via the kidneys, while others can be excreted through both the bile and kidneys, such as benazepril and fosinopril. In patients with renal insufficiency and heart failure, drugs excreted via the kidneys should be reduced in dosage or replaced with drugs excreted through dual pathways. Elderly individuals have reduced kidney function, leading to higher blood concentrations of ACEIs excreted via the kidneys, so consideration should be given to using lower doses.

3. Status in antihypertensive treatment and medication precautions

ACEIs are particularly effective for renovascular hypertension due to their high renin levels. ACEIs protect organs such as the heart, kidneys, and brain, reduce myocardial hypertrophy, and prevent or reverse cardiovascular pathological remodeling. The renal protective effect of ACEIs is not just a result of their antihypertensive action; it involves dilating the efferent arterioles more than the afferent arterioles, thereby reducing intraglomerular pressure, reducing proteinuria, and providing renal protection. When accompanied by renal vascular diseases (such as renal artery stenosis), caution should be exercised regarding renal function. Cardiac remodeling changes such as ventricular dilation and hypertrophy and vascular hyperplasia and thickening caused by myocardial infarction and hypertension are beneficial for cardiovascular functional recovery and prognosis. ACEIs can prevent and reverse myocardial hypertrophy and vascular remodeling. Extensive clinical observations have shown that ACEIs reduce mortality in heart failure, improve the prognosis of congestive heart failure, and prolong life, surpassing other vasodilators and cardiotonic drugs. ACEIs improve hemodynamics and organ perfusion and are the most effective treatment for heart failure when combined with diuretics. Extensive clinical observations have shown that ACEIs improve right ventricular remodeling after myocardial infarction and reduce myocardial infarction mortality. Therefore, for hypertensive patients with heart failure or diabetic nephropathy, hypertension with heart failure or myocardial hypertrophy or myocardial infarction, ACEIs are the first choice.

(4) Angiotensin II receptor antagonists (ARB)

1. Antihypertensive mechanisms and pharmacological characteristics

Pharmacological actions: AT1 receptor antagonists block AT1 receptors, thereby antagonizing the potent vasoconstrictor effect of Ang II and water sodium retention, leading to antihypertensive effects. They simultaneously protect renal function, primarily through regulating the biological effects produced by the binding of Ang II to receptors. AT1 receptor antagonists can antagonize Ang II-induced renal vasoconstriction, reducing the resistance of afferent and efferent arterioles simultaneously, increasing effective renal plasma flow, while having no effect on glomerular filtration rate. ARBs and ACEIs have similar or identical efficacies in treating hypertension and heart failure. The difference is that ARBs do not inhibit ACE, therefore not producing bradykinin, P substance, and other substances causing cough, hence no cough-related adverse reactions. In patients who cannot tolerate ACEI-induced cough, ARBs should be used instead. On the other hand, ACEIs not only prevent the production of Ang II but also preserve the cardiovascular protective effects of bradykinin through inducing NO and prostaglandins. This advantageous aspect is lacking in ARBs.

2. Pharmacokinetics

ARBs are orally effective, consisting of compounds with a benzimidazole ring basic structure, non-peptide compounds, each with unique characteristics. After oral administration, all six drugs are absorbed fairly completely through the digestive tract. Except for telmisartan with a shorter Tmax (0.5–1 h) and candesartan with a longer Tmax (3–5 h), the Tmax of the remaining ARBs is around 2 h. Eating can delay the absorption of losartan, advancing Tmax, but with little impact on the area under the concentration-time curve (AUC). Co-administration with food reduces the AUC of valsartan by 48%, and telmisartan by 6%–20%. Irbesartan is incompletely absorbed orally, and high-fat diets can slow absorption speed, and intestinal pH may affect absorption levels. Eprosartan absorption is unaffected by food. All six ARBs lack first-pass effects. The bioavailability (F) of losartan, valsartan, and candesartan are 33%, 23%, and 42%, respectively. Telmisartan's bioavailability is independent of oral dose (42% at 40 mg, 58% at 160 mg). Eprosartan has the lowest bioavailability at 13%–15%, while irbesartan has the highest at 60%–80%. Losartan, candesartan, and irbesartan are cleared through both hepatic and renal pathways, with more than 60% excreted in feces, and the remainder in urine. The T1/2 of all six ARBs is relatively short. Telmisartan's T1/2 is 24 hours, the longest among the six ARBs. Candesartan and irbesartan's T1/2 exceed 10 hours. Simultaneously, candesartan binds strongest to AT1 receptors, detaching very slowly, thus producing rapid and potent antihypertensive effects. Oral administration leads to antihypertensive effects within 2 hours, with peak effects appearing at 6 hours. Therefore, ARBs are unsuitable for emergency use. Repeated dosing results in antihypertensive effects within 2 weeks, with maximum effects occurring between 3–6 weeks. Thus, ARBs are not suitable as multiple medications for severely hypertensive patients. All six ARBs have relatively long T1/2, with valley-to-peak ratios exceeding 50%, ensuring stable and prolonged antihypertensive effects lasting 24 hours, making them suitable for once-daily dosing.

3. Status in antihypertensive treatment and medication precautions

(1) ARBs are another class of preferred antihypertensive drugs like ACEIs, without affecting bradykinin metabolism and without causing cough-related adverse reactions.

(2) ARBs share the same advantages and indications for treating hypertension as ACEIs. Hypertensive patients intolerant to ACEIs can choose ARBs.

(3) Losartan requires hepatic CYP enzyme metabolism to form the active product EXP3174, which plays a major antihypertensive role. In patients with mild to moderate alcoholic liver cirrhosis, losartan blood concentrations increase fivefold, EXP3174 concentrations increase 1.7 times, oral bioavailability doubles compared to those with normal liver function, and clearance rates decrease by 50%. Therefore, initial doses should be halved (25 mg, qd) in patients with liver dysfunction or insufficient circulating blood volume. Telmisartan is primarily cleared through bile, and biliary obstructive diseases or severe liver dysfunction can reduce drug clearance, contraindicating telmisartan use. Individuals with mild to moderate liver dysfunction should use telmisartan cautiously, reducing the initial dose. Valsartan is primarily excreted as-is through bile, and clearance rates decrease with biliary obstruction, requiring cautious medication use. Other ARBs do not require dose adjustments in patients with mild to moderate liver damage or biliary obstruction diseases, though initial doses should be halved in patients with severe liver damage, with liver function monitoring required.

(4) In patients with renal impairment taking candesartan, important pharmacokinetic parameters change, such as increased AUC, decreased Cmax, and extended T1/2. Therefore, initial doses should be reduced in patients with severe renal impairment. Other ARBs do not require dose reductions in patients with renal impairment. However, losartan and EXP3174 cannot be removed through dialysis, and patients undergoing dialysis often have insufficient circulating volume or frequently coexist with renal vascular diseases, so initial doses must be reduced. Additionally, although the pharmacokinetic changes in patients with renal impairment taking valsartan have no clinical significance, these patients must monitor their hemodynamic parameters, renal function, and blood electrolytes since the RAS is influenced by various humoral factors.

(5) Calcium Channel Blockers

1. Mechanism of antihypertensive action and pharmacological characteristics

Calcium channel blockers lower blood pressure through the following mechanisms: (1) Effects on the heart: Calcium antagonists prevent calcium ions from entering cells, reducing intracellular calcium concentrations, weakening excitation-contraction coupling, and thus decreasing myocardial contractility. In isolated hearts, all calcium antagonists exhibit negative inotropic effects. However, in intact hearts, due to the vasodilatory effects of certain dihydropyridine drugs, peripheral vascular resistance decreases, reflexively exciting the sympathetic nervous system to enhance myocardial contractility, offsetting their negative inotropic effects. Natural sulfur similarly offsets some of its negative inotropic effects due to overall regulation. Only verapamil exhibits significant negative inotropic effects in both isolated and intact hearts. Calcium antagonists reduce calcium ion influx, decreasing sinoatrial node excitability and atrioventricular nodal conductivity. Verapamil significantly increases sinoatrial node excitability and slows atrioventricular conduction, whereas diltiazem accelerates atrioventricular nodal conduction, partially offsetting its reduction in sinoatrial node excitability. Ineffective dihydropyridine drugs reflexively excite the sympathetic nervous system, offsetting their effects on the sinoatrial and atrioventricular nodes, with no reduction in heart rate. (2) Effects on vascular smooth muscle: Calcium antagonists possess potent vasodilatory effects on small arteries, primarily blocking extracellular calcium ions from entering vascular smooth muscle cells through L-type calcium channels, reducing intracellular calcium ion concentrations, weakening excitation-contraction coupling, reducing the contraction responsiveness of resistance vessels, and causing vascular smooth muscle relaxation. Calcium antagonists primarily affect small arteries and precapillary sphincters, with minimal effects on venous smooth muscles, thus reducing cardiac afterload without significantly affecting preload. (3) Other antihypertensive mechanisms: Calcium antagonists also reduce angiotensin II-mediated vasoconstriction and inhibit angiotensin II stimulation of adrenal hormone synthesis and aldosterone secretion. Additionally, studies indicate that some dihydropyridine drugs (e.g., nifedipine and nicardipine) may interfere with alpha-receptor-mediated vasoconstriction. Like other vasodilators, calcium antagonists do not cause sodium and water retention; they can increase renal tubular sodium reabsorption, possessing inherent diuretic effects. Other organ-protective mechanisms beyond blood pressure reduction include: ① Anti-myocardial hypertrophy effects; ② Protection of vascular endothelial cell structure and function integrity, possible mechanisms include: inhibiting endothelin synthesis; inhibiting endothelin-mediated vasoconstriction; enhancing endothelium-dependent relaxation; antioxidant effects; reducing vascular internal pressure. ③ Anti-arteriosclerosis effects; ④ Inhibition of vascular smooth muscle proliferation; ⑤ Renal protective effects: reducing efferent arteriole resistance, lowering glomerular filtration pressure, inhibiting intracellular calcium overload, reducing free radical production, providing protection for renal cells. ⑥ Improving cerebral circulation: Strongly lipophilic dihydropyridine drugs such as nimodipine and nicardipine can improve cerebral circulation even before significantly lowering blood pressure.

2. Pharmacokinetics (1) High oral absorption rate, all > 90%. (2) Significant first-pass liver elimination effect, protein binding rate > 90%, large apparent distribution volume, mainly metabolized and eliminated through the liver, extremely low renal clearance rate. (3) Rapid onset after medication, definite antihypertensive effects, relatively strong blood pressure reduction, general blood pressure reduction of 10%~15% after short-term treatment; dosage correlates with efficacy, with small individual differences in efficacy. (4) Can be combined with multiple other antihypertensive drugs, significantly enhancing antihypertensive effects. (5) Few contraindications for calcium antagonist treatment, no obvious effects on lipid and glucose metabolism, poor long-term blood pressure control ability and medication compliance.
