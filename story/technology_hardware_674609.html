The making method of gold bricks

by kycangbao on 2012-02-24 14:49:13

When ancient craftsmen made golden bricks in those years, they first selected soil with a texture that was sticky but not loose, powdery but not sandy. The selected soil was then left exposed for an entire year to remove its "soil nature." Afterward, the soil was soaked in water to soften the clay, and several oxen were used to repeatedly trample the mud to remove bubbles from the mud lumps, ultimately refining them into dense mud blocks. These blocks were then repeatedly beaten before being placed into molds, covered with flat boards, and two people would step on the boards until the mud was compacted. The brick blanks were then air-dried for more than seven months before being fired in a kiln. During firing, chaff was used to smoke the bricks for a month to remove moisture, followed by split firewood for another month, whole firewood for another month, and finally pine branches for 40 days before the bricks could be removed from the kiln. After removal, the bricks underwent strict inspection. If six of the golden bricks in a batch did not meet the standard of "producing sound when struck and having no holes when broken," the entire batch was considered defective and had to be re-fired. Thus, it took about two years for the process from soil to golden bricks to be completed. The production of "golden bricks" required several steps including soil selection, brick making, firing, removal from the kiln, polishing, and soaking. Although these steps may not seem particularly complex, each major step actually included many smaller steps, making the production of "golden bricks" an extravagant affair.

The first step is soil selection. Before selecting the soil, experienced masters are needed to identify where the soil not only has viscosity but also contains a higher level of aluminum, which can be ground into powder. After selecting the soil, it goes through seven steps: digging, transporting, drying, pounding, mixing, grinding, and sieving, taking up to eight months to complete.

The second step is brick making. The prepared soil is shaped into brick blanks using semi-handcrafted and semi-mechanical methods. Ordinary Beijing bricks just need to be shaped according to the required dimensions and thickness. More complex are the decorative bricks with special requirements, such as some bricks needing patterns drawn on them. To make the patterns vivid after firing, the depiction of the patterns during the brick-making process must be perfectly executed.

The third step is firing. Once the blanks enter the kiln, the process of igniting the kiln is extremely complicated: smoking with chaff for a month, burning with split firewood for a month, burning with whole firewood for a month, and burning with pine branches for 40 days. After 130 days of burning with four different types of fuel, the bricks can be cooled down by pouring water into the kiln. This process refers to watering the kiln once the bricks are done to cool it down. Workers have to carry water up steep and high brick ladders to the top of the kiln and pour it into the kiln.

The fourth step is removal from the kiln. On the day of removal, the small kiln interior is filled with choking dust. Even though water has been poured into the kiln for cooling for four to five days before removal, the temperature inside is still very high, and the "golden bricks" after long-term firing are scorching hot. The workers quickly pass the hot and heavy "golden bricks" among themselves. To urge their companions to speed up and also to encourage themselves, the workers emit a strange hissing sound while moving the "golden bricks."

The fifth step is polishing. The "golden bricks" just removed from the kiln can only be considered semi-finished products. To turn them into radiant gems, detailed polishing is necessary. The polishing of "golden bricks" is done using extremely simple tools in a round water tank, while grinding and flushing water, not only making the surface of the Beijing bricks smooth but also ensuring that the longer they are used, the brighter they become, even to the point of being used as mirrors. Unfortunately, the polishing technique that makes "golden bricks" mirror-like has now been lost.

The final step is soaking in oil. The "golden bricks" after polishing are immersed in tung oil block by block. Tung oil not only gives the "golden bricks" a glossy finish but also extends their lifespan. Only after this step can the entire process from soil to Beijing bricks be considered complete. Adding up all the time consumed by the above steps already takes over a year, and each kiln can produce no more than 7,000 Beijing bricks at a time, with a certain proportion being substandard or defective.
