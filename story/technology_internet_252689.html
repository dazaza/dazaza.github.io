What Should Be Done About the Yuanmingyuan Artifacts Lost Overseas?

By Xu Shuguang, Chairman of GreenTree Hotels & Resorts and www.519.com Wine Direct Sales Network

Recently, there has been a hot news story about the famous French director Roman Polanski. Last month, while attending an award ceremony at a film festival in Switzerland, he was arrested by American prosecutors who had been waiting for him. The reason? A case of raping a 13-year-old girl that occurred 31 years ago.

This incident has sparked strong opposition from both France and Polanski's home country, Poland. French media condemned the late Judge Lawrence Ritterband for breaking his promise, and many commentators believe that Polanski's crime was merely not understanding the boundaries of sexual liberation!

I think the U.S. authorities did the right thing! If a fugitive escapes for decades and the law doesn't pursue him, then he would think that committing a crime is no big deal, as long as he waits a few decades, it will be fine. This is encouraging crime! Similarly, the looting of the Yuanmingyuan artifacts can be seen as a crime committed by the Eight-Nation Alliance against China. Whether it has been one hundred or two hundred years, this responsibility should still be pursued, and the stolen items should rightfully be returned to China.

On October 18th, the Yuanmingyuan Management Office, in collaboration with Tsinghua University and CCTV, launched a series of activities titled "Searching for Yuanmingyuan Artifacts Lost Overseas." This marks the first large-scale dispatch of experts overseas to investigate the lost artifact records since the disaster of Yuanmingyuan 149 years ago. The topic of how these artifacts should return to their homeland has once again become a focus of discussion.

Currently, the returned Yuanmingyuan artifacts are mostly purchased at high prices by patriotic overseas Chinese, who then donate them to the motherland. However, I strongly oppose this method of high-price auctioning. If we allow auctions, then we are essentially acknowledging that the initial looting was justified. But the evidence of the plundering, destruction, and burning is clear for all to see; this crime cannot be erased, even after over a hundred years have passed. The Chinese government has the right to reclaim what originally belonged to its own people and to uphold justice. At least, the source of circulation of these artifacts should be blocked, and under no circumstances should these "stolen goods" be allowed to circulate again.

From ancient times to the present, only when greed and plunder are strictly punished does civilization flourish. This applies to nations as well as service industries. China has a large population, but it could win in the world not by total numbers but by per capita figures, lacking only the legal awareness and national quality of its citizens. Why hasn't China's service industry yet produced internationally first-class chain brands? This has a direct relationship with the inability to fully punish violations. We advocate background checks on employees, which will greatly help improve the professional ethics and competence of white-collar workers. Otherwise, employees will lack the motivation to standardize and constrain their professional behavior, which is not a good thing for either their current or future employers.
