Yang Mei, an office worker, has recently spent a lot of time with her computer. A thick red patch of flaky skin appeared on the outside of her right wrist. It itches occasionally, and when she works overtime and has a heavy workload, the itching becomes unbearable. After visiting a dermatologist, it was discovered that Yang Mei has an allergic constitution and had started using a new computer, which caused an allergic reaction to the plasticizers and synthetic rubber materials.

The dermatologist stated that if the processing workshop is close to the restroom, and if your hands have developed calluses or you have red itchy patches on your face, or if taking medicine and applying ointments does not alleviate the discomfort, you might be suffering from "computer user skin syndrome"! Dr. Zheng Huiwen, the attending dermatologist at Shudian Clinic, pointed out that "computer user skin syndrome" mainly manifests as red, flaky, itchy patches on the hands. Some people may even develop vesicular dermatitis; others may experience easy vasodilation and congestion due to prolonged hand pressure, and in some cases, there may be bleeding, also known as "early pressure sore manifestations," although such cases are quite rare. On the face, it often presents as redness, swelling, and pain, with localized papules appearing. In severe cases, pustules may develop.

So, how should computer users take care of their skin? Experts suggest regularly cleaning the computer screen or adding a screen protector to avoid static electricity, radiation, or dust between the computer and the face causing irritation to the face. Maintain a distance of 40 to 50 cm between the screen and your face. Additionally, it's better to apply a physical barrier cream on your face. To reduce hand pressure and friction, doctors recommend taking breaks every 30 minutes, keeping your hands clean, and applying hand lotion.

Long-term use of computers combined with poor hygiene habits may be one of the reasons for recurring skin problems on the hands and face.

Office workers who frequently use computers should pay attention. Long-term computer use can not only cause back and neck pain but may also lead to allergic eczema, acute dermatitis, and hand hypoxia [computer user skin syndrome].

About 10% of cases seen in clinics involve hand eczema patients, said Dr. Zheng Huiwen. Whether it's eczema or atopic dermatitis on the hands or face, it could be due to an individual's allergic or atopic constitution. If they are also long-term computer users, repeated hand friction and the stimulation of dust between the screen and the face could exacerbate the skin problems.

The common types of "computer user skin syndrome" seen in clinics include "computer palm," "mouse finger," "keyboard wrist pad dermatitis," and "screen dermatitis."

Experts particularly pointed out that these symptoms usually develop gradually and are not limited to contact with computer-related products. For example, screen dermatitis manifests as frequent small red rashes or papules on the face, with sensations of redness, swelling, pain, and itching. Repeated occurrences can even lead to rosacea-like skin inflammation with dilated capillaries.
