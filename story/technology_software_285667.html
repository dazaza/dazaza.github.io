Two-thirds of infertility cases are due to female factors, with blocked fallopian tubes being a major cause. However, since the widespread emphasis on infection prevention and the advent of broad-spectrum antibiotics, gynecological inflammations that lead to blocked fallopian tubes have significantly decreased. On the other hand, advancements in understanding and diagnostic techniques for endocrine diseases have improved the diagnosis rate of ovarian dysfunction-related infertility, making ovarian infertility an important factor as well.

Ovarian factors account for about 15% to 25% of infertility cases. Ovarian infertility can be caused by various factors:

1. Local ovarian factors: such as congenital absence of ovaries or underdeveloped ovaries, premature ovarian failure, polycystic ovary syndrome, certain ovarian tumors like granulosa-theca cell tumor or teratoma, which can affect ovarian hormone secretion and ovulation.

2. Systemic diseases: severe malnutrition or lack of essential nutrients in the diet can impact ovarian function and lead to infertility; chronic illnesses, metabolic diseases such as hypothyroidism, hyperthyroidism, diabetes, adrenal dysfunction, etc., can also result in infertility.

3. Central effects: imbalance in the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian axis, pituitary tumors or scars can cause ovarian dysfunction leading to infertility; psychological factors such as stress or excessive anxiety can inhibit ovulation by affecting the hypothalamus-pituitary-ovarian axis.

Besides ovarian factors causing female infertility, depending on the location and causes, they can be roughly divided into:

1) Infertility caused by external genitalia and vaginal factors: such as imperforate hymen, vaginal septum, congenital absence of vagina, etc., which hinder sexual intercourse; severe vaginal inflammation where large amounts of white blood cells can engulf sperm, reduce sperm motility, shorten their survival time, thus affecting conception.

2) Uterine factors causing infertility: accounting for about 10% to 15% of infertility cases. A normal uterus is anteverted and anteflexed, with the cervical os facing backward, allowing it to soak in semen after intercourse, which is conducive to conception. If the uterus is retroverted or retroflexed, making the cervical os face forward and upward, it may affect conception; inadequate progesterone secretion from the ovaries results in poor secretory response of the endometrium; uterine developmental abnormalities and endometrial inflammation such as tuberculous endometritis, submucosal fibroids, etc., can all affect implantation of the fertilized egg.

3) Cervical factors causing infertility: one of the more significant reasons for infertility, accounting for about 10% to 20% of infertility cases. During ovulation, the cervical os enlarges from 1mm to 3mm in diameter, and cervical mucus increases, becoming clear and transparent with a pH of 7.0 to 8.2, which neutralizes the acidity of the vagina, favoring sperm activity and passage. Chronic cervicitis or low estrogen levels can make cervical mucus viscous or contain large amounts of white blood cells, which is not conducive to sperm activity and passage, thus affecting conception. Additionally, cervical polyps or cervical fibroids can block the cervical canal, affecting sperm passage, and cervical stenosis may also be a cause of infertility.

4) Infertility caused by tubal factors: tubal inflammation and adhesions lead to tubal blockage, preventing the meeting of eggs and sperm and resulting in infertility. Endometriosis in the pelvis can also cause tubal adhesions and twisting, leading to infertility.

5) Other factors: after systematic examination, about 10% of couples do not find obvious causes of infertility. In recent years, it has been suggested that it is related to immune factors. In some infertile women's serum, anti-sperm antibodies are found. These antibodies can cause agglutination reactions with semen, which is detrimental to sperm activity. For these infertile couples, using condoms for contraception for a period can make these antibodies disappear and achieve pregnancy. Other factors include incompatible blood types between partners, which some people believe may be a cause of infertility. Chromosomal abnormalities in sex chromosomes, such as karyotypes of 45XXO or 46XXP- or mosaicism, sometimes patients appear normal but have ovarian dysgenesis, no ovulation or amenorrhea, often seeking treatment due to infertility.

Male factors account for about one-third:

1. Affecting sperm production, such as congenital testicular dysgenesis, postnatal inflammation (tuberculosis), varicocele, etc., painless abortion.

2. Affecting sperm transport, such as epididymis disease, vas deferens obstruction, etc.

3. Affecting semen entering the cervical canal, such as short penis, hypospadias, premature ejaculation, erectile dysfunction, etc.

4. Abnormal semen, such as asthenozoospermia, sperm incompetence, etc. Zinc elements can maintain and enhance male sexual skills and increase sperm count. Zinc deficiency reduces male sex hormone secretion, leading to sexual dysfunction, testicular shrinkage, affecting sperm generation and maturation, ultimately reducing sperm count, decreasing vitality, and delaying liquefaction of semen. Selenium is an important component of peroxidase in seminal plasma. When the selenium content in semen decreases, the activity of this enzyme decreases, unable to inhibit lipid peroxidation of sperm cell membranes, causing sperm damage, increased dead sperm, and reduced activity. Men who frequently eat animal kidneys are prone to infertility

Many men like to eat grilled kidneys at barbecue stalls to replenish their kidneys, but in fact, this practice may backfire, failing to replenish the kidneys and instead reducing sperm count, leading to infertility.

Theoretically, animal organs contain androgens and corticosteroids that can indeed enhance sperm vitality and increase libido. However, Dr. Hu Haixiang, chief physician of the Department of Andrology at Air Force General Hospital, pointed out that while animal organs and sex organs do contain some androgens, their content is very low, making reliance on them for aphrodisiac purposes impractical.

Director Feng Lei of the Health Management Department at Zhejiang Lin College's School of Tourism Management said that if the feed and water consumed by animals are contaminated with heavy metals like cadmium, either they are transferred to the bones or excreted through the kidneys. People eating animal kidneys contaminated with heavy metals might excrete them through urine. However, heavy metals in urine will inevitably damage male reproductive organs, and eating barbecued food itself can easily trigger cancer.

Dr. Hu said that occasionally eating once or twice might not pose much harm, but if consumed frequently, heavy metals accumulate in the body and cannot be excreted, inducing structural and functional degenerative changes in male testes, epididymis, and other tissues, ultimately leading to a decline in male reproductive capacity.

The Hubei Provincial Health Department once announced the monitoring situation of food pollutants in Hubei Province: the metal cadmium content in animal kidneys in Hubei Province exceeded the national standard value by 100 times. Besides harming reproductive organs, cadmium can also cause joint pain, tooth damage, liver enlargement, and other symptoms in humans.

In addition to cadmium, animal kidneys may also contain lead, another lethal killer of the male reproductive system, easily causing a reduction in sperm count, increased sperm deformity rate, weakened activity ability, thus affecting fertility.
