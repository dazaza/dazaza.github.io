What's HP Afraid Of? Lists Microsoft as Direct Competitor

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:41:31

Microsoft previously announced its new device and service strategy. As part of this strategy, Microsoft launched its own Surface tablet and later acquired Nokia's mobile phone division to enhance its device manufacturing capabilities. Coincidentally, Microsoft's partner HP is also transitioning into a device and service company, making competition between these two companies seemingly unavoidable.

HP has also recognized this point. The company’s CEO, Meg Whitman, recently publicly stated, “The two companies have transitioned from partners to direct competitors.”

Whitman made this statement before the annual financial analyst meeting. Many of Microsoft's recent actions have impacted HP's interests, including contributing $2 billion for Dell's privatization to help the company out of trouble, thereby better promoting Windows 8 in the enterprise market. She also mentioned Microsoft's own Surface tablet, despite HP previously stating that the Surface would not pose a threat to the company. Previously, Acer's CEO had warned Microsoft to "think twice" before launching the Surface.

In the early stages of the first-generation Surface release, Microsoft was asked by Windows OEM manufacturers to explain its own Surface launch. Senior advisor to Microsoft's CEO, Craig Mundie, claimed that Microsoft had considered leaving the design drawings of the device to hardware partners, but doing so would "make it difficult to ensure the end-user experience." HP's released Slate tablet indeed performed poorly, failing to make much of an impact.

Whitman also expressed concerns about the PC business: "HP's traditionally high-profit markets are being severely disrupted. Wintel devices are being challenged by ARM devices, and the competitive landscape of the market has undergone significant changes." HP launched Android tablets and Chromebook laptops this year, no longer relying solely on Microsoft's Windows operating system. Whitman said this is the company's "multi-operating system, multi-chip architecture" strategy, "We are rapidly transforming to create products that consumers like." However, Whitman had previously also expressed loyalty to the Windows platform: "We will continue to stick with this platform, I am a loyal believer in Windows." Currently, PC business revenue still accounts for 20% of HP's total revenue. According to the latest report from IDC, HP is the second-largest PC manufacturer after Lenovo.

HP released its third-quarter earnings report at the end of August. The company achieved a net profit of $1.39 billion, significantly better than last year's net loss exceeding $8.8 billion. However, net revenue was only $27.2 billion, down 8% from last year, with PC business revenue declining by 11%. Whitman may fail to fulfill her promise of achieving revenue growth before 2014, which could damage her personal reputation.

Microsoft's device and service strategy inevitably created competitive relationships with other OEM manufacturers, but the relationship between Microsoft and its device partners remains very close. For Microsoft, whether they are Windows device partners or Windows Phone device partners, they are essential partners that the Microsoft system platform must rely on, as they have promoted the vigorous development of the Windows platform. Meanwhile, the Windows platform has long been the main development platform for PC manufacturers. Currently, the Windows operating system still holds over 90% of the PC market share and is attempting to capture the tablet market dominated by other platforms.

In today's rapidly changing technology industry, the business intersections and mutual dependence among various companies have altered traditional partnership relations. Most companies simultaneously engage in both competition and cooperation. The competition and cooperation between Apple, Microsoft, and Google coexist. For instance, Bing search is the cooperative search engine for Apple's Siri voice service, many of Google's software services are popular on the iOS platform, and Microsoft has also released Office Mobile clients for the iOS and Android platforms.

Meanwhile, after acquiring Motorola, Google also became a competitor to other Android manufacturers. Samsung and Apple are fiercely competing in the smartphone market and have engaged in intense patent battles, but Samsung is also an important parts supplier for Apple, and Apple is an important customer for Samsung Electronics. To jointly address market competition and establish unified action standards or strategies, all major tech companies have joined some technical standard or action alliances. Nokia and Samsung have joined the Internet.org internet accessibility alliance, while their partners, Google and Microsoft, are key members of the A4AI affordable internet alliance, making for two very interesting alliances with very interesting members.

Competition does not affect cooperation, and cooperation does not affect competition. Ultimately, everything revolves around the core interests of enterprises. In the future, the coexistence of competition and cooperation in the technology industry will continue for a long time.
