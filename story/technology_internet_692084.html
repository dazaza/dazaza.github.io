A Brief Discussion on the System of Actual Carriers in China's Maritime Cargo Transportation

by mncvty on 2012-03-05 22:01:24

Abstract: The Hamburg Rules was the first to clearly define the system of actual carrier on an international scale. China's Maritime Code draws from the Hamburg Rules in defining this system, and establishes its own system of actual carriers in maritime cargo transportation. This article attempts to explain this system from the establishment of the legal system of the actual carrier, the concept of the actual carrier system, the nature and scope of responsibility, and propose suggestions for its imperfections.

Keywords: Actual Carrier, Identity Recognition, Scope of Responsibility, Nature of Responsibility, Statute of Limitations

I. Establishment of the Actual Carrier System in Maritime Cargo Transportation

(A) Based on the following background, the 1978 United Nations Convention on the Carriage of Goods by Sea (Hamburg Rules) established the system of actual carriers in maritime cargo transportation for the first time to balance the interests of both ship and cargo parties.

1. In shipping practice, it often happens that the carrier who signs the transport contract with the consignor is different from the person actually carrying out part or all of the goods transportation under that contract. For example, in time charter transportation, the charterer signs a maritime cargo transportation contract with the consignor, but the actual transportation is completed by the shipowner or the bareboat charterer of the vessel.

2. Due to the carrier and the person actually engaged in the transportation not being the same, a series of legal issues arise, where the rights of the bill of lading holder are not effectively protected. The bill of lading holder often cannot find the true party to claim compensation, and by the time they do find the correct party under a certain legal system, the statute of limitations has often expired. Most countries believe that in time charter transportation, if the name of the consignee on the bill of lading is the time charterer, and the bill of lading is signed by the charterer or his agent, then the bill of lading is considered issued by the charterer, and the charterer is the carrier in maritime cargo transportation. However, a few countries believe that in time charter transportation, the bill of lading serves as a contract between the shipowner or the person actually engaged in the goods transportation and the bill of lading holder, and the shipowner or the person actually engaged in the goods transportation should directly bear the liability for compensation for the loss of goods. The company issuing the bill of lading is merely the agent of the carrier. The above differences have caused confusion in identifying the subject of the carrier.

(B) To solve the above problems, the Hamburg Rules defined the concept and responsibilities of the actual carrier:

1. Distinguishing between the carrier and the actual carrier. If the bill of lading is issued by the captain of the carriage vessel, it is deemed to be issued on behalf of the carrier. In time charter transportation, if the shipowner actually engages in maritime cargo transportation, but the agent at the loading and unloading port is appointed by the charterer, and the agent issues a bill of lading in the name of the charterer's company, then the carrier under the bill of lading is the charterer, and the shipowner is merely the actual carrier.

2. The carrier shall be responsible for the entire transportation, and some of the carrier's responsibilities also apply to the actual carrier. It should be emphasized that the responsibility of the actual carrier is limited to part of the carrier's responsibilities. Under certain circumstances, the carrier and the actual carrier should directly bear the responsibility for the loss, damage, or delay in delivery of the goods, and the bill of lading holder can directly make a claim against one party or both parties simultaneously. However, the actual carrier only needs to directly assume responsibility to the bill of lading holder when the cause of the loss, damage, or delay in delivery of the goods occurs during the transportation phase under the actual control of the actual carrier and is due to the negligence of the actual carrier.

(C) The above system of the Hamburg Rules was directly cited in China's Maritime Law.

China's Maritime Law incorporates the actual carrier system from the Hamburg Rules. Article 42, paragraph 2 of the Maritime Law defines: [actual carrier] refers to the person who accepts the commission of the carrier to engage in the transportation of goods or part of the transportation, including other persons who accept sub-commissions to engage in such transportation. The Maritime Law further determines the legal liabilities of the actual carrier from Articles 61 to 64.
