"I was still wearing old clothes from junior high school at that time."

by lieencq09 on 2011-09-13 18:53:51

From the crossroads to the bustling city of Hangzhou, Wan Xinhua, who was introverted by nature, felt an overwhelming sense of arrogance. "At that time, I was still wearing old clothes from junior high school, and in the dormitory, there were classmates with better conditions, all of them had brought computers. It was my first time seeing a computer."

Playing games caused his studies to be neglected, and during the second semester of his freshman year, many of Wan Xinhua's courses failed. In his sophomore year, Wan Xinhua didn't attend any classes, ultimately failing all of them, and the school also revoked his scholarship.

That "Zhejiang University withdrawal certificate," Wan Xinhua always carried with him. He said it reminded him of the two years he went from being a top student at Zhejiang University to becoming addicted to online games and getting expelled, then going through hardships to get admitted to Wuhan University. "Every time I see it, it encourages me to cherish the present."

But it seemed like everything was already too late. He described his situation at the time as "beyond redemption." "I had dropped too much coursework; how could catching up be easy?" Additionally, after being long immersed in an illusory world, Wan Xinhua found it difficult to concentrate, making studying very arduous.

After several rounds of wandering and returning home, Fang Xinhua helped his aging parents farm the land. Last year, Wan Xinhua developed a desire to return to university. In 2010, without reviewing for a single day, he scored over 500 points on the college entrance exam. Subsequently, he chose a relatively distant preparatory school to focus on his studies. "One, the preparatory school promised that as long as I reached the first-tier admission line, they would waive my tuition, and additionally, I wanted to find a place where no one knew my past so I could study in peace."

For this, Wan Xinhua fell into deep self-reproach.

Seeing him indulging in games, classmates and teachers tried to persuade Wan Xinhua, but to little effect. "When classmates tried to dissuade me too much, I got annoyed and fought with them, so no one dared to advise me anymore." Wan Xinhua said, "The counselor also talked to me multiple times, but as soon as the teacher left, I immediately turned on the computer to play."

Choosing Zhejiang University over Tsinghua University due to poverty

He began to immerse himself in studying, ignoring meals and sleep. Every day, he was among the first students in the library study room and also among the last to leave.

A year of hard study resulted in Wan Xinhua scoring 611 points on this year's college entrance exam, allowing him to enter Wuhan University's Telecommunications Engineering program.

In the winter of 2008, Wan Xinhua voluntarily applied for withdrawal from school. Returning home during the Spring Festival, Wan Xinhua stayed indoors. "I didn't dare to go out, nor did I have the face to do so," Wan Xinhua said. Villagers who learned about his withdrawal would often point fingers and call him crazy.

In 2005, Wan Xinhua made a comeback, scoring an impressive 654 points. When Tsinghua University's admissions staff contacted him, Wan Xinhua realized that his score wouldn't qualify him for their freshman scholarship, so he changed his application to Zhejiang University instead.

Wan Xinhua (the first on the left) registering at Wuhan University

However, since childhood, Wan Xinhua had been a local celebrity. "Since I was young, my academic performance has been outstanding, always ranking among the top few in my grade," Wan Xinhua said.

In 2005, Wan Xinhua became a freshman in Zhejiang University's Information Engineering major. The school arranged student loans for the financially struggling Wan Xinhua and provided an 8000 yuan scholarship.

Wan Xinhua was born in a small town in Fuzhou, Jiangxi. His parents are both over seventy years old and plagued by illness. The family barely scraped by on two acres of thin farmland, living hand-to-mouth.

Due to financial difficulties, a month ago he came to work odd jobs at a hotel in Wuhan, earning 800 yuan. The other day, with his 800 yuan salary, Wan Xinhua came to register at the school. Even through the green channel, he still had to pay more than 2000 yuan in fees, which worried Wan Xinhua greatly.

It was his father's kneeling plea that finally awakened Wan Xinhua completely. He decided to reform himself and start studying again.

One day, while engrossed in a fierce battle on the computer, his elderly parents appeared at the dormitory door. "Son, can't you try a little harder? We don't expect you to support us in our old age, but you need to survive yourself," said his father, leaning on a cane, as he suddenly knelt before his son.

After his parents left, Wan Xinhua threw the computer out of the fourth-floor window. "I want to bid farewell to my past and start anew," he said.

Carrying his withdrawal certificate, he struggled to make a living

His parents, who had never ventured out of the mountains, visited Hangzhou four times to work with the school to help Wan Xinhua, and even counselors slept in the dormitory to supervise him. However, his internet addiction wasn't cured.

During the second semester of his freshman year, Wan Xinhua made a startling decision: using his scholarship to buy a computer. Smart as he was, he quickly mastered its use and was soon captivated by the virtual world of the game, "addicted like a madman, starting to play Warcraft fervently."

Recalling those experiences too painful to remember, Wan Xinhua wished he could slap himself twice. "I didn't go to class, and when I occasionally went out, it was just to buy some steamed buns or instant noodles, then returned to the dormitory to stay up all night playing games."

Rekindling his passion for studying, he entered Wuhan University

An unnamed teacher from Wan Xinhua's department helped him by giving him 1500 yuan, allowing him to become a new student at Wuhan University. "I plan to complete my credits in two to three years and apply for early graduation," said Wan Xinhua, talking about his future plans. "I want to catch up on the time I lost."

By the time he reached his third year, his long-term obsession with online games finally led to abnormal behavior. "At that time, walking around the campus, I would punch trees or hit my head against walls for no reason."

By this time, it had been two years since he withdrew from Zhejiang University.

After the Spring Festival, Wan Xinhua followed a fellow villager to work in Xiamen, later moving to Luoyang and Nanchang, "unable even to solve his own hunger and cold."
