xianni5200 Ouyang Haiyun's first gray micro-novel - "Waiting for the Rain"

by anonymous on 2012-02-27 16:53:25

Inscription: What I am waiting for is not the rain, but the person who appears in the rain. - Ouyang Haiyun

My name is 17, everyone calls me 17. My real name isn't actually 17, it's just a nickname. But since so many people call me this, everyone knows me as 17 and my real name has been gradually forgotten, even by my best friends who now also call me 17.

It's said that in this world, the ratio of men to women is always disproportionately unequal. Initially, the male-to-female ratio was 117:100. So some say that among 117 men, 17 are destined to never find their other half. And I am one of those 17. But none of this really matters to me because regardless of how this ratio changes, I remain one of the bachelors. I am acknowledged as such, so this title won't change. Perhaps one day, others might call me 18 or 19. Who knows? Haha.

I am a college student studying art. Every day is pretty much the same routine: classes, the studio, the dormitory - three points on a line. Our classrooms and our studios are separate places. I usually like to stay in the studio after class because there are fewer people there. Sometimes, perhaps, only I am there. Although there are many art students in the university, most of them go out with their girlfriends to paint landscapes or portraits in the wild. But I, when not in class, will foolishly stay in the studio, eating and drinking nothing, painting still life sketches of stones and potted plants for a couple of days. It seems, in my world, everything except myself is an inanimate object, and I am forever dealing with dead things.

My mentor once told me I should paint more lively subjects. He said my paintings were too lifeless, lacking any sense of vitality. He suggested I paint animals next time, or people. I didn't know what kind of animals or people he meant. I had once thought about painting these things, but when the brush was in my hand, I couldn't move it. In the end, I helplessly sketched stones. It was as if I could only paint this and nothing else.

Stones, inanimate objects, canvases - every day I do these things. University life is inherently boring and tasteless, and I've found my way to pass the time.

On Monday, it's me, the canvas, and the stone. On Tuesday, it's me, the canvas, continuing yesterday's stone. On Wednesday, it's me, the canvas, and the palette, thinking that color would be better than a sketch. On Thursday, it's me, the canvas, and the potted plant. A friend came over and said painting just a stone was too monotonous, so illogically, I painted a potted plant beside the stone. On Friday, it's me and a torn painting. That friend came again, laughed at the painting, and I smiled, deciding to repaint it. On Saturday, it's me and the canvas, but I didn't paint because I didn't know what to paint.

Sunday, it was raining outside, but I still went to the studio to paint, not knowing why. Maybe it was because the dorm was too noisy and I wanted a quiet place.

But I was wrong. I thought no one would be in the studio, but unexpectedly, there was a girl there. She held a palette, focused on her painting in front of her, but hadn't started yet.

Who was she? Why had I never seen her before? Was she a student at this school? An art student? If so, why had I never met her? Or was she just visiting a friend? My heart kept asking questions, generating one doubt after another.

Curiosity drove me closer to her step by step. When I got near, she was still staring at her painting, completely unaware of everything around her, as if from that moment on, her world contained only that painting. Her painting, funnily enough, was just a potted plant. Haha, in this day and age, there were still people who loved inanimate objects like me.

I quietly watched her for a long time, watching her add color to the still life. But I never said a word to her. In the end, I stupidly painted the same potted plant on the easel behind her. She, too, remained busy with her own painting from the start.

I don't know how long it lasted, but I only knew that it was already dark, and the rain outside was still falling.

"Hey, classmate," she turned around and asked me, lightly patting my shoulder.

"Can I share your umbrella?" She hesitated, barely managing to ask. It seemed awkward to ask a stranger for help.

"Hmm," my heart flustered a bit because it was the first time someone had asked me this. It took me a while to nod.

She was happy after hearing this, didn't say anything else, picked up the umbrella I had left by the canvas, grabbed my hand, and ran into the rain.

She lived in the girls' dormitory, while I lived in the boys'. This was the first time in my 24 years of life that I went to the girls' dormitory, and with a strange girl. I didn't enter their building, although the dorm supervisor wasn't there, and many boys had entered before. These seemed common in universities, but I chose to send this strange girl downstairs without entering. She smiled at me, as a thank-you. But I stood there dumbly, saying nothing. I didn't ask her name, what she studied, or which year she was in. The only thing I knew was that we were both students at this school. From that moment, watching her disappearing back figure, I felt something indescribable. Was it liking? No, was it love? Not quite. After all, we were just two strangers who met by chance. Haha.

That night, I caught a cold, the first time in my life, for a girl I had only met once. Remember that night's heavy rain, and my single-person umbrella. I had no idea how to take care of someone, to care about someone, but that night, I followed the movie plot and held the umbrella high above her head. I didn't know why, maybe because I am a man, and some things are simply what a man should do.

My roommate saw me catch a cold that day and found it strange. He rummaged through his drawers and gave me a few cold pills, asking why I was drenched even though I had an umbrella. I just smiled and said the umbrella was broken.

In the following days, besides going to class, I spent my time in the studio painting. She hadn't appeared since last time, looking at her unfinished painting, I told myself, she wouldn't come, wouldn't.

Another rainy day, and I had no classes. Wanting to find a way to pass the time, I thought again about the studio. There, she appeared, still working on the piece she had worked on before, her eyes still so focused, still forgetting that I was behind her watching her. They say familiarity breeds contempt, having seen each other twice, we should be familiar. We should introduce ourselves, but she was currently occupied, so let's not disturb her. Let's just paint separately.

I painted the same piece I had painted when I first met her. During this time, I have been painting this one piece, daily just refining the lines and adding a little color I liked. I didn't know why, but ever since last time, my mind has been unsettled.

She painted until evening, and so did I. She still didn't bring an umbrella, asking if she could share mine. I neither said yes nor no, just nodded.

That night, I sent her back to the dormitory entrance again, still not saying much, still not asking her name, she still smiled beautifully, as a thank-you.

And I, upon returning, caught a cold again because I was still using that "broken" single-person umbrella. My roommate somehow placed a cup of hot water and some cold medicine on my table, telling me to buy a new umbrella next time.

In the following days, I still occasionally went to the studio to paint. The friend who said my painting was too dull came again, repeating the same words. I began considering what my mentor had said. This time, I didn't add any more inanimate objects; instead, I painted a few flowers, a window beside her, and casually added a little sunlight. This time, my friend said it was good, but I felt this painting was still missing something. What was it? Even I couldn't explain.

Afterwards, I continued improving that painting, which still wasn't perfect in my eyes, until one day, I was painting, and it rained again, but fortunately, I brought an umbrella.

"Nice."

Who? Who spoke? I quickly turned around, it was her, the girl from last time. When did she appear in front of me? I didn't notice at all. I gave her a foolish smile, saying casually. She didn't say anything else, just returned to her canvas, copying the flowers, window, and sunlight from my painting. Seeing this, I laughed, not knowing whether I was copying her or she was copying me.

That day, she still didn't bring an umbrella, and I still sent her back to the dormitory. She still smiled, and we still didn't talk much, not even knowing each other's names.

That night, I still caught a cold. My roommate helped get some cold medicine from the school clinic for me, saying all the medicine in the dorm was gone. I smiled, and my roommate didn't say anything else, just occasionally complained to others about how many unscrupulous umbrella factories there were, making umbrellas that were so unreliable.

Afterwards, I went to the studio to paint again. Only used to painting alone, I now felt a bit uncomfortable. I thought, she would come again, because her painting wasn't finished, colors weren't applied yet. I thought, maybe she would only come on rainy days. And she always forgot her umbrella. If she came and I didn't, then who would send her?

March 17th, sunny, no rain. I thought she wouldn't come, and indeed, she didn't.

March 18th, cloudy, no rain. Heard it might rain today, but it didn't.

March 19th, sunny, no rain. I decided not to paint that painting, wait until she comes, then paint. That's settled.

March 20th, sunny, no rain. Still no sight of her in the studio. I thought, she probably wouldn't come again.

March 21st, cloudy, no rain. I bought an umbrella, thinking I'd give it to her next time I meet her.

March 22nd, sunny, no rain. I exchanged the umbrella for a radiation-proof one, heard it's good for girls' skin.

March 23rd, sunny, no rain. I decided to carry two umbrellas next time, one for her, one for me.

March 24th, cloudy, no rain. I thought of going to her dormitory to find her, wanting to ask why she hasn't come to the studio to paint recently, but thought about it and couldn't find any legitimate reason.

March 25th, sunny, no rain. I think what I'm waiting for is not the rain, but a person. Could that person be her?

March 26th, sunny, no rain. Today, I've gotten used to checking the weather forecast, and marking a red X on every good weather day. I think, I've fallen for her, a girl I've only met three times. Maybe this is called love at first sight. Haha.

...

April 12th, sunny, then rain. Today, she came, still painting that painting. But on that painting, she painted a person's back, seemingly a boy's, but I didn't know who it was, or why it was a back view. Following her example, I similarly painted a person's back on my painting, a girl's back. I wondered what she would say if she saw it now. She still didn't bring an umbrella today, and I thought soon she would surely ask if she could share mine.

But I was wrong. Because at dusk, a boy appeared at the door, and she didn't ask me, but instead went with him, sharing an umbrella under the rain. Who was he? Was he her boyfriend? How had I never seen him before? I wanted to go up and ask her, but then thought how silly and naive I was. To her, I was just a kind-hearted person, beyond that, perhaps not even a friend. Between us, our conversations were unusually scarce. Besides the relationship between passerby A and B, I couldn't find any other words to describe it. She could share an umbrella with anyone, not just me. Everyone could, and I was just a passerby. Suddenly, I felt not only silly and naive, but also funny, a girl whose name I didn't know could influence my entire life. Maybe, the mistake was that rain, it shouldn't have come, it didn't need to come. Coming would only make me feel how meaningless I was.

That night, I carried two umbrellas, but I still caught a cold, soaking wet. I didn't know why. Maybe it was sadness, maybe sorrow, but what did I have to be sad or sorrowful about? My roommate prepared a full drawer of cold medicine for me, telling me he knew an umbrella maker who made quality products at reasonable prices, suggesting he'd take me to pick one out next time, telling me not to use those broken umbrellas anymore. But I didn't tell my roommate that the umbrella I bought for her was from there.

Sometimes, it still rains, and every time it does, the table in my dorm room is filled with cold medicine. But in the studio, I haven't seen her again. That painting should be finished by now, she won't come again, I don't need to carry an umbrella every day like a fool, letting people call me delicate. The studio remains the same as before, with only me painting those inanimate objects, everything unchanged, as if she had never come. Finally, peace returned, everything returned to normal, great.

One day, two months later, I was in the studio, and she appeared again, still facing her painting. She didn't have a palette or a paintbrush, just stared at the painting for a long time, then picked up a pencil and wrote "To that special he" at the bottom right corner. Who was he? Was it the man waiting for her at the door last time, or someone else? Whoever it was, it seemed unimportant to me now.

It rained again. She asked if I had an umbrella, I said no. She smiled, saying she had one, asking if I wanted to share it with her. I didn't say anything, just nodded. That night, she dropped me off at the boys' dormitory entrance and told me to be careful not to catch a cold again, to take care of myself. I smiled and said the same to her. This was the first time she spoke to me with concern, and the last.

That night, I still caught a cold, possibly because I intentionally moved the umbrella handle towards her so she wouldn't get wet. My roommate prepared a whole drawer full of cold medicine for me, saying his girlfriend's roommate's boyfriend, who runs a hospital, gave him free medicine, so he took a bit extra. By the way, he also asked me who the girl was who dropped me off, was she my girlfriend? I awkwardly said I didn't know her, and my roommate didn't say anything more, just discussed behind my back that there must be a story between her and me.

Three days after graduation, I saw her for the sixth time, and also the last. It was raining outside, but it wasn't in the studio, it was at the school gate. She was carrying a lot of luggage. I wanted to help, but realized I wasn't needed because the boy from last time drove to pick her up and helped her load her luggage into the trunk. My roommate told me that man was her boyfriend, and told me there was no hope, then handed me a box of cold medicine, saying to be prepared.

After graduation, I got a new name, 20, but none of my friends called me that, maybe they thought it was too repetitive. I opened a gallery with this number 20 as its theme, specializing in portraits for single people, earning a little money. My roommate opened a bar, and sometimes I would go there for a drink. One day, by chance, he told me some things about that girl. She was a transfer student, from the film department. Her parents hoped she would become an actress, but she preferred painting. The film department required her to shoot short films and outdoor scenes everywhere, so she could only secretly go to the studio on rainy days when she had nothing else to do. But she only went a few times because her younger brother had leukemia and needed her care. Their family didn't have much money, but fortunately, the son of the hospital director liked her and offered to waive the medical fees if she became his girlfriend. She agreed, unable to refuse, and she couldn't make another choice. But the man treated her well. Recently, a new movie was being filmed, and her boyfriend used his connections to get her the role of the second female lead.

I asked my roommate how he knew all this, and after a long pause, he told me it was from her ex-girlfriend.

Dedicated to:

The brief guests in our lives. - Ouyang Haiyun
