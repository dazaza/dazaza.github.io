Twitter and Facebook are becoming increasingly identical.

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 21:00:27

September 18th, Beijing time - According to an article published in the prestigious MIT publication, *Technology Review*, the two largest social platforms globally—Facebook and Twitter—are becoming increasingly similar in their services and functions, even showing signs of mutual imitation. Is this due to the profit pressures brought on by being publicly traded or preparing for IPOs, pushing these two platforms to gain advertising revenue through similar services?

Are Facebook and Twitter becoming more alike?

Traditionally, users have had entirely different habits when using Facebook and Twitter: the former is for conversations between friends, while the latter is more like a personal radio station broadcasting individual opinions to the world. The former's comment walls are often filled with photos and multimedia, whereas the latter is mostly pure text sharing.

However, according to *Technology Review* (MIT Technology Review), judging from the latest services introduced by these two platforms, there are increasing signs that they are learning from each other.

Firstly, let’s look at Facebook. Last month, Facebook launched "Trending Topics" and "# tags (Hashtags)" on personal news pages, allowing users to insert keyword tags in their posts for easier tracking of related topics. In fact, these reading services have been part of Twitter’s offering all along.

Now, let’s examine Twitter. Twitter has rolled out a new message page that enables users to fully view the retweets or external discussions of a single message, with an interface design similar to Facebook's commenting function. Additionally, Twitter has added multimedia video ads within message threads, resembling Facebook's ad design.

"The increasingly similar developmental trajectories of Facebook and Twitter seem to indicate their eagerness to generate advertising revenue rather than meet user needs," *Technology Review* analyzes. Twitter has struggled to balance sponsored content with its clean interface design, moving towards 'Facebook-ization'; meanwhile, Facebook seems to want to learn from Twitter's real-time interaction with TV programs, developing into a 'second screen' platform.

*Technology Review* points out that despite existing research indicating that Facebook's adoption of Twitter's "# tag" feature hasn't achieved the expected results and hasn’t led to viral spread of key topics, there isn’t yet much evidence proving whether the "mutual imitation" strategy between Facebook and Twitter truly benefits either party. However, from the user's perspective, if these two social platforms continue to become more alike, people might start to miss the diversity in social interactions.
