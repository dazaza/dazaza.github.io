Qing Dynasty Culture: Emperor Xianfeng and His Tutor Du Shou Tian

Throughout his life, Emperor Daoguang had a total of nine sons. In his later years, he particularly favored the sixth prince Yizhin, also known as "Sixth Brother". On several occasions in front of ministers, he hinted that he wanted to pass on the throne to Yizhin. However, because Yizning, the "Fourth Brother", was older and well-known in the palace for his "virtue", he hesitated.

At that time, Du Shou Tian, from Binzhou, Shandong, who served as the head tutor of the Upper Study Hall and teacher of the Fourth Brother, was an outstanding scholar with great talent and strategy. He sensed the Emperor's hesitation and became anxious. From his own interests, he had to fully support his student. Thus, he racked his brains to find ways to help the Fourth Brother make up for his shortcomings.

Once, Emperor Daoguang ordered all the princes to go hunting at Nanyuan, actually testing their martial skills. According to Qing tradition, when princes went out for study, they had to ask leave from their teachers. After deep contemplation, Du Shou Tian whispered into the Fourth Brother's ear: "When you get to the hunting ground, just sit and watch others ride and shoot. Do not fire a single gun or arrow yourself, and restrain your followers from capturing any wildlife. When you return, the Emperor will surely ask why. You can answer: 'It is springtime, and birds and beasts are breeding. I do not want to harm life and disrupt the harmony of heaven. Moreover, I do not wish to compete with my brothers using only my archery and horsemanship skills.'"

On the day of the hunt, the Sixth Brother had captured the most game and was basking in self-satisfaction when he noticed the Fourth Brother sitting silently with his followers standing respectfully by his side. Feeling curious, he approached and asked: "All my brothers have returned laden with game. Why has the Fourth Brother come back empty-handed?" The Fourth Brother calmly replied: "I am feeling unwell today and cannot race with my brothers on the hunting field." As evening approached, the princes presented their game to the Emperor. Sure enough, the Emperor inquired about the reason, and the Fourth Brother repeated what Du Shou Tian had taught him. Emperor Daoguang was greatly pleased and said to the ministers around him: "This is the demeanor of a ruler."

Frankly speaking, whether in terms of literary and military strategy or health, the Fourth Brother was no match for the Sixth Brother. Even until his death, Emperor Daoguang still could not decide whom to pass the throne to.

One time, Emperor Daoguang was gravely ill, knowing there was no hope for recovery. Before his death, he conducted a final examination of the two princes' abilities and demeanor to decide the successor. The Sixth Brother's teacher advised: "If the Emperor asks about state affairs while lying on his sickbed, you should speak freely and fully." Du Shou Tian, however, told the Fourth Brother: "If you present political strategies, in terms of intelligence and eloquence, you cannot match the Sixth Prince. There is only one plan. If the Emperor mentions his illness and impending death, you should simply kneel and weep sincerely to express your filial piety." During the audience, the Emperor indeed questioned them about governing after his death. The Sixth Brother ignored the Emperor's suffering and spoke fluently about his ideas and aspirations for governing the country; whereas the Fourth Brother, following his teacher's advice, was so saddened by his father's questioning that he burst into tears and could not answer. Emperor Daoguang carefully observed their words and actions from his sickbed and was moved by the Fourth Brother's behavior. He said to the ministers beside him: "The Fourth Prince is benevolent and filial, capable of assuming great responsibility." The next day, Emperor Daoguang passed away, and the lead minister read the secret edict: "The Fourth Prince Yizning shall succeed to the throne." Thus, the Fourth Brother defeated the Sixth Brother and ascended the throne, adopting the era name "Xianfeng."

After Emperor Xianfeng ascended the throne, he was grateful for his teacher's support and appointed Du Shou Tian as Minister of Personnel and concurrently as Grand Secretary. He followed Du's advice on everything, treating him like a father. The position of Grand Secretary during the Qing dynasty was equivalent to the prime minister of the Ming dynasty, wielding the most power in the court. Du Shou Tian also wished to rely on Xianfeng's trust to realize his grand plans for governance. Shortly after Xianfeng ascended the throne, Du first suggested reinstating Lin Zexu, Zhou Tianjue, and other officials dismissed during the Opium War for advocating resistance, to suppress the newly erupted Taiping Heavenly Kingdom peasant uprising. Then, he personally went to the Jiangnan region to investigate relief efforts to ease class conflicts and consolidate the feudal regime under Xianfeng. Unfortunately, before he could fully implement his political ideals, he died suddenly during his investigation mission.

After Du Shou Tian's death, Emperor Xianfeng mourned deeply at his desk, crying as if he had lost his father. He personally led both groups of ministers to pay respects and posthumously bestowed the title of Grand Preceptor and Grand Secretary upon Du, with the posthumous name "Wenzheng." "Grand Preceptor and Grand Secretary" and "Wenzheng" were among the highest honors granted to subjects. Since the Jiaqing era, Du Shou Tian was the only Han Chinese minister to be posthumously honored as Grand Preceptor and Grand Secretary.
