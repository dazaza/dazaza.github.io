Lenovo's Liu Chuanzhi: A Do-or-Die Battle Against iPhone

by nemo on 2010-04-20 20:08:38

Lenovo's first batch of mobile Internet products include the smartphone "LePhone", the netbook "Skylight", and the notebook "IdeaPad U160". Among these, the "LePhone" is the most important, shouldering the heavy responsibility of competing against Apple's smartphone, the iPhone.

Apple launched the iPhone in the United States in 2007, integrating a phone, a widescreen iPod, and a mobile network terminal into one device. It then began aggressive global marketing, requiring telecommunications operators offering its applications to share voice call revenue with it. Last October, Apple cooperated with China Unicom to launch the "Unicom version of iPhone" domestically.

With the improvement of 3G networks, more and more users are seeking smartphones that can connect to the Internet at any time. "Now it's like having a highway, but there aren't many cars running on it yet," said Yang Yuanqing, CEO of Lenovo Group. He predicted that within the next five years, the market size of mobile Internet terminals will surpass that of traditional personal computers, reaching hundreds of millions of users.

"If we take a step back now, it might be like 1994 all over again." Liu Chuanzhi recalled that in that year, international big-name computer brands entered China, causing the domestic PC manufacturing industry to collapse. Lenovo, the only company that managed to survive, retained only 3% of the market share. Now, China's mobile Internet faces another "siege" from competitors like the iPhone, Nokia, and Samsung, making the situation less than optimistic.

"We have resolved to fight a decisive battle against the iPhone!" Liu Chuanzhi said firmly.

The "Assembly Call" of Local Forces

Lenovo understands that relying on just one company is not enough to compete against the iPhone; it needs to unite local forces. "The iPhone has swept the world, even strong players in the mobile phone industry like Nokia and in the computer industry like Dell cannot compete with the iPhone; all its negotiations with customers are conducted in a dominant manner. Its advantage lies in its comprehensive momentum, especially in brand strength; our only advantage is 'this is China,'" Liu Chuanzhi said.

The Chinese market has its unique characteristics. The IT industry is a rare sector in China where domestic companies have achieved complete victory, because domestic enterprises better understand the local culture and demands.

The "LePhone" has formed alliances with domestic telecommunications operators and various service websites, providing more localized services —— its partners include China Telecom, China Unicom, Baidu, Sina, Shanda, Tencent, Phoenix New Media, Youku, Kaixin001, Renren, Yonyou... Jack Ma of Alibaba, Charles Zhang of Sohu, and Charles Chao of Sina also attended to show their support.

Hartwell Cheung, Chief Technology Officer of Lenovo Group, stated that the "LePhone" uses Google's Android operating system, optimized according to the needs of Chinese users, and includes built-in functions such as Sina News, QQ, and Kaixin001. The "LePhone" is more like a product of industry collaboration: telecommunications operators, content providers, and application developers each conduct research to make their services an integral part of the device.

Local Enterprises May Still Engage in "Civil War"

However, if the entire matter is described as "the joint resistance of national industries against foreign capital," it would be overly simplistic. The domestic mobile Internet market has always been dominated by telecommunications operators, and Lenovo's entry has made the situation extremely delicate.

Previously, China Mobile developed the smartphone "Ophone". In September last year, the first Ophone was launched, which was actually a customized "Lenovo 01" jointly developed by China Mobile and Lenovo Group —— essentially also a mobile Internet terminal.

Thus, this situation arose: both the "Lenovo 01" and the "LePhone" are Lenovo products, but they belong to different camps.

In addition, China Unicom is Lenovo's partner in the mobile Internet business, but the iPhone, explicitly named as a competitor to the "LePhone," is operated in China by Unicom. This means that under the "assembly call" of local forces, complex competitive and cooperative relationships still exist among all parties involved.
