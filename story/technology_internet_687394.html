[Qingdao Health] Spring health care: enjoying flowers and nurturing the liver to regulate Qi and blood

We are currently experiencing the transition between seasons. At the end of winter and beginning of spring, our bodies also undergo a seasonal change. This is an important turning point for health preservation. Spring is the time for growth, summer for flourishing, autumn for harvest, and winter for storage - this is the natural law. If we follow this rhythm, we will align with the natural flow; if not, we will go against it.

During this season, enjoying the spring scenery and flowers can regulate blood and energy, while maintaining a pleasant mood helps to nourish the liver. One should remain calm and avoid irritability or frustration.

In modern society, middle-aged people experience more psychological problems due to life and work stress, while elderly people often suffer from mental disorders caused by cerebrovascular diseases. Human emotional changes and mental illnesses are closely related to seasons and climate changes. Generally speaking, emotions tend to be low and depressed in autumn and winter, while they become overly excited and irritable in spring and summer. At the end of winter and beginning of spring, when the weather changes drastically, some people may experience poor sleep, chest tightness, dizziness, fatigue, bad mood, and unstable emotions. Not only do weak individuals easily get sick, but those with pre-existing conditions may also relapse. Human emotional changes and mental illnesses are closely related to the seasons. As the saying goes, "When rape flowers turn yellow, madness runs rampant." Spring is the red alert period for the onset and aggravation of mental illness. Professor Xia Zhongyuan explained that in spring, the liver can become overactive, affecting emotions. Liver Qi stagnation leads to feelings of melancholy, while excessive liver fire causes irritability and anger. Emotional distress and anger harm the liver, leading to disrupted circulation of Qi and blood throughout the body, which interferes with other organs and causes disease. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), among the five internal organs, the liver corresponds to spring. The liver plays a crucial role in regulating the flow of Qi, managing emotional well-being, and storing blood. In spring, the liver's overactivity can affect emotions. Liver Qi stagnation leads to feelings of melancholy, while excessive liver fire causes irritability and anger. Emotional distress and anger harm the liver, disrupting the flow of Qi and blood throughout the body, interfering with other organs and causing disease. TCM often says, "Anger harms the liver, thought harms the spleen, and emotions cause all kinds of diseases." This is the principle. When people get angry, their Qi and blood rush upward, raising blood pressure and sometimes causing cerebral hemorrhage and endangering life. Like Zhou Yu in "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," who had a narrow mind, frequently got angry, and ultimately died from rage. Therefore, in spring health preservation, one should adapt to the natural rise and smooth flow of liver Qi, with liver nourishment as the primary task. Nourishing the liver first requires maintaining a pleasant mood and preventing emotional-related diseases. Middle-aged and elderly people, having experienced the ups and downs of life, should gradually maintain a peaceful mindset, avoiding irritability and anger. Any matter is like a coin with two sides. We cannot change everything, but we can change our understanding. The Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon records: "When a person sleeps, blood returns to the liver." In spring, one should "go to bed late and get up early" to maintain stability within the body and resist various infectious diseases in winter and spring. Soaking feet in hot water before sleeping also has a sleep-promoting effect. For patients with mental illness, preventive measures should be taken in advance to avoid stimulation, allowing them to smoothly pass through the high incidence season of mental illness.

It is recommended that when people are unhappy, they can go out with family and friends to enjoy the spring scenery, climb mountains, play in water, walk, or practice martial arts to cultivate their temperament, making Qi and blood circulate smoothly, keeping the spirit cheerful, and forgetting troubles. People with mental illness should take preventive measures in advance to avoid emotional stimulation. Liver protection can also be achieved by regularly massaging the Taichong acupoint on the liver meridian. When feeling dizzy, massaging the Taichong acupoint can make one feel refreshed; when feeling palpitations and insomnia, massaging the Taichong acupoint can stabilize the mind; when feeling extremely angry, massaging the Taichong acupoint can calm down. Rubbing the Taichong acupoint from Taichong to Xingjian and transferring the pain point from Taichong to Xingjian can improve the effect.

Massaging the Du meridian can prevent infectious diseases.

At the transition between winter and spring, the number of people catching colds often rises sharply, especially among middle-aged and elderly people and those with weaker constitutions. A little carelessness can lead to illness, resulting in painful medical treatments. The reasons include the warming climate in spring, which facilitates the growth and spread of viruses and bacteria; many people hastily shed their coats at the start of spring, and those with weaker constitutions often cannot withstand the cold wind and thus catch a chill.

How to prevent catching a cold? Strengthening resistance is fundamental. As the weather warms up, it is suggested that citizens gradually increase outdoor exercise to absorb the essence of heaven and earth to replenish Yang Qi. For elderly people, exercising without sweating or slightly sweating is ideal. Excessive exercise can lead to excessive consumption of body fluids and damage Yang Qi. Additionally, excessive sweating opens pores, making one susceptible to cold winds. During exercise, one should avoid exposing limbs excessively to prevent catching a chill and injuring joints.

In TCM, spring health preservation can involve regularly massaging or moxibusting the Du meridian and Baihui acupoint, which is beneficial for preventing infectious diseases in winter and spring and strengthening the body. The Du meridian is located in the center of the human back. In TCM, the Du meridian is considered the main channel of the whole body's Yang channels, referred to as the "Sea of Yang Meridians." Baihui is located right at the top of the head, which can be said to be the most Yang-filled part of the human body.

Living in accordance with regular routines to nourish Yang Qi, preventing wind and keeping warm

In TCM, the five internal organs correspond to the four seasons. Therefore, the general principle of seasonal health preservation is "nourishing Yang in spring and summer, and nourishing Yin in autumn and winter." "Yang" refers to Yang Qi, equivalent to human power and vitality; "Yin" refers to Yin essence, equivalent to the body's nutritional substance base. Winter belongs to Yin, and spring belongs to Yang. The transition from winter to spring is the process of changing from Yin to Yang. After a winter of consumption, the body's Yang Qi is insufficient and needs support to adapt to the transformation of spring when all things begin to grow. During this period, although the climate begins to warm up, it remains cold intermittently, even experiencing a "spring cold snap," with large temperature differences between day and night. Daily life should pay special attention to preventing wind and keeping warm.

Outdoor activities suitable for the season, such as walking, enjoying the greenery, practicing Tai Chi, and playing ball games, can make the human body's Qi and blood flow smoothly, promote renewal, strengthen the body, and also soothe the emotions and nourish the liver. Combined with the windy spring weather, pollen, dust, and animal fur can easily spread, making it a high-incidence season for allergic dermatitis, easily causing pimples on the face, skin itching, mild peeling, and other discomforts. Avoiding prolonged exposure to wind and sun and eating less allergenic food can help prevent spring dermatitis.
