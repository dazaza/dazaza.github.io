Ecobank's water project loan from ICBC accused of destroying ecology

by ditan360 on 2010-06-30 14:58:07

Exclusive to Lowcarbon Network - It was recently reported that the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China (ICBC) has agreed to provide a loan of about 400 million US dollars for the Gibe III dam project in Ethiopia. This move has attracted attention from numerous international environmental organizations, who believe that if this project is implemented, it will have a significant impact on the local ecological environment, and ICBC will also bear huge financial risks.

The Karo tribe in the Omo River relies on dugout canoes to travel between the two banks. Image source: Data Africa

On June 26th, Wei Guoxiong, Chief Risk Officer of ICBC, exclusively told reporters in an interview that "the loan for the Gibe III dam by ICBC has not been actually disbursed yet, it is still under review." "For projects that have an impact on the environment, whether domestically or internationally, as a global bank, ICBC will not support them."

"I have an ICBC savings card with money deposited in it. To be honest, I don't know what the bank does with my money? Of course, I do not want to see the money in my card being used to destroy the ecological environment," said Peter Bosshard, holding up his savings card somewhat excitedly.

Peter Bosshard is the policy director of International Rivers. On the afternoon of June 12th, he repeatedly mentioned a name to reporters in an office near the Bird's Nest in Beijing: the Gibe III Dam.

And he came to Beijing specifically for this reason.

"About three weeks ago, we heard that the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China agreed to provide a loan of approximately $400 million for the Gibe III dam project in Ethiopia. We quickly wrote a letter to Mr. Jiang Jianqing, Chairman of the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China," Bosshard told reporters, explaining that he came to Beijing to exchange opinions face-to-face with relevant officials of the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China.

"But unfortunately, I haven't received any reply," he said.

A three-page letter

On the afternoon of June 12th, the reporter obtained a copy of the letter sent to Mr. Jiang Jianqing. The letter was jointly submitted by the Bankwatch Network, International Rivers, and Friends of Lake Turkana, signed by the responsible persons of these three organizations.

According to Bosshard's introduction, the Bankwatch Network is a network composed of international civil society organizations and individuals, aiming to monitor the operations of the financial industry and its impact on people and the environment; International Rivers is an influential international environmental organization, aiming to protect rivers worldwide and communities dependent on rivers.

Lake Turkana (Lake Turkana) is located in northern Kenya, bordering Ethiopia. In this area, local residents established their own environmental protection organization to protect Lake Turkana and its ecosystem.

The Gibe III dam is located in southern Ethiopia, construction started in 2008, with a total investment of 16 billion birr (approximately 1.75 billion US dollars), power generation capacity of 1870 megawatts, expected to be completed in 7 years, with ten sets of power generation units, currently the largest hydropower project in Ethiopia.

This letter was dated May 21st. The letter stated, "According to a notice from the Ethiopian Electric Power Corporation, the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China has agreed to provide a loan of approximately $400 million for the Gibe III (Gibe III) hydropower station project on the Omo River in Ethiopia. For many years, we have paid attention to bank loans for hydropower projects and related environmental issues, respectfully writing to you on behalf of Kenyan residents affected by the dam."

The letter states, "If the announcement from the Ethiopian Electric Power Corporation is correct, we hope to raise the following concerns regarding your bank's funding of the Gibe III hydropower station project."

The concerns raised by the above three organizations include:

The Omo River is the lifeline of the downstream Omo Valley and the Turkana Lake region, both areas having extremely fragile ecosystems. Due to their unique biodiversity and cultural heritage, UNESCO has included both areas in the World Heritage List;

The Gibe III dam will terminate the natural flood cycle downstream of the Omo Valley and reduce the water flow entering Lake Turkana. Lake Turkana is a saline lake, once the inflow decreases, the salinity of the lake water will increase, leading to the collapse of the lake's ecological system;

Lake Turkana is the livelihood of 300,000 residents in northern Kenya. Any reduction in the runoff of the Omo River will affect the fisheries, agriculture, and animal husbandry in the Lake Turkana region, thereby exacerbating the already existing famine and resource disputes in the area.

In the letter, the above three organizations particularly mentioned, "The Gibe III dam has serious technical, economic, and financial problems." Bosshard explained that despite the project starting four years ago, the economic, financial, and technical evaluations of the project have not been completed.

The letter states that although the environmental and social impact assessment report of the dam points out that there are no seismic activity risks in the area, in reality, since 1913, the area has experienced 13 earthquakes ranging from magnitude 5 to 6.3, "the recent tunnel collapse accident at the Gibe II hydropower station may very likely be caused by tectonic movements."

The Gibe I hydropower station is located upstream of the Omo Valley, completed and put into operation 10 years ago, with a power generation capacity of 184 megawatts; the Gibe II hydropower station was completed and put into operation in January this year, with a power generation capacity of 420 megawatts. Due to the small scale of the two hydropower stations, there has been no obvious environmental harm.

According to Bosshard's introduction, Ethiopian hydroelectric engineers had previously issued a warning that if due attention is not given to the seismic activity in the Gibe III dam area, it will lead to "unbearable dangers in the future."

The letter mentioned another important situation: the contractor of the Gibe III dam applied for loans from the African Development Bank, the World Bank, and the European Investment Bank several years ago, but these three banks have not responded for a long time.

"Friends of Lake Turkana and International Rivers have raised objections to the African Development Bank, pointing out that the project violates a series of environmental and social security policies of the bank," Bosshard told reporters, explaining that the above-mentioned banks have entrusted relevant institutions to conduct further research on the project and have not agreed to fund it until now.

The letter points out, "The Gibe III dam project violates the principles of green credit policy and is an environmentally non-compliant project. In the eyes of the international media and civil society, the project has become a symbol of trading development for social and environmental hazards."

Ikal Angelei, chairman of Friends of Lake Turkana in Kenya, said, "The Gibe III dam will destroy the lifeline of the Lower Omo Valley and Lake Turkana regions. We hope that Chinese banks and enterprises can stay away from these disasters."

Johan Frijns, coordinator of the Global Bankwatch Network, also pointed out: "China has made remarkable progress in banking reform through its green credit policy. Sponsoring an environmental disaster project like the Gibe III dam is detrimental to the development of China's banking industry."

Water Resources Dispute Flares Up Again

In fact, regarding the water resources issue of Ethiopia and neighboring countries, an article titled "Nile Water Resources Disputes Flare Up Again, Upstream and Downstream Countries' Contradictions Worsen" published in the People's Daily on May 21st pointed out that Ethiopia's "full exploitation of water resources" could exacerbate water use conflicts with downstream countries and intensify national interest conflicts.

Taking the Nile Basin as an example, historically, the Nile originating from the Ethiopian Highlands was mainly used for irrigation and providing drinking water. In the last century, several dams were built on the Nile, with two dams located within Sudan; another one allowed Egypt to build the Aswan Dam through negotiations with Sudan and signing the "Nile Waters Agreement" (1959), which allocated the annual total volume of the Nile water (about 74 billion cubic meters) between the two countries, with 55.5 billion cubic meters going to Egypt.

In recent years, with the acceleration of economic development and population growth in the upstream countries of the Nile, the demand for agricultural, industrial, and domestic water has increased annually, intensifying the water use contradictions between upstream and downstream countries.

In the same day's People's Daily, two scholars' views were also published:

Xu Weizhong, deputy director of the Middle East and Africa Institute of the China Institute of Contemporary International Relations, said that Ethiopia, located upstream of the Nile, is relatively deficient in oil and coal resources, lacks electricity and other energy sources, but has abundant water resources, known as the "water tower of East Africa", so Ethiopia has always hoped to fully develop its water resources, such as building dams upstream of the Nile to increase power supply and simultaneously accommodate agricultural irrigation, etc.

However, Ethiopia's actions have caused tension among downstream countries. According to An Chunying, a researcher at the Institute of West Asian and African Studies of the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, the dependence of Egypt, Sudan, Eritrea, and Kenya on Nile water resources is 96%, 77%, 68%, and 33% respectively, therefore, considering social and economic interests, the countries in the basin are seeking greater water rights.

On May 17th, an article titled "Nile Water Resources Dispute Flares Up Again" written by the newspaper's correspondent in Cairo was published in Guangming Daily, disclosing that recently, "some (Nile) basin countries ignored the strong opposition of Egypt and Sudan, signing a new framework agreement on river water distribution in Uganda, reigniting the Nile water dispute." The focus of contention among the countries lies in whether dams can be built on the Nile belonging to their own countries.

"The Nile dam case" as a widely influential case has been compiled into the "Environmental Law Case Tutorial" by Southwest University of Political Science and Law.

Bosshard told reporters that scientists from the African Resources Working Group (ARWG) previously clearly pointed out in investigating the possible economic, social, and environmental impacts of the Gibe III dam that the Gibe III hydropower project will not only destroy the ecosystem of the downstream Omo Valley and Lake Turkana regions, but also trigger contradictions and conflicts between nations.

"We are concerned that the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China approved a huge loan without fully understanding the environmental, social, and financing issues of the project," Bosshard said.

However, Ethiopia, which is vigorously building dams and power facilities, is determined. According to a report by China Dialogue, Dula Haqim Mohamed, head of production and construction at the Ethiopian Electric Power Corporation (EEPCo), said: "For developing countries like us, dams are essential. With electricity, everything is possible."

With the help of foreign companies, Ethiopia is making large-scale investments in hydropower station construction, having already invested billions of dollars. It is expected that by 2012 to 2013, six hydropower stations will be built, which will not only meet the country's electricity needs but also allow for the export of electricity abroad to earn foreign exchange.

There are reports that last year, the Ethiopian Electric Power Corporation signed agreements with foreign companies to plan the construction of two more hydropower stations with an installed capacity exceeding 2000 megawatts, including the Gibe IV dam and the Halele Werabesa dam.

In late April this year, Ethiopian Finance Minister Sofian Ahmed visited China, meeting with leaders of the Gezhouba Group and expressing the hope that the Gezhouba Group would "more extensively participate in the development of electricity, energy, roads, and other fields in Ethiopia, making greater contributions to Ethiopia's economic development."

Previously, the Gezhouba Group undertook the construction of the Tekeze Hydropower Station in Ethiopia, and in October last year, the Gezhouba Group signed another important hydropower agreement with the Ethiopian Electric Power Corporation - the general contracting contract for the Genale-Dawa 3 Hydropower Hub.

The press release issued on May 17th by the above-mentioned three institutions, International Rivers, etc., regarding the Gibe III dam stated, "According to Ethiopian news reports, the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China has agreed to extend a loan for a contract. According to this contract, a state-owned enterprise in China - Dongfang Electric Corporation - will provide equipment for the Gibe III dam." The equipment purchaser is the Sinohydro Corporation.

Sinohydro Corporation is the largest and most capable hydropower construction company in China, owning 50% of the global hydropower construction market share. On September 25th last year, the corporation signed a general contracting contract for the Zimogoyeda Hydropower Station project with the Ethiopian Electric Power Corporation, with a contract value reaching 555 million US dollars.

Banks Need to Strengthen Green Credit

In the letter sent to the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China, the Bankwatch Network, International Rivers, and Friends of Lake Turkana expressed admiration for the efforts made by the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China in environmental protection in recent years.

"Currently, the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China is the most internationalized bank in China and has become the largest bank in the world," Bosshard said, adding that the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China had firmly committed to the green credit policy. Due to its corporate social responsibility policy, the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China received 131 awards in 2008, including the Best Corporate Citizen Award.

In April this year, nine domestic civilian environmental organizations, including Green Watershed, Green Home, Public and Environmental Research Center, Global Environment Institute, and Friends of Nature, jointly released the "Environmental Record of China's Banking Industry (NGO Version)" report. This report also expressed approval for the efforts made by the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China in recent years in implementing the green credit policy.

The report states that as one of the four major commercial banks, the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China has made great efforts in adjusting the credit structure and constructing a green credit system. At the same time, the bank also set goals, ideas, frameworks, and implementation steps for green credit construction over the next three years. In terms of policy formulation, the bank implemented an environmental "one-vote veto system" according to national environmental protection policies and regulations.

However, the report also pointed out that the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China only briefly mentioned setting a three-year goal in its social responsibility report, without detailing the specific content. This not only has the defect of lack of transparency and clarity but also makes it difficult to reflect the bank's commitment to adhering to the green credit goal and direction.

The report also pointed out, "From the data disclosed in the social responsibility report, the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China has a relatively good record in exiting 'high pollution' industries and entering the environmental protection industry. However, the proportion of this amount in the total credit volume of the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China is still limited. The absence of these data and contents has discounted the overall evaluation of the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China in the aspect of green credit."

In July 2007, the original State Environmental Protection Administration, the People's Bank of China, and the China Banking Regulatory Commission jointly issued the "Notice on Preventing and Controlling Loan Risks in High Pollution Industries," which was called China's "green credit policy." Subsequently, the Chinese banking industry implemented and followed the green credit policy.

The report by the nine environmental organizations believes that overall, Chinese-funded listed banks have accepted the concept of green credit and have performed well in implementing the green credit policy.

Bosshard said that so far, the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China has not responded to the letter. But he told reporters that a report in the Hong Kong South China Morning Post on June 7th said that the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China responded to concerns about the Gibe III dam, stating that "ICBC always attaches great importance to environmental protection and will not fund any project that does not meet environmental standards. We will closely monitor the development of the situation and the environmental assessment of the project."

On the afternoon of June 26th, at the scene of the "2010 Fifth Green Wealth (China) Forum," the reporter interviewed Wei Guoxiong, Chief Risk Officer of the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China as a forum guest. He said that the current loan for the Gibe III dam by the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China "has not been actually disbursed yet, it is still under review."

"We (for this project) are very cautious, very cautious!" Wei Guoxiong told the reporter.

"Although ICBC is a commercial bank, we are not profit-driven," Wei Guoxiong said, adding that for projects that have an impact on the environment, even though the benefits are relatively large, ICBC will not support them. "Regardless of whether domestically or internationally, as a global bank, we will not support them."

Wei Guoxiong said that for international business, ICBC refers to the Equator Principles (a financial industry benchmark established by major financial institutions worldwide based on the policies and guidelines of the International Finance Corporation and the World Bank, aimed at judging, assessing, and managing environmental and social risks in project financing), but not limited to it, "because the Equator Principles themselves have some limitations, in some places we (ICBC) are stricter than the Equator Principles."

Chinese Enterprises Lack Policy Guidance for Overseas Investment

A statistic from International Rivers shows that by May 2008, Chinese enterprises and financial institutions had participated in at least 97 large dam projects in 39 countries worldwide. Many of these projects are located in Southeast Asia and Africa, but governments in Latin America, the Middle East, and Eastern Europe also cooperate with Chinese enterprises in building new dams. Most of the dams are built for hydropower generation.

Why are more and more dams being invested in and constructed by Chinese enterprises? The analysis in the "Overseas Dam Industry Guide of China" by International Rivers indicates that China owns half of the world's large dams and has many dam construction companies and dam equipment suppliers. Meanwhile, fierce competition exists in domestic dam construction in China, prompting many Chinese engineering and construction companies to seek overseas business opportunities. The "going out" strategy formulated by the Chinese government also provides motivation for enterprises to develop international markets.

In the international market, Chinese enterprises have low operating costs and often win bids for dam construction projects over competitors from other countries. Additionally, traditional dam investors internationally have gradually withdrawn from investing in large dam projects, creating space for financial services provided by Chinese banks and export credit agencies.

However, environmental issues in Chinese enterprises' overseas investments and aid also deserve attention. At the academic seminar on "Reform and Innovation of Environmental Economic Policies in the New Era" held in 2008, the Environmental Planning Institute of the Ministry of Environmental Protection published the investigation report "Environmental Protection in China's Overseas Investments and Aid."

The survey shows that the regions where China's overseas investments and aid attract attention are concentrated in Africa and Southeast Asia. From an industry perspective, they are mainly concentrated in mining, dams, construction, timber, and other industries. The report states, "For example, China's dam construction and oil extraction projects in Africa have been frequently criticized by Western media."

The report states that oil fields and other key resource developments, processing and assembly manufacturing, and labor-intensive construction and service industries are relatively concentrated industries in China's overseas investments. These industries are closely related to the local environment, and without strengthening environmental management, they can easily cause certain environmental problems locally.

"Our overseas enterprises mostly do not have environmental safety departments or specialists set up in overseas branches or project teams," the report also points out. Currently, the development of external credit business in China's banking industry is rapid, but there are many environmental problems, one of the main reasons being that environmental issues have not been incorporated into their credit management and risk management, resulting in environmental risks not being effectively controlled.

The survey shows that financial institutions in our country also lack relevant standards in industries such as mining, forestry, and others that affect the environment. Currently, only the China Development Bank has adopted independent environmental fiscal standards. The Export-Import Bank of China also has corresponding environmental policies. In addition, the Shanghai Pudong Development Bank has published a cooperation responsibility report.

The report suggests that the country should soon formulate and issue environmental behavior guidelines for China's overseas investments and aid, adopting measures such as environmental impact assessments, environmental planning, and community development throughout the project life cycle to reflect the concepts of environmental protection and sustainable development in the entire process, achieving a "win-win" situation of overseas investments and protecting the local environment.

Regarding the banking practices in overseas investments and aid, the report suggests that the country should formulate and issue China's overseas green credit guidelines to guide and urge banks to better consider environmental issues in financing decisions. Banks should actively incorporate environmental factors into overseas credit decision-making. At the same time, the country should establish a special fund for green economic cooperation, alleviating international pressure through environmental demonstration projects.
