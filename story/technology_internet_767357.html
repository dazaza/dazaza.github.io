KitKat is pictured alongside the Nexus 5, with Android 4.4 just around the corner.

by anonymous on 2013-10-13 13:53:09

After the hustle and bustle of the smartphone world in September, the phone that has likely captured our attention the most this year is undoubtedly the much-talked-about Nexus 5. The high-end configuration and reasonably priced Nexus 4 was a big hit in sales last year, and it’s rumored that the Nexus 5, also featuring top-tier specs, will continue with an affordable price tag. Another concern we have is whether Android's next version, codenamed KitKat (Android 4.4), will arrive alongside the Nexus 5?

It's worth noting that the first unofficial leak of the Nexus 5 came from Google's official KitKat promotional video. Additionally, according to CNET, Google will simultaneously release Android 4.4 and the Nexus 5 on October 15th, though no media outlets have received invitations yet. Last night, the Italian website Tutto Android leaked a set of the latest spy shots of Android 4.4 and the Nexus 5.

This is the most extensive leak of Android 4.4 since the dialer interface was previously exposed. From these photos, we can clearly see some changes in Android 4.4. Most noticeably, there is less of the black and blue color scheme commonly used in Jelly Bean, with increased transparency in the interface.

In the photos, the small blue icons in the top notification/status bar have turned white, and the status bar itself has become almost transparent. In the lock screen state, a camera icon appears in the lower-right corner, likely for quick access to the camera. Compared to the Nexus 4, the Nexus 5 retains its rounded design but appears slimmer, with a more elongated screen aspect ratio.

The most significant changes seem to be in the app drawer section, prompting Ars Technica's editors to carefully compare the differences between KitKat and Jelly Bean.

A close comparison of the leaked KitKat and Jelly Bean reveals that the top status notification bar and the bottom system key area are more transparent in the app drawer interface. The background is no longer black but continues the wallpaper from before, reducing the visual fragmentation of the display. It remains unclear whether this transparency will change when running applications.

Additionally, the previous 5x5 grid layout of icons per screen has been replaced by a 4x5 layout. With the larger screens and changed layout, the icons in KitKat appear significantly larger than those in Jelly Bean. The line indicating the current page position in the status bar has been replaced by a row of dots above the Home key. The APPS and WIDGETS tabs at the top have also been removed.

Notably, the icon designs have changed as well. The Google and Google Settings icons have transitioned from square to circular. The original Gallery (photo gallery) icon has disappeared; it was previously located between Email and Gmail. A new icon for Google Photos has appeared, which looks identical to the previous G+ Photos icon, suggesting that Google has merged the defunct Gallery and G+ Photos into one service. Furthermore, the Messaging icon has disappeared. According to Tutto Android, Hangouts now supports messaging functionality, so Messaging has been replaced by Hangouts. If the icons are arranged alphabetically, Google and Google+ should be grouped together rather than being placed at opposite ends, though this could simply reflect Google's testing software.

There are also some changes on the home screen, similar to the app drawer layout. The line above the Home key has been replaced by dots, and the app drawer icon has become flatter and fuller compared to its previous dotted-line structure. The phone icon has also become more abstract. Additionally, Tutto Android mentions that besides using the search function in the screen's search bar, swiping left from the last screen on the left will also activate the search function, similar to iOS. In KitKat, the voice control feature is always active, akin to Moto X, though the exact working mechanism remains unclear. It’s rumored that the Nexus 5 will use Qualcomm's Snapdragon 800 processor, which supports always-on voice control, though no device currently uses this feature.

However, this is still a test version of KitKat, and it's uncertain how the final product will look when it reaches consumers. Previously leaked dialer information showed that the Messaging feature was retained, but it mysteriously disappeared this time, highlighting the uncertainty surrounding KitKat. Nonetheless, we know for sure that Android 4.4 is not far away, and the Nexus 5 is on its way.
