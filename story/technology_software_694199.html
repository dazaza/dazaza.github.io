Buffett reveals his stock portfolio, which A-share stocks benefit from the influence of the sage

by df1iaiw7 on 2012-03-06 23:00:44

On the evening of February 25th, Warren Buffett, known as the "Oracle of Omaha," published his annual letter to shareholders on Berkshire Hathaway's website. The letter summarized the company’s performance in 2011, highlighting both successes and failures. Written entirely by Buffett, it spans 21 pages. Every year, Buffett writes a letter to shareholders in Berkshire Hathaway's annual report, which has been an uninterrupted tradition since 1970. This letter, available for free to global investors, garners significant attention worldwide.

Buffett's investment portfolio shows that he significantly increased holdings in tech stocks such as IBM and Intel, as well as shares in Wells Fargo and preferred stock in Bank of America. At the same time, he reduced positions in consumer stocks like Johnson & Johnson and Kraft Foods. However, domestic market analysts believe that Buffett's purchasing behavior in the U.S. stock market does not constitute a sufficient condition for buying stocks in the A-share market, advising caution against speculative concepts.

Continuing to advocate for equity assets, Buffett first brought good news to shareholders in the letter: In 2011, the book value per share of Class A and Class B stocks grew by 4.6%. Over the past 47 years, under the current management, the book value has increased from $19 per share to $99,860, with an annual compound growth rate of 19.8%.

The letter states that last year, the main businesses performed well. The five largest non-insurance companies under Berkshire Hathaway—BNSF, Iscar, Lubrizol, Marmon, and MidAmerican Energy—all set record operating profits. In 2011, their combined pre-tax profit exceeded $9 billion. Seven years ago, Berkshire only owned one of these five companies—MidAmerican Energy—with an annual pre-tax profit of $393 million at that time. Unless the economy declines in 2012, Buffett expects these five outstanding companies to set new records, with total profits easily surpassing $10 billion.

Buffett mentioned that with IBM, he now owns substantial stakes in four very excellent companies: American Express (13%), Coca-Cola (8.8%), IBM (5.5%), and Wells Fargo (7.6%). "We view these stakes as partnership interests in great companies, not listed securities acquired or sold based on short-term prospects."

In addition to adhering to value investing, Buffett continues to promote the benefits of investing in equity assets, advocating for equities over bonds, gold, and other products. This reflects Buffett's preference for buying opportunities during bear markets. Buffett believes that volatility is not the ultimate measure of risk; the purpose of investing is to ensure safe assets and achieve stable returns higher than inflation. Holding cash, government bonds, or bank deposits may seem safe but inevitably carries the risks of inflation and currency devaluation, which is essentially an invisible tax. Analysts argue that stock investments are the only assets capable of achieving annualized returns higher than CPI, which is the essence of Buffett's warning.

However, Buffett's theories clearly cannot be directly applied to the A-share market. Cao Ling, General Manager of the Investment Department at Anzhou Investment in Guangzhou, analyzed that from an environmental perspective, the A-share market is still immature, with significant fluctuations in the economy and policies affecting the stock market. From an investor standpoint, the U.S. market is dominated by institutional investors, while the domestic market is primarily retail-driven, leading to significant differences in operational styles. Additionally, while U.S. listed companies have standardized disclosure practices, it is challenging for retail investors in China to conduct long-term value research independently. Furthermore, the volatility in the A-share market is much greater than in the U.S. market, making it difficult for both retail investors and funds to endure. Dividend ratios of listed companies and other factors are key influences on value investing, and the A-share market lacks necessary conditions. Therefore, learning from Buffett's long-term investment philosophy requires combining it with the realities of the A-share market, integrating value investment with trend investment.

Misjudging the timing of the U.S. real estate recovery, besides sharing successful investments, Buffett also admitted to mistakes. Buffett stated that a few years ago, he spent about $2 billion purchasing several types of bonds issued by Electric Future Holdings, a power company serving parts of Texas. This was a mistake—a very big one. Broadly speaking, the company's prospects were linked to natural gas prices, which fell and remained low shortly after purchasing the bonds. Despite receiving approximately $102 million in interest annually since purchasing the bonds, unless gas prices rise significantly, the company's payment ability will soon run out. Buffett noted that in 2010, he wrote down $1 billion on this investment, followed by another write-down of $390 million last year.

At the end of last year, Buffett valued these bonds at their market price of $878 million. If gas prices remain at current levels, there could be further losses. Conversely, if gas prices rebound significantly, some or all of the write-downs might be recovered. Regardless of the outcome, Buffett admitted that he miscalculated the probability of gains/losses when purchasing the bonds.

Another mistake pertained to real estate. Buffett stated, "Last year, I told you that the recovery in housing would likely begin within about a year. Actually, my prediction was way off. You should be sure that the housing market will recover eventually. Over time, the number of houses will match the number of households (of course, allowing for the usual vacancy rate beforehand). However, in the years before 2008, the United States built more houses than the number of households. This ultimately led to us building too many houses, and the bursting of the bubble impacted the entire economy."

This time, Buffett's conservative estimate regarding the recovery of the real estate market aligned with market trends. On the morning of February 25th Beijing time, St. Louis Federal Reserve Chairman James Bullard said last Friday that the recovery of the U.S. real estate market still needs time because U.S. homeowners are burdened with too much debt.

"Buffett Concept" has strong appeal. It is well-known that Buffett has always been a rejector of tech stocks. However, in November last year, during an interview with CNBC, he announced for the first time that he had already held 5.51% of IBM's shares, causing a stir in the market. Since then, Buffett has opened a window into the tech stock sector. In the fourth quarter of last year, Buffett also added more than 5.16 million shares of Intel, bringing his total holdings to over 11.49 million shares, worth about $3 billion.

Every move Buffett makes overseas stirs up excitement in the A-share market. In November last year, when the news broke that Buffett had become IBM's largest shareholder, Yangtze Communication, which established a joint communication solutions center with IBM, quickly hit the daily limit and achieved two consecutive daily limits in a weak market environment. The other two stocks known as the "three Musketeers of Buffett" - CITIC Guoan and Great Wall Computer - also performed well, demonstrating the strong market appeal of the "Buffett Concept."

On February 14th, the investment portfolio disclosed in regulatory filings by Berkshire Hathaway showed that in addition to increasing holdings in IBM and Intel, Buffett also increased his stake in Wells Fargo by 46.6 million shares.

Data showed that in the fourth quarter of last year, Buffett added 22.33 million shares of Wells Fargo, increasing his total holdings to 383 million shares, accounting for 7.3% of Wells Fargo's equity, worth approximately $11.7 billion, making it his third-largest holding. Not only did Buffett increase his holdings of bank stocks in the secondary market, but he also acquired $5 billion worth of 6% preferred stock in Bank of America, along with warrants to purchase 700 million shares of Bank of America common stock at $7.14 per share by September 2, 2021.

In the regulatory filings submitted by Berkshire Hathaway, it was also disclosed that the company purchased new shares in media company Liberty Media Corp and San Francisco-based kidney dialysis treatment equipment provider DaVita. Additionally, it increased its holdings in CVS Caremark, the largest pharmaceutical retailer in the United States, satellite TV company DirecTV Group, data analysis company Verisk Analytics, and Visa.

Due to the lack of clear corresponding stocks, Buffett's actions did not cause ripples in the A-share market, though the A-share media stocks have remained active due to domestic policy support.

From the investment portfolio, it is evident that Buffett has clearly abandoned some stocks. Besides selling all of his ExxonMobil shares, Buffett also reduced holdings in some consumer stocks, such as reducing his position in Johnson & Johnson to 29 million shares (from 37.4 million shares last year) and continuing to reduce his stake in Kraft Foods to 87 million shares (he held 138.3 million shares two years ago).

Cao Ling believes that Buffett's purchasing behavior in the U.S. stock market is insufficient to form a sufficient condition for buying stocks in the A-share market. Previously, the rise of tech stocks and other concept stocks was based on low valuations and small increases. The Buffett concept would find it hard to drive up bank stocks, as domestic bank stocks had already seen a rebound. Therefore, he advises investors to rationally view Buffett-related stocks.

Link: Buffett has chosen his successor

In his annual public letter, Buffett mentioned that the board has selected a person to succeed him as the next CEO but did not provide further details, only mentioning two backup candidates. Buffett also noted in the letter that newly hired investment managers Ted Weschler and Todd Combs are capable of managing Berkshire's investment business.

On December 11th last year, during an interview on a program, Buffett announced that he had chosen his eldest son Howard as his successor. Previously, David Sokol was considered a potential successor, but he resigned amid allegations of insider trading. (Source: Southern Metropolitan Daily, Author: Chen Yuanyuan)
