Roundup of Rumors for the 2013 Google I/O Conference: Home Automation System Included

by geekzhang on 2013-05-16 17:16:20

The Google I/O Global Developers Conference this year will be held at the Moscone Center in San Francisco from the 15th to the 17th of the month, local time in the US. What new products will Google bring us? Below we have compiled rumors about this year's I/O conference, so let's organize our thoughts and wait for surprises from Google.As the highlight of Google's annual event, the I/O conference attracts a large number of developers to gather in San Francisco every year to witness the launch of Google's new products. Although countless developers hope to attend in person, there are only about 5,500 tickets available, making the ticket grab very competitive. This year, all tickets were snapped up in just 49 minutes. Moreover, the tickets for this tech extravaganza are not cheap, priced at $900 each (approximately RMB 5600), but students and faculty members enjoy significant discounts, paying only $300 (approximately RMB 1860) for a ticket.The I/O conference lasts for three days. Unlike last year, Google is holding only one live release event this year, which will take place from 9 am to noon on the 15th. Last year, there were two press conferences, focusing on Android and Chrome respectively.At last year's I/O conference, Google launched Android 4.1 and the Nexus 7 tablet. With only a few days left until the opening of this year's I/O conference, what surprises will Google bring us?Android 5.0 Key Lime PieConsidering that Android 4.0 has been out for over a year, it makes sense for the next generation of Android 5.0 to appear at the Google I/O conference. There have been rumors that Android 5.0 will see significant changes compared to previous versions, with Google adding many new features based on Android 4.2. According to informed sources, although the upgrade from Android 4.0 to Android 5.0 won't be as big as from Android 2.3 to Android 4.0, the UI will undergo substantial changes compared to Android 4.0.However, recent reports indicate that devices running Android 4.3 have already appeared in the visit logs of some websites, suggesting that Google may release Android 4.3 instead of the more anticipated Android 5.0 at this year's I/O conference. But considering that most Android devices haven't even upgraded to 4.2.2 yet, releasing Android 5.0 now might indeed be too hasty.New Generation of Nexus 7 TabletKGI Securities analyst Ming-Chi Kuo believes that after the success of the Nexus 7, Google will launch a new generation of Nexus 7 at this year's I/O conference. The configuration will be significantly improved, but the price will remain the same as the previous generation of Nexus 7, starting at $199 (approximately RMB 1230). Given recent rumors about manufacturing issues causing a delay in the release of the iPad mini 2, launching a new generation of Nexus 7 at this time would undoubtedly help Google further capture the small tablet market.Kuo believes that due to its low-price strategy, Google will lose $5 to $10 (approximately RMB 30 to 60) for each new generation of Nexus 7 sold. Its sales method will also remain the same as the previous generation, being sold through the Google official website.Kuo also predicted the hardware specifications and corresponding component suppliers for the next generation of Nexus 7. From the possible configuration information, the new generation of Nexus 7 will be equipped with Qualcomm's APQ8064 quad-core processor, a 7-inch LTPS (low-temperature polysilicon technology) touch screen with a resolution of 1920x1200, a 5-megapixel rear camera, and a wireless charging module supporting the Qi standard manufactured by Texas Instruments. However, some people believe that Google will continue its partnership with NVIDIA, adopting the next-generation Tegra 4 processor, and the screen size will increase to nearly 7.8 inches, similar to the iPad mini.In terms of appearance, the new generation of Nexus 7 will adopt a narrow-bezel design similar to the iPad mini. As for its operating system, the new generation of Nexus 7 will run Android 4.x, optimized specifically for tablets and with some new functions added. Like the previous generation, the initial shipment volume will be relatively limited.Besides, consumers certainly hope that after the launch of the new generation of Nexus 7, Google can reduce the price of the older Nexus 7, because even today, one year after its launch, the configuration of the Nexus 7 is still not outdated and offers excellent value for money. Compared to Apple's App Store, Android's software store still lacks optimization support for tablet applications, so a low-price strategy might also be an effective competitive means.Nexus 5 or LTE Version of Nexus 4There have been rumors that LG will continue to collaborate with Google to produce the next generation of Nexus 5, as the Nexus 4 was based on the LG Optimus G, so the Nexus 5 could likely be derived from the LG Optimus G Pro. However, Google just released the Nexus 4 in late October last year, and launching a new generation of Nexus 5 now would make the seven-month update cycle seem too short. Would the Nexus 4 become a tragic product like The New iPad?Although the possibility of releasing Nexus 5 is slim, there are rumors that Google will introduce an LTE-supported version of the Nexus 4 at the I/O conference. Considering the widespread adoption of LTE networks in the U.S., the lack of LTE support is indeed one of the drawbacks of Nexus.Moto X PhoneSince Google acquired Motorola, there hasn't been any substantive product collaboration between the two parties yet, but the appearance of the X Phone might break this awkward situation. Since the exposure of the X Phone information, various rumors have emerged, ranging from custom services to hardware configurations and even waterproofing features, and multiple devices suspected to be the X Phone have been exposed. However, there are also rumors that Google is not satisfied with the current status of the X Phone and plans to withdraw from the project, leaving Motorola to develop it independently. It's difficult to distinguish truth from fiction among these numerous rumors, and we hope that Google will bring us some definitive news about the X Phone at the I/O conference.Google Now Landing on Chrome BrowserGoogle Now was introduced alongside Android 4.1 at last year's I/O conference and is one of Google's key products. Currently, the development momentum of Google Now seems better than Siri, especially when combined with wearable devices like Google Glass, its practicality has indeed improved significantly. Now, Google Now has broken free from the restrictions of Android and officially landed on the iOS platform, further expanding its application scope.In addition to mobile platforms, there are reports that Google is attempting to integrate Google Now with the Chrome browser and allow customization of a personalized search bar according to individual preferences. Perhaps we will see the Chrome browser with Google Now next Wednesday.In addition, Google seems to plan to add Google Now voice search functionality to its homepage and allow users to customize their homepage.Google Babel Instant Messaging SoftwareAccording to foreign media reports, Google is developing an instant messaging software called Babel, which will consolidate all of Google's current communication tools including Google Talk, Hangouts, Gmail Chat, and Google+ Messenger, and support group video chat functionality. Although Babel's functions are similar to iMessage, unlike iMessage, Babel will be a cross-platform application supporting multiple platforms such as Android, iOS, and Chrome, greatly enhancing its practicality.Foreign media have also released several screenshots of Babel. From the screenshots, the video chat button is located at the top left, and there are many emoticons, symbols, and animal expressions used for chatting. Perhaps we will see Babel at the I/O conference on Wednesday.Android@Home Home Automation Network SystemThe goal of Android@Home is to build a home device automation network system and allow Android devices to connect. This concept was proposed as early as the 2011 I/O conference, but there has been no further news since then. However, some signs indicate that Android@Home might regain focus this year.When Android 4.2.2 was updated back in February this year, the code for Android@Home was found in the network's update configuration file. In fact, this is the second time that the Android@Home system has been hidden in a version update. With the upcoming Google I/O conference, the Android@Home home automation system might become a reality, allowing us to use Android devices to control washing machines, ovens, dishwashers, and even toasters.In addition to the above rumors, Google might also bring us the latest news about Google Glass, Google Wallet, and even the long-absent Nexus Q at the I/O conference on Wednesday. As for what new products or services Google will bring, and what future development plans they have, all the answers will be revealed on Wednesday.
