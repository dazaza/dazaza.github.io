### Lesson Six: The Journey of Seeking Truth

#### From Where Does Human Knowledge Come?

**Preparatory Knowledge:**

1. Practice is the foundation of knowledge.

- Practice is the source of knowledge.

- Practice is the driving force for the development of knowledge.

- Practice is the sole criterion for testing the truthfulness of knowledge.

- Practice is the purpose and ultimate goal of knowledge.

**Learning Method:** Group cooperative inquiry.

**Example Demonstration:**

**The Invention of the Lint Filter**

How could clothes washed by a washing machine avoid being contaminated with small lumps or similar debris? This was once a daunting challenge for technicians. A Japanese housewife, while pondering this issue, suddenly remembered her childhood experience of catching dragonflies with a small net on a hillside in the countryside. If a small net could catch dragonflies, wouldn't it also be possible to catch small debris like lumps by placing a net inside the washing machine? Many technicians dismissed her idea as lacking scientific basis and ignored it. However, this housewife was not deterred by authority. After three years of repeated research and experimentation, she finally achieved satisfactory results, inventing the lint filter now used in washing machines. The patent lasted 15 years, earning her a royalty fee of 1.5 billion yen.

**Commentary:** (See mine—) Dialectical materialist epistemology holds that practice is the source of knowledge. Knowledge is an active reflection of the subject on the object, which can only be completed in practice and through the interaction between the subject and the object. In practical activities, people use certain tools as means to interact with objective objects, causing them to undergo some changes, thereby gaining knowledge about objective things. The invention of the lint filter by a Japanese housewife solved a problem that even technicians found challenging, illustrating that practice is the driving force for the development of knowledge, the sole criterion for testing the truthfulness of knowledge, and the purpose and ultimate goal of knowledge.

**Simulated Exercise:**

**Watt and the Steam Engine**

James Watt (1736–1819), an English inventor, was born on January 9, 1736, in Greenock, England. Although he attended grammar school as a child, he did not receive systematic education. Watt learned much about mechanical manufacturing in his father's factory and later became an apprentice at a London clock shop. In 1763, Watt worked at the University of Glasgow repairing teaching instruments. He often discussed theoretical and technical issues with professors there. Once, a Newcomen steam engine broke down and needed repair. While repairing this steam engine, Watt understood its entire working principle and identified the reason for its excessive fuel consumption. In 1765, he began designing a separate condenser and installed an insulating jacket around the cylinder to keep it consistently hot, significantly improving the efficiency of the new steam engine. Unsatisfied with his achievements, in 1781, he created a double-acting steam engine powered from both sides of the cylinder and adopted a crank mechanism to convert reciprocating linear motion into rotational motion. After a series of reforms, the steam engine was rapidly adopted by various industrial sectors, paving the way for the Industrial Revolution. In 1785, Watt was elected as a member of the Royal Society.

### Lesson Six: The Journey of Seeking Truth

**Pursuing and Developing Truth in Practice**

**Preparatory Knowledge:**

1. Truth is objective.

2. Truth is specific and conditional.

3. Pursuing truth is a process.

**Learning Method:** Group cooperative inquiry.

**Example Demonstration:**

**Misunderstood Intentions**

One day, Zhang Fei returned from a banquet hosted by Cao Cao, fuming and glaring at Liu Bei. Zhuge Liang asked him why, and Zhang Fei replied, "Everyone says Cao Cao is generous and magnanimous, but no, he’s the stingiest person in the world." Zhuge Liang said, "Please explain further." Zhang Fei said, "When I arrived at Cao Cao's camp, without saying a word, he extended both hands and made a circle. I thought he was suggesting lunch should be pancakes. I waved my hands and stretched out my arms, indicating I wanted noodles instead. Then he raised his right thumb, so I followed by raising three fingers on my right hand, meaning one bowl isn’t enough; I want three bowls. Cao Cao shook his head and sighed, so I left." Zhuge Liang laughed after hearing this and said, "General, you misunderstood Cao Cao. He was giving you a riddle in silence." Zhang Fei stared wide-eyed at Zhuge Liang. Zhuge Liang explained, "Cao Cao’s circle meant 'I will unify Central Plains.' Your gesture meant, 'Will my eight-zhang spear agree?' Cao Cao raising his right thumb meant 'I am the Grand Chancellor of Han, controlling the emperor to command the feudal lords.' Your raising three fingers meant 'Liu, Guan, Zhang, the Peach Garden Oath, together we are stronger than you!' Cao Cao shaking his head meant 'They say Zhang Fei is rough but meticulous, indeed deservedly so.' At this moment, he also misunderstood you, and this silent riddle ended with your victory." Hearing this, Zhang Fei was relieved and burst into laughter.

**Commentary:** (See mine—) Dialectical materialist epistemology holds that this story is akin to proverbs such as "A guilty conscience makes a sorry face" and "An honest man fears no knock at midnight." It illustrates that due to different positions, viewpoints, methods, cognitive abilities, and levels, people may form different understandings of the same definite object. However, only one correct understanding exists, i.e., only one truth. The boundary between truth and error must not be confused, and truth is equal before all people.

**Simulated Exercise:**

**The Pig's Response**

A little pig, a sheep, and a cow were kept in the same pen. One day, the shepherd caught the little pig, who screamed loudly and fiercely resisted. The sheep and the cow were very annoyed by the pig's cries and said, "He often catches us, and we don’t scream." The pig replied, "People in different positions and environments find it hard to understand each other's feelings. Catching you and catching me are entirely different matters. He catches you for your wool and milk, but catching me means taking my life!"

### Lesson Seven: The Perspective of Dialectical Materialism on Connections

**The World is Universally Connected**

**Preparatory Knowledge:**

1. Meaning of Connection

2. Characteristics of Connection:

- Universality

- Objectivity

- Diversity

- Conditionality

**Learning Method:** Group cooperative inquiry.

**Example Demonstration:**

**The Guardian Angel of the Taj Mahal**

The Taj Mahal was built by Shah Jahan, the fifth emperor of the Mughal Dynasty, for his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal. It stands on the vast Agra plain south of the Yamuna River in India. Mumtaz Mahal was exceptionally beautiful but died during childbirth while accompanying Shah Jahan on a distant expedition due to overwork. Upon his return from the expedition, Shah Jahan built her a magnificent mausoleum to express his grief. The central part of the tomb is a round marble chamber, flanked by elegant mosques featuring gold columns and silver gates. The garden is planted with rare flowers and grasses, making the Taj Mahal dignified and elegant, known as the Pearl of India. With the development of Indian industrialization, the Taj Mahal was attacked by acid rain. The white marble turned yellow, the smooth tomb developed tiny holes, the silver gate turned black, and the vegetation gradually withered, losing its enchanting brilliance. The Indian government decided to take measures to protect this pearl. Several ecologists proposed planting mulberry trees around the Taj Mahal because they are resistant to acid rain and absorb sulfur dioxide, making them a pollution-resistant species with strong pollution-clearing capabilities. The Indian government adopted this plan because planting mulberry trees is convenient, economical, and aesthetically pleasing. The polluted air coming from the direction of the chemical factories was blocked outside by the mulberry trees. After multiple layers of filtration, the air entering the mausoleum area was greatly purified. The Taj Mahal was effectively protected.

**Commentary:** (See mine—) Dialectical materialism holds that the relationship of mutual influence and constraint between things and among the elements within a thing is called connection. Any thing is interconnected with other things around it, and the whole world is a unified whole of interconnections. Connections are universal and objective, not subject to people's will. However, people can change the state of things based on their inherent connections, establish new specific connections, and make things tend towards success.

**Simulated Exercise:**

**Winning the Nobel Prize with Drinking**

The principle behind the invention of the bubble chamber, an instrument for detecting the trajectory of high-energy particles, came to its inventor, American physicist Donald Glaser, while he was drinking beer. Watching the bubbles rising in his glass, he suddenly thought about the problem he had been researching—how to detect the flight trajectory of high-energy particles. He replaced the medium traversed by high-energy particles with beer and used a few small chicken bones to represent high-energy particles. After the bubbles in the beer disappeared, he dropped them into the beer in the glass. As the broken bones sank, bubbles continuously emerged around them, showing the trajectory of the sinking bone fragments. He immediately returned to the laboratory, conducting continuous experiments. When charged particles passed through liquid hydrogen, a string of bubbles appeared along their path, clearly presenting the trajectory of high-energy particles. Glaser won the Nobel Prize in Physics for this invention.
