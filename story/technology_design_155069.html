[Regarding design] Would Microsoft's Bing be a good name?

by nemo on 2009-06-01 23:23:09

Microsoft is about to launch a new search brand: Bing, with the Chinese name "Bing Ying."

Microsoft refers to Bing as a "Decision Engine," targeting four major areas: shopping, travel, health, and local services (via). These services may sound familiar, right? Indeed, today's Bing is yesterday's Kumo, a Japanese-sounding brand name that was mocked. Microsoft hopes to replace the existing Live Search brand with Bing. For a company not known for its naming skills, Bing is indeed a short and interesting name. With Bing, the previously complex and mysterious Live Search Farecast has now become the simple and intuitive Bing Travel.

Transitioning from a product to a commodity requires brand design, and among the many steps of brand design, the most fundamental one is choosing an appropriate name.

Speaking of naming, let's start with a positive example. Palm had a product called Palm Treo. After several years of declining business, Palm reinvented itself by launching a new phone and a new operating system. Alright, the new things needed new names. The new operating system’s name was quite straightforward: Web OS, highlighting its web-centric feature. But what should the new phone be called? Palm turned to the naming company Catchword. Starting from the internal product name "Nova," after considering thousands of potential names (including avoiding hundreds of legal obstacles and ensuring global applicability), the final choice was: Palm Pre (via). Catchword is truly an expert in naming, as evidenced by their slogan: "If you can dream it, we can name it. And give it wings."

Now for a rumor, still about Palm and Pre. As the highly anticipated new smartphone, American carriers fiercely competed for the exclusive rights to sell it, and Sprint eventually won, leaving AT&T envious. It was rumored that after Sprint secured the rights to sell the Palm Pre, they made an astonishing decision: to rename the Palm Pre as "Jones." Wow! Are you Indiana Jones or Jack Jones? Thankfully, this was just a rumor; otherwise, young and attractive ladies might have been embarrassed carrying a "Jones" every day. In comparison, the Chinese name given by Palm fans for the Palm Pre is excellent: "Pang Li" (Fat Pear), which is not only cute but also directly targets the dominant fruits in the mobile market: Apple and BlackBerry.

There's also a cold joke about brands, coming from BlackBerry. There are two cold jokes about the BlackBerry brand. One is about the name – when BlackBerry users use their devices on the street, they are often approached by passersby who say, "You're using a blueberry, right!" Another joke is about appearance – when BlackBerry users use their devices on the street, they are often ridiculed by passersby who say, "Look at the person next to you using a fake E71, so ugly." Actually, BlackBerry is quite innocent. "BlackBerry" is a very fitting name, derived from two early characteristics of BlackBerry products: black color and a QWERTY keyboard that resembles the seeds of a strawberry.

So, back to Microsoft's issue: Will Bing be a good name? Given that it's in Microsoft's hands, it's hard to say. A company powerful enough to discard a great name like Live clearly has the ability to push Bing worldwide and also has the ability to turn Bing into the next Live.

Moreover, even if Bing is a good name, it doesn't necessarily mean Bing will be a good brand. And even if Bing is a good brand, it doesn't necessarily mean Bing will be a product that users love.
