Twitter Co-Founder Talks About the Future of Media

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:54:36

On September 16, Evan Williams, co-founder of the blogging platform Blogger and microblogging pioneer Twitter, launched Medium, a content platform emphasizing "perspectives" in an era where news is increasingly fast-paced. Although Medium has been online for over a year, it remains shrouded in mystery. Recently, Williams revealed Medium's algorithm for the first time in an interview with TechCrunch and outlined his vision for the future of the media industry.

The following are the main points:

1. Good News Can Enlighten the Masses

Williams pinpointed the awkward pain point of the news industry: the accumulation of large amounts of news is merely to keep readers hooked, creating a 'dependency' where they feel uncomfortable without daily news consumption. However, these news items often lack insight and do not make people more knowledgeable.

In his view, people should focus more on insightful and brilliant opinions: "Generally speaking, most news is trivial, and if people read less news and more well-founded opinions, most would benefit greatly." He believes that the perspectives and stories in publications have the power to change lives, and his motivation to enter the media industry stems from his childhood passion for reading books. He also pointed out that the internet can significantly release the thirst for knowledge that was previously satisfied by books, thereby promoting comprehensive social progress.

Williams' ideal is for people to gain enlightenment through reading, where the importance of news is directly linked to its knowledgeability and inspirational significance. Additionally, he believes that industry experts should write news more comprehensively and objectively — the media's coverage of "global warming" being an example, as the one-sided argument of "human causes leading to global warming" is actually incomplete.

2. Tech Blogs Are Seriously Ill

Regarding tech blogs like TechCrunch, Williams bluntly stated: "Current tech blogs are completely garbage." He believes many tech blog authors are not competent in technology and expressed his desire to redefine "professional writing" in the industry. He also noted that compared to everyday trivial business news, the weekend columns written by business celebrities for TechCrunch are "absolutely more valuable."

However, Williams admitted that Medium cannot overcome the problems of tech blogs, and there will be garbage content on Medium as well. He cited examples such as Peter Shih's "10 Reasons Why I Hate San Francisco," and Michele Catalano's article about how searching for "backpack" and "pressure cooker" on Google led to a police visit — both were modified or removed due to factual inaccuracies.

But Williams defended by saying: From Twitter to blogs, there is always junk content on the internet, and the key lies in whether one can mine and emphasize excellent content.

3. Simple but Not Crude Medium

"Everyone has a story or opinion worth sharing, just lacking a place with an audience," said Evan Hansen, former editor at Wired and currently Senior Editor at Medium. Medium positions itself as a "composite" of professional news media like The New York Times and blog sites like The Huffington Post, aiming to become a platform for spreading excellent views and stories.

Medium has already seen many posts with over a million views, such as Nick Crocker's pictorial post reflecting on the prevalence of junk food in American grocery stores, and Aron Solomon's exposure of Uber's price gouging during a Toronto downpour. Moreover, the timeliness of these hot posts is largely attributed to Medium's simplicity and ease of use.

Writing articles on Medium is very simple, requiring no website registration or design consideration. As Williams put it, "When inspiration strikes, just write." Medium's simplicity has earned praise from many famous writers and designers, such as Facebook designer Julie Zhuo, who praised it for being "what you see is what you get, no need to worry about trivial details like correct single quotes," while The New York Times tech columnist Nick Bilton considered it "truly putting technology behind the scenes," humorously adding: "Thankfully, it doesn't plan to become an independent blog platform, otherwise WordPress would lose its bread and butter."

Nevertheless, Medium understands that relying solely on trendy hot posts won't take them far. Therefore, Medium has invested a significant budget in commissioning long-form reports with professional magazine standards, such as the recent ten-thousand-word article "Mercenary" narrating the legendary life of a 62-year-old retired commando veteran who went to Peruvian mines to find gold bars. In addition, Epic Studios, collaborating with Medium, promises to publish five more captivating stories within the next 18 months, and war journalist David Axe plans to publish a series of long articles introducing military technology on Medium.

Hansen pointed out: If you only pursue low-quality content, advertisers won't value you, nor will high-end readers and industry authorities, eventually making you worthless.

4. First Disclosure of Intelligent Algorithm

Traditional news editors are often quite sensitive to content that attracts reader attention but do not know whether readers can gain more knowledge from the news.

To address this issue, Williams disclosed Medium's intelligent algorithm for the first time — it recommends stories based on the amount of time users spend reading specific articles, similar to how Pandora's music streaming service recommends music.

Williams explained that Medium focuses more on the time users spend on articles rather than just page views because this metric can well reflect whether people derive value from the articles — in this information-explosion era, getting readers to patiently finish a long article is not easy, and articles that can "stick" to readers must have something special.

However, advertisers pay based on page views, so even "orthodox" media like CNN places sensational content like "Miley Cyrus' bizarre dance at the MTV Awards" on their homepage to attract clicks.

In the online news industry, deep articles filled with real substance are certainly commendable, but if no one clicks on them, they cannot generate revenue for websites — so how does Medium, which has no ads, plan to make money?

5. Staying True to Innovation as a "Web Person"

Medium's current biggest concern is how to reach and maintain a certain scale, while the issue of profitability remains unclear. Perhaps Medium could consider selling compelling stories like "Mercenary" as e-books or authorizing other media to publish or reprint articles and earning a share of the revenue.

However, Williams divides the managers of Silicon Valley internet companies into two categories: "Web people" and ".com people" — the latter exited the industry after the dot-com bubble burst, while the former successfully persisted. Williams explained that "Web people" love the internet and are enthusiastic about exploring various innovative possibilities, not solely for profit — these people are more resilient and won't be deterred by the inability to get rich quickly, so some of them eventually created today's user-rich, profitable products.

From Blogger, which made bloggers equal with media heavyweights, to Twitter, allowing everyone to broadcast 140-character messages worldwide, to Medium, a quality content platform spreading good ideas and stories, Williams has completed his "three jumps as a Web person."

Williams is optimistic about Medium's future development, and his biggest bet is: humans have a higher-level pursuit of ideas and stories, thus long-form content will also be popular.
