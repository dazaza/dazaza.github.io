What should I do?

by haiptaoazr on 2011-09-13 21:36:50

1, The Prelude

What should I do? Below is my statement:

One

In the year I was in the third year of junior high school, my father passed away due to illness. It was just after the last subject of the middle school entrance examination. Our head teacher, who was waiting outside the examination room, wiped her tears secretly and took me home without a word. She first let me have dinner (much earlier than usual. As a boarding student, I had always eaten and drunk for free at her home during this special period), then she told me a shocking news: My father had died in the hospital at noon. My father was injured by the Red Guards when he was young and didn't finish his studies. His health has been fluctuating ever since. Although I was mentally prepared, I still cried when I heard the bad news.

That year, I was fifteen years old and I got into No. 1 Middle School. My first love, the only daughter of my head teacher, Hong, was fourteen years old, and she went to study at the Normal School.

During my three years of high school, my studies went smoothly. After graduation, I was admitted to a prestigious university in Beijing. Hong graduated from the normal school and became a teacher at the attached primary school. During my freshman year, I still kept in touch with Hong through letters and phone calls. We traveled together during holidays like siblings. In my sophomore year, either she or I were busy, so our contact gradually decreased. That summer vacation, I often went to her house, but it seemed that she had a boyfriend. I wasn't sure and it wasn't appropriate to ask the teacher. In my junior year, the teacher finally informed me over the phone that Hong had a boyfriend and I needed to be strong. By the time I was a senior, Hong was already married, barely exceeding the legal age. Apparently, the love her husband gave her was something I couldn't offer.

I didn't come back for the wedding banquet, nor did I rush to find a job after graduating from my undergraduate program. My mother and older sister had immigrated to the United States because of relatives abroad. Originally, I was also on the list of applicants, but considering Hong's factors (the teacher was so good to me, and I planned to become a son-in-law, which was my inner thought that I hadn't told anyone), and considering that I could also attend a prestigious domestic university, I gave up.

Now, Hong has become someone else's wife, and I have truly become an abandoned child. Without much hesitation, I chose to pursue further studies. Since there were no family members in my hometown, and it was meaningless to stay long-term at my uncles' houses, I wanted to visit the teacher's house, but I hesitated as Hong was already married. When I got accepted into the graduate program, I reported the good news to the teacher. The teacher said, "You will make progress, I haven't misjudged you." After hanging up the phone, I cried for a long time. This was the last time I cried profusely. From then on, if I had tears in my heart, I would force myself to smile and endure them.

Graduate school life was actually harder to endure than undergraduate studies. Being an undergraduate still felt like being a student, but being a graduate student felt more like being cheap labor. At that time, the number of graduate students had expanded, and it was rare to see my mentor (this made me miss my junior high school head teacher, my benefactor, Mr. Li even more). However, I can say that I didn't waste my time. I studied and worked hard like a madman. Even some private projects that my mentor undertook, I was willing to help him work on them almost无偿ly.

At the age of 24, I graduated with a master's degree. I could have chosen to continue my PhD studies or go to the US for further education, but pursuing a PhD would make me too old, and as for going to the US, I had already given up on that opportunity before. Therefore, I decided to join the workforce. By this time, Hong already had a daughter.

My first job was one I should have been satisfied with; it was of a civil servant nature. But I didn't want to live like this for the rest of my life. Even while I was still working full-time, I was doing side jobs, including tutoring. After tutoring, I would return home and continue writing papers until late at night. During this period, I earned a lot of money by helping companies with various projects. I urgently needed money because I was planning to quit my job and start my own business. Relatives and friends overseas also provided a lot of financial support.

Heaven didn't treat me unfairly. Regardless of whether it was through legitimate means or unconventional methods, all my efforts were not wasted. Knowing gratitude, if I had enough to eat, I shouldn't forget to give those who helped me at least a bowl of porridge. When my company entered a stable phase, I could take some time off to donate and help with poverty alleviation. Of course, I didn't forget about my teacher.

The teacher had already retired, and we hadn't seen each other for many years. The elderly woman wasn't in very good health and wasn't as optimistic as before. She still lived in the same old residence, lonely and alone. I chose to visit her the day before her sixtieth birthday to avoid meeting Hong, who would probably come the next day.

When I knocked on the familiar, weathered wooden door, I saw my teacher whose white hair now dominated her appearance, and whose face was lined with more wrinkles. Only her beautiful big eyes retained a bit of their former charm and kindness. The relaxed and proud mood I had tried to cultivate on the way disappeared, and I chose to kneel down...

The teacher wasn't surprised. She came over and almost hugged me, gently stroking my head and saying, "I knew you would come."

I smiled and forced myself to hold back my tears, saying, "I've come too late. In the future, I will come back often and see you frequently."

The teacher helped me up.

That evening, the teacher and I sat together for dinner. I talked about my experiences, and she told me about her life after I left. Seemingly tacitly understanding each other, neither of us mentioned Hong. Zhang Xiaoxian once said, "Love makes people forget time, and time makes people forget love." Why must we painfully forget someone? Time naturally makes you forget. If time cannot make you forget the person you shouldn't remember, what is the meaning of the years we have lost? Yes, when the love between Hong and me has become a thing of the past, let time continue to fade it away.

When we parted ways, I explained to the teacher that I had to go out of town and might not be able to come back to celebrate her birthday. The teacher said, "Today, you have already celebrated my birthday for me. For so many years, today I feel the happiest."

After leaving the teacher's house, I immediately called some classmates nearby and asked them to inform others to try to visit the teacher tomorrow if possible. If feasible, invite the teacher to a restaurant for a birthday banquet. I found the class monitor who lived closest and gave him a sum of money to pay for the meal.

2. The Teacher's Birthday Banquet

Two

The teacher's birthday banquet was held at a restaurant. All the classmates who could make it showed up, around two-thirds of them. Of course, Hong and her husband couldn't be absent. Only the teacher and the class monitor knew why I, her favorite student back then, wasn't present.

According to what the class monitor later told me, Hong specifically asked why I didn't come. The monitor told her that I had gone far away and couldn't make it back in time. Hong seemed disappointed. Well, let her be disappointed. In fact, I didn't go anywhere. When the banquet was about to end, I specially drove near the restaurant, but I didn't get out of the car or go inside. Actually, I am a very rational man. I can face my classmates and even Hong's husband, but I simply couldn't face Hong. Teacher, I can only wish you a happy birthday and longevity in this way. I gripped the steering wheel and gazed at the entrance of the restaurant, feeling a little melancholy.

After the banquet ended, the classmates successively thanked the teacher and left. Finally, the teacher and Hong's couple took a car home. With the night scenery and some distance, I didn't clearly see how they looked. Vaguely, I felt that Hong still looked very elegant, and her husband wasn't as tall as me.

When their car passed by my car, I didn't look out but closed my eyes involuntarily. A moment later, I suddenly realized that I wanted to catch up and talk to them, so I started driving after them. But I just followed them, and when their car entered the school gate, I didn't have the confidence to catch up with them. Watching their car disappear from sight, I slowly drove away.

On the way home, I remembered the happy days I spent with Hong in the past. We were classmates since the first grade of elementary school. This kind of feeling is hard to explain rationally. Even during the gender exclusion period in grades five and six, Hong and I could still hug and be intimate.

When we entered the first year of junior high school, I was selected into the key class, and I was separated from Hong. I was almost furious. At that time, Hong's mother, Ms. Li, happened to take over our class's Chinese subject and become our head teacher (a cycle from the first to the third year of junior high school). As soon as class was over, I pestered Ms. Li to let Hong join my class. The teacher smiled without saying anything. (This story is touching.)
