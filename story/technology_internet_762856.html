When the world revolves around Mobile...

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:39:54

If we say that the world in which my father's generation, commonly known as the Baby Boomers (post-war baby boom), grew up revolved around "television," then the world in which my generation, commonly known as Generation Y, grew up revolves around "PC" and "Web."

Various signs indicate that my son’s generation, commonly referred to as Generation Z, should be a world revolving around Mobile – the shipment volume of Smartphones + Tablets officially surpassed PCs in 2011, and by 2015, this gap will widen to five times.

A world revolving around Mobile will differ from the previous world revolving around PC + Web. Bill Gurley of Benchmark Capital and Bubba Murarka of DFJ recently wrote separately on Transitioning To A Mobile Centric World and 9 Ways A Billion Dollar New Mobile Company Might Be Created to outline their observations and predictions.

Combining the points they raised with my own observations, I believe there are several threads worth considering for entrepreneurs:

Saturation? – Although the penetration rate of smartphones has exceeded 50% in many countries, which may indicate that the growth rate could start to slow down, from another perspective, there is still another half of consumers to penetrate. Moreover, the penetration rate of tablets still has a long way to go.

Time Share – More importantly, the amount of time Mobile occupies for consumers will continue to increase, especially tablets, which will take away consumer attention from PCs, TVs, and magazines.

Design – On Mobile, due to the smaller screen space and the primarily "touch" interaction method, which is very intimate, the importance of design (not beautification) will only continue to grow.

Android – Now, when developers discuss, it’s no longer about whether Android or iOS takes priority, but rather which versions and models of Android should take priority. This, of course, reflects the fragmentation of Android, but also shows its significance.

Apps – In the Web world, the browser is the most important channel for accessing the internet, but in the Mobile world, the browser is just one of the apps, and its position is becoming increasingly weak. Previously, we accessed 200 million websites worldwide through browsers, but in the future, we will discover and use millions, tens of millions, or even hundreds of millions of Apps through Mobile Desktops, App Stores, and Play Markets.

Cross-border – Internet companies starting from Desktop Web now must consider what apps to create to capture users migrating to Mobile. Internet companies starting from Mobile cannot do without the Web either, so the complexity of development and operation for future internet companies will inevitably increase.

Search – In the Mobile world, the importance of search has significantly decreased, and Google Search's dominant position faces serious challenges.

SEO – Since there is no unified search engine, the effectiveness of driving traffic through search engine optimization (SEO) will naturally decrease significantly, and thus the importance of SEO will gradually weaken.

Promotion – Promoting apps will continue to be difficult because there lacks an Earned Media channel like SEO that is effective in the long term.

User Value – Leaving a website requires just one mouse click, whereas uninstalling an app involves several more steps. Combined with the aforementioned high promotion costs, the result is that an app installed on a user's Smartphone/Tablet will have far greater value than a website visitor.

Payment & Commerce – Convenient payment channels on Mobile are expensive – both Play Store and App Store charge a 30% transaction fee. Inexpensive payment channels are inconvenient, so mobile payments and mobile commerce still have a long way to go (my personal estimate is 5-10 years).

The above are some observations about the impending Mobile world, hoping to give you some food for thought.
