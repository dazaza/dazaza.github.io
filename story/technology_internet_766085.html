Google Uses 'Big Data' to Trim Workers' Waistlines

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 15:02:10

According to the U.S. Washington Post, last year Google Inc. faced an "M&M candy" crisis - employees ate too much of the freely supplied candies, causing concerns over potential impacts on their health.

To address this, Google's data analysis experts launched a project known as the "M&M Project." The aim was to investigate patterns in employee consumption of free snacks, referencing papers on food psychology and conducting experiments.

The "M&M Project" experiment group found that if chocolate was stored in opaque containers while healthier snacks like dried figs and pistachios were placed in transparent glass jars, would the total calories consumed by employees from free snacks decrease? The answer was yes - in seven weeks, employees at Google's New York branch consumed 3.1 million fewer calories just by eating less M&Ms. This is equivalent to each of the 2,000 employees in the New York branch eating nine fewer packets of M&Ms.

Many tech companies provide extra benefits to their employees, such as free snacks and buffets. However, at Google, every benefit transforms into data, including salaries, vacations, the size of plates served in the canteen, and even the happiness index in the workplace.

Google is utilizing its expertise in data analysis to boost employee morale and productivity.

Jennifer Cookski, a member of Google's human resources team with a doctorate in organizational behavior, said that for large companies like Google, reforms can only be implemented through small steps, "We treat our employees as seriously as we do analysis and experiments in the technology field."

Google stated it is difficult to say whether the "M&M Project" directly led to employees becoming slimmer or if some employees felt happier due to consuming fewer calories. However, Google consistently ranks high in various "Best Workplaces" surveys. Experts believe experiments like the "M&M Project" demonstrate that scientific methods should be used in human resource management rather than relying solely on intuition.

Lazlo Bock, senior vice president of People Operations Group, who studies human resource issues, said, "Data leads directly to the truth. When people talk about data, it includes facts and figures that describe an actual problem." Some company leaders overly trust their own ideas, making decisions based on imagination without collecting data to understand the truth.

However, Bock also emphasized that while using data, one should not ignore the intuition or common sense of managers.

In August last year, Google began offering death benefits to employees because "it was the right thing to do." Bock said this decision was not based on data analysis.

Some workplace issue experts question whether Google analyzes every corner of the office. Some experimental results are obvious, such as providing unlimited free fruit which will always be taken.

Other analysts question whether Google's provision of free food, rest stations, and massage services leads employees to stay longer in the office, fostering an overtime culture.

Miriam Salpeter, owner of Keppie Careers, expressed doubts about the intentions of companies offering extra benefits, "If a company provides you with lunch and dinner, you're likely not going to leave before mealtime. Extra benefits shouldn't only include fun activities and games. A company may have good intentions, but for a profit-driven business, the ultimate goal is still to make money. Everyone is a gear creating wealth for the company."

However, some professionals believe that Google is trying to show its care for employees. In the economically struggling United States, many companies have repeatedly cut core benefits such as pensions and medical insurance, and Google's efforts undoubtedly receive welcome from employees.

Career analyst John Nelson said, "The 'M&M Project' has symbolic significance. Employees perceive this experiment as originating from a culture of caring for employees, where the leadership is closely connected with the employees."

For Google, the reason for implementing this project might simply be that employees were eating too much free candy.

Previously, Google tried to encourage employees to drink more water and less sugary drinks. To achieve this, bottled water was placed on shelves at eye level with transparent glass doors. Sugary drinks were placed at the bottom of the fridge with opaque doors. After several weeks, the proportion of employees drinking bottled water increased by 47%, while the total calories consumed from sugary drinks decreased by 7%.

Google publishes the results of some experiments and surveys in the cafeteria and office corridors. Subsequent surveys showed that 70% of Google employees said they were happy to know about their nutritional intake.

Google co-founder Sergey Brin insists that every Google employee should have access to free food within 200 steps of their seat. Therefore, the New York branch alone has four large cafeterias and 35 "mini kitchens."

Brin's idea is that eating brings people closer together; when engineers and company leaders meet in the kitchen or cafeteria, they may come up with new products and ideas.

However, the plates used for serving food at Google are also "Google model." To help employees eat less, the company experimented with plate sizes. The results showed that when both large and small plates were provided, nearly a third of employees used small plates and did not return for seconds. After the company published the results of the experiment, the proportion of employees using small plates increased, exceeding 50%.
