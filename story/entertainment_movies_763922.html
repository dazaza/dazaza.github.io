Behind-the-scenes Production:

The 1962 black-and-white film "To Kill a Mockingbird" remains a favorite among many cinephiles to this day. In an online poll conducted by IMDB, the film was ranked as one of the greatest movies in history, placing 29th. The lead actor Gregory Peck, known for his handsome and charming young leading man image, won the double crown of Best Actor at the Oscars and Golden Globes for his refined and steady performance. The image of the lawyer he portrayed, who disregarded personal danger to fight for justice for blacks, became the number one screen hero in film history.

The film is adapted from the best-selling novel of the same name. Female writer Harper Lee, using the Great Depression-era life in America as a backdrop, unfolds the social life of the small Southern town of Maycomb through the childhood perspectives of two children, Jem and Scout. In the book, the siblings' father Atticus is a lawyer who takes on a so-called rape case, defending the accused black man. As the litigation progresses, the once stagnant town becomes lively, with various characters emerging one by one. Through the narration and feelings of the children, the book gradually draws readers into the deep recesses of human behavior.

Director Robert Mulligan depicts Maycomb as a dull old town, full of dirty streets, pointed fences, climbing vines, houses supported by brick pillars, and all sorts of rocking chairs and Panama hats. The film shifts the focus of the narrative to Scout's father Finch. He is a single father of two children, a lawyer representing kindness and justice, a great hero in an ordinary world. The only mention of the mockingbird in the entire film occurs during a discussion about bird hunting, where Finch repeatedly advises the children not to harm mockingbirds because they only sing for humans and never harm them. The humanity shining through this small-town lawyer is enough to make many war heroes pale in comparison.

In fact, actor Peck earned his title as America's top hero in just this way. Director Robert initially approached him to play the role in the film, and Peck was very happy. Peck believed he didn't need to exert much effort to identify with the character; he could step into Finch's shoes without any acting. In real life, he was an active participant in the black civil rights movement. In the famous nine-minute defense scene in "To Kill a Mockingbird," Peck's passionate speech moved everyone on the set. When the director called cut, thunderous applause immediately echoed throughout the crew.

Peck once claimed that "Roman Holiday" was his favorite movie, but he also believed that only "To Kill a Mockingbird" was "closest to my heart, the peak of my acting career." It's worth noting that this film is also the favorite movie of another well-known American hero, Clark Kent, the real identity of Superman.

Trivia:

-On the evening of June 11, 2003, Gregory Peck passed away at home at the age of 87. Not long before that, the American Film Institute selected the 50 greatest heroes and villains in the history of cinema over 100 years. The lawyer Atticus Finch played by Peck in "To Kill a Mockingbird" was chosen as the greatest screen hero in film history.

-"To Kill a Mockingbird" is an autobiographical novel by American female writer Harper Lee, her first and only work. The novel was published in 1960, won the Pulitzer Prize in 1961, and was adapted for the big screen in 1962. To this day, the novel has been translated into more than 40 languages, with sales exceeding 15 million copies, becoming a recognized classic of American literature. Afterwards, Harper retired to her hometown in Alabama, refusing interviews and living a quiet life. When her nephew asked her why she didn't continue writing after gaining fame, she replied: "After having done something like that, what else is there to write?"

-Finch is the maiden name of Harper Lee's mother, and Dill's prototype is Harper's childhood friend Truman Capote. Gregory Peck was not the first choice for the film; originally, Rock Hudson was considered to play the lawyer Finch.

-In the film, Arthur Radley is believed by people to be mentally ill and is mostly locked in the basement. To play this role, Robert Duvall avoided sunlight for six weeks and dyed his hair blonde. This film was also his debut.

-The watch in the movie was just a prop. After filming was completed, Harper Lee actually gave Gregory Peck her father's watch because seeing Peck reminded her too much of her own father. For example, during the filming of a street scene, Peck, immersed in his role, suddenly noticed tears in Harper's eyes standing nearby, so he walked up to her and kindly asked. Harper replied: "Because I just saw your protruding belly, exactly like my father's." Peck laughed and said: "That's not a belly, but a wonderful performance."

-Phillip Alford told his mother he didn't want to audition for the role of Jem Finch, but his mother said if he went, he wouldn't have to go to school for half a day. With that, Phillip immediately agreed to go.

-Without any rehearsal, Brock Peters burst into tears right on the spot during the actual shooting. Gregory Peck later said that to keep himself from choking up, he had to avoid looking directly into Brock's eyes and instead look past him.

-During the filming, Mary Badham, who played Scout, and Gregory Peck gradually became good friends. They maintained contact throughout Peck's lifetime, and he often called her Scout.
