        Are you planning to open a dry cleaning franchise laundry? Or are you thinking of completely reforming your laundry? Both tasks need careful consideration. And with your schedule always packed, you have no time for this work. As an outsider, how can you deal with all these questions? For this reason, "Today's Washing" visited Mr. William Taylor, the chief engineer of the famous washing industry planning company in Syracuse, New York - William Taylor Company, to learn from him. Below is his invaluable experience provided to all friends who aspire to join the laundry store industry, truly detailing the entire design process of a qualified laundry. Dry cleaning franchise chain

   

        Hire an expert 

        You must remember, you're not decorating an apartment, neither the mailman nor the delivery person can offer any useful advice. Therefore, hiring an expert in this industry is very necessary, which can avoid many unnecessary troubles.

Your first step is to hire a senior washing consultant and an excellent engineering designer, ensuring their participation in various stages of the start-up phase.

"The washing consultant can supervise the project from beginning to end as we imagined," Taylor explained. "They will also propose many constructive suggestions based on improving efficiency and diversity, making adjustments from time to time."

Designers often notice issues related to the washing environment before you do. They usually request a small amount of funds to create the best washing environment in the most economical way.

        Measure your needs 

        The next step is to determine your needs. How many customers will a new store attract? Is the old store really bustling with customers, requiring expansion? Have you calculated the total cost and time needed for the entire construction project and equipment purchase?

Once the basic investment is determined, you can start formulating a basic financial plan. Is this expenditure applied for from a government foundation or borrowed from a bank? Don't rush to get the money; first observe whether there are tax incentives locally that promote financial development, or some environmentally-friendly governments may provide you with some green facilities!

"Set a time limit," Taylor said. "How long it takes to design, how long it takes to buy machines and put them into use, etc., should all be planned out. Also reserve some emergency funds, knowing - regardless of how cautious you are, there might still be some bumps along the way."

If you buy a ready-made store, you also need to consider whether the local government permits it, whether it violates urban planning, and whether there are serious pollution problems around; if you buy a piece of vacant land, you and the architect need to investigate whether there are complete water supply and drainage systems, whether normal electricity and gas supply is available. Of course, you also need to consider the sources of customers and employees. "If there is a bus stop near the store or a subway line passes by, workers won't mind slightly lower salaries," Taylor said.

Climate change is also a reference factor, he continued: "Opening a laundry in Arizona and in Chicago would be very different. In Chicago, you don't have to worry about the doors of the cargo dock being blown open by the Mediterranean wind; in Phoenix, you need to think about how to let employees work at 115 degrees Fahrenheit."

        Pre-construction design 

        The next step is pre-construction design, starting with some necessary topographic survey work, "Choose earthquake-resistant soil structure, the hardness of materials is especially important in some earthquake-prone areas," Taylor added. Next, look at the public environmental protection facilities in that area, such as wastewater and exhaust emission pipelines. Pay attention to the designated planning of shops in that district, signs, parking spaces, and shop occupancy must follow the rules.

Your design company will undertake all the design work for drainage pipes, restroom facilities, water and gas input pipes, and even the washing equipment you need.

"The consultant's job is to organize and coordinate the placement of equipment in space because it's also a science. Machines need to be frequently replaced, so he must make comprehensive designs leaving room for future changes."
