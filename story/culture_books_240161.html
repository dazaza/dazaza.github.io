"Is the Psychologist In?" originally named "Human World," is a secret emotional回忆of a red girl.

A bold and rigorous narrative structure, a suspenseful emotional story in the style of Rebecca, a Lolita-like virgin monologue...

The story is about a girl who grew up in the red era, personally experiencing an emotional tale of intertwined destinies between herself and the older generation. The novel, seen through the eyes of a growing girl, examines decades of friendship between her father and Uncle He, encompassing cooperation, betrayal, anger, mutual appreciation, redemption, and self-redemption.

The work has a daring structure and unique mode of expression. What was avant-garde back then reads as fashionable today - the entire book is narrated in the first person by a mature woman, detailing her confessions to a psychologist. The secret encounter between the 11-year-old "me" and Uncle He in the train compartment becomes the "suspense" that guides the direction of the entire book...

This book inevitably draws the reader into its world, sometimes identifying with the unseen "psychologist", other times following the emotional trajectory of the female protagonist.

"Is the Psychologist In?" Part One

It was shameful and exciting, like putting on a swimsuit for the first time and going into the water. My feet touched his broad foot, and I felt a breeze pass over my body hair.

I think Uncle He also sensed my unease; my face must have turned red. He teased me about something, and I laughed. Water spilled over the edge of the basin. Even though he was one of my closest elders, such contact brought a closeness neither of us had anticipated. It was like the limb of some animal or the root of some plant - things that shouldn't be exposed, shouldn't be touched when exposed, and certainly shouldn't be touched by another exposed thing. From the start, he had foreseen the discomfort, and now it had arrived.

He realized the eleven-year-old girl was secretly enjoying a comfort she shouldn't feel. He wanted to correct this inappropriate feeling. Chuckling, he said that these little feet were just like a tiger's face. He often revealed unexpected imagination without thinking.

"Is the Psychologist In?" Part Two

Without Uncle He's help, my father would now be picking up manure alongside other rightists, filling explosives to blast rocks for building dams. At best, perhaps teaching first graders aged seven to sixteen in mud classrooms. Stronger still, being sent to a remote town as a cultural cadre, handling the red and white affairs of wealthy families in small cities.

The reason my father is still drawing a salary and grain tickets in this U-shaped red brick office building, still maintaining a familiar reputation in provincial newspapers, you know, is thanks to Uncle He. One day, Uncle He mentioned wanting someone to help him organize a rough draft of a novel, and my father immediately said: Let me do it. When those words slipped out, my father was very embarrassed, his earlobes flushed with blood, glowing red. He feared that his eagerness to repay kindness would be seen through by Uncle He, making him look small.

Afterwards, behind the green gauze screen, Uncle He would often listen to my father tell him about the progress of the novel.

"Is the Psychologist In?" Part Three

My father's actions made me feel utterly humiliated. It was entirely the false bravado of a lowly creature, taking advantage of the injury and predicament of a powerful being, suddenly pouncing, pitiful and ugly. Uncle He shouldn't have been so strong, but my father's petty act inadvertently highlighted his greatness.

As my father walked past his fifteen-year-old daughter, she stepped aside, feeling she needed to distance herself from this inferior lineage. You see, the fifteen-year-old girl was the sole witness to everything that happened both on and off the stage. She was the only one who divided her conscience equally into two halves: one half for her father, sympathizing with his lifetime of cowardice finally erupting in rebellion; the other half for her Uncle He, grieving over the trust and friendship he had given away.
