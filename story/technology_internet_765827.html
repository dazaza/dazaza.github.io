The Inevitable Fate of the Status Quo: The Thirteen Years of Ballmer's Leadership at Microsoft

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:06:48

I don’t know Ballmer well, but Gates knows him pretty well. In 1980, Gates lured him into the fledgling Microsoft. By 1998, he had become the president, responsible for daily management and operations. In 2000, he became the CEO, beginning his 13-year tenure leading Microsoft through many ups and downs. Today, he finally announced that he will step down as CEO soon.

Then, Microsoft's stock instantly surged by 8%.

The rise and fall of stock prices isn't everything, but it does reflect the attitudes of a significant number of people. Overall, the public or media tends to have a negative view of Ballmer. The general impression is that he’s a big fat guy, graduated from business school, doesn’t understand technology, mocked Apple when the iPhone first came out, is autocratic, and somewhat inept.

Of course, this might just be my personal impression of him. Below, we'll try to take a more objective look at how Microsoft changed during these 13 years.

Objectivity requires looking at the numbers. When Ballmer took over in 2000, Microsoft's stock price was $35, right before the bursting of the previous tech bubble. It has gone up and down since then, and now the stock price is $34, basically unchanged.

So Ballmer must be terrible, standing still, right?

No, inflation hasn’t been considered yet. The $35 in 2000 is roughly equivalent to $47 today, meaning the stock price has dropped by nearly $12. That’s even worse.

No no no, Ballmer did go to business school after all; the data shouldn’t look so bad. When he took over, Microsoft had already started falling from its peak. Within a year of taking office, the stock plummeted to $18 due to market conditions. Additionally, Microsoft has paid out $7.69 per share in dividends over the years. So, purely from stock market data, he has basically maintained Microsoft's ultra-high market capitalization, keeping it among the top five most valuable tech companies.

Also, Microsoft’s revenue has doubled over the past decade, and net profit has tripled. It wasn’t until the end of 2011 that Apple caught up.

But numbers aren’t everything. A great CEO doesn’t just produce great financial reports; they must see the company’s future and lead the team to achieve its vision starting from the present.

First, let’s talk about the bad. Over these years, which opportunities has Ballmer missed?

Search—Google rose; social networking—Facebook rose; mobile—Apple rose. Although Microsoft later launched Bing, partnered with Nokia to release Windows Phone, and made its own Surface, these moves were always late and had mixed results.

Is this his fault? Every large company has its own DNA, and sometimes it simply lacks the ability to seize opportunities that don’t fit its core competencies. Didn’t Jobs also miss out on search and mobility? Still, missing the first two could be forgiven, but missing the mobile revolution was a fatal mistake because mobile devices are devouring exactly what Microsoft relies on—the PC market, its stronghold.

In the areas where Microsoft excels, such as operating systems and enterprise services, it has maintained its advantages. However, the reality is that the PC market is declining sharply, and progress in new markets has stagnated. Microsoft can continue to rely on its legacy business, but without finding new growth engines, it’s inevitably heading toward a dead end.

Microsoft has attempted various breakthroughs, some of which were relatively successful, such as the Xbox-led home entertainment business, acquisitions like Yammer and Skype, and self-manufactured products like Surface (though sales weren’t strong). But so far, none of these initiatives appear capable of opening up a broad new market in the future (Xbox might have some potential).

Microsoft once controlled the future of the computer industry, but that characteristic has faded away. Now, it’s just a company focused on enterprise software. Indeed, it still makes a lot of money now, regularly releasing applications or systems that slightly improve our work efficiency, but it no longer has much to do with the future. Meanwhile, its competitors—Google, Facebook, and Amazon—are doing things that seem unrealistic but visionary.

From the perspective of executing well in the present, Ballmer’s performance is passable. However, in terms of envisioning the future and expanding horizons, he clearly didn’t do enough.

Perhaps this is the fate of a successor, especially in the tech field.
