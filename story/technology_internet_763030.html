Panyue Fei: Editor-in-chief? Gone.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 12:39:07

Recently, I came across the following passage:

When the Internet was first born, its purpose was to ensure that the damage of any single node would not affect the normal operation of the system.

Clearly, the Internet has achieved this goal, and this influence is now spreading to the media industry.

The most obvious change is that there is no longer a position called "chief editor."

I. Origins

It was probably when I forwarded Chen Xu's article "The Chief Editor is Dead" that I began to reflect on this issue.

Until recently, after I accidentally found myself holding this title, I realized I could self-deprecate and introspect.

II. Impressions

In my mind, the chief editor always appears well-dressed, like an imperial envoy riding a high horse, looking down on the media machine; they always have a stern face, bearing supreme power, making judgments on all issues, even acting more like God than God Himself; they never produce content, only responsible for reducing it, to maintain the correctness of the media machine; either hiding in a separate office far from everyone else, quietly muttering with the door closed, or appearing at private gatherings to complete transactions on issues; they enjoy natural media immunity, cannot be commented on, cannot be questioned, being the only invisible person in a public platform.

Every employee thinks the chief editor is impressive, but no one knows what the chief editor actually does.

III. Stupidity

A few days ago, I experienced my first power outage in Beijing. My first reaction wasn't to buy a local evening paper to check the power outage schedule at any time, but to try to find an app that automatically pushed notifications before the blackout in our neighborhood - why should I purchase a media product filled with useless demands just for a small need? Why can't a comprehensive media product package its essential needs separately for sale?

The traditional approach is to cram the page with a large amount of information, imagining with a gambler's mentality that there might be something suitable for readers, and then they will spend money.

The new chief editor should slap themselves twice and quickly stop this foolish practice. They should directly create that power outage notification app and figure out how to deliver it to users who need it.

If the chief editor doesn't subtract but adds to the media, this kind of stupidity will drag a whole boatload of people into trouble.

IV. Schools

I think future news will fall into two categories: data streams and experience streams.

I once heard a giant shouting at his team of chief editors, "What do I keep you around for? Isn't it just to put some materials online on the page? You can't even do that well. I might as well create a program that replaces your work in minutes, and it would be cheaper." This statement may sound awkward to proud media professionals, but it’s the truth.

Media is changing, possibly in two directions:

Data Streams: The ultimate form of fast-consumption information, it is a vast silent machine that senses your information needs through data, automatically grabs information from various social circles, packages it into a quickly accessible format, and pushes it to you. In this school, there are no positions for reporters and editors, only architects, analysts, and product managers.

Experience Streams: The ultimate form of slow reading. It is a large organizational structure that processes emotional opinions, personalized interpretations, the charm of opinion leaders, and immersive integrated content into beautiful media products, waiting for users from various circles to interact. In this school, there are no positions for jack-of-all-trades reporters and editors who know nothing deeply; only brokers, opinion leaders, and producers exist.

V. Disappearance

A few days ago, while drinking with a friend, we talked about their product with over 100 million users. I laughed, “When you push a message to everyone, your reach is higher than any media outlet, so why look for channels to publish articles?” That guy smiled mysteriously, “We’ve actually considered it, but there are still some concerns.”

Don’t worry. Next, the rights of traditional media and traditional chief editors will continue to diminish.

In the data stream, the pen that decides the life and death of content is taken away from the chief editor, replaced by social platforms where everyone voices, judges, and shares. Every company and individual becomes part of the media, and every service is a form of media presentation.

In the experience stream, the throne of the chief editor is taken away, replaced by opinion leaders with greater charisma, brokers with greater resource allocation ability, producers with stronger collaboration skills, and common users who follow opinion leaders.

VI. Forms

I've met a few new types of chief editors who do a good job because they give themselves new forms.

Craftsman Type: This type of chief editor is himself a great writer, leading the frontlines, producing content, becoming an opinion leader among opinion leaders, standing firm with content as the foundation.

Beauty Type: This type of chief editor is the image spokesperson of a team, energetic, full of drive, handsome, talented in multiple areas. Externally, they continuously add value to the brand of the media team; internally, they are a highly motivating engine.

Product Type: This type of chief editor, mostly from internet backgrounds, firmly embraces the data stream, reorganizes the form of content production, redistributes the media architecture, rebuilds media products, and quickly allocates resources based on data feedback to produce appropriate media products.

Nice Person Type: This type of chief editor is the most popular internally because their goal is to make journalism easy for everyone, ensuring both material and spiritual rewards for journalists are maximized.

Businessman Type: This type of chief editor understands early on that media is not just a channel. They bundle influence, brand, and users for sale, using the model of content as marketing to gain income for every bit of influence.

I think the combination of Beauty Type and Product Type is the best, yet hardest; the Craftsman Type is the easiest to find, but its effects come quickly and go quickly; the Nice Person Type and Businessman Type are the two mainstream types at the head and tail of this wave of changes, more suitable for maintaining the status quo.

VII. Prototype

Self-media is the beginning of this transformation, but not the end.

There are always people questioning some current issues with self-media. For example, when drinking with someone, I was asked: If 5000 yuan can get you a soft article, and I, Pan Yuefei, as a self-media outlet, don't accept it, but another self-media named person would, does this mean this wave will eventually decline?

I said, on a smaller scale, when the forest grows larger, all kinds of birds appear; on a larger scale, in self-media, I see the prototype of opinion leaders, the prototype of media node restructuring, the prototype of users as brands, and the prototype of services as media. The value of this wave far outweighs the hidden shortcomings.

As I mentioned in "The Truth is Dead, Experience Reigns," I predict the path of self-media is: individual self-media — small organization self-media — large organization self-media platformization — new organization self-media structuring — self-media becoming standard equipment. Everything is just starting.

Text naturally suits content generated by individuals and has already been self-mediated. The next step is video, audio, images, products, etc., which are suitable for small teams to generate content and begin to self-mediate. Only when self-media architecture reaches that stage will new business models be opened up.

A qualified chief editor needs to anticipate this trend.

VIII. Exploration

I take the opportunity to advertise: the editorial staff of Sohu IT is exploring full-team self-media.

A colleague opened a self-media account called "Argon Media" on the Sohu News client, discussing matters related to virtual online idols, which I completely don't understand, but instinctively judge as potentially good.

The rapid flow of information and the coverage of flat architectures make encyclopedic authoritative chief editors unnecessary. Media attention is divided among individuals and small teams, who vouch for their own credibility, and they are all chief editors.

This is just the beginning of a media exploration. Just as shutting down newspapers and opening websites is a restart, turning editors into self-media is another dimensional reboot.

In yesterday's [Talk Topic] interaction, Shen * said, "I remember a scene from 'House of Cards' where the heroine left a traditional large newspaper for online media. On her first day, she said, 'I've already sent the article to your email, please review it.' The female chief editor replied, 'You don't need to send it to me. If you think it has value, just send it out. I don't need to see it. I trust you.' I think this is very representative."

I don't know what is right, but at least I know what is wrong.

IX. Structure

How videos become self-media, I'm not skilled in discussing this topic. What I want to talk about is how new types of chief editors should architect self-media.
