The third difference, as probably seen by those familiar with digital cameras and projectors, is the difference in lens aperture: Digital camera lenses usually have a small aperture, while projector lenses have a much larger aperture. One reason is that projectors' chips (LCD, DLP) are generally larger, usually around 1.3, 0.9, or 0.7 inches, so the required lens aperture is also larger when achieving the same aperture value. On the other hand, the chips (CCD, CMOS) in digital cameras are generally smaller, usually around 1/4 inch, requiring a smaller lens aperture when achieving the same aperture value (f-value can be simply understood as the ratio of lens focal length to optical lens diameter). To obtain the smallest f-value (i.e., the largest aperture), the lens aperture must be increased as much as possible. This leads to projector lenses having relatively larger apertures compared to digital camera lenses. Another reason is that digital cameras require less light flux internally (if insufficient, artificial lighting can be added), while projectors need a large amount of light flux to meet viewing requirements, and there is no way to remedy insufficient brightness. Therefore, projectors are designed to use the maximum aperture (smallest aperture value), the largest lens aperture, and the highest lamp power to ensure the minimum brightness level is achieved. In designing projectors, these three parameters are not always set to the maximum value but rather find the best balance between price and performance. This is determined by the characteristics of current industrial production aimed at maximizing profit.

When choosing a projector, under normal usage scenarios and at the same price, priority should be given to selecting a standard lens. Standard lenses provide the best color reproduction and image geometric distortion correction! For confined spaces where large-size images are desired, short-focal-length lenses should be prioritized. Alternatively, if conditions allow, consider using optical reflection methods with a standard lens to achieve large-size image projections. For spacious venues and with sufficient budget, prioritize long-focal-length lenses. This method offers many advantages: first, the noise from the projector's fan is significantly reduced for viewers; second, factors such as cigarette ash, food residue, and hot beverage steam from viewers have minimal impact on long-focal-length projectors, providing a quieter working environment for the projector and facilitating its long-term operation.

The first difference is the presence or absence of the aperture component. For digital cameras, since the sensitivity range of the sensor (CCD or CMOS) is extremely narrow, these devices require a component that controls the amount of incoming light when external light exceeds the sensor's sensitivity range. This is the aperture, which consists of a group of thin curved metal blades installed around the lens. Users can adjust the aperture value to make these blades open and close evenly, forming different-sized openings to control the incoming light and adapt to various shooting needs. Generally speaking, cameras will have an aperture. However, currently, most projectors do not have an aperture component that controls the amount of light passing through. Nevertheless, due to recent advancements in projector technology, apertures have started to be used in projector lenses. A notable example is the Panasonic PT-AE700 projector, which uses "Dynamic Iris" technology. This "Dynamic Iris" is a type of aperture that expands and contracts according to the brightness of the projected image. Its purpose is to reduce the projected light by several times when the image contains large dark areas, thereby improving the image contrast. With technological progress, similar aperture technologies will likely enhance the appeal of projectors significantly.
