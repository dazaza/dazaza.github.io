15 Ways Tech Is Changing Society: Google Glass Brings the Internet Closer

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 17:06:12

Introduction: The US tech blog BusinessInsider recently published an article listing 15 ways technology has changed society, with smartphones, self-driving cars, Google Glass, and the internet all becoming major forces for social change.

The full text of the article is as follows:

Technology has the potential to greatly alter our way of life and even change how we communicate with one another.

Technologies such as 3D printing have already transformed manufacturing, allowing the general public to participate in creative industries. Mobile apps have also changed our shopping habits, with mobile commerce accounting for up to 20% of e-commerce activities. Nanofiltration has changed how we drink water. Meanwhile, Google Glass has brought the relationship between humans and the internet closer than ever before.

Here are 15 ways technology has changed society:

1. Self-driving cars are changing transportation methods.

Google's self-driving cars are already on the roads in California, where the company is testing this project. However, they won't be available for sale until 2018. Each year, 1.2 million people die in traffic accidents, and Google estimates that self-driving cars could reduce this number by half.

Whether society is ready or still watching, self-driving cars will become an option. Toyota and Audi are also developing their own self-driving cars.

2. Google Glass is turning us into cyborgs.

Google Glass is one of the most exciting products from Google in the short term because it is so significant.

Google Glass will bring daily life closer to our senses and enhance our interaction with the world around us. Our connection to the internet will be closer than ever before, and we will be able to overlay text messages, news, Twitter updates, and navigation information onto real-world scenes.

With Google Glass, we can also record various pieces of information and store them in the cloud, ensuring that we do not forget any moment of our lives.

3. Wearable fitness trackers improve health levels.

With applications and services like Nike FuelBand, Fitbit, and ZEO, we are generating more data in our daily lives. These devices help us track calorie consumption, steps taken, and even sleep patterns.

These devices are part of the quantified self movement, which aims to increase our understanding of ourselves through our own data, telling us various things about our bodies and previously unclear biological data.

4. The internet promotes sharing economy activities.

There are now a series of startups in the sharing economy that help people rent out their homes, cars, parking spaces, tents, and other items.

Airbnb allows you to rent out your home, RenttheRunway lets you temporarily own luxurious clothing and jewelry, and SideCar can immediately provide carpooling services. Similar products are countless.

But none of this would be possible without the internet acting as a matchmaker, nor would it be so convenient.

5. Robots are changing the labor market.

In the US market, many low-cost industrial robots are gradually emerging. They can perform many simple tasks and work alongside human employees.

For example, Baxter, the first robot from Rethink Robotics, can handle various materials, place items on or take them off conveyor belts, detect parts, operate equipment calmly, and even pack or unpack goods.

Rethink Robotics hopes to enhance America's competitiveness by creating cost-effective manufacturing technologies and processes. If everything goes according to plan, Baxter will be able to work alongside human employees and help the US better deal with the impact of low-wage overseas markets.

Baxter's ultimate goal is to perform more complex tasks, such as assembling components on electronics production lines. The company is also developing software to allow Baxter to exchange information with conveyor belts or other equipment.

6. Smartphones cause over-connection.

Smartphones are extremely useful tools that allow us to stay informed about the world around us at all times. Through our phones, we can check emails, news, and stock prices anytime. As long as there is a signal, phone maps ensure we never get lost. At the same time, dating apps like Tinder and Grindr help us meet new friends around us.

However, the frequency with which we check our phones is somewhat worrying. According to recent research from the American market research company IDC, 79% of users check their smartphones within 15 minutes of waking up, 48% use their smartphones while exercising, and 50% use Facebook on their smartphones in cinemas.

Text messaging is becoming increasingly popular, leading to fewer voice communications as we shift to text-based communication with each other.

7. Social networks change human-computer interaction.

Facebook and Twitter provide platforms for us to stay connected with friends and help us keep track of the movements of loved ones and even strangers anytime, anywhere. Meanwhile, social media is driving social activities because it can conveniently bring together people with the same goals.

8. 3D printing reshapes production methods and enhances creativity.

3D printing brings ordinary people into contact with the manufacturing industry and has even led to significant breakthroughs in the medical field. Just this month, a 3D printer created the world's thinnest human liver.

3D printers can print objects bit by bit in 3D space. Thanks to companies like MakerBot, Printrbot, and Shapeways, affordable 3D printers are even beginning to enter households.

3D printing has even spawned a dedicated market for selling 3D printed objects. Thanks to this technology, we have a whole new way of producing and consuming products.

9. Nanofiltration technology helps people produce and drink clean water.

Unclean water poses a significant threat to health. According to the World Health Organization, approximately 3.4 million people die each year from water-related diseases. Lifesaver was developed to address this problem. This bottle uses nanofiltration technology to purify water, removing bacteria, viruses, cysts, parasites, fungi, and other waterborne pathogens.

10. Gesture-controlled devices awaken our senses and strengthen collaboration.

The computer mouse is becoming outdated, and a series of recent technologies allow control of computers simply by waving hands. Leap Motion can better shape ideas on the screen using natural gestures.

MYO, developed by Thalmic Labs, is a sleeve that controls multiple devices through its own motion. Unlike Leap Motion, this technology does not use cameras but instead controls computers, games, and even drones by sensing muscle electrical activity.

11. Touchscreen devices and WiFi networks in underground transportation systems significantly improve travel convenience.

New York City plans to install at least 77 new touch-screen kiosks in subway stations across five boroughs. These new devices will include subway planning tools and provide the latest subway train information and delay alerts, as well as play advertisements.

Each screen is 47 inches, equipped with sensors, video cameras, microphones, and WiFi networks, transmitting information between subway drivers and the Metropolitan Transportation Authority (MTA) of Greater New York. In the future, streaming media and gaming content may also be introduced.

Meanwhile, New York is deploying WiFi networks in subway stations, making it easy for passengers to go online and make calls while waiting. San Francisco Bay Area takes it a step further, allowing users to access cell phone networks on subway trains, and free WiFi may soon be offered.

12. iPads and phones are changing the retail experience.

Once upon a time, interactions in retail stores were entirely dependent on human involvement. Now, however, companies like Apple are implementing a new model where the entire process from payment to leaving the store doesn't require interacting with anyone.

Apple retail stores are often crowded, making it hard to find staff to check out. In such cases, the EasyPay app allows users to self-checkout items like chargers, cases, and software.

The rise of tablets has also reshaped the retail experience, with many physical retailers using tablets to handle checkout. For instance, mobile payment startup Square helps retailers completely change traditional checkout modes. Its card reader and Register app enable merchants to accept credit and debit card payments via iPhones, iPads, and Android devices.

13. Smartphone apps bring convenience but also make us lazier.

It's hard to imagine the impact apps have had on society. Apple claims there's an app for everything, and that's not an exaggeration; it's actually quite true.

Tech companies continue to develop new apps to ensure our lives are more convenient. Fantastical is currently one of the best calendar apps; Evernote allows note-taking anywhere and is compatible with multiple platforms; Uber makes it easy to call a taxi whenever needed.

But these apps also make people lazier and lead to unnecessary expenses. With restaurant delivery service Seamless, theoretically, you don't need to leave home to enjoy various delicacies. Grocery delivery app FreshDirect has a similar effect.

14. Image-sharing websites change privacy concepts.

Facebook and Instagram have jointly fostered an over-sharing economy: we always like to post pictures of ourselves and our friends, whether we're eating or running naked on private beaches.

When we choose to use products like Facebook, Instagram, and Flickr, we often overlook personal information, and many people forget the fact that once posted, this information will remain online forever.

Snapchat, a self-destructing image-sharing app, solves this problem to some extent. But these disappearing images can still be seen by unexpected people.

15. Drones are becoming widespread.

The use of drones by governments and law enforcement agencies has raised privacy concerns, but some believe there is no need to worry. Moreover, the advent of smartphones has brought many advanced technologies, such as gyroscopes, accelerometers, GPS, and powerful processors, making cheap drones possible.

People have started using drones to search for survivors in rescue operations, take photos of houses and other assets, and even conduct archaeological research in Africa.

Additionally, companies like FedEx are awaiting the legalization of commercial drones. The Federal Aviation Administration will begin allowing commercial use of drones from 2015, but the flight altitude must not exceed 400 feet (approximately 122 meters), and the distance from airports must be at least 5 miles (approximately 8 kilometers).
