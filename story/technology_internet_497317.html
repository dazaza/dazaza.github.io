Kindle Fire: Low Price Doesn't Necessarily Mean Loss

by diglog on 2011-09-30 08:51:24

The release of the Amazon Kindle Fire has sparked a lot of discussion. Some analysts claim that Amazon loses $50 on every Kindle Fire sold, while others believe that the better the sales of the Kindle Fire with its deeply customized system, the greater the negative impact it will have on Android. Is this true?

**Hardware Costs**

First, let's look at the hardware costs. The claim about losing $50 per unit comes from Gene Munster, an analyst at Piper-Jaffray who specializes in analyzing Apple device sales. The problem with this analysis is that Munster provides no basis for his claim, simply stating in a report to clients:

"Apple can achieve over 30% profit margins through the hardware of the iPad, whereas Amazon is likely to lose $50 for every Kindle Fire sold."

That's it—no evidence provided. It’s impossible to accurately assess the cost without physically disassembling the device, but a relatively reliable estimate can be obtained by comparing it with products that have similar hardware specifications, form factors, and are produced by the same manufacturer.

There were reports suggesting that Amazon outsourced the design and production of the Kindle Fire to Quanta in order to expedite its market launch, rather than designing it themselves. Quanta had previously handled the hardware design and production for the PlayBook, which serves as a template for the Kindle Fire. Based on hands-on experiences reported by media present at the launch, the Kindle Fire's appearance closely resembles the PlayBook, and both devices use a dual-core 1GHz processor, lending credibility to the rumors. Therefore, without having conducted a teardown, comparing it with the PlayBook would provide a more suitable cost estimation.
