The 6th day is the seventh day of the seventh lunar month, China's traditional Qixi Festival. Many people are accustomed to calling this festival "Chinese Valentine's Day". Folklore experts say that the Qixi Festival is not "Chinese Valentine's Day", but rather "Chinese Love Festival". With a long history in China and rich connotations, it is a festival of dexterity, skill, and intelligence, which cannot be equated with the content-simple Western Valentine's Day.

Qixi originates from a tragic and moving myth: the Weaver Girl in heaven and the Cowherd on earth fall in love and marry, but are separated by the cruel Queen Mother. They are transformed into the Cowherd Star and the Weaver Girl Star, separated by the Milky Way, and can only meet once a year on the seventh day of the seventh lunar month on the Magpie Bridge.

Feng Jicai, chairman of the Chinese Folk Literature and Art Association, said that the Qixi Festival is a holiday carried by the folk legend of the Cowherd and Weaver Girl, themed around love, and starring women. According to folklore, both the Cowherd and Weaver Girl are "married individuals," and the Qixi Festival expresses the feelings of fidelity between married men and women, representing a commitment to their love, not the emotions of pre-marital lovers or sweethearts. This represents two different types of feelings at different stages of life.

In our country, the seventh day of the seventh lunar month is commonly known as the Qixi Festival, also called "Begging for Dexterity Festival," "Seven Bridges Festival," or "Daughters' Festival." It is one of the most romantic traditional festivals in China and was once the most important day for young women. On this evening, women would thread needles and beg for dexterity, praying for blessings of longevity, worshiping the Seven Sisters, with sincere and solemn rituals. They would display seasonal fruits, flowers, and needlework, with various furnishings and utensils being delicately small and charmingly attractive.

People say that on the night of Qixi, you can see the celestial meeting of the Cowherd and Weaver Girl across the Milky Way, or hear their tender whispers under the grapevine trellis.

Girls on this romantic evening face the bright moon, place seasonal fruits, pray to the heavens, and beg the heavenly maidens to grant them clever minds and skillful hands, so their needlework becomes proficient. They also pray for a good match in love and marriage. In the past, marriage was a lifetime matter for women, determining their lifelong happiness. Countless couples would quietly pray under the starry sky on this night for a perfect marriage.

In 2006, the Qixi Festival was included in the first batch of national intangible cultural heritage lists by the State Council.
