Taobao "sealed"? Maybe it was never open

by anonymous on 2013-08-20 16:07:00

By Zi Cai

Is Taobao "blocked"? Perhaps, it has never been open.

After blocking WeChat, the former Tao800 and now Zhe800 didn't escape Taobao's clutches either. Following closely on this hit list is Pocket Shopping. The recent rumors about Taobao modifying its search sales calculation adjustment rules have finally settled down. Among these rules regarding third-party platforms that direct traffic to Taobao sellers, it was stated, "For websites like Pocket Shopping and Zhe800 that attract traffic at low prices, as well as U stations, their sales will not be counted in the main search ranking and display."

In Taobao's view, these channels have not been blocked by Taobao, but the gentle flapping of the giant's wings indeed caused a catastrophic impact on these websites.

Taobao has always been extremely cautious about controlling traffic. Jack Ma once said that Taobao's traffic entry point is a grassland rather than a forest, but the need for external traffic has made it take an inconsistent path towards openness. In 2004, Taobao sent a legal notice to Yahoo, demanding it immediately stop scraping Taobao data and destroy any already scraped data. A few years later, Taobao's YiTao faced resistance from other e-commerce platforms, being blocked by JD.com and others from using its price comparison program YiTao. However, YiTao cited openness and user experience as reasons to demand JD.com follow the trend.

During the media open day in 2010, Taobao expressed its determination: Taobao must move towards openness. At the time, Taobao's CFO, Zhang Yong, stated that Taobao should become the water, electricity, and coal of all e-commerce in the internet age, becoming the infrastructure for e-commerce. Subsequently, more than 20 independent B2C companies collectively moved into Taobao Mall. Alibaba Group's Chief Strategy Officer, Zeng Ming, indicated that according to the original B2C system, Taobao intervened too much in traffic distribution. Therefore, starting from early 2012, Taobao began to strongly promote market mechanisms, with Juhuasuan as the starting point, allowing traffic to naturally match through the system and machine learning across the entire system. Jack Ma also said: "In the 21st century, enterprises must adhere to 'openness, sharing, transparency, responsibility'."

However, in reality, Taobao has been vacillating between openness and closure.

Autism Sufferer

The three major giants of China's internet industry place great importance on their rankings. Baidu calls the trio "BAT" (.bat, an executable command in DOS), while Tencent refers to them as "TAB" (the jump key on the keyboard). Compared to these two, Ali seems less concerned about this issue. Some comments suggest that this might be due to the scarcity of words starting with "A". But this company, whose future listing destination no one knows, is now associated with an A-starting word—Autism.

A few days ago, Taobao announced that images containing external QR codes within the site would be unusable. Taobao explained the purpose of this move as ensuring transaction safety, avoiding potential security risks by restricting QR codes that bypass Taobao's normal transaction process and/or management system.

About half a month ago, Taobao blocked WeChat for reasons of "controlling shopping experience and controlling Taobao transaction risks."

A few months ago, Taobao announced that external domain image links would no longer be editable or usable. Taobao's reason was "ensuring merchant data security and enhancing buyer user experience."

...

About five years ago, Taobao even blocked China's largest search engine: Baidu. The reason was still to prevent online fraud.

These blocking actions demonstrate Alibaba's determination to build a closed e-commerce ecosystem. With features like WeChat's "Scan" payment function enabled, users can complete the entire purchasing process within WeChat without needing to go through Taobao. In such a situation, Taobao cannot tolerate WeChat forming a large traffic entry point upstream, nor can it allow users to develop shopping habits within WeChat.

The website that might face blocking soon is Zhe800, which directs traffic to Taobao. Recently, Hu Chen, co-founder of Tuan800 (to which Zhe800 belongs), went to Hangzhou to communicate and negotiate with Taobao. Taobao denied the accusation of "blocking."

These actions may indicate that in the future, Taobao sellers will only be able to search for content within the Taobao business circle, making it impossible to direct traffic through external links.

Besides closing off, Taobao has even resorted to deceptive methods to obtain traffic. Currently, multiple mobile applications for shopping guides have been automatically linked to Taobao client download links, and directly redirect to the Taobao client in the product detail page. For example, in the iOS mobile client of a certain shopping guide website, when entering the product detail page, a recommendation appears to download the Taobao client. Clicking activates the Taobao client on the device and directly purchases through the Taobao client, bypassing the purchase link of the Guoku app.

An industry insider stated that Taobao's approach mainly utilizes an iOS system's native activation protocol, initially used by developers to activate their own mobile clients in their wap version webpages. Taobao might be using this syntax to activate the Taobao app within shopping guide apps.

Traffic Paradox: The More Desirable, the Harder to Obtain

KFC's delivery menu does not include fries, whereas McDonald's specially designed fast delivery boxes to maintain the texture of its fries. This is because KFC's meals have been modified in various ways to suit local tastes, while McDonald's represents more authentic American fast food. Fries are the lifeblood of McDonald's, so Uncle Mac is willing to do everything for them.

Compared to other e-commerce websites that control more supply chain links, traffic is the lifeblood of Taobao. Its essence is doing the traffic business, so when the traffic content is threatened, it reacts intensely like a cat whose tail has been stepped on.

Taobao's argument is based on the value of traffic within its closed system. Many merchants have already paid too high a cost for traffic within the closed ecosystem created by Taobao, gradually becoming overburdened and craving external sources. However, based on its strategic considerations, Taobao must cut off or reasonably control all of this. In the first half of the year, Taobao's strategic investment in Sina Weibo and its re-initiation of cooperation with Baidu clearly demonstrated its thirst for external traffic.

On August 5th, Sina Weibo announced account interoperability with Alibaba, allowing users to shop on Taobao using their Sina Weibo accounts and post on Sina Weibo using their Taobao accounts. After investing $586 million in Sina Weibo, turning it into a showcase and an inexhaustible source of traffic, Alibaba has taken another step closer to "building a closed traffic ecosystem." From traffic importation, to monetization, and then exportation, the entire process takes place within Alibaba's empire.

Some sellers expressed that what is hardest for merchants now is obtaining traffic, with traffic costs being driven up significantly by Taobao's bidding system. Some small sellers cannot acquire any traffic from Taobao and have to seek traffic sources outside the platform. If Taobao cuts off external traffic entirely, it will have a significant impact in the future.

According to relevant data, the number of small and medium sellers with annual sales below 100,000 yuan has increased significantly (by over 60%), but the growth of large sellers with annual sales exceeding 1 million yuan remains slow (only 33%). This clearly indicates that although there are many new entrants in Taobao, growing larger is becoming increasingly difficult. Moreover, the homepage of Taobao is no longer the only entry point; mobile and social traffic are continuously increasing in the overall store traffic. Meanwhile, Taobao is beginning to support "small but beautiful" stores, promoting shops that are small in scale but high in quality and uniqueness.

Currently, Taobao's traffic distribution resembles a pyramid, with traffic flowing naturally from the top of the pyramid downward. Only an absolute minority of small and medium sellers receive any traffic. Taobao currently has over 7 million sellers, and competition for internal advertising resources will become increasingly fierce. However, more and more small and medium sellers no longer have the money to buy expensive直通车 advertisements or pay for Juhuasuan slot fees.

The desire for traffic is becoming increasingly evident among small and medium sellers. On the Taobao forum, the announcement regarding the adjustment of the search and sales display for low-price traffic diversion received over 500 pages of replies, with more than 10,000 floors of responses.

In the process of manipulating traffic, opportunities for third-party internet companies within Alibaba's vast shadow are limited. Any enterprise that grows under Alibaba's influence faces a head-on blow from Alibaba.

What is Alibaba Afraid Of?

When Alibaba blocks third parties, has it considered: Why do users prefer to come to Taobao via other websites? What can others offer that I cannot? What makes users uncomfortable when they enter through my front door? Users search for products using search engines because they are convenient and easy to use. Is Taobao's own search and the experience of YiTao comparable to Baidu or Google? Shopping guide websites have many innovations, helping users conveniently find, discover, and explore products. It's difficult for Taobao to achieve this.

But this isn't the core issue yet. The important reason is that Taobao's entrance capability on the internet side is too weak, with most of its traffic coming from its own brand effect. The entry points for PC-based internet are desktop clients, browsers, and search engines. Although Alibaba has Wangwang and a browser, it has almost completely failed in terms of entry channels.

More frighteningly, in the mobile internet sector, Taobao has no control over entry points. Tencent has WeChat, Baidu has 91 and Maps, and even 360 has a Mobile Assistant. The blockage of WeChat and the defense against WeChat Pay through the blockage of QR codes reflect Taobao's helplessness in mobile internet and its fear in the face of WeChat, a powerful weapon.

The increasingly closed-off Taobao is making its partners increasingly uneasy.

Milisay's web traffic peaked in March 2012, after which its visits steadily declined, roughly returning to the level around September 2011. LC Style Network, facing financial difficulties, had no choice but to sell. At its most critical moment, the team was reduced to just 15 people, almost resorting to mortgaging personal properties to keep the company running. "Guan," founded by Baiya, reportedly underwent significant layoffs within just a few months of its launch, with one founder leaving.

Mushroom Street chose to transform itself, officially launching its group-buying section - Brand Group Buying, currently in testing phase, where brands can freely register. It was understood that the online exchange meeting for brand merchants held by Mushroom Street was aimed at recruiting for Brand Group Buying. Currently, brands like Crazy Monkey and Crocs have already joined.

Hu Chen expressed on his blog: "If the rule adjustment only restricts a specific website, it will cause confusion among Taobao affiliates and third-party platforms, interpreted as a dangerous signal of mutual destruction, potentially leading them to switch to other platforms."

Blocking will be Alibaba's only choice before its IPO, but how long can Taobao continue to dance alone in the future?
