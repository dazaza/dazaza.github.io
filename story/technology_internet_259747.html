The "foolish child" who learned 6 languages at the age of 8

Written by Xu Shuguang, Chairman of Green Tree Inn and 519.com Wine Direct Sales Network

More than 200 years ago, there was a village called Loschau near Halle in Germany. A rural pastor named Karl Witte believed that children must start education from infancy. If this could be achieved, ordinary children could become exceptionally intelligent. However, his first child soon passed away, and his second child appeared particularly dull during infancy. Although deeply saddened, Karl Witte did not despair and seriously followed his plan to educate young Witte. Unexpectedly, by the age of 8, young Witte had already mastered six languages: German, French, Italian, Latin, English, and Greek, growing up to become a legal scholar.

— *Harvard Educational Bedtime Reading*

Many Chinese parents now realize the importance of learning foreign languages, and quite a few also understand that the time for children to start learning foreign languages should be advanced. Around ten years ago, the beginning of secondary language teaching in compulsory education started at the age of 13 when children entered junior high school. Now, it has basically started from elementary school or even kindergarten. I believe that if conditions permit, this time can be further advanced, creating a bilingual environment for children right after they are born.

A research project led by Professor Su Tingbi of City University of Hong Kong titled "Neuroscience and Language Teaching Research Plan" proved that children can systematically learn Chinese and English by the age of four. The research results suggest that formal and systematic learning of the mother tongue can begin during the kindergarten stage, and phonics learning of a second language such as English can start from the first grade of primary school. For children in multilingual environments, it is very likely that they will start speaking two or even more languages as soon as they begin talking. My children are like this too; both my wife and I speak both Chinese and English to them without any specific boundaries. Therefore, they became familiar with bilingualism at an early age.

Some people may worry that exposing young children to different languages might cause confusion. In reality, the human brain's language function is like a wide-open door when we are young, but as we grow older, some parts close. Therefore, it is much easier for children to learn languages than adults. I know many children who have grown up in multilingual environments. Their parents generally speak more than two languages, such as Chinese and English, and the children quickly learn to speak two languages as well. As long as the teaching process is natural and progresses from simple to complex rather than rote memorization through cramming, children generally do not experience language confusion or aphasia. They might just start speaking a little later because they receive more information compared to other children.

In the era of increasingly globalized modern market economies, mastering multiple languages means taking the lead in communication. It not only helps children better understand the strengths and weaknesses of different civilizations in the future but also makes it easier for them to stand out in fierce talent competition. Although my children use English as their mother tongue, I require them to report their daily study and life situations to me in Chinese to exercise their Chinese writing skills. After several years, their progress has been very noticeable. This way, even if they come to work in China in the future, they will face few difficulties in language communication. Nowadays, many people in the United States are learning Chinese, and I tell the students of my alma mater, the Marshall School of Business at the University of Southern California, the same thing: under equal conditions, having one more language skill gives you an absolute advantage. I hope every child can master more language skills and make themselves stronger!
