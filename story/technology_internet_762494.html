How Startups Can Compete With Big Companies for Talent

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:46:43

A Former Google Exec On How Startups Can Compete With Google To Score Great Talent

Editor's note: The author of this article is Douglas Merrill, the founder and CEO of ZestFinance. He was formerly the CIO and Vice President of Engineering at Google. ZestFinance is a financial services technology company located in Los Angeles that uses big data to help make better credit decisions, providing alternatives for people without bank accounts.

For tech startups, finding good talent is undoubtedly a very headache-inducing issue. Especially when competing with large companies like Facebook and Twitter for talent.

First, let’s face reality: there is a misconception that good software engineers won’t leave tech hubs to work in remote areas. For example, in the U.S., many people believe that good software engineers won't leave the San Francisco Bay Area. If your company isn't nearby, you can't compete with other companies for talent.

Actually, it doesn't matter where your tech company is located. Finding the right talent is what matters most. With a limited budget, you can't compete with large companies on salary; that will be your weakness.

The recruitment process involves various trade-offs, including whether to use internal recruiters or seek help from headhunting firms. Based on my experience — using these two recruitment methods in both small and large companies — the best employees are often found by internal recruiters. Below are some experiences I've gained along the way:

Use Your Personal Network

Whether you're a two- or three-person company, there's really only one way to expand your core team: use your personal network. Pay more attention to friends, family, former colleagues, and even LinkedIn and Facebook contacts. This will save you valuable time and money, and I've also found that members acquired through this method usually stay longer and integrate more quickly into the team.

The challenge with this method is how to find smart people who differ from you. I know this sounds counterintuitive and difficult because some people who aren't quite like you may also be someone you don't particularly like. However, you want your team members to dare to question others' views and voice different opinions. Otherwise, team discussions will lose their meaning. You must find a balance within this great but diverse team.

Once you've exhausted your personal resources, what's the next step? Your team has expanded to a certain extent. At this stage, I recommend hiring someone internally as early as possible to focus on recruitment.

Internal Recruitment vs. Agency Recruitment

From my own young company's perspective, hiring someone to help with recruitment is more cost-effective than seeking external headhunting firms. Let's look at some relevant data:

Typically, recruitment agency fees range from 20% to 30% of the new employee's salary, which makes the cost of recruiting an employee approximately $20,000 to $30,000 per person.

An internal recruiter typically earns between $75,000 and $150,000 annually but can help you recruit dozens of engineers each year.

Although early-stage startups tend to avoid fixed costs and prefer flexibility, in this case, fixed costs are much cheaper than third-party recruitment agencies. For companies planning to hire over 20 engineers in the next year, hiring an internal recruiter can save over $500,000. This is no small amount for many startups.

Don't Restrict Your Recruiters

When searching for an internal recruiter, it's best to find someone from your personal network who has worked with you before and can be trusted. I found a colleague who had worked with me at Google and participated in internal recruitment. I had personally seen how he built our team, and we felt comfortable working together.

Once you've found an internal recruiter, provide them with necessary tools — from job tracking systems to LinkedIn's paid recruiter subscription.

Then, let them take full control. Don't forget, you hired them because you trust them and recognize their capabilities, so try not to restrict them. Let them show their full potential.

Company Culture Is Key

Third-party recruitment agencies don't work in your office, so they have no way of knowing your company culture. It's difficult to understand from the outside exactly what kind of talent you need or who fits your company culture.

In contrast, internal recruiters can focus on more than just resumes. They can better assess whether candidates will adapt to the company culture.

Recruitment Agencies Benefit More Than You

Ultimately, internal recruiters can attract more top-tier talent. But that 1% of the best candidates constantly receive messages from recruiters — receiving anywhere from 15 to 120 emails per week. Most of the time, these people won't agree to switch jobs.

Why? Because they believe if the company didn't pay the recruitment agency's fee, they should get a higher salary.

Their thinking is correct: usually, internal recruiters will be your advantage in competing with large companies. They can help you save on recruitment agency fees and assist you in offering more attractive packages within a limited budget. This will help you compete with large companies for talent. Believe me.
