Steamed Bun: A Traditional Chinese Food

Steamed bun is a traditional Chinese food made from wheat flour, water, sugar and other ingredients mixed evenly, fermented and then steamed. The finished product usually has a semi-spherical or elongated shape. In the Jiangnan region, steamed buns with fillings such as meat, vegetables, and bean paste are generally called baozi, while plain ones are called white steamed buns. They have a soft texture, rich nutrition, and are one of the indispensable staple foods on the dining table.

Introduction to Steamed Buns

What is a steamed bun? (Steamed bread)

A steamed bun is a food made by mixing flour with water, sugar, etc., fermenting it, and then steaming it until cooked. The finished product usually has a semi-spherical or elongated shape. Generally, steamed buns made with fillings such as meat, vegetables, and bean paste are called baozi, while plain ones are called white steamed buns. They have a soft texture, rich nutrition, and are one of the indispensable staple foods on the dining table.

Steamed buns come in two types: with and without filling. Plain steamed buns usually have a smooth, semi-circular surface. During festivals, they often have a red seal on top. In the Jiangnan region, filled steamed buns mainly have red bean paste or pork fillings. Baozi have a semi-circular shape with pleated tops and no red seal, and can have various fillings like meat, red bean paste, oil greens, cabbage, vermicelli, radish shreds, pumpkin, and chives eggs.

Ingredients needed to make steamed buns: flour, fermented dough, (white sugar), water, baking soda, (green and red threads).

Origin of the Name

Legend has it that during the Three Kingdoms period, Zhuge Liang led his troops to attack the southern barbarians, capturing Meng Huo seven times before finally making him surrender. On their way back, they had to cross the Lu River.

When the army was preparing to cross, suddenly strong winds and waves arose, preventing them from crossing. Zhuge Liang asked Meng Huo for the reason. It turned out that fallen soldiers from both sides couldn't return home to reunite with their families, so they were causing storms on the river to block the army's return. To cross the river, they would need to sacrifice 49 human heads to appease the spirits.

Zhuge Liang thought: war inevitably causes casualties, but how could he sacrifice another 49 lives? He came up with a plan, ordering the cooks to make dumplings using flour as the skin and beef and mutton as the filling, shaping them into 49 human heads. Then he set up an altar, sprinkled wine, and offered sacrifices to the river.

Since then, "steamed bun" has become common in folk culture, and Zhuge Liang has been revered as the ancestor of dough modeling. Ming dynasty scholar Lang Ying recorded in his notes "Seven Revisions": "Steamed buns were originally called 'man tou'. In the barbarian lands, human heads were used to sacrifice to the gods. When Zhuge Liang campaigned against Meng Huo, he ordered the use of flour wrappers and meat to make human head shapes for sacrifice, calling them 'man tou'. Nowadays, the name has been corrupted to 'mantou'."

The steamed buns created by Zhuge Liang originally contained beef and mutton fillings, requiring complex procedures and higher costs. Later generations simplified the process by removing the filling, creating what we now know as mantou. Those with filling became baozi.

Making Method

1. Mix fermented dough (old dough) with flour and water to form a dough, place in a bowl or proofing box to ferment (fermentation time depends on room temperature and amount of old dough);

2. Add baking soda (homemade mantou generally doesn't add soda as it affects the flavor) and white sugar (adjust according to taste preference, northern regions usually don't add sugar except during festivals), knead thoroughly, roll into long strips, divide into portions, place on steamer trays with portions facing up, sprinkle with green and red threads, steam for twenty minutes over high heat.

Note:

1. Ferment the dough sufficiently, ensure portions face upwards (northern regions generally don't reshape portions, which should face downwards; plain white mantou is commonly eaten without additional ingredients except during festivals when jujubes or peanuts may be added);

2. Be careful not to touch portions after placing on steamer trays, otherwise they won't "bloom";

3. Steam over high heat once water boils.

Features: White color, flower-like shape, sweet and fragrant taste.

Use 35-37 degree Celsius warm water to dissolve yeast, mix this water with flour until it reaches an appropriate consistency, knead until smooth, cover with a damp cloth and let rest for fermentation. In current weather conditions, two hours should be sufficient for fermentation. Then place the dough on a cutting board, add dry flour and knead again, let rest for a while. At this point, you can choose to add bean paste, meat and vegetables or other ingredients to make various types of buns, baozi, flower rolls, sugar triangles depending on your preference. After shaping, let rest for about half an hour. Pay special attention during the steaming process - use medium heat and cold water in the pot. Do not rush this step. Using boiling water and high heat is only suitable for dough fermented with sourdough.

Small Tips for Steaming Mantou

(1) If the dough hasn't fully risen, make a small indentation in the center, pour in two small cups of liquor, wait ten minutes and the dough will rise.

(2) If you don't have yeast, honey can be used as a substitute. For every 500 grams of flour, add 15-20 grams of honey. Knead the dough until soft, cover with a damp cloth for 4-6 hours and it will rise. Honey-fermented dough produces soft, fragrant mantou with a slightly sweet aftertaste.

(3) In winter, indoor temperatures are low, so adding a bit of sugar can speed up fermentation.

(4) When adding baking soda to neutralize acidity, check if the amount is appropriate by cutting off a small piece of dough. If there are sesame-sized uniform holes, the amount of soda is suitable.

(5) If mantou turns yellow due to excessive soda and smells strongly of soda, add 100-160 grams of vinegar to the water used for steaming, re-steaming the mantou for 10-15 minutes will restore its whiteness and remove the soda taste.

(6) Adding a little salt water to the flour can promote fermentation, resulting in soft and fluffy mantou.

Precautions

Do Not Use Hot Water for Steaming Mantou

Many people prefer to use hot or boiling water to steam mantou, thinking it heats up faster. However, this isn't scientific. Cold mantou suddenly exposed to hot steam can cause uneven cooking.

The correct method is to start with cold water in the pot, place the mantou inside, then gradually heat it up. This allows the mantou to be evenly heated, resulting in soft and delicious mantou.

Water Temperature for Fermentation

Dissolve yeast in warm water with temperature not exceeding forty degrees Celsius.

Sour Taste of Steamed Buns

If the fermented dough has a sour taste, you can add a bit of baking soda to neutralize it. Usually, unless overly fermented, it's fine without adding anything.

During Steaming Process

Do not open the lid during steaming.

Mantou Collapse

After steaming, do not immediately open the lid. Let it sit for ten minutes before opening to prevent collapse.

Judging Doneness

There are several ways to judge if mantou is fully cooked:

(1) Gently tap the mantou, if it bounces back, it's done;

(2) Tear off a piece of the mantou's skin, if it peels off easily, it's done, otherwise not;

(3) Gently press the mantou with your finger, if the indentation quickly rebounds, it's done. If it remains indented, it's not fully cooked.

Hamburger-style Mantou

Ingredients: Several mantou, ham, lettuce, tomatoes, fried eggs, chili sauce or noodle sauce.

Instructions:

1. Slice the ham and tomatoes, wash and dry the lettuce with purified water.

2. Cut the mantou in half. Spread sauce on one side, place a slice of ham, tomato, and lettuce. Don't forget the most important ingredient - the egg!

3. Place the other half on top.

Small Steamed Buns

Ingredients: 5 kg flour, 500 g minced meat, 15 g salt, 25 g soy sauce, 35 g sugar, 5 g sesame oil, cooking wine, green onions, monosodium glutamate as needed.

Instructions:

1. Make Filling: Remove skin from meat, wash and mince. First add seasonings, then add 300 g water and stir in one direction until the meat absorbs the water and swells. Finally, add sesame oil and mix.

2. Make Dough: For every kilogram of flour, add 4 kilograms of water, mix well and knead until the dough is smooth and elastic.

3. Wrap Filling: Rub oil on the work surface. Place the dough on the surface, roll into a long strip, divide each kilogram of dough into 120 pieces, press into circular wrappers with thicker centers and thinner edges, wrap the filling and shape into baozi.

4. Steam: Place individually in the steamer, steam over high heat and boiling water for about 8 minutes until the baozi turn jade white and the bottom doesn't stick to the hand.

Fried Mantou Slices

Ingredients: One mantou, one egg, a little salt, a little oil.

Instructions: Beat the egg and add a little salt, stir well. Slice the mantou thinly, soak in the egg mixture for about ten seconds, coat evenly with egg batter, and fry in a pan until golden brown on both sides.

Tips: You can choose to add oil or not in the frying pan. Serve with a glass of milk and a plate of cucumber slices for a perfect breakfast.

Sugar Mantou

Ingredients: 25 kg premium flour, 3 kg white sugar, 1 kg yeast dough, about 7 kg water, 600 g sodium carbonate, 500 g peanut oil.

Instructions:

1. Make Fermented Dough: Place white sugar and oil in the mixer, add 3 kg of hot water around 70°C and stir to mix sugar and oil. Then add the yeast dough cut into small pieces and 24 kg of premium flour (reserve 1 kg of flour for dusting during operation). Stir slightly first, then gradually add 4 kg of hot water between 50-60°C, mix thoroughly. Do not add all water at once. Generally, use less water when temperature is high and more when temperature is low. Control water temperature appropriately. Too hot water can damage gluten and reduce elasticity; too cold water prolongs fermentation time and results in insufficient fermentation, leading to less full-bodied final product. Cool the mixed fermented dough slightly, then maintain the dough temperature around 25°C (usually use cotton fabric for insulation), ferment for about 10 hours. When the fermented dough expands to twice its original volume, has air bubbles in the cross-section and a sour smell, it's ready.

2. Stir in Alkali: Dissolve alkali in 250 g of water before stirring, then gradually add to the fermented dough and mix thoroughly in the mixer. The amount of alkali solution added depends on the fermentation degree of the dough and the temperature. More alkali solution should be added when the dough is well-fermented and the temperature is high; less when the fermentation is poor and the temperature is low. Generally, the cross-section of the dough after stirring in alkali should have uniform round holes, and sound crisp when tapped. Alternatively, take a small sample of the dough after stirring in alkali, bake it first to check the amount of alkali solution, and adjust accordingly based on the situation.

3. Shape: Weigh 70 g of dough, press a smooth surface on the operating table, use your right hand to fold the remaining part into the smooth surface, rotate the dough with your left hand continuously, shape into a round ball, set aside for later use.

4. Steam: Place the shaped dough balls smoothly facing up in the steamer. Brush oil on the steamer cloth to prevent sticking. When steaming, use low steam first for about 5 minutes, then high steam. The steam pressure should not be lower than 3 kg/cm², and the time should be 10 minutes.

Quality Standards: Shape: Round, full and intact. Color: White.

Texture: Soft and free of impurities.

Taste: Soft and sweet with moderate sweetness.

Jujube Flower Mantou

Ingredients: Fermented dough, jujubes.

Instructions:

1. Make fermented dough.

2. Boil jujubes: Wash and boil jujubes, skim off any foam, cool down, drain water and set aside.

3. Divide the dough, roll into finger-thick long strips.

4. Take one jujube, wrap it with dough in a circle, pinch off and complete the first jujube dough ring. Dust with flour to prevent sticking.

5. Similarly, make another jujube dough ring, attach it on top of the first one. Continue to surround the first one with a circle. Depending on the situation, attach 5~7.

6. Directly attach the second layer on top of the first, with 5~3 pieces. Use just one jujube dough ring for the third layer.

7. Cover with cling film and let rise for 15 minutes.

8. Add water to the pot, lay wet steamer cloth, bring to a boil, then place inside. Steam for 20 minutes.

Small Tips:

1. Number of jujube mantou per layer: Generally decreases from bottom to top as 7, 5, 3 or 6, 3, 1. Increase number or layers for larger sizes.

2. Suggest boiling jujubes first to remove bitterness before using.

Spicy Meat Sauce Mantou

Ingredients: 1/2 pound lean meat, 1 tablespoon chopped bird's eye chili, 1 tablespoon doubanjiang, 2 tablespoons sugar, 6 steamed buns (ready-made)

Seasoning: 3 tablespoons soy sauce, 1 teaspoon salt, 1 tablespoon ginger juice wine, oil as needed

Instructions: Finely chop the lean meat and mix with chopped bird's eye chili, doubanjiang and sugar. Heat oil in a pan and stir-fry the mixture over high heat, then add seasoning and continue to stir-fry for about 5 minutes. Steam the mantou, slice open and fill with meat sauce to eat.

Tricks for Eating Stale Mantou

Stale mantou can become dry and hard, making it difficult to eat and wasteful to discard.

Here are some clever ways to enjoy stale mantou:

1. Slice the stale mantou, dip in salt water, then deep-fry in hot oil to create crispy snacks.

2. Grind the stale mantou into crumbs, add seasonings like salt, monosodium glutamate, soy sauce, green onions, ginger, garlic powder or five-spice powder according to taste, mix with water, shape into balls and either steam or deep-fry.

Other Names

The terminology for mantou remains confusing today. In the north, unfilled mantou are sometimes called "mo", "juan zi", or even "baozi". In the south, filled mantou are also called "mian dou zi" or "tang bao". Nowadays, regardless of whether they're filled or unfilled, all types of mantou have strayed far from the original mantou created by Zhuge Liang. The character "mantou" originally used the character "man" with a different radical.

Alternative Explanations

According to Tang Dynasty records, mantou may have originated earlier than Zhuge Liang's time. Some suggest it was already a Sichuan dish before Zhuge Liang's campaigns. Though legend attributes its creation to Zhuge Liang replacing human sacrifices with dough figures during his southern campaigns, this explanation seems questionable given Zhuge Liang's emphasis on psychological warfare and strategic thinking.

Modern Usage

In addition to its culinary meaning, "mantou" has taken on new meanings in contemporary culture. Fans of the magazine Zhiyin Manke call themselves "mantou". According to satirical regulations in the People's Republic of China Mantou Law (Second Revision 2009), mantou refers not to the food item but to enthusiasts and followers of the magazine and its serialized works.
