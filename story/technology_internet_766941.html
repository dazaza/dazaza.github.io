Wang Jian: The Outsider of Alibaba

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:40:17

Yesterday, an internal email from Alibaba Group revealed that the group's CTO and President of AliCloud, Wang Jian, would no longer serve as President of AliCloud and will focus solely on his role as CTO. The position of President of AliCloud will be taken over by Jiang Peng, Deputy CTO of Alibaba Group.

In 2008, Wang Jian left Microsoft to join Alibaba, gaining the appreciation of Jack Ma and leading the development of AliCloud OS despite objections. However, it wasn't until September 2012, when Google halted a joint phone launch event between Alibaba and Acer, that people began to take notice of Wang Jian. At that time, his title was Chief Technology Officer of Alibaba Group and President of AliCloud, a position he had held for only 17 days.

After Jack Ma, who originally discovered Wang Jian, stepped down as CEO, Wang Jian's position at Alibaba became quite awkward. He was not one of the "Eighteen Arhats" (founding members) of Alibaba. All that was left for Wang Jian was the AliCloud division, while the Cloud OS division was placed under the control of current CEO, Eric Jing.

Now, with Wang Jian focusing solely on his role as CTO, perhaps he can infuse some engineering culture into Alibaba's existing corporate culture.

French writer Albert Camus once created a classic literary figure - the outsider, but such stories actually continue to play out in real life.

If Alibaba had established the position of "Chief Smell Officer" earlier, perhaps we wouldn't be seeing Wang Jian step down as President of AliCloud today.

In fact, Wang Jian’s joining of Alibaba was closely related to Jack Ma. Starting from 2007, Alibaba hosted several "Net Hero Conferences," which Ma paid great attention to and attended personally to deliver speeches. This platform was seen as an important entry point for Alibaba to attract technical talent, and Wang Jian was one of those talents.

In the press release announcing Wang Jian's official joining of Alibaba, there was this description: “Dr. Wang Jian will help Alibaba Group build a world-class technical team.” This was also Jack Ma's expectation for Wang Jian, who then gained the nickname "Doctor" within Alibaba, which was quite different from the martial arts-themed nicknames commonly used within the company.

In September 2012, due to Google's influence, the joint phone launch event between Alibaba and Acer was canceled at the last minute, drawing people's attention to Wang Jian, the main project leader on Alibaba's side. At that time, his title was Chief Technology Officer of Alibaba Group and President of AliCloud, a position he had held for only 17 days. Around the same time, a major discussion about Wang Jian's background erupted on Zhihu.

However, there isn't much actual content available about Wang Jian's past. Records show that Wang Jian, born in 1962, graduated from Zhejiang University's Psychology Department with a doctoral degree. He has published numerous academic papers and authored the first domestic monograph in the field, "Human-Computer Interaction and Multi-Channel User Interface."

In 1999, at Li Kaifu's invitation, Wang Jian joined Microsoft, serving as the Principal Researcher of the Multi-Channel User Interface Group. In its early stages, he was the sole employee of this project, later rising to become Deputy Director. During this period, Wang Jian's primary achievement was inventing digital ink, which was later applied to Microsoft Tablet PC but did not make a significant market impact.

Afterward came the rumors of 2008, with Wang Jian and former Yahoo VP Qi Lu being the main characters. The news at the time claimed they would both join Alibaba. However, Qi Lu joined Microsoft, while Wang Jian entered Alibaba, serving as Chief Architect and reporting directly to Jack Ma. Nevertheless, those familiar with Wang Jian knew that "Doctor" always remained somewhat outside of Alibaba's inner circle.

Many people say they can't understand what Wang Jian is talking about, which became the biggest question mark regarding his capabilities, both externally and even among some Alibaba employees.

There are some jokes circulating online: First, when Alibaba initially started working on Cloud OS, apart from Wang Jian, there weren’t many experts in cloud computing within the company, so many of Wang Jian's ideas couldn't be smoothly communicated or implemented; Second, when Koubei was merged into AliCloud, Wang Jian held two meetings with all staff. However, by the time he had finished half of his speech, most of the audience was already asleep.

As a technologist with an engineering culture background, Wang Jian often appeared somewhat inadequate in management roles. When explaining many concepts and visions, he tended to be very cautious. A journalist friend once replied on Zhihu saying that conversations with Wang Jian felt like he repeated himself a lot, and it took effort to organize the entire conversation.

In an interview about cloud computing, the first question the journalist asked Wang Jian was "What is your definition of cloud computing?" Despite being responsible for Alibaba's cloud computing business, Wang Jian did not directly provide an answer but instead gave responses to a few other questions before finally sharing his understanding.

This kind of scene occurred repeatedly during that interview. However, Wang Jian is not someone without ability; his ideas are actually quite forward-thinking.

The first time I met Wang Jian was at Alibaba's Cloud Computing Developer Conference, which could be considered Wang Jian's home turf. Standing confidently on stage, he explained every detail and possible future of Alibaba's cloud computing vision. In subsequent interviews, Wang Jian was always someone who could talk at length once he got started, offering sharp insights into the prospects and applications of various technologies, sometimes even sketching diagrams on a small board behind him.

For example, from the initial design of AliCloud OS, it was evident that Wang Jian had great foresight in his specialized field.

In fact, the early version of AliCloud OS did not support Android applications; it was a completely cloud-based operating system, or somewhat similar to Web OS. However, due to an incomplete ecosystem and insufficient domestic network infrastructure to support a good user experience, especially under the strong request of Tongyu President Rong Xiuli, the AliCloud OS team had to modify the original operating system to achieve compatibility with Android. This was Wang Jian's compromise with the market, and he once lamented how difficult it was for engineers.

I remember Wang Jian once telling the media, "In the year since launching AliCloud OS, I've been criticized more than ever in my life." However, Jack Ma's support still gave Wang Jian courage. Ma once said to Wang Jian, "If it's a child, it should be born. Maybe it's ugly at birth, but beauty needs to be cultivated."

Wang Jian also supported his products through action. In Hangzhou, Wang Jian's office walls were covered with product design sketches, and his phone was consistently the unreleased AliCloud OS phone co-developed with Acer. When asked about this again, he showed a hint of bitterness.

Although Alibaba merely responded that this was an internal adjustment, the external world still held Wang Jian accountable for the Google halt incident. As a result, the story of organizational restructuring took on many versions. After Alibaba split into 25 divisions at the beginning of the year, all that was left for Wang Jian was the AliCloud division, while the Cloud OS division was placed under the control of current CEO, Eric Jing.

After the Hangzhou interview, I saw Wang Jian again on July 23rd of this year. That day, Alibaba Group officially launched the Ali Smart TV operating system, seen as another product of AliCloud OS. However, sitting next to Eric Jing, Wang Jian didn't have the same broad smile as Jing. He neither gave a speech nor posed for photos with guests, quietly coming and leaving.

If it weren't for the nameplate on the seat, I might not have even known he was attending. Remembering when I raised my camera lens toward him, he deliberately avoided it for the first time. Later, after exchanging a few words, Wang Jian lacked the naturalness and sincerity he usually displayed during interviews, speaking just a few words before closing his mouth.

After the structural adjustment, Wang Jian rarely appeared in media reports. Therefore, after Jack Ma, who originally discovered Wang Jian, stepped down as CEO, Wang Jian's position at Alibaba became quite awkward. He was not one of the "Eighteen Arhats," he didn't have a flower name, and he was always considered someone carrying a burden, making Wang Jian an outsider in Alibaba's corporate culture that emphasizes cultural identity.

Upon hearing the news of Wang Jian stepping down as President of AliCloud, I really don't know whether I should be happy for him. I remember many years ago, he told the media, "A person should feel satisfied with what they do every day, rather than the opposite." Hopefully, in his new role as CTO, he can add some engineering culture to Alibaba's existing corporate culture.
