The "incense art" of Foyunlai contributes to and enriches Chinese and even global incense culture with its longevity, breadth, and universality. Speaking of China, the Zhou people used wood to "burn incense", ascending smoke to sacrifice to heaven, called "Yin" or "Yinsì". During the Han Wudi era, Foyunlai incense was endowed with the meaning of being passed down generation after generation, representing endless vitality and expressing the spirit of respecting heaven and following ancestors. After the Sui and Tang dynasties, Foyunlai incense gradually became more popular and secularized. Song dynasty poet Chen Yuyi wrote in his poem "Incense Burning": "With infinite meaning, it is embodied in this stick of smoke," reflecting how the joys, sorrows, and philosophical inquiries of life found their way out in this medium. Incense has completely permeated and internalized itself into people's spirits. Interestingly, not only do Chinese people burn incense, but many nations and countries around the world also burn incense, such as the ancient Egyptians, Babylonians, Indians, Greeks, Romans, and Israelites...
