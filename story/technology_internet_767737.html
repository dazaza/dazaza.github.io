Windows integration with cloud storage services may render hard drives obsolete

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 13:18:12

Windows 8.1 integrates Microsoft's online storage service, SkyDrive. Previously, SkyDrive was just a cloud storage solution, but now it has evolved into an automatic online backup service that allows users to transfer their Windows settings from one PC to another.

As cloud storage increasingly replaces local storage systems, the demand for hard drives will decline, putting pressure on Seagate, one of the world's largest hard drive manufacturers.

One of the best features in Windows 8.1 is the integration of SkyDrive. Similar to Apple's iCloud, SkyDrive is an online backup service that stores local files and settings on Microsoft's servers.

In the past, transferring files from one PC to another required physical media, such as a USB drive. The emergence of cloud storage services like DropBox and Google Drive reduced the need for physical media, and the integration of SkyDrive into Windows 8.1 takes this a step further. Once integrated into Windows 8.1, SkyDrive users no longer have to manually upload files to the cloud storage service—Microsoft handles this automatically. When users log into different PCs with their Microsoft account, the necessary files are automatically downloaded onto the PC.

Previously, users had to keep copies of the necessary files on each PC, which consumed a large amount of storage space. The integration of SkyDrive into Windows will make this practice obsolete. According to Microsoft, SkyDrive can reduce the amount of storage space users need by about 80%.

This clearly poses a problem for companies like Seagate that rely on hard drive sales. With traditional PCs being replaced by smartphones and tablets, Seagate's price-to-earnings ratio has dropped to single digits.

Most mobile devices do not come equipped with hard drives, and due to consumers and businesses purchasing tablets instead of traditional PCs, Seagate's revenue has declined, falling by 24% year-over-year in the third quarter. Worse still, hard drives may be phased out even in traditional PCs. Microsoft's Surface Pro uses a solid-state drive rather than a traditional hard drive, and many of Samsung's ultrabooks also feature solid-state drives.

Industry insiders point out that Samsung's decision to sell its hard drive business reflects this trend. One of Samsung's greatest strengths is vertical integration: Samsung produces a variety of components, including displays, chips, and memory, giving it a significant advantage over competitors—one of the main reasons Nokia stayed away from Android.

Samsung used to produce hard drives, but two years ago it sold its hard drive business to Seagate. The primary reason for selling the hard drive business was that Samsung believed future hard drive usage would decrease; otherwise, it would have kept the business.

Fairly speaking, this trend is not entirely bad news for Seagate. SkyDrive files are stored on servers, and these servers are likely to use hard drives.

The shift towards cloud storage in storage technology has been ongoing for several years, and the integration of SkyDrive into Windows will accelerate this trend. An increasing number of PC users will store their files on cloud storage services rather than on local hard drives.
