Nokia's Farewell Performance: Microsoft is Happy, OEM Vendors are Crying

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 13:22:15

Today, Elop completed his first public appearance since Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia, but it could also be his last as CEO of Nokia. At this pivotal point of change and transition, he should be satisfied that he has successfully completed the first task given by Microsoft. At the same time, he has drawn a conclusion for Nokia, albeit not a perfect one.

The key products unveiled today fall into three categories: Asha series entry-level phones, the much-anticipated 6-inch 1080P HD screen phone running on the WP system, and Nokia's first tablet. However, we believe that only Asha retains some essence of Nokia, while the other two products are heavily imbued with "Microsoft-made" characteristics.

For the Lumia 2520 tablet product, there are many intriguing aspects worth noting.

Firstly, in terms of naming, Microsoft and Nokia did not create a separate naming convention for the new tablet series but instead continued to use Lumia. According to the acquisition terms, this trademark has been transferred to Microsoft, which intends to unify its mobile portable devices under a highly recognizable brand. They also hope that Windows 8.1 and the WP system can smoothly interconnect between the two major platforms of smartphones and tablets. Therefore, the name of the first tablet product feels familiar and intimate.

However, you will soon discover that the Lumia 2520 lacks some sharpness, and most of its configurations form a subtle "complementary advantage" relationship with the Surface 2 released not long ago. For instance, the processors belong to Qualcomm Snapdragon 800 and Nvidia Tegra 4 platforms, making it difficult to quickly determine their superiority or inferiority under different platforms. Furthermore, the main camera of the Lumia 2520 is a 6.7 million pixel lens certified by Carl Zeiss, while the Surface 2 has a 5 million pixel camera, which looks about the same. In addition, including the screen, system, RAM, ROM, and other key components, almost all configurations are similar, even adopting the same form factor with an external keyboard. Placing them side by side makes it hard to distinguish their appearances at a glance. This cannot help but be seen as a kind of tacit understanding and compromise between Nokia and "godfather" Microsoft. Is this the posture of Nokia's first tablet computer? A kneeling posture completely "subservient" to Microsoft's tablet product line.

Of course, how to enrich the product line without harming each other while effectively combating the iPad is the primary challenge facing both parties. Unfortunately, what we see is Microsoft's solution approach - a continuation of the "minor modification style" of the Surface 2, which does not directly threaten the iPad family.

Therefore, it can be said that today's release of the Lumia 2520 was already within Microsoft's control. Although Microsoft views it as a routine success in market segmentation deployment, just like Elop's "routine" return to Microsoft, it must be admitted that Microsoft's layout capability for the tablet computer market is still weak.

Market research company Gartner stated on Monday this week that tablet products have consistently maintained strong demand. The total shipments of traditional PCs, tablets, and smartphones this year will reach 2.32 billion units, an increase of 4.5% compared to 2012. It predicts that tablet shipments will be 184 million units, an increase of 42.7% compared to last year.

Gartner specifically pointed out that the most fiercely price-competed product area is the 7-inch tablet. Gartner research vice president Carolina Milanesi said: "We expect that the peak season at the end of this year will be dominated by small tablets, as even the smartphone products that sell well every year pale in comparison to small tablets."

Although iPad sales figures declined slightly in the last quarter, Apple has successfully segmented the market into the 7-8 inch small size category. Therefore, the iPad mini 2 (name undecided) to be released tomorrow morning has garnered significant attention. For the Microsoft and Nokia camp, there is now an urgent need for a small-sized Windows system product to compete with iOS and Android.

From another perspective, although the Lumia 2520 follows Microsoft's "godfather" product route and successfully joins the Windows 8.1 RT version family, it lacks its own distinctiveness. If users were to ask: "Please give me a reason to buy the Lumia 2520 instead of the Surface 2," Elop's response would likely be awkward.

No problem. For Microsoft and Elop, the upcoming first WP8 system 6-inch 1080P full HD large-screen phone should thoroughly bring glory and eliminate any awkwardness, because neither Microsoft nor OEM manufacturers have launched similar products. Elop wears the "first" crown proudly.

Yes, Microsoft's own-brand phones have not been active for a long time, and the 6-inch 1080P phone on the WP8 system is also a significant gap in the market. With Android manufacturers aggressively entering this ultra-large size domain this year, both Microsoft and Elop are undoubtedly anxious.

In terms of hardware configuration, except for the brick-like weight of 209g (we believe Nokia has worked hard to solve this issue, but...), the Lumia 1520 should be impeccable, fully meeting the standards of a flagship model. Even its expected price of $749 is very "flagship."

But in doing so, while Elop enjoys the limelight, has Microsoft considered its relationship with other OEM manufacturers?

It is well known that Microsoft's WP system has always placed numerous restrictions on hardware itself, whether it be processor cores, speed, screen size, resolution, or even minimal differences in wallpapers and backgrounds. For a long time, it has been difficult for different WP phones to highlight their individuality and differences. When the crucial difference in screen and resolution is broken, only Microsoft's "main wife" Nokia gets to enjoy the benefits, leaving no opportunity for other "concubines" to even "drink the soup."

Why say this?

Currently, within the WP system camp, Nokia holds more than 80% of the market share, while other manufacturers have taken a wait-and-see attitude after launching their first batch of WP phones, resulting in few products under their respective brands. This is extremely detrimental to the construction of the entire ecosystem. In the smartphone market, WP systems account for only a pitiful 4%, and Microsoft's goal is to increase this number to 15%. Relying solely on Nokia is difficult to sustain, yet Microsoft frequently gives Nokia the opportunities for "firsts" and "debut performances," inevitably causing other OEM manufacturers to feel disheartened and distance themselves.

A corresponding example is Google, which after acquiring Motorola, intentionally reduced the attention and investment in its products but continued to support the Nexus series and other manufacturers' products. This shows that Google has a broader view regarding the construction of the ecosystem.

Therefore, today's press conference is qualified for Elop - he showcased the "imperial sword" bestowed by Microsoft; it is also successful for Microsoft - it boasted to the world and competitors about the strength of its "arsenal." However, from the perspective of long-term ecosystem construction, potential problems may have already been sown.
