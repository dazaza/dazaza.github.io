Behind-the-scenes production:

The latest movie "The Adventures of Tintin" was jointly created by Steven Spielberg and Peter Jackson, making it one of the most anticipated blockbusters of 2011. Compared to the comics, the main character Tintin, played by Jamie Bell, has not changed much in appearance, still wearing his iconic blue sweater and brown pants, but now with black socks instead of white ones.

The film was made using "motion capture + CG animation," and Spielberg introduced that the reason they didn't use live-action was to keep Tintin as close as possible to the original comic's unique image. Motion capture not only maintains Tintin's original look but also creates a highly realistic effect when combined with the latest animation technology. Before filming "The Secret of the Unicorn," Peter Jackson had Weta Workshop produce a 20-minute test segment where Tintin retained the original design but looked less cartoonish with incredibly detailed realism in hair strands, skin, and clothing fibers. This test convinced Spielberg to proceed with filming Tintin.

Throughout the post-production phase, Spielberg maintained constant communication with Peter Jackson at Weta Workshop in New Zealand to supervise the entire animation process. Weta Workshop developed a video conferencing system so Spielberg could see identical footage from Los Angeles. Given the $120 million budget, this collaboration was crucial. Jackson explained, "In traditional performances, actors imagine themselves as Tintin or Captain Haddock, trying to get close to the characters. However, they can never fully transform into them. Through CGI, we can recreate the world of the comics, making everything look like bringing Hergé's original illustrations to life."

Jackson also revealed some possibilities for sequels: "My favorite story is 'The Seven Crystal Balls.' The Eastern European and Balkan Peninsula parts are also great. I think the moon adventure chapters would be suitable for the third or fourth movie. If the story continues smoothly, we might consider letting Tintin venture further away from the 'Earth surface' and do something more daring."

From Indiana Jones to Tintin:

In the '80s, the first "Raiders of the Lost Ark" sparked a global "treasure hunt" craze. However, the French remained confident in their cultural superiority and compared Tintin with Indiana Jones in reviews, which inadvertently became the key for Steven Spielberg to embark on "The Adventures of Tintin."

Spielberg recalled: "In 1981, I read a review that likened my 'Raiders of the Lost Ark' to a tribute to 'The Adventures of Tintin,' mentioning that Indiana Jones was inspired by Tintin. But before that, I had never seen Hergé's comics and didn't even know there was a character named Tintin. Out of curiosity, I immediately asked my assistant to buy a few Tintin comics. I remember one should have been 'The Seven Crystal Balls.' Even though I don't know French, I understood the entire plot, character personalities, and humor through the pictures, expressions, and actions. Hergé's work is truly remarkable."

Inspired, Spielberg approached his producer partner Kathy Kennedy to inquire how to bring this fascinating story to the big screen. She suggested meeting Hergé. Unfortunately, Hergé passed away just weeks before their meeting. Fortunately, Spielberg's talent, understanding of the story, and sincere enthusiasm impressed Hergé's widow Fanny, allowing him to secure the film rights in 1984.

Perhaps good things are worth waiting for, and soon after, the project was shelved due to technical issues. Spielberg was unclear about what kind of Tintin movie he could make with existing technology, and it's said that Hergé himself wasn't particularly interested in either live-action or animated films. Clearly, performance capture technology did not exist at the time, leaving Spielberg hesitant and contemplative about the gap between live-action and animation.

Peter Jackson brings Tintin back to life:

In 1989, when Spielberg thought it was time to bring "Tintin" to the cinema, script issues arose. He reportedly disliked that version of the story and decided to put Tintin aside to focus on "Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade." Consequently, the remake rights returned to the Hergé Foundation. Thanks to filmmakers' relentless exploration, filmmaking technology rapidly advanced, from models to CGI, from computer-generated imagery to motion capture. Finally, Spielberg prepared to be a trailblazer again—buying back the rights to "The Adventures of Tintin" in 2002, with Peter Jackson being the secret weapon behind the scenes. At that time, PJ had just completed the legendary "Lord of the Rings" trilogy and hadn't yet planned his next project when Spielberg eagerly pulled him in.

It's clear that Spielberg made a good deal; with Peter Jackson's full support, his biggest technical concerns were resolved. Why would the Oscar-winning director, whose box office prowess rivals the older generation, willingly work for Spielberg? As the saying goes, both parties were willing participants, and the story goes back to the beginning.

Peter Jackson became a superfan of "The Adventures of Tintin" since childhood and eagerly awaited its big-screen debut upon learning Spielberg had secured the rights. But the wait was long—until he won an Oscar, Spielberg's movie barely showed signs of progress, prompting his personal desire to direct it. Another reason was working with his idol. Back then, seeing the stunning special effects in "Jurassic Park" inspired him to pursue "technological filmmaking" and establish WETA Digital. Now, talking about their collaborative process, Spielberg expressed deep gratitude: "Peter and I are like two different types of scientists. There's no competition or boasting between us, but rather a united front to create a Tintin movie that everyone loves."

Moving from Universal to Paramount:

When Steven Spielberg + Peter Jackson seemed destined to sweep global box offices, the financially powerful Universal Pictures hesitated. Ultimately, this small hiccup just before production resulted in Paramount acquiring the film rights.

Initially budgeted at $130 million, the total cost was shared by Universal Pictures and Spielberg's DreamWorks, which was then under Paramount Pictures. Following Spielberg's usual practice, he would receive 30% of the box office revenue. Considering factors like the 2008 global financial crisis, Universal decided to withdraw at the last moment. More dramatically, two months later, DreamWorks and Paramount ended their three-year partnership, officially splitting up. To ensure the film could start shooting in October as planned, Spielberg dismissed the idea of taking Tintin elsewhere, forcing Paramount to reluctantly take on the entire investment.

Ultimately, the release date was delayed, and Thomas Brodie-Sangster, originally cast as Tintin, couldn't continue due to scheduling conflicts. However, this delay brought in another financier—Sony Pictures' Columbia Pictures, which decided to co-produce and distribute the first two installments of "The Adventures of Tintin" with Paramount.

Animation or Live Action:

Since the goal was to create a Tintin story as compelling as the original comics, the question arose: Should it be animated or live-action? This issue seemed to circle back to the starting point of 1983, when Spielberg had already pondered the film's form. Years later, the director hesitated again, asking himself: "Should I make a live-action movie with an animated dog, or a pure animated film?" This was a tough choice. Fans naturally hoped for a well-made live-action movie, while comic enthusiasts might prefer a faithful adaptation capturing the essence of Hergé's works.

Undecided, Spielberg consulted his partner Peter Jackson, hoping Jackson could create Snowy to see what WETA Digital's dog would look like. Since the master came seeking advice, PJ couldn't neglect it, so he personally dressed up as Captain Haddock from "The Adventures of Tintin" and performed opposite the digital Snowy. In that video, Captain Haddock stood on the dock, with Snowy running back and forth, barking to get the photographer's attention. Seeing no hope, Snowy reluctantly took a sip of Captain Haddock's drink, got drunk, and fell into the sea.

This video convinced Spielberg of Jackson's passion and WETA Studio's capabilities. Eventually, he chose the then-popular motion capture technology to shoot a non-live-action, non-animated movie. "Choosing this technology feels like finding the right partner; it seems as if the motion capture technology was begging me to use it. We want to express our respect for Hergé, and from the current situation, motion capture is the best method and path."

Peter Jackson, who achieved success with "The Lord of the Rings" and "King Kong," was equally confident that WETA could make "Tintin" a top-tier motion capture movie. Jackson said: "Although the characters have cartoon faces, their hair, skin, expressions, freckles, and sweat droplets can rival those in live-action movies. Neither Steven nor I are adept at computer operations; I even dread simple tasks like sending emails. But motion capture is a new tool that allows Steven and me to truly enter the digital world, blending live-action and animation, creating a digitally produced movie like shooting a live-action film."

A Movie Technology, Not a Genre:

Nowadays, more and more movies adopt motion capture technology, and some mistakenly treat it as a movie genre. Fundamentally, it is merely a filming technique. As Peter Jackson said: "Motion capture is a movie-making tool, not a genre like Westerns. Using this tool, Steven and I can change the methods and dimensions of live-action movie-making, entering the virtual world created by computers to shoot a live-action movie. We can set regular camera angles, control light sources, etc., all similar to traditional filming, except that it happens in a virtual space."

Such an understanding reflects the brilliance of the two filmmakers behind "Tintin." The latest technologies in the hands of tech enthusiasts often become constraints, whereas in the hands of story-focused creators, they become powerful tools. The former, like Robert Zemeckis, although a pioneer in motion capture technology, produces films like "Beowulf" that appear forced and rigid, overly imitating humans unnaturally. The latter, like Spielberg and Jackson, use technology as a means and pathway, opening up a new world. Jamie Bell, who plays Tintin, said: "I've never seen motion capture presented in such a way in movies. Although 'The Polar Express' was also a great achievement of motion capture, it didn't give me this feeling. 'The Adventures of Tintin' is a perfect blend of technology and art."

For a movie heavily reliant on computer special effects, the contributions of WETA Studios cannot be overstated. According to the shooting ratio, motion capture accounts for about 15% of the entire movie content, while the remaining 85% relies on technicians completing it on computers. Thus, for every frame of actor performance, animators must work five hours to achieve a perfect frame of the movie, excluding Snowy's scenes—fully created through digital technology without any motion capture elements.

Returning to Childlike Wonder in Filmmaking:

Reflecting on the six-year shooting experience, both Steven Spielberg and Peter Jackson have many stories to share. Jackson recounted: "The process of making the movie was indeed very moving. Initially, all the images were rough, and we could only slowly add layers bit by bit to enrich the details. That's why it took Steven and me six years to complete 'Tintin.' However, during this long period, we always stayed motivated because almost every week, we saw fresh things in the footage, new shots, and our old friends gradually becoming believable."

Spielberg admitted that "Tintin" would change his future working methods: "Making movies is like mastering one's nature. Filming such a movie requires childlike wonder. During 'Schindler's List,' my inner child rested, but with 'Jurassic Park' and 'Tintin,' it returned. Throughout the process, I felt more like a painter than a director. This movie is like painting rather than shooting."

In fact, the filming was filled with surprises and childlike fun, especially when actors wore motion capture "workwear," helmets with cameras, and had dense data recording points pasted on their faces, causing everyone present to burst out laughing. Spielberg described it: "We laughed for ten minutes straight because we'd never seen anyone dressed like that. However, as work began, they quickly entered professional mode and started playing their roles. I believe this is the common secret of all great performances: actors need imagination to shape their worldview and personality. Their success depends on imagination and acting skills, not on costumes or the type of movie."
