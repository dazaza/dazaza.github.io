Hakka women's shoe insoles, which are symbolic of love tokens

by dunk9371 on 2010-04-12 19:28:45

【China Shoes Network】"Sing a folk song over the hill, promise to make shoe pads for my dear brother; if you have the heart, sew from beginning to end with needles; spent countless sleepless nights, broke several embroidery needles." In the colorful Hakka culture garden, embroidered shoe pads are as brilliant and unique as Hakka folk songs. The alluring embroidered shoe pads are the "patent" of Hakka girls. They are not only tokens of love but also simple gifts expressing the goodwill of friends and family.

Wang Guiyu, who lives near Tongtianyan in Ganzhou City, is an ordinary Hakka woman. Influenced by her family, she picked up needlework at a young age and learned to make cloth shoes from her mother. In 1964, at the age of 16, she, along with other educated youth from Ganzhou, was sent to Cuilei Mountain Reclamation Farm in Guangchang County. The tough rural life seemed somewhat monotonous and boring, so the ingenious and diligent Wang Guiyu began using her spare time to embroider shoe pads to pass the time. Under her leadership, more and more educated youth picked up needles and threads, learning stitching techniques from each other and exchanging patterns. Wang Guiyu, who could sit still and focus, made increasingly beautiful shoe pads with more intricate patterns. Whether working in the logging group or the afforestation group, or serving as a broadcaster or typist, she always had her needlework in hand, finishing a pair every two or three days.

In 1975, after returning to the city, Wang Guiyu was assigned to the Installation Company of the Construction Bureau in the Ganzhou region. While continuing her work, she still persisted in embroidering shoe pads, making them for her parents, brothers, in-laws, husband, and later for her children, weaving her aspirations for a happy life into layer upon layer of the shoe pads with every stitch. Each pair of shoe pads represented a portion of genuine affection. The shoe pads, closely adhering to the feet of her loved ones like shadows, followed them wherever they went, step by step, bringing joy to Wang Guiyu's heart.

Now 61 years old, Wang Guiyu has been embroidering shoe pads for over 40 years. She says: "Although my eyesight isn't very good now, it takes me ten days to half a month to finish a pair. But my family doesn't get used to store-bought shoe pads and insists on using the ones I make by hand. Last year, I made 10 pairs for my mother and younger brother, and this year, I made 14 pairs for my distant children and son-in-law."

The embroidered shoe pads, tightly adhering to the feet of her loved ones like shadows, follow them wherever they go. Wang Guiyu, meticulously embroidering auspicious characters onto shoe pads on the balcony, is silently blessing something in her heart...

**Term Explanation:**

Embroidered shoe pads are made from old and broken scraps of fabric glued together to form a hard cloth piece about three to five layers thick. Two additional layers of bottom fabric (lining) are added before shaping them into shoe soles and then embroidering various patterns to create embroidered shoe pads. Traditionally, the design is divided into three parts according to the realms of heaven, man, and earth: the toe (representing heaven), the arch (representing man), and the heel (representing earth). The toe often features patterns inspired by celestial bodies such as the sun, moon, and stars. The arch, representing humanity, showcases elegant and meaningful patterns, sometimes even featuring text. The heel, symbolizing the earthly realm, often displays patterns like crosses and intestines of good fortune. Some shoe pads break these traditional divisions, presenting a unified design with just a border and continuous, full patterns inside. There are many embroidery designs, mainly divided into concrete figures and abstract symbols, each carrying special meanings. Embroidery methods include paper-cut applique embroidery, flat needle embroidery, grid-patterned embroidery, etc. Grid-patterned embroidery is commonly used, where grids are first drawn on the shoe sole, patterns are embroidered, and then filled using cross-stitch or diagonal arrangement methods.
