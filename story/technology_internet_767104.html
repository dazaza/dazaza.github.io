iPhone becomes a handheld console?

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:57:12

If you're observant enough, you may have noticed the phrase "MFI game controllers" appear during Apple's iOS7 Keynote presentation at WWDC.

This doesn't just mean that developers can design games for iOS7 that support controllers; it also means third-party manufacturers can start thinking about creating "iPhone controllers".

Of course, for someone like me who usually only plays iOS games that can be managed with one finger, a controller isn't very appealing. However, for those who enjoy playing big titles like FIFA Soccer or Call of Duty on their iPhones, this is quite a significant development.

As early as June this year, the gaming website Kotaku released a spy photo of what appeared to be Logitech's iPhone game controller. Recently, renowned leaker evleaks posted a clear concept image (the title image) of what seems to be the same controller on Twitter.

From the image, we can see that the left side of the controller has a directional pad, while the right side features four control buttons. Below these four buttons appears to be a pause button. There are also buttons located on both shoulders of the controller.

The back of the controller leaves space for the iPhone 5/5s camera, possibly designed for augmented reality games. However, from the image, there doesn't seem to be an obvious place for batteries, meaning the controller could be powered through the iPhone's lightning port, or it might contain an internal battery.

Previously, controllers designed primarily for Android mobile smart devices never really took off. Whether Apple, by including this feature in iOS7, will ignite this market remains questionable.

The user base is certainly there. In May this year, IDC and App Annie released a report stating that in the first quarter of 2013, consumer spending on games from Apple's App Store surpassed spending on handheld consoles from Nintendo and Sony for the first time; In August of this year, these two companies again released a report indicating that in the second quarter of 2013, consumer spending on games from Google Play also exceeded spending on handheld consoles from Nintendo and Sony, though it was still far behind the iOS platform.

The key lies in the quality and weight of the games. The most popular games on mobile smart devices are mostly of the Fruit Ninja or Angry Birds variety, which handheld console players would likely consider "not worth mentioning." However, the number of "blockbuster" games on the iOS platform is increasing, turning the combination of controllers and games into a classic "chicken and egg" problem.

Another X-factor is Apple TV and the potential Apple television. When players are looking at a TV screen instead of a phone screen, a well-designed controller becomes essential.

Whenever Apple enters any market, regardless of whether it will become a gamechanger, it at least draws a lot of attention. If you're a hardcore iOS gamer, it might be worth keeping an eye on future developments. Who says an iPhone can't turn into a handheld console?

The iPhone event will be held on September 10th.
