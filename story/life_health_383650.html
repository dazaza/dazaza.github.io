Cervical hypertrophy: What are the main symptoms? Cervical hypertrophy is one type of chronic cervicitis. It is caused by pathogen infections leading to inflammatory changes in the cervical mucosa. The pathogens responsible for this condition include mycoplasma, chlamydia, bacteria, and viruses. Simple hypertrophy does not affect pregnancy; however, severe cervicitis can lead to endometritis, salpingo-oophoritis, tubal adhesion, or obstruction, resulting in infertility.

Cervical hypertrophy generally does not require treatment unless there is erosion, in which case treatment for cervical erosion may be necessary. What are the main symptoms of cervical hypertrophy? Experts from the Gynecology Department of Zhengzhou Anti-aircraft Hospital have listed the following symptoms:

1. Typical clinical manifestations: The primary symptom is an increase in vaginal discharge. In acute cervicitis, the discharge is purulent and may be accompanied by lower abdominal and sacral pain, or urinary frequency, urgency, and dysuria. In chronic cervicitis, the discharge is milky white and mucus-like, or pale yellow and purulent. Severe cervical erosion or the presence of cervical polyps may result in bloody discharge or post-coital bleeding. Mild cases may present without systemic symptoms. When the inflammation spreads along the uterosacral ligaments to the pelvis, it can cause sacral pain, a feeling of heaviness in the lower abdomen, and dysmenorrhea, which worsen during defecation or sexual intercourse. Moreover, thick, purulent discharge can hinder sperm passage, potentially causing infertility.

2. Gynecological examination: In acute inflammation, the cervix appears congested and edematous, or eroded, with purulent secretions discharged from the cervical canal. Touching the cervix may induce pain. In chronic cervicitis, the cervix may exhibit varying degrees of erosion, hypertrophy, polyps, glandular cysts, eversion, or purulent secretions at the cervical os. Palpation of the cervix may feel firm. If there is cervical erosion or polyps, contact bleeding may occur.

3. A Pap smear test may show a Papanicolaou grade II.

4. For more severe cases, a cervical biopsy may be performed to confirm the diagnosis.

5. Cervical erosion or polyps can be difficult to distinguish from early-stage cervical cancer. The latter's tissue is usually harder, more brittle, and prone to bleeding. A cervical Pap smear must be done to look for cancer cells, and if necessary, colposcopy and cervical biopsies should be conducted for differentiation. The surface of an enlarged cervix can also appear squamous epithelial shedding and columnar epithelial hyperplasia due to injury or inflammatory stimulation, forming erosion. After the inflammation is treated, the local congestion and edema of the cervix subside, and the cervix surface is re-covered by squamous epithelium, restoring its smooth appearance. However, the proliferated connective tissue does not regress but remains, maintaining the hypertrophic appearance of the cervix, sometimes even increasing in size by 1-2 times or more.
