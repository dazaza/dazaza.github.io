Stock Index Futures Launch - Hexun Exclusive Interview with Analyst Yuan Lei of Hengji Investment

by huaxun399 on 2010-04-18 12:25:33

Interview Time: April 16, 2010 & nbsp; 14:55-15:20

Host: We are now broadcasting a series of interviews on the stock index futures from Hexun.com. Here with us is Mr. Yuan Lei, analyst from Hengji Investment. Hello, Mr. Yuan! (14:57)

Yuan Lei: Hello to all internet users and investor friends! Hello, host! (14:57)

Host: Let’s get straight to the point. Has our market seen any stock index futures trend? (14:57)

Yuan Lei: First, I think the trend isn't very clear. From today's market, we can see that the correlation between the stock index futures and the A-share market isn't obvious. The market is currently transitioning smoothly, as it has just launched. The number of stock index futures accounts isn't particularly high—currently at 9,137. Although there has been some increase in enthusiasm compared to before, overall, it hasn’t reached a truly mature or ideal level yet. This will likely take some time. Stability remains the most important factor. (14:58)

Host: Why haven't financial institutions entered the market yet? (14:58)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (14:58)

Host: What do you think about the management not allowing financial institutions to enter the market? (14:58)

Yuan Lei: It might be to protect the interests of small and medium investors. Their investment and participation methods could be affected. Current policies and strategies aren't yet perfect, so they may wait until they are improved before letting them enter. For investors, this is a more stable and better approach. (14:58)

Host: Is the risk higher before financial institutions enter the market? (15:01)

Yuan Lei: There is definitely some risk. One view in the market is that the launch of stock index futures marks the arrival of an institutional era. Many investors might worry whether after the launch of stock index futures, individual investors like us won't have a place to play anymore. I don't think so. Participating in this market involves significant risks, and one needs to enhance their professional knowledge. Not only is the risk high, but participating in stock investments also requires a certain level of professionalism—it's impossible to invest blindly. (15:01)

Host: The biggest issue brought by stock index futures is that the 5 million RMB threshold excludes 90% of individual investors. Do you think having stock index futures increases or decreases the risk for those who cannot participate? (15:02)

Yuan Lei: I believe the risk for them has decreased. The 5 million RMB threshold is just that—a threshold. Many investors are excluded not because of these 5 million RMB, but because of the special nature of stock index futures. They involve leverage principles, meaning that after investing 5 million RMB, the actual amount invested could far exceed this figure. (15:02)

Host: It's not just the threshold but the need for additional investment behind it that stops them, right? (15:02)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:02)

Host: Let's continue discussing the stock index futures market. You mentioned earlier that there seems to be no stock index futures trend based on the divergence between spot and futures markets. However, starting from 2890 points, the main driver has been the rise of large-cap stocks ("two"), while smaller stocks ("eight") were still falling sharply a few days ago. Could this be considered somewhat as a stock index futures trend? (15:02)

Yuan Lei: Personally, I think this is still mainly due to the inherent operating rules of the A-share market, and the impact of stock index futures is very limited. In the early stages of the A-share market, there were regional investments, including the Expo concept and "map trading" concepts. Small-cap stocks and concepts performed well, especially the "eight" among "two-eight." Afterward, there was a slight shift, which I thought happened at least three times at the beginning of the year, but none were successful. This time, it quietly shifted, and at the start of this week, the weighty stocks rose for a day and then fell back again, especially real estate and finance, which led the market down. The "two-eight" effect wasn't particularly evident. Overall, the best method for the market is that stock index futures will have more long-term impacts rather than immediate ones. (15:02)

Host: If stock index futures cannot change the current market structure, what is your outlook for the next seven or eight months—bullish, bearish, or neutral? (15:02)

Yuan Lei: I think neutral. The A-share market has its own operational rules. Weighty stocks, such as banks and real estate, have a clear advantage. A straightforward investment strategy would be: the more they fall, the more we should buy, because their valuation and price are low, and the advantages are very obvious. Additionally, the performance of listed companies is good, so we focus on investing in them. On the policy side, recent real estate policies have been introduced and tightened, which will certainly limit the upward trend of real estate stocks. So, there's both bullish and bearish sentiment. The final result of this tug-of-war is likely to be a smooth transition, as the forces are evenly matched, making it unlikely for there to be a major move. (15:02)

Host: It seems there's also a lack of market liquidity supporting the "two" stocks. (15:09)

Yuan Lei: Yes, the volume isn't sufficient. (15:10)

Host: What level of volume do you think is needed to support a sustained rally in the "two" stocks? (15:10)

Yuan Lei: I think it needs to exceed 20 billion yuan. (15:10)

Host: For one market or both markets? (15:10)

Yuan Lei: Both markets. Without sufficient volume, the "two" still need observation. (15:11)

Host: Many bullish people shout loudly, believing there should be a rally in weighty stocks, but they may not notice the impact of trading volume on the market. Do you think that after the introduction of stock index futures, the trend of the spot market will not be greatly affected by stock index futures, but will there be a significant impact on arbitrage and profit models? (15:11)

Yuan Lei: I think there will be arbitrage opportunities, where some companies can make a certain profit. Investors need to enhance their professionalism. Old investors who previously traded both spot and futures markets can find techniques even though the underlying assets have changed, as the model remains the same. For new investors, enhancing professionalism is crucial before engaging in arbitrage or hedging operations. (15:12)

Host: Since you are neutral on the market, if we use the Shanghai Composite Index as a benchmark, what is the range of market volatility? Below 3000 points or above 3000 points? (15:13)

Yuan Lei: Optimistically, it should be between 2900 and 3300 points, which is a relatively large range. Pessimistically, it could adjust to around 2800 or 2700 points. I think the possibility of sharp rises or falls is not very high. (15:13)

Host: So the range of volatility isn't very large? (15:13)

Yuan Lei: Yes, probably within a 200 to 300-point trend. (15:13)

Host: Historically, such a rise in the A-share market represents only about a ten percent range. It has already been sideways for eight months. Do you think it can remain sideways for another eight months within the year? (15:13)

Yuan Lei: Not eight months, but five or six months is possible. (15:15)

Host: Neither bulls nor bears can break through directionally? (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:15)

Host: If external forces change this balance, what do you think could change it? (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Policy. China is a policy-driven market, so attention to policy is indispensable. We need policy support first, followed by volume support. (15:15)

Host: What kind of policy support? End of real estate regulation? (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Now house prices are too high, so that's not realistic yet. If the Renminbi appreciates... (15:15)

Host: Do you think Renminbi appreciation would be beneficial? (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:15)

Host: The short-term possibility of Renminbi appreciation is also not very high. (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:16)

Host: If the government does not introduce any adverse policies, there will be no downward breakthrough, right? (15:16)

Yuan Lei: There might be a balanced state. (15:16)

Host: Would adjustments in overseas markets drag it down? (15:16)

Yuan Lei: The A-share market is a relatively closed market. Many people think that if the U.S. stock market falls, the A-share market will also fall, or if the U.S. stock market surges, the A-share market will also surge. I completely disagree with this theory. The A-share market has its own unique characteristics and is relatively closed. Recently, the U.S. stock market has risen significantly, but the A-share market hasn't moved much. While there is some influence, the trend of overseas markets is not the decisive factor determining the direction of the A-share market. Last year, when the U.S. stock market kept adjusting downwards, the A-share market rebounded independently upwards. Actually, I think the divergence between them is not normal联动性. The A-share market adjusted earlier than the U.S. stock market. When the A-share market had already adjusted, the U.S. stock market just started to adjust, so it should have rebounded. Before the adjustment, we saw a rebound without obvious联动性. (15:17)

Host: Do you think changes in the external market will not alter the market trend? (15:17)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:17)

Host: At 6124 points, the U.S., A-share, and Hong Kong markets hit bottom at the same time in October 2008. Of course, the Hong Kong market had a secondary bottom problem. Historically, there has never been such good联动性. Of course, this round of rebounds in the A-share market preceded the U.S. stock market rebound, but the A-share market also adjusted earlier than the U.S. stock market. Adjusting took longer, much longer than the starting time. Can you explain the联动性 between them, from adjusting together to rebounding successively? (15:18)

Yuan Lei: Such phenomena exist in the market—peaking together, hitting bottom together, rebounding successively, and situations where the U.S. stock market rises while the A-share market falls, or vice versa. Therefore, the theory that the external market directly determines the direction of the A-share market is invalid and unreasonable, as it lacks valid evidence. Thus, this matter must be viewed dialectically and divided into time windows. The divergence between the A-share and U.S. stock markets occurs at different time windows, leading to different trends. (15:18)

Host: Now mainstream views believe that after the introduction of stock index futures, the volatility of the A-share market will decrease, and part of the risk will be hedged. How do you explain that despite having stock index futures, the Dow Jones Industrial Average and Hong Kong stock market experienced severe declines after the financial crisis, especially the Hong Kong market, where the decline was similar to ours? State-owned enterprise stocks fell by seventy percent, and we didn't see stock index futures reducing their volatility. Historically, a seventy percent drop in Hong Kong stocks is also very rare. How do you view this issue? (15:18)

Yuan Lei: Stock index futures in the U.S. were introduced early, in 1987, and initially had a minimal impact on the U.S. stock market. Only after improvements did it form a more mature market. Currently, the A-share market has always been a one-sided bullish market, and only now has it completed the process of being one-sidedly bullish. This improves the entire mechanism of the A-share market, involving hedging and risk avoidance. The purpose of stock index futures is to avoid risk, which is the fundamental reason. However, when the overall market and macroeconomic environment face issues such as the U.S. subprime crisis, financial crisis, or Hong Kong's Asian financial crisis, one must consider the dynamics and mindset of funds. They may believe that no matter which side they choose, they cannot hedge against the risk, resulting in leaving the market temporarily to avoid it. Consequently, when significant economic problems arise, both markets will experience adjustments as funds and institutions withdraw, believing they are unsafe anywhere. This leads to simultaneous declines without achieving the purpose of risk avoidance. (15:18)

Host: Risk hedging via stock index futures is also subject to certain conditions. (15:18)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:18)

Host: Another interesting viewpoint is that after the Dow Jones Industrial Average was introduced in 1987, it rose 15 times over 30 years, increasing just as much as the A-share market. Do you think stock index futures cannot change the mechanism of a one-sided market? (15:19)

Yuan Lei: Stock index futures have just been introduced. In this stage, the number of short sellers exceeds that of longs, given our prolonged single-sided market. The trend hasn't fully changed yet, requiring a process. Initially, longs outnumber shorts, potentially driving the market upward. As it matures over two or three months, our thinking gradually evolves, and short positions appear. When combined, both sides might stabilize. Subsequently, we must judge based on the broader economic environment, policy environment, and intrinsic market rules which force dominates at different times, enabling us to determine the market trend. (15:19)

Host: We've considered both the normal and extreme states of the market. In an extreme situation, when the market forms a unanimous opinion, either unanimously bullish or bearish, do you think the futures market would lose liquidity? Stock index futures require counterparties. If everyone is bullish and no one is bearish, long contracts cannot be established, and vice versa for short contracts. In such a scenario, how can stock index futures hedge risks? (15:19)

Yuan Lei: This is a very extreme phenomenon, and I can confirm that it is impossible. Regardless of the environment, each investor's direction will surely differ; complete unanimity is impossible. (15:19)

Host: Let's discuss relatively extreme cases where most people look one way, and a minority looks the other. What happens if they can't find sufficient counterparties? What if they want to go long or short but can't? (15:19)

Yuan Lei: I think the market would enter a temporary stagnant phase. When the A-share market is active, volumes are high, and when inactive, volumes are low. During such times, the market clearly lacks money and volume, but it won't disappear; only a stoppage will occur. (15:19)

Host: Discussing the turnover of stock index futures with colleagues, after calculating, today's actual turnover might be only tens of billions, very little. If the turnover is so low, indeed, no one would be willing to go long or short for just tens of billions; it's not worth it. Does the sluggish turnover mean, as some say, that the market will stay flat for a while, waiting for sufficient turnover before choosing a direction? Otherwise, people might feel it's not worth the effort unless they treat it as neither a hedging nor arbitrage tool but something else entirely. To proceed, they must patiently build up the turnover and establish necessary positions before acting. (15:20)

Yuan Lei: Today's turnover isn't large, but this is normal. There are only over 9,000 accounts opened, and not all 9,000 will act. I believe at least half will be in a wait-and-see mode, observing trends today or next week before taking action. With the market just starting, they won't sell easily. Some will move, but true activity will only occur once volumes increase and the market becomes gradually more active, enabling hedging or risk avoidance to be realized. (15:20)

Host: I'm concerned that the market might still have more short sellers than longs. If public mutual funds are required to hedge, who can be their counterparty with such a massive market value? Public mutual funds must open short contracts to hedge, but how many longs can absorb such large contracts? What size of contract is needed to support them? (15:20)

Yuan Lei: There must be a sufficiently strong counterparty; otherwise, it cannot be achieved. Public mutual funds hedging will only prevent the market from rising, possibly leading to a downward trend or stagnation, with a downward trend more likely. However, not all public mutual funds can hedge simultaneously. (15:20)

Host: Currently, only 20% hedging is allowed. (15:20)

Yuan Lei: Yes, there are restrictions, and national policies regulate this. Even if extreme phenomena occur, the state stands behind everything. The "national team" will ultimately resolve the issue. If there isn't a sufficiently large counterparty to handle extreme situations, the "national team" will step in. (15:21)

Host: We don't have a stabilization fund now, and social security can only make profits, not losses. Regardless, let it activate the market first? (15:21)

Yuan Lei: That's impossible. (15:21)

Host: Then who will step in? (15:21)

Yuan Lei: That depends on the state's planning and strategy. Large funds must be restricted, as risks are controlled within limits. They cannot act recklessly or go to extremes. Policies and planning must be determined. (15:21)

Host: The series of interviews on stock index futures concludes here. Thank you, Mr. Yuan Lei, analyst from Hengji Investment, for exchanging views with netizens, and thank you all! (15:21)
