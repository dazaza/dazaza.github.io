In general, at the moment of a car accident, a person's reaction time is approximately 0.75 seconds. Making a quick decision to escape within such a short period is crucial.

Car Submersion: Escape through the window or door

When a car submerges, it usually remains on the water surface for one to two minutes. If the car has an engine in the front, it will begin to sink from the front. In this situation, it's most important to stay calm, assess the situation, and then use an appropriate method to escape.

When the water level rises above the car windows, slowly lower the window and escape as water enters the vehicle. If the electric window cannot be lowered, you need to break the window with a tool or high heels. If the side window cannot be broken, try to escape through the sunroof or the front/rear windshield. If you can't escape through the window, try to slowly open the car door. However, due to the water pressure outside the car, the door may be difficult to open. When the door cannot be opened, remain calm and wait until the car is filled with water up to the top before attempting to open the door and swim out. At this point, the pressure inside and outside the car will be similar, making the door easier to open.

When the water hasn't reached the window yet, immediately lower the window and climb out before the water rises to the window height.

When a car is washed into a river, it usually floats on the water surface for one to two minutes. First, stay calm, take a few seconds to relax, assess the situation, and decide which method to use for escaping. Unbuckle the seatbelt; if it cannot be undone, find a sharp object to cut it, and quickly leave the car.

Rollover: Turn off the engine first to prevent gasoline explosion

During a rollover, the first step is to turn off the engine to prevent leaking gasoline from catching fire or exploding. Second, support the roof with both hands and place your feet firmly on the dashboard near the steering wheel. This helps fix your body in place, then unbuckle the seatbelt and let your body slowly fall onto the roof. Passengers should quickly use their hands and feet to tightly grip the seats or inner side panels, allowing their bodies to rotate with the car.

Moment Before Collision: Control the steering wheel

Before a collision, facing an impending crash, the only thing that can be done is to control the steering wheel to minimize the impact of the collision. Rear passengers should lean back as much as possible, arms crossed over the chest, hands covering the head, adopting this posture to reduce injury. The driver should also lean back as much as possible, hands supporting the steering wheel, minimizing the excessive impact on the chest and abdomen by the steering wheel. Jumping from a fast-moving vehicle requires attention to posture: curling the body into a ball, head close to the chest, knees and feet tightly together, elbows close to the sides of the body, hands covering the ears, jumping down while curled up, and rolling after jumping to reduce the impact with the ground.

Trapped in a Car Accident: Side windows are easiest to break

In rescue operations, rescuing people trapped in car accidents is the most common. If the car doors are jammed, regardless of the model, the only option under self-control is to break the glass to escape. Choosing which glass to break is important. The front windshield is double-layered composite glass, which even if broken, won't shatter, so the side windows must be chosen, using a sharp object like an escape hammer to break them.
