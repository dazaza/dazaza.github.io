Goodbye Mary 1

by nnphotowyl on 2011-09-19 10:42:45

After living in one place for a long time, suddenly changing residence makes everything feel awkward. For example, here the tap water is shared, and so are the toilets, which is very uncomfortable. On the first night after moving, I suffered from serious insomnia. The lack of a sit-down toilet directly led to constipation, making me extremely uncomfortable. My husband is the type who can fall asleep as soon as his head touches the pillow, but I could only close my eyes and silently count numbers in my mind. Counting from one to a hundred and then back down to one, repeating several times still didn't work.

At that time, the city was undergoing demolitions, bustling everywhere with a sense of upheaval. Many people were looking for housing all over the place. After much effort, my husband and I found a house in the northern part of the city to temporarily settle down. It was close to the road, making entry and exit relatively convenient. The owners were farmers who had just transitioned from rural life, probably holding a considerable amount of land development funds. They built small western-style houses that were square and rigid, resembling cement cages. Standing in the courtyard and looking up, there always seemed to be a screen-like object flashing with varying shades of blue light, yet without any images, appearing quite hollow.

This city is indeed small, incomparable to provincial capitals elsewhere. If avoiding rush hours, driving a Santana would take about an hour to traverse the city from east to west and north to south. The size is almost embarrassing, but there are advantages to being small. Going out to handle affairs is very convenient, and you don't have to worry about wasting all your time on the road. Also, people meet each other more frequently. Walking around the streets, it feels like there aren't many completely unfamiliar faces. Occasionally encountering an unfamiliar smile feels incredibly precious, making you want to look back a few more times.

When I finally felt some sleepiness, suddenly from the apartment directly across from us came an untimely scream, abrupt and irritating. Then came intermittent crying and cursing, a woman's voice, and upon listening carefully, there seemed to be a child involved. Occasionally, there were escalating male-female arguments, tense and unyielding. The sound insulation in these simple buildings was really poor. With the hot weather, everyone kept their windows open, making the sounds especially clear at night.

I could only endure patiently. Usually, my husband and I rarely quarrel so dramatically. Even if we have differences, we just express our emotions briefly and resolve them. We would never choose to endlessly yell at midnight disturbing the neighbors. At least, as a married woman, I don't want to bear the stigma of being a shrew too early. In this aspect, my husband also behaves appropriately; he knows how to yield to a woman. He often says good men don't fight with women. In fact, I've always felt he truly loves me. Love is mutual tolerance, and because of love, no conflict cannot be resolved internally.

Upstairs, they continued to confront each other sharply, and the shouting became increasingly fierce. Soon, we heard the sounds of physical struggle. The bed began to creak, the legs of chairs and tables banged against the floor, and the dull thuds of fists and feet hitting bodies could be heard. Items were thrown onto the floor or walls with a loud clatter. Intermittently, there were sharp sounds of glass breaking, the woman's desperate screams, the child's uncontrollable cries of terror, and the man's coarse and excited curses. It was as if they were rehearsing a play, fully utilizing their potential to create exaggerated and distorted sounds, turning what should have been a peaceful night into a violently noisy and screaming scene. At this point, I could distinguish a little boy whose muffled cries of "mommy" sounded truly pitiful. I couldn't lie still anymore, so I sat up and turned on the bedside lamp. My husband was also awakened by the noise. He had been enduring silently in the dark, now squinting and frowning at me.

We heard the woman on the second floor shouting something like "I can't live another day with a man like you, I want a divorce." The man wasn't yielding either. Despite his voice being hoarse, he roared like a beast, saying, "Divorce then! Don't threaten me with this. Whoever doesn't divorce tomorrow isn't human."

My husband and I didn't know what to do at that moment.

Fortunately, two or three other residents couldn't bear it anymore. They stepped out of their rooms and stood in the courtyard or corridors, yelling dissatisfaction at the family upstairs. There's strength in numbers. The second floor quieted down somewhat, the volume gradually decreasing. Occasionally, there were still some sounds, but the momentum wasn't as aggressive and no longer posed much threat. A while later, it really stopped, even the child ceased crying.

I buried my head back into my husband's arms and said, "One day, let's have a big argument and make the people upstairs angry!" My husband lacked humor; he said, "Just go to sleep," then turned over and snored off. The echoes in my ears persisted, like a group of feline creatures wildly biting each other in the dark corners. The previous intense and chaotic noises made the current silence feel strange. All my sleepiness disappeared, and I felt dry-mouthed and uncomfortable all over.

I quietly got up and felt around for the cup on the nightstand in the dark. The tea was already cold, and aged chrysanthemum tea has a peculiar taste. In the darkness, I couldn't see the flowers settled in the water, silently piled together like reefs without sound or movement.

My husband often says that chrysanthemum tea tastes best when hot. Drinking it cold loses its meaning, leaving no sensation.
