What to do if a mosquito flies into your ear - What to do if a foreign object enters the ear

[Overview]

Foreign objects in the external auditory canal refer to injuries caused by accidental entry of foreign objects into the external auditory canal. Foreign objects in the external auditory canal are more common in children, as they intentionally put small objects into their ears. It can also happen to adults, often due to leaving small objects or insects entering during ear cleaning or trauma.

[Causative factors]

1. Flying insects entering the ear.

2. Children playing and putting toys into their ears.

3. Adults dropping small objects into their ears while cleaning them.

4. Foreign objects entering the ear during work or labor.

[Consequences]

Larger foreign objects can cause hearing impairment, tinnitus, ear pain, reflexive coughing, etc. Contact with the eardrum may cause dizziness, or even block the ear canal, affect hearing, and lead to external auditory canal inflammation or middle ear inflammation. Live insect or animal-based foreign objects can crawl and move around, causing severe ear pain and noise, which may even make patients feel anxious. Sharp foreign objects entering can cause unbearable pain and tinnitus for the patient, and may even damage the eardrum leading to bleeding. Therefore, foreign objects should be promptly removed.

[Treatment Methods]

Below are emergency handling methods for home use:

1. If a small insect flies into the ear, take the patient to a dark place and shine a flashlight towards the ear, or direct the affected ear towards a light source. The insect will often fly or crawl out towards the light on its own. If the small insect cannot be made to exit, you can fill the external auditory canal with plant oil, suffocating the insect until it dies. Then lay down on your side with the affected ear facing downwards, allowing the dead insect to flow out of the ear canal.

2. If beads, toys, buttons, or similar items enter the ear, you can jump up and down on one foot several times, which might make the foreign object bounce out. You can also use an earwax hook to carefully navigate around the object and pull it out from deeper within the ear. Be sure not to push the object further in.

3. If beans, seeds, peanuts, or other expandable objects enter the ear, you can first drop 95% alcohol into the ear to shrink the object, then pour it out or remove it manually.

4. If mud enters the ear and is difficult to remove, you can rinse with warm water or warm saline solution. For cases of tympanic membrane perforation due to otitis media, rinsing is not recommended. Instead, use cotton swab fibers to sweep it out, or carefully dig it out with an ear pick or small spoon.

5. Flat or stick-shaped foreign objects can be clamped out with ear forceps.

Note: If the above methods fail to remove the foreign object, you should quickly go to the hospital and have an ENT doctor remove it. Foreign objects remaining in the ear for too long can cause external auditory canal inflammation.

[Prevention and Care]

1. Do not allow children to play with objects that could easily enter the ear, and frequently educate them not to insert small things into their ears.

2. Do not use wooden sticks or matchsticks to clean the ears.

3. When camping outdoors, strengthen protection to prevent insects from entering the ear.

4. Eliminate cockroaches to prevent them from entering the ear while sleeping.

5. Prevent ear trauma.

6. After removing the foreign object, disinfect the ear canal and ensure it remains dry and clean.
