The science connecting the increased frequency and intensity of extreme weather to the climate crisis has matured dramatically in the last few years. Think about the past year—we’ve had flooding in Pakistan that displaced 20 million people, and further destabilized a nuclear-armed nation. We’ve had drought and wildfires in Russia. In Australia you’ve had floods on the scale of France and Germany combined. Then there’s drought in Texas—out of 254 counties in Texas, 252 are on fire. I’m speaking to you from Nashville, where the city lost the equivalent of an entire year’s budget due to recent flooding—an area that had never flooded that way before, so no one had flood insurance. As Gore and others in the environmental movement know all too well, those attackers have been winning the battle over climate change in American public opinion—if not the science itself. In 2007 and 2008—in the wake of Gore’s Oscar-winning documentary An Inconvenient Truth—a solid majority of Americans saw climate change as a direct threat to their families. But that support has eroded—a recent Gallup poll showed that just 53% of Americans saw climate change as a threat, and Washington has remained gridlocked on the issue.
