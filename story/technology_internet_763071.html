One Glass Explorer returned their Google Glass.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 14:00:59

For tech enthusiasts, Google Glass is one of the most exciting new devices, but currently, it's only available as the Explorer edition for developers, leaving us envious. However, someone who got their hands on Google Glass decided to return it after a period of use. Why did this happen?

Ron Miller had made up his mind before purchasing Google Glass. Within a month, he would decide whether to keep the device or return it. After using it for some time, he felt that while the device had many appealing aspects, he didn't like its interaction method, and at a price of $1500, it failed to provide an impressive experience.

Firstly, he found that the way of using the device could easily become tiresome. Due to the limitations of the device itself, human-computer interaction could only be done through tapping or voice commands, with voice limited to a few commands. Therefore, tapping was more commonly used. Based on the characteristics of Glass, Google designed a circular interface (carousel). The problem was that as you used it more, the number of cards increased. Although Google had some settings, such as certain cards disappearing over time and only important emails being displayed, using a computer in this way wasn’t something he enjoyed.

Besides, Glass lacked any form of documentation. There wasn’t even a website for setting up Glass or contact information for the Glass team on the packaging. Of course, from his interactions with Google employees, they really hoped developers would fall in love with the device, and the Glass community was very helpful.

In summary, he believed these were all insufficient. He still thought smartphones were a better option. "I really prefer pulling out my phone from my pocket. It’s easier for me, and compared to looking up at a small screen and trying to control the device, using a phone causes less embarrassment."

He still believed wearable devices were the future, but Glass wouldn’t become a killer device. "It feels like a prototype, and I can't help but think it might be a prelude to a better device Google is working on." When he asked the Glass customer service if Explorer users would get the next generation device or at least a discount, the employee said they were unaware.

When he returned the device, the Glass customer service representative kindly assisted him. He would receive a refund within a few weeks.

This experience left him somewhat disappointed, but ultimately, he felt the device wasn't worth the investment:

I’d rather wait for a finished product than spend money to participate in a beta test. I still believe in the potential of wearable devices. I remain optimistic about the future prospects. But I’m not willing to invest $1500 in an uncertain future — which makes me sad because I really want the future to have already arrived.
