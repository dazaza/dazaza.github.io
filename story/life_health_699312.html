Does autonomic neuropathy affect the gastrointestinal tract? Manifestations of autonomic neuropathy in the gastrointestinal system:

(1) Gastric tension is low, gastric expansion and weakened gastric motility. Most people have no symptoms. A few patients experience delayed emptying of stomach contents due to reduced gastric tension, leading to gastric expansion, causing discomfort in the upper abdomen, loss of appetite, abdominal distension after eating, and even nausea and vomiting.

(2) Esophagus is in a state of low tension, primary peristalsis weakens or disappears, emptying is delayed, and contraction decreases. Generally, there are no symptoms, but severe cases may occasionally experience difficulty swallowing.

(3) Gallbladder tension is low, and contraction force is relatively weak.

(4) Constipation and diarrhea. Due to chronic dehydration, most diabetic patients suffer from constipation, but some patients also experience diarrhea, ranging from several times a day to over twenty times. The stool is watery without pus or blood, and infection evidence is not found upon examination. Such diarrhea often occurs after meals, before dawn, or in the middle of the night, and severe cases may present as fecal incontinence. Patients become extremely emaciated, resembling cachexia.
