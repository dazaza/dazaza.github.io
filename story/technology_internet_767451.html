Hong Kong version iPhone may not be under warranty due to overwhelming after-sales service

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:30:59

A news report stating that "Hong Kong versions of the iPhone 5s and 5c cannot be serviced in Mainland China" caused a significant stir among iPhone users in China. This is because many budget-conscious Apple fans have chosen to buy cheaper Hong Kong versions of the iPhone and enjoy warranty services on the mainland. The official reason given by Apple is that some components of the iPhone are not interchangeable between the two regions. This rationale seems rather weak, as apart from the baseband chip responsible for wireless communication, there are no differences between various versions of the iPhone. Even the baseband chip merely disables certain frequency bands via software, with the hardware using the same Qualcomm chip.

The multitude of models for the 5s/5c has left users confused.

The iPhone 5s can be considered the generation of iPhone with the most models ever produced, with five versions available internationally alone. The division of LTE frequency bands is meticulous, almost obsessive-compulsive. If following the old method used for the iPhone 5 and 4s (where the Chinese version of the iPhone 5 lacked 4G-LTE support compared to the Hong Kong version, but both were A1429 models), the domestic and Hong Kong versions of the iPhone 5s should both be A1533. However, Apple provided a separate A1528 version for the Chinese mainland market, which contradicts its previous claim that the iPhone is a "World Phone."

Apple's approach of complicating instead of simplifying may have various considerations, but the specific reasons remain unclear. However, the complex model settings for the iPhone 5s and 5c create barriers for global warranties. As 4G becomes more widespread, the LTE evolution paths adopted by different countries are very complex. Even within FDD-LTE, the frequency bands used in the Asia-Pacific region and North America differ significantly. In China, 4G involves a mix of TD and FDD networks. Dividing models based on LTE frequency bands and distinguishing them during the warranty process can effectively prevent iPhones from flowing from low-price areas to high-price areas, where high-price areas would bear the after-sales costs for low-price areas.

The Everlasting Apple

Discussions about Apple product warranties and value retention are popular among Apple fans. Products like iPads and Macs are globally warrantied, while the iPhone, due to carrier issues, cannot be globally warranted. However, it can still be warrantied within regions like the EU and Greater China. When quality issues arise, products are directly replaced with new ones (or refurbished units). Apple products maintain strong prices in the second-hand market, staying close to new prices. However, Apple fans may not have considered the connection between these two factors. Apple’s "direct replacement" after-sales service has directly fostered a dark industry, mainly in mainland China.

Last year, while handling second-hand iPads, I had a conversation with an Apple product recycler in Zhongguancun. He told me that the price difference between products under warranty in mainland China and those not under warranty could reach up to 1000 yuan. For products under warranty, they are quickly returned to Apple after-sales service for replacement with new units. After obtaining new machines, merchants equip them with high-quality or original accessories and packaging, then sell them through Taobao and second/third-tier markets. This keeps second-hand prices firm while also encouraging theft of Apple products.

The Darkening Apple After-Sales Industry

If the iPhone or iPad isn't broken, how do you get it replaced at after-sales service? After consulting with some merchants, I learned that initially, relatively violent methods were used, such as damaging the motherboard without affecting the appearance through electric shocks. Later, some recyclers colluded with Apple after-sales personnel (third-party authorized after-sales), using hard-to-determine reasons like screen light leakage, slow touch response, and camera color bias to declare the machine damaged and replace it with a new one. Currently, Apple products under warranty can still be directly exchanged for new ones in the market, typically costing between 200 to 300 yuan. If there is water damage or dents, additional fees are required. Such services are mostly completed jointly by recyclers and after-sales personnel.

Even more astonishingly, earlier this year, media reports revealed that Apple after-sales personnel were exchanging new iPhones for counterfeit iPhone 4s units. Their method involved extracting the serial numbers and motherboard identification codes of entire batches of iPhone 4s units, writing them onto assembled motherboards to impersonate iPhones under warranty. The affected iPhone 4s units could still function normally, but when checked on the official Apple website, it would show "serial number has been replaced." Upon investigation, this method had been prevalent for some time until it was discovered by an inspection team Apple sent from Singapore. Some after-sales personnel were sued by Apple after leaving their jobs and bore criminal responsibility.

Who Pays for Apple's After-Sales Service?

Such dazzling fraud tactics, combined with normal after-sales services, have extracted a large number of new units from Apple's after-sales service. How are accumulated old units handled? In May this year, multiple domestic media reported that Foxconn had once recovered 5 million defective iPhones from Apple, resulting in a direct loss of 9 billion yuan. Apple and Foxconn have a quality control agreement, and any problematic units exceeding the agreement limit are returned to Foxconn for reprocessing. These reprocessed iPhones are referred to by Apple as official refurbished units and are reintroduced into the after-sales service for users who need replacements.

Throughout this process, both Apple and Foxconn expend substantial human and material resources. This is precisely why Apple recently launched on-site repair services for iPhones in some countries. According to media reports, this policy update will save Apple 1 billion dollars annually in after-sales expenses. Currently, Apple has yet to take action in mainland China after escaping the shadow of 3·15 Consumer Rights Day. Ceasing warranty services for Hong Kong versions of the iPhone 5s and 5c can alleviate pressure on after-sales services without causing legal consequences. Apple's notification in the GSX system implies that it may reconsider offering warranties for Hong Kong and Macau versions of the iPhone in the future. Perhaps by then, Apple will also cease replacing units with new ones in mainland China.
