[Nurturing Experience] Practicing in the coldest days of winter, will children practice?

by kaning05 on 2010-05-26 18:31:49

Winter exercise has many benefits, however, no matter what kind of sports parents choose for their children, they must pay attention to keeping warm. Do not let the pursuit of "winter freeze" make the child sick. There is a Chinese saying: "Summer practice in the hottest days, winter practice in the coldest days." The hottest days are the hottest, and the coldest days are the coldest. This reminds people to adapt to nature through exercise. Humans and nature are harmonious and unified. The human body also changes with the natural changes. Therefore, for children, winter exercise is particularly important.

Old Zhou's grandson hasn't been to kindergarten for two weeks. Why? Because as soon as the weather turns cold, his originally weak grandson starts coughing, which then develops into bronchitis after a few days. With daily trips to the hospital, the child naturally can't go to kindergarten.

Old Zhou gets anxious whenever he talks about his grandson's physical condition. He said, "The child is weak and gets sick every winter. These days I don't even dare to let him out of the house, for fear of catching a cold."

In fact, there are quite a few parents like Old Zhou. Worried that their children cannot withstand the cold of winter, they adopt a greenhouse protection method, restricting the children's activities indoors and not letting them go outside. However, this will only make the children less adaptable to the winter climate, making them more prone to repeated illnesses.

Here I would like to remind parents not to raise their children like flowers in a greenhouse. The weaker you do this, the worse the child's constitution will be. On the contrary, if you let the child go out more at appropriate times, it will enhance their ability to withstand the cold winter. "Practice in the coldest days," winter is precisely the good season for children to engage in physical activities. Only by continuing to exercise can children become healthier.

Exercising outdoors in winter can promote normal development and healthy growth in children. Winter days are short and nights are long, and the sunlight is relatively weak. Engaging in physical exercise allows one to bask in the sun. The ultraviolet rays in sunlight can convert dehydrocholesterol in the human skin into vitamin D3, which regulates the absorption of calcium, phosphorus, and other minerals, making bones strong and teeth firm. This has special significance for children. In addition, outdoor activities not only allow children's skin to synthesize vitamin D, thereby promoting calcium absorption, but also have good effects on the development of muscles, bones, respiratory, and circulatory systems, as well as the entire body's metabolism. It can also increase appetite. Winter, especially after snowfall, has fresh air and natural ice and snow that can be used as toys, making it the best time for children to go out and exercise.

Also, children exercising in winter should be persistent. Many children are afraid of the cold in winter and do not actively participate in exercises. In fact, sticking to winter exercise not only enhances physical fitness, but it is also a great opportunity to exercise a strong will and enhance resistance to the cold.

In winter, choosing running and jumping-based exercises is the best for children, such as skipping rope, playing football, or running. If conditions permit, skiing and skating are also the best winter exercises. If exercising indoors, windows can be opened to let the child contact sunlight and air. If it's too cold, indoor exercises can also be chosen, such as the simplest skipping rope, which has many variations, simple or complex, and can be done anytime. It's easy to learn and particularly suitable for low-temperature seasons as a fitness activity. From an exercise volume perspective, continuous skipping rope for 10 minutes is comparable to jogging for 30 minutes or doing aerobics for 20 minutes, making it a highly effective aerobic exercise. Skipping rope enhances the function of the cardiovascular, respiratory, and nervous systems.

Any movement requires gradual progression, persistence, and proper nutrition and lifestyle. Parents should appropriately increase various nutrients for their children, especially heat supplementation.

If a child has asthma, they should drink some warm water and wear extra clothes before going out. It’s best to wear windproof and waterproof clothing to help keep the child warm. Also, wearing a scarf around the mouth and nose can help them breathe warmer air. Inhaling cold and dry air is not good for them, and they should avoid going out when an asthma attack occurs. If the weather is too cold, walking indoors or elsewhere is also a good method.

Winter morning exercises are best conducted after sunrise because the temperature rises after sunrise, and air pollutants decrease. Additionally, there is the benefit of sunbathing. Adequate, reasonable, and scientific warm-up exercises can improve neuromuscular excitability and prevent discomfort or injury during exercise. Appropriate walking, slow running, and calisthenics can all be organically connected to the exercise program, gradually progressing.

To improve the body's "cold adaptation" ability, the most effective measure is to undergo cold-resistant exercises. The ancient Chinese medical concept of "autumn freeze" also contains the meaning of "cold-resistant exercise."

Do not wear too much clothing during outdoor exercise. Before starting, you can first do some warm-up exercises to stretch muscles and joints, preventing muscle strains and generating heat to resist the cold. Children are not suitable for too intense exercise, such as sprinting or playing soccer, as excessive exercise consumes too much energy and poses risks. Parents can accompany their children in jogging, skipping rope, dribbling balls, or let the child swing on swings, throw sandbags, ride children's bikes, or use community exercise equipment for climbing and crawling activities, paying attention to equipment selection to prevent adult equipment from harming children.

Parents must control the intensity of their children's exercise, avoiding excessive sweating. Exercise is appropriate when the child sweats slightly, has a pulse rate of about 100 beats per minute, and does not feel tired. Excessive sweating not only consumes the child's energy but also easily leads to catching a cold, especially after sweating, hats should not be removed immediately to avoid catching a chill. Some children may experience coughing or even vomiting after intense exercise, mainly due to allergic reactions of the respiratory tract to cold air. At this point, stop exercising immediately, and limit exercise intensity in future activities.

Children should not sit down to rest immediately after exercise. Walk slowly for a while before returning home, avoiding stopping in cold air. After returning home, wash hands and face with warm water. Cold water will cause open pores to suddenly contract, which is detrimental to health. Do not let children drink water immediately when they are breathing heavily; wait until they calm down before drinking water or eating.

Due to the characteristics of winter's natural environment being yin-heavy and yang-declining, cold air can easily damage the body's yang energy. Therefore, children should sleep early and wake up late in winter, maintaining a longer rest period, allowing the body's latent yang energy to remain undisturbed, which is the first thing to note for winter health care.

Engaging in physical exercise in winter is beneficial to health. However, if sports hygiene is ignored, it could harm the body. Therefore, winter sports must pay attention to sports hygiene.

Never forget to do warm-up exercises before exercising. In cold conditions, human muscles stiffen, and joint flexibility decreases, making it easy to strain muscles or injure joints.

Exercise intensity must be properly arranged, especially the speed of running should gradually increase from slow to fast, and should not start with intense running, as this could cause dizziness, nausea, and other adverse reactions. The amount of exercise should vary according to the individual, gradually increasing, especially for children, the exercise intensity should not be too high, and the running distance should not be too long.

Winter exercise also needs to pay attention to breathing methods. People usually breathe through their noses because cold air filtered through the nose can not only maintain cleanliness and humidity but also increase temperature, protecting the respiratory system. However, during intense exercise, using only the nose to breathe affects lung ventilation. To increase lung ventilation, exercise should involve simultaneous nasal and oral breathing. However, when cold air and dust directly stimulate the throat and trachea mucosa, it can cause coughing, even leading to colds and respiratory inflammation.

In low-temperature environments, if necessary cold prevention measures are lacking, limbs may get frostbite. The most common occurrence is localized chilblains. Chilblains often occur on fingers, toes, backs of hands, heels, earlobes, and tips of noses. These areas are at the ends of the limbs, with slow blood circulation and frequent exposure, so the local temperature differs greatly from the body's core temperature, easily causing tissue damage due to cold stimulation.

Outdoor sports in winter, without proper cold prevention, or in windy, damp environments, or with weak constitutions and less limb activity, can lead to chilblains. Even in early winter, before the "coldest days," some people are already troubled by chilblains, affecting normal limb activities.

When chilblains occur, the cause should be identified to take targeted preventive measures. If a child develops chilblains, parents should not roast or soak the limbs in hot water, nor rub them with snow or cold water. Doing so could worsen local injuries, especially causing small vessel ruptures, affecting local blood circulation. Generally, when no ulcers appear, apply chilblain ointment or petroleum jelly and gently massage with fingers to accelerate local blood circulation. Simultaneously, boil water with chili peppers, mugwort, and eggplant stems, wait for the boiling water to cool down to near body temperature, then soak the frostbitten limbs, once or twice daily, each time for about 10 minutes. If blisters or ulcers appear on the skin, disinfect the wound, puncture the blister, apply gentian violet or anti-inflammatory ointment, then bandage it properly.

To prevent chilblains, it is essential to stay warm during outdoor sports in winter, especially in areas prone to chilblains. Begin by dressing warmly, wearing caps, gloves, ear muffs, etc. As the body warms up after a certain period of exercise, reduce clothing gradually. If sweat wets the underwear during exercise, change it afterward to maintain cleanliness and dryness.

The main features of winter climate are low temperatures, dryness, and windiness. If the temperature drops drastically and the wind intensifies suddenly, outdoor exercise should be avoided. Similarly, foggy days are unsuitable for exercise. Fog forms when ground-level water vapor cools and condenses with airborne dust particles, forming opaque droplets floating near the ground. During foggy weather, the air contains more moisture and dust, the atmospheric pressure is lower, breathing becomes difficult, and sweat doesn't evaporate easily. Therefore, strenuous exercise should be avoided on foggy days.
