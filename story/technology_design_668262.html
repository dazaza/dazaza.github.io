Low-field MR in the clinical application of three-dimensional contrast-enhanced magnetic resonance angiography (3D CE-MRA) for diagnosing renal artery lesions with reconstruction using maximum intensity projection (MIP) and multi-planar reconstruction (MPR):

1.3 Image Quality Rating: The quality of three-dimensional reconstructed images is rated as excellent, good, or poor. Excellent shows a strong signal from the superior renal artery without artifacts or overlap with the renal vein; Good generally shows a strong signal for the renal artery, with a small number of artifacts and partial overlap with the renal vein shadow; Low-quality images show a clear difference in the renal artery with low signal artifacts and overlap with the renal vein shadow, which cannot meet diagnostic requirements.

2 Results: In 23 patients, CE-MRA showed clear image quality reaching excellence, meeting clinical diagnosis needs. It matched the clinical and DSA diagnosis at 96%. Four cases involved kidney transplants, where 10 renal arteries were consistent with clinical surgery findings. Among patients who underwent DSA, in 15 cases involving 35 renal arteries, CE-MRA showed normal intrarenal branches in one patient with unilateral renal artery stenosis during DSA examination. CE-MRA detected mild stenosis (1% to 49%) in 4 out of 5 patients and moderate stenosis (50% to 99%) consistent with DSA findings.

3 Discussion: For kidney transplantation, assessing the donor's renal vascular system is crucial. First, determine the normal kidney and assess the number, length, and location of renal arteries. Secondary hypertension, although only accounting for 3% to 5% of all hypertension cases, has renal artery stenosis as its most important cause. Renal artery angioplasty, stent implantation, and vascular surgery can completely cure it. Thus, clinically, timely and accurate depiction of the renal artery and its causes is essential for diagnosis. Digital subtraction angiography (DSA), considered the gold standard, clearly shows blood vessels and causes of diagnosis. However, its high cost, surgical trauma, X-ray radiation, and toxic side effects of iodine preparations make it less acceptable to patients. Enhanced spiral CT renal artery angiography offers clearer imaging than low-field MR but shares shortcomings like X-ray radiation and toxic iodine preparation effects. Previously reported enhanced MR renal artery angiography used high-field strength and high gradient switching rates. There are no similar reports on diagnosing renal artery causes using low-field MR with continuously improving gradient field strength and switching rates along with various software applications. Low-field MR 3DCE-MRA can satisfy the imaging technology requirements for low-field MR renal arteries... I believe that:

(1) Injection speed: In low-field MR, most use high-pressure syringes. When manually injecting 30ml of contrast agent, it should be completed within 10 seconds to help form high concentrations of contrast agents in blood vessels within a short period, which is the key to successful low-field 3DCE-MRA renal artery angiography.

(2) Scan timing and breath-hold time: In low-field GE signa profile 3DCE-MRA, K-space cyclic symmetry fills the signal acquisition, starting the scan at half the time, lasting 3J, 19s. The peak of the renal artery occurs between 20-25 seconds, with the blood vessel signal acquisition at the center of K-space aligning with the peak of the renal artery for the clearest display. The optimal scan time should be 20-25S + injection time / 2 - 19S after injection, i.e., 6-11S post-injection. The renal artery is mostly horizontally positioned, susceptible to respiratory motion artifacts. Artifacts appear before the K-space center signal acquisition due to breathing. Try holding your breath for 20 seconds to make the image clearer. Among this group of patients, four cases underwent renal vascular and surgical procedures, with only one case showing a normal renal artery and unable to display the narrow branch of the renal blood vessels. Therefore, compared with DSA, CE-MRA shows fewer small vascular lesion details, a view shared by most scholars. Images from this group of patients have reached superior quality, with high-resolution MIP three-dimensional vascular displays allowing observation at any angle and rotation. MPR eliminates structural overlaps, expands distorted vascular strokes, and makes lesions less likely to be missed. This method is simple, noninvasive, inexpensive, free of radiation, side effects, and more acceptable to patients, thus fostering better doctor-patient relationships.
