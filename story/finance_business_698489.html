Youku and Dangdang trigger a surge in listings, but blindly going public is a tragedy.

by fkfykpllj on 2012-03-09 09:44:30

Youku and Dangdang have been listed in the US. Youku's issue price was $12.8, closed at $33.44, increased by 161%, while Dangdang's issue price was $16, closed at $29.91. Online customer service, it increased by 87%. On the first day of listing, the increase was gratifying. It is estimated that Mecox Lane is quite conflicted at this time. Dangdang's move seems to be speaking to Mecox Lane: doing B2C e-commerce, if you want to be the leader, you're too young! No matter what, let's celebrate Youku and Dangdang first! But facing the wave of listings triggered by this move, I would like to share some personal ideas. Although Youku and Dangdang have both been listed, the pressure and challenges continue. For Youku, its revenue growth situation is gratifying compared to before, but the website has not yet made any profit. How to establish an effective profit model still needs to be explored. Fake high-definition has always been a criticism of Youku. Now through the listing, getting more market funds, it is hoped that the funds can be effectively used to solve the video high-definition problem. Youku's listing, to a certain extent, lets the capital market confirm the development and improvement of the video industry, but the chaotic situation of the industry has not stopped, market competition is still fierce, and industry development still needs to be standardized. Currently, Feng暴 Video is also ready for listing, which has caused quite a bit of pressure on Youku. As for Dangdang's listing, it could be said to be very timely. On one hand, after more than ten years of accumulation, from logistics to supply chain, they have developed relatively maturely. On the other hand, facing Mecox Lane's listing, Dangdang has also given its own response. However, Dangdang should pay attention to Mecox Lane's stock market turmoil. On the day of the listing, Mecox Lane was widely favored as the first B2C stock, but the subsequent turmoil has caused it to plummet in the stock market. Therefore, Dangdang still needs to work hard and continue to build up its B2C e-commerce foundation, not repeating Mecox Lane's turmoil, the pressure on Dangdang is still considerable.

Mecox Lane's stock price crash sounds the alarm for listings. On December 1st, Mecox Lane's stock price crashed, breaking below the issue price, surprising investors greatly. The next day, Mecox Lane CEO Gu Beichun stated: "The company's operations are all normal, with no changes. Just that the capital market and operations are two different things." It is evident that the capital market and operations are indeed two different things, and whether the company is prepared enough for the listing is very important. Although Mecox Lane encountered stock market turmoil, Gu Beichun does not think so: "As enterprise operators, we will look lightly on the recent performance of the stock price, focusing on the long-term development of the enterprise." We hope Mecox Lane can develop well. However, was Mecox Lane excessively packaged before the listing? Was it just choosing the best time to list? This has always been everyone's doubt. Only Mecox Lane knows, as an outsider, we can only wait for time to test. Mecox Lane's stock market turmoil should sound the alarm for listings, such as Youku, Dangdang, and other domestic internet companies planning to go public, need to weigh their current situation and strength, not listing for the sake of listing.

Why do companies list? Facing this wave of listings, we can't help but ask, why do companies list? Nowadays, more and more companies are planning their listing plans, putting listings on the agenda. Of course, listing can bring huge wealth to shareholders, increase the value of the company, reduce the cost of capital, and increase opportunities for market expansion. However, listing requires companies to have sufficient awareness of the capital market, understand the capital structure of the company, and know the impact of financial systems on the value of the company. Besides, how is the development status of the stock market, how can companies participate in capital operations, etc., these series of problems need to be resolved. It is well known that Dangdang is a famous "husband-wife store", with a strong desire for control by the couple. Now that Dangdang is listed, whether the couple can handle Dangdang after the listing remains uncertain. Many people believe that "IPO may bring the effect of being popular among the public", but the popularity effect does not necessarily mean profit growth. Although Dangdang made a profit of $16 million in the first nine months of 2010, increasing significantly compared to the same period last year, what kind of development Dangdang will have next still awaits market testing. Facing the wave of listings, we should consider why companies list?

Do good companies have to list? I would like to ask: "Do good companies have to list?" Not necessarily. There is no denying that listing can make companies more publicized, market-oriented, and socialized, able to absorb large amounts of capital for the development and further expansion of the company. But from another perspective, listing means selling the company, selling the future of the company. If the company needs a large amount of capital to promote further development, and there is such a demand, then it can be, listing is a pretty good way. But if you don't know how to use the money after you get it, then why are you listing? If your company is currently developing in an orderly and healthy manner, following market rules and developing steadily, then why pursue listing?

Of course, it is not to say that listing represents unstable company development. Not listing does not necessarily indicate that the company is unprosperous, and listing does not necessarily mean that the company is developing well.

Blindly pursuing listing is not advisable. Famous economist Lang Xianping said, "The inexplicable pursuit and admiration for listing is a tragedy of a nation." "A listed company is restless, and does not think about continuous improvement." Sina CEO Zhang Chaoyang believes: going public in the US is the overall sadness of China's Internet, in order to follow the investor's baton, these companies are busy with income, looking at stock prices, often neglecting long-term benefits and missing many opportunities, and ignoring the real needs of users. Take a simple example: Shenzhen Wandelei Communication Technology Co., Ltd., everyone should remember this former "cordless phone king". The company was once a key high-tech company of the national Torch Program, and one of the first enterprises recommended by Shenzhen to the CSRC for listing application on the Growth Enterprise Market. However, it was precisely such a company that went bankrupt tragically before the opening of the SME board. Wandelei's mistake lies in blindly pursuing listing, disrupting the company's long-term development plan and seriously violating the development laws of the enterprise.

China's Internet industry has experienced three waves of listings. Today, Youku and Dangdang's listings will trigger a new wave of listings. In the coming days, more Internet companies will IPO in the US. Here, I only hope that those companies preparing for listing do not blindly follow trends and do not list for the sake of listing.
