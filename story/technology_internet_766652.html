20 Quotes from Amazon CEO Jeff Bezos

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 21:11:38

　　English Original: The 20 Smartest Things Jeff Bezos Has Ever Said

　　When the world's enthusiasm for a company veers off course, it often leads to arbitrary comments. Amazon has certainly experienced this. Around the year 2000, within a span of 14 months, headlines such as "Amazon founder named Time Magazine’s Person of the Year" and "Analysts worry Amazon is about to go bankrupt" appeared one after another, a rare occurrence in business history.

　　But thirteen years later, Amazon remains vibrant and stable. Its current business is vastly different from its initial book sales. The company’s stock price has now reached three times the peak during the internet bubble period. And today, Amazon owes its success to its eccentric CEO, Jeff Bezos. He pioneered a commercial culture that most business leaders disdain. Below are twenty insightful suggestions from Bezos on commerce.

　　1. All companies need to stay youthful. If your customer base ages with you, you'll end up like Woolworth's.

　　2. There are two kinds of companies in the world: those always thinking about how to raise prices, and those always thinking about lowering them. We're the latter.

　　3. Helping customers make money is our opportunity.

　　4. If you only do things where the answers are right under your nose, your company is doomed.

　　5. For eighteen years, three concepts have been consistently upheld at Amazon: customer obsession; innovation; patience.

　　6. I'm often asked: "What will change in the next ten years?" It's a common and interesting question. But I rarely hear this question: "What will remain constant over the next ten years?" However, I urge you to consider the second question. Because over time, you can build a consistent corporate strategy around these constants. In retail, what customers want is lower prices; efficient shipping; and more choices. Finding out who our customers will be in ten years and knowing exactly what they will want then is impossible. “Jeff, I love Amazon; please raise the prices,” or “I love Amazon, but slower shipping.” Unthinkable. Therefore, the efforts we put in today will still benefit our customers in ten years. If you find this constant truth in business, it's worth investing effort over the long term.

　　7. If you’re flexible enough, you’ll abandon certain experiments when the time is right. If you're too rigid, you might easily miss out on good solutions to problems because you're stuck on one idea.

　　8. Any first-time business plan is bound to hit obstacles when implemented. Reality always differs from the plan. Reality is reality, and plans are just plans.

　　9. In the past, you had to spend 30% of your time on service and 70% on promotion. Now, the situation is reversed.

　　10. We've conducted price elasticity studies, and the results always suggest we should raise prices. But we don't do that because we believe maintaining low prices wins us customer trust, which maximizes cash flow in the long run.

　　11. I’ve established a very simple decision-making mechanism (starting in 1994), which I call the "regret minimization framework." When I look back on my life at 80, I hope there will be few regrets. I’m confident that at 80, I won’t regret trying the things I’m doing now. I won’t regret being involved in the amazing internet industry. Even if I fail, I won’t regret it. What would torment me daily is not having tried.

　　12. Our innovations always start with customer needs, and customer needs are always the touchstone for renewal.

　　13. While taking a morning shower, our competitors think about how to surpass others and become number one, while we focus on innovating from the customer's perspective.

　　14. A company shouldn’t get drunk on its own glory, because glory often fades quickly.

　　15. I believe frugality drives innovation, just as other constraints do. One way to break through innovation is to forge a new path.

　　16. If you double your experiments, corresponding innovative results will also double.

　　17. If you fear criticism, you'll never innovate.

　　18. If you act based on long-term goals, the interests of customers and shareholders are actually aligned.

　　19. Understanding innovation takes time. You need to have faith in what you're doing, but eventually, some well-meaning people may criticize what you're doing, and at that point, you should ask yourself, "Are they right?" If they are, you need to adjust accordingly. If not, you need patience for their understanding. This is inevitable with innovation.

　　20. You need to moderately pay attention to other companies' moves. Avoiding isolation is crucial. Paying attention to other companies isn't about copying what they do, but rather asking, "What inspiration does this give us?" Then you need to create something new of your own.
