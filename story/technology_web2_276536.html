Wubi word root: a method that beginners of Wubi should master

by henguai on 2009-11-25 01:35:28

If you are a beginner learning the Wubi input method, or if you have already basically mastered its usage but cannot improve your speed to a satisfactory level, then please carefully read this post. This is my own experience from learning Wubi over the past ten years, using Wubi, and teaching it. I believe it will greatly help with your Wubi typing skills and boost your confidence.

**Confidence Section**

Let's start by boosting your confidence. Any kind of learning should be targeted, meaning you need to first know what you want to use, and then learn based on your needs. To become a qualified or even excellent Wubi user and proficient in using Wubi, how many characters should you focus on practicing? What specific characters should you practice?

Any language with a long history and cultural tradition usually has a large vocabulary. English and Russian each have millions of words. However, Shakespeare and Pushkin only used more than ten thousand words. I don't know how many Chinese characters there are, but it is said that there are one million in total. GB2312-80 includes 6763 characters, divided into two levels: Level One consists of 3755 commonly used characters, and Level Two consists of 3008 less commonly used characters. The usage frequency of Level One characters totals 99.7%, meaning that in a ten-thousand-character article, 9970 characters belong to the Level One common characters. Among these, 2475 characters have a usage rate of 99%, 1375 characters reach 95%, 950 characters reach 90%, 560 characters reach 80%, 365 characters reach 70%, 244 characters reach 60%, 163 characters reach 50%, 104 characters reach 40%, 59 characters reach 30%, 28 characters reach 30%, 8 characters reach 10%, and the Wubi radicals.

From the above introduction and statistical data, we can see that to quickly master Wubi and achieve a high typing speed, mastering the 1375 most frequently used characters is the goal. These characters appear at a frequency of 95% in general articles. In practical operations, if 95% of the characters are smoothly typed, then you can achieve a relatively fast speed and accuracy. Typing eighty to one hundred characters per minute? That would only be considered barely passing. Among the last batch of students I taught, one person had an ordinary typing speed of between 130 to 180 characters per minute in daily work, and during tests, they exceeded 200 characters per minute several times. Of course, this requires extraordinary practice, memorizing a large number of four-character or longer word inputs, and more importantly, having a certain amount of natural talent. However, typing 100 to 120 characters per minute is just a very normal speed, a level that ordinary people can reach.

How about it? Are you confident now? 1375 characters isn't actually that many.

**Misconception Section**

The worst thing when learning a skill is falling into a misconception. Not only does it not help with learning, but worse still, it severely damages a person's interest and confidence in learning, ultimately leading to giving up.

Wubi learning, of course, also has its misconceptions. First, let's talk about the learning of radicals. Specifically, do you need to memorize the radicals, and how should you memorize them?

I just counted the radical table, and the 86 version of Wubi actually has two hundred radicals (I didn’t count it twice, so there might be a difference of one or two). I want to clarify that you must distinguish between radicals and the radical chart. Mr. Wang Yongmin, while inventing Wubi, created a mnemonic rhyme for radicals to help learners remember. Ironically, this rhyme actually caused trouble for beginners. The purpose of the rhyme is to make you aware of which radicals are included under a particular key. However, Wubi users all know that the thinking process in using Wubi is: character - radical - keystroke. What we practice is knowing which key a radical is on when we see it, not knowing which radicals are included under a particular key. Therefore, I want to tell everyone that you should not memorize the radical chart or that ridiculous rhyme. Instead, reverse the process: memorize the key positions through the radicals, and combine this step with actual practice, allowing your brain's memory to gradually transform into muscle memory in your fingers, rather than forcing yourself to memorize it in a study room. As for how to practice, I will mention it below.

The second misconception is about key names and the twenty-six English letters. I have encountered many people who already have a good foundation in typing English letters, but this actually doesn’t help much in the early stages of learning Wubi, and may even cause some trouble. Because they can never forget the positions of the English letters, when hitting the radical keys, they always use English letters as a medium. That is, their first reaction upon seeing a radical is not to hit which key, but to determine which English letter the radical belongs to, and then make the key-hitting reaction. This slows down the speed and increases mental effort. Therefore, in the early stages of learning Wubi, you should completely separate the English letter keys from the Wubi key names. Moreover, the twenty-five key positions of Wubi themselves have their own names: gold, person, moon, white, grain, speech, stand, water, fire, etc., all of these are names and radicals themselves.
