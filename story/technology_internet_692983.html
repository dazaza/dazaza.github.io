Mainland pregnant women arrested for using false documents to give birth in Hong Kong

by lovei0c1 on 2012-03-06 13:49:58

Nanfang Daily News: Reporter Wei He reporting from Hong Kong. Starting on the 1st day of this month (last Thursday), Hong Kong police conducted two consecutive days of arrests at Kwong Wah Hospital in Kowloon Bay, detaining six people suspected of conspiring to forge and use false documents to give birth in Hong Kong. This included three mainland pregnant women and two intermediaries. Four of them appeared in court today at the Kowloon Bay Magistrates' Court. Hong Kong's Secretary for Food and Health, Yorkin Monday, stated that the Hong Kong government would use various channels and methods to crack down on such activities, especially targeting intermediaries.

The attempt to use forged documents for booking delivery services was discovered.

The Hong Kong government has been tightening the quota for non-resident pregnant women coming to Hong Kong to give birth due to a shortage of beds. Some intermediaries and non-resident pregnant women have altered medical certificates to come to Hong Kong for childbirth. Last week, the Hong Kong police solved two cases within two days at Kwong Wah Hospital (a public hospital) involving fraudulent reservation of delivery services, arresting one man and five women (including one Hong Kong resident).

According to Hong Kong media reports, a mainland couple attempted to book delivery services at Kwong Wah Hospital last Thursday. Staff noticed suspicious medical certification documents and suspected that they were using fabricated pregnancy dates on "fake doctor's certificates" to fraudulently obtain public hospital delivery services, prompting an alarm to be raised. The police arrested the man and woman at the hospital and, after investigation, detained another mainland woman and a Hong Kong woman suspected of providing the couple with false medical certification documents. The four detainees, aged between 24 and 43 years old, were charged with conspiracy to use false instruments and appeared in court today at the Kowloon Bay Magistrates' Court.

Additionally, at the same hospital, on Friday last week, two mainland women aged 38 and 48 holding double-entry permits attempted to book delivery services using false medical certification documents. Both were arrested on suspicion of conspiring to obtain services through deception and are currently being held for investigation.

The Hong Kong government is strictly cracking down on related activities.

Currently, Hong Kong public hospitals reserve a certain number of slots each month starting on the first day for non-resident pregnant women to book deliveries, but the limit depends on the number of available beds each month. A spokesperson for Kwong Wah Hospital stated that they will fully cooperate with the police investigation and reminded registration staff to pay special attention when checking required documents to ensure services are provided to those truly in need, prioritizing local pregnant women.

Regarding the use of false medical certification documents to book delivery services in Hong Kong, Hong Kong's Secretary for Food and Health, Yorkin Monday, said that the government will definitely take measures to combat it. Yorkin Monday said that the Hong Kong government will use different channels and methods to crack down on these activities. The government is now studying ways to combat intermediaries through different departments, and border control and hospitals will refer suspicious cases to the police for follow-up. Relevant departments will verify the authenticity of registration information, and once any falsification is found, it will be handed over to the police for handling. The Home Affairs Department will also strengthen inspections of "maternity apartments" where mainland pregnant women reside.

Illegal actions

Some intermediaries and non-resident pregnant women jointly forge or alter the expected delivery date on doctors' certificates to give birth in Hong Kong. Hospitals have tightened verification of relevant documents for non-resident pregnant women booking deliveries, requiring not only a doctor's certificate but also fetal ultrasound photos to confirm the expected delivery date. However, this time the police discovered that the expected delivery date listed on the fetal ultrasound photos had also been altered.

Recent focus

The Hong Kong Private Hospital Association publicly stated that it hopes the number of non-local pregnant women admitted next year will remain the same as this year, at 31,000, pointing out that all hospitals have increased manpower and equipment.

However, Hong Kong government officials said they can understand the situation of private hospitals doing business, but must consider the overall interests of Hong Kong society, and the number of non-local pregnant women admitted must be reduced.
