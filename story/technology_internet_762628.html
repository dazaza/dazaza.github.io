Dianping.com: A Bone Too Big for the Giants to Chew, Living Outside the Comfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 16:27:13

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, the CEO of Dianping.com, has once again postponed the company's IPO.

In 2011, Dianping secured a $100 million Series C financing round from Sequoia Capital and others, at which point its valuation had already reached $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an appropriate time to go public within the next one to two years, but it didn’t. Instead, in 2012, it conducted another $60 million Series D financing round.

Given Dianping's scale and development momentum, going public wouldn't be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that an IPO now would be too costly. He breaks down the large internet (PC + mobile) into four elements - people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point presents an opportunity for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is the seat for the fourth giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating resources for this very purpose, finally waiting until the explosion of mobile internet when smartphones bridged "location" and "services." Following Zhang Tao’s logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the imagination of the fourth giant.

However, the outside world doesn’t see it this way. In recent months, with frequent acquisition strategies from BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a presumed acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; regardless of which family name among BAT it carries, it could help them achieve great success early on. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it's still too early to predict whether it can become a giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after acquiring Gaode and Meituan, all are directly targeting Dianping's territory – local life services focused on food, drink, entertainment, etc.

Before proving the following two points, Dianping's IPO would equal a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs some time to prove that what Dianping does now, BAT cannot replicate; second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top position, its valuation could increase multiple times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Regarding speculation and rumors, Zhang Tao remains deaf to them. He is clear: "The internet has indeed entered a great era of integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type possesses platform attributes but is more suitable to develop within a giant's system, such as maps and emails. These products can easily attract a large number of users but struggle to find independent business models. Some, like browsers, may make money, but the revenue earned through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money maintaining email products because they help with branding and user stickiness, but there hasn’t been any successful standalone email company." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type is companies encountering financial difficulties who sell themselves to survive.

Now Meituan has been recruited under Alibaba's wing. Theoretically, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated due to rapid expansion fueled by capital. However, Zhang Tao says, "The giants' assistance to us is limited. We do need users, but execution is even more important. Mergers and acquisitions would decrease execution efficiency and risk marginalization by larger companies." Not only that, he also wants Dianping to maintain the most precise "distance" from the giants. If it gets too close to any one of them, it would be equivalent to "taking sides," making it an enemy of the others.

Thus, logically, the acquisition of Dianping ends up in a deadlock. Zhang Tao counters, "Why should we take their money? We don’t lack funds; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to invest a small amount of shares and provide us with many resources, I can consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they own only a small percentage of shares?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for companies doing purely internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities come from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that life services mainly revolve around eating, drinking, and entertainment. Other niche markets, such as ride-hailing, can form good business models, but it's hard to imagine them growing into giants.

For dining, drinking, and entertainment, user needs are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, newcomers find it hard to surpass.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Now group buying has entered the championship match, and Dianping plans to face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as there are two strong players coexisting, the industry's gross margin will be pulled down, making stable profits impossible.

A foreseeable future includes reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but they must be built on the foundation of Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, feedback from users and merchants is good, but if we were to focus solely on reservations, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services offer some even "heavier" opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid demand for users and helps merchants increase sales. In the past couple of years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet Society" have thrived. However, the core of food delivery is distribution – delivering within a short time frame while solving issues like soup spilling. The logistics requirements are higher than those for fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If food delivery is executed well, there's potential, but it's harder dirty and hard work, and Dianping is already doing enough dirty and hard work."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has worked hard to accumulate merchant data and user reviews, which Zhang Tao calls "dirty and hard work." This primitive growth model once restricted its speed, but past hardships have become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the life services sector.

Still, he must remain vigilant against BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, and users can use the map to find nearby dining and other life services. Tencent, believed to have secured a ticket to mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and expanding goods from physical items to services is natural. Moreover, it has included Meituan, Gaode, and others in its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there hasn't been an explosive growth in either reality or data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to eat, drink, and entertain.

Moreover, in the vast field of life services, competition among giants is inevitably cross-sectoral, and no one can completely monopolize the area. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that this makes Dianping safer instead. "In niche areas like car rentals and business travel, if giants redirect their massive users, they could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which invested in Gaode, invading Dianping's territory isn't so simple. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized no matter how peculiar. A user's experience of a meal depends on factors such as the day's environment, waitstaff, and chef. Managing merchants can't be done using systems and tools like Alibaba; it requires building a 'ground force' managed by humans.

"The three giants all want to follow this path, and there's room for imagination, but it's not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing life services requires ground teams deployed across various levels, something BAT lacks the genes and ability to manage offline teams. Second, they lack the willingness; life services require focused effort, not just sending out a small team, and making money is slow. "It's hard for a company to simultaneously excel at two important things, let alone consolidate their advantage in their original fields."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Are Easier to Maneuver

In 2011, when Dianping secured $100 million in funding, Zhang Tao told the outside world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, the world changes fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogies fell from grace.

He wasn't overly worried about this. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they all want to be America's Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang Tao has gradually explored. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, like Americans, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform, offering one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized that group buying had transactional attributes. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a life service trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used for Dianping's transformation from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this transformation, Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth seat among giants today. But saying it is easy; implementing it is akin to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, an extremely painful process.

Firstly, compared to selling advertisements separately, the gross margin of a trading platform decreases, requiring stricter cost control and higher efficiency. Secondly, a trading platform demands stronger cross-department communication capabilities.

Previously, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and the sales team were entirely separated. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments – even for important clients, sales couldn't modify platform information for commercial purposes, leading to mutual distrust between content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication to craft effective marketing plans, which require planning and editorial cooperation. He said, "Maintaining information quality is still crucial, but there's no need to reinforce hostility; the firewall must be dismantled."

Though editors may logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and combined across the entire company, the inertia is greater. To address this, Zhang Tao held several meetings to encourage interdepartmental communication. Months later, an editor supervisor casually told a sales supervisor, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needs transformation. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team dual identities. First, for merchants, salespeople must become marketing consultants, not only selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants act as procurement agents. Dianping is like a supermarket; products must be of good quality, and varieties must be diverse. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Naturally, dual identities will inevitably generate complex issues. Frontline personnel find this challenging as they must balance the scales themselves.

In essence, Dianping's "dual identity" is quite similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to the frontlines allows for faster response times but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffers more during the transformation.

Each year, Zhang Tao decomposes Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, then senior executives further break them down layer by layer. However, breaking down isn't simple arithmetic. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds unresolved merchant demands are due to sales issues, affecting sales performance. Therefore, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists to gather information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being adept at embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change, and everyone feels uncomfortable when encountering change. All employees must go through discomfort zones, including me."

The numbers reflect the "weight" of Dianping's execution team. Of the current 3,000+ employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground force directly communicates with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group-buying products, and combination marketing solutions.

For this core team resisting BAT's clutches, Dianping has a unique management evaluation system.

They aren't called "salespeople"; from the first day, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the era of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging at least three to five years behind Taobao merchants in terms of internet knowledge. Marketing planning consultants must inform merchants of online user needs and guide them in internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing advisors wouldn't recommend group-buying abalone or shark fin dishes; perhaps they'd suggest other dishes, as online users differ from offline users."

An excellent marketing advisor must be a versatile player. They must explain to restaurant owners or hair salon owners the essential differences between discounts and group-buying; among various discounts, which aligns better with their positioning. If facing a hotpot restaurant owner, the marketing advisor must know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise during certain seasons, and which fall, proposing a group-buying menu liked by both merchants and users.

This places high demands on marketing advisors, requiring them not only to be internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every advisor to be a double expert, so we've set star ratings." Similar to hotels, marketing advisors range from one-star to five-star, with the best being called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline workers aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making understanding internet products difficult. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing advisors, containing knowledge points and relevant cases, accessible to both new and old employees. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, where thousands of consultants nationwide will assist in answering.

Many knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experience. Dianping has established a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, directly linking results to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to obtain than internet knowledge. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after decades of experience in the industry. A Super Star marketing advisor told Li Jing that the beauty industry should avoid group-buying packages for washing, cutting, and blow-drying, as men lack motivation to buy, and women prefer blow-drying without washing or cutting together. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing advisors are not only divided by cities but also by industries such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hotpot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing advisor responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing advisors can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, ensuring merchants won't resist when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what challenges a particular type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, which company products are used in the solution, and the outcome. Outstanding cases are reported upward, and Dianping officially shares one case weekly.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition – Entrepreneurial Winner. Within the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If responsible for a hotpot restaurant, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hotpot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees present their defense on-site, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, with the company providing rewards.

Strategically speaking, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, the tasks ahead are quite similar. Now, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with basic industry knowledge, and the words spoken are almost identical. "The era when success could be achieved merely by storytelling or concept creation has passed. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
