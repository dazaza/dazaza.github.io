New battery activation, charge for 12-14 hours, correction of misunderstanding.

by james997 on 2007-02-03 00:12:52

1. Basic Concepts of Lithium-ion Batteries:

1. The nominal voltage of a lithium-ion battery is 3.7V (or 3.6V), and the charging cut-off voltage is 4.2V (or 4.1V, depending on the brand of the cell design). (The formal term for lithium-ion cells is: lithium-ion secondary battery.)

2. Charging Requirements for Lithium-ion Batteries (as per GB/T18287-2000 standard): First, constant current charging is applied, meaning the current remains fixed while the battery voltage gradually increases during the charging process. When the battery terminal voltage reaches 4.2V (or 4.1V), constant current charging switches to constant voltage charging. In this mode, the voltage stays constant, but the current decreases gradually as the cell becomes more saturated during the charging process. Charging is considered complete when the current drops to 0.01C.

(C is a way to express current in relation to the nominal capacity of the battery. For example, if the battery has a capacity of 1000mAh, 1C means a charging current of 1000mA. Note that it's mA, not mAh, so 0.01C would be 10mA. To simplify, I use 0.01C here instead of the formal notation 0.01C5A.)

3. Why is 0.01C considered the end of charging: This is specified by the national standard GB/T18287-2000, which was determined through discussion. Previously, most people used 20mA as the endpoint, as stipulated by the postal telecommunications industry standard YD/T998-1999. That meant, regardless of the battery’s capacity, the stopping current was always 20mA. The national standard's specification of 0.01C helps achieve a fuller charge, which is beneficial for manufacturers during certification. Additionally, the national standard specifies that the charging time should not exceed 8 hours, meaning that even if 0.01C has not been reached, after 8 hours, charging is considered complete. (For batteries with no quality issues, 0.01C should be achieved within 8 hours; for poor-quality batteries, waiting longer serves no purpose.)

4. How to distinguish between 4.1V and 4.2V batteries: Consumers cannot tell the difference; this must be checked in the product specifications provided by the cell manufacturer. Some brands produce cells that are compatible with both 4.1V and 4.2V, such as A&TB (Toshiba). Most domestic manufacturers produce 4.2V cells, though there are exceptions, like Tianjin Lishen, which produces 4.1V cells (though they now also follow the 4.2V standard).

5. What happens if a 4.1V cell is charged to 4.2V: It will increase the battery capacity, making it feel more efficient with longer standby times, but it will reduce the battery's lifespan. For example, a battery originally rated for 500 cycles may only last 300 cycles. Similarly, overcharging a 4.2V cell will also shorten its lifespan. Lithium-ion cells are quite delicate.
