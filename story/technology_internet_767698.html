The 5-Person Backbone Group at GitHub: How Early-Stage Startups Can Hire the Right People

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 12:38:44

The first 5 years are crucial for start-ups. According to data from the U.S. Small Business Administration, about 1/4 of start-ups close down within the first year, and less than half of the businesses survive to the 5th year. For GitHub, which was founded in 2008, this year is undoubtedly a milestone.

GitHub believes in the power of teams, especially the top 5 veteran members who are still with the company today. GitHub CEO Tom Preston-Werner said: "You must find people who are better than you in certain areas." While he and other founders were exhausting their efforts on the growth of the company, he quickly realized that many things were beyond their capabilities. It was time "to seek new skills for tasks that must be completed."

GibHub did not blindly recruit several excellent developers for the heavy programming work. Instead, they carefully built a core team of five people covering multiple fields from a strategic and future-oriented perspective. The skills of these five people include design, customer support, and human resources, among others.

No.1 Expert Scott Chacon

If you doubt the validity of social media in helping you land your dream job, then Scott Chacon's example will surely alleviate some of your concerns. Chacon met Preston-Werner at a gathering called Ruby on Rails, and they hit it off immediately.

Preston-Werner said: "I instantly believed that Git had the potential to become a very popular and cool product." He also clearly understood that for most people, Git was still quite complex, and it needed to become simple and easy to use.

Through this meeting, Preston-Werner discovered that Chacon not only knew Git inside out, but the first book about Git was also written by Chacon. Chacon's insights were legendary in the developer community. Such a person was naturally highly sought after. Unfortunately, as a start-up just getting off the ground, Preston-Werner did not yet have the ability or resources to hire Chacon full-time right away, so Chacon initially worked on the Gist project as a contractor.

Chacon is now GitHub's Chief Information Officer.

No. 2 "The Customer Whisperer" Tekkub

As the company grew, Preston-Werner found that the initial four-person team was overwhelmed by user requests for help. While Preston-Werner tried his best to help people solve problems, he noticed that someone was managing N open-source projects and still had time to help others.

Preston-Werner personally contacted this user named Tekkub (his/her real name is known only to a few founders). Tekkub agreed to help GitHub part-time. Afterward, Tekkub, based in Colorado, remained in remote contact with the company, discussing work details via email and online tools. Tekkub was the first GitHubber located far from the company. Now, 70% of the company's employees work remotely. After working part-time for a year, Tekkub finally met face-to-face with the founders and joined GitHub full-time.

No. 3 Administrative Expert Melissa Severini

After development and customer support were on track, administrative pressures began to mount. Preston-Werner recalled that the team at the time was clueless about administrative management matters, "basically learning on the fly, as long as nothing went wrong."

The company's needs evolved from basic survival to higher-level issues such as legal matters, business trips, and daily office management. Melissa Severini appeared at this time. She was also introduced to Preston-Werner through an insider and already had considerable work experience. What caught Preston-Werner's attention was Severini's ability to "keep a company running smoothly," and she was "very confident; always got things done."

Severini's management style once caused the first tension in the small team when she required everyone to keep receipts. Preston-Werner was initially reluctant to accept this because he thought it contradicted the company's "simplicity above all" philosophy. However, under Severini's persistence, everyone eventually made mutual concessions.

No.4 Design Master Kyle Neath

Preston-Werner was GitHub's first designer, but since his focus shifted to the backend, the product design started to falter. Thus, he began looking for someone who could "think about the design, usage, and appearance of the product every day," because "if the development lacks practicality, the end result will be a bad product."

This time, Preston-Werner used the same approach, seeking designers through his network. They had long admired Kyle Neath from ENTP, a consulting and incubation company, and GitHub was even a client for a support tool designed by Neath. "If you yourself are a user of something TA designed, that speaks volumes."

No.5 Sharp Engineer Ryan Tomayko

Tomayko's name was well-known in the open-source community; he once referred to GitHub as the "Myspace for Hackers," giving the then-beta and revenue-less GitHub greater visibility.

Preston-Werner still holds Tomayko's technical skills in high regard, saying, "He is amazing, and his technical expertise is what I've always dreamed of." However, at the time, Tomayko was employed by Heroku, a cloud application platform, and also had ties with Salesforce. Perston-Werner admitted afterward: "It was indeed a bit risky for him. I guess he believed GitHub would become great."
