What are the basic facts about axillary odor? Axillary odor, also known as body odor, underarm smell, or simply "fox smell," refers to an unpleasant and distinctively foul odor emanating from the armpits and other parts of the body. Although it poses no significant health risks, it can cause discomfort to those around the sufferer, especially during hot summer days when the smell becomes more pronounced, affecting social interactions and causing distress to the patient. Fox odor predominantly affects young adults, with symptoms often lessening or disappearing entirely with age. Many people afflicted by this condition lack a comprehensive understanding of it. Basic Facts About Axillary Odor:

Axillary odor significantly impacts a patient’s social interactions, learning, employment, romantic relationships, and family life. It creates psychological stress, leading to feelings of inferiority and trauma, severely affecting mental health. Most patients with axillary odor have a family history, and the condition is most severe during puberty.

Factors Contributing to Fox Odor:

1. **Genetic Theory**: It is widely believed to be an autosomal dominant genetic trait. If one parent has fox odor, their children have a 50% to 75% chance of inheriting it, and it can pass down through generations regardless of gender. However, in clinical practice, women seem to be affected more than men.

2. **Glandular Scent**: Studies have shown that the sebaceous glands of individuals with fox odor produce a distinctive smell even without bacterial involvement. This indicates that the sweat glands themselves contribute to the odor.

3. **Bacterial Influence**: Research shows that the bacterial composition in the sweat glands of individuals with fox odor differs from those without it. Normal human armpits contain bacteria, but studies found a higher concentration of Corynebacterium species in the armpits of 20 female patients with fox odor—up to 3 to 7 times more than in normal individuals. These bacteria break down fatty acids and nitrogenous substances into compounds that emit strong odors, contributing to the characteristic smell of fox odor.

Why Does Fox Odor Occur More During Puberty?

This phenomenon is closely related to the heightened activity of sex hormones during puberty, which also affect the apocrine glands. As people age, particularly after 40, the intensity of the odor tends to decrease.

Dietary Advice:

Experts suggest avoiding or reducing the intake of strongly stimulating foods like garlic, onions, scallions, and strong tea to alleviate the severity of the odor. Stimulating spicy foods, alcohol, and tobacco interfere with normal physiological functions and hormonal balance, increasing perspiration and unsaturated fatty acids that can stain clothing yellow and leave lingering odors. Alcohol, being highly volatile, exacerbates sweating and worsens fox odor. Individuals with this condition should avoid alcohol to prevent worsening symptoms.
