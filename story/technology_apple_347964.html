Lithium-ion battery

You can also find this industry-leading battery technology on many other devices. The Apple batteries have common characteristics with other devices using lithium technology. These batteries, like other rechargeable batteries, need to be replaced eventually.

Industry-leading technology

Lithium-ion batteries have a higher power density than nickel batteries. Since lithium is the lightest metal, these batteries are lighter and last longer.

You can charge lithium-ion batteries whenever it's convenient for you. Unlike nickel batteries, there is no need to maximize their performance by fully charging or discharging them regularly. (If nickel batteries are charged for too long, the liquid inside will crystallize, preventing you from fully charging them and leading to the inconvenience of complete discharge.)

Standard Charging

Most lithium-ion batteries use a fast charging method to charge the device to 80% of its capacity and then switch to a continuous trickle charging mode to continue charging.

For example, if you charge an iPhone, iPhone 3G, iPhone 3GS, or iPod touch without using it at the same time, it takes about 2 hours to charge it to 80%, and another 2 hours to fully charge the iPhone, iPhone 3G, iPhone 3GS, or iPod touch. You can charge all lithium-ion batteries multiple times according to the charging cycle requirements, but the number of charges is limited.

A charging cycle means using up all the battery power, but it does not equate to charging once. For example,

if you listen to your iPhone, iPhone 3G, iPhone 3GS, or iPod touch for several hours in a day and only use half of the battery, then fully charge it again. If you do the same thing the next day,

it counts as one charging cycle, not two. Therefore, it may take several days to complete a cycle. After each charging cycle, the battery capacity decreases slightly,

even so, before replacing the battery, you can still charge the laptop, iPhone, iPhone 3G, iPhone 3GS, and iPod touch batteries many times.
