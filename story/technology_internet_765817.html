Amazon invests $13.9 billion in three years to expand logistics and storage warehousing

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 13:58:37

According to a Bloomberg report, on the afternoon of August 23 Beijing time, Amazon has spent $13.9 billion over the past three years to expedite the construction of logistics and warehousing centers. This move shows that as competition with eBay and Walmart intensifies, the company is eager to deliver goods more quickly to customers.

Responding to Competitive Threats

Amazon's determination to accelerate warehouse construction is evident. For example, it took only 10 months to complete the construction of the warehouse center in Chattanooga, Tennessee, which was officially launched in 2011, whereas this process previously often required up to two years. This warehouse center has more space and advanced technology, making it easier to find products.

Since 2010, Amazon has invested nearly $13.9 billion in warehousing and logistics, including the construction of 50 new warehouses, one of which is located in Chattanooga. The $13.9 billion expenditure exceeds the total investment Amazon has made in warehousing since its inception. By the end of 2012, Amazon had a total of 89 warehouses. The company also announced plans to build five more warehouses in the U.S. this year.

Dave Clark, Amazon's vice president of global operations and customer service, said in an interview: "We have set uniform standards for our warehouses, which will speed up their activation and expansion." However, this warehouse construction strategy does carry risks. Warehouse logistics costs are the largest item in Amazon's operating expenses, putting pressure on the company's profit margin and contributing significantly to Amazon's $39 million loss last year.

As eBay, Walmart, and other competitors plan to introduce faster delivery models, Amazon CEO Jeff Bezos is under pressure to speed up. Matt Nemer, an analyst at Wells Fargo in San Francisco, said: "Everything Walmart and eBay are doing now is aimed at delivering products to customers faster than Amazon. Their profit margins may not be the highest in the world, but they might be able to deliver products within an hour."

Rapid Increase in Warehousing Costs

Currently, in the rapidly developing e-commerce market, speed is key for any vendor aiming to establish dominance: being able to deliver products to customers within hours of placing an order or to nearby stores. eBay has already launched same-day delivery services in some U.S. cities, while Walmart is gradually increasing its investment in online business. It is estimated that Walmart's sales in the fiscal year 2014 will reach $482 billion, and the company has more than 4700 stores across the U.S., most of which are only a few miles away from customers' homes.

Some of Amazon's competitors either build their own warehousing networks or rely on third-party companies like UPS. For Prime users, Amazon charges $3.99 for same-day and next-day delivery services. This service also includes unlimited two-day delivery for an annual fee of $79. For non-Prime users, the charge for same-day and next-day delivery is $8.99 or more.

So far, investors have mostly responded positively to Bezos' large-scale investments in warehousing and logistics. Since 2010, Amazon's stock price has at least doubled. On Monday, the stock closed at $285.57, up about 1% from the previous trading day. Bloomberg data shows that Amazon's expenditures on warehousing and logistics increased by more than 40% annually from 2010 to 2012, higher than the 24% in 2009.

Squeezing Profit Margins

Warehousing and logistics costs are squeezing Amazon's profit margins. Bloomberg data shows that Amazon's operating profit margin over the past 12 months was only 0.95%, placing it among the bottom 3% of companies in the S&P 500 index in terms of operating profit margin, even though its revenue ranks among the top 10% of companies.

As costs related to warehousing continue to grow, Amazon's profit pressure further increases. Last month, the company said it would hire more than 5,000 full-time employees at 17 U.S. warehouses. Previously, Amazon's total number of employees had already exceeded 20,000. Amazon also stated that the after-sales service department would hire 2,000 new employees, including part-time workers.

Multiple Amazon executives said that increasing investment is necessary given the increasingly competitive environment in the e-commerce industry. Amazon CFO Tom Szkutak said in the earnings call on July 25: "With the continuous strengthening of our delivery capabilities, the company's performance has grown."

Same-Day Delivery Service

Supply chain consulting company MWPVL International believes that if Amazon can establish distribution centers near the country's 20 largest cities, its same-day delivery service could cover 50% of the U.S. population, compared to only 15% currently. The company points out that Amazon needs to add just 12 more warehouses based on its existing and planned ones to achieve this goal.

However, competitors still believe that Amazon's delivery system has flaws. For instance, Instacart, a daily necessities e-commerce company, guarantees delivery within two hours, faster than Amazon's fresh food business "Amazon Fresh" in Seattle and Los Angeles.

Apoorva Mehta, founder and CEO of Instacart, said: "We don't need to build our own warehouses, rent trucks, or manage perishable goods, which greatly reduces our operating costs." He himself used to work at Amazon.

Improving Delivery Efficiency

In 2011, Amazon began accelerating the construction of warehouse space when the company agreed to start collecting online sales taxes in more U.S. states. Previously, Amazon did not establish warehouses in some U.S. states due to issues with physical operations and sales tax collection, which would weaken the company's cost advantage.

Meanwhile, Amazon continued to improve the efficiency of its new warehouses. In the old warehouses referred to by Clark as "first generation," sorting and packaging were mainly done manually and occupied more storage space.

Amazon later decided that new warehouses should adopt multi-level dense shelving. In addition, Amazon increased the height and floor area of the warehouses to improve space utilization. Clark said, "Now, this building holds twice the number of products it did four or five years ago." He also revealed that the number of items delivered weekly from places like the Chattanooga warehouse has roughly doubled, mainly due to improvements in sorting process efficiency.

Clark stated that over the past five years, thanks to internal software and "daily material handling equipment" called "Amazon Fulfillment Engines," the automation of Amazon's sorting processes has further improved. The fulfillment engine determines the next item employees need to retrieve, which distribution center the order should be transferred to, and where the goods should be consolidated for shipping.

Amazon currently uses a standardized architectural design to accelerate the scaled operation of each warehouse. Now, Amazon can complete the construction, shelf arrangement, and personnel recruitment of a warehouse center in just 10 months, a process that took two years five years ago.

Timely Summarization of Experience

According to Bezos' plan, Amazon will also build new warehouses closer to urban areas, which is expected to increase operating expenses because land prices are often higher near city centers. Szkutak said that over time, with the increase in product delivery efficiency, these costs may decrease. In the second quarter of this year, delivery expenses accounted for 4.6% of Amazon's total revenue, the same as the same period last year, indicating that this move has yet to bring scale benefits to Amazon.

Additionally, Amazon's distribution centers will undergo other changes. Last year, the company acquired Kiva Systems, a robot development company. Kiva Systems' robots can slide under products and then transport them around the warehouse. However, Amazon's warehouses currently do not overly rely on robot technology.

Amazon has also added freezers and transportation fleets at its warehouses in Seattle and Los Angeles to expand its fresh produce delivery business. This means that Amazon's warehouse model in 2014 may differ from this year. Clark said: "Each time we activate a warehouse, we summarize the lessons learned and make corresponding adjustments to the design. This fall, we will summarize the entire year's warehouse construction experience and apply it to the construction in 2014." (Qingchen)
