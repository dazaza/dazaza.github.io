Let the Facts Speak: A Summary of the Google I/O 2013 Keynote

by geekzhang on 2013-05-16 17:18:24

[Image] The keynote speech on the first day of Google I/O 2013 has just ended, and it was not quite as expected. Only one hardware device was introduced on-site: a Samsung S4 with native Android 4.2 system, which will be sold directly on the Google Play Store. On the software side, there were many exciting updates. Although Google did not use many exaggerated words in the presentation, the live demonstrations spoke for themselves. [Image] Without further ado, let's take a look at what surprises Google I/O 2013 brought us. One of the more disappointing aspects of Android was that neither Android 5.0 nor Android 4.3, which everyone had anticipated, appeared. However, Google still presented a lot of new content, most of which was featured on Google Play. First, the achievements of Android: In 2011, there were 1 billion activations; in 2012, it reached 4 billion activations; now, it has reached 9 billion activations. The total number of installations of Google Play apps has reached 48 billion. Then came a series of updates to Google Play. The new Google Play will provide different recommendations based on individual preferences. GCM (Google Cloud Messaging) is now included in Google Play Services. Google Play has added a highly visible feature called Google Play Game Services. The new features of this service's API include: cloud storage; achievements; leaderboards; multiplayer online games. [Image] Google Play Music launched an All Access music subscription service: After starting to play a song, you can activate radio mode, and All Access will automatically select similar songs to play; or simply choose Listen Now, using Google's power to help you find your favorite music from millions of tracks; this is a paid service, costing $9.99 per month, currently only available in the United States. [Image] Google Play also introduced Google Play Education, which will provide more high-quality educational applications, making it convenient for teachers and students to use. Chrome At this conference, Chrome advanced alongside Android, showcasing its charm on the web: The speed of JavaScript on the desktop version of Chrome is 25% faster than before, while the mobile version improved by 57%; it began supporting WebP file format, which is over 31% smaller than JPEG under the same image quality; in terms of streaming media, it supports VP9 format, saving about half the resources compared to H.264 under the same quality, and YouTube will support VP9. The conference site demonstrated real-time multi-player gaming across devices using Chrome, with excellent synchronization effects. Additionally, Google gave each attendee a Chromebook Pixel. The Chromebook Pixel features a 12.85-inch (3:2) high-definition touch screen, supporting multi-touch operation, with a resolution of 2560x1700, 239 PPI, brightness of 400 nit, viewing angle of 178 degrees, equipped with a dual-core 1.8 GHz Intel Core i5 processor, Intel HD 4000 graphics card, 4GB RAM, and options for 32GB or 64GB SSD. [Image][Image] Google+ At this conference, Google+ received a major upgrade. The new interface looks better, offers higher customization, and photo processing is the biggest highlight. The new interface focuses more on design and depth; Google+ automatically adjusts the number of rows in the card interface based on screen width; it automatically tags photos based on their content. Google believes taking pictures is labor-intensive work, so Google aims to reduce the burden on users: There are tools like darkroom and highlights in the data center that can make different improvements depending on the type of photo. A tool named auto-enhance can make images stand out even more and is very intelligent: It can pick out the best photos while discarding blurry, repetitive, poorly exposed ones, and specially selects photos with important people, valuable compositions, landmarks, and smiling faces; it can smartly process photos in the cloud, improving image quality and providing effects like skin smoothing, noise reduction, sharpening, etc.; it can automatically handle burst shots and create dynamic photos, HDR, panoramas, etc. [Image][Image][Image] Google also launched a new IM application called Hangouts, which might be the previously speculated Babel. This app spans Android, iOS, and web platforms, supporting multi-person video calls. However, according to internal sources, Hangouts will not display online status like the new version of QQ Mobile. Google Search The new Google Search is defined by Google as the end of search, showing Google's confidence in its technology. During the live demonstration, Google Search indeed showed extraordinary strength. Especially the performance of Google Now and Knowledge Graph: Knowledge Graph will add chart functionality and newly support Traditional Chinese and Simplified Chinese; voice search is smarter and faster; on the Chrome end, just say OK, Google to activate voice search; Google Now adds some new keywords. [Image][Image] Finally, the grand finale was Google Maps. Google first provided a set of data: Google Maps now covers approximately 200 countries, with street view in 50 countries, and currently over a million websites use Google Maps. The new map is simple, beautiful, and accurate. The new version of Google Maps made many improvements: Redesigned interface integrates Zagat reviews experience, Google Maps wants to tell you where is worth going; Google Offers group buying and discount services have been integrated; when selecting a target, Google Maps automatically marks the route to that target and similar destinations; redesigned navigation system directly compares various modes of transportation on the map, as well as a new travel time interface, adding unexpected notifications and automatic recalculation of detoured routes; 3D dynamic views generated from user photos demonstrate Google's massive data volume and powerful data processing capabilities; merged with Google Earth, the Google Earth mode cloud map is real-time. [Image][Image][Image][Image] At this year's Google I/O conference, Daniel Graf, head of Google Maps, said: As we all know, Google defined the modern concept of maps, and today, we intend to redefine it. Undoubtedly, Google Maps was the biggest highlight of this conference, no doubt about it. Alright, let's dive into the details and see what updates Google Maps has undergone. Below is a diagram showing the existing 199 countries covered by Google Maps, with green, blue, and red areas representing coverage by Google's own maps, user-generated maps, and third-party maps respectively. If you look closely, you'll notice a small white area, yes, this is North Korea. The latest Google Maps will release North Korea maps, thus including maps of 200 countries and regions worldwide. [Image] Moreover, Google Street View already has street view images from 50 countries, with the Street View collection vehicles having traveled 5 million miles for data collection, and these maps may all be converted into 3D maps. The head of Google Maps introduced that the richness and response speed of Google Maps have far surpassed Apple, and currently, over a million websites globally use Google Maps. During the conference, Google showcased the iPad version of Google Maps and the new map UI. It is reported that the new mobile version of Google Maps will land on both Android and iOS platforms. From a content perspective, the new version of Google Maps will be divided into four layers logically: base map, street view map, real satellite map, and local data map. Let's first look at the local data map at the bottom, which is a data layer formed by overlaying local information and social attributes onto the map. Users' Google+ information is integrated into the map, allowing users to see places visited by friends during map searches. Additionally, a five-star rating function has been added. By sliding the information bar at the bottom of the map interface, users can see different annotations and evaluations. Google will also collaborate with local businesses, aggregating local lifestyle content into the map. Does this sound familiar? [Image][Image] The topic shifts to Google Navigation. Currently, the total mileage of Google's turn-by-turn navigation paths has reached 50 billion kilometers, which could circle the equator approximately 1.25 million times. Additionally, Google Navigation has added an event warning function, allowing users to receive real-time alerts about global incidents and flexibly change navigation routes. The new mobile version of Google Maps will meet users this summer. Next up is the new desktop version of Google Maps. In the new interface, the sidebar will no longer exist, expanding the map display area to the entire screen. This interface is called "more immersive" (more immersive), with all additional elements, including search results and controls, feeling as if they are floating above the main map window. The button to switch to satellite view has moved to the lower left corner, and the lower section displays user-uploaded photos of specific locations. The zoom control is located in the lower right corner, and the upper left corner is the search bar. Locations, routes, etc., search results are displayed in the upper left corner of the search bar, with slight changes in the style of icons and controls. [Image][Image] The accuracy of rumors is extremely high, with the map pages being simpler than ever. Similar to the mobile version, the desktop version of Google Maps can also integrate users' friend information from Google+, allowing each user to use their own personalized map. Operationally, it feels smoother; just click on a location, and the map will automatically highlight optional routes, generating a separate route map with each click. The navigation interface is simpler; users click on a location, then click on their home location, and the route map will automatically appear. This is the future of navigation. Another very cool new feature: seamless switching between realistic satellite maps, street view maps, and indoor panoramic views, allowing users to fully tour a place on their computer screens. This feature seems very similar to Google Earth, but in fact, this is one of the big surprises of the conference—Google Maps will merge with Google Earth. The system will capture user-shot photos on-site, generating a complete 3D virtual scene, serving both navigation and touring purposes. The new desktop version of Google Maps is open to everyone, with the first batch of trial invitations to be released tomorrow. [Image][Image][Image] Additionally, with the release of the Android API v2 for Google Maps, Google has opened up three new positioning APIs: FusedLocationProvider: Faster and more accurate location acquisition, with a new low-power mode added; Geofencing: Defining virtual fences for geographic areas, triggering automatically upon entering or leaving the fence area; ActivityRecognition: Collecting user data to distinguish user activities, such as walking, cycling, or driving (not relying on GPS data and classification). Finally, let's revisit the original Google Maps. What better way to conclude this article than with this picture? The final part of the conference was Larry Page's speech and Q&A session. [Image] Despite concerns about Larry Page's health, surprisingly, Page appeared at the last part of today's conference. Although he has always been very low-key, he envisioned many aspects of the future of technology, computers, and society, reflecting on the current situation, and engaged in a Q&A session with the audience, demonstrating an extremely open attitude. Through Google I/O 2013, we found that Google is committed to becoming the largest internet service provider and intelligent computing platform, and Page is the creator behind it. Search and smartphones have changed the world, a victory of technology. Page first recalled a story: His father was passionate about technology, driving the whole family across the country to participate in a robot exhibition, which deeply influenced Page. Today, technology gets better every day; smartphones can do anything, and search will become smarter. Page said: The perfect search engine is the computer from Star Trek, it knows what you're thinking. Page talked about some exciting projects, like self-driving cars. Americans spend 50 minutes a day commuting, but with autonomous driving, you can free up your hands to do other things. The widespread adoption of affordable smartphones in developing countries is also incredible. Computers are developing at unimaginable speeds. Page described many scenarios where technology changes the future. But at the same time, he mentioned many factors hindering technological progress. He thinks that what Google has done is just a drop in the ocean, and the pace is still too slow. I see many reports about Google focusing on how we compete with other companies, but I don't find it interesting. We should create things that don't exist yet, and not every technology is a zero-sum game. Pessimism is not our way forward. For example, the Android vs Oracle patent issue. During the Q&A session, Page believed that Oracle values money more than cooperation, making it difficult for them to collaborate again, forcing them to resolve issues in court. And Android will ultimately succeed. Additionally, Page believes that societal acceptance of new technologies is still low, and the adoption rate is far from enough. Many people fear change, and government institutions adapt to changes even harder, with many still using products from 50 years ago. Despite the significant influence of the Internet, many people still don't have access to it. In the medical field, technological progress is also slow, mainly due to government systems, where technology contributes little to the system, only achieving 1%. Page envisions a place unrestricted by regulations, where any new technology can be tried, thereby promoting the true popularization of technology. We should establish mechanisms that allow extensive experimentation, exploring the impact of these experiments on humanity and society. Additionally, Page also discussed the increasing importance of women in the tech industry, with Google accelerating the recruitment of female employees. Talking about Google Glass, Page said there is no specific launch time yet, and the current goal is to collaborate with developers to bring practical functions, more apps, where developers play an extremely important role.
