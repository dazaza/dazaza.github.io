How to do a good job in evaluation before patent application?

by keyizhang008 on 2012-03-06 09:35:43

A patent only has value when it serves a function. In recent years, the number of patent applications in our country has increased dramatically. In 2011 alone, the total number of domestic patent applications for inventions, utility models, and industrial designs exceeded 1.63 million. Therefore, how to make patents worth their value and maximize their appreciation is a new challenge faced by intellectual property work.

Currently, professional technology transfer agencies or corporate intellectual property managers have already placed great emphasis on developing and converting existing patents. However, in reality, whether a patent can be commercialized and how it can be commercialized should be considered right from the start of the patent application process. I believe that only by strengthening pre-application assessments and formulating targeted application strategies can we change the passive situation of "selling what we have," and create a new type of patent trading market where "we have what we sell."

A patent is the protection of a technical solution. Before applying for a patent, one needs to consider: whether the definition of the invention has been comprehensively described, how visible its effects are, whether it is a core technology or an improvement. As the source of a patent, only with technological innovation does the patent, as a carrier of technology, have a solid foundation. At the same time, the comparative advantage in technology preliminarily determines the scope and direction of patent protection.

Patent rights are the exclusive or proprietary rights granted by law to inventors or their assignees over their inventions within a certain period. Therefore, evaluating the expected exclusive status of the invention is also indispensable. For example, the scope and strength of patent protection, the degree of freedom to use the patent, the ability to monitor infringement, and the capacity to bear litigation costs. If after disclosing the technical solution through a patent application, it becomes difficult to monitor whether others' products or systems use your solution, or if monitoring requires significant cost, then even if the technology is very advanced and could obtain a wide range of protection, it may not be advisable to apply for a patent. Other forms of intellectual property protection, such as trade secrets or software copyrights, might be more appropriate.

The development stage of patent technology largely determines the cost and cycle of implementation, which is a highly concerning issue for the patent transferee or licensee. Therefore, during the application stage, it is necessary to understand its state: whether it is just a concept, ready for practice, or ready for production. If it is just a concept, further investment and secondary development may be needed to achieve practical application in the industry.

The purpose of assessing patent applications to be submitted is to better grasp the commercial potential of the technology at an earlier stage, such as the ability to define products, expected demand, end-user identity, market size, and maturity. Whether to license it to a company of a certain scale, seek venture capital, or implement it oneself to prevent competitors from following. These all require in-depth research and investigation, and cooperation between patent workers, marketing, sales, and technical personnel. If the conclusion is positive, then investing quality resources in the patent application process, hiring experienced patent agents, deploying a complete patent portfolio, and selectively submitting international patent applications through the Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) route will all be valuable investments.

In addition, since both the application and maintenance of patents require expenditure, especially when submitting international patent applications through the PCT route, financial analysis is also essential. This includes R&D costs, patent costs, expected licensing revenue, the possibility of obtaining funding for subsequent research, product cost allocation, etc. In fact, sometimes the result of cost analysis may show that obtaining a patent license from others is more economical than applying for a patent oneself. Therefore, introducing patents, digesting, absorbing, and re-innovating them can shorten the gap, enhance competitiveness, avoid risks, and achieve twice the result with half the effort.

In summary, I believe that pre-application assessment of patents is of great significance for controlling patent quality and subsequent conversion and implementation.
