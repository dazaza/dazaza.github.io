Alibaba's journey to brush up its valuation

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 15:59:54

According to the agreement signed in 2012 when Alibaba repurchased 20% of its shares held by Yahoo, if Alibaba Group completed a "qualified" listing before December 2015, it would have the right to repurchase half (10%) of the remaining shares held by Yahoo at the IPO price. A qualified listing refers to an IPO valuation reaching $73.5 billion, with an issue price exceeding $28.35.

Would Alibaba be willing to repurchase 10% of the shares within a valuation range of $60-80 billion? This is clearly a question, especially given that the voting rights of Yahoo and SoftBank have already fallen below 50%. It depends on the overall confidence of Alibaba's management in the future business of the group. From another perspective, Alibaba Group has no reason not to actively promote the listing because otherwise, it would lead to serious "loss of confidence". All businesses are now major players, and without going public to make money, divide the pie, and distribute profits, it would be difficult to push the business forward and consolidate the territory.

From a valuation of $35 billion in 2012 to a qualified IPO valuation of $73.5 billion, it is obviously not just selling phones or boxes that can solve the problem. For a company with a valuation in the billions, through intensive investments, capital cooperation, and mergers and acquisitions, relying on Alibaba Group's existing industrial chain to enhance the imagination space for future business, commonly known as brushing up the valuation.

Sina Weibo: In April 2013, Alibaba announced that it would invest $586 million to acquire an 18% stake in Sina Weibo. Prior to this, there were rumors that Baidu and Tencent had also discussed investing in Sina Weibo, which was considered a competitive social service in China’s internet industry. Brushing up Weibo’s valuation brings in revenue, while brushing up Alibaba’s valuation brings social traffic and data.

Momo: In August 2012, rumors circulated that Alibaba Capital invested $40 million in Momo, valuing it at $200 million for a 20% stake. Later, Momo CEO Tang Yan confirmed that they had completed a Series B financing round valued at $100 million but did not publicly respond whether Alibaba Capital followed up.

Xiami Music: In January 2013, Alibaba announced a restructuring of its 25 business units, among which the music division was formed by Xiami Music, though the specific acquisition completion time was not disclosed. Xiami Music is a well-known online music community in China, four out of five founders are former Alibaba employees. Xiami COO Wang Xiaowei stated that in 2012, Xiami had been in contact with several internet investment departments, including Tencent, Baidu, and Alibaba.

Autonavi: In May 2013, Alibaba invested $294 million in Autonavi Software, acquiring a 28% stake and becoming the largest single shareholder. The main businesses include in-car navigation, government and enterprise applications, internet and mobile internet.

Cainiao Network: Jointly established with multiple express logistics companies, aiming to build a logistics network capable of supporting a daily online retail volume of 30 billion yuan. Alibaba contributed 2.15 billion yuan, holding a 43% stake.

Kuaidi Dache: Investment amounting to several million RMB, with an unclear equity stake.

Dingding Network: In November 2012, Alibaba and Citi jointly invested in Dingding Network. In April 2013, Alibaba made a second investment of an undisclosed amount. The website mainly focuses on mobile lifestyle services.

Qyer: In July 2013, Alibaba announced a strategic investment in Qyer, which has 10 million registered users and 5 million mobile users. Rumors suggest the amount was nearly ten million dollars.

Umeng: At the end of March 2013, it was rumored that Alibaba acquired Umeng for $80 million. After the transaction, the Umeng team would integrate into Alibaba. The company's main business is data statistics and precise advertising services in the mobile field.

The above are some of the achievements that we can currently see, where Alibaba has intensively achieved in the past year. Both the amount and quantity are worth mentioning. From the layout perspective, social networking, mobile, and life services are the three major directions of Alibaba's capital actions, all of which can connect with Alibaba's concept of e-commerce life.

At the same time, Alibaba does not forget to cause trouble for others by raising prices on their acquisitions and investments. Sogou, 91 Wireless, PPTV, UCWEB... Basically, any imaginable capital cooperation possibility involves Alibaba's presence.

From the perspective of increasing valuation for the purpose of going public, and from the perspective of a company expanding its business through capital cooperation, what Alibaba is currently doing is correct. However, for the companies being acquired or invested in, one should not hope that selling oneself to Alibaba will bring sudden prosperity. Take the money offered, understand that it's their strategy, and whether you can thrive still depends on yourself. With too many adopted sons to take care of, the integration mortality rate after five years may not be optimistic.

Quoting a comment from the web as the conclusion of this article: Someone once referred to Alibaba's business as SoLoMoCo, meaning social, local, mobile, and commerce, forming a relatively complete commercial ecosystem centered around e-commerce. Such a description fits perfectly with Jack Ma's previously mentioned "one eco-system" concept. Now it seems that everything is progressing according to this vision. Alibaba wants to become the underlying infrastructure of the e-commerce system, rather than e-commerce itself.
