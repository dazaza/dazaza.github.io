Foreign publishers are putting up "paywalls", but it's hard to stop the financial deterioration

by bingo123 on 2012-03-08 17:38:24

Introduction: Although the strategy of charging for digital newspaper content has been progressing slowly, this business model not only generates revenue from digital subscriptions but also helps prevent the decline in traditional print advertising income, thus attracting an increasing number of publishers to join this trend.

The following is a full translation of the article:

Trend Towards Charging

Despite more and more newspapers beginning to close the free reading access on their websites, some publishers are still waiting for a large influx of paying users.

Current market trends indicate that for many newspapers, the "paywall" cannot reverse the worsening financial situation. However, this outcome has not discouraged industry executives, who say these measures are successfully supporting the core print business that has declined over the years.

The Los Angeles Times, under Tribune Co., will begin charging readers who wish to access all online content starting from Monday. Publisher Gannett also stated it will join the charging ranks. Including these companies, in the past year, about 10 major U.S. newspapers and hundreds of smaller ones have announced that they have or will soon limit free website access, allowing only those who subscribe to the print or digital editions to view all online content.

Since it began charging website readers last November, the Star Tribune of Minneapolis has attracted 14,200 digital subscribers, equivalent to 5.7% of its weekday print subscribers. Since last autumn, the Boston Globe website has charged for three months, selling a total of 16,000 digital subscriptions with approximately 200,000 print subscribers. Meanwhile, Newsday in New York also has nearly 1,000 digital subscribers, two years after the site initiated its "paywall."

True Purpose

Apart from continuous experimentation, newspaper companies have few other options. Over the past decade or so, as newspaper content spread freely on the internet, their financial conditions have been severely impacted. According to data from the International Yearbook of Editors and Publishers, the weekday circulation of newspapers from 1999 to 2009 decreased by nearly 10 million copies, accounting for about 17% of the total. Data from the Newspaper Association of America also shows that during this period, print advertising revenue was almost halved.

The recent rate of decline has slowed, but the growth in online advertising revenue is still insufficient to offset the contraction in print advertising.

Industry executives stated that the main purpose of these measures is not to increase digital subscribers but to reduce the appeal of free online content to protect the print business. In the television industry, cable channels are adopting similar methods to protect existing businesses: they only allow cable TV or satellite TV subscribers to watch programs online. However, the issue with newspapers lies in whether the paywalls come too late and are too small in scale after years of declining subscribers and advertising?

Successful Cases

Some major newspapers have already succeeded with paywalls. The Wall Street Journal began restricting access to online content in the mid-1990s. According to data from the Audit Bureau of Circulations, as of last autumn, the paper had approximately 537,000 digital subscribers. About 80,000 use versions specifically designed for tablets, smartphones, or e-readers, while others use the web version. Pearson Group's Financial Times implemented its current payment model at the end of 2007, revealing that as of the end of last year, it had 267,000 digital subscribers.

Since the New York Times started charging for online content in late March last year, it has attracted 380,000 paid digital subscribers, about half of its print circulation.

However, these might just be isolated cases rather than common situations. The Wall Street Journal and the Financial Times lead the industry and provide specialized business news. According to data from the American internet traffic monitoring agency comScore, the New York Times had over 30 million monthly unique user visits in the United States when it first started charging. Martin Nisenholtz, who retired last year as Senior Vice President of Digital Business at the New York Times Company, said this vast user base gave the paper an advantage.

Metered Strategy

For all newspapers, the key challenge lies in the abundance of free content available online. Many newspapers cannot offer much incentive to attract new digital subscribers. Most publishers allow existing print subscribers to continue accessing all online content, resulting in a relatively small pool of potential digital subscribers. Moreover, many publishers are using what is known as the "metered strategy," allowing non-subscribers to read a certain number of articles for free. Typically, only 10% or 20% of online readers exceed the limit of free articles, depending on how high the threshold is set. The Boston Globe and Newsday have adopted stricter paywalls.

Newsday allows print subscribers of the newspaper, as well as Optimum Online subscribers belonging to Cablevision Systems Corporation, to read online content for free. Newsday claims that these two categories cover 70% of the market.

The benefit of the "metered strategy" is that it prevents a sharp drop in website traffic, allowing the site to retain most readers and online advertising revenue.

Other Factors

Many newspaper executives state that the number of subscribers is not important because the cost of publishing content online is far lower than for newspapers, meaning digital readers generate higher profit per person. The Star Tribune has 14,000 users who pay $100 annually for the paper's digital subscription service, generating around $1.4 million in revenue. Publisher Michael Klingensmith said that without printing and distribution costs, more of this revenue counts as profit.

Meanwhile, since implementing charges, the page views of the Star Tribune have only dropped by about 12%, so the newly established digital subscription business generates income that is 15 times the revenue lost due to the traffic decrease. "This is the deal I dreamed of," Klingensmith said.

However, from a broader commercial perspective, these figures do not matter much. In the six months ending in September last year, the weekday print subscribers of the Star Tribune decreased by 100,000 compared to five years ago. The annual print subscription fee for the paper is currently about $244.

But there is evidence showing that limiting access to digital content can help stop the decline in print circulation. The New York Times launched its metered system a year ago, and the paper stated that in the six months ending in September last year, Sunday circulation increased year-over-year for the first time in five years.

Despite mixed results, the latest entrants into this field remain hopeful. Gannett CEO Gracia Martore said the company expects paywalls to add $100 million in profit by 2013. Combined with the impact of price increases, this will boost the annual revenue of the company's 80 newspapers deploying the paywall model by 25%. In 2011, Gannett's newspaper circulation revenue was approximately $130 million, down from levels three years ago.
