Apple said to team up with China Mobile to sell a low-cost iPhone

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:53:57

On the morning of September 7 Beijing time, The Wall Street Journal cited sources saying that Apple is preparing to ship iPhones to China Mobile. A partnership with China Mobile would greatly enhance Apple's distribution capabilities in China, the world’s largest smartphone market.

Cooperation with China Mobile

Apple’s negotiations with China Mobile have continued for many years. China Mobile has 700 million users, seven times the size of the US’ largest mobile operator Verizon Wireless. Currently, by volume, Greater China is Apple's third largest market after the United States and Europe.

Apple will hold a launch event next Tuesday at its headquarters in Cupertino, California. Industry insiders expect that Apple will release two new models of iPhone during this event. Sources said that these new phones will go on sale later this month.

A source said that Apple had informed Foxconn, its contract manufacturer, to add China Mobile to the carrier list of the new low-cost version of the iPhone. It is currently unclear whether Apple and China Mobile have signed a formal agreement or when China Mobile will begin selling this phone.

China Mobile is currently the largest mobile operator that does not yet operate the iPhone. Selling the iPhone through China Mobile represents an important milestone for Apple. As Apple's best-selling product, the iPhone's market share is being challenged by competitors such as Samsung, and observers feel that Apple is gradually losing its innovative edge.

In the third fiscal quarter ended June 29, Apple's sales in Greater China fell 14% year-over-year to $4.6 billion. According to data from market research firm Canalys, Apple's market share in China's smartphone market dropped to fifth place in this quarter, accounting for 5%. Samsung led with an 18% share. Domestic brands like Lenovo and Huawei also outperformed Apple.

Apple CEO Tim Cook stated in April that the Chinese market represents an important growth opportunity for the company because there are many first-time smartphone buyers in this market. At the time, Apple announced plans to double the number of Apple retail stores in China within less than two years.

Canalys expects that the total shipment volume of China's smartphone market will increase by 84% year-over-year this year to reach 352 million units, more than twice the size of the U.S. market. By 2015, this figure is expected to reach 421 million units. Last year, China surpassed the United States to become the world's largest smartphone market, making it increasingly important for Apple.

Sources said that Apple initially refused to develop a dedicated smartphone for China Mobile's unstable 3G network. This network has slower data download speeds, and the cost of developing a phone is higher. However, China Mobile was dissatisfied with the guaranteed sales terms insisted upon by Apple.

Low-Cost Version of iPhone

Another factor affecting the development of the iPhone in China is price. For mainstream consumers, the iPhone is too expensive, especially compared to domestic brands. Smartphones from these brands have similar hardware specifications but typically cost only a fraction of the price of the new iPhone. In the U.S., carriers subsidize phones, reducing the upfront cost for users, but in China, subsidies are usually provided in the form of monthly bill credits, so their effect on reducing the upfront cost of buying a phone isn't very noticeable.

However, both Apple and China Mobile need each other now. Apple's sales in the Chinese market are declining, while China Mobile has suffered from user loss in its competition with China Unicom and China Telecom. These latter two operators already sell the iPhone.

The current unsubsidized price of the iPhone 5 is approximately 5,000 yuan RMB (or $815). However, most smartphones shipped in China's market in the second quarter were priced below $200. Although Apple has not yet revealed any details about the low-cost version of the iPhone, analysts estimate that the price of this phone will drop to around $400, targeting China's mid-range market.

TZ Wong, IDC's analyst for China's smartphone market, said: "The low-cost version of the iPhone will undoubtedly change the market landscape. For Apple, this will be a turning point."

Morgan Stanley analyst Katy Huberty estimated in a report in August that if a $400 iPhone is launched, Apple's iPhone sales in the Chinese market will increase by 20 million units, and market share will rise by 13%. This phone could attract users from Samsung, HTC, and Lenovo. Cooperation with China Mobile will further increase sales to 32 million units. This is roughly one-quarter of the global total sales of 125 million iPhones in the past fiscal year.

Currently, most iPhone users in China are located in wealthy cities such as Beijing and Shanghai. The growth opportunities lie in the shift of users in small and medium-sized towns dominated by China Mobile from feature phones to smartphones. Sales networks in these areas are not well-developed, largely depending on carriers.

Nicole Peng, head of China market research at Canalys, said: "If Apple wants the low-cost iPhone to perform well, the key lies with China Mobile."

Consumers and Competitors

In Shijiazhuang, a city with 10 million people not far from Beijing, 22-year-old Guo Jie represents the demographic Apple is trying to target. After browsing in an electronics mall in the city center, Guo Jie said he hoped to upgrade his Samsung feature phone to a simple and inexpensive one. However, he mentioned that if he were to buy an iPhone, he would be willing to spend more money. He said: "If the iPhone is priced at 3,000 yuan RMB (approximately $490), I would buy it."

Ma Tao owns a stall in this mall, selling various goods including smartphones, laptops, and electric shavers. He said that many customers consider purchasing the older model iPhone 4 because it is cheaper than the iPhone 5. But ultimately, many choose other phones because the iPhone 4 looks outdated. He believed that if Apple can provide a lower-priced iPhone with a design similar to the latest model, there would be no doubt about its huge sales potential.

Apple faces significant pricing pressure in the Chinese market because leading Chinese smartphone manufacturers, including ZTE, Coolpad, and Xiaomi, are willing to sacrifice short-term profits to gain market share. These companies all believe that today's entry-level consumers will become the foundation for the future.

Xiaomi is representative of this view. In recent years, Xiaomi has rapidly developed by selling top-tier hardware configurations at low prices and through extensive publicity. Although Xiaomi is still relatively unknown outside of China, it is attempting to generate revenue through internet services such as games and messaging apps installed on its phones. Earlier this month, Xiaomi announced the hiring of Hugo Bara, Google Android's business director, to oversee the company's international business expansion.

This week, Xiaomi launched its latest flagship smartphone, Xiaomi 3. Priced at 1,999 yuan RMB (approximately $320), it will be sold through China's three major carriers. Xiaomi recently also released a smartphone priced at 799 yuan RMB (approximately $130) aimed at China Mobile users. In the second quarter of this year, Xiaomi's market share in China's smartphone market has surpassed Apple's.

In June, Huawei launched its flagship smartphone, Ascend P6. This phone is thinner than the iPhone 5 and features a 4.7-inch high-resolution screen. Despite the P6 being Huawei's highest-priced product at 2,688 yuan RMB (approximately $440), it is still only half the price of the unsubsidized version of the iPhone 5.

Shao Yang, Chief Marketing Officer of Huawei's terminal business, said that at the same price, consumers can choose between top-tier configured smartphones or slightly weaker iPhones. He said: "Users who buy iPhones generally don't like putting them in their bags; they prefer placing them on the table so everyone can see. But if it's the low-cost version of the iPhone, then it's not worth showing off." (Wei Jin)
