Explanation of the main drive and auxiliary main drive system of the rotary kiln

by hx515317 on 2012-03-01 13:45:57

A rotary kiln has a main drive system and an auxiliary drive system. The drive systems of the kiln and cooler are relatively simple. There are two major drive systems in a rotary kiln and their functions:

(1) The main drive system consists of: the main motor, the main reducer, gear and toothed ring, and the cylinder body. When there is a lack of a reducer with sufficient speed ratio and capacity, an intermediate semi-open gear transmission can be added between the reducer and the gear-toothed ring. In some cases, a belt transmission stage is added between the motor and the reducer.

(2) The function of the auxiliary drive system is to periodically rotate the kiln when the main motor or main power source fails, preventing the cylinder body from bending due to temperature differences between the top and bottom, and ensuring that during bricklaying or maintenance, the cylinder can remain at a specified position. The prime mover for the auxiliary drive is usually an electric motor, which is supplied by a different power source than the main motor. An internal combustion engine may also be used to eliminate the need for a separate power source. Between the auxiliary motor and the main reducer, there is an auxiliary reducer to achieve a higher speed ratio requirement.

(3) A brake should be equipped on the auxiliary motor to prevent the kiln from reversing due to the uneven weight of materials and bricks after the motor stops. The reverse speed of the kiln often approaches or even exceeds the normal speed. If the auxiliary drive is not disengaged at this time, the reducer will act as a speed increaser, causing excessively high speeds in the auxiliary reducer and motor, potentially reaching tens of thousands of revolutions per minute, leading to dangerous situations. Therefore, the brake and auxiliary motor should be connected or disconnected simultaneously. The brake can be electromagnetic or YWZ hydraulic push-rod type. Due to poor lubrication of the bearing parts of the support wheel, gear, and transmission under slow-speed conditions, prolonged use of the auxiliary drive is not suitable, generally not exceeding 15 minutes of continuous operation.

(4) A clutch is set up between the output shaft of the auxiliary reducer and the input shaft of the main reducer, allowing the auxiliary drive to disengage from the main drive during normal operation. There are no special requirements for the clutch; common types include helical (friction disk) and pin types. Overrunning (one-way) clutches can also be used. An overrunning clutch can transmit the torque of the auxiliary motor to the main reducer. If the main motor drives the active shaft of the main reducer in the same direction at a higher speed simultaneously, the clutch will disengage, separating the two.

(5) Besides using an overrunning clutch, the main motor and auxiliary motor must not turn simultaneously if the clutch is not disengaged. Therefore, electrical interlocking is required between the two motors. To ensure safe and reliable operation, a mechanical-electrical interlock device should also be installed between the clutch and the main motor to prevent the main motor from starting when the clutch is not disengaged.
