When selecting cutting inserts, the entire machining task needs to be evaluated. If the dimensional accuracy and surface finish requirements of the workpiece can be met, and processing time and insert replacement have been considered, choosing a relatively inexpensive hard metal insert can achieve good processing economy. By accurately understanding and comprehensively weighing production batches, processing time, and insert performance, cutting inserts can be reasonably selected to achieve improved productivity in machining.

Taking milling of gas turbine blades made from sintered titanium carbide as an example, when the batch size is small, coated hard metal inserts can also yield good machining results. At a cutting speed of 35m/min, the tool life of hard metal inserts is only 5-10 minutes, while the reasonable tool life for large batch processing of difficult-to-machine materials generally requires reaching 15-30 minutes. In small batch processing, shorter tool life and more frequent insert changes do not significantly impact productivity; however, in large batch full-load processing, longer tool life is crucial for reducing auxiliary time spent changing tools, lowering labor intensity, improving machine utilization, and increasing capacity. Therefore, when the batch size for turbine blades is large, selecting harder but more expensive CBN (cubic boron nitride) inserts may be more reasonable.

To fully utilize the cutting performance of advanced material inserts, correct feed rates and cutting speeds must also be selected. Taking Sandvik Coromant's CBN inserts as an example, these inserts have undergone reinforcement and blunting treatment on their cutting edges, effectively avoiding chipping when machining materials with hardness > 50HRC. Despite excellent toughness, CBN inserts still require strict selection of cutting parameters; if the selected cutting speed is higher or lower than the ideal value by 10%, it may significantly reduce the cutting performance of the insert.

To implement the machining of difficult-to-machine materials, technical support from professional tool suppliers can be sought. Tool suppliers can provide reasonable solutions based on other similar machining examples. When cutting trials are needed, trial-and-error methods are usually adopted, i.e., first using hard metal inserts for cutting, then switching to new material inserts for comparative cutting, comparing the machining effects of different inserts. Using advanced insert shapes, high rigidity holders, and optimized machining programs typically makes lower-cost hard metal inserts suitable for machining difficult-to-machine materials. Whether to switch to new material inserts should be determined based on specific machining tasks and conditions. For the same broad category of difficult-to-machine materials, there is generally some commonality in the selection of cutting inserts.

Hardened Steel Machining

Currently, many alloy steel components are requiring increasingly higher hardness. In the past, the application hardness of tool steel was typically 45HRC, whereas now tool steel used in the mold industry commonly requires hardening to 63HRC. To avoid heat treatment distortion, some molds that could only be machined before heat treatment in the past now need to undergo precision milling in a fully hardened state. When milling fully hardened steel, the generated cutting heat and cutting pressure may cause plastic deformation of the cutting insert and lead to rapid tool failure. For instance, when milling hardened steel with a hardness of 60HRC (the carbide particles in the material have a hardness of up to 90HRC), ordinary coated hard metal inserts will experience rapid wear on the back face.

Although hardened steel is difficult to machine, economic machining of fully hardened steel components can still be achieved using hard metal inserts. For example, in the aerospace component machining, a large aircraft manufacturer replaced the originally used ceramic metal inserts with Sandvik's GC1025 hard metal inserts, successfully completing the secondary hole machining of large forged pieces made from hardened 3000M steel (4340 tempering treatment). Most of the machining allowance for the holes had already been removed before heat treatment (material hardness 30-32HRC), but due to heat treatment distortion correction, precise holes on these large workpieces must be machined again after the workpiece is fully hardened (hardness reaches 54-55HRC). Since the holes to be machined are located deep within the workpiece, the special shape of the workpiece makes the machining quite challenging, thus requiring three passes to achieve the required dimensional accuracy and surface finish. The original ceramic metal inserts would fail with chipped cutting edges even before completing one pass, posing a risk of scrapping the workpiece. After switching to fine-grain hard metal inserts coated with PVD, the toughness and sharpness of the tools were significantly improved, allowing them to complete 6-9 passes successfully. After switching to hard metal inserts, the tool supplier recommended reducing the cutting speed from the original 90m/min to 53m/min, keeping the cutting depth unchanged.
