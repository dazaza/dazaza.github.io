Dianping: A Bone the Giants Can't Chew, Living in the Zone of Discomfort

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:08:33

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com, has once again postponed the company's IPO.

In 2011, when Dianping received a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, its valuation reached $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an appropriate time to go public within the following one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it carried out another $60 million Series D funding round.

With Dianping's scale and development momentum, going public would not be difficult at all. The issue is that Zhang Tao believes going public now would be too costly. He breaks down the big internet (PC + mobile) into four elements—people, information, goods, and services—and each connection point offers the opportunity to create giants. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. Between people and services lies the seat for the fourth giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been working hard to accumulate data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where phones connect "location" and "services." Following Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the image of the fourth giant.

But outsiders don't think so. In these past few months, with BAT's frequent acquisition strategies, Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; regardless of which surname it takes on from BAT, it could help them achieve great success early on. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it's still too early to declare that it will become a giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat’s official platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after acquiring Gaode and Meituan, they all directly aim at Dianping's territory—local life services mainly centered around dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two things, Dianping going public would be equivalent to a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs some time to prove that what Dianping is doing now, BAT cannot do. Second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." For speculation and rumors, Zhang Tao pays no attention, and he is very clear: "The internet has indeed entered a great era of integration, but Dianping will never be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is better suited to develop within a larger ecosystem, such as maps and emails. These products can easily attract a large number of users, but it's difficult to find an independent business model. There are also products like browsers, which can make money, but the revenue earned through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Big players are willing to spend money maintaining email products because they help with brand and user stickiness, but there hasn't been any successful standalone email company heard of." The second type involves industry mergers, like in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type includes companies facing financial difficulties, selling themselves for survival.

Now Meituan has already been brought under Alibaba's wing. In theory, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated due to the rapid expansion of competitors after securing capital. However, Zhang Tao says, "The help from giants is limited. We indeed need users, but execution is even more important. Mergers and acquisitions would lower our execution ability and may even marginalize us in large companies." Furthermore, he wants Dianping to maintain the most precise "distance" from the giants. If Dianping gets too close to any one of them, it would be equivalent to "taking sides," making it an enemy of the others.

Thus, logically speaking, the acquisition of Dianping has hit a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why should we take their money? We're not short of money; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to take a small stake and give us many resources, I could consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only hold a small percentage of shares?"

Weapon One: Dirty Work, Hard Work

In the mobile internet era, opportunities for companies that only do pure internet products are becoming fewer and fewer. New opportunities come from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that the main part of life services is eating, drinking, playing, and having fun. Other细分 markets, such as ride-hailing, can form good business models, but it's hard for them to grow into giants.

For eating, drinking, playing, and having fun, user needs are simple: information and discounts. Among them, dining is the core of life services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether it's content, member accumulation, or brand, latercomers find it hard to surpass.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Now group buying has entered the championship race between the top two players, and Dianping plans to battle Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market. As long as there are two strong players coexisting, the industry's gross profit margin will be pulled down, and stable profitability will be hard to achieve.

Another foreseeable future is reservations. Reservations can form a true online-to-offline closed loop, but this must be built on the foundation of Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began trying out reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, both users and merchants give positive feedback, but if we only focus on reservations, it's hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services offer some more "heavy" opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid demand for users and helps merchants increase sales. In the last couple of years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet" have thrived. However, the core of food delivery is delivery—getting meals delivered within a short period of time while dealing with issues like soup spilling. The logistics requirements are higher than even fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If food delivery is executed well, there is still an opportunity, but this is harder dirty work and tiring work, and what Dianping is doing now is already dirty and tiring enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant information and user reviews, which Zhang Tao refers to as "dirty work, hard work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but the pain of the past has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the life services field.

However, he still needs to be wary of BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, and users can use the map to search for nearby dining and other life services. Tencent, considered to have secured a ticket to mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and expanding goods from physical items to services is logical, especially since it has incorporated Meituan and Gaode into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there are no signs of explosive growth in reality and data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is important, and the key is which product users turn to first when they want to eat, drink, play, and have fun.

Moreover, in the life services sector, which has immense imaginative space, competition among giants is bound to cross over, and no one can completely monopolize the field. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that this makes Dianping safer instead. "In niche markets like car rentals and business travel, the massive user base of giants could flood and overwhelm the original companies."

As for Alibaba, which invested in Gaode, invading Dianping's territory isn't that simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can always be categorized no matter how peculiar. A user's experience of a meal depends on various factors such as the day's environment, waiters, and chefs. Managing merchants can't rely on systems and tools like Alibaba does; it requires building a 'ground force' managed by people.

"The three giants all want to take this path, and there is imaginative space, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road." Zhang Tao believes that first, doing life services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, which BAT lacks the genes and capability to manage offline teams; second, they lack the willingness. Life services require focused effort, not just sending a small team to accomplish it, and earning money is slow. "It's hard for a company to excel at two important things simultaneously, let alone consolidating its advantage in the original field."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Are Easier to Maneuver

When Dianping raised $100 million in 2011, Zhang Tao told the outside world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogies fell from grace.

He wasn't overly anxious about it either. "They didn't become big companies because they lacked offline teams, and now they all want to be America's Dianping."

The current path is something Zhang Tao has gradually explored step by step. When he felt that the model of selling keyword ads and coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying lit up his eyes. Initially, he also understood group buying as Americans did—as a marketing platform, one deal per day—but as orders increased, he realized that group buying had the attributes of a trading platform. Thus, he saw Dianping's future as a life services trading platform, and the two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used for Dianping's transformation from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this "genetic modification," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth seat among the giants today. But genetic modification sounds simple, yet in reality, it's equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process extremely painful.

First, compared to separately selling ads, the gross margin of a trading platform decreases, requiring higher cost control and efficiency. Second, a trading platform demands higher inter-departmental communication capabilities.

Previously, to maintain the fairness and independence of Dianping as an information platform, the content team and the sales team were completely separated. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments—no matter how important the client, the information on the platform couldn't be modified for sales purposes, which once led to mutual hostility between the content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication, and a good marketing plan requires the editor's cooperation beyond planning. He said, "Maintaining the quality of information remains important, but there's no need to reinforce animosity anymore; the firewall needs to be dismantled."

Though editors can logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, the inertia becomes even greater. To address this, Zhang Tao specially held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. After a few months, an editor responsible for sales casually remarked, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I see you as a colleague."

The sales department also needed genetic modification. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team dual roles. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants—not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants are the company's buyers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety of goods must also be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Of course, dual roles will inevitably lead to a series of complex issues. For frontline personnel, this can be quite headache-inducing, as they must balance the scales themselves.

In substance, Dianping's "dual role" is very similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to the front lines allows the company to react faster, but it increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffered more after the genetic modification.

Every year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's company goals into various departments, then executives further break them down layer by layer, but decomposition is not simple arithmetic. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping, who oversees the sales team, sometimes finds that unresolved merchant needs stem from sales problems but affect sales. Therefore, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists who collect information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change doesn't mean liking change. No one likes change; everyone feels uncomfortable when faced with change. All employees must go through uncomfortable zones, including me."

Numbers reflect the "heaviness" of Dianping's execution team. Currently, out of more than 3,000 employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground forces are responsible for communicating directly with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and combined marketing solutions.

For this core team capable of resisting BAT's clutches, Dianping has a unique management evaluation system.

They are not called "salespeople"; their business cards have always been labeled as "marketing planners" from day one. Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the stage of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in terms of internet understanding. Marketing planners need to tell merchants what online users need and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing advisors wouldn't suggest offering abalone or shark fin group buys, possibly switching to other dishes, because online users and offline users have different needs."

An excellent marketing advisor must be a versatile player. They need to explain clearly to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the essential differences between discounts and group buying and which discount suits their positioning better. If facing a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing advisor should know whether it's off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise during this season, and which season sees a decrease in costs, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users like.

This places high demands on marketing advisors, who must not only be experts in internet marketing but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every advisor to be a double expert, so we set star ratings for advisors." Similar to hotels, marketing advisors range from one-star to five-stars, with the best ones referred to as Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees are not tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it difficult for them to understand internet products. Dianping established a knowledge base for marketing advisors, containing knowledge points and relevant cases, accessible to both new and experienced employees. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, and thousands of consulting advisors nationwide will help answer them.

Most of the knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experiences. Dianping set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Each quarter, Dianping also conducts skill assessments for employees, with results directly linked to promotions.

Compared to internet knowledge, industry knowledge is harder to acquire. Some experiences are insights that traditional merchants have gathered after decades of practice in the industry. A Super Star marketing advisor told Li Jing that hair salons should avoid offering wash-cut-blow dry package deals, as men lack motivation to buy them, while women prefer blow-drying without washing. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never have thought of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing advisors are divided not only by city but also by industry, such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hotpot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with each marketing advisor responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing advisors can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, ensuring merchants don't reject product sales," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing session: What difficulties did a certain type of merchant encounter, what marketing needs they have, what solutions were provided using the company's products, and how effective they were. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneurial Winner." Within the first three months of joining, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products, but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If they are responsible for hot pot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a commercial plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees present their defense, and industry supervisors evaluate winners, with the company providing certain rewards.

Strategically speaking, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, their tasks are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O, anyone familiar with the industry would say almost the same things. "The era where telling stories or creating concepts leads to success is over. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model is management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
