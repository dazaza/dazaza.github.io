The Five Reasons Why Nine Out of Ten Stock Traders Lose Money

by longersing on 2009-06-08 12:27:14

I still have some stocks on hand, and I lament that I haven't made money on the stock market in these three years. When others made profits, I didn't, and when others lost, I followed suit. That's the nature of the stock market. Let's take a look at them.

In domestic and foreign stock markets, 90% of investors are in a loss-making state, each for their own reasons. The typical ones include the following:

1. Not stopping losses in time. Many people don't follow this principle because they are too soft-hearted to act decisively. There must be a stop-loss point because you can never know how much a stock will fall. Setting a stop-loss point or level is like installing a "fuse" for the stock you bought. If the stock price plummets continuously, you will only lose one "fuse" (stop-loss price). In my opinion, whether a person can be a securities investor does not depend on their intelligence or sharp thinking, but rather on having the courage to stop losses.

2. Always pursuing maximum profit. This mainly manifests in the following three aspects.

1) Originally, through fundamental and technical analysis, you selected a good stock with a promising trend, but it rises slowly or is consolidating strongly. You cannot wait patiently and decide to catch a hot stock for short-term trading based on rumors or market trends, planning to return to your original stock later. The result is often getting slapped both ways. The difficulty of switching from a slow-moving stock to a fast-moving one is very high, and it involves two risks: by the time you discover a hot stock, it has already risen significantly and may drop at any moment; after a small rise or strong consolidation, a fundamentally and technically sound stock may surge at any time, and selling it easily leads to missing out on gains. Moreover, if short-term trading fails and you don't stop losses in time, you will definitely miss subsequent opportunities.

2) Many people recognize that selling high and buying low, rolling operations, can yield relatively large profits and resolve to do so. However, at the end of the year, they find they haven't rolled successfully. The reason is that after selling, they lack the patience to wait for the price to drop and cannot resist the temptation to grab hot stocks for short-term trading. The result is counterproductive.

3) Being fully invested all year round. The stock market shows obvious fluctuation cycles, and during the falling cycle, more than 90% of stocks offer no profit opportunities. Yet many investors refuse to believe this and feel itchy when they see red stocks on the board. They always think they can buy stocks that buck the trend and trade short-term differences, staying fully invested every day. Hoping to improve the utilization rate of funds, they often get stuck —— not stopping losses —— deeply trapped. After all, only a few stocks can buck the trend, and in the falling cycle, today's strong performers may weaken tomorrow, making them difficult to operate. Additionally, being constantly fully invested makes people physically and mentally exhausted, losing their keen market sense and missing true opportunities. Many investors cannot keep money in their hands for more than three days, fearing missed opportunities. Their psychology boils down to the pursuit of maximum profit. Regardless of whether they are major or retail investors, this type of investor suffers heavy losses.

In fact, as long as you seize a few opportunities each year, the returns over a period of time will be quite considerable. That's why Buffett became a master with an annual average return of only 30%. If you are solely focused on maximizing profits, you will end up like a bear picking corn, ultimately achieving the least profit.

The stock market is a place full of opportunities, temptations, and traps. You must learn to resist temptation and give up some opportunities to seize others.

3. Not believing in oneself but blindly trusting others. Many retail investors have learned various analytical methods and techniques and possess a certain level of analytical ability. However, when they carefully study a stock and are ready to buy, they immediately abandon the purchase or switch to another stock just because someone casually says, "This stock isn't good; XX has better themes..." This is baffling! And when the stock they originally chose starts rising, all they can do is regret.

4. Engaging in short-term trading based on already publicly disclosed information or themes. Although everyone knows to sell upon hearing good news, many retail investors (including some seasoned traders) cannot resist placing orders before the afternoon session opens when they see a company's excellent annual report or news of restructuring. They plan to buy at the daily limit price and sell the next morning when the price surges... More than 80% of the results are immediate high-position trapping. It's undeniable that the current market is not yet standardized, and by the time a company announces excellent performance, the major players have already known about it and driven up the stock price significantly. If you were the major player, wouldn't you sell at a good price? Even if you really intend to drive the price higher, would you immediately lift these followers up? Since there are so many people willing to take delivery, why not sell part first and then repurchase after a decline for rolling operation?

5. Spreading rumors around and using hearsay as a basis for selecting stocks. These investors are the easiest victims when major players exit.

Of course, after reading the previous articles, I believe you won't make these mistakes anymore - just like ninth-degree masters don't make simple mistakes, but even chess saints sometimes make blunders. Therefore, psychological training in the stock market should always be kept in mind.

A famous Wall Street trader once said: "Making money in the stock market is fast, but losing money is also fast, and most of the times I lose money happen right after I've made money and become complacent."

Although the current market is weak, I believe these words of experience are still very useful.
