The death of a toddler after eating a hamburger from a chain fast food restaurant uncovered the horrifying secrets hidden in industrially produced beef. Even more terrifying is the truth about the shocking face of large-scale farming, the fatal lies behind excessive corn cultivation, and how major conglomerates monopolize the global food industry for profit, leading to virus outbreaks that endanger human lives. The focus directly points to the United States, the world's largest food supplier, where toxic foods are produced earlier than expected. The documentary delves into the Mexican slaughterhouse of Smithfield Foods, a major U.S. pork supplier, which is identified as the source of the H1N1 virus, revealing shocking truths about food contamination. Your dining table could be filled with ticking time bombs. Can you remain indifferent?

In the film "Food, Inc.", director Robert Kenner unveils the veil of the American food industry, exposing a series of phenomena hidden under highly mechanized industrial processes. For example, the relationship between American consumers and regulatory agencies, the subtle situation between the U.S. Department of Agriculture and the U.S. Food and Drug Administration, and how America’s food supply is now controlled by a few corporations that often prioritize profit above all else, disregarding issues such as customer health, the livelihoods of American farmers, worker safety, and urban environments.

In the market, we see meat larger than ordinary chicken breasts, flawless pork chops, antibiotic-resistant soybean seeds, and even tomatoes that rarely spoil. However, behind their seemingly perfect appearances, new strains of harmful E. coli bacteria have been discovered, causing at least thousands of Americans to fall ill each year. Obesity is also a prominent issue in America, especially among teenagers and those suffering from diabetes.

The film uses interviews with experts like Eric Schlosser, author of "Fast Food Nation," and Michael Pollan, author of "The Omnivore's Dilemma," to convey the director's perspective. It also presents forward-thinking ideas through dialogues with social entrepreneurs like Gary Hirshberg of the world's third-largest yogurt company and Joel Salatin of Polyface Farms located in the Shenandoah Valley of Virginia. The movie exposes alarming truths about the food industry, including how the food people eat is produced and the challenges the nation needs to address.
