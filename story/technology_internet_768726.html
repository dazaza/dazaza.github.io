The core value of Sina Weibo is diluted in the continuous trial and error.

by anonymous on 2013-11-20 11:55:08

Whether it's Sina Weibo or Twitter, the starting points of both are relatively simple: a 140-character text message. Yet, this very message has led the two companies down completely different paths (in terms of their latest capital market performance: Sina at $56.68 billion, Twitter at $239.56 billion—keep in mind that Sina has more than just Weibo as a product).

Weibo is a groundbreaking product that reshapes the relationship between people and information. However, the executor of this breakthrough might no longer be Sina. Sina mimicked the Weibo product concept from Twitter but failed to fully leverage its product philosophy (let alone take it to the extreme). Instead, WeChat outflanked it.

Objectively speaking, the decline in Weibo’s activity does not pose a fatal threat to its brand construction. The prevailing notion in public opinion that WeChat has decisively triumphed over Weibo stems from Sina taking a wrong step at the right time, while its competitor acted decisively at the opportune moment. For such an outcome, I am more inclined to subjectively view it as a coincidence. From the early product iteration of WeChat, these two platforms were far from being incompatible.

Therefore, whether we refer to domestic or international environmental factors, the reason for Sina Weibo's decline lies within itself, specifically with Sina, not Weibo.

The Web 1.0 internet mindset has yet to shift.

Sina is a prime practitioner of genetic determinism.

Weibo, this quasi-Web 2.0 concept of internet service, aims to unleash the immense value of ordinary users as core service objects. To achieve this, it needs to completely overhaul the traditional internet mindset. For users, attracting them to fragmented information displays is the most important and critical task. This is a key characteristic of user experience that differs from the Web 1.0 era (and also a golden rule of the mobile internet era). Clearly, Sina did not pay enough attention to this aspect, so they crammed as many features as possible alongside the user’s information stream.

In 2011, under the backdrop of Sina Weibo accumulating substantial capital, users could temporarily filter out these bulky designs that strongly interfered with their access to information, but this laid the groundwork for Sina Weibo's decline. Competing for space with users is something all internet companies should avoid. This essentially also exposed a common flaw in Sina management’s thinking: treating users as fools.

Unclear self-positioning: failing to clarify the relationship between social networking and media.

If the interface design was just a hidden danger, what follows will be the powerful hand pushing Sina Weibo into the abyss, leading to a series of chemical reactions that directly severed its ties with the mass audience. Given the nature of Weibo as a social media product combined with a weak foundation of robust social relationships, the system inevitably falls prey to the Pareto Principle (the 80/20 rule). That is, less than 20% of the people provide more than 80% of the information—a sociological law verified in the internet ecosystem, determined by mass psychology, which is a hard flaw of Weibo products. Even in America, where people are less inclined to join the crowd, this issue exists. But this is not the crux of the problem; the crucial part lies in Sina's approach to this issue, which truly reveals Sina's behavioral philosophy.

Sina was clearly dissatisfied with its own social media attributes, or rather, it didn’t want to follow the media route. Sina envied Facebook, and FB's brand value along with its own arduous commercialization path solidified its determination to pursue a social network. Thus, Sina Air and WeeMee appeared out of nowhere and then vanished without a trace. Sina wanted to label itself as social while trying to remove the inherently unremovable media label, with predictable results.

Chaotic commercial layout: horizontally, it failed to identify the main body and ignored the value of user information; vertically, it failed to balance commercial promotion with user experience.

Many people often overlook a basic life philosophy: don't forget why you started because you're in a rush to get somewhere. Yes, applying this to Sina, it plays out as a tragedy.

All of Sina's monetization efforts up to now easily prove one fact: there is no reliable data support based on user information behind it, or rather, Sina has not elevated user-generated content to the core area of its commercial strategy. Consider the insertion of recommended messages in the user's information feed, which only began recently, or the still-haunting urn incident. Sina's insufficient ability to mine and integrate data has been directly exposed at the user level. In reality, Sina doesn’t have to chase after others’ footsteps. It can look at what it already possesses, then focus its efforts in one direction (the dispersion of equity most directly impacts the weakness of product R&D), and the result might not be what it is today.

Take a look at how Twitter finds solutions between advertising promotion and user experience. Look at the figure below for a glimpse into the mindset of that blue bird. The image contains three forms of Twitter's business model, corresponding to the positions marked by three yellow arrows. I believe the reason this mindset translates into product implementation lies in the Silicon Valley behavior philosophy practiced by tech giants like Apple and Google.

This behavior philosophy has a basic principle: design can serve as a lubricant between commerce and users. Sina clearly deviates significantly from this theory. But relying solely on design is far from enough to escape the commercial dead end. Design, after all, is a technical compromise. The core value trigger point lies in the user's information flow and timeline. The engineers at Twitter dig much deeper into each tweet beyond its literal value, even reconstructing the user profile behind the information. This is the foundation for precise ad placement.

Twitter, with its superior product development capabilities and highly innovative ad platform (Promoted ad series), broke a logical paradox about microblogging: social value and economic value are inversely correlated. I believe some people still support this view today. This seems like an unbalanced, seemingly correct conclusion, but it’s just that. Because many people judge the commercial operations of Web 2.0 products according to the business models of the traditional internet era, this is where the seeming comes from, and also the hidden logic starting point behind that paradox.

Sina is merely an operator of Weibo, and so is Twitter. What I’m saying is that the decline in the status of Sina Weibo is not a problem with Weibo itself, but Sina's inability to amplify its value. Product and operation are often two different things, and we shouldn’t abandon one because of the other.
