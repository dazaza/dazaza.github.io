India's Mars probe failed to change orbit and did not enter the预定 orbit

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 19:05:40

India's space dream has been expanding rapidly in recent years. On the afternoon of November 5th, the country launched a rocket carrying its first Mars probe, "Mangalyaan," from the Sriharikota Space Launch Center in the Bay of Bengal. Subsequently, the probe successfully entered the geosynchronous orbit and gradually increased its altitude.

However, just as everything seemed to be going smoothly, an accident occurred. According to reports by the Press Trust of India, the Indian Mars probe did not enter the planned orbit after a recent orbital maneuver. On the 11th, India made the fourth adjustment to the orbital height, with the apogee only reaching 78,000 kilometers, while the original plan was for 100,000 kilometers.

According to the plan, "Mangalyaan" will officially head to Mars after circling the Earth 20-25 times and is expected to enter Mars' orbit around September 24th next year. However, this unexpected event will undoubtedly have a significant impact on the implementation of this plan.

It should be noted that currently, Indian media has not clarified whether this failed orbital maneuver will lead to the entire mission's failure or if there is still room for recovery. However, it appears that India's aerospace technology still has deficiencies. Even if "Mangalyaan" eventually gets back on track, it will likely encounter more difficulties on its way to Mars, making the chances of a smooth arrival very slim.

Previously, after the successful launch of the rocket on the 5th, Indian media extensively reported that the country might indeed leapfrog China and Japan, becoming the fourth globally (after the United States, Europe, and Russia) and the first in Asia to possess the capability to conduct Mars missions. However, reality indicates that achieving the dream of being a space superpower is not easy.

According to American media reports, nearly two-thirds of all Mars exploration missions have failed so far. Many of these missions didn't even leave Earth's orbit before failing, while most others encountered accidents when the spacecraft attempted to enter Mars' orbit.

Update:

The latest information shows that the Indian Space Research Organization (ISRO) has clarified the details of this incident.

As per the plan, the main and backup electromagnetic coils are supposed to work simultaneously when needed. However, during the fourth orbit-raising operation, the liquid engine suddenly stopped working, although the attitude control engine continued to function. This resulted in a reduction in incremental speed during the orbital change, achieving only 35 m/s. This figure is much lower than the planned 130 m/s, causing the apogee height to reach only 78,000 kilometers.

For the next step, ISRO plans to perform an auxiliary orbit-raising operation on the rocket at 5:00 AM on the 12th, aiming to increase the apogee to close to 100,000 kilometers.
