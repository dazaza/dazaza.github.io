My 177 Days Immersed in GitHub

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 21:11:48

177 Days of GitHub

This is the story of how I spent 177 consecutive days (almost half a year) on GitHub, consistently contributing code. I'll talk about why I did this and why you should follow my example, or at least do something similar. It's a story about accepting challenges, changing old habits, and knowing when to stop. I benefited greatly from this process and hope it will offer some inspiration. Let me start telling this story.

Github’s 30-Day Challenge

The story begins with a 30-day challenge. On February 21st, my good friend Shuhao messaged me and challenged me: I had to commit code updates to GitHub for 30 consecutive days, and he would do the same. At that time, my longest "streak" on GitHub was only 5 days. This "streak" record was too weak, and I always wanted to improve it but lacked the motivation to take action. This challenging opportunity was exactly what I needed. I accepted the challenge and set out a few simple rules:

Editing the README file does not count as an update unless there are significant changes.

Using scripts for automatic editing is prohibited; all commits must be manually completed.

Committing humorous or irrelevant updates to program logic is forbidden.

I started with a burst of energy—on the first day, I committed nearly 30 updates to my website and other projects. In fact, it was also the most productive day during my nearly half-year "streak." I was determined to complete the 30-day goal, and even more, until one of us gave up. This thought filled me with fighting spirit—I thought it was the best start.

One Week

By the end of the first week, the difficulty of the challenge began to show. There was once when I almost forgot about it and hurriedly submitted the day's update at the last moment. Shuhao gave up on the eighth day; I think he probably just forgot—it happens.

Two Weeks

By the second and third weeks, remembering to submit updates every day became easier. I turned it into a habitual action. Soon, this habit was tied to brushing my teeth, which is something I rarely forget to do. Meanwhile, I started reading a book called *The Power of Habit*, which helped me understand habits and how to change them for better results. If you're interested in how your brain processes habits, I recommend this book. It's fascinating, and it includes a story about a person who could never remember the way home but could walk around the neighborhood without getting lost due to the power of habit. But let's not get sidetracked. Before I realized the power of habit, it had already led me to...

Thirty Days!

Thirty days was an exciting milestone! I made it! I just wanted to keep going! After thirty days, the "streak" had become a lifestyle. Eating, sleeping, submitting updates—it had become routine, and I could easily incorporate it into my daily schedule. Like all habits, it was hard to maintain 100% consistency, so sometimes I had to scramble at the last minute. I even submitted updates from my phone, after late-night gatherings with friends, and even in a "Ballmer Peak" state. This reminded me of some small tricks I learned about submitting code to GitHub:

GitHub Code Update Tips:

GitHub counts time based on Pacific Standard Time (PST), meaning I could submit code between midnight and 3 AM Eastern Standard Time (EST) and it would still count as the previous day's task. This trick saved me several times when I got home very late.

Recording an issue counts as a submission, which has saved me a few times when I couldn't think of anything new.

Commits and code uploads are counted separately. Submitting now and uploading later will still count by the time of the initial commit.

The first commit of a new repository is not included in the statistics, but the second commit will count both times, which is a bit weird.

Merge requests count as one submission when created and another when merged. If the two branches don't merge, then the second commit doesn't count.

Although these tips undoubtedly saved my "streak" multiple times, I still adhered to the agreements I initially listed. You can find more GitHub tips here.

One Hundred Days

One hundred days was also an important moment for me. I was interning at Mozilla in San Francisco at the time. You can read all about that experience on my blog. One of the best things about interning at Mozilla is that everything is open source, so I basically just needed to work to keep my "streak" uninterrupted. Although it wasn't as easy as it sounds, being at Mozilla definitely helped a lot.

There were also some competitors within Mozilla, including Shuhao, who restarted his "streak" this summer while interning with me, and he succeeded much more this time. According to rumors at Mozilla, there was also another competitor silently competing with me, though I didn't know who they were.

One Hundred Fifty Days

At this point, my motivation to continue the "streak" suddenly dropped sharply. I attributed this to a lack of new ideas, a desire to pursue new challenges, and the realization that the "streak" would inevitably end someday. These factors, combined with the numerous ways to cheat (like what this guy did), made the challenge feel increasingly meaningless.

I wanted to escape. I wanted a break, to completely forget about GitHub. I just wanted the freedom to think of new ideas without worrying about committing just for the sake of committing. So, I decided to end the "streak" with a whole number. I decided to continue until 200 days, and then I could sit back calmly and watch my "streak" record gradually fall apart.

Uh-Oh!

I ultimately failed to reach the 200-day goal, forgetting to commit on the 178th day. Realizing the tragedy, I also felt a weight lifted off my shoulders, feeling a sense of relief. It was finally over.

Retrospective

These 177 days of "streaking" taught me a lot about habits. It taught me that good habits, if followed too rigidly, can also turn into bad things; it made me realize the power of "Don't Break the Chain," which was the driving force behind the success of the GitHub "streak." I still regularly commit updates to GitHub now, but the "must" has decreased, and the "need" has increased. Sometimes I step away from my computer for a few days to refresh my ideas, and when I come back, I feel more energized. I recommend everyone try using this method to break an old habit or build a new one, but it might be too powerful and make you addicted, so be careful!

If you know anyone who has a longer GitHub "streak" than mine, please let me know! I haven't found anyone yet! :)

Happy coding! Follow me on GitHub! Here's the discussion on HN.

[Translator's Notes]

Ballmer Peak: A legendary rule discovered by Microsoft in the late 1980s, suggesting that programmers have superhuman programming abilities when their blood alcohol concentration is between 0.129% and 0.138%. However, this ability comes with side effects; don't give a team enough whiskey to last a year and expect them to work efficiently—Windows ME is a cautionary tale.

Don’t Break the Chain: A self-motivation method where each day you achieve your goal, you mark it on a calendar. Over time, the marks form a chain, motivating you to continue achieving your daily goals without breaking the chain.
