What are the types of vaginitis?

by tttt755 on 2011-06-08 15:04:18

Under normal physiological conditions, the anatomical and biochemical characteristics of vaginal tissue are sufficient to defend against microbial invasion. However, once this defense is compromised, pathogens can take advantage of various factors, leading to vaginitis. What types of vaginitis are there? Experts from Beijing Jington Hospital mainly introduce the following types:

1. **Candidiasis Vaginitis**

Candidiasis vaginitis is caused by an infection of a type of fungus called *Candida albicans*. Contrary to trichomoniasis, this fungus grows particularly well in acidic environments and is generally transmitted through contact.

2. **Pediatric Vaginitis**

Commonly seen in infants aged 1-5 years. It often occurs due to sitting or crawling on the ground or due to foreign objects entering the vagina. Pathogens can also be transmitted via the child's mother or washing utensils. Symptoms include redness and swelling of the vulva, watery vaginal discharge, and itching of the vulva.

3. **Trichomonas Vaginitis**

The *Trichomonas vaginalis* parasite has strong adaptability and can survive for a day in semi-dry towels, up to 21 days at temperatures of 3℃-5℃, and even five days in tap water. Therefore, this condition is quite common among women. It can be directly transmitted from male carriers during sexual intercourse, indirectly through bathhouses or swimming pools, and also through medical instruments.

4. **Simple Vaginitis**

The most common cause of simple vaginitis is damage after childbirth or abortion, long-term use of uterine supports, or mechanical stimulation and infections by pus-forming bacteria. Additionally, infectious secretions from the uterus or cervix that frequently irritate the vaginal mucosa can also lead to simple vaginitis.

5. **Suppurative Vaginitis**

Commonly seen in women with vaginal tears or obstetric injuries. Symptoms include increased vaginal discharge, which appears yellow and purulent, with a fishy odor, a burning sensation and pain in the vagina, and redness and swelling of the mucosa.

6. **Non-specific Vaginitis**

This is a mixed bacterial infection. Any vaginal inflammation not caused by *Candida albicans*, *Trichomonas vaginalis*, or gonorrhea is classified as non-specific vaginitis, also known as bacterial vaginosis.

7. **Menstrual Vaginitis**

Often results from poor menstrual hygiene, especially the use of unclean menstrual products that contaminate the external genitalia. Symptoms include a feeling of heaviness and burning in the perineum and increased vaginal discharge.

8. **Honeymoon Vaginitis**

Commonly seen in newlywed women. Primarily caused by poor hygiene of the sexual organs and sexual activity. Symptoms include increased vaginal discharge, itching and pain inside and outside the vagina, and redness and swelling of the mucosa.

9. **Atrophic Vaginitis (Senile Vaginitis)**

Commonly seen in elderly women post-menopause. Due to the decline in ovarian function and insufficient estrogen secretion, physiological defenses decrease, allowing pathogenic bacteria to invade and cause infection. Therefore, it is also called atrophic vaginitis. Symptoms include increased vaginal discharge, which may appear like yellow water, and in severe cases, blood-tinged purulent discharge, along with itching and a burning sensation in the vulva.
