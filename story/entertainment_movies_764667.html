Synopsis:

A responsible man always feels that working hard and earning money to provide a comfortable life for his family is the duty of a husband and father. At this point, he doesn't realize that the opportunities missed to share happiness with his family will become permanent regrets... Frank Goode (Robert De Niro) spent the first half of his life working at a local wire manufacturing plant, using every penny he earned to support his family. Recently, not long after retiring, Frank gradually realized that in the past, he had spent too little time with his four children, and now it was time to rebuild warm family ties.

For a long time, Frank’s deceased wife served as the communication hub between him and his children. Now he has made up his mind that it is his responsibility to take care of the children. So he suddenly decided to invite all his family members for a picnic on the weekend. Everything was going smoothly at first, but as the helplessness of modern life would have it, they one by one informed Frank that they could not attend the family gathering, each with an irrefutable good reason.

Despite serious warnings from the doctor about his health condition, Frank still decided to solve all the problems on his own. He packed his bags and prepared for a cross-country trip through the United States, just to give his children scattered across the country a surprise, then immerse himself in their success and happiness, enjoying true family joy.

Frank decided to go see his son David (Austin Lysy), who is an artist in New York; his daughter Amy (Kate Beckinsale), who has achieved great success in the advertising industry in Chicago; his son Robert (Sam Rockwell), who is now the conductor of an orchestra in Denver – lastly, his youngest daughter Lucy (Drew Barrymore), who is a well-known dancer in Las Vegas… But soon after, Frank immediately understood that his children were not nearly as happy or successful as his wife often said.

Upon returning home, Frank had to face this heavy reality, as his journey revealed tragic secrets within the family… However, Frank is both insightful and confident, and he decided to fulfill the wish his wife often talked about, which is to help the children get out of their deep troubles and live truly perfect lives.

Behind-the-scenes Production:

[The Value and Importance of Family]

"Everybody's Fine" is a remake of the famous filmmaker Giuseppe Tornatore's Italian film of the same name from 1990. It tells about a current family issue that cannot be ignored: parents and grown-up children living far apart. Because life is so stressful, they can't find time to call their parents and chat, focusing too much on their own families and friends, forgetting to "come home often"... No family is perfect. If a family strives to present a beautiful and harmonious appearance, it means it hides more secrets than others - the Good family is also like this. Frank works hard to create a better living environment for his family and help them achieve their possibilities. Therefore, when Frank reaches 60 years old, he always feels that he has lost a lot of life's pleasures because he indeed missed his children's growth. With free time finally available, Frank decides to make some amends and re-establish good connections with his children. Without hesitation, he embarks on a "family search" journey across the entire country. However, he ultimately realizes that the information about his children provided by his wife constantly was not true. She automatically ignored the bad things and only told him the good ones.

It seems that such a warm story exploring the origin of family values could only be nurtured and grow in a country like Italy, which emphasizes the importance of family values the most. The director of the original film, Giuseppe Tornatore, read the original script of "Everybody's Fine" in Italian in the early 1970s... Producer Gianni Nunnari kept imagining that a film about fathers and families, brought to the top level of Italian cinema by Tornatore, if placed in the broad national environment of the United States, would surely generate the same emotional impact, and then combine into the broader framework of American comedy - thus, Nunnari directly went to Italy to buy the film rights, hoping to retell this story one day with a different cultural sensitivity.

The next problem Gianni Nunnari needed to face was finding an English-speaking filmmaker to "translate" the story in the film again and transplant it into the modern American environment. After watching the highly praised box office hit comedy "Waking Ned," he felt that screenwriter and director Kirk Jones was the person he was looking for - Jones injected a very obvious British style into the film, which could perfectly showcase the bright, cheerful touches and profound emotions of the original work... Thus, Nunnari immediately sent Tornatore's Italian original version of the film to Jones, wanting to know if it could spark his inspiration.

Since directing his feature film debut "Waking Ned," Kirk Jones has watched the film achieve astonishing results as the second-highest-grossing film of that year - of course, this number is compared to the film's budget. In 2005, Jones directed "Nanny McPhee" for Universal Pictures and the UK-based Working Title Films, starring and adapted by Emma Thompson... However, it wasn't until 2007 when Jones received Nunnari's original version of "Everybody's Fine" that he finally decided on his next project. Jones admitted that he has always been a fan of Giuseppe Tornatore, so for him, seeing an Italian-language film first and then transforming its story into a script was definitely an unusual experience, but he was quickly deeply attracted to this idea. Jones recalled: "I've been looking for my first fully Americanized film, while also expecting to write and direct it myself as I did before. This means I need a film that resonates with me right away upon arriving in America, rather than being constrained by the studios and shooting a proceduralized work that has passed through a dozen screenwriters' hands. Sometimes, my idea is simple - writing a script alone and then presenting it as a director, just like the original 'Waking Ned.' For me, finding a suitable and worthwhile story to develop is like buying a house - you instinctively know whether it fits your imagination from the start. When I watched the Italian version of 'Everybody's Fine,' it took only a few minutes for me to emotionally connect with it, deeply moved and laughing out loud. I never thought that such a simple story could release such immense passion."

[An Englishman's Perspective on American Families]

When Gianni Nunnari received the reply and learned that Kirk Jones really wanted to take over the adaptation of this film, he was extremely happy. Nunnari said: "Jones clearly expressed his attitude - he wants to start from scratch, borrowing the central topic of the original work to create a completely new story... The script he wrote not only includes some slightly bizarre characters but also unfolds a great American journey. I found that Jones expresses his cinematic talent in this way to be incredibly dreamlike - he directly grasps all human emotions." Jones continued: "I only watched the original three times because I didn't want to simply 'translate' it into another language... I think Giuseppe Tornatore wouldn't expect me to do that either. I need to retell it in my own way, and what truly attracted me is the theme of family inside, which is a very universal social issue that everyone is currently facing or will face in the future."

But Kirk Jones also realized that he faced an undeniable challenge - as a Briton, he now had to create an American road movie. Jones said: "I never start working without preparation. I specially flew to New York and then began a cross-country journey in my own way - just like Frank does in the movie, staying in the cheapest motels, only taking long-distance buses and trains, and constantly talking to passengers beside me, learning about some hidden aspects of this country in the best possible way... I took nearly 2000 photos and recorded more than 100 interviews, all from people willing to chat with me, from taxi drivers to hotel clerks, and strange passengers on long-distance buses. A lot of inspirations for making the film come from here, including telephone lines, bizarre personalities and real people, and wheeled packages, all seen and used during my journey."

Perhaps precisely because Kirk Jones's perspective is different, as an "outsider," he has uncovered some deep secrets of American families, which makes his screenplay have such significant characteristics. Producer Callum Greene explained: "As a Briton, Jones brings a completely different view and opinion to 'Everybody's Fine,' yet can still gain recognition from us Americans. He finds many good things in the daily life of a working-class family, which is exactly what we are gradually losing."

Kirk Jones himself is also a father of three children, so he naturally connects with such a story personally. He understands the feeling of doing everything possible to provide the best life for his children, but he also knows the complex alternation of parental emotions. Jones said: "Any dad understands similar feelings, that is, the irreconcilable contradictions Frank experiences in the story - he realizes that he spends too little time with his children and family because he has to work long hours to provide a secure life for his beloved relatives. Working in a wire manufacturing factory, Frank needs to work two shifts a day, leaving home before the kids wake up and coming back when they're already asleep... Balancing work and family is the most challenging difficult choice modern parents face. Although it seems unchanged, computers, emails, text messages, and phones allow us to stay connected all the time, but they also prevent us from truly relaxing and paying attention to the importance of family and relatives."

Frank finally wakes up, realizing what he has lost in his life, and he also learns that the children are not living the perfect life described by his deceased wife - this deeply moves Kirk Jones, who says: "There are no perfect children in this world, nor are there perfect families... 'Everybody's Fine' focuses on a process of cognition and growth. People become more patient, which is important not only for parents but also for children. But in modern society, the expectations placed on children are extreme and radical. From a young age, they are taught reading and writing under their parents' instructions, including playing musical instruments, then comes the increasingly heavy academic burden at school, leaving children almost no time to rest properly, including listening to stories or taking naps. We discuss the environment of the modern world where the 'American Dream' is so popular, and the people I met during my travels, who all believe that if you work hard, you can harvest everything you want, so I begin to question whether children have become these people's unrealistic expectations? They impose their will on the next generation, claiming it is for the good of their children?"

Trivia:

- The film is a remake of Italian director Giuseppe Tornatore's 1990 film of the same name. The original film starred Italian actor Marcello Mastroianni, representing his later years.

- The main creator of the Beatles, Paul McCartney, specifically composed a new song "I Want to Come Home" for the film.

- The film won Robert De Niro the Hollywood Actor Award at the Hollywood Film Festival.

- Before Kirk Jones brought the script to Robert De Niro, De Niro had never seen any films directed by Jones.
