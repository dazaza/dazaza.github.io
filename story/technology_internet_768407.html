A dramatic opening

On the morning of November 7th, Eastern Time, microblogging master Twitter officially listed on the New York Stock Exchange. Around 9:20 am, the three co-founders of Twitter (Jack Dorsey, Biz Stone, Evan Williams) and CEO Dick Costolo were all present, like four Batmen, looking very spirited! But just as everyone was eagerly waiting for these four well-dressed gentlemen to walk up to the bell-ringing platform, what came into view was a rather 'quirky' combination. Hmm? The scene suddenly became restless. Where did this group of old, middle-aged, and young people come from (please see the picture)? Originally, the middle-aged woman on the left is a police officer from Boston; the nine-year-old girl in the middle works at a lemonade stand, and all her earnings are used to fight against slavery; the bald grandpa on the right is the famous British actor Patrick Steward. Many media friends at the scene couldn't help but wonder, why is Patrick up there, haha.

All three are Twitter users! In order to express gratitude for everyone's love and support for Twitter along the way, the leadership team believed there was no one more suitable than the 'users' to ring the bell! Such originality, we must admire!

An epic opening

Previously, Twitter's IPO issue price was increased twice (from $17-$20), and finally set at $26, with a financing scale reaching $1.82 billion, making it the second-largest IPO in the history of the internet sector (Facebook ranks first). CEO Dick Costolo said before the bell-ringing ceremony, "Twitter has now become an indispensable part of everyone's life. Our investors are all loyal fans of Twitter, loving Twitter from both the 'user' and 'investor parent' perspectives." Cough cough, such investors indeed expressed their love for Twitter with action: after a long IPO allocation, Twitter finally opened at an epic $45.1 per share, up 73% from the issue price.

By the close of US stock market trading, Twitter's closing price on its first day of listing was reported at $44.90, up 72.69% from the issue price, with a market value reaching $24.46 billion!

What do Silicon Valley's tech circle and investment circle think?

Everyone, stay calm! In the two days before the listing, the editor was also busy, interviewing many tech experts and investment figures in Silicon Valley, collecting their views here, absolutely factual!

1. Are you optimistic about Twitter's listing? Is Twitter worth investing in?

More than 80% of respondents are optimistic about Twitter's listing, believing that Twitter will definitely not disappoint. Some investors believe that Twitter will be more successful than Facebook and LinkedIn because it has captured the crucial 'interest graph', which has more potential than the 'social graph' of the latter two. Someone mentioned that although today's Twitter has never made a profit, we can see shadows of LinkedIn and Amazon in it; investors will receive substantial returns. Twitter's revenue growth will reach 85% in the next 12 months, increasing from $535 million to $990 million. When Facebook went public, it was only predicted to have a 36% revenue increase (over a 12-month period). Moreover, Twitter's mobile business is growing rapidly; it was born for mobile devices, without a doubt.

Of course, the remaining 20% of respondents maintain a neutral stance towards Twitter's listing, believing that the profitability challenge of its advertising solutions is a 'perpetual theme'. Additionally, multiple respondents mentioned that Twitter needs to further innovate in advertising. Moreover, the internet, especially social networks, changes in the blink of an eye, and users themselves don't even know what they will need or like tomorrow; more meaningful websites/platforms keep emerging, and Twitter faces challenges no smaller than anyone else's. Furthermore, many respondents question Twitter's high valuation. The valuation of most companies is about 6-7 times their total revenue from the past year, which is relatively reasonable; however, Twitter's current valuation is 15 times the revenue forecast for the year after next, which seems a bit unreliable, and a bubble may be coming.

2. Is Twitter's profit model reliable? Current advertising solutions etc.

More than half of the respondents believe that although Twitter's advertising solutions haven't made the company profitable, they still have their merits; they're definitely more reliable and humanized than traditional media and Sina Weibo's advertising placement methods! Making money through advertisements (which is inherently nothing wrong), simply means trying not to harm user experience while running ads. How much innovation can you expect?! Whether on PC or mobile, Twitter has done quite well and continues to actively explore.

The remaining approximately 40% of respondents, however, believe that Twitter's current profit model still doesn't impress them.

3. With Twitter being so hot, what does it mean for Sina Weibo?

All respondents believe that Twitter's successful listing will have a positive impact on Sina Weibo.

But more than 80% of respondents simultaneously believe (anyone with eyes can see) that Twitter and Sina Weibo have already become different in nature; the former focuses on 'news', while the latter emphasizes 'social'. Twitter's competitor is Facebook, but the two are more like occupying two different camps. Sina Weibo, on the other hand, is different; its main competitor is the social giant WeChat, facing far greater competitive pressure than Twitter. However, such stories might not be fully understood by general investors in the U.S., who see Sina Weibo as merely a Chinese version of Twitter. So, Sina Weibo can at least continue selling the beautiful myth of 'news revolution' that Twitter left behind, hehe.
