In the blink of an eye, half a year has passed.

by was65752 on 2011-06-13 16:27:57

My baby has transformed from a skinny tiger with just skin and bones to a little tiger with a proper weight. Having been a mother for half a year, while working on my physics graduation thesis, I have gained some small insights which I would like to share as advice to my fellow classmates who are about to become mothers.

Breastfeeding

Nowadays, if conditions allow, breastfeeding is definitely the way to go, because its benefits for both mother and child are self-evident. During pregnancy, I seemed to have read in a book that if a woman has her first child after the age of 30, breast milk usually isn't enough for the child, so I was scared enough to carry back a lot of formula from afar. After giving birth, because the child was too small (four catties and four taels), she would always take a few bites and then stop, resulting in each feeding taking around an hour. The elders said there wouldn't be enough milk, so they added formula. Each time, after drinking breast milk, she would drink another 20-30ml of formula, and during this period, her weight increased quite well. However, after more than a month, she refused to touch the bottle nipple anymore, and every time she drank formula, she would cry loudly, forcing us to temporarily stop the formula. Because she didn't drink formula, she had to breastfeed more frequently, sometimes almost continuously breastfeeding all day except when sleeping. As a result, a few days later, the breast milk gradually increased and became sufficient. Therefore, as long as there is breast milk, let the child eat more, it should generally be enough, but you must withstand the pressure from family members, as they always think the child will starve.

Nasal Congestion

I don't know how other children are, but our child experienced nasal congestion at one month old, especially worse at night (always getting choked and crying). I always thought it was a cold, so the vaccination date was postponed by more than half a month due to nasal congestion. After going to the hospital, it turned out that the nose wasn't very congested that morning, and the doctor even scolded me, saying not to bring the child to the hospital unnecessarily. However, they still prescribed Johnson's medicine, and after taking it at home, the symptoms improved significantly at night. However, this medicine should not be used for more than 5 days, as excessive use is harmful to children. After stopping the medication, the nasal congestion returned. We tried many methods, including applying warm towels to the top of the head and warm gauze to the nose, but with little effect. For nearly a month, the nasal congestion gradually resolved itself. Therefore, nasal congestion in children might be due to incomplete development of their nasal parts, and it naturally improves as they grow older. So, if the child only has nasal congestion without coughing or yellow pus-filled nasal discharge, there's no need to worry too much.

Child-rearing Books

I've read several books, and currently, the most practical one is the Japanese Child-rearing Encyclopedia. I believe that for first-time parents, this book is essential. The American Attachment Parenting Encyclopedia focuses on one main idea: satisfy your child, give him security, and don't fight with your child; pick him up quickly when he cries. I read a post suggesting that when a child cries, leave her aside, and she will eventually stop crying. At first, our baby cried a lot, and when I left her alone, she stopped crying, but I noticed that she became less affectionate towards me. From this book, I learned that our baby could be considered a high-needs child who needs to be held more often to provide ample security. Thus, I would hold her whenever possible, even if she cried, allowing her to cry in my arms. Gradually, I understood her life patterns, such as what expressions and actions indicate tiredness or hunger, immediately satisfying her needs, which reduced her crying significantly. Now, she mainly cries when I go to work; upon waking and finding me absent, she will cry loudly until falling asleep again. This might be because I raised her alone since she was young, and she doesn't trust others yet. I hope this gets better as she grows older.

I think every child is different, and finding the best way to interact with yours is key.

I am quite supportive of attachment parenting. Don't apply adult methods to deal with your child; he is so small, and all his needs are for a sense of security. We lose only a bit of freedom and time, but this lasts just two or three years. Later, when you want to be close to your child, he might not even want to anymore.
