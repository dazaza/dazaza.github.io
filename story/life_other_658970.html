Memories Washed by Tears

To paint a person, one would use the most beautiful colors in the world; yet to write about someone, I cannot find a single word or phrase to commemorate. Tonight is the Lantern Festival, and I am alone in my room, sitting on the bed. The fatigue from a day of travel still lingers, and my empty stomach has been hastily filled.

In just one Spring Festival, I have already felt as if I've lived a lifetime. Or perhaps it could be said that though it was a brief period, it may take a lifetime to forget, and when we can't forget, we can only reminisce through countless sleepless nights.

My nose is already tingling, and the eyes that I once thought were dry and incapable of shedding another tear are now moist. Mentioning a person brings me to tears because she was my grandmother, someone who loved me selflessly without me ever having to ask for it.

I don’t understand, and even now, I cannot accept, believe, or come to terms with the fact that this has become reality!

When my brother’s daughter turned twelve, I returned home and had dinner with my grandparents. She told both my grandfather and me that she couldn’t eat anything, and we both assumed it was just her appetite being off. And I, too, regarded my grandmother, who never took medicine or got injections, as invincible, focusing all my attention on that little niece.

We ate at a restaurant, and everyone was busy fussing over the child. Relatives and friends drank and ate, and no one thought about the two elderly people at home. I knew my grandmother cherished my brother deeply, and seeing his child must have brought her immense joy deep down, knowing that her grandson had become a father.

The next day, I was set to return to Jinan. Before leaving, I decided to visit my maternal grandmother. Early in the morning, my paternal grandmother came over to ask when I would leave. She had boiled salted duck eggs for me, prepared pastries and egg cakes, and gathered many things she herself wouldn’t dare to indulge in. She always believed I didn’t eat well outside and that my life was harder than anyone else's. When I told her I would leave at noon, she went home reassured, planning to come see me off before noon. But then I changed my mind, and my mother and I decided to go visit my maternal grandmother earlier.

When I went to my paternal grandmother's house, my grandfather said she might have gone out to pull weeds. I made two trips back and forth, waited a while, but she still hadn’t returned, so I had to head home. On my way out, I saw people sitting on the front street, chatting idly—cousins, aunts, and grandmothers.

My mother took me to the riverbank, hoping to spot my grandmother pulling weeds in the small forest, but ultimately, I couldn’t find her. Instead, I remembered how every time I left, she would stand far away watching me until neither of us could see each other anymore, and she would walk home her way while I departed mine.

This time, she didn’t see me off. It wasn’t until later that I found out she had been sitting on the front street that day, and no one had told her I was looking for her. She also didn’t know I had left. When she returned home, my grandfather scolded her, saying I had already gone!

It was only then that I realized my growth was actually depriving me. Before and after her surgery, I witnessed her suffering in the hospital, and we naively believed that everything would get better afterward. A gastric tube was inserted into her mouth, causing unbearable pain, but she endured it silently, not telling anyone. Her tolerance extended to people, situations, and even her own pain.

At a friend’s wedding, she briefly appeared, taking out some preserved ham that she rarely ate to give me. Another month passed, and during New Year's Day, she was making a comb for me using sorghum stalks. After her surgery, her health deteriorated significantly, but I didn’t realize that while I was chasing happiness elsewhere, I was gradually losing something far greater.

As I embarked on a path from which there was no return, so did she. The distance between us grew increasingly wider. By the time I turned back, it was too late, and an entire lifetime had passed between us.

The last time I called home before the New Year, my mother said they had taken my grandmother for a follow-up checkup, and aside from emphysema, there were no other issues. At that moment, I was grateful to heaven for once again favoring our family. However, my mother also warned me not to worry, hinting that this disease would eventually take her life. Despite my intelligence, I failed to foresee this outcome.

Over the phone, my grandmother mumbled, "Fei, I'm fine, don't worry about me!" My nose tingled as I hung up, repeatedly questioning whether I should continue holding on.

By the time I returned home at year-end, she was already bedridden, unable to move. Her condition worsened day by day. We tried everything to save her, convincing ourselves that all her symptoms were merely due to the cold weather and flu. Counting the days, we eagerly awaited the end of the bitter winter, hoping she could bask in the sun once more.

After eating, her stomach would bloat. I cried and pleaded with her to eat, saying, "I'm not married yet, they're all grown up, but I'm not!" She replied that she wanted to eat too, wishing desperately to recover quickly so she could see us all happy.

I knew she didn’t lose to the illness but to fate.

She advised me not to meddle in others' feelings and not to interfere with their love. I promised her that I would get along well with my siblings, strive hard, and let her rest assured.

Because I left home that time without letting her see me off, she used her departure without my farewell as retribution.

But that night, I stayed awake with tears, wondering if this was a form of spiritual companionship. Everyone consoled me, saying that old age is inevitable, and death is a part of life that no one can escape. These words I can accept and understand, but what I cannot acknowledge is that her figure will never appear before my eyes again, and her voice will never reach my ears.

I’m not afraid that she will be lonely there because her eldest son has already preceded her, and there will still be someone to honor her. What I cannot face, however, is this world without her—it is so pale, so cold.

Perhaps it’s because I’m tired and my immunity is low that I feel so disheartened. Now that my grandmother is gone, there will never be another person who loves me in her way, cherishing me as she did. Her rough hands are still so vivid, as if they gently caressed my face just yesterday. But now, no matter how many tears fall, I can only wipe them away myself.

Should I learn to let go and allow her to depart peacefully?

In memory of my grandmother
