Opportunities or Threats in the New 200 Series Steel Grade Stainless Steel Pipe Industry

The Opportunity or Threat of Old 200 Series Steel Without Rust Stainless Steel Pipe Industry

The chromium-manganese austenitic stainless steel pipe (the "standard 200 series") has clear, systematic, and documented technical performance specifications and materials. Long-term practices in South America and India indicate that this material can be used in some specific applications. However, it is worth noting that recently, especially in Southeast Asia and foreign countries, there has been a large-scale use of new, more economical chromium-manganese steel grades. These grades can be referred to as the "new 200 series" grades.

These new grades have similar chemical compositions, primarily characterized by reduced chromium content (≤15%) and extremely low nickel content. In some cases, these grades also include copper elements. Additionally, the content of trace elements in these grades can be difficult to control. The expansion of production and usage of these "new 200 series" grades does not align well with the reality that post-steel users lack sufficient understanding and awareness of these products. Therefore, under the misguided mindset of users solely pursuing cost reduction, or even completely ignoring the conditions required for these materials to perform normally, there is a risk of inappropriate use of these materials.

Currently, there are no domestic standards or references for the proper use of "old 200 series" steel grades. It should be pointed out that many properties of certain so-called "new 200 series" grades cannot match those of high-quality traditional stainless steel pipes, such as corrosion resistance, formability, and weldability. It is well known that chromium-nickel austenitic stainless steel is non-magnetic, but the "new 200 series" grades are also non-magnetic, leading users who purchase these products to mistakenly believe they are chromium-nickel austenitic stainless steel. In fact, there are currently fraudulent practices in the market regarding the original steel grades, which pose a serious threat to the good image of stainless steel pipe products!

Concerns about the "old 200 series" steel grades go far beyond just these issues. A very important aspect lies in the problems during the scrap steel recovery process. If scrap steel is not properly managed, we will be unable to monitor the mixing of this scrap steel with other types of scrap steel. The success of stainless steel relies on features such as high product quality, long service life, and durability. Without these guarantees, whether for end-users, stainless steel product enterprises, or stainless steel manufacturers, the various risks mentioned above will eventually turn into a nightmare. Starting from providing long-term solutions, the International Stainless Steel Forum (ISSF) believes it has a responsibility to organize dialogues between product users and suppliers, helping users fully understand the products and make further reasonable choices. All ISSF member companies are committed to protecting the interests of the stainless steel industry, and the quality of products produced by member companies should always be trustworthy.

Any user using stainless steel products afterward should consider the performance of the steel grade, compare it with the natural conditions and application conditions of the steel. We strongly recommend that end-users consult reputable and knowledgeable suppliers when considering the use of the 200 series grades. Only such suppliers can provide products of unknown origin and reliable quality and offer correct advice. Any place considering the use of the 200 series grades can find the most suitable chromium-nickel steel (300 series) and ferritic steel (400 series) products as alternatives.
