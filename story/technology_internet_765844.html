Reuters: Ballmer's Microsoft era nears end

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:09:40

Introduction: Reuters published an article on Friday titled "Steve Ballmer Ends Microsoft's Super Salesman Era" (Steve Ballmer ends run as Microsoft's relentless salesman). The article comments that under Ballmer’s leadership, Microsoft focused more on sales and marketing rather than technology and innovation. However, with his impending retirement, the company's development priorities may shift.

The following is the full text of the article:

Bad Timing

Few CEOs have wielded as much power as Steve Ballmer of Microsoft.

However, despite helping Bill Gates grow this small Seattle suburb company into the global leader in software, this top Microsoft salesman never received the respect he deserved.

Ballmer, who announced his retirement plans on Friday, had bad timing. He took over from Gates at the end of the company's explosive growth phase, coinciding with the rapid rise of Apple.

Born in Detroit, Ballmer met Gates at Harvard and officially became CEO of Microsoft in January 2000. However, given that this was just before the .com bubble burst, his luck was indeed poor. Microsoft's stock price subsequently suffered greatly and has yet to recover.

Despite tripling Microsoft's revenue during his tenure and leading the company through crises that devastated other tech companies, Ballmer continued to face criticism for management errors.

During his term, Apple and Google far outpaced Microsoft in emerging fields such as online search, smartphones, and tablets, relegating it to a relic of a bygone era.

“He tried his best; no one doubts his dedication to Microsoft,” said McAdams Wright Ragen analyst Sid Parakh, “What he lacked was execution.”

In 2008, he attempted to acquire the declining internet giant Yahoo for $47 billion, but the company's value has since significantly decreased. Although Yahoo rejected the offer at the time, this surprising decision ironically saved Ballmer.

Just last month, Ballmer initiated a major restructuring of Microsoft, aiming to transform the company into a "devices and services" company. But most shareholders seemed unmoved.

Ballmer has never lacked passion and energy for Microsoft, but throughout his career, he has always been more of a salesman and cheerleader than a technological visionary.

"This is the most expensive phone in the world, and it won't appeal to business users," Ballmer once mockingly commented on the iPhone in a television interview in 2007. Five years later, the sales of the iPhone alone exceeded the entire revenue of Microsoft.

To bond with employees, Ballmer once wildly danced on stage screaming. That video has nearly 5 million views on YouTube, earning him the nickname "Monkey Boy." Additionally, his manic shouting of "Developers, developers, developers" at a company meeting continues to make him a laughingstock even years later.

Personal Traits

Claimed to be a math prodigy, Ballmer met Gates at Harvard University in 1975. Not long after, Gates dropped out to found Microsoft. Five years later, Gates convinced Ballmer to leave Stanford Business School to become Microsoft's first business manager as its 30th employee, receiving an 8% stake in the company.

As Microsoft's first salesman, he seemed to always lack people's trust.

"The first time we met, I thought this guy was like someone from the Russian internal security force," said Microsoft co-founder Paul Allen in his memoir. "His eyes were very piercing, and he was physically strong. However, as we got to know each other better, I discovered he also had a gentle side."

Allen mentioned that Ballmer enjoyed basketball and jogging when he was younger. If they stayed in the same hotel room while traveling for business, he would wake him up at 7 a.m. to do push-ups.

When Microsoft drove the PC revolution in the 1980s and 1990s, the scale and importance of the sales department, led by Ballmer, grew increasingly significant. Given Ballmer's bald head, tall stature, and loud voice, he was always noticeable whether in the company campus or at industry conferences.

In Microsoft's internal language, employees were either "Bill's people" or "Steve's people," referring to technical personnel and sales/marketing personnel respectively.

When Ballmer, known as "SteveB" among employees, took over as CEO of Microsoft from Gates in 2000, some saw it as a victory of sales over technology and innovation, predicting it would ultimately destroy the company.

According to Walter Isaacson's biography of Steve Jobs, the late co-founder of Apple, Steve Jobs, once said: "I have my own understanding of IBM's or Microsoft's decline: they started to care less about product quality and more about having excellent salesmen."

Acceptable Performance

Jobs was partly right. During Ballmer's tenure, Microsoft did miss the transition to smartphones and tablets, and its stock price remained virtually unchanged for a decade. Meanwhile, Ballmer seemed relentless in sidelining executives who might threaten his position.

"Ballmer has always been the biggest obstacle to Microsoft's stock price," famous hedge fund manager David Einhorn said in 2011, working hard to promote Ballmer's departure. After the news of Ballmer's retirement was released, Microsoft's stock surged 7% on Friday.

But Ballmer's CEO record isn't as bad as many imagine. People close to him say that his boisterous public persona masked his calm and thoughtful private nature. And as Microsoft matured, his management brought it to its peak potential. No one else would have done any better.

He not only boosted Microsoft's revenue but also doubled its already substantial profits. When Novell and Sun disappeared, he successfully steered Microsoft away from doom and avoided the turmoil experienced by HP and AOL.

"I believe anyone else managing Microsoft wouldn't have done much better than Ballmer," said Eric Jackson, manager of the hedge fund Ironfire Capital.
