The Six Worst Apple Acquisitions In History

by anonymous on 2013-10-13 14:12:07

On October 13, Beijing time, according to foreign media reports, over the years, some of Apple's major successes have been the result of the company acquiring other promising companies and integrating these acquired companies' technologies into their own products. Through such business acquisitions, Apple has been able to provide users with genuine and valuable products. For example, the success of Mac OS X and iOS was the result of Apple's acquisition of NeXT in 1997. Additionally, after Apple acquired SoundJam MP in 2008, they subsequently obtained iTunes, which indeed helped iPod significantly outpace its competitors.

Of course, not all of Apple's acquisition deals have been successful. In fact, many of the technologies Apple has acquired never made it to market, and some were simply unsuitable for Apple's products.

Below is a list of Apple's six worst and most failed business acquisitions:

1. Apple's acquisition of Power Computing Corporation

In 1995, Apple authorized Power Computing Corporation to create products similar to Macs, hoping to transform Power Computing's business into a model akin to Microsoft’s, aiming to counter Microsoft's Windows 95. After Steve Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, he realized that it was too late to beat Microsoft in the operating system domain, and that Mac clones themselves were causing significant harm to high-margin Macs. That year, Apple acquired Power Computing Corporation for $100 million and shut down the Mac clone business. Although this move was embarrassing, it was necessary.

2. Apple's acquisition of Orion Network Systems

In 1988, Apple acquired Orion Network Systems. At that time in the enterprise sector, IBM computers had already captured a large share of the market, and Apple made this acquisition to boost its computer sales in the enterprise field. In reality, IBM had captured about 80% of the market share, while Apple only had around 6%. Despite Apple computers having software that was easier to network, factors such as price, compatibility with other software, and difficulties in employee training prevented Apple from gaining better market share.

3. Apple's acquisition of graphics chip manufacturer Raycer Graphics

In 1999, Apple acquired graphics manufacturer Raycer Graphics and brought on Bob Mansfield, the vice president of engineering at Raycer Graphics. Although Raycer's technology wasn't crucial to Apple, this acquisition was still seen by some in the industry as a success due to Mansfield's later performance. After joining Apple, Mansfield continued to oversee Mac hardware engineering, helping Apple launch its best-selling computer product in history, and later became Apple's senior vice president of technology.

4. Apple's acquisition of PowerSchool

In 2001, Apple acquired the private PowerSchool company for $62 million in stock. This company was an internet provider specializing in developing student information systems for schools. Apple claimed that PowerSchool could help school administrators and teachers easily and effectively manage student grades, while also allowing parents real-time access to their children's grades. However, this deal puzzled the industry because the company's software didn't align well with Apple's style.

5. Apple's acquisition of Placebase

In 2009, Apple acquired the mapping company Placebase, leading some industry insiders to question whether Apple intended to use the company's technology to replace Google Maps in the iOS system. However, three years later, Apple released iOS 6 and Apple Maps, but its mapping service was heavily criticized by the market due to numerous errors.

6. Apple's acquisition of NetSelector

In 2000, Apple acquired NetSelector. NetSelector mainly aimed to prevent children from accessing adult websites. Unlike traditional adult website blocking systems, NetSelector had a team of experts composed of teachers and computer administrators who could select websites suitable for children and provide them to child users. Of course, most of these websites were likely geared towards children's education. Eventually, most people believed that this system was too restrictive, so it gradually fell out of favor. Later, Apple integrated NetSelector's technology into the software of products like iMac and iBook. (Niu Niu)
