Summary of Wiring Techniques in PCB Design

In PCB design, wiring is a crucial step in completing the product design. One might say that all preparatory work done beforehand is for this purpose. In the entire PCB process, the wiring design phase has the strictest limitations, requires the most intricate techniques, and involves the largest workload. PCB wiring can be single-sided, double-sided, or multi-layered. There are also two types of wiring methods: automatic wiring and interactive wiring. Before using automatic wiring, interactive wiring can be used to pre-wire lines with stricter requirements. Input and output edge lines should avoid adjacent parallelism to prevent reflection interference. If necessary, ground lines should be added for isolation. Adjacent layer wiring should be perpendicular to each other; parallelism tends to produce parasitic coupling.

The wiring completion rate of automatic routing depends on good layout. Wiring rules can be pre-set, including the number of line bends, vias, and steps. Generally, exploratory routing is done first to quickly connect short lines, followed by maze routing, which optimizes the global routing path before connecting individual lines. It can disconnect already laid lines if needed and attempt re-routing to improve overall results.

For today's high-density PCB designs, through-holes are becoming less suitable as they waste many valuable routing channels. To address this issue, blind and buried vias have emerged. These not only serve the function of through-holes but also free up many routing channels, making the routing process more convenient, smoother, and more complete. The PCB board design process is both complex and simple. To master it well, electronic engineering designers need to experience it themselves to truly understand its essence.

1. Handling Power and Ground Lines

Even if the wiring of the entire PCB is completed excellently, interference caused by insufficient consideration of power and ground lines can degrade product performance and sometimes even affect the success rate of the product. Therefore, power and ground line wiring must be handled carefully to minimize noise interference and ensure product quality.

All engineers involved in electronics design know the reasons for noise between ground and power lines. Here, we will discuss measures to reduce or suppress such noise:

It is common knowledge to add decoupling capacitors between power and ground lines.

Try to widen the power and ground lines, preferably making the ground line wider than the power line. Their relationship is: ground line > power line > signal line. Typically, the signal line width is 0.2~0.3mm, with the finest width reaching 0.05~0.07mm, while the power line is 1.2~2.5mm.

For digital circuit PCBs, wide ground wires can form a loop, creating a ground grid (this cannot be done for analog circuits).

Use large copper areas as ground lines. Connect any unused areas on the printed circuit board to the ground. Alternatively, make it a multi-layer board, dedicating one layer each for power and ground.

2. Common Grounding for Digital and Analog Circuits

Nowadays, many PCBs are no longer single-function circuits (digital or analog), but rather a mix of digital and analog circuits. Therefore, during wiring, it is necessary to consider the interference between them, especially noise interference on the ground line.

Digital circuits operate at high frequencies, while analog circuits are highly sensitive. For signal lines, high-frequency signal lines should be kept as far away as possible from sensitive analog circuit components. For ground lines, the entire PCB should have only one external connection point, so it is necessary to handle the common ground problem between digital and analog circuits within the PCB. Internally, digital and analog grounds are actually separate and do not connect to each other. They are only short-circuited at one point where the PCB connects to the outside world (such as at connectors). Note that there is only one connection point. There are also cases where the PCB does not share a common ground, depending on the system design.

3. Routing Signal Lines on Power (Ground) Layers

When routing multilayer printed circuit boards, if there are not many remaining lines left to route on the signal layers, adding more layers would cause waste and increase production workload and cost. To resolve this conflict, consider routing on the power (ground) layers. First, consider using the power layer, then the ground layer. Ideally, the integrity of the ground layer should be preserved.
