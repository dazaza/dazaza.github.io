Android's rise leaves developers caught in a 'matrix of pain'

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:11:40

In May this year, Google Play reached 48 billion downloads with over 480,000 apps in total. In comparison, Apple's App Store has exceeded 50 billion downloads and offers more than 610,000 apps. In terms of the number of applications and downloads, the gap between Android and iOS is not significant.

Another piece of good news is that Android phones have captured 79.5% of the smartphone market, taking an absolute leading position, with as many as 11,000 different models available. From the data perspective, it’s a thriving landscape.

Beneath the prosperity, however, there are hidden concerns. As Android continues to flourish, developers are faced with a major issue: dealing with multiple system versions, numerous brands, various screen sizes, and diverse hardware specifications. This problem has been discussed for years, but now it's time to face the consequences.

Wierd refers to the adaptation of various Android versions and devices as the "pain matrix," implying the suffering endured during the process of ensuring that code runs properly on all devices.

Todd McKinnon, co-founder and CEO of Okta, an enterprise management login authentication company, said:

"When you decide to develop an Android app, you first have to deal with six main platform versions. And that's not enough; beneath the main versions, there are sub-versions. You must also handle various devices, from large screens to small ones."

This is undoubtedly painful, much like how programmers in the 1990s had to face various versions of Windows systems and try to adapt to as many hardware devices as possible. Compared to developing apps on iOS, Android developers face a more complex situation. The upgrade from iOS 6 to iOS 7 has already stretched many iOS developers thin, and many have indicated they will abandon iOS 6 to focus on the iOS 7 platform.

To reduce development difficulty and enhance user experience, many Android developers have to make compromises. McKinnon ultimately decided to support only the latest version of the Android system, which meant giving up a large portion of users. According to surveys, at that time, only about 37% of Android users were using the latest Jelly Bean system.

Many developers follow a rhythm where they launch the iOS version first, and then Android users demand an Android application. On the other hand, with Android users accounting for nearly 80% of the smartphone market, developers are unwilling to give up this segment. The larger the market, the more severe the fragmentation, and this contradiction remains unresolved for now, leaving developers to face it silently.

Yesterday, we also wrote about the many challenges facing the new generation of Android leaders, including the long-standing issue of ecosystem fragmentation.

Nirav Tolia's team launched their Android app in less than four months. To cover 95% of the Android market starting from version 2.2.3, they had to abandon native Android code and instead use web technologies.

Companies like Symantec and Mozilla have no choice but to test automation software across over 200 devices. Trent Peterson, co-founder of AppThwack, said:

"It's clear that Android has problems. You have to deal with tons of devices just to access this open platform."
