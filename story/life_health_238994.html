Retinal detachment

by weixing123 on 2009-09-16 18:24:43

Retinal detachment refers to the separation of the neural retina from the retinal pigment epithelium at the fundus of the eye, resulting in incomplete or total loss of images transmitted to the brain. Based on its cause, it can be categorized into rhegmatogenous, tractional, and exudative retinal detachment.

1. Primary Retinal Detachment (also known as Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment, RRD)

Symptoms:

Flashes of light in front of the eyes, floaters, a curtain-like shadow blocking vision from above; metamorphopsia; decreased peripheral or central vision, or both.

Main Signs:

Elevated retina combined with retinal tears.

Other Signs:

Pigment cells may appear in the anterior vitreous, vitreous hemorrhage, posterior vitreous detachment, intraocular pressure in the affected eye is often lower than that of the opposite eye, subretinal fluid is clear but does not change with body position, sometimes there are fixed retinal folds. The detached retina often appears wavy. An afferent pupillary defect may occur. There may be areas of retinal degeneration.

2. Exudative Retinal Detachment (ERD)

Symptoms:

Mild to severe visual or visual field impairment.

Main Symptoms:

Serous retinal detachment, accompanied by movable subretinal fluid (when the patient changes position, the location of the retinal detachment changes: in a sitting position, subretinal fluid accumulates below, causing detachment of the inferior retina; in a supine position, the fluid accumulates at the posterior pole, causing macular retinal detachment), without retinal tears.

Other Signs:

The detached retina is smooth and can appear distinctly spherical. A mild afferent pupillary defect may occur.

Causes:

1. Tumors: such as choroidal melanoma, metastatic tumors, choroidal hemangioma, multiple myeloma, retinal capillary hemangioma (FFA and ultrasound examinations help in differential diagnosis).

2. Inflammatory diseases: such as Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada syndrome, posterior scleritis, and other chronic inflammatory processes.

3. Congenital abnormalities: such as optic disc pits, morning glory syndrome, choroidal defects, and Coats' disease.

4. Microphthalmia: microphthalmia combined with small cornea, shallow anterior chamber, but large lens and thick sclera.

5. Multiple retinal pigment epithelial lesions at the posterior pole: spherical retinal detachment caused by multiple, large-scale retinal pigment epithelial detachment can be seen.

6. Uveal Effusion Syndrome: bilateral peripheral choroid, ciliary body, and retinal detachment; leopard-patterned retinal pigment epithelial changes, scleral vascular dilation.

3. Tractional Retinal Detachment (TRD)

Symptoms:

Visual impairment and visual field defects; may be asymptomatic.

Main Signs:

The detached retina appears smooth and concave downward; vitreous membranes pulling on the retina are present. If a retinal tear forms, the detachment becomes a protruding primary (rhegmatogenous) retinal detachment.

Other Signs:

The retina is fixed, and detachment rarely extends to the ora serrata. A mild afferent pupillary defect may occur.

Causes:

Fibrous strands in the vitreous, such as due to proliferative diabetic retinopathy, sickle cell retinopathy, retinopathy of prematurity, toxocariasis retinopathy, and previous large retinal tears.

Differential Diagnosis for Three Types of Retinal Detachment

1. Senile Retinoschisis: Generally bilateral, often located temporally below, no pigmented cells or bleeding in the vitreous, blood vessels around the inner layer of the retina often have sheaths, white "snowflakes" are often visible in the inner layer of the retina; patients are asymptomatic. In contrast to the relative scotomas seen in primary retinal detachment, absolute scotomas can be found during visual field testing. Boundary lines generally do not appear. When boundary lines do appear, retinal tears must be checked because long-term detachment may look like schisis, even leading to absolute visual field defects.

2. Juvenile Retinoschisis: Bilateral onset, petal-like changes can occur after schisis at the equator, schisis does not extend to the ora serrata. X-linked recessive inheritance.

3. Choroidal Detachment: Brownish-yellow, more substantial than retinal detachment, the ora serrata is often visible without scleral indentation, the range of detachment can reach 360° around the globe. Generally associated with low intraocular pressure.

Prevention

Long-term visual fatigue, excessive eye strain, high myopia, and heavy blows to the eye can all lead to retinal detachment.

1. Avoid over-fatigue when using your eyes;

2. People with high myopia should avoid lifting heavy objects and strenuous activities; those with high myopia should not donate blood;

3. Prevent ocular trauma;

4. Patients with myopia should regularly visit the hospital for check-ups, especially those with poor retinas;

5. Regular eye health checks are recommended every six months.
