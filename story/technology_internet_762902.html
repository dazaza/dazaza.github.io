The Economist: Chinese returnees face some difficulties in finding jobs

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:57:35

The Economist reported that the importance of "sea turtles" in China has declined for various reasons. Sometimes companies hire returnee talents but find that after studying abroad, they are not very familiar with the rapidly developing industry situation in China. They stubbornly adhere to Western concepts and cannot manage local employees. The quality of returnee talents also varies greatly, and many of the truly talented ones remain overseas.

Following The Economist's article, let's take a look at the current status of returnees in China:

Li Sanqi recalled: "I left China in 1980 with only $3 in my pocket." After the Cultural Revolution, he was one of the first students allowed to study abroad. Like most successful people, his performance was outstanding; he was promoted to a coveted position at the University of Texas and even founded several technology companies. He returned to China because he had the opportunity to help establish world-class multinational enterprises there. Now, he is a senior executive at Huawei, a telecommunications giant (data shows that he serves as the Chief Technology Officer of Huawei's operator network business).

Li Sanqi is an outstanding representative of the "sea turtles," which refers to "returnees." For a long time, all sectors of Chinese society have praised the contributions of overseas returnees in introducing advanced technology. In the past, these individuals were unstoppable in the job market and enjoyed generous compensation. Unfortunately, those good times are over. Returnees no longer receive universal admiration, the wage gap is gradually narrowing, and some can't even find jobs. Some jokingly refer to them now as "seaweed." Reflecting on the past contributions of the returnees, this is a surprising change.

This year marks the 100th anniversary of China's "China-Europe Alumni Association." Wang Huiyao, a member of the association, believes there have been five waves of returnees. The first occurred in the 19th century, including the builder of China's first railway and the first university president. The second and third waves both happened before 1949, with many being political leaders. In the 1950s, many went to the Soviet Union for further studies, marking the fourth wave.

The fifth wave began in 1978 and is currently the largest returnee trend. Since then, about 2.6 million Chinese have studied abroad. In recent years, the number of Chinese students going abroad has expanded to 400,000 annually. Although most stay overseas, the 1.1 million who have returned to China have already brought about changes. Wang Huiyao believes that the first three returnee waves prompted revolutionary changes in China, the fourth modernized China, and the fifth globalized it.

Returnees helped integrate China's economy with the world. They founded heavyweight tech companies like Baidu, and many became senior executives in the Chinese branches of multinational corporations. They helped connect China with global commerce, politics, and popular culture.

So, why has their importance declined? Several studies show that returnees on average take longer to reach relatively lower positions, and the wage gap with local employees is smaller. One reason is the weak employment market for new graduates. Another factor is that with the rise of China's domestic market and the continuous development of industries such as e-commerce, these developments make the returnees who have been abroad for several years unfamiliar with the local scene. Gary Rieschel of the venture capital firm Qiming Venture Partners said that 10 years ago, investors only invested in those returning from Silicon Valley in the U.S., but now they support entrepreneurs who graduated from local universities, who are more familiar with local consumer behavior, online gaming habits, and social media platforms like Weibo.

With the prosperity and rapid growth of China's economy, local managers are beginning to shed their inferiority complex. A senior executive at Tencent, a Chinese social media company, said that he still poaches returnees from foreign companies, but finds that they have difficulty managing local engineers. A European investment banker pointed out that returnees often insist on Western concepts such as transparency, elite management, and ethics, which puts them at a disadvantage in China's super Darwinian economy. In China, what matters more is what the boss or client wants, and doing everything possible to achieve the goal.

Even foreign companies in China are becoming more selective when hiring. Yannig Gourmelon of the German management consulting firm Roland Berger said that the profits of multinational companies have been significantly compressed, leading to reduced salaries for gilded returnees.

There is another explanation: many newly returned sea turtles are of low quality. In the past, only top students were allowed to study abroad, and competition for government scholarships was fierce. However, with rising incomes, many students of medium caliber can afford to pay to study abroad, attending universities of questionable quality whose degrees don't contribute much to employment. Additionally, with the economic downturn in Western countries, many return without any work experience.

There are fewer capable returnees, and the best Chinese students still remain overseas. Research funded by the U.S. National Science Foundation (NSF) found that five years after graduation, 92% of Chinese students with Ph.D.s from the U.S. remained in the U.S., compared to 81% of Indian students, 41% of South Korean students, and 32% of Mexican students.

To attract these superstars back home, China offers generous subsidies and allowances. But do these measures work? The results seem unpredictable. Despite these policies, returning entrepreneurs still face many issues: rising labor and land costs, rampant intellectual property infringement, and severe corruption. Very few top scientists return. In a paper by David Zweig, a scholar at Hong Kong University of Science and Technology, it is written: "If China hopes to attract top talent back, it must fundamentally reform academic and scientific institutions to break the power of administrative agencies in recruitment and funding."

A troubling fact is the inner conflict of overseas Chinese towards their homeland. Li Sanqi, who works at Huawei, looks like a typical sea turtle, but his wife and children still live in the U.S. Besides increasing subsidies, China needs to strengthen the rule of law, eradicate corrupt officials, purify the air, clean water resources, and ensure food safety. If all these are achieved, the sea turtles will certainly take notice.
