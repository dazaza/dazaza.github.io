An 8-day trip to Hunan and Guizhou has come to an end, and I have to say "good job" to myself again! Although it's very inconvenient to go online, I still need to sort out my "Hunan-Guizhou trip". Below, I will share my journey with you.

This trip was a group tour organized by my mother's workplace, so it was my first time joining a travel group within the country. It was obvious that I didn't have to worry about anything; wherever they went, I just followed them. There are advantages and disadvantages to this approach. This way is not as free-spirited, which makes me even more appreciate the comfort and ease of independent travel.

Itinerary One (Tianjin - Hohhot - Guiyang)

Travel:

First, we boarded train No. 1136 from Tianjin to Wuhai West. Although there has been a nationwide speed increase, there is no way to significantly increase the speed of this train, so it continues to slowly wander to Hohhot. We couldn't get a sleeper ticket, so I had to endure another 12 hours on a hard seat. After having experienced a 17-hour hard seat ride to Yan'an during the May Day holiday, this one didn't seem like much.

The next morning at 9:30, we arrived in Hohhot. My sister had arrived a day earlier and came to pick me up. After a brief rest at the hotel where they were staying, we directly headed to lunch and then caught our flight.

A four-hour flight with a half-hour layover in Xi'an. It seems that if you don't want to go somewhere, you won't, but once you decide to go, you end up going twice in a year. Two months later, we landed at Xi'an airport, and it felt very familiar indeed. We reached Guiyang at around 5:30 PM. The weather was nice and cool, and I was finally looking forward to getting to bed soon.

Scenic Spots:

To say that we spent the whole day traveling by vehicle, there wasn't much scenery to see, but I did manage to capture some sights.

Firstly, on the road from Tianjin to Hohhot, it had been a long time since I last saw the sunrise over the grasslands. At 4:30 AM, the sky started to brighten. As the sun rose, it turned the eastern sky red, and the entire nature seemed to gradually awaken, basking in the morning sunlight. The brightness in front of my eyes woke me up too.

Secondly, on the plane. Fortunately, I didn't fall asleep. Flying above the clouds, I could see the clouds interweaving and changing shapes. Suddenly, I yearned for the mythical image of walking on clouds. As we approached Xi'an, it started raining, and the clouds disappeared. The plane traveled through dark clouds, and in just a few minutes, we experienced the myriad changes of nature.

Thirdly, as we neared Guiyang, the plane slowly descended below the cloud layer. I could clearly see a winding river, reminiscent of imagining whether I was walking on a rainbow. I've seen rainbows many times, but seeing one from the sky was the first time, truly a unique experience.

The weather in Guizhou is indeed pleasant, and it stays cool in summer. Our hotel was relatively remote, so due to the limited time, we only took a bus to the Dashaizi area for a short stroll. We also encountered a light drizzle.

Food:

They say that the food arranged by travel agencies isn't good, but I think it's okay. Maybe it's because I'm not a picky eater. Upon arriving in Guiyang and checking into the hotel, during dinner, we could enjoy the wine songs of the Buyi ethnic group. They are different from Mongolian wine songs. Mongolians use silver bowls for their wine, while the Buyi people use buffalo horns, and they pour the wine directly into your mouth without hesitation.

There were also local specialty dishes on the table, mainly spicy dishes. They say that people in Guizhou are not afraid of spiciness, they're only afraid of it not being spicy enough. Unfortunately, I didn't have many opportunities to take photos of local snacks, and following the travel agency, we didn't encounter much local cuisine. I'll reluctantly post a few pictures from the park.

Guiyang has plenty of snacks. From memory, I recalled students in Guizhou and sent a message asking for advice, and indeed, they provided detailed information. However, we didn't have much time to sample everything. For instance, there are "silk babies," "Leave One Hand" grilled fish, everywhere you find "chuan chuan xiang" (spicy skewers), sour soup fish, love tofu fruit, beef noodles, mutton noodles, ice powder, etc., mostly concentrated around Xiaoshizi. In just one day, with limited stomach capacity, it's impossible to try everything.

I did buy some Bobo sugar to bring back, available in various flavors, and it tastes pretty good. As for Guizhou's other specialty, beef jerky, forget about it. Beef jerky is everywhere in Inner Mongolia, and it's better than what they have here. We brought a lot of it on the road.

Itinerary Two (Tianxing Bridge, Huangguoshu Waterfall)

Travel:

Nowadays, the roads in Guizhou are much better, but they are still quite bumpy, feeling like electric massage therapy. The guide said that previously, the roads were very difficult to navigate, making travel inconvenient everywhere. Guizhou has three famous sayings: "No land is flat for three li, no weather is clear for three days, and no person has three cents." The first sentence fully expresses this situation. Because Guizhou is full of hillocks, road construction is extremely challenging.

On the way to Tianxing Bridge and Huangguoshu, we passed through Pingba and Anshun. Pingba is Guizhou's largest granary, as its terrain is relatively flat. People say that Anshun has two roads for women: the white road and the black road. The white road refers to opium, and the black road refers to coal.

We stayed overnight in Xingyi, a five-hour drive from Huangguoshu Waterfall. Interestingly, we discovered another characteristic of Guizhou: sunny days turn into rainy nights, which is nice as it doesn't interfere with sightseeing and helps purify the air and improve the climate.

Scenic Spots:

Guizhou tourism is still in its early stages, so the Guizhou trip is still quite enjoyable, with not too many shopping stops arranged. The first shopping stop was an herbal medicine procurement point. We didn't give them much face; after listening to their lengthy explanation, we all got up and left. Sneaking a glance at the staff, they looked bewildered and helpless.

We were quite lucky. On the first day in Guiyang, the guide mentioned that we might not be able to visit Tianxing Bridge due to heavy rains in Guizhou recently, making it inaccessible. The next day, we received good news. Although the innermost part was still off-limits, we could still enjoy most of the scenic area.

The environment at Tianxing Bridge is pleasant, with rocks of various shapes, very beautiful, all naturally eroded, showing the power of wind, water, and nature. (The two hollows resembling footprints in the photo are naturally eroded caves.)

Another feature here is the growth of cactus. Normally, cacti grow in desert areas, but here, in a humid climate, they thrive even in stone crevices.

The Num Sheng Stepping Stones traverse the scenic area. It turns out that the number of steps coincidentally matches 365, representing each day of the year. You can stand on the step corresponding to your birthday and stomp on it. Above it, it says "Gary Borthday," but I have no idea who this person is. After searching online, I found out why they didn't write "American Independence Day."

At Tianxing Bridge, there is a large banyan tree, and it is said that you can see four beauties in the trunk. I could only see two. Strangely, all the interesting banyan trees here are about 500 years old. Another feature of the landscape here, in my opinion, is the tree roots, growing everywhere, whether in stones or exposed outside.

Huangguoshu Grand Waterfall is China's largest and the world's third-largest waterfall, with the world's only water curtain cave that can be traversed from front, back, left, and right. The path is slippery, and the water volume is significant, so we had to wear disposable raincoats. The outside of the raincoat got wet from the splashing waterfall, and the inside got wet from sweating. It was almost super refreshing. It is said that the Water Curtain Cave in "Journey to the West" was filmed here, which made me think of Sun Wukong repeatedly.

When my parents visited, it wasn't the tourist season, so there were fewer people, allowing them to leisurely enjoy the beautiful scenery. When we visited, there were more people, making it harder to seriously appreciate the views. Nevertheless, I took advantage of every opportunity to run around and try to take in the natural scenery. Walking through the water curtain cave and looking outward reveals a unique view, reminding me of Jackie Chan's movie "The Myth," leading to endless imagination.

At Huangguoshu, there is also what they claim to be the longest escalator in the country, measuring 340 meters. I've ridden many escalators before, but spending 30 yuan to ride such a long escalator and saving nearly an hour of walking was a first for me.

Food:

Anshun dog meat is relatively famous, and it feels similar to Northeastern China. Here, the dogs are specially raised for consumption, which is quite distinctive.

Other meals were eaten with the travel group. Others may find the food unappealing, but I don't care much about that; as long as I'm full, it's fine. What's good or bad-tasting, Google Maps download, still tastes delicious to me.

Itinerary Three (Wanfeng Forest, Malin River Grand Canyon)

Travel:

The whole day was spent traveling by bus, ranging from 2 hours to 4-5 hours. Luckily, I don't get carsick.

Scenic Spots:

Guizhou has many ethnic minorities, approximately 40 types, with the Miao and Buyi being the main ones. In Wanfeng Forest, the Buyi people predominantly reside, followed by the Miao. The Buyi usually live at the foot of mountains, while the Miao live halfway up. Based on these characteristics, it's easy to identify which village belongs to which ethnic group.

Wanfeng Forest lives up to its name, with mountains stretching endlessly. Looking from various directions, you can associate them with camels, turtles, generals leading troops, peacocks flying southeast, and so on. The surrounding fields complement the mountains beautifully, showcasing the creativity of human labor combined with nature. According to the terrain, the fields are cultivated in a Bagua pattern, creating a stunning picture when viewed from the mountain top. In this natural setting, whether it's the peaks, valleys, or fields, everything remains harmonious.

The Nahui River winds through the fields, eventually converging with groundwater at the valley bottom, forming a small branch of the Pearl River. This Nahui River adds boundless vitality to the entire natural landscape. We walked through the fields to the end of the Nahui River to see the convergence point, which was quite interesting and different from rivers flowing into larger bodies of water.

Walking into the village, the houses are all built with stacked stones, with stone tiles on the roofs, commonly known as "stones as roof covers." Moreover, the roofs serve as shallow water reservoirs to prevent cracking during intense sun exposure and help keep the house warm in winter and cool in summer. Passing through the houses, we could see a 500-year-old banyan tree. This tree is massive, requiring over ten people to encircle it. The villagers worship here during festivals, praying for favorable weather in the coming year.

Malin River Grand Canyon is also a wonderful place, formed by tectonic activity, featuring roughly 200 waterfalls of varying sizes. There are two ways to enjoy different views: hiking up and down the mountain or taking the water route. My sister's group of ten chose the former, resulting in them being "in tears" due to the steep paths and hot weather, making it very exhausting. Ten of us chose the latter, rafting along the canyon floor, enjoying the scenery without the heat, appreciating the calm waters, and experiencing the thrill of rapids. We exchanged smiles with them, amused by their weary expressions.

Food:

Still the meals arranged by the travel agency, nothing special. Of course, we brought our own fruits and beef jerky, and we still ate happily.

Itinerary Four (Longgong, Tiantai Mountain)

Travel:

The whole day was spent traveling by bus back and forth. In the evening, we returned from Guiyang to Zhangjiajie by train. After a long absence from non-air-conditioned green-skin sleeping carriages, the rumbling sound of the train mixed with the whirring of fans, yet I slept soundly, much to the envy of others who couldn't sleep.

Scenic Spots:

Because we stayed overnight in the Longgong Scenic Area the previous night, we were the first to enter Longgong, with hardly any tourists ahead of us, making boarding the boat convenient. By the time we left Longgong, the main group had just arrived, and many people looked at us curiously as we exited, filled with doubt.

Longgong is also a karst cave, stretching over five thousand meters, but currently only eight hundred meters have been developed, and we needed to enter and exit by boat. Just for this distance, the ticket price was already 120 yuan. If the entire five kilometers were developed, how much would it cost? Inside Longgong, it is said to be the place with the least radiation in the world, with an index of only 2.9. Taking deep breaths here expels negative ions from the body, greatly benefiting health.

Inside Tiantai Mountain, the highlights are Wulong Temple and Tianlong Tunpu. Wulong Temple has a long history, located in the mountain gorge, with even the outer pillars artificially stacked stone walls (looking similar to other sections). Likewise, here too, there are five-hundred-year-old banyan trees, which is why I initially found it peculiar.

Tianlong Tunpu is a Buyi ethnic village preserved to this day, with a six-hundred-year history. Here, you can see their traditional costumes, houses, etc. The houses here are constructed with stone, as mentioned earlier. Additionally, there's a custom: if a family has a boy, they start building a house from his birth until he gets married, only then sealing the roof.

The ancient town feels similar to Yangmei Ancient Town in Nanning that I visited last year, but it's more complete here. Even entering the ancient town, elderly nienie (a term I can't quite spell correctly, referring to unmarried girls called xiaonienie and unmarried young men called xiaoyeye) freely offer cool tea.

Here, you can also watch the performance of ground theater, which is simple and rustic, showcasing local characteristics. Ground theater is not regional theater but performances staged on level ground. It reminds me of shaman dances, though I didn't understand any of it.

Before returning to Guiyang to catch the train, we visited Jiaxiu Tower, a symbolic building of Guiyang. It's a Ming Dynasty structure, originally a gathering place for literati, and doesn't seem particularly remarkable because I've seen too many Ming Dynasty buildings.

Food:

Similarly, the meals were arranged by the travel agency.

Itinerary Five (Zhangjiajie Jinbian Stream Route)

Travel:

The train arrived in Zhangjiajie at 8:00 AM. The weather was fine upon arrival, but it started raining heavily shortly afterward.

Scenic Spots:

Zhangjiajie is also a gathering place for ethnic minorities, perhaps with 19 different groups, mainly Tujia and Bai.

As soon as we entered the Zhangjiajie Scenic Area, it started raining heavily. People rushed to buy disposable raincoats, but fortunately, I had the foresight to purchase high-quality raincoats beforehand. Despite this, we were still drenched, with wet pants and shoes, feeling as if we were soaking our feet.

Due to the heavy rain, we changed our itinerary and directly took the Jinbian Stream route, informally referred to as a "fitness journey," requiring a 7.5-kilometer hike. Along the way, we could admire different strange mountains. The rain-soaked forests looked different from those on sunny days. As you walked, it felt like you were weaving through mist-shrouded mountains, changing poses as if dancing. Of course, looking up at the scenery comes at a cost—getting more soaked, squinting to look, unable to fully open your eyes, making the scenery appear even more misty.

Due to the heavy rain, we had to change our itinerary, leaving us with no scenic spots to visit in the afternoon. The guide took us to a free foot soak, ostensibly to promote medicinal products, and a free tea tasting session to sell tea. We joked that since we were soaking our feet and tasting tea, shouldn't we also wash our hair?

Food:

Meals arranged by the travel group.

We also tasted the three-course tea here, finding the Du Zhong tea quite tasty. The bitter jujube tea tasted different from what we usually drink, possibly because it was made from fresh leaves.
