Quantity model speculates on World Cup champion, Brazil is favored to win again ultimately

by jueshid977 on 2010-06-02 10:55:26

For example, the famous "King of Football" Pelé is known for his notorious predictions. Whenever he supports a team in the World Cup, their fate tends to be rather unfortunate.

A renowned investment company speculates through a quantitative model that Brazil will once again be the champion of the South Africa World Cup?

There are some things in this world that are truly hard to predict, while others are surprisingly accurate. For instance, an investment company successfully predicted the 2006 Germany World Cup, forecasting Italy as the winning team when no one else believed in them. In the end, Italy lifted the trophy for the fourth time.

This investment company is UBS Wealth Management. In the 2006 World Cup, they developed a quantitative model that not only accurately predicted the champion but also forecasted 50% of the semi-finalists, 75% of the quarter-finalists, and 81% of the round-of-16 teams. However, using the same model, they failed miserably in the 2008 European Championship, where the model successfully predicted 63% of the teams advancing to the second round but failed entirely in predicting the semi-finalists. With the South Africa World Cup approaching, UBS Wealth Management has once again used a quantitative model to evaluate potential winners. To avoid another debacle, UBS has decided not to risk predicting all outcomes of the World Cup but instead focus on forecasting the possible 16 strong teams, the semi-finalists, and the eventual champion.

What kind of model does UBS use for its predictions? It has been reported that they use a system called the ELO rating system to analyze data from the teams' rankings in March. The ELO system was created by American physicist Dr. Arnold Elo, who originally applied this method to predict chess game results. Later, people adapted this formula to predict football matches. Since Uruguay's unexpected win in 1950, every World Cup-winning team has scored at least 1820 points in the ELO rating system.

According to the model’s data analysis, Brazil will ultimately be crowned champions. Some believe this model is unfair to Spain or England. In fact, in the past three World Cups, both Spain and England have consistently ranked among the strongest teams but were eliminated in the quarter-finals. Meanwhile, since Brazil's victory in 2002, they have been relatively quiet for eight years, making now the perfect time for a resurgence.
