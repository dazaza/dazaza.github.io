Why is it so hairy?

by 865512 on 2008-12-29 09:49:28

It can be divided into idiopathic, adrenal and ovarian diseases, iatrogenic causes, etc.

Idiopathic refers to those other symptoms and signs of endocrine dysfunction except for hirsutism, which may actually all be caused by abnormalities in androgen metabolism. It often occurs during menopause, sometimes during pregnancy, and may partially subside after childbirth, but increase again during subsequent pregnancies. Hirsutism in women who develop the condition during or after menopause is often limited to the face.

The symptoms caused by adrenal tumors are related to the age of onset. In older girls, menstruation does not occur until puberty, and breast development does not happen.

Generalized hirsutism in women may be due to polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), which causes hirsutism accompanied by amenorrhea or oligomenorrhea, decreased or absent libido, infertility, obesity, and enlarged polycystic ovaries. Hirsutism mainly occurs on the face, breasts, and abdomen, but without masculinization. Sometimes there is hirsutism and infertility without polycystic ovaries. Sometimes there are polycystic ovaries without hirsutism or obesity, and instead, menstrual flow increases.

Iatrogenic hirsutism in women is often caused by the use of sufficient amounts of androgens or metabolically active androgens, as well as oral intake of certain contraceptives. Glucocorticoids can also cause hirsutism in women. Of course, there are also many endocrine and congenital diseases that can cause this phenomenon.

Congenital hypertrichosis: This is an autosomal dominant genetic disease caused by gene mutations, characterized by excessive hairiness present at birth. The skin covered with hair has excessive lanugo that is not replaced by vellus or terminal hair. The hair and body hair do not reach the diameter and texture of adult hair; eyebrows are thick and long, often connecting the two brows. The forehead, face, neck, trunk, and limbs have dense and long hair. There may be poor tooth development and external ear deformities. Such children are commonly referred to as "hairy children" or "werewolves," etc.

Acquired hypertrichosis lanuginosa: This refers to fine, long vellus hair appearing on the face, usually accompanying or following severe diseases, spreading over the entire skin except for the palms and soles. Hair growth may be relatively rapid. Other hirsutism phenomena: Each has different causes. Symptomatic hirsutism refers to hirsutism caused by porphyria, bullous epidermolysis, congenital gingival elephantiasis, hypothyroidism, hyperthyroidism, dermatomyositis, infantile limb pain syndrome, primary malnutrition, etc.

Elbow hypertrichosis manifests as excessive hair on the elbows at birth, beginning to regress before the age of 5, also known as hairy elbow syndrome.

Nevus-like hypertrichosis appears at birth or in early childhood. It may exist independently as a local developmental defect or coexist with other nevus-like abnormalities. The length, diameter, and color of the hair are disproportionate to its location and the patient's age. A melanocytic nevus with a large number of coarse hairs is called a hairy nevus.

In addition, many external stimuli can also cause localized hair growth, such as friction, scratching, burning, plaster application, etc., which can all lead to hirsutism. Long-term persistent skin congestion can also cause hirsutism, generally referred to as acquired localized hypertrichosis.
