China plans to implement a real-name system for express mail deliveries

by jintaimqr on 2012-03-05 16:28:07

Aftermath of the parcel bomb: Real-name system planned for domestic express deliveries. With more and more people joining the ranks of online shoppers, the express delivery industry has also developed rapidly. However, this emerging industry has potential safety hazards, namely the mailing of hazardous materials. The recent express parcel bomb case has left frequent online shoppers with lingering fears, and they have also raised questions about express delivery companies: why don't they inspect parcels before accepting them? In response, express delivery companies claim they are wronged because many consumers refuse to allow inspection of their parcels in order to protect their privacy. For this reason, China plans to implement a real-name system for express mail, and parcels must be inspected before being sent.

Let's take a look at the detailed report from the Guangzhou Daily:

In fact, as early as before the Tianhe District parcel bomb case, a similar case occurred last year at an express delivery company in Hangzhou. And in other countries like the United States, bomb parcel incidents have also occurred frequently. With the increasing popularity of online shopping, consumers use express delivery services more frequently. Is it safe to send parcels via express delivery? This question is vividly put before citizens. Yesterday, the general manager of the Guangzhou branch of a large express delivery company told reporters that regulations actually require opening parcels for inspection: if the sender refuses to open the parcel, the express company will not accept it.

Express Delivery Company: Lack of consumer cooperation

However, in reality, it is difficult to enforce this rule. A courier named Yang Cheng (a pseudonym) from a large express delivery company told reporters that the company has always required them to inspect parcels when collecting them, but consumers simply do not cooperate. Some colleagues have even lost their jobs because they insisted on inspecting parcels. Yang Cheng said: "Actually, consumers often do not want to show the contents to couriers because they think it is their privacy."

Some consumers also told reporters that none of the express delivery companies they used ever required inspections. "Especially during peak delivery periods, couriers wish they had three heads and six arms; how would they carefully inspect parcels?" said Mr. Ye.

No clear requirements for express parcel security checks

Another step that could have prevented the express bomb case is X-ray machine security checks for parcels. However, after reviewing relevant regulations, reporters found that there is no requirement for express delivery companies to conduct such checks, only air express parcels are required to undergo X-ray machine inspections. The aforementioned general manager told reporters that they conduct security checks on inter-provincial express deliveries, but intra-city express deliveries do not need to go through transit stations or sorting centers and are often delivered directly. "Even if security checks were required, some prohibited items cannot be fully detected," he said.

Real-name system for sending parcels already piloted

It is reported that since the express explosion incident in Hangzhou last year, China has been planning to implement a real-name system for sending parcels. Currently, the system has been piloted in Shaoxing, Zhejiang Province. During the Shanghai World Expo, it was also piloted in Shanghai for a period of time. Once officially implemented, customers will need to show their ID cards when sending parcels, express delivery company staff will need to open and check the parcels, verify the items being sent, and input these details into computers. Recipients will also need to show their ID cards to receive the parcels. Industry insiders say that this can increase the safety coefficient of express deliveries to a certain extent.
