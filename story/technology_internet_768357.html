Samsung Phone Repair Exposes Black Market: A 3 Yuan Cost Component Sold for 240 Yuan

When a phone malfunctions, its screen gets bigger but its lifespan gets shorter. Consumers treat their phones like "pets," but phone manufacturers treat consumers like "playthings." The chaos in the mobile phone repair industry is rampant, with frequent part replacements and core changes, costing at least hundreds to thousands of yuan, leaving consumers with high repair costs and headaches, leading them to exclaim, "I can afford to buy it, but I can't afford to fix it."

Who is the main culprit behind the excessive profits in phone repairs? Amidst the hidden "traps" in the mobile phone repair market, how can consumers protect their own interests? A reporter from China News Weekly conducted an undercover investigation.

Affordable to Buy, Unaffordable to Fix

Replace a charging tail plug for 240 yuan, available on Taobao for just 3 yuan

After a phone develops issues, most people choose to go to authorized repair centers of the phone manufacturer for testing and repair. However, according to investigations by China News Weekly, these so-called "official" after-sales repair centers are not necessarily that official in the eyes of consumers.

On November 4th, the reporter went undercover to the Samsung Electronics After-Sales Service Center located at No. 2 Building, No. 2 Courtyard, Congdian Hutong, Xizhimen, Beijing. This is an authorized Samsung repair center, with the business license of "Beijing Taihong Yingye Communication Technology Co., Ltd." hanging on the wall.

At noon, there were about 15 people waiting for repairs. The reporter approached them to learn that everyone had different issues with their phones, some were experiencing system crashes, others had blank screens upon startup, some couldn't charge, while others had software problems. However, without exception, everyone was holding large-screen smartphones.

The reporter met Wang Liang (a pseudonym) who came to repair his phone. His "tragic experience" was this: two months ago, he spent nearly four thousand yuan on a Samsung Galaxy S4. One evening, his phone suddenly started having issues where it couldn't charge, and all the contacts disappeared mysteriously. The after-sales repair personnel determined that Wang Liang's phone had a hardware issue and took it into a room where others couldn't enter for inspection.

During the one-hour wait for the inspection, Wang Liang chatted idly with the reporter, saying that he was a fan of Samsung and had used multiple Samsung phones before, all of which gave him a good impression. However, an hour later, the results of the Galaxy S4 inspection broke his heart. The after-sales repair personnel told him that the phone couldn't charge because the tail plug interface (or tail plug flex cable) was corroded by liquid. This was classified as human-induced damage, not covered by warranty, requiring Wang Liang to pay 240 yuan for part replacement with no room for negotiation. Wang Liang expressed that his phone had never been submerged in water, so how could it be corroded? The after-sales personnel did not show the phone to Mr. Huang to see the extent of the corrosion; they only informed him verbally. Due to his urgency to retrieve his phone, Wang Liang reluctantly paid the money without fully understanding.

The reporter asked the after-sales repair personnel where the price standard for Samsung phone components could be found. The after-sales staff stated that Samsung regulations do not allow public disclosure of component price lists, but prices are unified nationwide and can be checked through their computer backend system.

After bidding farewell to Wang Liang, the reporter contacted Zhang Ming (a pseudonym), who runs a mobile phone repair shop in Shenzhen. Zhang Ming graduated from junior high school in 2004 and studied mobile phone repair for half a year at a technical school in Guangzhou. He then opened a mobile phone repair shop on Huaqiang North Road. After several years of development, besides himself, his shop now employs four Apple phone repair technicians and two Samsung phone repair technicians. On average, they receive dozens of mobile phone repair orders per day. It's worth noting that Zhang Ming's repair shop is not an authorized after-sales repair center for branded mobile phones. Zhang Ming once considered applying but abandoned the idea due to the need to pay a certain deposit and the limited permissions granted by branded mobile phones to their after-sales repair centers.

After telling Zhang Ming about what the reporter saw and heard during the day, Zhang Ming quickly provided the price quote for the mobile phone tail plug interface via QQ, "Taking the Samsung 9300 as an example, the market purchase price is around 25 yuan." The "market" that Zhang Ming referred to includes places like Yuanwang and Longsheng Digital City on Huaqiang North Road. Prices for mobile phone components obtained here are generally lower than other places because Huaqiang North Road is one of the global electronics manufacturing centers and one of the largest electronic component distribution hubs in the world.

If you search for the tail plug interface of the Samsung Galaxy S4 on Taobao, the price ranges from 3 yuan to 40 yuan.

The reporter was confused, and two questions became apparent:

First, why does the price difference between the 240 yuan charged by the after-sales repair center and the market purchase price reach nearly ten times? Where does the profit go?

Second, without issuing any inspection report or showing the damaged parts of the phone to the consumer, simply stating orally that the "tail plug interface was corroded by liquid," does this comply with the standard repair process?

Uncovering the Excessive Profits in Mobile Phone Repairs

Pointing the Finger at After-Sales Repair Centers

To unravel these puzzles, only the after-sales repair personnel of the brand mobile phone manufacturers can provide answers. However, when the reporter communicated online with several after-sales repair centers, none responded. Eventually, the reporter managed to find Huang Dong (a pseudonym), who worked at Nokia's after-sales repair center for five years through a friend.

Huang Dong told the reporter from China News Weekly that Nokia's after-sales repair centers are divided into three levels: the after-sales repair stores in county-level cities are called level one, municipal-level city repair centers are level two, and there are only two level three repair centers nationwide—one in Beijing and one in Shenzhen. All these repair centers are outsourced, meaning they are authorized by Nokia and do not have direct-operated stores. Level one and level two repair centers have similar authorities, while the level three repair center has the highest authority. For instance, it can determine whether a phone is beyond repair—scrapped. Additionally, if consumers take their phones to level one or two repair centers for repairs, and these centers cannot fix them, they will inform the customers that the phone needs to be sent back to the factory for repair, which means returning it to the level three repair center.

Huang Dong, who worked nearly a year at Nokia's level two repair center and four years at the level three repair center, understands the rules of this trade clearly. When asked who is driving up the cost of smartphone repairs, he casually replied, "Mobile phone repairs typically lack transparency."

Huang Dong then analyzed three reasons causing the high cost of mobile phone repairs:

First, the price of mobile phone parts given by the manufacturers themselves is already quite high. CCTV once exposed that the glass cover of Samsung's mobile phone screen costs only 8 yuan, yet the repair price is over 1,000 yuan (including the glass cover and touch screen replacement). However, in reality, Zhang Ming, who runs a repair shop on Huaqiang North Road in Shenzhen, told the reporter that such low prices are hard to obtain on the market, and even if they are, the quality would be very poor, and certainly not original equipment.

Nevertheless, Huang Dong firmly stated that the high price of mobile phone parts provided by manufacturers is not the main reason for the high repair costs. The logic is simple: the primary goal of mobile phone manufacturers is to sell more phones. They naturally hope consumers have a good experience from pre-sales to after-sales, thereby forming a positive reputation and promoting more sales. Therefore, Huang Dong does not agree with media reports that claim "mobile phone manufacturers deliberately leave defects in their research and development to profit from repairs later on." "In terms of warranty repairs, mobile phone manufacturers lose a lot of money every year."

Second, after obtaining the mobile phone parts, repair centers naturally mark up the prices and pass them on to consumers. There are many tricks in this process. For after-sales repair centers, the labor fees they earn from repairing phones within the warranty period are minimal. Only by repairing phones outside the warranty period can they make more profit. Under the influence of profit motives, some after-sales repair personnel exploit loopholes—by finding traces that indicate the phone is not eligible for warranty even if it is within the warranty period (such as water exposure, falling, etc.), thus making consumers who should be entitled to free repairs pay. This problem can occur in both level one, two, and three repair centers. The next section will detail the various tactics used by after-sales repair centers to inflate repair prices.

Third, Huang Dong pointed out that some mobile phone after-sales repair centers do not always get parts directly from the phone manufacturers but also through a mobile phone parts supplier. For the phone manufacturers, the parts suppliers are first-tier distributors, while the after-sales repair centers are second-tier distributors.

Generally speaking, after-sales repair centers sign agreements with parts suppliers, who stipulate the number of motherboards, screens, and other parts the repair center must purchase each month. Isn't this strange? If fewer customers come to a particular repair center in a given month, how can the repair center meet the agreement with the parts supplier?

That's right, as mentioned earlier, "find ways" to make phones within the warranty period appear as non-warranty phones as much as possible.

Now, everyone understands who is the main culprit behind the exorbitant profits in mobile phone repairs, right? In order to satisfy their own interests and achieve performance targets, after-sales repair centers turn users into victims.
