A die-hard Apple fan's "defection" voice: Android is no longer the same Android

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 13:45:51

Whenever someone asks me which phone is the best, I always recommend the iPhone. From the perspective of phone experience, smoothness, and excellent applications, the iPhone is undoubtedly still the best. But the more people ask, the more uneasy I feel - my experience with Android phones is still stuck three years ago.

Three years ago in the Android world, friends around me had just switched from the controversial HTC Hero (G3) to Google's flagship Nexus One. Whether it was G3 or N1, compared to the iPhone 4 released in the second half of 2010, as Steve Jobs put it when he first launched the iPhone: “five years ahead of the industry.” Comparing the iPhone 4 with the Android devices of the same year, the "five years ahead" claim was not an exaggeration.

Three years have passed, and the more people ask, the less confident I feel. After all, the changes between the iPhone 4 and the iPhone 5 are really small. What changes have happened in Android over these three years? I don't know.

More than half a year ago, I used the Xiaomi 1S and Xiaomi 2 for a few weeks. Saying "a few weeks," in reality, it was just playing with them for half a day before setting them aside and eventually giving them away. The last time I truly used an Android phone was four weeks ago. Initially, I swapped my old iPhone 4 with a friend for a Samsung Galaxy SII (which is quite outdated now), and out of curiosity about Chinese brands like ZTE and Huawei, I also tried the 5.7-inch ZTE U5 and the Huawei Ascend P6 that had a grand launch in London. Of course, the Nexus 4, Google's flagship, was the one I was most interested in.

It’s not enough to just "experience" a few phones. You must use an Android phone as your daily driver — ideally leaving your iPhone at home. After all, many times we reject new things simply because of habit. Only by letting go of the habit of using an iPhone can you truly understand Android.

After putting my iPhone 5 in my bag (I have two SIM cards; the iPhone 5 is my main phone for receiving calls, so I have to carry it, but it’s almost like leaving it behind), I installed my daily apps on the borrowed Nexus 4— WeChat, Baidu Maps, Gmail, Instapaper, Dropbox, Didi Taxi, Douban Movies/ QQ Movie Tickets, Dianping, Evernote, Phoenix/Tencent News Apps, Path, Duokan Reader, Bloomberg Businessweek, Flipboard, Chrome, Douban FM, CamCard, as well as Android-specific apps like Wandoujia and Sogou Input Method—and began living as a complete Android user after being a complete iPhone user.

After using the Nexus 4 for three weeks, my feeling is: I want to get rid of my iPhone 5.

I'm not saying that the Nexus 4 surpasses the iPhone 5 in all important features/experiences. In fact, the flaws of the Nexus 4 are very obvious. For example:

- After running multiple apps on the N4, the battery life that could last a whole day on the iPhone 5 only lasts half a day on the N4, requiring frequent charging.

- The screen resolution of the N4 reaches 318 ppi, almost the same as the iPhone 5's 326 ppi, but the display quality is clearly inferior to the iPhone 5.

- The automatic brightness adjustment capability of the N4 is far inferior to that of the iPhone 5. In a completely dark environment, the N4 can be used as a flashlight, but reading on it is too glaring. During the day under strong sunlight, it is almost impossible to see the N4 screen clearly, while the iPhone 5 performs well in both completely dark and sunny conditions, although not perfectly satisfying, it is generally comfortable. The N4 requires manual brightness adjustment to be usable, which is far from comfortable.

- The hardware of the N4 is relatively rough. Both the power button and volume keys feel soft. It took me more than ten tries to successfully take a screenshot by pressing the power key + volume down simultaneously. When swapping SIM cards on the N4, I encountered some difficulties. If you're not careful, it's easy to insert the card backward, and if forced, it might easily get stuck inside or damage the SIM card or slot. The design of the iPhone 5 is excellent, with a pinhole on the SIM card tray. As long as the pinhole aligns, there won't be any mistakes. However, this isn't too critical, as regular users don't usually swap SIM cards daily.

- Weight. After using the iPhone 5 for some time, picking up the iPhone 4/4S feels clumsy (heavy). Most of the time, after using the N4, picking up the iPhone 5 feels light and hard to put down, making you want to play with it more.

- The fast scrolling experience on the N4 lags significantly behind the iPhone. The text blurs during quick swipes and takes a couple of seconds to become clear, lacking fluidity. Meanwhile, the bounce-back effect when browsing web pages on the iPhone is irresistible.

Although there are quite a few drawbacks, the following three advantages of the N4 are enough to make me abandon the iPhone and switch to Android:

1. The feature that attracted me the most initially was the N4's pull-down notification center.

The iPhone 5 also uses a pull-down curtain for notifications, but the way to clear messages is awkward: you need to click the small "x" in the upper right corner of each app's notification list and then click "Clear" to merely remove one app's notifications.

Conservatively estimated, text messages, Sina Weibo, news apps, WeChat, calendar, emails, air quality index, TV series reminders, subscribed magazine alerts, etc., even for a slightly heavy user, there are more than 10 apps' notifications, multiplied by 5 or 10 items each — I don’t know how long other people's iPhones have their notifications piled up, but my iPhone 5 has accumulated eight and a half screens of notifications, including news from two days ago, spam SMS about Beijing apartments at so-called bargain prices, schedules from three days ago, repeatedly forwarded but identical posts and tweets, and air pollution indexes from the past five days.

I never bother to clear each app's notifications individually. Think about it, clearing each app requires two clicks, and doing that for dozens of apps would require countless clicks. I’m thoroughly annoyed. Notifications pile up, and after a while, I just ignore them. Those eight and a half screens of expired notifications are like a morgue of outdated notifications, left there untouched.

However, the N4's notification center reduces the steps by just one click — in fact, the N4's notification center can only swipe away one message at a time rather than a group (Note: I know it can clear all, here I refer to a group of similar notifications, such as three text messages or five news alerts). But the psychological satisfaction of swiping away a notification with a single finger movement is very pleasant. Because the process of swiping itself is fun, often eliminating a notification becomes enjoyable, and when there are too many, one click can clear everything instantly. This unloads the mental burden.

Swiping to eliminate notifications seems simple, but it has greater value — it unconsciously cultivates consistent user habits. Previously on the iPhone, I wasn't accustomed to gesture operations. After using the N4 for a few days, at some moment, I suddenly realized how much I enjoyed swiping away junk emails in Gmail, just like eliminating notifications in the manager center. Swiping once, and the junk email disappears. That is to say, the gesture operation of swiping to eliminate notifications in the pull-down curtain unconsciously cultivated a consistent experience across other apps. It is efficient, natural, and without any learning cost.

I didn’t have such a habit on the iPhone because the split between system gestures and app gestures led to inconsistency. Gesture operations were originally promoted by Apple to the masses, but now they are best experienced on Android, which is somewhat ironic.

2. Multitasking Management

N4's multitasking management is similar to its pull-down notification center, belonging to a smart and insightful design in the current Android system (4.2.2/4.3). I can't find particularly good words to describe it, but compared to the N4, iPhone's multitasking management requires double-clicking the Home button to bring up, long-pressing icons, and clicking the "x" to exit. A series of operations feels very clumsy, similar to the feeling of the gear icon used in the "Settings" before iOS 6: it looks intricate, but actually, it's very mechanical and industrial.

N4's multitasking management, on the other hand, is different: one touch brings it up, and a finger slide exits the app, light and agile. The N4's interface looks fresher. The visual and psychological feelings are consistent. When I first got the N4, I was attracted by the simplicity of these two icons.

Additionally, in Apple's iOS 7 introduced this year, besides changing the design style from skeuomorphism to flat design, iOS 7's multitasking management also learned from the Android system, allowing swipe-to-exit apps, which feels great.

3. Seamless information sharing across apps

This is what impressed me the most about the N4. iOS controls inter-app connections too strictly. If users could set Google Maps as the default map, even the terrible iOS 6 maps wouldn't have caused such a huge backlash among users, and Scott Forstall, the first hero of iOS, might not have lost his job due to his mistake. Seamless information sharing across apps has many benefits, such as:

1) I use the default mail application for work emails and Gmail for personal emails. Every time I see good content or take photos to share with friends, the default application is always the iOS default mail application, not Gmail. This leads to me frequently sharing private information via my work email or not sharing at all. On the N4, this problem doesn't exist; I can change the default to open either work or personal email.

2) When reading articles on my iPhone's browser and encountering exciting passages, I can only share them via email (as mentioned earlier, it defaults to my work email). To save good passages to Evernote, I copy them through email. With the N4, I can directly click "Share" and save them to Evernote. Although the operational steps haven't been simplified, the logic is more direct. I can directly save to Evernote instead of going through email. If I need to edit document keywords and folders, the N4 allows direct modification, something email forwarding cannot achieve.

At this point, I can't help but criticize domestic applications.

The most typical example is WeChat, praised by countless people, which has disabled inter-application information sharing. To create a so-called closed-loop ecosystem, it has built a wall for itself. Seeing a good article, I can't directly save it to Instapaper or Evernote via the default share button (because there isn't one) and have to open the link in Chrome and then save it to Instapaper or Evernote in Chrome, creating unnecessary obstacles for users. Very regrettable.

Even more regrettable is that after reading so many articles discussing WeChat, none criticized this point. — This is a key function where Android surpasses iOS, breaking down the walled gardens of applications. I wonder if domestic commentators have seriously played with the products they talk about every day.

In conclusion, whether it's design style or multitasking management, iOS 7 has started learning from Android, but iOS's control over applications remains too strict. Early high standards helped produce quality applications and prevent system ecology from being disrupted, but six years later, iOS's ecological environment has matured, and Apple's official control is rapidly weakening.

Aside from the Apple Maps example mentioned above, when iOS 6 integrated Sina Weibo, it was already during Sina Weibo's declining period. By the time it finally integrated Tencent Weibo services this year, even Tencent no longer discusses the product in its financial reports, showing how slow Apple's response is to integrating popular market products. More Chinese users may prefer iOS to integrate WeChat rather than outdated Weibo products.

In the iOS world with over 900,000 applications, it's becoming increasingly unrealistic for the "big brother" to provide the optimal service combinations.

Postscript

After this article was published on GeekPark, it sparked quite a bit of controversy. There were more criticisms on Weibo and in the comments section, but over 180 people quietly liked it, and more than 50 people shared it. Thank you all.

After writing such a long piece of "rambling" (which might seem like rambling to many people, but not to me—I only write my own thought processes), my overall evaluation of the iPhone and Nexus 4 can be summarized in one tweet:

The overall experience of the iPhone is undeniably good. In comparison, the advantages of the Nexus 4 are mainly these: convenient notification center and multitasking management, as well as inter-app communication. Compared to the iPhone's good experience, I am more likely to choose the Nexus 4 for these three reasons. That's all. At this stage of smartphone development, there is no single player who can completely dominate others.

For those who closely follow Android developments, there's nothing new here. However, I believe many people are still unaware of Android's progress, so these experiences are still worth writing about.

Additionally, some comments pointed out that starting Dropbox on the iPhone allows automatic background photo uploads. I tested it, and indeed, it works. My previous example was incorrect, but it does not affect the core meaning.

Regarding the N4's notification center, a good friend gave the following opinion:

We can think about why Apple designed it this way. Personally, I think it might be because they want everyone to carefully review each message from each app. For Chinese users with low signal-to-noise ratios, this is obviously overestimating the importance of the notification bar information. Actually, the notification bar messages are not as important as we think, so designs that overly emphasize the notification bar do not conform to people's usage patterns.

My thoughts are: regarding Apple's notification center, if Apple really thinks this way, then it's a bad design because you cannot "force" users to operate something specific. Clicking the small "x" is actually quite troublesome and shifts focus. The "notification bar" is not that important, which makes sense. My job involves dealing with information every day, so I need to push information to the first screen as much as possible, allowing me to see it without unlocking or opening the app, improving efficiency. But for regular users, they only have one screen of apps, and notifications won't be excessive, so my emphasis on the notification bar represents non-typical user behavior.

Finally, let me stress: I am clearly not a "typical user" or a "novice user," but I am not entirely a non-typical user either. All habits are "personalized." That is, my experiences include both "typical parts" and "non-typical parts." I still emphasize personal experiences, even though this is the part that causes the most controversy. So, take what suits you best.
