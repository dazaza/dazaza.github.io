So let's start a business.

by hxnyfi64 on 2011-06-11 17:26:47

Not long ago, a report titled "IT Talent Investigation Report" showed that due to the rapid updates in technology and concepts in the IT industry, it has become an industry that favors youth, with many IT professionals facing age-related career barriers by the age of 35. As a result, amidst hesitation and uncertainty, many IT professionals have considered starting their own businesses. According to data from this survey, 32% of IT professionals have aspirations to be their own boss. So, is 35 the golden age for IT professionals to start their own business? Let's look at some facts: Charles Zhang successfully launched Sohu website at the age of 34 and was named one of the world's digital elites by the American magazine "Time" that same year; James Liang, also at 34, saw the travel website he founded, Ctrip.com, go public on NASDAQ in the United States; and Wang Wenjing, CEO of UFIDA Financial Software, became China's youngest president of a private listed company at the age of 36... These IT tycoons have shown through their own experiences that at 35, it's just the right time to start a business.

35: The Crossroads of Career Growth for IT Professionals

It is understood that the "35 phenomenon" is very common in today’s IT industry. It is well known that the IT industry is the fastest in terms of survival of the fittest. IT technology evolves rapidly, and technical talent must keep up with advancements to avoid being eliminated. However, from a psychological perspective, a 30-year-old programmer compared to a 20-year-old programmer will inevitably show differences in learning ability, response speed, and work efficiency. Therefore, many IT companies, in order to reduce development costs, tend to choose younger employees. Thus, IT professionals around the age of 35 often face a crossroads in their career growth.

Zhang Ling is an old IT professional with more than seven years of experience, currently working as a senior engineer for a renowned software company, but is also facing a decision regarding his future in the field. Zhang Ling told reporters that part of the reason he chose this line of work initially was the high income, but more importantly, it was because he saw great potential in the growth of the IT industry. However, reality hasn't been as rosy as he imagined. Working in IT is tough work, facing cold machines every day, repeating programming and debugging routines, making the job monotonous and tedious, leading easily to fatigue. Externally, IT technology advances rapidly, and you must constantly replenish your knowledge, thus the pressure to stay relevant is significant. I am already in my thirties, and I can't keep wasting my life like this, always busy like a bee without tasting the honey. Recently, I've been pondering whether I should step back and start a new venture?

Zhang Ling's concerns are shared by many IT professionals today. As the luster of IT careers fades and youth increasingly becomes a cost of career advancement in IT, some people in their mid-30s within the industry have to reassess their future. Some transition into management roles, while others even decide to leave the field and start their own businesses.

35: Can it Become a New Starting Point for IT Professionals' Careers?

Regarding the "35 phenomenon" in the IT industry, Bian Bingbin, Chief Career Consultant at Kerui Management Consulting Co., Ltd., believes that after about 5 to 8 years of work, there is a possibility of experiencing fatigue. This is especially true in the IT industry, where the pace of technological change and work is fast, making it easier for professionals to feel fatigued. However, this isn't necessarily a bad thing. At 35, professionals have mature minds, experienced judgment, rich experience, and advanced expertise, fully capable of entering another phase of career growth. Thus, this "35 phenomenon" sometimes can serve as a catalyst, breaking old habits and finding a new starting point for one's career.

Nowadays, many IT professionals around the age of 35 have thoughts of starting their own businesses. Regarding this, Bian Bingbin believes that although 35-year-old IT professionals may not have the energy of younger people, they do have the advantages of experience and accumulated knowledge, along with financial resources and networking, giving them the conditions to start their own businesses and reach another peak in their careers. However, one point to note is that IT professionals’ entrepreneurial direction should remain within the IT field. If one abandons their professional capital advantage to pursue an unfamiliar field or industry, everything would have to start anew, significantly increasing both the difficulty and risk of entrepreneurship.

Bian Bingbin also points out that transitioning from being an employee to a boss is a big leap, so it must be carefully considered. First, one needs a correct self-assessment, considering their strengths, suitability for independent entrepreneurship, and whether they have sufficient financial resources. Second, one must shift from a technical mindset to acquiring new knowledge in management and marketing for future career growth. Third, a comprehensive objective analysis of the intended entrepreneurial field must be conducted, and based on research, the best entrepreneurial model designed.

35: Just the Right Time for IT Professionals to Start Their Own Business

The "35 phenomenon" is an undeniable fact in the IT world. For many IT professionals, 35 signifies the end of their technical career. But if viewed from another angle, 35 also marks the beginning of a new career. The key lies in whether one can timely switch perspectives, seize fleeting opportunities, and smoothly transition during a critical period in their career, thereby gaining control over their career growth.

Hu Liwei, at 36 years old, is the general manager of Wei Yi Software Technology Co., Ltd. Five years ago, he was merely a software engineer at a multinational electronics company. Reflecting on that transformation, he vividly described it as: “You suddenly feel like you’ve grown wings, flying freely in the sky. When the weather is clear, you can fly freely; even if you encounter storms, you can still control your direction.” The immense joy after successful entrepreneurship and the boundless scenery after reaching a higher level is entirely different from the feeling of receiving a regular paycheck each month.

Of course, gains come with sacrifices. Hu Liwei gave practical advice: There are significant differences between being a boss and an employee. Transitioning from an executor to a decision-maker requires supplementation and accumulation of much new knowledge and skills, adjustment of one's mindset, and adaptation to a new external environment—far from being as simple as imagined. Especially for IT professionals starting a business, who were originally skilled in technical fields, it can be difficult to quickly adapt to managerial responsibilities. Therefore, IT professionals must prepare more thoroughly when starting a business, continuously strengthening their technical foundation while familiarizing themselves with enterprise management, market marketing, financial operations, human resources, and other related matters.

For IT professionals planning to start a business, Hu Liwei suggests: If you truly have the ambition to achieve something great, the determination to overcome difficulties, and the patience to make adequate preparations, then go ahead and start your business—35 is just the right time.
