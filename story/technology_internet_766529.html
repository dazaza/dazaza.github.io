Smartphone Market: A Paradise for the Strong with a Whiff of Bloodshed

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:44:27

As a continuously high-growth industry that is deeply favored by investors, the global smartphone market has experienced rapid expansion over the past four years with double-digit compound annual growth rates. The smartphone market has become a paradise for strong companies with excellent products to develop rapidly. However, the decline of former giants in the mobile phone market such as Nokia and BlackBerry tells us that companies unable to adapt to market changes will only wither away in this burgeoning green field.

Smartphone shipments surpass feature phones for the first time

The smartphone market has seen explosive growth over the past four years or so. In addition to developed economies like Europe and America, smartphones have become increasingly popular in many emerging markets. Their sales growth rate in recent years has far exceeded that of traditional mobile phones.

This year, smartphones finally surpassed feature phones in shipment volume. According to data provided by the authoritative market research firm IDC, global smartphone shipments in the first quarter reached 216 million units, accounting for 51.6% of total mobile phone shipments. "Mobile phone users want a computer in their pocket; the era when phones were used just for calls and text messages is quickly fading," said Kevin Restivo, a senior researcher at IDC.

Data shows that smartphones can conveniently download and install software to expand phone functionality, whereas feature phones have poor or no extensibility.

IDC predicts that this year's global mobile phone market shipment volume will grow by only 7.3%, but global smartphone sales will maintain a high year-on-year growth rate of 40%. Based on last year's global shipment of about 700 million smartphones, this year's shipment volume is expected to approach one billion units.

From 2008 to last year, the global smartphone shipment volume had an annual compound growth rate of 46.6%, and the global smartphone sales volume doubled in the past two years.

The booming development of the smartphone market also allowed some of China's smartphone manufacturers to stand out. In the top 10 global smartphone shipments in the second quarter this year, besides Lenovo, ZTE and Huawei are also included, with market shares of 4.3% and 4% respectively.

Huge potential in emerging markets

After several years of rapid development, concerns about the saturation of the vast U.S. market have become increasingly evident, but emerging markets remain vibrant. This month, the research agency BI Intelligence interviewed more than a dozen leaders and entrepreneurs in the mobile device industry regarding opportunities in the BRICS countries and concluded that there is a huge demand for smartphones in emerging markets.

Take India as an example, where smartphones are becoming the primary gateway for consumers in emerging markets to access the Internet. In the country, smartphone data usage has already surpassed that of desktops. Additionally, since many devices in emerging markets are sold through prepaid plans without operator subsidies, the rapid price drop has made it easier for consumers to purchase smartphones. In the past three years, smartphone prices in India have fallen by 30% to 35%.

The situation of declining smartphone average selling prices is common in emerging market countries. IDC's "Global Mobile Phone Quarterly Tracking Report" published in June this year showed that due to the enormous demand for smartphones in emerging markets, the global smartphone average selling price has dropped by 8.5% from last year to $372.

Research agency TechNavio even boldly predicted that under the stimulus of continuously declining smartphone average selling prices, the compound annual growth rate of the smartphone market in BRICS countries will reach as high as 75% between 2011 and 2015.

Fierce competition for market share

However, the high growth of smartphones does not mean that this market is an opportunity for all manufacturers. Market data from the past few years shows that the basic market structure can change dramatically in a short period of time.

Public data shows that as early as the second quarter of 2008, Nokia's Symbian operating system and BlackBerry's (formerly RIM) operating system held market shares of 47% and 17%, respectively. But within just three short years, the market share underwent a complete transformation, with Google's Android and Apple's iOS systems capturing market shares of 48% and 19%, respectively, while Symbian's market share was quickly compressed to 16%.

Likewise, some powerful companies in the smartphone industry that cannot adapt to market changes can quickly decline in a short period of time. For instance, Nokia's smartphone shipment volume in 2008 was as high as 40.2%, far ahead of Samsung and Apple. But by 2011, its market share had plummeted to 15.6%, and market data from 2012 showed that once-dominant Nokia's market share was only one-fourth of Apple's.

The Decline of BlackBerry

A company's product determines its standing in the smartphone market. If it fails to gain consumer favor, it will be quickly abandoned. BlackBerry is a typical example. Before the rise of Android smartphones, BlackBerry was a standout in the global mobile phone market, with user numbers reaching 10 million by the end of 2007.

Initially, BlackBerry did not take the rise of Android and Apple phones seriously, but its market share was quickly eroded. In 2009, BlackBerry's market share based on shipment volume was nearly 20%, but by 2012, its market share in the global smartphone market had dropped to around 4.5%.

In an effort to reverse its decline, BlackBerry launched smartphones equipped with the newly developed BlackBerry 10 operating system at the beginning of this year: the touch-screen "Z10" and the "Q10" that retained the traditional physical keyboard. However, this product failed to provide BlackBerry with a comeback opportunity. Currently, BlackBerry has only 120,000 apps, which is far fewer than the rich software ecosystem of Android's nearly 860,000 apps, significantly reducing its product appeal.

BlackBerry's smartphone shipments in the first quarter of fiscal year 2014 (March to May ending June 1, 2013) were 6.8 million units, much lower than the 7.5 million units analysts had previously estimated. Of these 6.8 million units, 2.7 million were equipped with the BlackBerry 10 operating system, which also fell short of the analysts' prediction of 3.6 million units.
