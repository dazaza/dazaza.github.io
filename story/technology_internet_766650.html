iPhone 5s Review Roundup: Touch ID, Camera, and A7 Processor

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 21:11:17

The brief hands-on with the iPhone 5s after its release didn't leave a deep impression on the media, and feedback at the time was quite limited. After a more in-depth experience with the iPhone 5s, more detailed review reports have been released today. This article summarizes reviews from five media outlets, focusing on the Touch ID fingerprint sensor, the main camera with a larger aperture, and the 64-bit A7 chip. Let's hear their feedback.

Touch ID

Engadget

There are many reasons not to want to see a fingerprint scanner on your phone: past models have proven how unreliable fingerprint recognition can be, causing more trouble than it's worth. For example, the Motorola Atrix 4G, which came equipped with an optical fingerprint scanner, required multiple scans but had a very low success rate.

Apple has embedded a capacitive fingerprint sensor under the Home button of the iPhone 5s, covering the sensor with a layer of sapphire glass for protection. The metal ring around the Home button collects fingerprint information from the user, then passes it to the sensor. The sensor captures images of the user's fingerprint from various angles, then hands them over to the A7 processor. Touch ID captures fingerprint information from beneath the outer layer of dead skin on the finger, with image quality reaching up to 500ppi.

The setup process is simple, generally taking no more than one minute. The iPhone 5s can store up to five different fingerprints, which can come from different fingers of the same person or from different people. Collecting fingerprints may seem cumbersome at first, but it becomes easier after a few tries. First, you need to touch your finger to the Home button several times. Once enough information is collected, the iPhone will prompt you to rotate your finger and rescan. After collecting the fingerprint, the phone asks you to name it so that there won't be confusion when using multiple fingerprints. To prevent fingerprint misidentification, the iPhone 5s also requires setting a passcode, allowing the phone to be unlocked by passcode if scanning fails.

Touch ID is incredibly fast, completing a scan in less than a second upon touching the Home button. The process is so natural that we almost forgot that the iPhone 5s has a passcode and lock screen set. We unconsciously tried unlocking the iPhone 5 and iPhone 5c in the same way countless times before realizing that only "old-style" slide-to-unlock would work. Of course, Touch ID isn't perfect: wet or partially placed fingers cannot unlock the phone, though we did not encounter issues with sticky fingers failing to unlock the iPhone 5s.

Out of privacy concerns, Apple states that all fingerprint information is converted into digital signatures, encrypted, and stored within the chip. In other words, fingerprint data will not be uploaded to Apple servers or iCloud. Additionally, cutting off the owner's finger to unlock the iPhone 5s will not work—Touch ID can only scan live tissue. Furthermore, after restarting the phone or going 48 hours without unlocking, the user can only unlock via passcode.

New York Times

The fingerprint sensor is one of the most important features of the iPhone 5s, cleverly embedded under the Home button. Tapping the Home button wakes up the iPhone, and half a second of finger contact unlocks the phone. During testing, Touch ID always worked effectively, unlike those annoying fingerprint sensors from before. It's truly remarkable, and those who dislike it might as well find a dock to jump off.

You can also use Touch ID to scan your fingerprint when purchasing books, music, and apps. Although there are currently some bugs, Apple says they will push out a patch fix by Friday.

TechCrunch

At first glance, people might easily think that fingerprint scanning is just a gimmick with little practical use. But this isn't the case. Touch ID is a mature feature that enhances the overall user experience of the iPhone.

The fingerprint registration process is also straightforward, with the iPhone 5s guiding the entire process. Users can register up to five fingerprints and decide which ones to use for unlocking the phone and which ones for app purchases.

Once registered, placing your finger casually on the Home button quickly unlocks the iPhone, almost as fast as having no passcode set. During use, we encountered some prompts asking for re-scans, but these gradually decreased and eventually disappeared as usage increased. Apple says that Touch ID learns users' scanning methods as usage increases, thereby improving success rates.

AllThingsD

Laptops and phones have already used fingerprint scanners in the past, but they were unreliable, leading users to generally avoid using them. The technology Apple uses is different; the Home button has become a fast and accurate fingerprint scanner.

Apple says that dead tissue cannot unlock the phone, and severed fingers or fingerprint copies commonly seen in movies have no effect on Touch ID.

If three scans fail, the iPhone 5s will display a password unlock option. After five failures, the iPhone requires unlocking via passcode. Apple says the chance of someone else unlocking the phone through Touch ID is 1/50,000, while the chance of unlocking with a four-digit passcode is 1/10,000.

iSight Main Camera

Engadget

Apple’s attitude towards iPhone imaging capabilities: constantly getting better, keeping it simple.

The daytime imaging effect of the iPhone 5s is roughly on par with the iPhone 5, with slightly more detail, but the low-light performance is clearly superior to the iPhone 5: sharper images, richer details, more natural colors, and fewer noise points. However, compared to the sample shots taken by the Nokia Lumia 1020, the iPhone 5s samples have more noise and fewer details. It must be acknowledged that the iPhone 5s excels in color reproduction, but it still isn’t the best player in this field.

Thanks to the new ISP used in the A7, the iPhone 5s supports automatic graphic stabilization and can take four photos with a single exposure. The phone automatically analyzes key nodes in each photo, such as exposure value, sharpness, and face recognition accuracy, selecting the best picture. The iPhone 5s also supports burst mode, shooting up to 10 photos per second. Photos are automatically saved in subfolders of the album and can be opened for editing. Panorama mode is faster than the iPhone 5, with Apple claiming imaging speed improved by up to 50%.

True Tone dual flash is another feature of the iPhone 5s. The iPhone 5s can determine exposure density and flash combinations based on the color temperature of the surrounding environment. The final imaging results in natural colors, with excellent still life and portraits.

SLR cameras and some high-end smartphones have supported slow-motion shooting in the past, but most could only reach a shooting speed of 60fps. The iPhone 5s can capture 120fps slow-motion video, allowing users to decide which parts of the video can be shown in slow motion.

New York Times

The new camera brings more content: the image sensor has increased by 15%, and the pixel size has also increased significantly. Compared to previous products, the low-light performance of the iPhone 5s is better: clearer, brighter, and more accurate colors.

The iPhone 5s supports burst mode, 3x video zoom, Instagram-style filters, and slow-motion video. However, strangely, filter images and slow-motion videos cannot be viewed on a computer.

TechCrunch

Apple is confident about the camera effects of the iPhone 5s, consistent with its view: higher megapixels do not necessarily mean better photos. The 8-megapixel iSight camera remains the same as the previous model, but the individual pixel size is larger, with an aperture size reaching up to f/2.2.

Improvements in the camera include the True Tone dual flash, which can adapt to background lighting and better reflect the surrounding environment. In actual use, turning off the flash remains the best shooting solution, but comparing the shooting effects of the iPhone 5c, it is clear that the iPhone 5s compensates for natural light much better than the iPhone 5c.

(Comparison of flash samples between the iPhone 5s and iPhone 5c, note the edge of the protective cover. Image from TechCrunch)

Lens improvements also include an automatic graphics stabilization system, taking four photos with a single exposure, and the system selects the best one. Burst mode supports shooting 10 photos per second, and burst photos can be used to create GIF images. I wonder why Apple doesn't have a built-in GIF creation tool?

AllThingsD

The new camera has a larger sensor and pixel size, capturing more light and color. All shooting effects are slightly sharper than the iPhone 5, with fewer details washed out by the flash in low light.

A7 Chip

Engadget

The A7 uses the ARMv8 instruction set, making the iPhone 5s the first smartphone to use a 64-bit chip. In most cases, a 64-bit chip allows games and high-throughput applications that support it to perform better without affecting battery life too much. The iOS 7 system based on the 64-bit processor also allows developers to achieve higher development efficiency. Apple says that the A7 runs twice as fast and delivers twice the graphics performance as the A6.

Although there hasn't been an opportunity to test applications and games that support the 64-bit chip yet, overall, it's evident that the iPhone 5s feels faster, with smoother application loading and operation. The GPU of the A7 begins supporting OpenGL ES 3.0, allowing developers to add more complex scene details and make game scenes more realistic.

The M7 coprocessor, used to reduce the load on the A7, mainly handles data from the gyroscope, accelerometer, and compass. With the help of the M7 coprocessor, health-related apps consume less power when running in the background.

(Performance comparison between the iPhone 5, iPhone 5c, and iPhone 5s, image from Engadget)

AllThingsD

The iPhone 5s adopts a 64-bit processor, meaning the system can handle even more massive amounts of data. However, during testing, there wasn't a noticeable speed improvement, possibly because developers haven't had time to rewrite programs targeting the A7 and fully utilize the high performance of the 64-bit chip.

CNET

We live in a great era for mobile chips, where phone chips rapidly improve like laptops and desktop computers did a few years ago. The iPhone 5 runs twice as fast as the iPhone 4S, and the iPhone 5s looks to run at least twice as fast as the iPhone 5.
