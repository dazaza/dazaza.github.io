Post-80s Graduate Returns to Village to Set Up Kindergarten, Says Entrepreneurship at Home Is Tough

by o6nihao8h on 2012-03-06 19:09:23

For the initial intention of setting up this rural kindergarten, as the name "common people's kindergarten" reveals, I want to fairly popularize "commoner quality education" in today's Chinese countryside starting from the early childhood stage. This mainly includes the morality, living habits, language habits, creative thinking, and physical health of rural children. In this process, courses related to rural culture, geography, science, art, and more will be offered, incorporating the advantages of urban early childhood education. This allows growing children to rebuild a new kind of local culture from themselves, changing their own and the countryside's reality at the spiritual level.

In October, I was ready to return home and start a kindergarten. My parents' doubts were very clear and realistic: first, there is a lack of funds for setting up a kindergarten, and I can hardly even solve my personal living problems. Second, being a graduate student in our family should represent status, but if I mess around like this, that status becomes a burden. Third, I cannot solve my personal life and marriage issues.

It was just the time when spring rain brought a chill, yet this rural courtyard located in western China was unusually lively. More than 20 rural children cheered and jumped out of the classroom, filling the small courtyard with laughter and joy.

I didn't expect that when I first came to the village to set up a kindergarten, I could enroll six children, later increasing to nine. In fact, I was already very happy, indicating that some villagers were beginning to trust me, which increased my confidence. All beginnings are difficult. The real pressures once almost made me unable to hold on. But I firmly believe, "As long as you persist, you will succeed."

Due to differences in educational philosophy and starting points with my friend, I was forced to temporarily leave in late April 2009. However, he later became one of the most powerful witnesses to the establishment of my kindergarten. I then drifted in Lanzhou helping others with exhibitions, publishing books, and making sculptures to solve my survival issues.

In July 2008, I went to Beijing alone, with empty luggage but full of hope. Passing by 798 Art Zone, I was excited. This contemporary art "hall" that had been legendary in books and online was right before my eyes. That year, it was a time of grand celebrations and festive holidays for the country, but for "Beijing drifters" like me, it carried indescribable pain.

"The Common People's Kindergarten" continued my idea of speaking for more grassroots people when I left Beijing, and it was further improved. The disparity between urban and rural areas in China has worsened the situation in rural areas. Under the existing conditions in the countryside, forming a professional and stable team of teachers for rural kindergartens is very difficult, and these realities will affect the lives and ideals of rural kindergartens.

In my mind, all the children who come to the kindergarten are equal, regardless of whether their parents are village officials or migrant workers. "The Common People's Kindergarten" should not be just mine; rural education should have its own characteristics, and this place should be an experimental base for rural education.

Spending time with these children makes me very happy, and it is what has kept me going until now. Now, the kindergarten has initially taken shape, with more than 20 children.

Persistence

After enduring great hardship to jump out of the "rural gate" and obtaining a master's degree, choosing to return to the countryside to be a "child teacher" is admirable. Hu Jianqiang's actions are commendable, but we must admit that among tens of millions of college graduates, Hu Jianqiang is a minority, a peer. We all know that university graduates going to the grassroots, going to the countryside, is key to solving college graduate employment problems, breaking the "urban disease" dilemma, and promoting the construction of a new countryside. Governments, educational departments, and public opinion have been vigorously calling for and implementing various incentive policies, but with little effect. Not to mention graduates from the city, even those born and raised in rural areas would rather wander in crowded cities, labeled as the "ant tribe," than return to their hometowns and families.

To be honest, I cannot answer all the doubts and suspicions, just as some issues cannot be solved by mere talk. I am too lazy to deal with those who look down on me and can only solve all the problems I encounter. The first step is conducting a survey of rural early childhood education. To ensure the future kindergarten could reach a certain scale, I spent several ten thousand yuan to buy an idle courtyard from my neighbor that had been unused for 30 years. Of course, the fundraising process was filled with difficulties and setbacks, during which I received substantial support from my father. Subsequently, with the help of my only supporter, my second brother, we tidied up the yard and classrooms currently used by the kindergarten and purchased tables, benches, blackboards, and other kindergarten teaching aids. I don’t know how to persuade my family, nor do I need to convince others in the village. I really started a kindergarten through my actions, appearing in a place where everyone thought I shouldn’t be and in a profession where they thought I couldn’t handle.

"Graduate school principal" Hu Jianqiang and his students. Currently, this rural kindergarten is facing its toughest time.

Of course, this is just my ideal. I face many difficulties, much like almost all rural kindergartens in China. Financially, relying solely on myself or the kindergarten itself to sustain is impossible. Attempting profit-driven early childhood education among farmers — the lowest class — sounds unreliable. I’m not foolish enough to return from Beijing to start such a venture in the countryside.

Despite unresolved personal living issues, for a long period afterward, I pondered what I could do, what I wanted to achieve, and from what standpoint a knowledgeable person should speak. What should be said, etc., and some trivialities in Beijing constantly pricked me. So, leaving Beijing wasn’t about fleeing for me.

This young man born in 1981, a rural "post-80s," relied on his talent and effort, graduating smoothly two years ago from the Graduate School of Fine Arts at Northwest Normal University. Afterwards, he went to Beijing and worked as an exhibition assistant at a famous gallery in the renowned 798 Art District, earning a monthly salary of 3000 yuan, like many "Beijing drifters," carrying dreams and hoping to carve out a space in Beijing.

In February 2009, on the eve of the Spring Festival, I left Beijing. The winter wind in Beijing cut like a knife.

On the low kitchen mud wall to the east, one can easily identify the black portraits of celebrities like Mao Zedong, Lu Xun, and Tao Xingzhi. These vivid portraits were all painted by the founder of this rural kindergarten.

He is Hu Jianqiang, the teacher of more than 20 rural children and the young principal of a struggling rural kindergarten. Recently, our reporter visited this western rural kindergarten located in Yebao Village, Qin'an County, Gansu Province, listening to the heartfelt voice of this rural college graduate. He said, "As someone from the grassroots, if I still have ideals, I should speak for more grassroots people" —

During a media debate, someone criticized me: Is it too idealistic for you to return to the countryside and open a kindergarten? This question was so silly that I couldn't answer it. Everyone has ideals; some people's ideals are skyscrapers, luxury cars, and beauties; some people's ideals are power and money; some people's ideals are simply to have enough food to eat. And some people's ideals are not about talking, but about doing.

Beijing Drift

This 29-year-old young man began his ideal experiment. He tried to rebuild rural culture and restore confidence and cultural roots to rural children. He collected specimens of animals and plants, treating embroidered shoe pads made by rural women as artworks hung in the children's dormitories; in March, he took the children up the mountain to sketch, understand the rivers of their hometown, allowing rural children to love the countryside and their hometown. He even conceived the idea of writing a local textbook...

The reason for painting these portraits is because he believes they are all "people who speak for the grassroots."

As someone from the grassroots, if I still have ideals, I should speak for more grassroots people. I feel uneasy being a so-called graduate student with nothing remarkable to offer, while simultaneously feeling proud that there are worse, more boring graduates from prestigious universities, overseas returnees, and graduate students. I have no hope of rising from the grassroots, but I hope more people can always clearly recognize themselves. Education is something I can do, and going to places that need me to teach is self-evident. Thus, I thought of my hometown—a place that remains poor but beautiful. Starting with the children.

My first job in Beijing was helping a family of four Americans with an exhibition, working for three days without receiving a penny in compensation. The first time I made money in Beijing now seems both frustrating and beautiful; once, I and a friend drew simple pictures for someone. We spent half the night completing 10 pictures, and they used four, earning us 40 yuan. Two months later, I accidentally entered the gallery of a famous Chinese curator to work as an assistant. But this also accelerated my decision to abandon the capital and choose my own path.

After arriving in Beijing, I spent two months wandering, learning, adapting, and waking up early and staying up late. First, I closely encountered so-called contemporary art. During these two months, I visited many galleries in 798 and Cao Garden Art District, looking at exhibitions, wandering, or looking for jobs. During this period, I continuously attended interviews and job fairs, realizing the prosperity and noise, diversity and complexity, sophistication and poverty, freedom and rigidity of Beijing. For someone who spends half the time in the city and half the time wandering in the countryside, floating between urban and rural areas, Beijing, with its distinctively Chinese characteristics, made me feel hopeful. Life in Beijing belongs to a few people; more people are just trying it out. Unfortunately, they lose themselves, leaving their fate to chance.

Thus, I felt: rather than live day-to-day within the system, I might as well walk my own path. After finding a job proved unfruitful, I prepared with a friend to jointly establish a kindergarten in the Qin'an county town, and conflicts with my family escalated. During this time, I conducted research on rural education, including early childhood education. Later, when it became unbearable at home, I moved to the county town and lived with my friend. Emotions fluctuated; this was after all the place where I was born and grew up, and doing my own thing became difficult and distant.

But he soon realized that Beijing was too big, and he was too insignificant. "Using art as a means of livelihood is too hard." At a crossroads in life, the soil that nourished him became the source of his continuing dream, "Rural matters attracted me." Therefore, he resolutely returned to his hometown and founded this rural kindergarten, naming it "Common People's Kindergarten."

Upon returning home, during the New Year, I announced to my family my intention to set up a kindergarten. Initially, my family did not agree; my mother and relatives hoped I would find a stable job. Like everyone else, I naturally wanted an ideal job, but I realized that my repeated failures in job hunting weren't entirely my fault. For rural college students like me, with no social capital or family background, finding a fair platform for cooperation isn't easy.

Villagers talked more: I have no skills, wandered around for a few years outside, couldn't make it, and came back to run a kindergarten in the village, deceiving children to make a living.

I have never thought that being a graduate student makes me extraordinary. If I hadn't put down the halo of being a graduate student, I couldn't do this. Perhaps, putting aside identity first would improve the situation.
