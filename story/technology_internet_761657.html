A Year After Leaving the Internet - I'm Still Here

by geekzhang on 2013-05-05 21:38:31

This guy left the Internet for a whole year, hoping it would help him. Now he's back, but he's not sure if that year away from the Internet was good or bad for him. This is what he said: "I learned how to make wrong choices in a new way without the Internet. I abandoned the good habits of 'offline life' and developed the bad ones... People always mock the concept of 'Facebook friends' as being wrong and ridiculous, but let me tell you, 'Facebook friends' are much better than having nothing."

I was wrong.

A year ago, I left the Internet. I thought the online world was meaningless; because of the Internet, I felt like I had run out of ideas, and it was corroding my soul.

For a year, I stopped clicking on thumbs-up buttons and instead surfed the web more vividly, checked emails, or liked things in a more specific way. As I had planned, I successfully stayed offline. I was no longer bound by the Internet.

And now, I should tell you how offline living solved all my online problems, I should have some insights, and I should be more real, more perfect.

But the truth is quite the opposite. At 8 PM, I just woke up. I slept all day, and when I opened my eyes, there were already eight voice messages from colleagues and friends. I had dinner at a coffee shop, watched a New York Knicks game, read two newspapers and a copy of The New Yorker. Now I'm watching Toy Story, occasionally glancing at the flashing cursor in this text box, hoping it will magically produce words, hoping it will brew the kinds of epiphanies I haven't experienced in my life.

At the end of my year-long journey, I didn't want to meet this kind of Paul.

At the beginning of 2012, I was 26 years old, physically and mentally exhausted. I wanted to leave this modern life and give myself a rest - like a hamster on a wheel, drained by an incessant email and endless internet information. I wanted to escape. I thought the existence of the Internet put humans in an unnatural state, at least for me. Maybe I was too deep into it to get out, maybe I was too impulsive to control myself. Since I was 12, I've been using the Internet frequently, and after 14, the Internet became my entire life. In ten years, I went from being a paperboy to a web designer, and eventually became a technical documentation engineer. I didn't know who I was without the omnipresent connectivity and endless information. I didn't know what else there was in life. Perhaps "real life" was waiting for me on the other side of the browser.

I planned to quit my job, move back in with my parents, read books, write books, and immerse myself in leisure time without work. I would take a grand stance, overcome all impending midlife crises. I would find the real Paul, far from the noise of the network, achieve self-elevation.

For some reason, Verge was willing to pay me for my plan to leave the Internet. I could stay in New York, share my discoveries, and write about my offline life for those Internet users I left behind, sprinkling wisdom down from my tower.

As a technical documentation engineer, my goal was to explore the various impacts the Internet has had on me over the years, study the Internet from afar, and understand it better. Not only would I elevate myself, but I would also help all humanity become better. As long as we understand how the Internet corrupts us, we can fight back.

At 11:59 PM on April 30, 2012, I unplugged the Ethernet cable, turned off Wi-Fi, and swapped my smartphone for a dumb phone. It felt really good, freeing.

A few weeks later, I found myself among 60,000 ultra-orthodox Jewish people, flooding into Citi Field in New York to learn about the dangers of the Internet from the most respected rabbis in the world. Everything seemed so natural. Outside the stadium, I was recognized by a man waving one of my articles about leaving the Internet. He was ecstatic upon seeing me. Many of the reasons why I chose to avoid the Internet align with the teachings of his religion: we should be cautious of this modern world.

One of the rabbis at the gathering said: It's changing our relationships, our emotions, and our feelings. It's destroying our patience, turning children into plant-like beings who only know how to click a mouse.

My new friend outside the stadium encouraged me to take full advantage of this year, to stop and smell the roses.

This year will be full of surprises.

I once had a dream.

Let me tell you, everything started smoothly. I did stop and smell the roses. My life was filled with unexpected surprises: face-to-face meetings in real life, Frisbee games, bike trips, and appreciating Greek literature. I don’t know how I did it, but I finished writing half of my novel and submitted an article to Verge every week. For the first few months, my boss was a little frustrated with the quantity of my writing, but during that time, I wrote more words than ever before, and after that, I never wrote so many words again.

I lost 15 pounds without much effort and bought some new clothes. People kept telling me how refreshed and happy I looked. Once, my therapist was even a bit proud.

I was a little bored and lonely, but I found a strange rhythm in life. In August, I wrote: Because of boredom and lack of motivation, I was able to do things I truly cared about, like writing and spending time with friends. I was very confident that I had figured it all out and shared my discoveries with as many people as possible.

My mind was no longer scattered, and my attention span increased. In the first one or two months of the year, reading 10 pages of The Odyssey was tough, but now I could sit there and read 100 pages, or hundreds if the content wasn’t hard and I was interested.

I learned not to appreciate an idea in the form of a blog comment, but to write them as novel-length insights. Stepping out of the echo chamber of online culture, I found my thoughts extending in different directions. This made me feel different, even a little strange, but I liked it.

Without the protection of a smartphone, I had to step out of my shell and talk to others in complex social situations. Without the constant distraction of a smartphone, I realized I was more aware of others' presence. I couldn't interact on Twitter anymore; now I had to find that interaction in real life. In the past, my sister was always annoyed when she talked to me because I was always on the computer while listening to her, but now she likes the way I talk to her. She says I’m less absent-minded and more concerned about her happiness—at least, I’m less of an asshole.

Beyond that, I don't know what else it has to do with, but I cried while watching Les Miserables.

In those first few months, my hypothesis seemed correct. The Internet prevented me from discovering the real me, a better Paul. But I had unplugged, and found hope.

Back to reality

When I left the Internet, I hoped this year's journey would start like this: Today I used a paper map, it's so funny! Or paper books? What are paper books?? Or who has an offline version of Wikipedia? Let me borrow it. But it didn't happen that way.

In many ways, the practical aspects of this year passed unnoticed. In New York, I could easily navigate by feeling, buying paper maps to go elsewhere. It turns out that paper books are really great. When buying plane tickets, I no longer compare prices, just call Delta, and buy whatever they sell.

In fact, whether or not there is an Internet, many things I've learned can be realized—you don’t necessarily have to give up the Internet for a whole year to realize how your sister feels.

But one thing changed significantly, and that was traditional mail. This year, I had a postal mailbox, and I can't describe how happy I was when I saw it stuffed with letters from readers. These letters were so tangible, something electronic cards could never give me.

A girl wrote on a piece of stationery in neat and cute handwriting: Thank you for leaving the Internet. This is not an insult, but praise. That letter meant the whole world to me.

But later, I felt guilty because I never replied.

After that, somehow, even going to the post office sounded like a chore. I began to fear and even loathe incoming letters.

As a result, dozens of letters per week overwhelmed me just as hundreds of emails per day did. And many aspects of my life became like that. Like when I had the Internet, it still took motivation to read a good book, and it took just as much courage to go out and hang out with friends as it did before I quit the Internet.

By the end of 2012, I had learned how to make wrong choices in a new way without the Internet. I abandoned the good habits of 'offline life' and developed the bad ones. I didn't convert my boredom and lack of motivation into learning and creativity, but rather passive consumption and avoidance of social interactions.

During this year, I didn't ride my bike often, and dust settled on my Frisbee. I rarely went out with friends for a long time. The couch became my favorite place, where I often propped my feet on the coffee table, played video games, and listened to audiobooks. I would pick some brainless games like Borderlands 2 or Skate 3, thumbing the controller to manipulate the game world while my mind wandered aimlessly thinking about the audiobook, or perhaps thinking about nothing at all.

The person who can't live without others

Without the Internet, moral choices didn't change. Getting used to practical things like maps and offline shopping wasn't difficult, and people were still happy to point you in the right direction. But without the Internet, finding someone definitely became harder. Sending emails is always easier than making phone calls, texting, WeChatting, or initiating video chats is always easier than visiting in person.

It's not that these obstacles can't be overcome; at first, I did overcome them, but not consistently.

It's hard to say exactly what changed me. I guess the ease I felt in the first few months was because I escaped the pressure of the Internet, and that freedom was real. But when I stopped consciously viewing my life as 'offline life,' everything became mundane, and my worst side began to emerge.

Sometimes I would stay home for days, my phone always off, unreachable by anyone. Sometimes my parents got tired of constantly doubting if I was still alive, so they sent my sister to check on me at my apartment. On the Internet, it's easy to let others know I'm still alive, I can communicate with colleagues anytime, and be part of society anytime.

People always mock the concept of 'Facebook friends' as being wrong and ridiculous, but let me tell you, Facebook friends are much better than having nothing.

For years, every week I would call my best friend who lived out of town, but this year he went to China, and I never spoke to him again. Because I failed to stick to our social plans, my best friend in New York disappeared into his busy work.

I couldn't keep up with the pace of life.

Ironically, this March, I attended a conference in New York called 'Theorizing the Web.' It was full of graduate students presenting papers about defining reality or feminism in the post-digital era, etc. At first, I was smug because I thought they were just studying theories that assumed the Internet was everywhere, while I was experiencing life without it.

Then I chatted with Nathan Jurgenson, the network theorist who organized the conference, and he pointed out that there is much reality in the virtual world, and much illusion in real life. When we use phones or computers, we are still flesh-and-blood human beings occupying time and space; when we play somewhere, our devices are far away, but the Internet still affects our thinking: Should I post this on Weibo when I get back?

My plan was to leave the Internet, find the real Paul, and touch the real world, but the real Paul and the real world were irreparably intertwined with the Internet. It’s not that my life didn't change without the Internet, but that my life wasn't real.

Family Time

A few weeks ago, I met my brother in Colorado, then he deployed to Qatar with the U.S. Air Force. He has another little angel, Kacia, who is five months old, and I’ve only seen her picture in the letters my sister-in-law sent me.

I spent a day with my brother, and the next morning, we went to the airport together. I was stunned when he kissed goodbye to his wife and children. It seemed unfair that he had to leave; to his children, he is a hero, and I hate not seeing him for six months, just like the kids do.

My colleagues Jordan and Stephen met me in Colorado, and we were about to start a road trip to New York. The purpose of this trip was to wrap up my year with a short documentary, spending these last few hours in the car accepting what has happened and what is coming.

Before leaving, I spent more time with the kids, helping my sister-in-law as much as possible, being a good uncle. Then I had to leave.

On the road, Jordan and Stephen asked me some personal questions. Do you think you're too cruel to yourself? Yes. Was this year successful? No. What do you want to do when you return online? I want to help others.

We stopped in Huntington, West Virginia, because I wanted to meet my hero, Justin McElroy, the editor-in-chief of Polygon. I had previously met Nathan Jurgenson in Washington D.C., and I seriously reflected on whether I could succeed online where I failed in real life. I sought some advice from him.

What I do know is that my problems cannot be blamed on the Internet or any adverse conditions. My top priorities haven't changed before and after leaving the Internet: family, friends, work, and learning. I can't guarantee that I'll stick to them when I return to the Internet—frankly, I probably won't. But at least I'll know it's not the Internet's fault. I'll know who is responsible and who can solve these issues.

On the last night of the journey, late Tuesday night, we captured a beautiful moment of the Manhattan skyline from this side of the river flowing from New Jersey to New York (Translator's note: The original "shot" refers to Jordan's critical shot in the 1998 playoffs that turned the game around, and "shot" also means taking a photo). That night was cold and clear, and I leaned against the wobbly railing by the shore, trying to pose casually for the camera. I was so close to New York, so close to completing this year. I longed to return to my comfortable apartment, but I also feared being isolated.

Two weeks later, I will return to the Internet. I feel like a failure. I feel like I've surrendered again. But I know the Internet is where I belong.

I've read enough blog comments, magazine articles, and books about how the Internet makes us lonely or stupid, or both lonely and stupid, and I started to believe them. I wanted to figure out what the Internet had done to me so I could fight back. But the Internet isn't an individual pursuit; it's a product of our interactions with each other, wherever there are people, there is the Internet.

On my last afternoon in Colorado, I sat with my 5-year-old niece Keziah, trying to explain what the Internet is. She has never heard the word "Internet," but she looks tall when she appears on Skype with her grandparents. I asked her if she wondered why I hadn't Skyped with her this year, and she answered yes.

"I thought you didn't want to," she said.

Tears welled up in my eyes. I drew a picture of what the Internet looks like for her, showing computers, phones, and telephones connected by short lines, which are the Internet. I told her which one was my computer and drew a line connecting to it, then erased the line.

I told her: "I spent a year without the Internet, but now I'm back, and I can Skype with you again."

When I return to the Internet, I may not use it well, I may waste time, or get distracted by other things, or just click on the wrong links. I won't have much time to read, reflect, or create the great American science fiction novel.

But at least, I will be connected.
