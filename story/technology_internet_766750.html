Cook: Apple is the BMW of consumer electronics

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 20:50:52

On September 22, Apple CEO Tim Cook recently gave an exclusive interview to Bloomberg Businessweek. Following the release of a summary of the conversation, Businessweek then disclosed the full interview transcript, which contained a wealth of interesting information.

1. Greeting the Journalist

Cook conducted the interview in a meeting room adjacent to his office at Apple headquarters. After greeting the Businessweek reporter with a smile and a handshake, he immediately asked: "What did you think of Tuesday's event? Tell me what you thought about the new phone – did you get a chance to try it?"

2. Innovation in iOS 7

"Some people equate innovation with change, but we've never seen it that way – innovation is about making things better, and iOS 7 is a great example of this. It’s much better than iOS 6 or any earlier version of iOS."

3. New Products and User Experience

"We are a product company, so our products reflect our values – they represent innovation, attention to every detail, and highlight the importance of user experience."

Cook praised Touch ID fingerprint scanning technology as "a pretty cool security feature… but using it for purchasing will be an even better experience," and lavishly praised the iPhone 5C as having "perfect feel" with "every detail from the button to the plastic edge carefully designed."

"Most tech companies don't worry about these details – no one cares about buttons and build quality, no one really worries about user experience, but we care deeply about these things."

4. Why Make the iPhone 5C?

Cook stated that people want different products, and Apple also wants to make its phones more accessible without lowering the quality of its products. "We would say: 'Let's consider a great product we're currently working on.' If that great product can be sold at a lower price, then we'll sell it at a lower price."

Cook used the iPod player as an example, pointing out that iPods aimed at different users, meeting different needs, with prices ranging from $49 to $400, were all great products. He indicated that today's iPhones are doing something similar. "The iPhone 4 may have been discontinued, but it's still a great product, and the 4S remains highly attractive in many places where carrier subsidies are strong... We currently have three different phones for sale: the 4S, 5C, and 5S. They all provide wonderful experiences for a wide range of users, and we’re proud of all of them."

"We’ve never set out to sell low-cost phones; our primary goal is to sell great phones and provide great user experiences, and then figure out ways to lower the price."

5. Views on Today's Mobile Market

Cook believes that the "duopoly" between iOS and Android is becoming increasingly fierce, but "Android fragmentation has become unprecedentedly severe, leading to a significant gap between Android and iOS in terms of user satisfaction and usage rates."

"We’ve never pursued selling the most units; we pursue selling the best products, creating the best experiences, and ensuring user satisfaction – which usually means more usage."

"Many people think the smartphone market has become too large and worry it can't continue to grow, but it will continue to grow rapidly... The tablet market in some ways is very similar to the smartphone market, but there are more manufacturers – all PC manufacturers believe they need to produce tablets to defend their market position, and all smartphone manufacturers see tablets as ‘icing on the cake’... But more manufacturers doesn’t mean more good products."

Cook criticized Android tablets as "devices that might leave users uncertain whether they want another tablet after use," but noted that tablets are accelerating their dominance over PCs. "To remain relevant in the PC market, you must show greater differentiation and give people new reasons to buy PCs."

6. On the Rise of Android

"Android isn't a single entity; Amazon, Samsung, and others do things quite differently, and the significance of Android's rise is often exaggerated – in fact, iOS 6 users outnumber any version of Android users, and iOS 7 will soon become the most popular mobile operating system. What's the point of just looking at market share and unrelated factors?"

"If no one uses it, what does market share matter? For us, what's important is that people use our products – we want our products to enrich people's lives rather than be left in drawers, so I focus heavily on usage rates. But we aren’t completely ignoring market share either; it's not a simple 'either-or' problem."

7. On Android Fragmentation

"This is an increasingly serious problem, and it's growing exponentially complex... Many people start using old versions of the operating system when they buy a phone, and by the time their contract expires and they replace the phone, that system could already be three or four years old – it's like my pocket phone still runs iOS 3, which I can hardly imagine."

Cook believes fragmentation creates many problems for Android developers and users, and he indicates that Android fragmentation presents opportunities for Apple.

8. On Changes in Apple's Stock Price

"I've never been swayed by stock prices, so I don't get overly excited when the price rises, nor do I lament when it falls. I've experienced too many ups and downs, and I've seen even bigger fluctuations."

Speaking about Apple's stock decline this year, Cook made a simple calculation: if Apple's stock price increases by 31% by the end of the year compared to the beginning (the same increase as in 2012), that would mean a $120 per share increase, multiplied by 900 million shares, resulting in a market value increase of over $100 billion. "How many companies can increase their market value by $100 billion in a year?"

Although Apple's stock price has fallen by 10% this year, Cook believes Apple hasn't encountered any major issues.

9. Reflections on the Fates of Nokia, Motorola, and BlackBerry

"They remind all businesspeople: you must continuously innovate, or you’ll stagnate. And innovation is deeply rooted in Apple's corporate culture."

Cook also believes that all companies are emulating Apple's strategy, such as integrating hardware and software, innovating services and products – "When Apple entered the smartphone market, Microsoft obviously hadn't thought of buying Nokia yet, and Google probably hadn't thought of buying Motorola either."

Cook also boasted about Apple's 64-bit A7 chip: "We started building our own chip team many years ago – the 64-bit chip is the result of our high vertical integration. Which of the other two companies (Google and Microsoft) is skilled at making chips?"

10. On the Low-End Smartphone Market

Cook believes that the segmentation of the smartphone and tablet markets is entirely normal, but he stated, "I don’t judge Apple's success based on market share... The consumer electronics market always has a lot of junk products, but we don't make junk products."

Cook said Apple doesn't want to make products that "parents buy just to pacify children who are nagging for a tablet," but instead hopes its products "can help children learn a lot and can change people's lives."

"I won't lose sleep over the low-end market because Apple isn't positioned there – fortunately, both the high-end and low-end markets are so large." Cook also mentioned Toyota and BMW, subtly implying that Apple is the BMW of consumer electronics, pointing out "BMW’s market share in the automotive industry is much smaller than Apple's in the tablet market."
