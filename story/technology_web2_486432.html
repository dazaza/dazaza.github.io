Comment啦: A socialized third-party comment system

by daodao on 2011-09-07 16:58:32

Comment啦 is a socialized third-party comment system, specifically providing comment hosting for netizens as well as related services for associating and integrating social network business cards. Users can embed a piece of JS code on their blog platforms to generate a complete guestbook system; unlike traditional independent internet comment systems, the advantage of Comment啦 is that it forms an extensive social network among isolated islands, allowing website owners to effectively increase user activity and revisit rates. Regular participants can centrally manage and track their comments, and can also use this as an opportunity or platform to understand the other internet social footprints behind the person who left the comment.

The concept of third-party comment hosting and social user behavior statistical analysis proposed by Disqus is something I've been advocating for four years. I firmly believed at the time that this would be a project with immense potential for success; the reason is simple, the idea of this product is sufficiently radical - it's not just a specific product, but rather a fundamental service construction proposal aimed at solving the problem of isolated internet user behaviors through the point of comments and messages.

What intrigues me is that, given our "persuasive" efforts and the unique background of the domestic entrepreneurial environment, Chinese entrepreneurs "should" have rushed in early; however, replicas of Disqus were nowhere to be seen domestically over the past few years, yet suddenly appeared en masse in the last half-month (Baye.me, Youyan, Comment啦, etc., all surfaced during this period). At this point, the only plausible explanation for my confusion might come down to this news.

Is there still an opportunity to enter this field now? How big is the market in China? Based on my personal observations, I am quite optimistic. In fact, I believe they may face even more mature and advantageous opportunities than those who entered a year earlier. This is mainly due to the gradual formation and standardization of domestic related open platforms and social relationship organizations, which effectively avoid many unnecessary troubles in the early stages. The future of Disqus-like products is still worth speculating about; the only hurdle they need to overcome is how to grow from 0 installations to 10,000, and then from 10,000 to 100,000. This isn't just about the product itself, but also involves various aspects of operations. On this issue, I think two domestic teams are worth deep engagement and learning: bShare and Wumii.
