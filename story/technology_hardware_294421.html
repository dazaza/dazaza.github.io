How should stock investors view these central state-owned enterprises (SOEs) that are heavily involved in derivatives? And how should they deal with listed central SOEs that engage in derivatives?

I. The Mission of Central SOEs

Central SOEs are supposed to shoulder the mission of national strategic economy rather than pursue high profits. Why haven't the high-ranking officials of these SOEs noticed this? Or do they understand it themselves but have their own difficulties? What are these difficulties?

II. Unclear Rewards and Punishments

The high salaries, high benefits, and high bonuses of the heads of central SOEs make us ordinary people drool. All can be forgiven when performance is good, but it doesn't make sense when negative performance still results in high salaries and high bonuses. When there are achievements, someone comes forward to claim rewards, but when problems arise, responsibilities become unclear! So, how were those bonuses distributed in the first place? Whoever benefits should bear the responsibility. Isn't this a simple matter that central SOEs can't figure out?

III. Lack of Supervision

From the coal explosion in Heilongjiang that occurred a week after the safety supervision department left, we can see that supervision in some areas and departments of current society has become virtually non-existent. The floating losses of 68 central SOEs are not accidental. The Zhuzhou Zinc Futures incident in 1997, the China Aviation Oil Singapore incident in 2004, and the Liu Qibing National Reserve Copper incident in 2005 all demonstrate this. But where was the supervision? Shouldn't we also consider the inaction of some supervisory bodies in the future?

IV. Habitual Losses

Almost all we hear about are the losses of central SOEs. Why are there so few profitable ones among so many central SOEs? Such coincidence, and for so many years, is hard to believe. But we hope it won't continue.

V. Hu Lu Seng's Affairs

Just take last year as an example, a person who couldn't make it in football suddenly became the CEO of a state-owned listed company. I'd rather die than believe he could run the company well. But he could become the CEO. Impressive, isn't it?

The process of buying and selling stocks is as follows: When buying or selling stocks, you must specify whether you're buying or selling, the name (or code) of the stock, the quantity, and the price. This order is only valid on the day it is placed. The content of the order includes the abbreviation (code) of the stock you want to trade, the quantity, and the price at which you wish to buy or sell. The abbreviation of the stock usually consists of three to four Chinese characters, and the code is six digits long. The code and abbreviation of the stock must match when placing an order. Also, there are certain rules regarding the quantity of stocks being traded: the quantity for buying stocks must be a multiple of 100, but the quantity for selling does not need to be.

There are four ways to place an order: counter submission, telephone automatic order, computer automatic order, and remote terminal order.
