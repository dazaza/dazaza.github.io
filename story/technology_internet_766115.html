Nokia's Grand Finale: Divergent Mindsets of Employees on Both Sides Face Four Major Challenges

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 15:53:21

When Stephen Elop, a current Microsoft executive, took over as Nokia's CEO three years ago, the Canadian was expected to lead the fading Finnish legend in a counterattack in the smartphone market. However, it now seems that Nokia's alliance with Microsoft and Elop's appointment merely marked the beginning of the former mobile giant's descent into despair on its "burning platform."

On the morning of September 3rd, Microsoft and Nokia jointly announced a shocking industry message: Microsoft would acquire Nokia's phone business department for $7.17 billion. The transaction is expected to be completed in the first quarter of next year. This means that Nokia has lost its once most dazzling pearl, leaving only maps and network communication businesses as non-core operations. The former mobile giant has disappeared.

This is undoubtedly sad news for Nokia's employees and fans. About 32,000 Nokia employees will join Microsoft, including Elop himself. However, Elop will take on the role of Nokia's Executive Vice President, ultimately concluding his mission as Nokia's CEO in an inglorious manner.

Microsoft Employees: A Long-Overdue 'Wedding'

On Facebook, Microsoft employees' statuses of "OMG" (Oh My God) lined up neatly.

However, their surprise wasn't about Microsoft buying Nokia but rather the timing of the purchase.

A few American Microsoft employees stated in interviews that due to the exceptionally close cooperation between Nokia and Microsoft, and Nokia's increasing reliance on Microsoft, the merger of these two companies had long been seen as an inevitable wedding. What surprised the employees was that this marriage happened today.

According to insiders, many Microsoft employees believed the deal should have come later for two reasons: First, Steve Ballmer hadn't stepped down yet, and there would be significant changes ahead; Second, the price was too high.

Ballmer had just announced he would soon leave Microsoft. Within Microsoft, it was widely believed that the next successor after Ballmer would bring tremendous change to the company. How strong was this tone? There were even rumors suggesting that the current Nokia CEO, Stephen Elop, might become the next leader of Microsoft. Tencent Technology learned from a Microsoft insider that while it was a long road for Elop to succeed at Microsoft, it wasn't entirely impossible. Currently, there were no ideal candidates within Microsoft. Given Elop's background and his ambiguous stance towards Microsoft, this wasn't baseless talk.

From Microsoft's perspective, acquiring Nokia was a way to supplement and expand its mobile strategy. Microsoft has always claimed it wants to transform from a software company into a hardware and service-oriented one. A senior executive at Microsoft China believed that this acquisition demonstrated Microsoft's desire to strengthen its hardware and services.

An ex-Microsoft employee in the U.S. thought that Microsoft was weak in the consumer sector, and transitioning to mobile required support from the consumer level. With Nokia, Microsoft could gain access to some channels and users in developing countries, which would help advance Microsoft's mobile strategy.

Nokia's sale price of less than $7.2 billion was even lower than Xiaomi, a Chinese company that had just announced a valuation of over $10 billion. Nevertheless, Microsoft employees interviewed by Tencent Technology still believed Nokia was bought at too high a price. "Everyone knows Nokia was struggling, if we waited a bit longer, Microsoft could have acquired Nokia at a much lower price. Why buy now?" said a technical staff member from Microsoft's headquarters.

Nokia Employees: A Day of Mixed Feelings

The first day back to work after the Labor Day weekend in the U.S., Nokia employees learned that the company's mobile phone business had been sold to Microsoft.

Located in Sunnyvale, the heart of Silicon Valley, Nokia's U.S. headquarters is next to another Apple office building; just a ten-minute drive north lies Microsoft's headquarters in Mountain View.

"I'm very confused. I've poured all my energy into Nokia. Even though I know this might be good for Nokia, I'm still unhappy. I feel like a large part of Nokia has disappeared with this deal," said a technical engineer who has worked at Nokia for 14 years, echoing the sentiments of many employees.

An employee revealed that two-fifths of the five-story Nokia building's staff would be affected by this acquisition, including those in the mobile phone business and support departments, who will move to Microsoft's campus.

Those staying behind include employees from Nokia Maps and network communication equipment departments. An unnamed Chinese employee remarked that from a broader perspective, "this is a blessing in disguise." He explained that the office staff understood this could be a good thing for the declining Nokia, but "employees still have deep feelings for the mobile phone department."

This Chinese employee noted that Nokia's biggest challenge currently is its slow execution speed and sluggish response to smartphones, which led to missing out on great development opportunities and hindering the company's progress. As Microsoft is one of Nokia's major clients and has deep pockets, "we sincerely hope this acquisition can accelerate Nokia's progress."

Without the loss-making mobile phone business, the remaining Nokia includes the Here map service. This employee mentioned that besides mobile phones, Nokia's map department focuses heavily on car navigation systems. According to him, Nokia holds seventy percent of the car navigation market. "Without the consistently loss-making mobile phone business, I believe Nokia's overall revenue will increase instead."

Silicon Valley Observers: A Good Deal

People in Silicon Valley seem quite accustomed to such company handover news.

A white Nokia employee who has changed jobs several times due to acquisitions expressed that before joining Nokia, he worked at Japanese company Access; then moved to Palm, which was later acquired by HP, making him an HP employee. He said, "In Silicon Valley, such acquisitions are normal. I estimate I'll change companies every three to four years, but as long as I have a job, I'm happy!"

American tech writer and journalism professor at Arizona State University, Dan Gillmor, stated: "It's clear that Microsoft wants to enter the global hardware platform. Compared to Google's acquisition of Motorola, Microsoft's $7.17 billion price tag to enter the mobile hardware market is indeed quite cost-effective."

Even though Windows phones currently hold only 4.3% market share and Nokia holds just 1.2% in the smartphone market, Dan Gillmor still believes that the combination of these two companies offers a chance to break into the mobile phone market. "Microsoft has a lot of money and substantial cash flow, so they certainly have a chance to turn things around."

"For Nokia, aside from selling to Microsoft, there doesn't seem to be any other way to make a comeback," he said.

In a public statement, Microsoft outlined three reasons for acquiring Nokia: accelerating profitability and market share in mobile phones, creating the best Microsoft mobile phone experience for users, and preventing Google and Apple from dominating the market, ensuring Microsoft has a place in the smartphone market.

Dan Gillmor, who closely follows technology and media trends, believes Microsoft's reasons are very reasonable, but in reality, in the smartphone market, Google isn't Nokia's rival, "Samsung is! So I'm not saying this acquisition is definitely a good thing for Nokia or Microsoft, but I think anyone who insists that Microsoft and Nokia have no chance at all is crazy!"

The "Weak-Weak Alliance" Faces Four Major Challenges

Now, the most pressing issue is no longer the suspicious actions of Elop over the past three years, but how Microsoft will proceed with Nokia in hand. The acquisition is just the beginning; whether it's brand and personnel management or strategy and direction decisions, Microsoft will face more challenges in the future.

Steve Ballmer recently announced his retirement within the next 12 months, making the new Microsoft CEO the first major challenge after acquiring Nokia's mobile phone business.

On the previous Friday, Microsoft just announced that Ballmer decided to retire within the next 12 months, with the successor yet to be determined. This means that during the critical process of acquiring Nokia, Ballmer could step down at any time, and how the new CEO participates in and positions the Nokia acquisition case remains unknown.

In the past few years, Microsoft not only missed the new opportunities brought by mobile internet but also suffered defeats against Apple and Google. Even the newly launched Win8 system did not receive the widespread welcome that Win7 enjoyed. Ballmer's proposed solution for this giant ship was to transition into a "service and device provider," and acquiring Nokia was a product of this thinking.

If the new CEO does not agree with Ballmer's approach, Nokia's value to Microsoft will significantly diminish. Although some voices suggest that the current Nokia CEO, Elop, may replace Ballmer to oversee the entire Microsoft, allowing Ballmer's ideas to continue to the fullest extent, this possibility is extremely slim. In the past three years, apart from facing accusations from outsiders labeling him as a "spy," Elop has not demonstrated any extraordinary talents.

Secondly, how Microsoft deals with Nokia's burden of 18,000 employees will be a tough task.

According to the latest data, Nokia's mobile phone business employs 32,000 people, of which 18,000 are directly involved in production, exceeding half of the total workforce. This highly Nordic-welfare-style personnel structure has been one of the main reasons dragging down Nokia in recent years. After Microsoft acquires Nokia, the retention or departure of this group of employees will become the hardest problem for Microsoft to solve.

In the telephone conference following the announcement of the Nokia acquisition, Microsoft CFO Amy Hood stated that Nokia's scale and opportunities do not lie in production. However, CEO Ballmer's statement was vague: "Many hardware manufacturers usually outsource production, but Nokia internalizes it. Nokia has relevant strategies for this, and they execute them well."

Evidently, from a financial perspective, these 18,000 burdens are difficult to resolve, but hastily laying off more than half of Nokia's employees would require facing social public opinion pressure. According to overseas media reports, just as Microsoft announced the acquisition of Nokia, Ballmer also announced an investment of over $250 million in a data center in Finland, aiming to alleviate the concerns of some Finnish citizens.

Thirdly, the decision regarding the survival of the Nokia brand will face a tough choice.

According to the agreement disclosed by both Microsoft and Nokia, after the acquisition is completed, the trademarks Asha and Lumia will be transferred to Microsoft. The Finnish company will continue to retain the intellectual property rights of the "Nokia" trademark, and Microsoft will only obtain the right to use the Nokia brand for ten years.

This poses a dilemma for Microsoft. Currently, Microsoft's Windows Phone market share is only 4%, and even under Microsoft's most optimistic estimates, it won't exceed 15% in five years. This means that even if the Windows Phone maintains a good development momentum after acquiring Nokia, it will lose the authorization of the Nokia brand just when it can truly compete with iOS and Android.

Neil Mawston, a market researcher from London, said that both Microsoft and Nokia brands have equally high recognition, so he suspects there will be a battle between the two, and this battle will ultimately decide which brand will become the hardware brand.

Finally, after Microsoft acquires Nokia's mobile phone business and personally participates in market competition, the attitudes of other OEM manufacturers will become ambiguous.

After launching its own branded tablet computer Surface, Microsoft caused dissatisfaction among many OEM partners. Acer Chairman Wang Zhentang publicly stated that this move brought a huge negative impact to the global PC industry. Other OEM manufacturers also reduced their cooperation with Microsoft. Most PC manufacturers have abandoned the Windows RT system that Microsoft placed high hopes on. Currently, Surface occupies a high share in WinRT devices, but due to its low popularity, WinRT is almost forgotten by the market.

Obviously, the same problem will reappear in the Windows Phone market. Currently, Nokia already occupies 80% of the Windows Phone market share. Now, this figure has turned into Microsoft occupying 80% of the WP system market share, making the already challenging task of attracting OEM partners to participate in the Windows Phone even more unclear.

A corresponding example is that after Google acquired Motorola, it intentionally reduced the release of Motorola products. Even Moto X, which once caught the market's attention, received far fewer resources from Google compared to the Nexus series. According to industry insiders, the reason Google neglected Motorola was to avoid angering other partner manufacturers.

Tencent Technology learned from a Win8 responsible person that Microsoft will fully and deeply strengthen its cooperation with Nokia. However, this will put Microsoft in the same predicament Google faced. Will Nokia transform from Microsoft's largest partner to its sole partner?

The above four challenges are only a small part of the numerous issues Microsoft needs to focus on. More challenges await Microsoft in the future.
