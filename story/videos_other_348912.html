But still said to his Chinese opponent across the table silently

by ztta801ay1 on 2010-04-20 14:07:23

Yesterday, we talked about the events of 1958. In that year, the Chinese People's Volunteers announced their withdrawal from Korea, ceasing hostilities with the Americans; the Chinese People's Liberation Army declared that they would no longer bombard the two islands of Kinmen and Matsu daily, but if there were any abnormal activities by American warships, they would fire at will; in sports, the United States led a campaign for "two Chinas", and the All-China Sports Federation announced its withdrawal from the International Olympic Committee and most international sports organizations. In short, that year, all of China's diplomatic affairs were targeted against American imperialism, and this situation did not change until 1971. In 1971, the event of "a small ball turning a big ball" occurred. On April 4, Zhuang Zedong, demonstrating an attitude of "friendship first," presented a gift to the "enemy," the 19-year-old long-haired American player Glenn Cowan, who mistakenly boarded the Chinese team bus during the World Table Tennis Championships in Japan, and reporters captured their photo when they got off the bus. Then Zhuang verbally expressed his hope that Cowan could visit China someday, and Cowan agreed. The US team then proposed visiting China after the table tennis championships, which was approved by the Chinese side, allowing them to stay in China for 8 days. In July, U.S. President Nixon's National Security Advisor Kissinger made a secret visit to Beijing. In February 1972, Nixon, known for his anti-communist stance, visited China. A few months later, the Chinese table tennis team returned to visit the United States.

For a long time, people have described the "ping-pong diplomacy" according to this cause-and-effect relationship because it is more logical and dramatic to tell the story this way. In a June issue of the American magazine Sports Illustrated, the story was retold, defined as a "state-scripted stage play." An American journalist interviewed Kissinger, who introduced that before the table tennis championships, both sides had already secretly contacted each other. Beijing sent an invitation to Washington, and Washington showed interest. During this period, the incident of the American table tennis team visiting China happened. According to Kissinger, the U.S. government did not entirely agree with the Chinese side's approach because "we didn't want to spark a debate about China within the United States, we wanted to be low-key." To confirm the beginning of "ping-pong diplomacy," the reporter asked him if he knew about the incident of an American player mistakenly boarding the Chinese team bus, and he said he didn't know. Regarding "ping-pong diplomacy," Kissinger said, "Only Mao could make the decision, only Zhou could plan it out."

In addition to the well-known story, Sports Illustrated also provided some interesting details. Before leaving Japan for Hong Kong, officials of the American delegation asked the U.S. Embassy in Japan's China observer whether they would be humiliated or threatened by the Chinese. That official replied: If the Chinese wanted to humiliate you, they wouldn't have invited you. Cowan wasn't just an ordinary hippie; he was basically a complete one. Before leaving Japan, he carefully considered and eventually flushed the drugs he brought from the United States down the toilet of the hotel. On the night before entering mainland China, he met a woman in a bar in Hong Kong and spent the night at her home. If it weren't for her setting the alarm clock, Cowan would never have been able to report back to the hotel at 5:30 in the morning. Due to concerns about Cowan's hippie appearance angering the Chinese government, the American delegation discussed until the last moment before deciding to take him across the Luohu Bridge into mainland China. When Cowan realized that the match he won in front of 18,000 spectators at the Capital Stadium was a gift from the Chinese side's "friendship first," he felt extremely ashamed but still quietly told his Chinese opponent across the table: "Damn it, no matter what, I beat you." During his time in China, Cowan was preoccupied with how to sell China's table tennis equipment to the United States. After returning to the United States, he indeed started doing business with the Double Happiness racket, but it wasn't successful. After returning to the United States from China, Cowan was diagnosed with "schizophrenia," and he was unable to appear in the U.S. team when the Chinese table tennis team revisited the United States in 1972. His health fluctuated, sometimes requiring hospitalization, and sometimes working a few jobs. At the age of 40, he married once, but it only lasted for two months. At his lowest point, he lost his apartment and could only live in his car. Even when homeless, he always kept the book he wrote, "The Book of Ping Pong," in his backpack. On April 6, 2004, 33 years and 2 days after mistakenly boarding the Chinese team bus, 52-year-old Cowan died of heart disease.
