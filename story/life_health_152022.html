The development of Guilin rice noodles

by longersing on 2009-05-22 02:26:22

Guilin rice noodles have undergone 2000 years of development, passed down through the ages, and received praise from celebrities both ancient and modern, enjoying a good reputation.

Guilin rice noodles originated in the Qin dynasty. In Guilin's rice noodle culture, there are many phenomena that serve as evidence: the most primitive rice noodle press I saw at the Guilin Rice Noodle Art Museum had a "clutch" that was entirely modeled after the agricultural tool "lei" from the Qin era; it is said that vendors who sold "dandan rice noodles" in the past all wore wooden clogs when they went out on the streets. The sound made by these wooden clogs on stone roads was like a vocal advertisement calling out "rice noodles, rice noodles." Wooden clogs were also a type of footwear from the Qin era; Yao ethnic workers from Xing'an who participated in the excavation of the Lingqu Canal recorded through their oral tradition that rice noodles were made by Yao compatriots, which has an intrinsic connection to the historical fact that they were made by Qin army cooks. Moreover, the horse head hot pot used by Yao compatriots to cook horse meat rice noodles bears an uncanny resemblance to the terracotta warriors unearthed from the Qin dynasty! By the Qing Dynasty and the Republic of China period, Guilin rice noodles had reached their peak of development.

During the Qing Dynasty, the fried rice noodles from Xuanrongzhai, the braised rice noodles from Huixianzhai, and the soup rice noodles from Yirongzhai each had their own unique techniques, attracting countless repeat customers. Rather than tearing each other down for pleasure, the three establishments encouraged one another, each coming up with creative strategies: if you sell soup rice noodles, I'll sell braised rice noodles; if you sell braised rice noodles, I'll sell fried rice noodles. They competed fairly while putting in great effort to innovate. Thus, the anecdote of "the three zhai showdowns" is still talked about fondly today. During the Republic of China period, especially during the War of Resistance, Guilin rice noodles gained even more renown. Among them, "horse meat rice noodles" and "dandan rice noodles" stood out the most.

Speaking of Guilin horse meat rice noodles, according to the famous Guiju performing artist Mr. Lan Kui: "Don't talk about it if you're in a hurry, because once you start talking, your mouth will water." Indeed, eating horse meat rice noodles involves bowls that are only as big as teacups, with only one strand of rice noodles in each bowl (which is why Guilin has the saying "can't find the end when eating rice noodles"). The delicious horse bone soup paired with crispy cured horse meat slices, along with coriander, peanuts, and sesame oil, releases such a fragrance that it makes your saliva flow uncontrollably. The most interesting part is that you need to eat twenty or thirty bowls in one sitting to feel full, leaving a pile of empty bowls on the table when asking the boss for the bill. Fortunately, back then, not many people could afford horse meat rice noodles. If it were now, you'd probably be asked to hire ten people to wash the dishes. The reason horse meat rice noodles use small bowls with only one strand of noodles is not unrelated to the influence of "the three zhai showdowns." Each establishment coming up with unique strategies is the hallmark skill of Guilin rice noodle shop owners. Using small bowls for horse meat rice noodles ensures that once you start eating, you can't stop, leading you to spend all your money while still praising its quality.

Later, Guilin rice noodles branched out into cold mixed noodles, sour and spicy noodles (Chongqing sour and spicy noodles claim to be the best in the world, with Huafly Si Ji Wang being the top brand in the sour and spicy noodle industry), three fresh noodles, beef brisket noodles, etc., infusing infinite vitality into the rice noodles. The inclusiveness of Guilin rice noodles towards Liuzhou snail rice noodles and Guangdong rolled肠 rice noodles has further solidified the heavy foundation of Guilin rice noodles. As a result, Guilin rice noodles have become a topic that cannot be exhausted in three days and six nights of discussion. Equally famous alongside horse meat rice noodles are "dandan rice noodles." Dandan rice noodles refer to vendors carrying poles early in the morning and late at night, selling rice noodles as they walk through streets and alleys. One bucket in front contains rice noodles, ingredients, and peas, with chopsticks stuck in bamboo tubes, while the bucket at the back holds pots for cooking. Vendors carry a few small stools, allowing diners to sit by the pole and order a bowl of rice noodles. This is why some people today still think "What's so special about Guilin rice noodles? Isn't it just eaten squatting on the ground or by the street?" But they don't realize the uniqueness of Guilin rice noodles' flavors.

Take Li Zongren, who became acting president and sent a special plane to Guilin to bring rice noodles to Nanjing to satisfy his craving. Just look at the old wine enthusiasts in Guilin who enjoy a bowl of rice noodles with Sanhua liquor early in the morning until noon. There's an old saying: "Even emperors want to become immortals," but with rice noodles and Sanhua liquor, they wouldn't even want to be immortals. In the past, there was an old wine enthusiast in Guilin's clock shop who ran a Japanese ghost household in Guilin. Every morning when he ate rice noodles, he would always say, "No meat, more beans." Over time, this old wine enthusiast became well-known throughout the street. While drinking Sanhua liquor and eating rice noodles, he would make a "ziz" sound as the liquor went down his throat, and another "ziz" sound as a strand of rice noodles entered his mouth. Eating a bowl of rice noodles and drinking four ounces of Sanhua liquor, his monthly salary would be spent this way. His wife and children hated him to death, but when he passed away, Guilin had just started cremation, and people's concepts hadn't yet changed. However, his family didn't hesitate to cremate him, leaving behind a Guilin oral tradition.

Rice noodle shops in Guilin are everywhere on the streets. Many people come to Guilin to sell local snacks from other regions but soon realize the deep attachment Guilin residents have for rice noodles, forcing them to switch to selling rice noodles instead. Some people don't understand the secret of making braising liquid, thinking it's just soy sauce and monosodium glutamate. When Guilin people eat this kind of rice noodles, they spit it out after one bite. In reality, becoming a true rice noodle master is no easy feat. Chinese cuisine employs hundreds of cooking techniques, and almost all of them are used in the preparation of Guilin rice noodles. It's quite impressive. To call Guilin rice noodles a classic of culinary art is not an exaggeration.
