Nanfang Daily News, reporter Ting Yang - 100,000 yuan may be "idle money" for some families, but for 12-year-old leukemia patient Li Rufen, it is a lifeline! He used the funds raised by his family to complete a bone marrow transplant surgery. However, he suffered adverse reactions post-surgery and urgently needed 100,000 yuan for follow-up treatment. Faced with the imminent loss of his son's precious life, in desperation, Hunan farmer Li Zhiwen sought help from Guangzhou citizens whom he had never met before. With tears streaming down his face, he wrote in crooked handwriting, "I am borrowing money from the citizens of Guangzhou," and promised to stay in Guangzhou to work and repay the debt.

Li Zhiwen and his wife are farmers from Guiyang County, Hunan Province. Their family income comes from growing flue-cured tobacco. They have an elder son who is 12 years old and a younger son who is 3. In late August last year, their sixth-grade elder son, Li Rufen, suddenly developed a fever and was subsequently diagnosed with acute non-lymphocytic leukemia. The medical journey pushed the entire family into dire straits. The family pooled together over 200,000 yuan from selling all their belongings, borrowed money from relatives and friends, and received some donations, barely managing to cover the cost of bringing Li Rufen to the Southern Hospital for a bone marrow transplant surgery.

On January 17th, using the bone marrow provided by his younger brother, young Rufen successfully underwent the bone marrow transplant surgery. Thirteen days after the operation, just as young Rufen was looking forward to being discharged and returning to school, he began experiencing a series of adverse reactions such as diarrhea and hematuria. The doctors suspected that he might have experienced postoperative rejection or infection, requiring around 100,000 yuan for further treatment, otherwise his life would be in danger. This amount was beyond Li Zhiwen's means, "We truly can't come up with even a single cent more!"

Li Zhiwen couldn't hide his grief, choking back sobs multiple times, unable to speak. On the other hand, young Rufen, who had endured a serious illness, acted like a little adult. On February 25th, his parents seriously discussed the situation with him. Li Zhiwen truthfully told his son, "Son, all the money is gone. Would you hate me?" The understanding young Rufen replied that he wouldn't, and voluntarily suggested being discharged to reduce the burden on the family. He said to his father, "Dad, leave some money to take me to see the sea." This child, who grew up in a Hunan mountain village, had never seen the sea, only hearing about its beauty from relatives in Maoming. He very much wanted to see it with his own eyes. He also specially apologized to his mother, saying that since he fell ill, "my personality has become a bit worse," asking her "not to blame me." These words didn't bring tears to young Rufen's eyes, but they made his parents cry uncontrollably.

Dr. Chunfu Li, head of the pediatric department at Southern Hospital, stated that given young Rufen's current condition, if he were discharged, he would likely die within seven or eight days. His postoperative adverse reactions were not yet determined to be due to infection or rejection, but neither was severe, falling between mild and moderate. "With money, there is hope," he said, indicating an 80% chance of cure if there were around 100,000 yuan available for subsequent treatment.

Yesterday, Li Zhiwen, who hadn't received much education, solemnly wrote on a whiteboard, "I am borrowing money from the citizens of Guangzhou." It was his first time in Guangzhou, and he earnestly appealed to the warm-hearted citizens of the city to lend him money. He had planned out his repayment strategy: staying in Guangzhou with his wife to work. "I have the strength; we grow more than 30 acres of land at home," he said. "It will only take a few years to pay off 100,000 yuan." If any company was willing to help him, he was ready to work for them for free for a few years, just needing enough money to satisfy basic living expenses.
