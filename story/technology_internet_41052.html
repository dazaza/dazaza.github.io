Wired 2008: Five Trends - Office Steps Aside for Free Software

by cacard on 2007-12-31 22:27:43

With the rapid spread of smartphones, the high-speed development of online advertising, and the quick growth of social websites, Apple, Google, and Facebook have become focal points in the industry since 2007. So, who will be the winners in 2008? Recently, the American magazine "Wired" has released five major technology trends for 2008 that are likely to answer this question.

Five Major Technology Trends for 2008:

1. Online Storage

Online storage was once not well-regarded by the industry but is now a hot market. Thanks to continuously declining storage costs and constantly increasing consumer demand, this market has developed rapidly. Small businesses and startups need to store their own and their users' digital assets, thus becoming increasingly reliant on massive storage. Meanwhile, to maintain market competitiveness, major internet companies like Yahoo have begun offering large amounts of storage space to users.

There are reports that Google will launch a free storage service, while Amazon's S3 service has already achieved great success. With the help of the S3 service, web service companies can lease storage space for their web applications. As these services roll out, the cost for startups will continue to decrease.

2. Commercial Applications of Social Websites

If 2007 saw the rapid spread of social websites, then the commercialization of social websites in 2008 will be an inevitable trend. Although MySpace and Facebook have monopolized the casual social networking sector, LinkedIn and other business-centered social websites, as well as productivity-focused web applications on casual sites, will quickly rise in 2008. Influenced by this, more developers will focus on creating business tools such as Rolodex and desktop calendars. Social website platforms like Google OpenSocial are expected to accelerate this process.

3. WiFi Systems on Airplanes

International air travel severely damages corporate productivity, but with the introduction of WiFi systems on airplanes by major U.S. airlines in 2008, this issue may be resolved. The main problem with this system is that the internet speed isn't as fast as wired access, and the fees might also be higher. However, regardless, users will be able to send and receive emails, use instant messaging, and other system services on airplanes. It's worth noting that if the U.S. Federal Communications Commission (FCC) allows VOIP services on airplanes, users will be able to complete daily work tasks while flying.

4. Microsoft Office Makes Way for Free Software

Five years ago, it was hard to imagine Microsoft Office becoming a loser. But with the rapid popularity of free web-based office software, the impossible has become possible. Currently, more and more users are starting to use free office software suites. Whether traditional desktop software like Sun’s OpenOffice.org or completely web-based applications like Google Docs, they all offer good choices for users. Although Google Docs still lacks some features, it remains the biggest change in the office software field since Microsoft launched Office, which is why it has the potential to become mainstream in 2008.

5. Mobile Advertising

Mobile advertising will hold a very important position in the future advertising market. Long seen favorably, mobile advertising is expected to see explosive growth in 2008, with both internet and wireless companies actively investing in this area. The development of mobile advertising has always faced two major challenges: first, how to display ads on small screens without annoying users; second, how to make ads more targeted and closely associated with users. As more people start using internet-enabled new phones and targeting ad technologies develop, these issues are expected to be resolved. (Moore)
