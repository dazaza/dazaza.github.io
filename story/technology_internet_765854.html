Even if Ballmer steps down, what can Microsoft do?

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:17:16

Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer has decided to retire within a year. Microsoft has formed a special committee including Bill Gates to coordinate the work before his retirement and select a successor both inside and outside the company. At the beginning of the year, all parties had predicted that Ballmer would face being kicked out. Because one can list dozens of places where Microsoft hasn't done well enough, especially missing the mobile internet. Everyone believes that mobile internet represents the future, and Microsoft is facing the crisis of being marginalized.

In recent years, it has been difficult for Microsoft to have an inspiring piece of news. Whether it's WIN8, OFFICE, Windows Phone, Surface, Bing or Xbox, none of them have proven to the world that Microsoft can restore its former glory. Ballmer's retirement does not mean that the successor will be able to turn things around, but Microsoft's stock price rose by 7% on the day, which became a way for people to vent their disappointment with Microsoft. People hope that Microsoft will start to truly transform from here, just as Ballmer mentioned "transitioning to a device and service company".

The direction of the captain is extremely important before the Titanic collides with the iceberg at high speed. Ballmer has done a lot for Microsoft, and the company's performance was good during the 13 years he was in charge. But he didn't change direction in time. When Microsoft had the best chance to transition to mobile internet, they missed the opportunity. Around 2000, Microsoft also failed to successfully transition to the internet, being overtaken by companies like Google, Facebook, and Amazon.

But is this really Ballmer's fault?

Who should be blamed for the Titanic hitting the iceberg? It's not because the lookout discovered it too late, nor is it because the captain failed to steer the ship, but because it was traveling at excessive speed. Similarly, Microsoft's failure to grasp the wave of mobile internet was not due to Ballmer's decision-making errors regarding one product or several products, but was determined by Microsoft's genes, mindset, and mode of operation.

Taking Microsoft's main business, the operating system, as an example, before iOS and Android, Microsoft had already launched systems like Windows CE that supported embedded devices. However, the model was still "selling operating system software." The root of the success of iOS and Android devices lies in "horizontal integration." iOS built a mobile application ecosystem through App Store; Android relied on open-source and free + attracting device manufacturers + building the ARM camp to form a kingdom that rivals iOS.

In other words, the defeat of Microsoft's mobile operating system was not a product failure, but a failure of the industrial model. The thinking of Apple and Google is something Microsoft could not easily transition to. Now, Microsoft's transformation to "devices + services" is too late.

Microsoft has never realized and will never realize "a computer on every desk." But it is undoubtedly the main driving force behind the popularization of computers. Microsoft represented an era and led a certain model. The product of this model is the "WIN-TEL" camp. Microsoft makes systems and basic software, while Intel is responsible for core hardware. Both are at the center of the entire computer industry.

On May 2, Intel also changed its leader. At that time, CEO Paul Otellini retired early, and Brian Krzanich took over under duress. The problem Intel faces is also transformation, catching up with the missed wave of mobile internet. There are currently no signs indicating that Intel can defeat the ARM camp and reclaim the throne in the chip market.

For decades, Microsoft and Intel have worked hand in hand. As described by the "Andy-Bill" law, "What Andy creates, Bill takes away." Andy's Intel improves performance, and Microsoft's software upgrades to consume these increased performances, making people have the need to constantly upgrade their computer configurations. Now, the WIN-TEL era has collapsed, and the "software model" has become ineffective. Activation codes, verification codes, and cracked software will soon become historical terms.

People will no longer install software but use some kind of service, which can be an App, a Web network, or a public account on WeChat. What comes after the mobile wave? Software as a service, platform as a service, device as a service, everything is a service. The idea of cloud-based operating systems represented by Chrome OS will completely change the way we use computers and phones.

But Microsoft seemed unable to accept this trend earlier, insisting on selling software by the portion. Regardless of the latest operating system or office software, or WINDOWS PHONE, we use the Android system, and phone manufacturers also need to pay Microsoft patent fees "by the portion." "Charging by the portion" is the thinking mode ingrained in Microsoft's bones.

How stubborn and important is the founding gene of an enterprise? The rise and fall of Apple is the best explanation. The closed Apple couldn't compete with the open Win-Tel camp, Jobs was squeezed out of Apple because his products couldn't open the market, but the successors John Sculley and Gil Amelio led Apple to even worse conditions, forcing them to invite Jobs back. After Jobs returned to Apple, a series of great decisions such as iPod, Opening iTunes to PCs, physical retail stores, iPhone, App Store, and iPad achieved today's Apple. Notably, Jobs' series of decisions did not deviate from Apple's gene: an independent ecosystem integrating hardware, software, and content based on devices. The core is product-first perfectionism. What Jobs did upon returning to Apple, on the surface, was to awaken the genes and capabilities that Apple itself possessed, allowing the stagnant Apple to continue moving forward; fundamentally, however, Apple's hard-software integration model happened to fit the rhythm of industrial changes.

A hypothesis is whether Bill Gates can save Microsoft? The answer is no. Irrespective of Bill Gates leaving the front line of Microsoft for many years, Microsoft's current state is determined by the "software gene" set when Bill Gates founded Microsoft. For decades, what Microsoft has never changed is the characteristic of being a "software company." The "charging by the portion" model has been very effective, making Bill Gates the richest man in the world. But this model is facing obsolescence, being replaced by a superior one.

What Microsoft needs to do now is to inject new genes and blood to respond to current trends. This is extraordinarily difficult. There have already been many lists of candidates for Microsoft's CEO, and I don't care about the results. If Microsoft is the Titanic, it may have already hit the iceberg. Appointing a new captain at this time can reduce losses, but it cannot reverse the situation. Ultimately, it cannot escape the fate of being marginalized, just like IBM's position after being disrupted by Microsoft and Apple, or like Yahoo's gradual decline despite multiple leadership changes.

When reality is dull like stagnant water, any ripple will spark people's expectations. The rise in Microsoft's stock price was not due to Ballmer's departure, but because "Microsoft finally made some changes." Despite the fact that these changes won't alter Microsoft much.
