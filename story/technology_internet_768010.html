Cook: Never Thought of Positioning iPhone 5c as a Cheap Phone

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 16:49:03

Apple CEO Tim Cook stated today that the company never intended to position the iPhone 5c as a "cheap" phone. Instead, they preferred to bestow this "honor" upon the iPhone 4s.

Cook mentioned during Monday's earnings call with analysts that offering the multi-colored iPhone 5c as an affordable option for new market consumers "was obviously never part of our plan." "Our entry-level iPhone is the iPhone 4s," he said. Before the launch of the iPhone 5s and 5c last month, there were multiple rumors suggesting that the 5c would be Apple's way to attract customers in emerging markets where consumers might not afford to spend hundreds of dollars on a phone. At $99 starting price, the iPhone 5c is indeed cheaper than the flagship iPhone 5s, but its role was merely to replace the iPhone 5 in Apple's lineup, providing more color options for those who wanted something beyond black and white.

This resulted in discussions about whether Apple truly understood what "cheap" really meant. Several reports after the new iPhone releases indicated that sales of the 5s outperformed the 5c. However, mobile analyst at PCMag, Sascha Segan, pointed out that this shouldn't come as too much of a surprise since most shoppers likely prefer the flagship version – especially one equipped with Touch ID fingerprint recognition and available in "gold."

Cook expressed today: "Sales of the iPhone 4 remain very strong, and as we start testing lower prices in different regions, we're seeing significant price elasticity," Apple now "expects and believes this will continue with the 4s."

He added that the iPhone 4s provides access to the entire Apple ecosystem at a lower price point. "We clearly understand that there is price elasticity in the market, and we will take appropriate actions accordingly."

Meanwhile, regarding the high-end iPhone 5s, Cook admitted that Apple is still dealing with "a large number of unfulfilled orders," but "we are very confident in our ability to continue expanding production."

When asked about potential shortages of Retina displays for the new iPad mini, Cook said, "It remains unclear whether our supply will meet demand for this quarter. We know how much supply we will get, but only after shipments begin will we know the actual demand, so we will monitor the situation closely."

However, Cook is confident that many consumers will be able to purchase the new iPad this year. "I think it will be an iPad Christmas, a very good holiday season."

Cook also discussed the free provision of iWork with new purchases of Macs and iPads, pointing out that "other companies charge $199" for similar software. Clearly, he was referring to Microsoft.

"But we really want it to be part of the user experience, so we decided to offer it for free. I think it’s another reason for everyone to buy a Mac," he said.

In the last fiscal quarter, Apple sold 4.6 million Macs, up from 3.8 million in the previous quarter, but down from 4.9 million in the same period last year.

In the fourth fiscal quarter, Apple also sold 33.8 million iPhones, up from 31.2 million in the previous quarter and 26.9 million in the same period last year. The company sold 14.1 million iPads, slightly down from 14.6 million in the previous quarter but up from 14 million a year ago.
