Path Growth Record: Once Rejected Google's Hundred Million Acquisition, Dare to Take Responsibility

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:34:08

Path co-founders Dave Morin (left) and Dustin Mierau (right)

American well-known private social network Path's co-founder and CEO, Dave Morin, once promised that Path would not embed any form of advertising. In the headquarters office located in downtown San Francisco, he said: "We believe that if we did, it would be hard for users to provide us with the privacy data we want."

Not Following the Usual Path

In today's startup world, the prevailing trend is "having users leads to having advertisements, which in turn leads to high valuations." However, Morin chose an unconventional development path for Path. In January 2010, Morin resigned from his high-paying job at Facebook to create the private mobile social network Path. Less than a month later, when Path only had 10,000 active users, Morin decisively rejected Google's $100 million acquisition offer. Recalling that time, Morin described it as the darkest period he spent at Path but dismissively stated: "At that time, we made such a decision because we didn't want to follow the crowd and continued to explore long-term development strategies."

During the subsequent period, facts seemed to prove that Morin's decision was wrong. Path's performance growth fell into stagnation, and market attention gradually decreased. Many Silicon Valley insiders wrote articles pointing out that Path was just a niche network and couldn't become a major player, and Morin thus became a representative figure of arrogance. Over the next two years, Path's user growth was slow, but the company managed to get through this most difficult period.

Surprising Developments Early This Year

Unexpected events occurred early this year. The number of Path users surged from just 5 million in January to over 20 million by the end of August. Investors also began paying attention to Path again and valued it at more than $300 million, more than twice Google's acquisition price.

Satisfying Basic User Needs

Path is a mobile application that allows users to share personal life information with close friends and family. Facebook and Twitter are filled with advertisements from advertisers and various media, as well as alumni whom users haven't contacted since graduating from middle school. However, Path provides users with a platform to share details of their lives with intimate people. If Facebook is compared to a noisy large gathering, then Path is a dinner party among close friends.

Initially, Path set a limit of 50 friends per user, which was later increased to 150. Path's Pixar-style buffering menu animation design has been widely praised by users, and its carefully designed emoticons are also very popular. Morin said that these features can show real emotions, feelings, or sarcasm, which are difficult to express through devices.

Billionaire and renowned venture capitalist John Doerr pointed out: "Path deserves admiration for its courage to create a new kind of personal social network." Ashton Kutcher, Path investor and famous actor, also said: "Path meets the basic need for people to communicate with close friends and family. Path is my default social website personally, and it is my safe haven for sharing life details with close friends."

New Features Drive Growth

In June 2012, Path had about 3 million users. Meanwhile, Instagram, a popular photo-sharing site created around the same time as Path, had nearly 30 million users. Later, Facebook acquired Instagram for $715 million. At that time, Path faced the dilemma of either pursuing scale or choosing failure.

In December last year, Morin and Path co-founder Dustin Mierau jointly decided to lower Path's threshold. They formed a partnership with Twitter and allowed users to share their Path profiles. In March this year, they added a private messaging feature, which greatly enhanced user engagement. In May, they launched a feature allowing users to invite friends to join Path via Twitter's direct message system and Google Gmail. Thanks to these series of moves, Path's registered user count reached 10 million in April and broke through 20 million by the end of August.

Moreover, Path's user activity level is also very high, with users running the Path app an average of 19 times a day. A typical active user spends an average of 20 minutes on Path daily, while spending 17 minutes on Facebook and only 3 minutes on Pinterest. Path has already raised a total of $41.2 million in funding and hopes to complete the next round of financing leveraging recent performance momentum. Some critics accuse Path of relying entirely on selling ads to support performance growth. Morin refuted this claim, stating that the majority of Path's growth is organic, with 60% coming from users inviting friends to join Path, 30% being completely organic growth, and only 5% from ad business.

India Journey Changed Life

Morin was born in Helena, the capital of Montana, USA, and was selected for the national youth Olympic ski team. He first experienced the technology wind of Silicon Valley in Apple's marketing department. In October 2006, former Facebook president Sean Parker invited Morin to join the company. Prior to this, Morin had been immersed in the excitement of founding his own company. Fortunately, Morin had already obtained a doctorate in internet research at that time. Morin was responsible for operating the developer platform at Facebook until a trip to India at the end of 2009 completely changed his life trajectory.

While attending the wedding of Aditya Agarwal, engineering vice president of cloud storage service provider Dropbox, and his wife Ruchi Sangvi in India, Morin accidentally read a report written by renowned technology industry analyst Mary Meeker, known as the "Queen of the Internet." Meeker predicted that by 2013, the number of global mobile devices would exceed desktop computers. Morin recalled: "At that time, I thought, 'Oh! My goodness! The technology I have now will be worthless in three years.' I have three years to create my own company so that it remains competitive when the era of mobile internet arrives." Thus, Morin left Facebook a month later to start his own company. All he needed was an idea.

During that time, Morin continuously discussed market trends and future company models with two good friends, Shawn Fanning, the founder of file-sharing service Napster, and Dustin Mierau, the founder of music software company Macster. One evening, the three discussed in Fanning's apartment in San Francisco how to make social networks a private communication platform rather than a noisy friend gathering. Consequently, the three agreed to start building a private social network.

Daring to Take Responsibility for Disputes

As a company focusing on user privacy, Path inevitably got involved in a series of embarrassing disputes. In February 2012, Path was accused of uploading users' contact lists to its servers to help the company find other friends of users on the network. Path paid a fine of $800,000 to the U.S. Federal Trade Commission (FTC) to settle this lawsuit. Other companies were doing similar things, but only Morin came forward to apologize and delete the uploaded information.

In April last year, Path also faced similar charges, as the company was accused of sending large amounts of spam to the friends of Path users in the UK market. Facebook soon after closed the function for Path users to find friends on Facebook. Although Facebook never explained this action, the spam incident was likely one of the main reasons, and Facebook increasingly viewed Path as a potential competitor. Morin pointed out: "We made a mistake at that time, and its influence indeed lasted for some time."

Challenges in Monetization

Another challenge facing Morin is how to make Path profitable. After firmly abandoning the path of making profits through advertisements, Path must rely on selling virtual goods like emoticon "stickers" and photo filters for profit. Although virtual goods bring substantial income annually to Japanese instant messaging software developer Line, the revenue rate of $0.48 per user can only generate $9.6 million in annual revenue for Path. Analyst Michael Pachter of the famous American investment firm Wedbush Securities pointed out that among the global 1.15 billion Facebook users, less than 2% of users have purchased virtual goods. Therefore, emoticon "stickers" alone cannot sustain Path's profit-making business.

Therefore, Morin plans to launch premium subscription services this quarter, allowing Path users to enjoy special content, functions, and storage services. This is the business model adopted by Evernote, the cloud note-taking application. Evernote has 65 million users, about 4% of whom choose to pay $5 monthly or $45 annually for additional service functions. However, an unfavorable point for Path is that Evernote and Dropbox have massive user bases, and most of their users still opt for free services. This is very disadvantageous for Path to offer paid services.

Path's Another Option is: Building an Application Platform. This is the experience Morin summarized from his work at Facebook. However, Path needs to have hundreds of millions of users similar to Facebook or Twitter as support. Morin pointed out: "Path still has a long way to go. And this road is often very bumpy."
