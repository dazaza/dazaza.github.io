The discovery that my stomach has stopped growling

by q57d818t86 on 2011-10-11 14:22:26

A Pilgrimage - A Visit to Wudang Mountain Last weekend, after many days of overcast rainy weather, the autumn sun finally came out, warm and very comfortable. We took a bus to Wudang Mountain, known as the "Number One Mountain Under Heaven," and the two days flew by in a flash. Once you arrive at Wudang Mountain, you truly understand the famous saying, "A mountain need not be high, as long as it has immortals, it will be mystical." To be honest, compared to other mountains I've climbed before, the scenery here isn't particularly outstanding. However, with the legend of Emperor Zhenwu's birth and ascension, along with its turtle and snake-shaped appearance, it adds an air of mystery. The distant history combined with modern packaging and promotion makes it seem even more worthy of a pilgrimage. Our tickets allowed for a second entry, and the ticket inspection process was quite modern, using fingerprint touch memorabilia. At the beginning, our tour guide told us: "Everyone must remember which finger you used today, because you must use the same finger tomorrow..." As we approached the gate, another group's guide was telling his team: "Everyone should use your right index finger when entering today, so do the same tomorrow..." When we started the ticket inspection process, the inspector told everyone: "Use your right thumb..." In the afternoon of the first day, we arrived at Tai Zi Slope. The steps here undoubtedly left the deepest impression on me during this trip. Walking up from the bottom, there were continuous steps, with flat sections in between. Reaching the top, the guide told us to look back. Wow, astonishingly, it looked like a flat road. Quite miraculous, isn't it? As someone who enjoys mathematics, I can't help but admire the greatness of our ancestors. (So I took a picture with my camera.) On the first day, we visited Tai Zi Slope and Nan Yan. In the evening, after dropping off our luggage at the hotel in town, we went to the street, all famished. We entered a fish barbecue shop. The town was quite lively, and the boss' business was booming, probably due to the large number of tourists in this season. After waiting for a while, a plate of boiled peanuts finally arrived, and soon it was empty. Then a dish of tofu skin mixed with chili peppers (probably stir-fried) came, and the hungry people began eating with big chopsticks... After finishing, we noticed that our stomachs no longer growled, so we started to marvel at the boss's astuteness, as customers wouldn't complain anymore... Early the next morning, we got up, had breakfast, and continued heading into the mountain... Around noon, we reached the highest peak - Jin Ding, which also holds symbolic significance.
