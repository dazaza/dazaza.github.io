The Alignment and Strategic Objectives of Domestic Kik-like Services

by xdash on 2011-09-23 11:32:28

The continuous increase in Tencent WeChat users (with the new feature of contact cloud synchronization) and the announcement this week by China Mobile and 360 for two products - FeiLiao and KouXin - seem to indicate a resurgence in popularity for Kik-like services domestically (for those unfamiliar with Kik, please refer to this link for more information).

Coincidentally, a colleague asked me today about my views on these recent "free, data-saving" products in China.

In my opinion, it's better not to speculate individually from each manufacturer's "point" perspective on why they launched such a product, but rather to take a broader approach. By analyzing their industry characteristics and team backgrounds, we can categorize them into different camps, thereby dissecting their strategic objectives. (Previously, I wrote an article called "The Seven Powers Battle in Cloud Storage", but the scale now far exceeds that of the original seven powers.)

1. Disruptors/Challengers

Abroad, Kik's sudden emergence is representative, while domestically, these are Kik-like copycat vertical small teams lacking support from large companies (including Xiaomi, which excels at playing with concepts, and its MiTalk service). They aim to disrupt the existing, operator-centered mobile point-to-point communication network using new technology (cross-platform + non-SMS channels + strong relationships via phone contacts + text/picture/voice chat), laying out a completely new communication network based on users' original strong relationships, bypassing operators.

This represents a disruptive innovation, one that conforms to the trends of the times and challenges old paradigms. Just like how Google redefined search engines and the iPhone reinvented phones, Kik was born with the goal of becoming a replacement for SMS. This will inevitably impact the old forces. If successful, it will be earth-shattering; if not, it could lead to self-destruction.

This is also why many small domestic teams choose to dive into the murky waters of instant messaging despite the presence of communications giants like Tencent. Growing quietly in the cracks, unfortunately, the rush of competitors has turned the original blue ocean into a red ocean, and even into a dead sea for small teams who couldn't keep up.

2. Defenders

For every attacker, there is a defender. Instead of passively waiting to die, watching as disruptors gradually take away advantages accumulated over a long period in the guise of conforming to trends, it's better to take active measures to control the situation.

Therefore, I believe that China Mobile's FeiLiao and China Unicom's WoContact reflect a sense of urgency in response to the aggressive challenge, adopting the attitude of "Rather than let you come up with something to disrupt me, I'll beat you to it, while maintaining good compatibility and complementarity with my original business."

Using an old saying, Kik is a territory, if you don't occupy it, the enemy will.

3. Consolidators/Pioneers

Those who already have a leading advantage and whose launch of such products can make up for deficiencies in their current product line and strengthen their own advantages, I collectively call consolidators.

Tencent's WeChat is the best example. The original IM system based on QQ accounts had its own limitations, while WeChat maintains another set of user account systems. It can connect with QQ accounts while establishing new strong relationship communication connections based on local phone contacts, fully utilizing the characteristics of mobile terminals, incorporating features like "finding friends within nearby range," thus greatly expanding the offline capabilities of the original QQ functions. It is also an important chess piece in Tencent's strategy to develop towards the future of mobile internet.

Another distant example is Facebook Messenger across the ocean. The Web-based Facebook provided users with stream dynamic flows and wall message walls as tools to maintain daily contact with friends, but could not fully meet users' needs for instant chatting or voice/video calls. After all, face-to-face interaction is the simplest, most direct, and human way for people to communicate, even through webcams and microphones, which is better than typing long strings of characters on a keyboard.

Compared to Google+ loudly promoting online chat as a killer feature, Facebook casually tossed out Messenger, enough to frustrate the former still in the user cultivation phase. Want to shake me? Little buddy, I've already got what you're selling.

Conclusion

After market validation (regardless of whether the results obtained from foreign markets fit the Chinese context), Kik represents a newly cultivable virgin land, so everyone is jumping in to play. As for who truly intends to make a name for themselves, who just dabbles, who plays wholeheartedly, and who just wants to ride the trend to get more money from investors, my personal view is that users only need such a contact network. Therefore, only those with the strength and resources to expand into the sole network encompassing the broadest user group can laugh all the way to the bank. Advantage accumulation, winner takes all. In China's internet world, the ecosystem is especially so.

Appendix: Some other domestic Kik-like products not mentioned in the text: DanZhi Chat, GeXin, SuLiao, YouNi, Vimi.
