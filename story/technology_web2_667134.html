Five people were arrested in a fight between Hong Kong and mainland tourists over the purchase of milk powder

by zsh0827347 on 2012-02-16 14:51:58

Nandu News - Reporter Fu Ke, Wang Rijiang reporting from Hong Kong - A small can of powdered milk has touched the hearts of many Chinese people. Yesterday morning, two groups of mainland travelers fought in Hong Kong just to buy a few cans of milk powder, which resulted in an alert for the Hong Kong police. These mainland customers who were upset over milk powder might have to stand at the defendant's bench for the first time in their lives.

The quarrel over queuing to buy milk powder led to five people being arrested. The fight over milk powder occurred at around 8:00 am yesterday when five customers had an argument while queuing to buy milk powder in a shop in Wan Chai, Hong Kong. This eventually led to a fight. The Hong Kong police subsequently arrested the five involved individuals.

A spokesperson for the Public Relations Department of the Hong Kong Police revealed to Nandu News that the fight involved two groups of people. One group consisted of two Hong Kong women and one mainland man, while the other group consisted of two mainland men. The spokesperson said that the two groups argued over queuing to buy milk powder, leading the two mainland men to lose their tempers and start the fight, which then evolved into a brawl involving all five people. During the fight, a 46-year-old mainland man was hit on the head with a metal can. When the police arrived, both groups claimed they were injured and were all sent to Tung Wah Hospital for treatment. The police then arrested the five people on charges of fighting in a public place.

Are these five men and women "water guests" (smugglers)? A spokesperson for the Hong Kong Police Public Relations Department said it is not yet clear. Whether these five people will go to court is also unknown as of now. As of the deadline, the five involved individuals were still detained at the Hong Kong police station for further investigation.

Shenzhen dismantles two milk powder smuggling dens

On the same day, Shenzhen Customs also announced that they caught smugglers who were shuttling between Shenzhen and Hong Kong for milk powder. In a crackdown operation on the afternoon of March 3, Shenzhen Customs destroyed two smuggling dens in the Zhongying Street area, arresting three smuggling suspects who were unloading goods. They also seized one smuggling vehicle, more than 2900 cans of illegally imported milk powder, as well as a large quantity of Hong Kong-version daily necessities such as laundry detergent and shampoo.

"Water guests," commonly known as "ants," are mainly employed by smuggling gangs and use the "ant moving" method at border crossings to engage in smuggling activities. This not only infringes on the interests of legitimate enterprises but also affects normal customs clearance environments, posing significant harm. They have been the focus of customs' close attention and crackdowns in recent years. A customs official introduced that in this case, the smuggling den was set up in a residential area not far from the periphery of Sha Tau Kok, making it difficult for outsiders to notice.

With the rapid economic development, the living standards of mainland residents have improved continuously, leading to an increasing demand for imported milk powder. Also, affected by some recent domestic milk powder safety issues, the demand for imported milk powder has further increased. Driven by profit, smugglers smuggle foreign-brand milk powder from the Hong Kong market through illegal channels like "water guests" to make profits.

Affected by this, there has been a temporary shortage of milk powder in the Hong Kong market, impacting the daily needs of Hong Kong residents. According to Hong Kong media reports, Hong Kong merchants once restricted the purchase quantities of some popular milk powder brands. According to customs, the recently seized smuggled milk powder entering the country illegally mostly consists of fixed brands, basically including Mead Johnson, Abbott, Friso, and other brands listed under limited-purchase categories by Hong Kong merchants.

Relevant officials from Shenzhen Customs disclosed that during the recently concluded five-month special operation against "water guest" smuggling, Shenzhen Customs conducted nearly 200 inspection actions, seizing a large batch of hot commodities such as milk powder, iPads, red wine, etc., with a case value exceeding 5 billion yuan. Over 20 "water guest" smuggling gangs were dismantled, and more than 2000 smuggling cases were investigated.

Nearly half of Hong Kong citizens complain about difficulties buying milk powder

The Hong Kong milk powder shortage is getting worse. A survey shows that nearly half of the respondents encountered difficulties buying milk powder. Additionally, the number of complaints received by the Consumer Council regarding infant milk powder has risen sharply this year.

A Hong Kong organization conducted a questionnaire survey last month, interviewing 185 Hong Kong citizens. Nearly half of the respondents faced difficulties purchasing milk powder, and over 60% believed that the milk powder shortage affected them. Seventy-seven percent even thought that the price of milk powder was unreasonable. More than half of the respondents believed that suppliers should establish a membership system allowing citizens to order directly from suppliers. Some also suggested setting up a quota system to prioritize milk powder purchases for Hong Kong citizens.

The organization stated that they hope the Hong Kong government can arrange monthly inspections to prevent shops from manipulating milk powder supplies and that law enforcement departments should crack down on water cargo guests.
