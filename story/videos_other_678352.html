When it comes to "military parade", today's friends naturally are not unfamiliar with it. For modern countries, military parades are both a way to declare national strength and enhance national cohesion, as well as an important means to expand national influence. The former Soviet Union and the United States have once had world-renowned military parades. In the history of the People's Republic of China, several previous grand military parades also continue to make the hearts of the Chinese people surge with emotion, recalling endlessly. And nearly six hundred years ago, in the Ming Dynasty, Emperor Yongle Zhu Di also had a world-shocking "grand military parade" - Zhu Di's Beijing Grand Military Parade.

Speaking of this military parade, we must mention the little-known "international situation" at the beginning of the Ming Dynasty. The Ming Dynasty was established by overthrowing the Mongol Yuan Dynasty, but at that time in the world, three of the "Four Great Khans" established by Genghis Khan still existed. In the second year of Hongwu (1369), Zhu Yuanzhang mobilized 400,000 troops, advancing northward in three routes, successively occupying the former Yuan capital and the Hexi Corridor, completely turning the far-fleeing Beiyuan court into a "nomadic tribe". It was also in the same year that the other "three great khans" formed a coalition army intending to move eastward for rescue, but when they reached the Onion Ridge, they received news that Feng Sheng's western route army of the Ming Dynasty had annihilated all the Yuan troops in the Hexi Corridor, so they immediately "ran away". Afterward, a large number of Mongolian and Hui officials who had served in the Yuan Dynasty fled to various Central Asian and Western Asian countries, quickly seizing key positions. These "foreign monks" desperately incited their respective monarchs to adopt a hostile attitude towards the Ming Dynasty. In 1370, Tamerlane, a powerful minister of the Western Chagatai Khanate, launched a coup, overthrowing the Western Chagatai Khanate and establishing the famous "Tamerlane Empire". Subsequently, he campaigned extensively, sweeping through Central Asia and Western Asia regions. Initially, Tamerlane took a respectful attitude towards the Ming Dynasty in the East, actively sending envoys to establish good relations, and recognizing China as the "superior country", paying tribute and declaring allegiance. However, the Ming Dynasty always maintained vigilance towards him, setting up defense zones in Hami, courting the Eastern Chagatai Khanate to the east of Tamerlane, and deploying heavy troops in the northwest under the leadership of Song Sheng, one of the founding heroes. After defeating the Ottoman Turks, Tamerlane began preparations for a long march to China and sent spies to Beijing. He detained the trade caravans and envoys sent by the Ming Dynasty to the Tamerlane Empire, and publicly humiliated the Chinese envoy Guo Ji, who had been detained for eight years, during the reception of Egyptian envoys. Subsequently, imitating Genghis Khan, he convened a "Mongol Congress" in Samarkand, raising the banner of "anti-Ming restoration of the Yuan Dynasty". However, the so-called "Mongol Congress" was attended mostly by nomadic tribes from Central Asia; representatives of the Mongol main body, such as the Tartars and Oirats, did not attend but instead sent envoys to Nanjing to pay respects to the Ming Dynasty and informed them of Tamerlane's intention to invade eastward. In the fourth year of Yongle, Tamerlane launched a large-scale eastern expedition, claiming to have a million troops, but actually numbering twenty thousand. At the same time, the Ming Dynasty, which had spread its eyes in the northwest, had already received the news and amassed heavy troops in Hexi and Hami, ready to face the battle. However, not long after, Tamerlane died on his way to the eastern expedition, and the war dissipated like smoke. Thereafter, the princes of the Tamerlane Empire fought for the throne, plunging the empire into civil war, making the so-called eastern expedition to China a dream of fools.

On the Ming side, after Zhu Di ascended the throne, he dispatched Zheng He to sail to the Western Seas to proclaim national prestige, while simultaneously sending envoys such as Chen Cheng to visit the Western Regions. Subsequently, Shah Rukh, the successor of the Tamerlane Empire, abandoned his anti-Chinese policy and sent envoys to establish friendly relations with the Ming Dynasty, restoring the "sovereign state" relationship of the Zhu Yuanzhang era. However, the "anti-Chinese forces" in Central Asian countries remained active. On another front, although Zheng He made five voyages to the Western Seas before the eighteenth year of Yongle, most of them returned upon reaching the Indian Ocean without entering the Red Sea or the Arab region. The reason lies in the fact that after Tamerlane's death, the Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt rose to power, controlling the Red Sea basin and strictly prohibiting Eastern fleets from entering, thus avoiding conflicts. Zhu Di's diplomatic actions were rewarded as Central Asian and Western Asian countries frequently dispatched envoys to China, formally recognizing the Ming Dynasty as the "Celestial Dynasty" after the Yuan Dynasty. However, Western countries still doubted the real strength of the Ming Dynasty. Therefore, choosing an appropriate opportunity under the premise of peaceful friendship to demonstrate the formidable strength of the Ming Dynasty to various countries and consolidate the "tribute relationship" between the Ming Dynasty and Central Asian and Western Asian countries became particularly important. And the military parade is precisely the best opportunity.

In the sixteenth year of Yongle (AD 1418), the Ming Dynasty sent Chen Cheng as an envoy to Central Asia. In January of the following year, the envoy arrived at Herat, the capital of the Tamerlane Empire. Subsequently, Central Asian and Western Asian countries all organized delegations, requesting to return with the Chinese delegation. In the seventh month of the eighteenth year of Yongle, this delegation, consisting of twenty Central and Western Asian countries including the Tamerlane Empire and the Egyptian Mamluk Sultanate, with a total of six hundred people, arrived at Jiayuguan Pass. Unexpectedly, Zhu Di dispatched six thousand elite cavalrymen to this place, subsequently escorting them along the Jiayuguan Pass through the nine frontier military towns of the Ming Dynasty for a "tour". As they passed through each Ming military town, the Ming Dynasty specially assigned Mongolian and Hui officers serving in the Ming army to fully receive them, introducing the Ming Dynasty's ethnic equality policy, dispelling the rumors spread by Central Asian "anti-Chinese factions" that "the Ming Dynasty mistreated Mongols and Huis". What surprised the various countries even more was that the number of soldiers in each "military district" of the Ming Nine Frontiers, after being secretly estimated by them, was equivalent to the entire force of a medium-sized country in Central Asia. And at the easternmost point of the journey, Beijing, a long-planned military parade was already prepared.

On November 17th of the eighteenth year of Yongle, the delegation arrived in Beijing, where troops were already gathered heavily. Coincidentally, this was exactly when Emperor Zhu Di was planning his third northern expedition against Mongolia, and a large number of troops were already gathered around Beijing. On November 20th, Zhu Di received the envoys of various countries in the Beijing Palace. All the envoys performed the kowtow ritual, except for the envoy of Tamerlane who bowed. When Chinese officials requested that he perform the kowtow, the envoy of Tamerlane replied, "Our country does not have such customs." He insisted on bowing. Zhu Di was not bothered by this. Of course, the envoy of Tamerlane had reasons to be so arrogant. The leader of this Tamerlane delegation was the prime minister of the Tamerlane Empire, Al-Dusha, and the vice-envoy was his trusted general, Gesuyeding, who had accompanied Tamerlane in many campaigns and was exempt from kneeling in the Tamerlane homeland. After the diplomatic etiquette, Zhu Di informed the envoys about the arrangements for their trip. They could first tour provinces such as Shandong, Henan, and Jiangsu, and were allowed free access to observe military installations without any "military taboos". Subsequently, these delegations were led by Ming officials to travel throughout China. Especially, the envoys from Tamerlane were arranged to visit the Confucius Mansion in Shandong and pay homage at the Confucius Temple. After two months of "observation visits," the various delegations received notification that Zhu Di would begin a large-scale "hunt" around Beijing, inviting the delegations to observe.

In March of the nineteenth year of Yongle, the "hunt" officially began outside Huailai City to the north of Beijing. A total of 100,000 troops from various units were mobilized, with the elite troops of the Ming Dynasty's "Five Armies Camp," "Three Thousand Camp," and "Divine Machine Camp" selected by Zhu Di conducting exercises and performances, showcasing maneuvers such as cavalry encirclement, infantry assaults, combined arms operations of infantry and cavalry, and Southern "Wolf Soldiers" and "White Rod Soldiers" summoned from Guangxi, Yunnan, and Sichuan, demonstrating massed crossbow volleys and spear infantry combat training in "military subjects." Historical records describe the scene as having "neat formations," "perfect synchronization," "bright armor," leaving the envoys from various countries astonished. Particularly impressive to the envoys were the firearms drills of the Ming "Divine Machine Camp" and the rapid assault hunting demonstrations by mounted musketeers, showcasing numerous "high-tech" weapons such as the "Tiger-Intimidating Cannon," "Fire Dragon Gun" (cavalry-dedicated musket), Annan gun (swivel gun), "One Nest of Bees" (rocket artillery), and "Fire Dragon Cart" (flamethrower), which were unheard of by the "foreigners" and left experts like Gesuyeding wide-eyed with admiration. The large-scale "hunt" lasted for an entire month. After the military parade, Zhu Di "warmly received" the envoys of various countries at his camp set up in Tumu Fortress (the place where the later Ming Yingzong was captured). This time, the envoys of Tamerlane led the way in kneeling and kowtowing, "touching the ground with their foreheads," disregarding the claim that "our country has no such custom." The envoy of Tamerlane flattered by saying that the famous horse presented by Tamerlane was the very horse that Tamerlane's father personally used during his campaigns, and giving it to Zhu Di was to express "the highest respect." Zhu Di was delighted upon hearing this and reciprocated with a "long gun" (musket) used by his son (second son Zhu Gaoxu) during the "difficult peace campaign," and ordered the envoy to "convey the sincerest gratitude" to the king of the Tamerlane Empire. The king of Tamerlane gave the horse his father rode during his lifetime to the Chinese emperor, and the Chinese emperor gave the musket used by his son to the king of Tamerlane, making this gift exchange between the rulers of the two strongest empires of the 15th century quite interesting.

This grand military parade undoubtedly achieved excellent results. The number of envoys attending this "ceremony" included delegations from Central-Western Asia, Southeast Asia, Indochina, and other countries, totaling 27 nations. The might of the Ming army caused a significant impact in various countries. Although Zhu Di passed away afterward, and starting from the Xuande period, the Ming Dynasty no longer sent envoys abroad, the friendly relations between Central-Western Asian countries and China continued until the end of the Ming Dynasty. Just the Tamerlane Empire alone sent envoys to China over sixty times throughout the Ming Dynasty. Details of this military parade were meticulously recorded in various classics of Central Asian countries. The deputy envoy of Tamerlane, Gesuyeding, frankly admitted in his memoirs about his views on the military strength of the Ming Dynasty: "I must admit that it was fortunate that the Great Emperor (Tamerlane) died on his way to the eastern expedition, which preserved his lifelong reputation." Another "chain reaction" brought about by this military parade was that after returning home, the envoy of the Egyptian Mamluk Sultanate lifted the ban on Eastern merchant ships in the Red Sea. From then on, Eastern silk, spices, and other goods could be transported more smoothly to the Red Sea area and enter Europe through this route. The last voyage to the Western Seas by Zheng He also finally reached the shores of the Red Sea, not only establishing good relations with the countries in the Red Sea region but also realizing his lifelong wish as a Muslim - the pilgrimage to Mecca.
