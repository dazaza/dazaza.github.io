Report: Google wants to develop its own self-driving car brand

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:07:28

According to reports on August 24th, Beijing time, Google has been developing self-driving car software for several powerful automakers. However, insiders revealed that Google is quietly developing its own branded self-driving car and plans to develop a self-driving taxi system called "ROBO TAXIS".

Foreign media reported that in the past few months, Google had discussions with parts suppliers about manufacturing new cars according to Google's technical specifications. The reason for this move is that Google could not reach an agreement with well-known automakers.

Google also has many new ideas on how to use these new cars. Apart from considering selling self-driving cars to individuals, Google hopes that its designed self-driving cars will become part of a self-driving taxi system to provide ride services to passengers who need them. It is currently unknown whether these services will be conducted through partnerships.

Negotiating with Manufacturers

Insiders disclosed that Google has negotiated with several automotive parts companies, including Continental AG and Magna International. Similar to how Foxconn helps Apple and other hardware companies produce phones and computers, these companies supply parts to large automakers and help assemble them.

This Thursday, German media Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung revealed that Google is close to reaching an agreement with Continental AG, one of the world's largest automotive parts companies. However, the report did not disclose more details about the self-driving car system.

Google’s actions indicate that self-driving cars may become another object of commodification in the hardware industry after computers and mobile devices.

The hardware of these devices can currently be manufactured by cost-effective suppliers, while the software and applications inside are becoming increasingly important.

Although Google lacks experience with relevant equipment or automobile manufacturing, it has accumulated some experience in automotive hardware. For example, Google is already developing LiDAR, which is used to detect the physical environment around the car and feed information back to Google's software for analysis.

Whether Google will continue to seek partners to produce its own branded cars remains unknown, but DIY has always been Google's style. In recent years, Google has designed its own brand of smartphones and tablets to encourage existing hardware developers to join its ecosystem.

Pressuring Car Manufacturers

An insider stated that Google wants to pressure well-known car manufacturers to adopt autonomous navigation technology. This source further mentioned that Google encourages car manufacturers to embed more self-driving functions.

Of course, car manufacturers have been researching self-driving car technology for many years, long before Google entered the field.

By designing and manufacturing its own branded cars, Google hopes to form direct opposition with existing car manufacturers. These brands have different attitudes towards autonomous navigation technology compared to Google.

A person familiar with Google's project said that Google believes most car manufacturers are unwilling to produce a truly fully autonomous self-driving car.

Daimler CEO Dieter Zetsche also expressed a similar view. At a conference in early summer, Zetsche reportedly wanted to automate annoying driving experiences, such as traffic jams, but would never automate the "cool part" of the driving experience.

For example, the 2014 Mercedes-Benz S-Class, expected to be available at the end of this year, can automatically accelerate or decelerate based on the movement of vehicles ahead, and its steering assist function ensures the car stays in the center of the lane.

Self-Driving Taxi System "ROBO TAXIS"

One of Google's ideas is that its branded cars could become part of a self-driving taxi system called "ROBO TAXIS." In this system, fleets of self-driving cars can pick up passengers and commuters on demand. Google believes such a system could potentially reduce people's need to buy cars and decrease traffic accidents.

Last year, Google considered American cities where this service could be launched. People familiar with the project said that to prevent emergencies, self-driving taxis might require passengers to sit in the driver's seat. Another person said that Google recently focused on finding a solution that benefits a wider audience.

How Google will ultimately implement its plan remains unclear.

"Moonshot Projects"

Assembling a self-driving taxi system or directly selling self-driving cars to consumers is a very complex matter. From an economic perspective, revenue will certainly be far less than Google's main business - online advertising.

Google's current small self-driving car, equipped with cameras, sensors, radar, and Google's special software (using Toyota cars), costs $150,000. Google is trying to lower costs by designing certain hardware itself.

Financial risks explain why this project was placed in the Google X laboratory. According to Page, Google X focuses on projects that are "moonshot projects" or high-risk projects with the potential for great returns.

Of course, political and administrative obstacles cannot be ignored.

Google is working hard to promote the legalization of self-driving cars in the U.S. It previously led legislative efforts in Nevada, California, Florida, and Washington to ensure legal compliance when testing self-driving cars on public roads.
