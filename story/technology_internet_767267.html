Twitter founder Dorsey was harshly criticized by a writer: accused of overstated boasting

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:12:02

Nick Bilton, a well-known columnist for The New York Times' Bits blog, recently published a new book titled "Hatching Twitter: A True Story of Money, Power, Friendship, and Betrayal." In this book, he claims that Jack Dorsey's status as the founder of Twitter has been exaggerated.

Bilton writes that Dorsey was often the person distracting the social media company rather than being a competent leader.

Bilton provides a nearly cruel depiction of the role Dorsey played in Twitter. He describes Dorsey as a nose-ringed NYU dropout who preferred to listen to punk music on his laptop in coffee shops and couldn't even secure a job at a shoe store.

The book starts from 2006 and points out that Dorsey was initially not interested in the company that would eventually create Twitter, instead having aspirations in the fashion industry:

"On the night of February 28, 2006, around 2 a.m., Dorsey sat in the car of Twitter co-founder Noah Glass while heavy rain pounded the windshield. After a night of drinking vodka and Red Bull, they were starting to sober up. As usual, their conversation revolved around Odeo (the company Dorsey and Glass were operating at the time, where Twitter was born). Dorsey, slurring his words, said he was preparing his exit strategy. 'I will leave the tech world and become a fashion designer.'

As a professional manager, Dorsey was an amateur, setting incorrect priorities for management tasks. Even after Twitter launched and grew rapidly, he still harbored dreams of designing fashion: "When Williams asked Dorsey to send an email to all employees to set Twitter's development goals, the title of his first draft was 'The Three Things I Want Twitter To Do To Be Successful,' with each goal starting unpleasantly with the word 'I.'

Dorsey frequently tried to project an image of having everything under control, pretending that all his actions were part of a larger plan. However, employees often found him wandering around South Park looking defeated. He also had a habit of leaving the company around 6 p.m. to attend drawing classes, hot yoga, and classes at a local fashion design institute (he wanted to learn how to design A-line skirts and jeans).

Once he had almost no social life, but later it became a distraction as venture capitalists frequently tempted him to watch San Francisco Giants baseball games and attend various parties across the city. Under Dorsey’s management, Twitter never underwent a complete update from its prototype and was plagued by severe infrastructure issues, often causing the site to crash for hours at a time.

Bilton writes that in 2008, Twitter ousted Dorsey, then serving as CEO, partly because of his obsession with fashion design:

"'You either become a tailor or be a good CEO for Twitter,' Twitter co-founder Evan Williams told Dorsey. 'But you can't do both.'

Upon being ousted, Dorsey received a portion of Twitter's stock, $200,000 in cash, and a promise that 'no one in the industry would know he had been fired.' This secret was concerning because Facebook was considering acquiring Twitter, and Dorsey was discussing this with Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg.

Clearly, this deal never happened, but Bilton writes that this secret allowed Dorsey to create a myth about his role in founding Twitter:

"After Dorsey was stripped of his power at Twitter, he began a media campaign claiming that his and Williams’ roles should be swapped. He started telling a carefully fabricated story about founding Twitter.

In dozens of interviews, Dorsey completely erased Glass' role in founding the company. Soon after, he changed his personal bio to 'inventor of Twitter' and began to exclude Williams and Twitter co-founder Biz Stone.

During one interview, Dorsey complained to Barbara Walters that he founded Twitter. The next day, Walters brought up this point when interviewing Stone and Williams on 'The View.'

Dorsey once told the Los Angeles Times: "In many ways, Twitter has always been my life's work." He denied Glass' contributions to the extraordinary achievements of Twitter.

Dorsey's story evolved over time. He later told Vanity Fair that the idea for Twitter could be traced back to 1984 when he was only 8 years old. The 60 Minutes program reported that Dorsey created Twitter because he was "obsessed with trains and maps" and the way cities worked. Later, he explained that he first fully proposed the idea of creating Twitter on a playground in South Park.

Throughout, Dorsey consistently portrayed himself in the image of Steve Jobs, calling himself an "editor" like Jobs and adopting the same "uniform": Dior shirts with white buttons, blue jeans, and black sportswear.

Bilton writes that Dorsey's media campaign was so successful that Williams even considered removing him from the Twitter board, despite Dorsey still holding an actual position at Twitter. But Williams worried that if Dorsey was completely expelled from the company, it would cause a major uproar in the media.

In the end, Dorsey re-entered Twitter's senior management. In 2011, he "returned" to Twitter and served as the company's executive chairman. Bilton states that Dorsey could make up to $500 million in Twitter's IPO:

"It remains unclear what responsibilities Dorsey can take. As one former Twitter employee once put it: 'The greatest product Jack Dorsey ever created is Jack Dorsey himself.'"
