If you take the civil service examination only for the sake of good treatment, then don't take it.

by alexsgxu on 2009-10-27 17:18:18

If you only covet the good treatment of civil servants, then do not take the exam.

Xu Shuguang, Chairman and President of GreenTree Hospitality Group

(Image source: Xinhua News Agency)

Yesterday was the first day of online registration for the 2010 National Civil Servant Examination. On the very first day, the registration process became highly intense. Within just 135 minutes of the start of registration, 22,000 people had already signed up through the internet. The competition ratio for the hottest position reached as high as 4723:1, whereas last year, at the end of the registration period, this figure was only 4584:1—quite a fiery situation!

Since the implementation of China's civil servant examination system in 1996, taking the civil servant exam has become a prevailing trend, one that has never ceased. Especially under the global financial crisis, this trend has only intensified. College students have also flocked to take the civil servant exam, with the number of college students registering each year continuously increasing. Is this phenomenon good or bad? Opinions vary, but ultimately criticism outweighs praise. What exactly makes college students so enthusiastic about becoming civil servants? And how many of them are actually motivated by the desire to better serve the public? This is indeed a profound and serious issue worth pondering. I remember during the "Two Sessions," a representative harshly criticized the strange phenomenon of civil servant fever, saying it does not create productivity and represents a regression in society.

A government official is like a parent to the people. How many college students know this before they register for the exam? I once saw a TV program about college student employment issues. One part that caught my attention was when it discussed how many college students now want to take the civil servant exam. When the host asked why they wanted to take the exam, their answers shocked me. I had hoped their purpose in becoming civil servants was to serve more people, develop themselves, and contribute more to society. On the contrary, their reason for taking the exam was because civil servants don’t have to do much work and receive good benefits.

This shocked me greatly. College students said they take the civil servant exam because the job is leisurely and offers good treatment; ordinary people say that becoming a civil servant is like getting a "lifetime job," making it easier to find a partner and settle down. Some even categorize the levels of success in civil service exams: passing into central state agencies earns you a "golden rice bowl," provincial or municipal levels earn you a "silver rice bowl," city-level positions are "copper rice bowls," and town or street-level positions are "iron rice bowls." It seems everyone is after the leisure and generous benefits of being a civil servant, which is quite tragic. However, if everyone becomes a civil servant, who will support the civil servants? If the initial motivation for taking the exam is to become a parasite, how can we not worry about government agencies and the pillars of our nation? No one wants to support a parasite or carry too much burden, as it’s unfair. So why treat others the same way, becoming someone who lives off others? If you think from another perspective, everything becomes clear.

Choices made when young are very important. Eating a little more bitterness when young is always beneficial. Between first enduring hardship and then enjoying sweetness versus first enjoying sweetness and then enduring hardship, no one would choose the latter. Being comfortable and unwilling to strive when young, only to regret it later in life, is too late. A strong youth leads to a strong China. If the youth of China strive hard only to secure an easy and stable job in the government, how can we expect the youth of China to shoulder the great task of reviving the Chinese nation? How can we achieve the self-standing of the Chinese nation among the world's nations? In today's era of deep international development, how can we compete with outstanding youths from all over the world? How can we avoid falling behind and being beaten again? These are all questions worth deep reflection for every responsible young person.

The civil servant fever also reflects another issue: Do people in our society really think civil servants are idle? We have many diligent civil servants and many excellent national cadres. I know many people who work tirelessly day and night for society, and we should publicize them more. At the same time, our government should further strengthen and improve administrative efficiency. I believe that only by allowing our civil servants to truly serve the people and make sacrifices that the people can feel, can we change the impression of civil servants in society, especially among college students. A gentleman loves wealth, but acquires it in a righteous way. The results obtained after genuine effort are called the right "path," and this path will become wider. Any other form is akin to robbery.

With so many people obsessed with taking the civil servant exam, it would be commendable if it were for the purpose of better serving the nation and its people. However, the question is, how many college students are driven by this ideal of serving the country and its people? Each of us should ask ourselves this question. If you only covet the good treatment, high welfare, and comfort, then do not take the exam! Because none of us want to see the state supporting another parasite, adding a heavy burden on the country and its people!
