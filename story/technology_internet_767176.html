No intention to return to Microsoft, Gates may take on more daily affairs in the future

by anonymous on 2013-10-10 18:28:12

Bill Gates and Steve Ballmer

Since Steve Ballmer (Steve Ballmer) announced that he will retire within the next year, what role Bill Gates (Bill Gates) will play in Microsoft's future has become the main topic of private discussions among employees.

Since 2008, when Gates officially announced his departure from Microsoft's daily operations to focus on the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation which he co-founded with his wife Melinda, his role at Microsoft has been a classic topic of public discussion. In recent years, as Microsoft continued to make mistakes, especially after Ballmer announced his resignation as CEO next year, this issue was discussed more and more.

Many employees and investors fondly recall Microsoft's heyday under Gates' leadership. Under the guidance of this tech giant, Microsoft became the dominant force in the industry. Some Microsoft employees have also indicated that since Ballmer announced his retirement, Gates has appeared more frequently at Microsoft's headquarters in Redmond. This has sparked some speculation - perhaps tinged with hope - that Gates may want to take on a larger role at Microsoft to restore its past glory.

Ed Lazowska, a professor of computer science at the University of Washington, said, "Microsoft is the company Gates founded and led to success; it's not easy to completely disengage. I think he will never be detached from this company, and I bet he will be more involved in Microsoft's affairs in the future."

No intention to return to Microsoft

Regardless of the position he holds in the future, Gates already has the power to decide the direction of the company. He is currently still the chairman of the board, the largest individual shareholder, and one of the members of the special committee responsible for finding Microsoft's next CEO. Compared to others, Gates has more influence in the selection process.

Despite many rumors, according to the current situation, the possibility of Gates taking on more daily work at Microsoft is not high. According to sources familiar with Gates, he does not intend to leave charity work and re-immerse himself fully into Microsoft's operational affairs. So far, Gates' spokesperson Jon Pinette has not commented on this report.

Even so, the story of a founder returning to save the company remains highly attractive, especially after Steve Jobs (Steve Jobs) returned to Apple and transformed it into the world's most profitable company. In recent years, Microsoft has struggled in some important new markets, especially in the smartphone and tablet markets, leading some tech executives to call for Gates to return to Microsoft as CEO.

CEO Candidates

The search for Ballmer's successor is still in its early stages. Sources revealed that although this process might suddenly accelerate, Microsoft's board has no plans before the end of the year. Another source familiar with Microsoft's CEO search stated that the current CEO candidates being considered include Alan Mulally, CEO of Ford Motors; Tony Bates, Microsoft's executive vice president of business development, who joined the company through the acquisition of Skype; Paul Maritz, former number three at Microsoft and current CEO of cloud computing company Pivotal; and Stephen Elop, former CEO of Nokia. After Microsoft completes the acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business, Elop will return to work at Microsoft. Microsoft spokesperson Frank Shaw did not comment on the potential CEO candidates for the company.

Among external candidates, Mulally from Ford Motors is a favored choice because, without government assistance and unlike the other two Detroit automakers General Motors and Chrysler that filed for bankruptcy protection, Mulally led Ford Motors to a revival. Regarding whether he is interested in the position of Microsoft CEO, Mulally himself did not comment. Indeed, he lacks a background in the software industry, which makes some current and former Microsoft employees feel uneasy, as they believe the company needs a leader skilled in technological vision. Another concern is that Mulally is now 68 years old, meaning he cannot serve as CEO for very long.

Mulally has consistently stated that he will remain as CEO of Ford Motors at least until 2014. Sources revealed that under pressure, Ford Motors' board is scheduled to meet this Wednesday to address rumors about Mulally's impending departure.

Ford Motors spokesperson Jay Cooney said, "There have been no changes since we announced the CEO plan last November. Mulally will continue to focus on executing the One Ford plan. We do not speculate or publicly comment on board-related matters."

Future Responsibilities

As one of the old faces of the company and one of the richest people in the world, Gates began focusing on charity work in 2008, dedicating most of his energy to education and health causes. In recent years, Gates has significantly reduced his holdings in Microsoft shares, channeling more funds into charitable endeavors. However, even so, Gates currently still owns 4.52% of Microsoft's shares, worth approximately $12.8 billion, far exceeding other individual shareholders. Ten years ago, Gates held 10.75% of Microsoft's shares.

However, Gates has always participated in Microsoft's product discussions and regularly held talks with company executives. Sources revealed that typically, Microsoft executives would brief him on products at his private office in Kirkland, a suburb of Seattle, not far from Microsoft's headquarters in Redmond. Such meetings would occur weekly or even more frequently, depending on Gates' travel schedule for his charity work and the progress of Microsoft's product development.

In product meetings, Gates has met with the heads of Windows, Bing search, and Office products. Sources said that Gates is still regarded as having a "fresh perspective," bringing value to Microsoft's executives. In the weeks following Ballmer's announcement of his retirement, for unclear reasons, Gates made appearances at a small number of product briefing sessions within the company campus. Microsoft spokesperson Frank Shaw stated, "Since transitioning to his current position, Gates has consistently been involved in Microsoft's product and technology teams."

A small portion of Microsoft investors and former employees believe that Microsoft needs Gates to participate less in company affairs. These people think that as chairman of the board, Gates should also bear responsibility for the company's struggles in recent years. Last week, there was news that three anonymous shareholders had pressured Microsoft's board to demand Gates resign as chairman, arguing that he would hinder the new CEO's strategic changes.

However, sources familiar with Gates and Microsoft's board said that it is unlikely that Gates will distance himself from Microsoft. According to documents submitted by Microsoft to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission, the company recommended last Thursday that Gates be re-elected as a director.

Gates will partially assume the responsibilities of the CEO in the future. Yale University management professor Jeffrey Sonnenfeld believes that it is very important for Microsoft's new CEO to keep Gates involved in the company. He said, "I think this is very positive."

Among many aspiring engineers, Gates is still regarded as the creator of the entire industry. Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg (Mark Zuckerberg) said last month, "When I was a child, Bill Gates was my idol. I think he is one of the greatest visionaries in our industry."
