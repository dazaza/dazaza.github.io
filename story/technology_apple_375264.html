Random Thoughts on the 3Q War

by diglog on 2010-12-19 13:51:26

In China, dust always settles easily. The 3Q War that began on November 3rd was once so breathtaking, but in the blink of an eye, it vanished into thin air. However, some things are destined not to fade away. I am inclined to view this battle as a rehearsal: China's mobile internet era has yet to truly arrive, and Tencent, the company that dominates both traditional and mobile internet, hasn't encountered a real disruptor yet. It is likely to maintain the style of "total warfare" from the past few years, engaging in blitzkrieg with most high-growth start-ups until the true battle of the century arrives. For those who can't bear to be idle, China's internet industry is currently in the Warring States period: the era of gentlemanly friendships where people calmly discussed heroes over wine has ended, and the age of warring for dominance cannot tolerate mediocrity. In other words, if you are building a company that truly influences the landscape of China's internet, you will inevitably encounter Tencent - it is the hurdle that one must clear to "leap over the dragon gate."

So, what did the 3Q war reveal? (If you consider yourself sufficiently familiar with Tencent, skip directly to the fourth point.)

1. **Tencent's Period of Confusion**

Many high-growth companies go through a phase of "multiplication": their next move makes their previous one seem more valuable. For example, Apple progressed from iPod to iPhone to iPad; Facebook moved from campus markets to full registration opening to OpenSocial; Amazon evolved from selling books to various goods, cloud platforms, and service systems for small and medium-sized merchants... This kind of growth is natural, entirely based on a company's understanding of a larger world and deeper insights into user needs after reaching a certain level of development.

Tencent also went through a similar growth phase. When it added features like QQ Show, QQ Groups, and QQ Space to its basic instant messaging service, a simple internet tool became "de-toolified," turning into many people's social network or even part of their lifestyle. At the time, Tencent wasn't yet the dominant force in China's internet, and people even worried whether its users would migrate to MSN. Many of its innovations (such as "hanging the sun") seemed insignificant, but precisely these seemingly minor yet user-demand-satisfying innovations made QQ indispensable for many users.

At the same time, this suppressed or masked another aspect of Tencent's nature: in major strategic directions, it never hesitates to copy, such as successful portals and games, and failed attempts in e-commerce and search.

In the last two years, it appears to be losing the awe-inspiring creativity based on user needs. A somewhat confused Tencent has become involved in everything: microblogging, group buying, mini-game sites, security, media players, downloaders, children's games, mobile browsers, and large-scale investments... As a company with hundreds of millions of daily active users, you could say all these align with real user needs, but is there anything it sees further ahead than the entire industry?

As the undisputed giant of China's internet, Tencent seems to have entered a stage of "addition."

For startups, this is both good and bad news.

2. **Curbing Strategy**

It naturally follows that before Tencent finds its next true breakout point, it is carefully protecting its foundation.

What is Tencent's foundation? If your answer is QQ or instant messaging, then you're wrong. Its foundation lies in its profound coverage of Chinese internet users, which is no longer simply represented by installation numbers: its active user count, the interpersonal relationships established among these active users, and the behaviors influenced by these relationships...

Within sight, no second product poses a threat to QQ's existing everything. If we had to stretch, it might be this year's rapidly growing microblogging (though we don't know how much of its popularity is driven by bots).

Because it lacks a clear target, Tencent's sense of crisis is instead stronger: it seems to worry about the rise of all products with large user bases, even though many products are bound not to "de-toolify" like QQ does.

51.com, Xunlei, Storm Media Player, Sogou Input Method, UCWeb, Mole Town, and Qihoo 360—these are the companies that have been caught in Tencent's relentless campaigns over the past few years. Except for 51.com, which struck at Tencent's core with Rainbow Hang, the outcomes of the other battles were similar: Tencent couldn't truly crush the leaders in each respective field, but it certainly kept these companies under constant restraint. Don't forget, in the past five years, no real internet company in China has grown big enough to go public—what if there hadn't been Tencent's restraint?

I remember at the beginning of the year during the Game Industry Annual Conference, Wang Haibing, who left Tencent to create Mole Town, said that competing with Tencent comes down to having a better product. Of course, the number of Chinese start-ups that can achieve this is already rare.
