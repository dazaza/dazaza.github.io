"Sky之城" Helps Twitter Set New Records, New Architecture Plays a Key Role

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:20:02

### English Translation:

**Original Title: New-Tweets-Per-Second Record and How**

#### Background:

Japanese internet users have a habit of live-tweeting or following along with the dialogue of TV shows as they air, a practice known as "real-time commentary." On the evening of August 2, 2013, Hayao Miyazaki's renowned animated film *Castle in the Sky* aired for its 14th television rebroadcast on NTV. As the plot reached the climactic moment where the protagonists Pazu and Sheeta recite the destruction spell "Blase" together, numerous viewers simultaneously tweeted this phrase, setting a new record for tweets per second.

According to Twitter's official Japan account, at 11:21:50 PM local time on August 2, the tweet rate surged to a peak of 143,199 tweets per second due to the "Blase Festival." This number surpassed the previous record of 33,388 tweets per second set during the Japanese New Year in 2013 and far exceeded other notable events such as the announcement of Osama Bin Laden's death (5,106 tweets/second), the East Japan Earthquake (5,530 tweets/second), and pop star Beyoncé announcing her pregnancy (8,868 tweets/second).

Below is a graph showing the frequency of visits near the time of the peak. Typically, we see about 500 million tweets per day, averaging roughly 5,700 tweets per second. The peak represented nearly 25 times the usual traffic!

During this peak period, users did not experience any temporary service disruptions. Regardless of what happens around the world, ensuring that Twitter remains available is one of our primary goals.

"New Tweets per second record: 143,199 tweets per second. Typical daily average: 500 million tweets; approximately 5,700 tweets per second."

Three years ago, achieving such performance was unimaginable. During the 2010 FIFA World Cup, Twitter became the global center for real-time communication. Every goal, penalty kick, yellow card, or red card prompted users to tweet, repeatedly straining system bandwidth and causing brief periods of unavailability. Engineers worked tirelessly through the night, striving to find ways to scale the entire system's capacity. Unfortunately, these improvements were quickly overshadowed by the rapid growth of Twitter's user base, leaving engineers feeling like they had exhausted all options.

After that painful experience, we decided to reflect deeply. We resolved to redesign Twitter so it could handle growing traffic loads smoothly. Since then, we've made significant efforts to ensure that Twitter remains stable even during major events worldwide, such as the airing of *Castle in the Sky*, hosting the Super Bowl, or celebrating New Year's Eve. The redesign not only ensured stability during sudden spikes in traffic but also provided a scalable platform, enabling easier implementation of new features like cross-device message synchronization, richer content through Twitter Cards, and enhanced search experiences incorporating both users and stories. More innovations are on the way.

In the sections below, we will detail the steps we took. We learned a lot, restructured our engineering organization, and over the coming weeks, we will publish more in-depth articles exploring some of the topics mentioned here.

#### Beginning the Re-Architecture

Once the 2010 FIFA World Cup concluded, we reviewed the entire project and identified several key findings:

We were running the largest Ruby on Rails cluster in the world, evolving the system rapidly — at the time, about 200 engineers contributed to this effort. Despite explosive growth in both user numbers and absolute load, the system remained intact. However, it was still a monolithic structure, with all our work concentrated within a single codebase. Managing pure database connections, memcache interactions, site rendering, and exposing public APIs were all bundled into one repository. This not only made it difficult for programmers to understand the entire system but also complicated managing and synchronizing various project teams. Our storage system had reached its limits — our reliance on MySQL involved temporary sharding with only a single master node. Handling the influx of tweets caused trouble, requiring us to constantly add new databases. All our databases were under heavy read/write pressure.

When facing issues, we simply added more machines without addressing the root problems from an engineering perspective — based on machine configurations, the transaction-per-second capability of our Ruby front-end machines was far below expected levels. From past experiences, we knew they should be able to handle more transactions.

Finally, from a software perspective, we found ourselves in an "optimization corner," sacrificing code readability and scalability for performance and efficiency gains.

Our conclusion was that we needed to start a new project to reassess our system. We established three goals to motivate ourselves:

- Ensure Twitter has a high-level architecture focused on performance, efficiency, and reliability. We aimed to maintain good average system response times under normal conditions while accounting for abnormal peaks, ensuring consistent service and user experience at any time.

- Reduce hardware requirements by tenfold and improve fault tolerance by isolating failures to prevent larger-scale service interruptions — crucial as the number of machines grew, increasing the likelihood of individual machine failures.

- Clearly define boundaries between related logics. Working in a single codebase across the entire company caused difficulties, so we began dividing modules into a loosely coupled, service-based model. Instead of promoting encapsulation and modularity at the class/module/package level, we embedded these principles deeply into the system architecture.

Most importantly, we wanted to accelerate feature development. Small, autonomous teams could make internal decisions and release changes independently of other teams.

To address these requirements, we built prototypes to validate the re-architecture approach. Although we didn't attempt everything, the prototypes allowed us to establish some guidelines, tools, and architectures that brought us closer to our desired state.

#### The JVM vs. the Ruby VM

First, we evaluated the front-end service nodes across three dimensions: CPU, memory, and network. Ruby-based machines encountered bottlenecks in CPU and memory usage — despite not handling as much load as expected, network bandwidth wasn't close to saturation. Our Rails servers were designed to handle one request at a time in a single-threaded manner. Each Rails host ran a certain number of Unicorn processors to provide host-level concurrency, but this replication turned into wasted resources (Rails services on a single machine could only run single-threadedly, wasting multi-core capabilities). Ultimately, Rails servers could only handle 200–300 requests per second.

Given Twitter's rapidly growing load, simple math showed that meeting demand would require a massive number of machines.

At the time, Twitter had experience deploying large-scale JVM services. Our search engine was written in Java, our streaming API infrastructure and social graph system Flock were implemented in Scala. We were impressed by the performance offered by the JVM. Achieving our desired performance, reliability, and efficiency goals on the Ruby virtual machine proved challenging, so we started writing code for the JVM. Rewriting our code yielded a 10x performance improvement on the same hardware — today, our single server can handle 10,000–20,000 requests per second.

Twitter placed significant trust in the JVM because many engineers came from companies operating large JVM clusters. We were confident that Twitter could undergo transformative changes in the JVM ecosystem. Now, we needed to decouple our architecture to determine how these different services would collaborate and communicate.

#### Programming Model

In Twitter's Ruby system, parallelism was managed at the process level: a single request was queued in a process waiting for processing. This process would be entirely occupied during the request's handling. This increased complexity and effectively turned Twitter into an architecture where a single service depended on responses from others. Ruby-based processes were single-threaded, making Twitter's response time highly sensitive to backend system delays. Ruby offered some concurrency options, but there was no standard method to coordinate them all. In contrast, the JVM conceptually and practically supported concurrency, allowing us to build a truly concurrent programming platform.

Providing a single/unified method for concurrency proved essential, especially when handling network requests. Writing concurrent code, including network handling, is notoriously difficult and can be implemented in various ways. Indeed, we started encountering these challenges. When decoupling the system into services, each team adopted slightly different approaches. For example, client-to-service failover lacked proper interaction: due to inconsistent backend pressure management, servers returned values to clients improperly, leading to a stampede of requests overwhelming delayed services. These failure areas highlighted the need for a unified, comprehensive client/server library encompassing connection pooling, failover strategies, and load balancing. To embed this idea deeply, we introduced the "Futures and Finagle" protocol.

Now, not only do we have a consistent way of doing things, but we've included everything the system needs in core libraries, accelerating new project progress. Simultaneously, we don't need to overly concern ourselves with how each system operates, allowing us to focus more on application and service interfaces.

#### Independent Systems

We implemented significant architectural changes, transforming the integrated Ruby application into a service-based architecture. We focused on creating Tweet timelines and user-specific services — our core functionality. This change brought clearer boundaries and team-level responsibility and independence. In our old, monolithic world, we either needed a guru familiar with the entire project or specific module/class owners.

Tragically, the codebase grew too fast, making it increasingly difficult to find someone who understood all the modules. Relying solely on a few module/class experts couldn't solve problems. Our codebase became harder to maintain, and teams often had to dig through old code like archaeologists to understand certain features. Otherwise, we organized "whale hunting expeditions," expending significant manpower to identify reasons behind large-scale service outages. By the end of the day, we spent much of our time on these issues and had little left for developing/releasing new features, which left us feeling frustrated.

Our philosophy has always been that a service-based architecture allows us to develop systems in parallel — we agree on network RPC interfaces and then independently develop internal implementations. This means the internal logic of each system is self-contained. If we need to modify Tweets, we can do so within the Tweet service, and the changes will propagate throughout the architecture. However, in practice, not all teams plan changes in the same way. For instance, changing Tweet display might require updates in other services first. Despite this, the approach still saved us time overall.

This system architecture reflects what we've always wanted and enables effective operation within Twitter's engineering organization. Engineering teams form highly independent groups capable of working quickly. This implies that we tend to let project teams launch their own services and call backend systems to complete tasks, involving substantial operational work.

#### Storage

Even after breaking down our monolithic system into services, storage remained a huge bottleneck. At the time, Twitter stored tweets in a single-master MySQL database. We used a temporary data storage strategy where each row in the database represented a tweet, storing tweets sequentially until the database filled up, then creating a new one and reconfiguring the software to write to the new database. This strategy bought us some time, but we still struggled with sudden spikes in traffic because large amounts of data needed to be serialized into a single master database, causing intense read requests on our local databases. We needed a different partitioning strategy for Tweet storage.

We introduced Gizzard, applying it to tweets to create sharded, fault-tolerant distributed databases. We created T-Bird (possibly referring to speed improvements). Gizzard acted as the frontend for MySQL clusters, hashing each arriving tweet and selecting an appropriate database for storage. Of course, this meant losing MySQL's unique ID generation capability. Snowflake addressed this issue. Snowflake enabled us to create globally unique IDs. We relied on it to generate new tweet IDs, though we lost the ability to incrementally generate IDs by adding one. Once we obtained an ID, we used Gizzard to store it. Assuming our hash algorithm was good enough to distribute tweets evenly across storage, we could store more data using the same number of databases. Our read requests were also evenly distributed across the entire distributed cluster, increasing our throughput.

#### Observability and Statistics

Transforming the fragile, monolithic system into a robust, well-encapsulated, yet complex, service-based application required tools to manage this beast. Given the rapid construction of various services, we needed a reliable and simple way to gather data on their operations. Data-driven decision-making became the default rule, necessitating easy access to relevant data.

As we launched more services on a rapidly growing large system, we needed to make this process seamless. The Runtime Systems team developed two tools: Viz and Zipkin. Both are exposed and integrated into Finagle, so all Finagle-based services can automatically utilize them.

```ruby

stats.timeFuture("request_latency_ms") {

// dispatch to do work

}

```

The above code is all a service needs to generate reports for Viz. From there, any Viz user can write queries to generate time/graphs for interesting data, such as the 50th and 99th percentiles of `request_latency_ms`.

#### Runtime Configuration and Testing

Finally, when putting everything together, two seemingly unrelated issues arose: First, coordinating multiple series of different services for the entire system was challenging — we lacked a place to bring together all the services needed for an application at Twitter's scale. Deployment alone could no longer expose new features to customers; coordination among services was necessary. Second, testing the entire system in a fully isolated environment became increasingly difficult as Twitter grew. While testing isolated systems was manageable, we needed a way to test large-scale iterations. We embraced runtime configuration.

We integrated all services through a system called Decider. When rolling out changes, it allows us to instantly interact with multiple subsystems in the architecture by simply flipping a switch. This means software and multiple systems can be productized when teams consider them ready, without activating specific features. Decider also allows binary or percentage-based toggling, such as enabling a feature for x% of users. We can deploy completely inactive and secure features and gradually activate/deactivate them until we're confident they function correctly and the system can handle the new load. All these efforts reduce the need for inter-team communication and coordination, allowing us to customize/configure as needed while the system runs.
