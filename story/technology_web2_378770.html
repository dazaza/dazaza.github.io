LBS is Check-in

by ybcz0519 on 2011-01-18 21:07:54

Do you still remember a few months ago when I was doing observations in the LBS field, there was a popular opinion at the time: "LBS is more than just check-in." But some recent thoughts and discoveries have changed my mind – LBS is check-in. I had written some analyses before, expressing my annoyance with services like JiePang (If it were four months ago, I would've used Foursquare as an example, but now it's completely different). Constant check-ins only brought me cheap badges which didn't give me much fun. The synchronization of social networks caused irritation to my friends, especially when it involved privacy, making me even less willing to use such services. I introduced some friends to use this kind of software. At first, they all thought it was cool, but after realizing there was no actual product benefit, they lost interest. The average active cycle did not exceed one month.

But when I recently restarted my JiePang, I found that LBS has already begun to evolve, moving beyond just the action of checking in:

Massive marketing activities support

If you look at the homepage of domestic LBS services, you'll find a lot of marketing activities. JiePang and most domestic LBS services, like their predecessor Foursquare, follow the marketing routine. Such services in China are definitely advertiser-driven models. Foursquare's first milestone was gaining recognition from Starbucks, then Subway, and the second milestone was the rapid growth of users. Interestingly, both Starbucks and Subway in China also recognized JiePang's business model.

Significant product improvements

A few months ago, when I chatted with many founders of LBS services, everyone was quite confused and very low-key. They had a lot of ideas, but they didn't dare to say them, nor would they quickly deploy them on the product, so at the time, it gave many "observers" the illusion that "domestic LBS is just check-in, lacking innovation." I think these founders all understand how to create products domestically. First, they exercise their teams, and then they do some innovations.

Still taking JiePang as an example, in their 2.0 version, they added a batch of powerful features: released an iPhone client, supported photo publishing (with effects similar to Instagram, which I love), location classification (long overdue), likes (helpful for improving recommendation algorithms), hot zones (convenient for marketing and queries), and the Crab Plan (convenient for marketing). For more details, see: http://we.jiepang.com/client-android/.

The sharing layer above check-in

Most importantly, I recently found that my JiePang friends have added a sharing layer (let's call it that for now) on top of my POI (point of interest) layer. The data from the sharing layer is already enough to attract me, providing rich product benefits. The data from the sharing layer comes from friends' "likes," guides, and related pictures. For example, when I check in somewhere, I don't just get badges and coupons; I also see places recommended by my friends (where I can go after dinner) and guides (comments from friends are more reliable than strangers).

During the weekend's LBS Viewpoint activity, another mainstream view was "if a certain website does xx, it will be better than startups." I don't agree with this view. The most fundamental thing about a team is its DNA. I've seen some LBS teams who can definitely create great value. Okay, I believe that JiePang has already paved a broad road on the path of check-in, and I hope they continue to work hard.
