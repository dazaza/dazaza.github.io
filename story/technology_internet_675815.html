The Feast of the Martyrs and the Running of the Bulls in Spain

by yinghua422 on 2012-02-25 13:25:19

**Martyrdom Festival**

According to legend, in 1227, the Moors captured the Segovia Castle of Zamora. When people were at a loss for what to do, some women, including Saint Agatha, stepped forward and led the men and other women in a special battle against the enemies. They eventually drove away the enemies and reclaimed the castle, with many women making heroic sacrifices. To commemorate the women who sacrificed their lives in this defense of their hometown, it was decided that every February, two days would be designated as the "Martyrdom Festival," during which women would take charge of all activities, also known as "Women's Rule Day."

During these two days, from public affairs to family matters, all powers held by men are handed over to women for management. The women "elect their own two mayors" who exercise mayoral authority. On this day, under the command of the "female mayor," the women carry a giant statue of Saint Agatha, one of the defenders of the castle, and march toward the church guarded by men. This symbolizes the recapture of the Segovia Castle in those days.

**Running of the Bulls Festival**

Spain's annual Running of the Bulls Festival is officially called the "[San Fermín Festival]," with San Fermín being the patron saint of Pamplona, the capital of the wealthy Navarre province in northeastern Spain. The origin of the Running of the Bulls Festival is directly linked to Spain's bullfighting tradition. It is said that initially, it was very difficult for the people of Pamplona to drive six large bulls from the suburban cattle pens into the city's bullring. In the 17th century, some onlookers had a sudden idea: they dared to run in front of the bulls, provoking them and leading them into the bullring. Over time, this custom evolved into the Running of the Bulls Festival. In 1923, the famous American writer Ernest Hemingway came to Pamplona to watch the Running of the Bulls and wrote the famous novel *The Sun Also Rises*. He provided a detailed description of the festival in his work, bringing it international fame. After Hemingway won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1954, the Spanish Running of the Bulls Festival became even more renowned. To thank Hemingway for his contribution to the popularity of the Running of the Bulls Festival, the locals erected a statue of him at the entrance of the bullring.

The "Bull Run Route" located in the old town of Pamplona is actually a narrow cobblestone street, 848 meters long. During the bull run, tens of thousands of bull-running enthusiasts crowd the streets. Weighing about 500 kilograms, the bulls burst out of the pens and race through the chaotic crowd at a speed of 24 kilometers per hour within four minutes, often resulting in falls and dangerous situations. The event is considered successful when the bulls are finally led into the bullring. During the festival, injuries occur every year, and there have even been fatalities. According to statistics, from 1924 to 2002, 14 people were gored to death, and more than 200 others were injured by the bulls. Despite the risks, adventure enthusiasts from around the world gather in this small city of only 180,000 people from July 6th to 14th each year to enjoy the excitement and thrills of this nine-day dangerous game between humans and bulls.
