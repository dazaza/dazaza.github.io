A 23-year-old college student's open-source project, Google will give the middle finger.

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:58:11

For most people, the shutdown of Google Reader was a troubling event, but for college student Jacob Cook, it represented an opportunity to showcase his aspirations. He independently developed an operating system called "ARKOS," which claims to replace all services provided by Google or other cloud companies, including email, chat, file sharing, and virtual hosting. It achieves this through a private server the size of a credit card.

ARKOS is a Linux-based server operating system that can run on compact Raspberry Pi hardware (and potentially on BeagleBoard development boards or even full-sized PCs in the future). As shown in the image, the open-source application Genesis sits atop the Raspberry Pi, providing a web-based interface to control the server's operation. All files within the Genesis application are also available on GitHub, allowing users to download and install them themselves. However, ARKOS is still in its early stages as a project, and it is not yet recommended for use in critical projects.

(Compact Raspberry Pi hardware)

There are similar products on the market today, such as PogoPlug. However, PogoPlug can only manage its own files and does not provide a comprehensive suite of internet services like chat, website hosting, or email. In contrast, ARKOS can offer these services, allowing control over any other resources within customizable security levels. Another example is Tails, a well-known Linux-based operating system. While Tails provides only a desktop operating environment, ARKOS is a server. However, if data is hosted on ARKOS and accessed via the Tails system on another computer, it forms a perfect combination.

(Jacob Cook)

The developer behind this project, Jacob Cook, is a 23-year-old college student. He stated that while Google's web services are indeed powerful, their actions have become increasingly concerning. Earlier this year, Google shut down its Reader service, signifying that no functional service is sacred without sufficient profit motives. We have become overly reliant on services provided by others. In reality, Google's services are not long-term reliable; they can close any application or discontinue any product at any time if they see no commercial benefit. Additionally, the "PRISM" scandal has been a significant driving force behind Cook's ArkOS project. If the NSA can arbitrarily collect data from Google, Microsoft, Facebook, AOL, and Twitter, users are essentially "naked" online, leaving little room for trust. Clearly, Cook has chosen to trust himself.

However, ARKOS has potential limitations. Most home internet service providers optimize their services to enhance download speeds, so users can comfortably watch videos at home. But if ARKOS hosts a large media library, accessing it outdoors could be slow. Cook attempted to mitigate this issue by running arkOS data centers remotely, thereby enabling the same web-based interface and giving users self-control over their data.

Cook has a keen interest in computer security, and ARKOS represents his attempt based on his experience as a Linux hacker and IT administrator. He admitted that he lacks deep knowledge of encryption, but his goal was not to create a new encryption system, so this limitation is not a major concern. He also invited other enthusiasts to participate in the development project, though he remains the central figure.

Perhaps he is still just a young coder, but Linus Torvalds (the world's most famous programmer) once started as a lone coder too.
