Hey, self-media, don't hype it up too much, WeChat is almost unable to keep up with you guys.

by anonymous on 2013-11-21 14:23:40

On November 18th, Tencent held a WeChat Partner Communication Meeting, after which analysis articles flooded the internet. According to these articles, it seems we should advise Jack Ma and Robin Li to just wash up and go to bed because WeChat can do everything, leaving no room for anyone else in the Chinese internet space. The tech community's admiration and anticipation for WeChat have been around for a long time. Several months ago when WeChat updated to version 5.0, a similar star-studded situation occurred. However, from today's perspective, the imagination of self-media at that time has not become reality. Clearly, they were praising too quickly, and WeChat simply couldn't keep up.

Let's start with "Scan". In version 5.0, Scan was upgraded from a secondary menu to a primary one, a small change that left some self-media stunned. In their eyes, this simple change turned Scan into an entry point for mobile internet, capable of scanning out O2O.

This cool interaction method, coupled with hundreds of millions of WeChat users, is simply invincible. Reading this, I am truly amazed by their imagination. Of course, the function of Scan is indeed good. For example, real-time translation of scanned text, location determination by scanning buildings, payment by scanning QR codes, etc. But currently, the first two functions are basically unused and have become redundant. Under the WeChat scenario, users more often scan QR codes to follow WeChat official accounts.

In addition, QR code payment was also praised at that time. Now, there has been some progress, such as Beijing subway vending machines supporting WeChat Pay. However, this payment method is not exclusive to WeChat; Alipay, LAKALA, and others can also achieve it. Even in offline proximity payments, Alipay's sound wave payment is more convenient than WeChat Pay. Moreover, QR code technology is already mature; if desired, platforms like 58.com and Dianping could implement it as well.

"Scan" has not become a barrier for WeChat to fend off competitors. On the contrary, under WeChat's promotion, this interaction method has become more popular, indirectly helping Alipay save on user education costs. It appears that WeChat hasn't scanned out O2O, but it has certainly lowered the IQ of self-media.

Now let's talk about the Moments feature. This originally clean place has now become the largest source of risk for WeChat. Nowadays, Moments in the morning serve up a bowl of chicken soup with quotes like, "If you are well, it is a sunny day," while midday sees friends selling counterfeit goods updating their inventory. By evening, someone will post a welfare message: "Today is Pony Ma's birthday; share this information on your Moments and get 10 likes to upgrade to VIP." These content spam severely harass users while gradually pushing WeChat towards a point of no return.

Although dressed up with the pretty facade of Moments e-commerce, the unchangeable fact is that Moments remain a hub for counterfeit goods. Due to low cost and strong privacy, Moments have replaced Taobao as a haven for high-quality fakes. Moreover, the rampant fake goods in Moments have caught the attention of regulators. Last month, China National Radio News began focusing on the sale of counterfeit goods in Moments, followed by major media outlets reprinting this report. In interviews, Tencent stated it would use technical means to combat this issue.

Recently, people may have noticed that keywords like Alipay or transactions are being blocked or even leading to account bans on WeChat. Some interpret this as WeChat trying to ban Alipay, but in fact, WeChat is cracking down on Moments e-commerce. Earlier, Xiamen captured a woman using Moments to sell counterfeit goods. It can be foreseen that future cases of arrests due to selling fake goods via WeChat will increase.

Additionally, since Moments does not have a good credit guarantee mechanism, the risk of being exploited for fraud under the guise of e-commerce is very high. So-called Moments e-commerce may very likely be halted due to crossing regulatory policy lines.

Now let's talk about the WeChat Official Accounts platform. A few days ago, WeChat announced that there are now over 2 million official accounts, increasing by 8,000 daily. Self-media immediately became energized upon hearing this, without considering how many of these 2 million accounts are still actively maintained, and among those that are, how many are doing anything meaningful?

If I'm not mistaken, from the birth of the official accounts platform until now, media continues to talk about the same few examples: China Merchants Bank, Pacific Coffee, etc. This situation is easy to understand. Whether for developers or enterprises, although the official accounts platform provides interfaces, it cannot solve user problems. No matter how perfect an official account is, it still needs to acquire users through other channels, and this user acquisition difficulty is very high. Therefore, until now, only a few big brands have managed to succeed.

Moreover, regarding the development of their own mall on the WeChat Official Accounts platform, B-end shops cannot meet C-end demands. For instance, if I want to buy clothes, unless I specifically want H&M or Uniqlo, I can directly enter their official accounts to make purchases. But most of the time, I may want to buy clothes, but I don't know whose brand, what style, or what price range I want. At this point, I need to browse. On the official accounts platform, I would have to check Official Account A, then Official Account B, and compare them to decide where to buy. Clearly, this experience cannot compare with other e-commerce platforms.

Of course, despite all this, there are still many people promoting the idea that WeChat uses its official accounts platform to disrupt mobile internet. All I can say is, WeChat hasn't disrupted mobile internet yet, but readers' worldviews have already been overturned.

The mobile IM market is no longer dominated solely by WeChat as it was a few months ago. YiXin and LaiWang, one backed by telecom operators and the other by the largest e-commerce group with the largest scale of e-commerce users, although it's still hard to say who will win, given the current investments of all parties involved, YiXin and LaiWang are fighting a desperate battle. The competition in IM will only become fiercer. Although WeChat has made excellent breakthroughs in gaming, it indeed faces significant crises in both Moments and the official accounts platform. How to resolve this crisis depends on Zhang Xiaolong's next move.
