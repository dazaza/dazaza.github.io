Liu Linsen: The Energy Revolution Enters the Era of the Masses

by ditan360 on 2010-06-17 17:00:43

Exclusive to Low Carbon Network, according to the latest issue of Germany's Der Spiegel magazine, Volkswagen Group recently announced that it will cooperate with Lichtblick Energy Company to launch a small home generator powered by natural gas. This home generator uses natural gas to generate electricity, and its working principle is similar to that of a natural gas car engine. In addition to meeting household electricity needs, this small home generator can also feed excess electricity into the public grid. The heat generated during the power generation process can be used for residential heating.

Due to the use of many new technologies for manufacturing environmentally friendly car engines, the energy utilization rate of this small home generator reaches as high as 94%, while the energy utilization rate of traditional large natural gas generators is only 40% to 60%. Such a high energy utilization rate makes home-based natural gas power generation more cost-effective than grid-supplied electricity, and unlike coal and oil-based power generation methods, natural gas power generation causes much less environmental pollution. According to the plan, starting from next year, at least 10,000 such small home generators will be produced annually, allowing tens of thousands of households to access environmentally friendly and affordable electricity supply.

In recent years, many countries around the world have been making every effort to develop renewable energy, one important feature has attracted public attention: focusing on popularization, especially promoting renewable energy entering homes.

☆ Fuel Cells Enter Homes

Japan is the country in the world that has promoted fuel cells for home use. As early as five years ago, Sanyo Electric Company launched a small home power generation device fueled by city gas (or natural gas). The company had previously introduced a polymer electrolyte fuel cell as an emergency power source, using pure hydrogen as fuel. This fuel cell was the earliest developed and marketed in the world, but due to the high price of pure hydrogen, it could not be widely promoted. For this reason, the company changed its research direction, aiming for a popular product, and instead developed a polymer solid electrolyte fuel cell fueled by city gas.

Using city gas as fuel for fuel cells requires a fuel reforming device. However, the hydrogen obtained from this device still contains trace amounts of carbon monoxide, which can adversely affect solid electrolyte fuel cells. Therefore, the small fuel reforming device launched by Sanyo Electric Company has successfully controlled the concentration of carbon monoxide to below 10 milligrams per cubic meter.

The home-type solid electrolyte fuel cell launched in the Japanese market features a catalyst element in its main body, not only enhancing resistance to carbon monoxide corrosion, but also achieving a power generation efficiency of up to 35% and a heat recovery efficiency of up to 30%, with a service life exceeding 40,000 hours.

When the output power of the home small fuel cell power generation device is 1 kilowatt, waste heat can be used to heat water to 60°C, stored in a dedicated hot water container for standby use, while the electricity is converted from DC to AC via an inverter for lighting and household appliances.

☆ UK: "Universal Power Generation" Plan

The UK government is implementing the "Universal Power Generation" plan, promoting "green houses." In the future, each household can install solar receivers on their roofs, use fuel cells in the basement, install steam generators under the stairs, and if possible, even set up a wind turbine in the garden. At that time, every household will achieve self-sufficiency in electricity consumption. The UK government's initial plan is to achieve 20% of total electricity generation from green technology by 2020, a figure ten times the current level.

The UK government has pledged that thousands of British households will receive government grants specifically for purchasing power generation equipment, converting their homes into "mini power stations." Furthermore, this plan will be formally written into the government white paper as a major reform initiative to meet domestic energy demands before 2050.

The UK government has committed that if the electricity produced by households exceeds their own consumption, the government will purchase the surplus electricity at preferential prices. The government believes that this measure will attract more and more people to invest in this new type of environmental protection industry.

To realize "universal power generation," the government will call on hospitals and schools to build wind turbines on playgrounds or large parking lots for power generation, for which these departments will be allocated a substantial amount of special funds. Additionally, the government will invest heavily in supporting families to purchase solar receivers, fuel cells, and small generators. Statistics show that the use of these energy-saving power generation devices will save one-quarter of the country's electricity consumption and significantly reduce industrial carbon dioxide emissions. Moreover, professionals introduce that the nationwide family power grid operates safer, more convenient, and more effectively than large power stations.

☆ Let More Ordinary People Use Wind Energy

John Smith, an engineer living in Flagstaff, Arizona, USA, recently installed a wind turbine in his backyard. The American Southwest Windpower Company in the city provided him with full-service support. This generator can meet one-third or more of Smith's household's daily electricity needs. Although the current cost is $13,000, Smith considers it worthwhile because of rising oil prices and electricity costs, reaching $300 per month. Using wind power can save him at least $100 per month.

It is reported that sales of such wind turbines in the United States are growing rapidly. Miriam Robbins, marketing manager of Southwest Windpower Company, said: "People are trying every means to find energy sources they can control, and the government provides favorable loans."

In the United States, more and more local governments are setting aside necessary budgets for developing alternative energy. The town of Rock Port in Missouri, USA, claims that they no longer need to rely on national electricity. Their town of 1,300 residents has become the first community in the United States to be entirely powered by wind energy. The town owns four wind turbines that not only supply electricity to the residents but also transmit surplus electricity to the power company. Besides generating clean energy, wind turbines bring other benefits. Wind farms can generate $1.1 million in real estate taxes annually. Landowners in Rock Port can also profit by renting out part of their land for installing wind turbines. Wind turbines also attract tourists to visit this area.

Since 2000, the U.S. wind power capacity has more than doubled. With funding from the Department of Energy, the cost of wind power in areas rich in wind resources has dropped twentyfold since 1982, down to as low as 4 cents per kilowatt-hour or even lower.

☆ Open a Biodiesel Gas Station

The financial storm swept across the globe, factories closed down, unemployment intensified, thus solving employment issues became the top priority for many countries. The origin of the subprime crisis, the United States, was hit hardest, with many unemployed individuals choosing entrepreneurship rather than waiting for government relief.

In Berkeley, California, there is a gas station called Biofuel Oasis. The fuel there is made from used cooking oil. This gas station is jointly operated by five women as a cooperative project. Margaret Farraway, the director, said that the purpose of opening the Oasis gas station is to give people a sustainable choice besides using diesel refined from petroleum sold by large oil companies.

Kevin Newman, residing in Los Angeles, is another example. His company went bankrupt, so he started a "home refinery" in his garage. He pours the used cooking oil from local restaurants for frying French fries and chicken legs into a small specialized device equivalent to the giant petroleum cracking tower of a large refinery. After heating and decomposition, it can refuel trucks. Newman meticulously calculates and continuously reduces costs, making his biodiesel very popular among users.

Although diesel is more expensive than gasoline per liter, driving diesel-powered cars is cheaper. Diesel engine design is more efficient than gasoline engines, so fuel efficiency is higher. However, finding a place to refuel diesel-powered cars can be quite challenging. Not all gas stations offer diesel, and those supplying biodiesel are even rarer. Currently, the United States has only 1,600 biodiesel gas stations, most concentrated in the Midwest and along the coasts. It can be expected that the Obama administration will encourage and support the development of such gas stations.

Used cooking oil has a chemical molecular formula similar to diesel. After a series of chemical reactions, used cooking oil can be converted into biodiesel with a conversion rate of about 10%. Diesel vehicles can directly use this biodiesel without modification, and due to its high combustion rate, adding 15%-20% biodiesel to regular diesel can reduce exhaust emissions from diesel vehicles by half. Moreover, due to the significant reduction in raw material costs, the price of batch-produced biodiesel from used cooking oil is approximately 15% lower than regular diesel.

Currently, there are two types of biodiesel. In addition to extracting from used cooking oil, the other is original diesel, processed and refined from crops.

☆ Citizens Invest in Building Solar Power Plants

Mr. Thomas Joch is the director of the Institute for Urban Research in Stuttgart, Germany. Ten years ago, he installed a solar power system in his villa. He installed 15 silver-white solar panels on the roof of the third floor of his villa, providing electricity for lights, vacuum cleaners, electric stoves, dishwashers, microwaves, and floor heating. He has two electricity meters installed at home, showing the electricity consumption and production of the house separately. Even on relatively cold and cloudy days, the needle on the production meter continues to move uniformly, indicating that electricity can still be generated on rainy days. The power generation device is almost noiseless, completely not affecting their lives. This device not only supplies electricity for his own use but also sells hundreds of degrees of electricity to the state each year, earning considerable subsidies.

Japan is one of the earliest countries in the world to popularize solar power generation. The government plans to expand household users from the current 450,000 to 1.4 million by 2030, accounting for about 30% of all households in Japan. Mr. Shinichi Mizuno, a resident of Hachiman City, Gifu Prefecture, has a two-story building covering about 155 square meters. Since installing a 4-kilowatt solar battery in 1995, the total power generation has exceeded 3,200 kWh. Mr. Mizuno introduced to the media that except for sometimes needing to buy electricity in winter, normal electricity demand can be met. During summer, when there is excess power generation, the surplus electricity can be sold to the power company at a relatively high price.

Currently, Japanese households installing solar batteries can save 200,000 yen in electricity bills annually. Calculated based on the installation cost of 3.2 million yen at the time, it would take about 16 years to recover the cost. The lifespan of solar batteries may even exceed that of the buildings themselves, making it very cost-effective in the long term. And for the country, the contribution is even greater; the 450,000 households nationwide using less national electricity amount to at least 1.5 million kilowatts, making a significant contribution to reducing carbon dioxide emissions.

To accelerate the development of renewable energy, many countries encourage ordinary people to participate in investments and have achieved expected results. In Germany, due to the introduction of the Renewable Energy Law, many ordinary people have invested limited money into solar power stations with low investment and long-term returns, greatly promoting the development of green energy in the country.

The Renewable Energy Law stipulates that developing renewable energy can receive national subsidies. For example, operators of solar power facilities can receive an additional subsidy of 43 cents for every degree of "green electricity" transmitted to the public grid. This is not only part of the country's economic stimulus plan but also a practical measure to alleviate global warming and promote the use of renewable resources.

By the end of 2008, ordinary investors in Germany had laid nearly 10 million square meters of solar panels, equivalent to nearly 1,300 football fields. Solar panels are distributed throughout Germany, such as on office buildings, residential buildings, stadiums, schools, farms, and industrial parks. Leipzig has a larger solar power plant, with a total investment of 110,000 euros, involving 80 investors, averaging just over 1,000 euros per person. Most of the investors are ordinary employees, including even college students and retirees, with an annual return on investment of 5%.

For ordinary people, partnering to open a bioenergy company, wind power company, or other green energy companies involves too much capital, making it less practical. However, investing in solar power generation requires both less capital and technical expertise, making it suitable for ordinary people to invest collectively. Moreover, Germany has relatively abundant solar resources, resulting in higher investment returns.
