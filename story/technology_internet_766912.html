Microsoft's Litigious Decade

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:18:48

**English Translation:**

From 1995 to 2005, Microsoft faced a series of comprehensive crises triggered by lawsuits. These crises not only affected Microsoft's market position, reputation, and focus but also significantly impacted its strategy and operations.

**Key Decisions:** Based on the characteristics of its products and market features, Microsoft actively defended itself in court, relying on the legal rights of enterprises, and responded to a series of lawsuits.

**Critical Moment:** On October 20, 1997, the U.S. Department of Justice filed a civil lawsuit with the Federal District Court of the District of Columbia, accusing Microsoft of violating the 1995 settlement agreement. It was alleged that Microsoft used its operating system monopoly to protect and expand this monopoly, improperly requiring personal computer manufacturers to install Microsoft's browser products while depriving users of their choice.

This action not only initiated a long-term antitrust litigation between Microsoft and U.S. courts but also triggered a series of events, including lawsuits from competitors and an antitrust lawsuit by the European Union against Microsoft. This placed Microsoft in a severe judicial environment and a challenging external environment. If the litigation had failed, Microsoft would have been forcibly split up, lost market share, and lost its competitiveness.

**Strategy Outcome:** Effective defense prevented Microsoft from being split up. Moreover, under the rapid development of the new internet era, Microsoft maintained high-speed growth and preserved its global competitiveness and influence.

**Response Strategy:** Microsoft adopted a comprehensive response strategy to protect its legitimate rights and interests. By targeting the weaknesses of its opponents and counterattacking, it gained consumer trust and support for its products, won social recognition for the company, and exerted pressure on judicial departments and judges.

**Company Background:** Founded in the 1970s, Microsoft began its journey in 1975 when 19-year-old Bill Gates dropped out of Harvard University to sell Basic language programs with his high school classmate Paul Allen. Within just 20 years, Microsoft became the leader in PC software development and a pioneer in the global internet industry. Bill Gates became the world's richest man. However, due to the characteristics of its products, sales methods, and the personal styles of its leaders like Bill Gates, Microsoft faced criticism, questions, and counterattacks from competitors and judicial departments (including Korea and the EU), leading to lawsuits accusing Microsoft of monopolizing the market and violating the "Antitrust Law." This placed Microsoft in a continuous series of comprehensive crises caused by these lawsuits.

**Analysis:** Examining Microsoft's series of antitrust lawsuits from a global perspective in terms of politics, economics, judiciary, and society provides insight into the company's development strategies, business tactics, and competitive strategies. Analyzing how Microsoft dealt with these lawsuits offers valuable lessons for other companies.

As Microsoft continued to grow, its products (mainly referring to its developed operating systems) and bundled sales methods were criticized and questioned by some industry professionals and others. Some pointed out that Gates and Ballmer "were always grabbing things," while others believed "all that will be left for us is washing machines and toasters."

**Litigation Battles:**

**First Round:** In 1991, Microsoft's competitors accused it of using its monopoly advantage in its operating system to bundle application software, prompting the Federal Trade Commission to begin investigating Microsoft. Although a settlement was reached in 1995, due to the strong personalities of Bill Gates and others, Microsoft repeated its bundling strategy in promoting Internet Explorer (IE).

On October 20, 1997, the U.S. Department of Justice filed a civil lawsuit with the Federal District Court of the District of Columbia, accusing Microsoft of violating the 1995 settlement agreement. It was claimed that Microsoft improperly required personal computer manufacturers to install its browser product and deprived users of their choice. Attorney General Janet Reno asked the court to declare Microsoft guilty of contempt of court and impose a daily fine of $1 million during the violation period.

In December 1997, the judge made a formal ruling: Microsoft violated the mediation agreement with the federal government by forcing computer manufacturers to adopt its browser. While appealing, Microsoft adopted a duplicitous approach—providing older versions of the browser for free to manufacturers using Windows 95 while accelerating the development of a new version integrated into the soon-to-be-released Windows 98. This move not only challenged the judge but also angered him. However, on May 12, 1998, the court made a partial ruling, and on June 23, the final ruling was made: the original ruling was incorrect.

Microsoft won the first round of litigation. However, this victory did not cause Microsoft to take the matter seriously enough, as it continued using the old sales methods, further infuriating relevant parties.

**Second Round:** The second round of litigation quickly followed. On May 12, the U.S. Department of Justice, having lost the previous case, re-sued Microsoft, accusing it of violating federal antitrust laws. On May 18, the U.S. Department of Justice joined forces with 20 states (one state later withdrew) to file a lawsuit with the Federal District Court of the District of Columbia. As Microsoft prepared to launch its integrated new version of the browser, Windows 98, on May 18, a clash with the U.S. Department of Justice was inevitable.

During this round of litigation, executives from Netscape, Sun Microsystems, IBM, and other companies testified against Microsoft's monopolistic practices. Additionally, it was reported that lawyers from the U.S. Department of Justice and representatives from 19 states held secret talks and submitted a proposal to break up Microsoft. In July 2000, the judge ruled to split Microsoft into two independent companies.

Faced with an unfavorable situation, Microsoft took active measures, beginning a counterattack by exploiting the weaknesses of its opponents and the judge, creating a favorable public opinion environment for Microsoft. First, it found statements from opponents who sought to exploit Microsoft’s difficulties. Second, it targeted biased and prejudiced remarks from the judge. Based on these critical points, Microsoft filed its appeal.

On June 28, 2001, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit voted to overturn the district court judge's decision from a year earlier to split Microsoft and ordered a retrial by a different judge. Thus, Microsoft achieved victory in the second round.

Microsoft's troubles were far from over, and it could be described as being embroiled in constant litigation. In December 2003, RealNetworks sued Microsoft in a federal court in San Jose, California, accusing it of violating federal antitrust laws and bundling media playback software with its operating system through illegal means. They reached a settlement agreement in October 2005, with Microsoft paying $460 million.

In foreign markets, Microsoft also faced numerous lawsuits. In April 2004, Korean portal Daum accused Microsoft and its Korean subsidiary of unfair business practices in instant messaging software.

In Europe, Microsoft faced a series of antitrust lawsuits, such as its defeat in March 2004. In February 2007, IBM, Nokia, Sun Microsystems, Oracle, and RealNetworks filed lawsuits with European antitrust agencies. The European Court of First Instance largely upheld the European Commission's 2004 antitrust penalty decision against Microsoft, maintaining the fine of 497 million euros.

Besides these representative cases, Microsoft faced doubts and lawsuits from the global industry and judicial departments of other countries. These lawsuits not only affected Microsoft's market position, reputation, and focus but also significantly impacted its strategic and operational development.

**Growth Through Litigation:**

By August 2011, Microsoft's market value reached $202.75 billion (rising to $260 billion by August 2013), ranking third globally behind Apple and ExxonMobil but ahead of tech and internet companies like IBM and Google. This indicates that despite enduring numerous lawsuits, which consumed many resources, Microsoft continued to grow and develop. From another perspective, without these lawsuits, Microsoft might have performed even better, with a potentially higher market value.

An analysis of the reasons behind Microsoft's many lawsuits reveals that national laws are fundamental. However, Microsoft's products and sales methods objectively affected competitors' markets and growth space. The personal style and personality of Microsoft's main leader, Bill Gates, also fueled dissatisfaction among relevant individuals, increasing attacks on Microsoft.

Clearly, repeated lawsuits not only consumed Gates' time and energy and drained Microsoft's resources but also influenced its strategy and operations, affecting the continuous improvement of corporate competitiveness. Perhaps because of these lawsuits, Microsoft failed to lead in its competition with emerging internet companies like Google and Facebook, failing to explore and capture new market opportunities. These experiences and lessons are undoubtedly important for Microsoft and the business community, especially the internet sector.

A comparison with other rapidly developing tech and internet companies of the same era, such as Cisco Systems and Intel Corporation, shows that these companies avoided antitrust liabilities by taking necessary actions viewed as essential by the industry and judicial departments. This serves as both a reminder to Microsoft and a reference for companies looking to expand into global markets.

**Litigation Prompting Change:**

Through these legal battles, Microsoft has been constrained by antitrust regulations. To win these cases, Microsoft had to respond. Besides actively defending itself, Microsoft underwent internal changes aimed at altering external perceptions and reducing the likelihood of litigation.

On November 5, 2002, Microsoft established an Antitrust Compliance Committee to strengthen internal management regarding antitrust laws, internal monitoring, and litigation responses.

Changing its aggressive style, Microsoft began focusing on communication, mediation, and low-profile actions. A New York Times article on May 15, 2011, noted, "If the Justice Department and 19 states hadn't sued Microsoft in 1998...this case indeed changed Microsoft's aggressive ways."

Respecting the market and respecting competitors. Alongside changing its aggressive style, Microsoft gradually demonstrated sufficient respect for the market and its competitors. From disregarding competitors to compensating them financially, supporting their development, and sharing the market, these actions resolved lawsuits, eased tensions with competitors, and brought new market opportunities for Microsoft.

Valuing the law and respecting courts and judges. From its experience of losing passive defenses, Microsoft realized the importance and necessity of "valuing" the law. Not only did Microsoft seek favorable methods from various U.S. antitrust cases, but it also learned from them, strengthening cooperation with courts and judges to strive for mediation during collaboration.

Handling various relationships outside of litigation, such as competition and cooperation, competition and monopoly, innovation and monopoly, market and government, market and judiciary. For rapidly growing enterprises, lawsuits may be unavoidable, but handling these relationships well can minimize them as much as possible.

Adopting specific strategies conducive to rulings. Microsoft's series of lawsuits revealed that gaining recognition and sympathy from society, the industry, and the public is a good way to obtain favorable judicial rulings. In its relentless "fight," Microsoft learned that aggression is less effective than playing the "sympathy card." Therefore, playing the "sympathy card" became one of Microsoft's choices in certain lawsuits and achieved good results. When Microsoft faced numerous attacks, it turned to the "retirement card," shifting attention and contradictions through Bill Gates' "retirement." This not only resolved conflicts but also created another topic that captured the world's attention.

Of course, for Microsoft to sustain its development, merely dealing with lawsuits is far from enough, especially since some lawsuits ended with Microsoft's loss or agreements where Microsoft paid compensation. While continuously responding to lawsuits, Microsoft did not forget to innovate and develop, achieving significant progress. However, in some new market areas, Microsoft still lagged behind some newer companies, such as Google and Facebook. How to create and dominate new markets remains a challenge not only for Microsoft's future development but also for the industry and future entrepreneurs.
