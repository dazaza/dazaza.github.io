Synopsis:

Main Cast: Shin Min-a, Shim Min-ah, Ju Ji-hoon, Kim Tae-woo, Lee Jong-hwan

The film uses food as a medium and the kitchen as a space to tell the story of a woman living under the same roof with two men.

On the eve of their first wedding anniversary, Sang-in (Kim Tae-woo) and Maeng-rae (Shin Min-a) plan to celebrate this special day. To mark the occasion, Maeng-rae goes to an art gallery to pick out a gift for her husband. There, she encounters a strange man and has sexual relations with him. Filled with guilt, Maeng-rae returns home and finally confesses everything to Sang-in, who shows understanding. In return, Sang-in makes a surprising move: he resigns from his well-paid position as a securities company manager and confesses his lifelong dream to his wife - to open a popular restaurant. Maeng-rae readily supports him, but little does she expect that the young man who helped her, Do-re (Ju Ji-hoon), is now helping Sang-in run the business! Back from France, Do-re has an outstanding talent for cooking, which greatly benefits Sang-in's business. However, from the day Do-re moves in with them, a dangerous triangle relationship begins...

Behind the Scenes:

"The Kitchen" is adapted from Japanese writer Banana Yoshimoto's debut work. In 1989, it was brought to the big screen by Masayuki Mori in "Kitchen no Himitsu," starring the then-popular Japanese model Ayako Kawahara. It was later remade in Hong Kong as "I Love Kitchen." What new elements can the Korean remake bring? Previously, adaptations of Japanese masterpieces such as "Maou" and "Western Antique Cake Shop" have caused quite a stir. Although the original works come from Japan, they have successfully achieved a "Korean localization" flavor. Film distributors from Japan, Malaysia, and Singapore purchased the screening rights of this movie even during its production stage, showing great confidence in it.

Love and stomach, who controls your love? - A stream-of-consciousness culinary film

When faced with two foods you want to own, how do you choose? If choosing food is simple, just eating both would solve the problem. But what if the objects are people? When two men with different charms both love you, even someone like Zhuge Liang reincarnated would find it hard to advise you. The heroine Maeng-rae in "The Kitchen" faces such a dilemma. The kitchen is not a traditional place for romantic love, but from works like "My Name is Kim Sam-soon," "The Foodie," "Western Antique Cake Shop," "Delicious Relations," and the Japanese version of this film, we can see that delicious food indeed can stimulate one's heart to bloom with the taste buds of love. This film inherits the unique vibrant color sense of culinary movies and will showcase various novel and tempting dishes. As the movie's dialogue suggests: a chef's mission is not to develop new dishes, but to dig deep into the taster's understanding of the meaning hidden in the dish. The desire to eat delicious food is like the natural instinctive wish to expect a wonderful life, a soft and kind ambition in everyone's heart. Through the painstaking process of making delicious food and savoring its flavors, one can gain a deeper evolution or even a complete transformation in terms of love, life, and the world.

The box office success of "The Foodie" set a high bar for filmmakers to create even more classic culinary films. Beyond the tranquil plots of small bridges over flowing streams, the design includes more dramatic twists and stimulating dishes. While "Western Antique Cake Shop" gained attention through its handsome male homosexual theme, "The Kitchen" attempts to return to the primal state of human desires - the house with many small windows in the film symbolizes the unstable and changing emotions within people. In a quiet space, release your sincere love akin to appetite, don't suppress your dreams.

Director: Justifying the Cheating Heart?

Although "changing hearts" condemned by public opinion and morality is often unappealing, director Hong Ji-young tries to analyze the brilliance and depth of this awkward triangular relationship. Graduating from a philosophy background, she has always been evaluated by people in the Korean film industry as "having somewhat bizarre ideas, a very interesting person," and this is her first feature-length film. The courtyard filled with green plants, the white house with exquisite edges, and the naturally smooth interior arrangement were all personally conceived by Hong Ji-young, exuding a feminine warmth. She mentioned that while staying in Paris, she once watched a film at the cinema about a triangular inappropriate relationship: "At that time, I had doubts why these improper relationships were defined by people as dark or even critical emotions." Hong Ji-young stated that she has no intention of justifying these improper relationships, but only wants to express: "In one's lifetime, there aren't many choices to experience or not experience certain things or emotions." Not all love remains unwavering, and a wandering heart is not shameful.

Between a husband with almost flawless conditions and a young man constantly releasing teasing information in words, where will the housewife with a unique personality go? To highlight the subtle emotional changes in each of the three characters, the scene movement inside their home takes up about one-third of the entire play, even omitting the appearance of other characters. Therefore, "The Kitchen" has a compact stage drama quality, testing the acting skills of the three leads even more, as the audience's attention is almost entirely focused on their dialogue and physical language performances. In the director's own words, it is "a simulated rebellion, an emotional journey hovering between love and non-love." Delicious dishes, sunny spaces, and flavorful relationships; Hong Ji-young revealed that the film incorporates her personal nostalgia for her first love. The structure of the story may make viewers involuntarily think of the intense and sad "The Bridges of Madison County." In the modern society where love and marriage are falling, seeking the abode of emotion and soul. And the director also mentioned in the interview that viewers don't need to bring tissues into the cinema, smiling brightly even while making painful decisions to separate or reunite. Because having a choice proves that you are still happy.

Fun Facts:

• Ju Ji-hoon + Shin Min-a —— The Couple Expected by All

• Their collaboration in "Maou" left a deep impression, so when the production announced that Ju Ji-hoon and Shin Min-a would reunite in "The Kitchen," many viewers expressed excitement. In 2008, they both made efforts on the big screen, but unfortunately, the box office and reputation of "Martial Arts Female College Students," "Rock 'n' Roll Seventies," and "Western Antique Cake Shop" did not meet expectations. Will this joint effort succeed?

• On one side is Ju Ji-hoon portraying a mysterious man returning from the romantic country of France, whose dishes are as witty and sweet as his words, and on the other side is a handsome and meticulous husband who constantly loves and tolerates his wife. Shin Min-a finds herself torn between the two, leaving behind a "beautiful regret" no matter which side she chooses. Shin Min-a was the last main actor confirmed for "The Kitchen." Hong Ji-young revealed that casting took over a year, "The moment I met her, I decided that Maeng-rae could only be her." Casting aside the dull and gloomy image of a housewife, Maeng-rae is a sunny woman full of restlessness, possessing a charm that makes men unable to resist upon seeing her. With a quirky neuroticism and a sensitivity unlike ordinary people: "Her most attractive charm lies in being able to smile even when facing the dark corners of herself and others." Shin Min-a admitted that playing Maeng-rae was not easy, stating that this role has a philosophical flavor of life: it has a whimsical and unfettered side, as well as a down-to-earth and real personality.

• Through films like "Return," "Apple," and "Tales of Strange Events," Kim Tae-woo gradually advanced towards becoming an acting powerhouse in the Chungmuro film industry, broadening his acting range. His deeply hidden masculine charm, coupled with gestures and movements that exude a gentle aura, portrays a husband with a nearly foolish deep love for his wife and an unrelenting pursuit of his career — such a man is probably the ideal type for most women in the world. If Ju Ji-hoon's youthful face already fits his role perfectly, then Kim Tae-woo's portrayal of Sang-in tests the depth of his acting skills. Having unwavering passion for family and cooking, yet not easily revealing emotions in actions and conversations, the husband in "The Kitchen" is also quite watchable.
