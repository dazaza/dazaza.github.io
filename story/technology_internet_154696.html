Defining the planning theme is the first step to doing a good job in planning.

by longersing on 2009-05-31 21:20:31

Longer/Author

Many of my clients have asked me after the end of a project: Is there a good way to approach a new planning project? I don't know how to answer, because this question is too broad. It depends on what type of new project it is. If it's architecture, there are architectural methods; if it's chain retail stores, then there is another set of methods. In short, it's about addressing specific issues.

Planning often emphasizes that the text conforms to the actual situation. No matter how good a plan is, if it lacks relevance to reality and cannot be implemented, it remains just empty talk. After all, every project has limited financial expenditures and human resource usage. If there were unlimited funds and resources, I think the concept of planning would not exist. Anyone could accomplish a magnificent project because there would be plenty of money and manpower, and even mistakes could be rectified with money.

Therefore, the ultimate goal of the planning we pursue is: focusing on the project's limited resources to attack, breaking down each problem until the final implementation and completion of the project. The beginning of a project originates from its theme, yet many so-called planners do not pay much attention to clarifying the planning theme. They believe that as long as the content is extensive and detailed enough, it will conquer the client. This cannot entirely be blamed on the planners. Social pressure and living pressures have caused many people engaged in planning and related work to submerge their own value in the rapid pursuit of money. Without considering the client's intentions, even the planning documents themselves are merely for show, just to get paid and leave. Over time, this has led to the current state of affairs. Several clients I've worked with had previously hired several firms to plan the same project, but they kept switching and ultimately gave up.

Regardless of how many pages your plan may have, in my view, a good plan is concise and allows the client to understand your meaning and appreciate the practical benefits it brings them. On top of that, when your plan is handed over to someone else for use, it should still be clear and allow the user to make adjustments suitable for temporary changes at any point without affecting the overall implementation of the project. Achieving these points, I believe, constitutes a complete plan, and also a successful one.

Actually, achieving all of the above isn't difficult. You just need to clarify the theme and proceed step by step.
