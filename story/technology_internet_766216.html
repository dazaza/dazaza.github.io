Nokia's feature phone tests Microsoft's wisdom and ability

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:53:36

Nokia's feature phone business cannot be overlooked

Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business has given this software giant control over its most important Windows smartphone partner. However, this acquisition also raises a question: how will Microsoft handle Nokia's feature phone business, which still dominates emerging markets.

Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer believes that feature phones are the gateway for users to access more expensive smartphones. In the second quarter of this year, Nokia shipped over 50 million feature phones. Ballmer stated in an analyst call held on Tuesday, "We see feature phones as an outstanding tributary system in the world of smartphones. Through feature phones, we can bring our services to a large number of low-end device users in many regions around the globe."

However, many market analysts warn that this is easier said than done. Jayanth Kolla, an analyst at Indian telecom research and consulting firm Convergence Catalyst, said that Microsoft lacks Nokia's retail and supply chain experience in most key markets. He said, "The terminal device business, especially the non-smartphone business in emerging markets, is a completely different field." This analyst pointed out that it requires close management of the supply chain and channels, and building a brand image through word-of-mouth effects. He said, "Microsoft does not have this gene."

The Indian market serves as a good example. Nokia entered this market in the mid-1990s, and by 2012, revenue from the Indian market accounted for 7% of the company's total revenue, higher than the 6% from the U.S. market. Nokia's roots in India run deep, with 200,000 cooperative stores, 70,000 of which exclusively sell Nokia devices. Additionally, Nokia's largest factory globally is located in Chennai, India.

It must be clarified that Nokia's market share in India, like elsewhere, has also declined: after dominating the market for 20 years, the company's Indian mobile phone sales were surpassed by Samsung Electronics for the first time last quarter, but its feature phone sales still hold an advantage. Market research company Nielsen rated Nokia as India's top mobile phone brand last October, and The Economic Times listed it as the third most trustworthy brand in the country.

User Loyalty

In places where power outages are frequent and roads are rugged, durability and long battery life have won Nokia phones a group of loyal fans — many of whom remain faithful to Nokia when upgrading to more expensive new phones.

For instance, Delhi's Sunil Sachdeva used a Nokia phone as his first mobile phone. He just bought his fifth phone: a Nokia Lumia phone running Microsoft's Windows Phone system. This Nokia fan said, "Technically speaking, Nokia is still the best."

However, Microsoft cannot take such user loyalty for granted. Local companies like Karbonn and Micromax pose a threat to Microsoft and Samsung Electronics, offering Android-based smartphones from Google. The lowest price for these products can be as low as $50. Moreover, these local manufacturers are hindering the development of Nokia's Asha brand, which positions itself between feature phones and smartphones. The global shipment volume of Nokia Asha phones was 4.3 million units in the second quarter of this year, down from 5 million units in the previous quarter.

Sumeer Singh, an analyst at Indian venture capital company BitChemy Ventures, said, "The sales performance of the Asha series has been poor. With the intensifying competition among low-end smartphone manufacturers, I don't know how long this business can last." Singh estimates that Asia-Pacific, the Middle East, and Africa account for about two-thirds of Nokia's feature phone shipments in the last quarter, with an average selling price between 25 and 30 euros (approximately $32.99 to $39.59).

Cannot Be Ignored

For Microsoft, the current dilemma is that although the feature phone market may decline, it is too large to ignore. Anshul Gupta, an analyst at market research firm Gartner, said, "In markets like India and Indonesia, over 70% of sales come from the feature phone business. Feature phones remain an important part of the overall mobile phone market."

This means that if Microsoft wants to cultivate feature phone users into potential consumers of Windows Phones, it needs to retain the Nokia and Asha brands and narrow the price gap between smartphones and feature phones and low-cost smartphones. Microsoft has hinted that reducing smartphone prices will be a priority. The Windows Phone series includes the Lumia 1020 with a 41-million-pixel camera, but also offers relatively simple Lumia 610 and 620 models for first-time smartphone buyers.

Terry Myerson, Vice President of Microsoft's operating system, said in Tuesday's conference call, "Low-cost phones will be one of the strategic options when the next generation of Windows Phones is released." Beyond that, Myerson refused to disclose further details.

Market analysts say that Microsoft can introduce Bing search, Outlook email, and Skype calling services to low-end phones to boost traffic for other services and enhance device appeal. Sami Sarkamies, an analyst at Finnish market research firm Nordea Markets, said, "By bundling these services with low-end products, we can reach more audiences."

However, since competitors in emerging markets see an opportunity to further erode Nokia's market share, Microsoft needs to prepare to face challenges from all sides. Analyst Kolla said that due to Nokia's strong promotion of the mid-range Asha brand, local low-cost Android phone brands in India have been suppressed. He said, "Now, I expect Nokia to make a comeback."
