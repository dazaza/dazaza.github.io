"Head-down" escapee

by gfrw0738 on 2010-04-13 23:19:09

This is a real new event that took place in our large county town in previous years. One evening, a residential building on the small street in the town caught fire. This house was built in the 1940s, with brick and wood structure, wooden stairs, wooden doors and windows, wooden floor, and it caught fire easily. In a flash, three or four households were engulfed, and the entire building was consumed by the flames.

The residents fled to the outside one after another. When only half of the people had escaped, the wooden staircase collapsed with a "boom." There were still nine residents upstairs who hadn't managed to escape. The passage to go upstairs was gone, and under the fierce fire and thick smoke, these people could only retreat to the topmost fourth floor of this building. This was the only place that wasn't yet destroyed by the fire.

The nine people crowded at the edge of the railing on the fourth floor shouting for help. The firefighting team arrived. But what baffled the firefighters was that the streets of this small residential area were too narrow for the fire trucks and ladder trucks to enter. The firefighting efforts were temporarily obstructed.

As they watched the fire gradually spreading towards the fourth floor, the captain of the firefighting team made a quick decision: rescue the trapped residents! Without a ladder truck, he ordered the firefighters to climb up the walls with ropes, planning to use the ropes to lower the trapped people down one by one.

Two firefighters followed the order and began climbing up, but when they reached the second floor, the wooden rafters they were holding onto burned and broke, causing both of them to fall down. With the wooden rafters gone, there was no foothold left, making it extremely difficult to climb up manually. At this moment, the supporting pillars on the top floor, which held up the entire building, were burning and making a "creaking" sound. Once these pillars gave way, the entire building would collapse.

There was no time for any kind of rescue now. The only thing the trapped people could do was to save themselves.

Without time to prepare, the captain of the firefighting team grabbed a shabby blanket that had been draped over one of the escaped residents, spread it out, and had several people hold it. Then, he shouted loudly to those upstairs: "Jump! Jump one by one! Jump onto the blanket! Back first!" To ensure safety, he demonstrated himself, performing a motion similar to the back-over jump. Only landing on the back was the safest, and since the blanket was old, landing on the back distributed the force more evenly, reducing the likelihood of the blanket breaking.

At the front of the railing on the fourth floor stood a woman wearing a coat. No matter how the captain shouted, she didn't dare to jump, hesitating all the while. Her hesitation blocked the others from jumping, and with each passing second, the danger increased. The people downstairs, growing anxious, shouted up: "If you don't dare to jump, let others jump first, and see how they do it."

The woman stepped aside. A female came to the edge of the railing and, encouraged by everyone, jumped. Her action wasn't as standard as the captain's demonstration, but she landed on her buttocks on the blanket, and there was no injury. The captain demonstrated again, reminding everyone of the correct way to jump. Next, the second person jumped down, with much better form—safe! The third, the fourth...the eighth—all jumped down safely, landing on their backs on the blanket without incident.

There was only one person left downstairs—a woman in a coat—but she was still hesitating. The people downstairs were almost driven mad, urging her fiercely. Finally, she made up her mind, stepped over the railing, bent down, head down, assuming a diver’s posture.

The captain was startled. Would she survive jumping like that? He shouted: "Back down!" But the woman ignored him, head down, diving down. Everyone's heart leapt into their throats as they watched her plummet like a bullet straight into the blanket. Due to the immense force, the blanket couldn't withstand the impact—it tore with a "tear" sound, and her head pierced through the blanket, hitting the ground.

"Why are you so stupid? So many people have jumped before you, you should have learned by now!" The captain rushed over anxiously. He saw that the woman's head was bleeding profusely, nearly lifeless. Yet her face showed a pale hint of relief. She patted her stomach and said with spirit: "A person can only jump this way to not... hurt... the child inside..."

Only then did the captain realize that this woman was pregnant.

The woman continued weakly: "If I don't make it, get the doctor to take out the baby from my stomach... It's already... nine months... I haven't... hurt it, it can live..."

Everyone immediately fell silent, solemn expressions on their faces. People finally understood why the woman hesitated, and why she chose such a foolish way to jump. She hesitated because she didn't know how to jump without hurting the child. Choosing to jump headfirst was the most dangerous for her, but the safest for the child inside her belly!

Leaving the most dangerous to herself and the safest to her child—that’s the choice every mother makes or prepares to make under heaven.
