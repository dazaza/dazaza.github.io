International Standards and New Detection Technologies for Indoor Air Pollution

For a long time, people have paid more attention to outdoor environmental pollution, especially toxic and harmful substances emitted by industries. However, research literature published by the World Health Organization (WHO) and the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency indicates that the level of indoor air pollution is generally much higher than outdoor pollution, typically 2-5 times higher, and in extreme cases, it can exceed 100 times. People spend 90% of their time indoors, and infants and children breathe more air compared to adults (by the ratio of breathing volume to body weight), thus inhaling more indoor pollutants.

As early as 1958, WHO recognized the threat of indoor air pollution to health; and starting in 1964, based on studies on the actual harm caused by indoor air pollution to health, WHO proposed and released the concept and definition of guideline limits for indoor air pollution. In 1969, WHO began classifying indoor organic pollutants. In recent years, research results reported by WHO indicate: globally, 3 million people die each year from diseases caused by indoor air pollution, accounting for 5% of total deaths; approximately 30-40% of asthma cases and 20-30% of other respiratory diseases are caused by indoor air pollution; inhalable suspended particles in the air are a major direct cause of respiratory diseases.

Despite WHO's reports indicating that indoor air pollution in developed countries is significantly lower than in developing countries, the United States also reports that tens of thousands of cancer deaths each year are directly caused by indoor environmental pollution, hundreds of thousands suffer from respiratory diseases, and tens of thousands of children develop hypertension due to prolonged exposure to polluted indoor environments.

Background and Significance of Establishing Guideline Limits for Concentrations of Indoor Air Pollutants

Since recognizing the potential serious health damage that various organic, inorganic, and radioactive pollutants in indoor air may cause to populations, WHO and governments of various countries have established corresponding guideline limits or threshold limits. It is generally believed that when exposed to environments below these limits, no direct or indirect adverse health effects will occur. These limits are only provided as guidance or reference for relevant departments in various countries to formulate standards and conduct management, not mandatory legal constraints, nor standards.

Based on the concepts and definitions of guideline limits and threshold limits for indoor air pollution published by WHO in 1964, the organization published its first document on guideline limits for indoor air pollution in 1972, listing SO2, CO, SPM (inhalable suspended particles), and photochemical oxidants as the first key pollutants. In 1987, the European version of WHO's guideline limits for indoor air pollution greatly expanded the types of key indoor air pollutants, increasing them to 27. This version more accurately revised the concept of guideline limits, believing there is no absolutely safe limit, and that guideline limits and threshold limits are merely the highest acceptable risk values.

Based on the document of guideline limits for indoor air pollution published by WHO in 1987, many meetings were held to continuously revise and upgrade the previous version of guideline limits, with WHO-1999 being the latest published version. WHO-196a was relatively comprehensive and basically finalized, with subsequent versions making only minor individual revisions.

The Healthy Cities Programme under WHO specifically implements the "Air Quality Management Information System" formulated and managed by WHO. Its function and purpose are to serve as a hub for transmitting and exchanging information and management regarding air quality between countries and cities worldwide. The content transmitted and exchanged includes: concentrations of urban atmospheric and indoor air pollutants, noise levels, health impacts, control measures and methods, air quality standards, emission standards, pollution sources, and pollution dispersion models and tools. Currently, WHO can provide materials on CDs related to air pollution from 1986 to 1999, covering 45 countries and 150 cities.

New Detection Technologies

In the standards established by various countries, one or several methods and means for detecting specific pollutants need to be specified, with other recommended methods and means also listed. Since most governments started formulating relevant standards after WHO published the European version (relatively comprehensive) of air quality guideline limits in 1987, commercialized electronic detection instruments at that time were basically non-existent, and the few available ones were of low level and poor quality. Therefore, the methods specified in the standards were mostly chemical analysis methods, using laboratory analytical instruments such as colorimeters, spectrophotometers, chemical titration, gas chromatography, and liquid chromatography. Although these methods have legal effect in arbitration, their drawbacks are obvious: it is difficult to achieve on-site real-time detection, they are labor-intensive and time-consuming, processes are complex, most processes involve manual operation, variability possibilities are high, costs are high, automation levels are low, and it is hard to carry out large-scale inspections.

With the continuous improvement and perfection of sensor and computer technologies since the 1980s, various on-site real-time detection technologies and corresponding portable instruments for harmful and toxic gases and other pollutants in indoor and outdoor air began to develop, going through a process from nothing to something, constantly improving. Currently, some well-known brands of instruments internationally have already approached or met the requirements of guideline limits, standards, and specifications formulated by WHO and relevant domestic and international departments in terms of testing range, resolution, accuracy, and stability. The greatest advantage of these new instruments is: on-site real-time, rapid response, convenient operation, low cost, high degree of automation, suitable for large-scale censuses.

Due to the above reasons, the concept of formulating standards is changing or undergoing changes. By reviewing standards or specifications recently issued by the United States, some European countries, and Japan, it can be found that the regulations and descriptions regarding detection methods and technologies are becoming increasingly abstract. Specific testing requirements are often given in detail, including errors or uncertainties, experimental conditions, and guarantees for test quality, but unlike previous standards, specific technologies and methods are not rigidly specified. Some standards give a wide range of testing methods, allowing testing departments to choose and determine which method to use. This means that as long as the experimental conditions are met, test quality is guaranteed, and the results fall within the specified error range, any method or instrument can be used. For example, in the EPA and OSHA standards related to indoor air quality in the United States, many options are listed for radon detection. Another example is in the material provided by the National Institute of Advanced Industrial Science and Technology Standard Material Information Center in Japan on July 24, 2002, where 170 selectable varieties are listed for air pollution detection methods, including electronic detection instruments based on electrochemical principles.

For the measurement of free formaldehyde, the Japanese Ministry of Construction conducted comparative experiments this year using portable electronic formaldehyde detectors (ESC) based on electrochemical principles and standard detection methods (absorption tube-colorimetry). There was no significant difference in the results, so it can be considered a recommendable new technology. On the webpage introducing indoor air quality and standards by the U.S. EPA, the PPM-400 formaldehyde analyzer is listed as a recommendable instrument for detecting formaldehyde levels in the air. In fact, in the "Hygiene Standards for Formaldehyde in Wood Panels" formulated by China's Ministry of Health, the formaldehyde automatic analyzer is also listed as a usable method, along with three other methods: AMHT spectrophotometry, phenol reagent spectrophotometry, and gas chromatography.

With the further improvement and enhancement of modern detection technologies and electronic automatic detection instruments, moving away from traditional time-consuming, labor-intensive, and expensive laboratory chemical analysis methods towards promoting and using portable on-site direct reading instrument analysis methods will gradually become possible. When revising standards domestically and internationally, listing on-site direct reading portable instrument measurement methods as one of the standard detection methods or recommended methods is just a matter of time.
