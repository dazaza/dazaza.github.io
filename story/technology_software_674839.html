A woman with a primary school education earns a self-taught law degree, and in one year reclaiming 100,000 yuan in salaries for migrant workers

by xue94fwsh on 2012-02-24 16:09:00

After the storm, everything returns to calm. After losing her job, Sister Chen had to "start anew" and look for work everywhere in order to make a living. With a rural household registration, elementary school education, and being of an age like "tofu dregs," not knowing Mandarin, and walking unsteadily, she asked herself, "With my conditions, do you think any big company would hire me?" Eventually, Sister Chen ended up as a cleaner at the Military District General Hospital, sweeping floors and toilets. Not long after, she moved to a restaurant near Jiqingmen to wash dishes.

There were 11 other coworkers who had the same experience as Chen Yiyi. "They all chose to accept their fate, but I didn't." So, Chen Yiyi spent her time running between various law firms, bookstores, and home in Nanjing, "I've been to every law firm in Nanjing, and read more than ten legal books." Through reading and consulting, Chen Yiyi not only figured out the ways to protect her rights, but also realized that she and her coworkers should receive economic compensation: "According to labor law, if the company terminates my employment contract without cause and against my will, they should give us economic compensation."

Chen Yiyi calculated for a long time using the formula and came up with how much money her coworkers were owed.

In one year, she went to court six times and helped migrant workers reclaim over 100,000 yuan of hard-earned money.

After quitting her job, Sister Chen relied on her husband's meager salary to get by. She bought several legal books from the bookstore and went to the Nanjing Collaborator Community Development Center every week to seek advice from teachers. She also continuously visited law firms, and this "coarse person" gradually found the way to learn about the law, becoming a legal consultation volunteer at Nanjing Collaborator.

Every time she received a request for help, regardless of whether she could help or not, she had to take leave and go personally. After two months, Sister Chen simply quit her job. "I was taking leave every few days, I felt sorry for my boss, and I even felt embarrassed to stay when he didn't fire me."

Gradually, Sister Chen gained a small reputation in the legal community, and whenever her coworkers encountered labor disputes, they would come looking for her. In 2011, Sister Chen handled six labor disputes involving 27 migrant workers, won four cases consecutively, and three migrant workers were waiting for the court trial, nine migrant workers were waiting for the labor arbitration committee's arbitration results, and seven elderly people were lost while looking for childhood playmates. Six lawsuits cumulatively reclaimed 101,858 yuan for twelve migrant workers, mostly in the form of economic compensation, overtime pay, and annual leave wages. Seeing everyone happily counting the money awarded by the court's judgment, she was delighted too.

She also set rules: "Unless it's a 'trusted person,' no one else is allowed near these two bags." Once, a party saw Sister Chen carrying two heavy bags and said, "I'm the party involved, I definitely won't lose them, let me carry them for you." Unexpectedly, Sister Chen frowned: "You're the party involved, I am more so than you. If you forget them on the bus, how are you going to find them again?"

Last week, in the sixth courtroom of the Jiangning District People's Court in Nanjing, 43-year-old Chen Yi once again faced off against the Nanjing White Elephant Food Company in court, acting as the agent for Yang Hewei, a former employee of the White Elephant Food Company. This was her sixth time in court; she had 27 cases in hand, but she did not have a lawyer's professional certificate. All her legal knowledge was self-taught. From 2010 to now, she has reclaimed over 100,000 yuan for migrant workers. Her name is Chen Yi, a laid-off female worker from Suqian City, Sihong County. Since October last year, due to a labor contract termination notice, her life changed—she quickly grew into a "lawyer sister" dedicated to handling labor dispute cases for migrant workers.

However, there is still a knot in Chen Yi's heart: Last year, two young migrant workers were dismissed by the company and couldn't get the compensation they deserved, leaving them with no choice but to drink pesticide. This incident, like a sharp thorn, deeply pierced her heart. "Now I feel that my legal knowledge is still lacking," Chen Yi said. She wants to recruit a legal volunteer to join her in protecting the rights of migrant workers. Those interested can contact "Dahai" at Nanjing Collaborator at 86516151.

Since getting involved with the law, she has become a true "legal enthusiast." For 365 days a year, she never leaves home without two eco-friendly bags. The red bag contains piles of case files and complaints; the blue bag holds her precious legal books, such as the "Labor Law of the People's Republic of China," "A Comprehensive Guide to Handling Labor Contract Disputes," "Typical Cases of Workers' Rights Protection"... On public buses, whenever the traffic light turns red, she immediately picks up her materials.

Although Sister Chen has stepped back from the "scene," the phones ringing non-stop daily with requests for help continue. Her fellow workers, hearing about Chen Yi's story, were greatly inspired. Word spread far and wide, making Sister Chen famous among the enterprises where she worked. "Sister, I work overtime every day until my waist hurts, the boss doesn't pay me and won't let me take leave, what should I do?" "Sister, I can't stand it anymore..." The callers come from various backgrounds: some were close colleagues on the same production line, others were acquaintances met only a few times, and still others were introduced through previous colleagues.

Intern Wang Yu, reporter Xu Yuanyuan

Originally directionally challenged, she became a "living map" of Nanjing for the sake of "cases"

"We'll give you 8,000 yuan, don't sue, okay?" Seeing Sister Chen playing it straight, the company's lawyer couldn't hold up. Chen Yi shook her head, "I worked four years, 8,000 yuan is almost enough for me, but others who worked six or seven years can only get 6,000 yuan at most, I feel it's not worth it for them." On January 18, 2011, Sister Chen led 12 brothers and sisters as their representative, finally using the law to protect their rights. After mediation by the Jiangning District Labor Security Bureau, everyone received their due compensation, with one colleague receiving over ten thousand yuan.

This victory made Sister Chen famous overnight among the common people.

After becoming a "lawyer," another change in her life was that her shoes wore out more frequently. Previously, a pair of sandals in summer and one pair of shoes for spring, autumn, and winter sufficed, but now she needs at least one pair per season.

Sister Chen used to be directionally challenged, having no sense of north, south, east, or west. However, by memorizing landmarks and asking passersby, she unknowingly ran all over Nanjing and became a genuine "living map." Now, this "directionally challenged" person who has "escaped poverty," can find her way just by instinct. But with at least four days a week spent outside dealing with "cases," how much transportation costs would that incur? When asked, Sister Chen waved her hand and smiled broadly, showing her round face: "I ride the bus for free, see, I have a disability certificate."

In 2010, Nanjing Bai Xiang Food Co., Ltd., where Chen Yi worked, issued her a labor contract termination notice. Clearly stated on the contract was her employment period from October 2006 to October 2011. However, someone added a semi-circle to the second "1" in 2011, changing her employment period to end in October 2010. "My legs are already disabled, I rely on this job to earn tuition for my daughter." Chen Yi was very sad and felt wronged.
