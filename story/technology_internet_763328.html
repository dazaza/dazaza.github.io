Will Google Glass erode our wisdom in the future?

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 18:34:57

There is no denying the disruptive experience offered by Google Glass. In comparison to other information devices that maintain a certain distance when in use - such as smartphones, computers, and books - Google Glass provides an immersive, all-encompassing information experience. Without question, it is both convenient and sophisticated compared to input operations on a smartphone.

Humans perceive most information through their five senses. Google Glass closely engages with vision and hearing, and perhaps in the future, even smell. As the saying goes: "Hearing is believing less than seeing." Google Glass partially takes over these two senses, exerting significant influence on these primary modes of perception. This transcends the product's current ability to serve merely as a tool.

When observing the world through Google Glass, you gain two helpful assistants: one that can slip you notes right in front of your eyes, and another that whispers into your ear. The issue lies in the fact that the source of information for these assistants is Google, and their mode of thinking is provided by Google's algorithms. Google Glass is a close companion styled after Google, but its intimacy surpasses the user experience of a smartphone. This could lead users to gradually become "Googlized," forming dependencies on this method of interaction. This is a potential problem in using Google Glass, as the provision of information is too intimate, and the quality of the information is also good. Therefore, if some viewpoints are consciously mixed in, most users would not notice. However, the power of this form of dissemination is stronger than that of Weibo or WeChat.

Yet, Google may lack the ability to regulate the quality and purpose of this information. Or, does Google itself truly do no evil?

At the same time, because Google Glass is an information instrument closely connected to the user's body, undoubtedly, if Google Glass is hacked or receives higher-level instructions, the erosion of privacy it could cause would be unprecedented. Everything in your life and work could be observed. The convenience of Google Glass's camera isn't just advantageous for the user; it's equally beneficial for observers from afar. With the recent revelations of the Prism scandal in the U.S., we know our phones and computers aren't secure. But there isn't a camera constantly watching us, so most people can still accept this. Google Glass could make this a reality.

Google Glass significantly impacts the learning process. Learning is a cycle of cognition, thought, feedback, and re-cognition. Google Glass can provide answers to most questions, far exceeding "Ten Thousand Whys". However, for children, learning isn't just about getting answers; it’s also about the methods and processes involved and recognizing the world. If you take a child to the mountaintop to observe the vast starry sky, the direct observation through the naked eye has a more profound impact on the soul than simply knowing every constellation. Too many easy answers erode curiosity and respect for the unknown. Google Glass allows people to see billions of galaxies, yet paradoxically makes them more shortsighted than ever.

In this era, information is incredibly abundant, offering an unprecedented experience. Google Glass is undoubtedly a faster way to access information, but more information doesn't equate to more happiness. A single smartphone has already made most people look down at their screens. People have little resistance against the complex influx of information. As previously joked, in the future, Google Glass might allow you to see the price and purchase link of every piece of clothing your date wears, along with their basic social network information. In the mutual scanning between two pairs of Google Glasses, what is missing is romantic imagination and tender gazes. Though physically close, it feels like being miles apart. Smart glasses replace human insight, and cold information replaces the warmth of the heart.

Google Glass can serve as a professional production tool, but it's not suitable for mass-market use. What suits the mass market are R2D2s and WALL-Es. Machines should be companions to humans, not parts of them.

We stand at the crossroads of technology and humanity, but simultaneously, we stand at the crossroads of human nature and machines. We still possess great imagination, boundless ambition in the face of nature, and steadfast adherence to human value.
