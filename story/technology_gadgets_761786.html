Is Asha501 Nokia's Plan B?

by geekzhang on 2013-05-10 11:11:52

This afternoon, Nokia released its first Asha-brand smartphone, the Nokia 501, in India and announced that Asha will become a smart system platform. Nokia will provide an open, standard development environment for this platform to create a complete ecosystem for the Asha platform. What is Nokia's intention with this move?To capture the low-end smartphone marketBefore the release of the Nokia 501, the industry generally believed that the 501 was just a feature phone, meaning Nokia's market strategy was simply to dominate the low-end feature phone market. Now, Nokia has defined Asha as a smartphone platform and is building a complete ecosystem for it, clearly targeting the low-end smartphone market. This can be seen from the origins of the Asha smart platform.The Asha platform is based on Smarterphone, which Nokia acquired in 2012, a Norwegian feature phone operating system developer. Smarterphone targets low-cost phones with slower processor speeds and less advanced screens. This system can enhance the performance of feature phones, allowing low-cost feature phones to run more graphical interfaces and provide services similar to AppStore.From the above introduction, we can see that the characteristics of the Asha platform are the ability to achieve smartphone functions with lower hardware costs. From a market perspective, it provides smartphone user experience at feature phone prices. From this point of view, Asha smartphones have a greater price advantage compared to low-end Android smartphones.In recent years, Samsung, Motorola, and Sony have all abandoned the feature phone market, essentially giving up the mobile phone market below 600 yuan. Nokia has consistently occupied this market with S30 and S40 platform feature phones. Now, Nokia is launching Asha smartphones with more added value at feature phone prices, which undoubtedly stabilizes the existing low-end feature phone market, gradually converting feature phone users into low-end smartphone users, and further expanding market share through cost-performance ratio and a gradually improving platform ecosystem.Nokia's Plan B?Although Nokia has always stated that it will stick to the Windows Phone platform, there is some subtext in creating a system platform that it can control. At least objectively, Nokia achieves a two-for-one effect.Regarding the Asha platform, the Asha strategy has a bit of the flavor of encircling the cities from the countryside. While other mobile manufacturers are fiercely competing in the mid-to-high-end market, the Asha strategy takes a different path, focusing on the low-end smartphone market. With Nokia's reputation in the low-end feature phone market and the high cost-performance ratio of Asha phones, the possibility of Asha platform success is quite high. Once Asha has enough users and a complete ecosystem, Nokia will inevitably begin the high-end road for Asha. If things go smoothly, a new Symbian could be born. Nokia would then become another mobile giant after Apple, fully controlling the system platform, application ecosystem, and terminal manufacturing. Such advantages would give Nokia more autonomy in terminal product design and manufacturing and user experience, making Nokia's revival not impossible.For the WP platform, the existence of Asha is beneficial to the development of the WP system and makes Nokia's risks more controllable. Since committing to the WP platform, Nokia has been relatively proactive, but Microsoft has been a bit too relaxed. The global market share progress of the WP system has been slow, one important reason being that Microsoft hasn't been active enough, whether at the WP system platform level or the marketing level. Nokia's launch of the Asha platform can exert some pressure on Microsoft. Moreover, even if the WP platform fails, Nokia will have more buffer space. However, judging from the current situation, the possibility of Nokia achieving dual-platform growth in both WP and Asha in the future is greater.
