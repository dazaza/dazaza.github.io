Chinese students spark controversy by giving RMB to foreign children during an Olympiad math contest

by xue94fwsh on 2012-03-02 18:36:37

The 2012 World Olympiad Mathematics Competition was recently held in Canterbury, UK. Chinese contestants won 10 of the 16 global gold medals, a feat that made everyone proud. However, one detail after the competition left many netizens feeling "uncomfortable." It was reported that when foreign and Chinese students exchanged gifts, foreign children presented carefully prepared water bottles and pens, but the Chinese children had not prepared anything and could only hastily give RMB to the foreign children. "Why do the Chinese children who excel at solving problems and come from a country known as the 'Land of礼仪 (Courtesy)' only know how to give money?" Netizens expressed their astonishment...

News Recap: Winning the Contest But Losing on Etiquette

According to reports by the Wuhan Evening News, during the 2012 World Olympiad Mathematics Competition held in Canterbury, UK, Chinese contestants won 10 out of the 16 global gold medals. In response, British media commented that Chinese people have a natural talent for math olympiads. However, Guo Hongbing, the head coach of China's Math Olympiad team, mentioned some shortcomings of the participants during a press conference the day before. When foreign and Chinese students exchanged gifts, foreign children gave carefully prepared items like water bottles and pens, while the Chinese children had not prepared anything and could only hastily give RMB to the foreign children. During the team event, compared to the foreign children who worked together with clear division of labor, the Chinese children's team actually started arguing. Another report indicated that Wu Lintong, one of the Chinese contestants from Xiamen Shuangshi Middle School, said during an interview: "When countries exchanged gifts, we realized we were unprepared and had overlooked this aspect." Fortunately, some foreign children had the habit of collecting coins, so quite a few Chinese contestants used RMB as gifts. Wu Lintong also gave away more than 60 RMB, equivalent to his pocket money. Meanwhile, the gifts he received included cards, backpacks, pendants, and exquisite water bottles.

Netizen Discussion: Adults' Lack of Etiquette Made Children Awkward

Using RMB as a gift became a hot topic on Sina Weibo, where many netizens felt that compared to the rich variety of gifts from foreign children, "the RMB from Chinese children seemed somewhat unexpected and lacked warmth." Some netizens criticized, saying that the pursuit of competition results directly caused us to forget about etiquette: "We came here just for winning or losing; things outside the competition such as etiquette were probably never considered." Others believed that the awkwardness of the children was due to the adults' oversight: "If the leaders had been more thoughtful and understood the procedures and details of such international events beforehand, the children wouldn't have been so at a loss, having to resort to giving money. The key issue lies in the lack of etiquette education and knowledge, how can we expect children to be 'proper' when adults themselves are 'impolite'?"

Coach’s Response: RMB is Also a Souvenir!

Yesterday, reporters contacted Guo Hongbing, the head coach of China's Math Olympiad team regarding this matter. He thought it wasn't a big deal, "Just like when we travel, we consider local currency as a souvenir; RMB is also a souvenir." Coach Guo explained that from the children's perspective, they were considering the commemorative value of RMB, which is a normal way of reciprocating. As for the netizens' criticism of "giving money being vulgar," this is clearly something the adults have overthought. Regarding whether the team knew there would be a "gift exchange" segment before the competition and if such segments existed in previous competitions, Coach Guo's response was vague: "I'm not entirely clear about the specific situation; this form is relatively novel." Coach Guo also mentioned that only a small number of children used money as gifts, while some Chinese children had prepared gifts like yo-yos. During the interview, Coach Guo repeatedly emphasized the impressive achievements of the Chinese team and stated that after this awkward incident, attention will be paid to cultivating the children's "comprehensive abilities."

Chengdu Evening News reporter Liu Yi
