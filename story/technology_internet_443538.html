1. Peter Principle

Every organization is composed of various arrangements of positions, ranks, or classes, and each person belongs to one of these ranks. The Peter Principle was derived by American scholar Lawrence Peter after studying the phenomenon of employee promotions within organizations. His conclusion was that in all types of organizations, employees tend to be promoted to their level of incompetence. Sometimes referred to as the "upward climb" principle, this phenomenon is ubiquitous in real life: a competent teacher becomes a university president but proves incapable; an excellent athlete becomes a sports official but accomplishes little. For an organization, once a significant portion of its personnel are promoted beyond their competence, it leads to organizational inefficiency, reduced productivity, mediocrity rising to the top, and stagnation. Therefore, it requires a change from the simplistic promotion mechanism based solely on contributions, meaning that just because someone excels in a particular position does not mean they can automatically handle a higher-level role. Promoting an employee to a position where they cannot perform well not only fails to reward them appropriately but also hinders their potential and harms the company.

2. Law of Wine and Sewage

The Law of Wine and Sewage states that pouring a spoonful of wine into a bucket of sewage results in a bucket of sewage; similarly, pouring a spoonful of sewage into a bucket of wine still results in a bucket of sewage. In any organization, there are usually a few difficult individuals whose sole purpose seems to be creating chaos. Worse yet, they are like rotten apples in a fruit box; if not dealt with promptly, they will quickly infect and spoil the other apples. The terrifying aspect of a rotten apple lies in its destructive power. A decent and capable person entering a chaotic department might get drowned out, while an incompetent individual can quickly turn an efficient department into one filled with disarray. Organizational systems are often fragile, built on mutual understanding, compromise, and tolerance, making them easy to damage or poison. Another reason why destroyers are so effective is that destruction is always easier than construction. A skilled craftsman spends days carefully crafting a ceramic piece, which a donkey can destroy in a second. If your organization has such a "donkey," no matter how many skilled craftsmen you have, there won't be much meaningful work accomplished. If your organization has such a "donkey," you should immediately remove it, or if you can't do that, at least restrain it.

3. Bucket Theory

Bucket Theory suggests that the amount of water a bucket can hold depends entirely on its shortest plank. This means that any organization may face a common issue: the components forming the organization often vary in quality, and the weakest component often determines the overall level of the organization. Unlike the Law of Wine and Sewage, which discusses destructive forces within an organization, the shortest plank is still an effective part of the organization, just less capable than others. You cannot discard them as rotten apples. Strength and weakness are relative; they cannot be eliminated. The question is to what extent you tolerate such weaknesses. If they become serious enough to become bottlenecks hindering work, then action must be taken.

4. Matthew Effect

In the New Testament's Gospel of Matthew, there is a story: before a king goes on a long journey, he gives each of his three servants a silver coin, instructing them to trade with it until he returns. When the king returns, the first servant says, "Master, the silver coin you gave me has earned ten more." So, the king rewards him with ten cities. The second servant reports, "Master, the silver coin you gave me has earned five more." So, the king rewards him with five cities. The third servant reports, "Master, I wrapped your silver coin in a handkerchief and never used it for fear of losing it." Then, the king orders the third servant's silver coin to be given to the first servant, saying, "To everyone who has, more will be given, but from the one who has nothing, even what he has will be taken away." This is the Matthew Effect, reflecting a widespread phenomenon in today’s society, namely, the winner takes all. For business growth, the Matthew Effect tells us that to maintain an advantage in a certain field, we must quickly grow larger in that area. When you become the leader in a certain field, even with the same investment return rate, you can more easily achieve greater profits than weaker competitors. If you lack the strength to rapidly grow large in a specific field, you must continuously seek new areas of growth to ensure better returns.

5. Zero-Sum Game Theory

Zero-sum game refers to a game where players either win or lose, and one player's win is exactly the other's loss, resulting in a total outcome that always equals zero. The reason why the zero-sum game theory attracts so much attention is mainly because people see situations similar to zero-sum games in various aspects of society. Behind the glory of winners, there is often the sorrow and bitterness of losers. In the 20th century, humanity experienced two world wars, rapid economic growth, technological advancement, globalization, and increasingly severe environmental pollution. The zero-sum game concept is gradually being replaced by the win-win concept. People begin to realize that benefiting oneself doesn't necessarily come at the expense of others. Through effective cooperation, a satisfying outcome for all parties is possible. Transitioning from a zero-sum game to a win-win situation requires genuine cooperation and courage from all parties involved. Avoid small tricks and trying to take small advantages over others. Follow the rules of the game; otherwise, a win-win situation cannot occur, and ultimately, the cooperating party will suffer losses.

6. Washington Cooperation Rule

The Washington Cooperation Rule states that one person will act hastily, two people will blame each other, and three people will never accomplish anything. It somewhat resembles our story of the three monks. Human cooperation is far more complex and subtle than simply adding manpower. In this type of cooperation, assuming each person's ability is 1, the result of 10 people working together can sometimes be much greater than 10, and sometimes even less than 1. This is because humans are not static objects but more like energy moving in different directions. When they push each other, they naturally achieve twice the result with half the effort, and when they conflict, they accomplish nothing. In our traditional management practices, research on cooperation has been minimal. The most direct reflection is that most current management systems and actions focus on reducing unnecessary manpower consumption rather than using organizations to improve efficiency. In other words, the main goal of management is not to make each individual better but to avoid excessive internal strife.

7. Watch Rule

The Watch Rule states that when a person has one watch, they know what time it is, but when they have two watches, they cannot determine the correct time. Two watches cannot tell a person a more accurate time; instead, they cause the wearer to lose confidence in the correct time. In terms of enterprise management, the Watch Rule gives us a very intuitive insight: managing one person or one organization cannot involve using two different methods simultaneously, setting two different goals at the same time, or having each person directed by two people simultaneously. Otherwise, it will leave the enterprise or individual in a state of indecision. Another layer of meaning in the Watch Rule is that each person cannot choose two different value systems at the same time, otherwise, their behavior will fall into chaos.

8. Not Worthwhile Law

The most straightforward expression of the Not Worthwhile Law is: things not worth doing are not worth doing well. This law is simple but its importance is often overlooked or forgotten. The Not Worthwhile Law reflects a psychology where if a person considers a task unworthy, they often adopt a cold, indifferent attitude, complete it perfunctorily, with low success rates, and even if successful, they feel little sense of accomplishment. Therefore, for an individual, one should select a struggle goal and value system from multiple options and strive for it. Choose what you love and love what you choose to ignite our fighting spirit and allow us to feel justified. For a company or organization, it is essential to analyze employees' personality traits and allocate work reasonably. For example, assign risky and challenging tasks to employees with strong achievement desires and give timely affirmation and praise upon completion; let employees with high dependency participate more in group activities; assign employees with strong power desires to roles as supervisors matching their abilities. Simultaneously, enhance employees' identification with corporate goals, making them feel their work is worthwhile, thereby igniting employees' enthusiasm.

9. Mushroom Management

Mushroom Management is a management method adopted by many organizations towards newcomers. Beginners are placed in dark corners (unimportant departments or menial tasks), drenched with criticism and blame (endless criticism, scolding, and scapegoating), and left to fend for themselves (without necessary guidance and promotion). Many people have likely gone through such a mushroom phase, which isn't necessarily bad, especially at the beginning. Spending some time in this phase can eliminate many unrealistic fantasies, bringing us closer to reality and making us view problems more realistically. Generally, organizations treat new employees differently, with little difference in starting salaries or jobs. Regardless of how excellent you are, at the beginning, you can only start with the simplest tasks. The mushroom experience is like a cocoon, a necessary step before becoming a butterfly. Therefore, efficiently navigating this period, learning lessons, maturing, and establishing a trustworthy personal image is a crucial topic for every young person entering society.

10. Occam's Razor

In the 13th century, William of Ockham advocated nominalism, acknowledging only things that truly exist and considering abstract universal concepts as useless burdens that should be ruthlessly removed. He proposed that entities should not be multiplied unnecessarily. This is commonly known as Occam's Razor. This razor has threatened many people, considered heretical, and caused harm to William himself. However, it has not dulled the sharpness of the razor. Over hundreds of years, Occam's Razor has become sharper and transcended its original narrow scope, gaining broad, rich, and profound significance. In enterprise management, Occam's Razor can further evolve into the simplicity and complexity law: making things complex is simple, but making things simple is complex. This law requires that when dealing with matters, we grasp the essential nature of issues, focus on the mainstream, solve fundamental problems, adapt naturally, and not artificially complicate things, thereby handling matters effectively.
