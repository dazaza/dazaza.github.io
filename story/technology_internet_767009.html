Why didn't Renren become China's Facebook?

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 11:08:08

　　Those who have been to university all know one website - Renren. When they first enter the school gate, many people's first thing after paying tuition fees is to register on Renren as advised by their seniors. Due to its campus origin and complete real-name system, Renren was once called China's Facebook. In the private market, it was also highly sought after, with investors valuing it at more than twice the multiple of Facebook.

　　However, since its IPO in 2011, Renren's stock price on the NYSE has fallen by four-fifths. What happened to Renren?

　　HSBC analyst Chi Tsang believes: "Renren was indeed China's Facebook at first, but its problem lies in not evolving." Although Facebook and Google have not entered China, Chi Tsang points out: "The Chinese market is a very competitive and very active market, which people rarely feel."

　　Recently, Renren closed down the once-popular "Happy Farm," marking another milestone in its decline. At its peak, "Happy Farm" had billions of users, but now user interest has waned. Analysts believe that Renren's downfall is mainly due to its slow response to the smartphone and mobile internet wave.

　　Macquarie analyst Jiong Shao said: "In the social networking segment market in China, Renren used to be the leader, but the problem is that China does not need a PC-based social network." The Chinese are moving away from desktops faster than Europe and the US, and Chinese smartphone manufacturers have rapidly lowered the cost of entry-level smartphones. As a result, all desktop internet traffic in China drops by 15% annually, while the number of smartphones increases by 20% each year.

　　Renren previously stated that by June, there were 54 million monthly active users, compared to 45 million during the same period last year. Among users accessing the site, 80% partially use mobile phones and tablets to log in, but it is difficult to monetize this part of the traffic.

　　Renren predicted that revenue for the next quarter would decrease by 3-7% year-on-year; lower than analysts' estimates. Recently, the stock price rose due to the sale of its group-buying department Nuomi, and Renren is currently trading at $3.38, far below its IPO price of $14.

　　Duncan Clark, chairman of Beijing technology advisory firm BDA, believes: "Most of Renren's stock valuation comes from cash balances." In contrast, those competitors of Renren who developed faster in mobile now see rising stock prices. 40% of Facebook's revenue comes from mobile, and its stock price recently hit a new high.

　　Renren CEO Charles Chen stated that the group sees little interest from advertisers in purchasing mobile ads, but the company is developing mobile games, believing that mobile games "are a huge undeveloped but highly fragmented market."

　　Renren CMO Johnson Jiang said that last year, Renren spent a year developing mobile applications and is now actively promoting to advertisers, who are still novices in mobile marketing. He also mentioned that operating a real-name social network in China is more challenging than an anonymous one, but Renren's focus is on students, making its user base more stable compared to sites with broader audiences. Johnson Jiang said: "In China, older white-collar workers are not loyal users. We cannot know what the next trend in internet form will be, but it will attract these people to join."
