Microsoft CEO Ballmer Talks About the Beginning and End of His Resignation

by anonymous on 2013-11-25 11:32:58

On a foggy morning in January, Steve Ballmer paced in his corner office at Microsoft Corp.'s headquarters, listening to a board call through a speakerphone. This teleconference marked the beginning of the end for Ballmer's 13-year tenure as CEO.

Despite Microsoft's awe-inspiring software business revenue, it lagged behind Apple Inc. and Google Inc. in crucial consumer markets. Ballmer had attempted to present his plan to reshape Microsoft, but one director interrupted him, telling Ballmer that he was moving too slowly.

Chief Director John Thompson said he told Ballmer, "Hey, let's get going, we're in limbo." Ballmer responded by saying he could move faster.

This contentious call left Ballmer in a difficult position, ultimately leading him to announce his retirement in August, causing further turmoil at Microsoft as it began searching for someone to replace the executive who had been with the company for 33 years.

At 57, Ballmer said, "Maybe I'm a symbol of the old era, and I have to leave." His eyes slightly moistened as he paused, then continued, "Even though I love everything about my job, the best way for Microsoft to enter a new era is to bring in a new leader to accelerate reform."

In a series of exclusive interviews, Ballmer explained how he realized he could no longer lead Microsoft—essentially, that Microsoft couldn't continue under his leadership because the corporate culture he helped create was part of the problem. In these interviews, he displayed his signature roaring style and deep attachment to the company.

Ballmer and the board agreed: if Microsoft wants to achieve future profit growth and reduce its reliance on the increasingly dim personal computer market, it must restructure its management while maintaining its strong software business and refocus on mobile devices and online services.

The board's dissatisfaction lay in the speed. Thompson said the directors didn't force Ballmer to resign but pushed him hard to act faster. Thompson, who has long been a technical executive at Microsoft and currently leads the committee searching for a new CEO, emphasized this point.

Investors were also pushing Microsoft for reform. Brent Thill, an analyst at UBS AG who has studied Microsoft extensively, said, "At this critical juncture, Wall Street wants fresh blood to bring fundamental reforms to Microsoft. Ballmer is an extraordinary leader who brought great profits and market share to Microsoft in the commercial sector, but the new CEO must innovate in areas where Ballmer fell short—mobile phones, tablets, internet services, and even wearable products."

According to informed sources, potential successors on Microsoft's board list include former Nokia CEO Stephen Elop, Microsoft enterprise software head Satya Nadella, and Ford Motor Company CEO Alan Mulally. Sources revealed that when Microsoft holds its annual shareholders' meeting on November 19th, the board plans to convene and discuss candidates.

Representatives of Elop and Nadella said they would not comment on Microsoft's CEO search. A Ford spokesperson stated that since November 2012 (when Ford announced Mulally's term would last at least until 2014), "nothing has changed," and the company added that Mulally remains fully focused on continuing the 'One Ford' plan. "We don't engage in speculation."

Microsoft's next CEO will be only the third in the company's history. In 1980, upon the recommendation of his Harvard University classmate and Microsoft co-founder Bill Gates, Ballmer joined Microsoft. He is the second-largest individual shareholder and a billionaire.

Ballmer grew up in Detroit, where his father worked as a manager at Ford. At Harvard, he lived on the same floor as Gates. He abandoned his MBA course at Stanford to become Microsoft's first business manager.

He was Gates' right-hand man, helping turn Microsoft into a giant that transformed how people use computers. After becoming CEO in 2000, he further solidified Microsoft's position in the software market and maintained rapid profit growth. During Ballmer's tenure, in the year ending June, Microsoft's revenue tripled to nearly $78 billion, and profits increased by 132% to nearly $22 billion.

However, while Microsoft generated substantial profits from its traditional markets, it missed transformative changes such as network search advertising and the shift of consumers towards mobile devices and social media.

Last year, Ballmer sought to transform the business. In a letter to shareholders in October, he announced that Microsoft would become a "devices and services" provider for both businesses and individuals.

He told the board that he hoped to continue leading the company until four years later when his youngest son graduated from high school. He started his succession plan by holding what he called "secret meetings" with potential candidates.

Ballmer's transformation plan required a complete overhaul of the company. He called an old friend, former Boeing Co. executive and current Ford CEO Alan Mulally, for advice. The two met at a Starbucks near Mercer Island outside Seattle on Christmas Eve.

Ballmer carried a satchel filled with a pile of phones and tablets from Microsoft and its competitors, placing them on the table. He asked Mulally how he turned Ford around. Mulally spent four hours detailing how teamwork and simplifying the Ford brand enabled its repositioning.

A Ford spokesperson said, "Ford and Microsoft have a long-standing business partnership, and many of our leaders frequently discuss business issues."

For Ballmer, this was like a wake-up call. He operated the software giant with high visibility and admitted, "I'm big, bald, and loud."

Microsoft's culture included a company "arena" where colleagues competed against each other. This competitive environment promoted innovation during Microsoft's peak period, but now sometimes caused teams to focus on their own reputation and profits rather than the overall technology industry or the entire Microsoft.

He recalled thinking, "I'll redraw the entire plan and rebrand the entire company."

The Microsoft board liked his new plan. But as Ballmer prepared to implement it, board members demanded faster progress during a January teleconference.

Those involved in the meeting said the most pressure came from Thompson, who had served as an executive at International Business Machines Corp. (IBM) and Symantec Corp., and Seagate Technology CEO Stephen Luczo. Luczo declined to comment.

Ballmer said he told the board members during the teleconference that he wanted to proceed after the launch of the next generation of Windows. He explained that the reason he didn't act faster in the second half of 2012 was because he focused on launching the next generation of Windows in October, which had always been Microsoft's cash cow.

Ballmer then began accelerating, drafting a management restructuring plan to discuss at an event held at a mountain resort in Washington in March. He invited Thompson and another director to understand the board's opinion on his plan.

However, he faced greater pressure. Thompson said he told Ballmer and his executives, "Either act fast or abandon the plan."

Ballmer said he viewed this as recognition of their plan. That night, some of them gathered by the fireplace to play cards and drink Scotch whisky.

The following month, hedge fund ValueAct Capital disclosed a $2 billion stake in Microsoft. ValueAct CEO Jeffrey Ubben said at a meeting that Microsoft's stock was undervalued. Other shareholders urged Microsoft to increase dividends and divest non-core businesses. A ValueAct spokesperson declined to make further comments. In September, Microsoft increased dividends but did not divest businesses like the Bing search engine as investors requested.

Ballmer adopted Mulally's suggestions. For years, he consulted individually with department heads at Microsoft, usually issuing action orders. Now, he began inviting these heads to sit in a circle in his office to foster camaraderie.

This was a sudden cultural change within the company. Microsoft Corporate Vice President Satya Nadella said, "This approach is completely different from how we operated during the more than 30 years Ballmer led Microsoft."

Ballmer said his executive team resisted this new way initially. Some executives resisted on major issues (such as merging engineering teams) and minor ones (like weekly status reports).

Microsoft Executive Vice President Qi Lu once submitted a 56-page report on applications and services, but Ballmer sent it back, insisting that Lu compress it to three pages—a part of the new requirement aimed at encouraging a simpler style to promote collaboration. Lu said he argued back, saying, "But you always want data and details."

Ballmer said he began realizing that he had trained managers to focus on trees instead of the entire forest, and many didn't take his new requirements seriously.

In May, he began considering whether he could keep up with the board's demands for speed. He said, "No matter how fast I hope to change, there will be people in employees, directors, investors, partners, suppliers, and customers who won't believe I'm serious, maybe even myself."

His life took a turning point on a street in London. While traveling in the UK in May, after a morning jog, he wandered for a few minutes, a rare leisure time in recent months. He began thinking for the first time that Microsoft might change more quickly without him.

He said, "We eventually need to break a pattern, and I am a pattern."

Ballmer began privately drafting his retirement letter, eventually writing about 40 versions, ranging from sentimental to radical.

When flying out of Europe at the end of May, he told Microsoft's chief legal officer Brad Smith, "It might be time for me to go." The next day, Ballmer informed Thompson by phone.

Thompson called two other directors, Luczo and Charles Noski, telling them, "If Steve leaves, can we continue?"

At a director's meeting held in Bellevue, Washington, in June, Ballmer told the directors, "Although I am willing to work here for a few more years, it makes no sense for someone else to interfere while I am transforming the company."

Considering the conversations between directors and Ballmer, Noski said the board wasn't surprised or shocked. Thompson said he and others believed that fresh blood might accelerate Microsoft's current transformation.

People familiar with Chairman Bill Gates' thoughts said Gates had told Ballmer that based on experience, he understood how hard it was to leave when Microsoft had become his life. Gates told the board that if Ballmer decided to leave, Microsoft would still succeed, and he supported Ballmer's decision.

At his son's high school graduation ceremony, his son sang a Coldplay song with lyrics roughly translating to: "It's so sad we're breaking up; nobody said it'd be easy; nobody ever said it'd be this hard." That evening, after watching his child perform, he told his wife and three sons that he might leave Microsoft, and they all cried.

On August 21st, the board held a teleconference to accept Ballmer's resignation decision. Gates and Thompson sat in Ballmer's office. The meeting lasted less than an hour.

Ballmer vowed not to become irrelevant.

He remained active, leading Microsoft to acquire Nokia's phone business for $7.5 billion, making minor adjustments to the holiday sales strategy for Microsoft's Surface tablet and the new Xbox gaming console. In October, Microsoft reported better-than-expected quarterly results.

At his last annual employee conference in September, Ballmer ended his speech to the tune of the Dirty Dancing theme song, "The Time of My Life."

Last month, while walking by Lake Washington, Ballmer met Pete Carroll, the coach of the Seattle Seahawks, who had been fired by a previous owner but whose career was now thriving. Carroll said he told Ballmer that he had gone through similar phases and expected things to improve in the future.

Ballmer said he was considering informal invitations, including being a university professor and coaching his youngest son's high school basketball team. He had no major decisions for the next six months, except that he wouldn't run another large company. He expressed openness to continuing as a Microsoft director.

At a recent executive meeting, he sat in his seat assessing the company's progress. On his third slide, it read: "New CEO."

He said, "Everyone in this room believes we need to complete the transformation." As he stood up, his voice rose higher: "I hope to continue as CEO; I still hold a large amount of Microsoft stock, and I will continue to hold them."

He walked back to his seat and turned around, whispering softly: "Please take care of Microsoft."
