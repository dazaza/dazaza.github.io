Some related and random thoughts: Labels: Wuxia,起点 (Qidian), writer, Hongxiu, imitation Shanda Legend private server. I played a Legend private server for two days and even paused updating my BLOG. Although I often joke with friends who open new private servers about resisting all "cabbage" games, my spirit hasn't been good these past two days. Occasionally playing some brainless private servers to relax is fine, just like how nobles who are used to eating delicacies might find coarse tea and simple meals delicious once in a while. Haha, this analogy may seem a bit pretentious. In reality, we're just common folk, and a half-broken computer is enough for our Legend SF!

Finally, I got bored at night! I went to play a round of Chenghai 3C on Hao, then read the updates of "Falling in Love with Xuanwu" on Hongxiu from the past two days. There were two chapters already, but unfortunately, due to my habitually fast reading speed, I couldn't find the link to the next chapter after finishing, which left me a little unhappy.

When I was young, influenced by my older sister, I started reading novels very early. Following the convention where boys read wuxia and girls read romance, I started with a wuxia novel. I still remember the name of that book; it was called "Wu Feng Chaoyang Dao." It was around the time when I was in third grade, about eight or nine years old. The school had just taught us how to write diaries, so when I encountered unfamiliar characters, I simply skipped over large sections. As I grew older, I read more varied books, maintaining my interest in wuxia series, but found fewer and fewer books with substantial content. Despite many authors claiming titles such as PhD or professor, whether they were locally grown or overseas educated, their works were truly unbearable. The male protagonists were invariably handsome, elegant, naturally talented in martial arts, gained extraordinary power after falling off cliffs, could kill several experts with a single palm strike, and kick to death numerous strong men with a foot. They laughed and scolded, seemingly making the vast China revolve solely around them. The female protagonists were invariably beautiful and gentle, sometimes even seven or eight of them, occasionally inserting some H scenes. TMD, even boar mating doesn't need to be that way. Wuxia, wuxia, there's wu (martial) but no xia (chivalry). Actually, I admire Mr. Cha the most. His definition of xia - for the people and the country - stands out. Some claim that xia is freedom, but compared to Mr. Cha, their realm is clearly lower. Moreover, most authors have chaotic language, numerous grammatical errors, and lack literary talent. Therefore, I stopped reading so-called wuxia works. Expanding further, much of the current so-called literature either consists of young upstarts writing nonsense claiming to be the latest works of pleasure writers, or some writers cursing at others' noses. Our so-called cultural elites, whose miracles are often undone by cheats. Even my beloved swordsmen stories couldn't escape becoming cruder than the farmers whom Confucius looked down upon. Thus, the reading habit I cultivated over seven or eight years has been abandoned.

Last year, out of sheer boredom, I visited Hongxiu and came across "Falling in Love with Xuanwu," which I've been following ever since. Simply put, it's genuinely original. Although the title is a bit cliché, I've read books on Qidian before, even as a high-level VIP member. However, Qidian's books are too varied, with both good and crude ones. While Hongxiu has fewer books, slower updates, and less popularity, it has more quality books. Of course, "Falling in Love with Xuanwu" is one of the few new Legend private servers I've kept up with. Before now, I didn't have the habit of waiting for updates daily. Generally, if I don't read an update for two days, I lose interest.

Nowadays, I feel that China's cultural scene is utterly chaotic. It seems as if Chinese people, constrained by feudal morals for thousands of years, suddenly gained a bit of freedom and forgot who they are! Wanting to discard everything from the past. While South Koreans still use Confucius as a textbook, we Chinese only know Han Han and Guo Jingming. Foreigners are learning about Spring Festival, but we Chinese only celebrate Christmas. The world praises China as a land of etiquette, yet Chinese people don't even know how to give up seats on buses anymore. Some so-called writers deliberately curse in the literary circle, and I really don't understand how they stir things up!

Tragic!
