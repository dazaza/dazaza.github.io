In the past, we had a mistaken understanding that if there was a central enterprise which invested in other companies, forming wholly-owned, controlling or participating stakes, creating a pyramid of capital structure, and the investors managed the various subsidiaries for compliance, then it would be considered a group company.

But today, we see that the above understanding is only superficial. Essentially, any group company involves an overall design of the relationship between the headquarters and the various subsidiaries, operating in a collaborative manner akin to "group fighting". Internally, it relies on planned economy, externally it engages in market competition. The stronger the internal planning and regulation capabilities, the stronger the external market-oriented operations become. And the importance of this overall design has reached unprecedented heights.

In the past, we thought that every industry under a group company should remain loyal to its natural attributes. For example, real estate should focus on land reserves, strengthen planning and design capabilities, and produce good real estate products. Tourism should act as a bridge between tourism capital and tourists. But this is a misunderstanding. In any group, the parent company will assign a group attribute to each subsidiary, meaning that although a particular sector may operate in a certain way in society, within our group, we believe that such operation might yield partial benefits but not overall benefits. Therefore, we require you to operate in a different manner. It might feel awkward for the industrial sector, even lead to losses or difficulties, but it brings greater comprehensive benefits to the group. This loss leads to the overall success of the group, maximizing overall group benefits. For instance, HNA's aviation sector provides a lot of support and synergy to other sectors, offering many opportunities and platforms for hotels, tourism, real estate, finance, and more. Perhaps from the perspective of the aviation sector alone, they might earn less, suffer losses, and fail to maximize efficiency, but through the operations of other sectors, not only do they make up for these losses, but they also gain more. Of course, there exists some level of synergy among HNA's internal sectors, it's just a matter of whether it is consciously designed and whether such design is amplified.

Therefore, each industrial sector of a combative group cannot decide how to grow or expand on their own. For instance, the real estate sector might say that Vanke’s methods are good and worth learning from, or the hotel sector might think Marriott’s approach is worth emulating. At this point, the parent company must intervene and tell the subsidiaries that they cannot act this way because they are not independent entities responsible only to themselves; they are sectors of a group and must comply with the group characteristics and roles assigned by the group company. Headquarters decides who will be the main force, who will protect, who will sacrifice, who will handle logistics, and who will flank – it’s an overall strategy aimed at achieving the maximization of group value. Subsidiaries have no authority to independently formulate strategies and can only develop and implement supporting strategies based on the group company’s determined strategy.

In the past, we believed that all groups face the same uncertainties and risks. But in fact, different group companies face different uncertainties and risks. The larger the group company, the longer the industrial chain, the stronger the internal integration, the smaller the relative uncertainties and risks for the group company. So when a group becomes large, not only does its risk resistance ability strengthen, but it can also utilize its risk mitigation capabilities to do things that other companies cannot or would find too risky, thus achieving super-speed growth and even market monopoly. For example, the recent phenomenon of state advancement and private retreat in China is actually due to the group operations of larger becoming larger and stronger becoming stronger.

In the past, we thought that all companies, whether groups or single entities, large groups or small groups, were engaging in market economies, meaning each subsidiary earns its own money. But in reality, group companies rely on internal macro-regulation. It’s not about how much money the subsidiaries earn; the parent company assigns roles to each subsidiary. Some subsidiaries act as capital centers, some as profit centers, some intentionally incur losses, some bear reform costs, and some specialize in relationship and network management. The parent company regulates between the subsidiaries, and ultimately, the roles assigned result in a chemical reaction where the money earned far exceeds the sum of what each subsidiary could earn independently. As everyone knows, XX Group's XX Appliances was once China's largest home appliance retail outlet with sales in the hundreds of billions, yet it did not make a profit because its payment terms were 90 days. It aggressively expanded inventory, reduced prices and profits, opened more stores, and replicated this model, resulting in extremely fast large-scale cash recovery. Within 20 days, the money was already in hand, leaving 70 days until settlement. At this point, the group used this cash flow to support another sector - ZZ Real Estate. Its real estate sector once had a land reserve of 120 million square meters, strongly supported by the cash flow of the commercial sector of XX Group. This kind of comprehensive benefit is unimaginable for traditional group enterprises.

Group companies also have an artificial economy, where they don't make money at first but gradually start earning as they accumulate. After investing $500 million, Avatar made over $1.6 billion in box office revenue in just 20 days, a typical example of artificial economy - spending money first before making money; losing money initially before profiting later; spending to cultivate habits and addiction before reaping profits; doing concepts first, guiding customers through deepening concepts before making money. Because group companies have large internal adjustment spaces, multiple related industries, long and controllable internal value chains, for example, Hangzhou West Lake doesn't charge admission fees, leading to a surge in tourists, which in turn makes Hangzhou's real estate and commerce highly profitable. Dubai in the UAE inexplicably rose in the desert, becoming the Wall Street and tourist hub of the Middle East, the entire CBD of the Middle East, entirely through the extraordinary thinking and grand moves of the Dubai Emir to create an artificial economy. A much smaller imitation of Dubai, Phoenix Island, has already shocked Hainan and even China, and the national policies and strategies favoring Hainan International Tourism Island will bring many opportunities for artificial economy to Hainan, largely testing whether HNA Group and even the leaders of each sector can seize this overall rise opportunity!

In short, group companies must also consider how to conduct artificial economies. Any group company relying purely on market economies without artificial economies will result in lower efficiency and investment returns compared to individual and single companies in the market due to internal bureaucratization and internal consumption affecting the group itself.

In the past, we believed that as a company grows and gets bigger, management skills would correspondingly develop and improve. However, in modern group companies, this concept has been abolished. Is it better for group companies to have business first and then develop management, or vice versa? This question has been studied little, similar to the chicken or the egg dilemma. But we have found that group companies should initially prioritize growth before management, but once they reach a certain size, business encounters various bottlenecks, the benefits brought by growth decrease, while management can generate more performance at this time. It must be noted that the group must establish some unique capabilities, and these capabilities can only be built by the group company, such as risk management capabilities, balancing capabilities for diversified subsidiaries, internal control and supervision capabilities for subsidiaries, and the ability to allocate limited resources with emphasis among subsidiaries, etc. These capabilities must be established by a group at a certain time, otherwise, continuous growth will be impossible, or even uncontrollable subsidiaries may lead to group disintegration.

But these capabilities must be led by the parent company, some must be fully constructed by the parent company, some require joint efforts between parent and subsidiaries. Fundamentally, subsidiaries will not have the initiative to build these capabilities and may even oppose such construction. But if the group company lacks these unique capabilities, it will be difficult to achieve greater profitability. The difference between black and white groups is very clear from the perspective of whether the group has these unique capabilities, including whether these capabilities were accidentally built or consciously and systematically designed and constructed. This is a touchstone for whether a group's future growth potential is broad.
