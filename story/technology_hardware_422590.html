How to choose the right video analysis product for facial recognition attendance machines

by neqctdyi on 2011-04-29 10:37:05

Article Origin: Intelligent Surveillance Expert - Shenzhen Bellsent (www.bellsent.com)

Video analyzing and recognition technology refers to a technique that uses computers to extract useful information from videos through computation and analysis.

For humans, when we see a video, it often carries meaning and a certain amount of information. We can intelligently extract this information. For instance, in a particular video clip, there might be "a white Jetta car with its right turn signal on preparing to turn right," which we can observe and deduce. However, for a computer, after receiving the same video (e.g., via a video capture card), it only perceives a data sequence — a series of grayscale or color values for every pixel in each frame — without any information about a "white Jetta car." In fact, such details are embedded within the planar image sequences formed by the pixel values and require an overall understanding to extract. Computers can compute statistical information like mean and variance from these data sequences but cannot achieve the "extraction and comprehension" of such information.

Video analyzing and recognition technology aims to enable computers to extract content information or individual activity information from video signals using specific core algorithms, thereby allowing computers to "understand" videos and "know" what is happening or what kind of "event" is occurring within them.

The scope of video analysis technology is broad; essentially, any extraction of useful information from videos through computation can be considered video analysis technology because it involves the "analysis, identification, and understanding" of "video content." From this perspective, technologies that have already matured and been productized for practical use, such as license plate recognition, video retrieval, and face detection in videos, all fall under "video analysis technology" since they involve extracting useful information from videos, like license plate numbers, text, or specific graphics.

What is the use of video analysis technology? In essence, video analysis enables computers to understand "what is happening" in videos. Knowing the events allows computers to compare them against corresponding rules and determine their characteristics. Analyzing, extracting, and identifying individual behaviors from videos allows computers to judge what actions individuals are performing, whether these actions conform to certain rules, or if they belong to a "specific type" of behavior. This capability enables computers to detect and alarm for events that do not conform to the rules, eliminating manual intervention and judgment, thus achieving "automatic surveillance" or "intelligent surveillance." To put it more vividly, camera and video transmission technologies in surveillance systems solve the "eye" problem, allowing monitoring personnel to view scenes remotely, even across long distances via digital network transmission. Video analysis technology adds a "brain" to the surveillance system, enabling machines to replace humans (at least to some extent) in watching these videos continuously without manual oversight.

Due to the complexity of algorithms and the diversity of target behaviors, the development of video analysis technology has been relatively slow. International anti-terrorism measures have significantly promoted the development of video analysis technology, as people urgently seek products that can "automatically alarm and intelligently monitor." Compared to domestic research results, foreign products have a certain level of advancement. Currently, mature intelligent video surveillance products are available internationally, capable of automatic alarm functions in surveillance systems. These products feature real-time alarms, shortened response times for abnormal events, reduced losses, and enhanced deterrent power, with several practical engineering cases. In contrast, domestically, due to slightly lagging technological development, most advanced intelligent video surveillance products in the market are still imported, representing foreign technology. Occasionally, some domestic manufacturers or research units launch their own intelligent video surveillance products, but these remain at a relatively early stage and have yet to reach maturity. It is worth noting, however, that while foreign products are more mature, they have not yet reached a level that fully satisfies users, possessing only a relatively basic level of intelligence, capable of detecting simpler behaviors automatically. Nonetheless, this step has been taken, and video analysis technology has begun to be applied in practical engineering systems. As this technology continues to advance and research deepens, it will increasingly enter our daily lives.

Whether now or in the future, anyone wishing to adopt video analysis technology products and achieve intelligent surveillance will face a question: how to choose suitable, better-performing video analysis technology products? What kind of product best suits one's needs? Due to differences in usage environments, objectives, and desired automatic detection behaviors, there may be slight variations in selecting video analysis technology products. This article does not aim to discuss whose technology is the best or whose product performance is superior.

In fact, when choosing different video analysis technology products, one should generally consider the following aspects preliminarily. If a product performs poorly in these areas, it may not be very suitable for practical engineering applications and should be carefully considered:

1. Environmental adaptability: Most surveillance systems are not used only at fixed times but are required to function normally most of the time. For long-term operating surveillance systems, usage environments vary greatly. Outdoor systems, for example, experience changing light conditions due to weather (sunny, rainy, snowy, foggy) and day-night cycles, including factors like strong light, reflections, backlighting, and shadows. Indoors, although simpler than outdoors, there are still changes in lighting due to brightness, turning lights on/off, shadows, and people moving around. Video analysis technology should not be limited to specific lighting conditions; instead, the core algorithm should be improved to ensure good surveillance performance under various lighting environments. Therefore, environmental adaptability is a crucial factor users need to consider when choosing video analysis technology products.

2. System stability: Most surveillance systems require 7*24-hour uninterrupted stable operation, with average fault-free running times typically exceeding ten thousand hours. Under this requirement, whether a video analysis technology product possesses high system stability is a noteworthy factor. Products with frequent crashes or errors and poor stability are impractical. From this perspective, embedded video analysis products have certain advantages over computer software-based products, mainly due to their high stability.

3. Recognition rate and false alarm rate: High-performance video analysis products have high recognition rates with relatively fewer false alarms and missed detections. In actual surveillance, cameras often encounter disturbances like flying insects, animals, leaf movements, and shadows. A video analysis product must possess a certain level of "intelligence" to distinguish between intrusion targets that trigger rules and these disturbances. Otherwise, a video analysis product intended to enhance security would repeatedly give "cry wolf" scenarios, leading to frustration. Moreover, during serious threats, frequent false alarms could mislead you, causing you to lose early warning opportunities. When choosing a video analysis product, it is essential to select one with extremely high recognition rates while minimizing false alarm rates to achieve precise warnings.

4. Resistance to harsh environments: In surveillance, we encounter various environments, from freezing northern winters to scorching desert summers, heavy rain, or snowstorms. Video analysis equipment must fulfill its mission wherever the client requires, so we must consider the impact of harsh environments on video analysis products. During actual selection, one should carefully understand the applicable environment for the equipment. Many video analysis products perform well in normal environments but falter in slightly harsher ones, either malfunctioning or experiencing significant performance degradation, with recognition rates dropping and false alarm rates increasing. Careful differentiation of video analysis products based on actual installation needs and testing ensures they function properly.

5. Ease of use: Despite the powerful algorithms and complexity of video analysis products, their presentation to installation engineers should be as simple as possible. Plug-and-play, appliance-like concepts represent our highest pursuit. Simple installation and use allow engineers to quickly set up systems without spending excessive time debugging and installing video analysis devices. Another advantage of simplicity is easier troubleshooting and maintenance. Easy-to-use video analysis products provide great convenience for both initial installation and debugging as well as later troubleshooting and maintenance.

6. Technical support and service: Currently, video analysis technology products are non-standardized and not very convenient to use. On one hand, installation and debugging require professional skills, often needing technical personnel to adjust parameters based on the usage environment. On the other hand, products and technologies are not yet fully mature, requiring substantial technical support during use. Therefore, users should also carefully consider the technical support and services provided by the products.

The above merely provides a brief discussion on selecting video analysis products. In practice, integrators face many choices, such as price factors, project requirements, end-user acceptance, and vendor services. Indeed, as long as integrators carefully compare, conduct on-site observations, and perform field tests, they will surely find suitable video analysis products.
