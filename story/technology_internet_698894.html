The most important mindset when you first enter the workplace

by qingian007 on 2012-03-09 11:47:34

When new employees join a company, they usually start with basic tasks and gradually get to know and become familiar with the entire workflow as they adapt. Career experts suggest that newcomers should be diligent in their work and honest in their dealings because no company likes employees who do things half-heartedly. Such employees also make colleagues and superiors feel that they are unreliable. Every time they report an issue or say something cannot be completed, others might suspect them of being lackadaisical. Although some tasks may seem complicated and trivial, only by going through this process will one gain a sense of accomplishment when completing a major task. During this long preparation phase, newcomers also become proficient in procedures and understand clients. For example, many newcomers may receive the task of making phone calls for visits or follow-ups. Some newcomers are enthusiastic at first, but over time, they find the job of making phone calls all day rather monotonous and start slacking off, ignoring many calls that cannot be connected or temporarily unreachable. Such irresponsible attitudes towards work can also cause companies significant economic losses.

No matter how successful a newcomer was during their student days, they must learn to reset once they enter the workplace, humbly learning from experienced colleagues about professional knowledge and industry information. With a humble attitude, newcomers will gain more and grow faster. The knowledge learned in school may be outdated in enterprises due to mismatches in knowledge structure. Therefore, university students entering companies need to keep learning and stay updated with knowledge while maintaining their competitiveness in the workplace and industry. Career veterans suggest that newcomers should always be in a state of continuous learning within the company, learning from others' experiences, good ways of handling affairs, and attitudes. They should update their knowledge in a timely manner and better understand the knowledge related to their industry and company. Even if you achieved a professional level 8 in English in university, you may still need to learn because there are many technical terms in an industry.

Due to their shallow experience and insufficient handling skills, it is inevitable for newcomers to make some mistakes. However, newcomers should not use their inexperience as an excuse for everything, thinking they can get away with anything because they are new. The author believes that what is taboo in the workplace is for newcomers to refuse to admit their mistakes, unable to take criticism, and shirk responsibility. Veterans warn newcomers that people who shirk responsibility are themselves disrespectful, let alone newcomers who have not fully adapted to their surroundings. How can one build a personal brand in the workplace? When making mistakes, it is suggested that newcomers reflect on the root causes of the errors and not complain based on their newcomer status. Only those who understand responsibility may be entrusted with important missions. Once caught doing something wrong, instead of finding excuses and adding reasons like "I'm new here, so I didn't originally know these rules," it's important to remember that occasional mistakes due to inexperience are forgivable, but consistently shirking responsibility will surely cause resentment among peers and often lead to interpersonal relationship crises due to an inability to take responsibility.

Everyone’s innate conditions may vary, but everyone inevitably goes through the transition from a student to a newcomer in the workplace. Should newcomers continue their self-proclaimed individuality from university, remain adept at navigating between departments and various cultural activities, or enter the so-called complex workplace and learn to 'keep their heads down'? What kind of workplace personality should newcomers cultivate to navigate the workplace with ease? Senior career experts analyze the personality traits of newcomers entering the workplace and teach how to cultivate the necessary personality traits to score points in workplace interactions.

Some newcomers appear extremely unsociable after entering the workplace, coming to work silently and leaving quietly, existing like air every day. Gradually, colleagues also ignore the existence of such newcomers, making it difficult for them to collaborate with other departments on tasks due to limited support. In fact, newcomers could quickly integrate into the larger group by chatting with colleagues during lunchtime. On weekends or holidays, when colleagues organize small gatherings such as dinners or singing, newcomers should not refuse participation due to unfamiliarity. Only by actively communicating with everyone can newcomers gain more understanding from colleagues. Each newcomer faces a strange environment. Adjusting one's mindset and quickly adapting to the environment is key. Besides waiting for colleagues to invite them to join discussions, newcomers should also show interest in participating in colleague activities at appropriate times, becoming a sociable person in the workplace.

There is a famous saying: "Character determines destiny." The so-called workplace character we refer to is not about erasing individuality or characteristics; it is not about having an all-purpose personality. Here, the so-called workplace character aims to encourage newcomers to improve their individuality gradually, being good at cooperation, willing to communicate, humble in attitude, and serious and responsible in spirit. These traits never go out of style and become the personality labels of newcomers advancing in the workplace. Only by continuously refining their individuality in the workplace can newcomers allow their character advantages to add value in workplace competition.

Newcomers usually undergo training when they first join a company, and veteran employees generally help them. However, many newcomers act like abacuses—only moving when prompted. If no one comes to teach them, they won’t learn and just wait for others to instruct them, lacking initiative.

Many newcomers claim during recruitment that they can do everything, but once hired, they say the company did not train them and they don’t know what to do. Career veterans suggest that newcomers, despite possibly not having specific tasks or clear performance indicators initially, should proactively learn about their industry and visit the company website to understand the operations and development of the company they've joined. After all, the identity of a newcomer is "new," and the industries and companies they are in are also "new," requiring them to actively acquire relevant business knowledge to facilitate future work. As a result, many newcomers spend their early days at work "online," browsing without actively communicating with leaders or colleagues about tasks that need assistance.
