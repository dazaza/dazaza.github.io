"Buffett's Advice to Chinese Stock Investors" - Grasping the Investment Philosophy of the Oracle

by jqrxass on 2009-09-02 10:59:05

Warren Buffett is globally recognized as an investment master, but whether his value investment and long-term investment philosophy can work in China remains controversial.

"Warren Buffett's Advice to Chinese Stock Investors" starts from Buffett's investment philosophy and combines it with the actual situation of China's stock market. It uses as many direct quotations from Buffett as possible to explain his core concepts, investment skills, and theoretical essence. Ultimately, it concludes that Buffett's investment philosophy is entirely suitable for China's reality and Chinese investors.

Advice 1: Don't buy stocks you don't plan to hold for 10 years

Because I believed in Jack Byrne and the competitive advantages the company originally possessed, Berkshire Hathaway bought a large number of shares in the Government Employees Insurance Company (GEICO) in the second half of 1976, followed by some additional purchases. By the end of 1980, we had invested $45.7 million to acquire 33.3% of the company's equity. However, over the next 15 years, we did not increase our holdings. But due to the company continuously repurchasing its own shares, our stake in GEICO gradually increased to about 50%.

Advice 2: Buying stocks means investing in the company

From my personal experience and observations, I have drawn a conclusion: Behind an excellent record (measured from the perspective of investment returns), the kind of boat you are rowing matters more than how you row it (although effort and talent are also important regardless of whether a company is good or bad). A few years ago, I said that when a management expert meets a company without prospects, it is usually the latter that prevails. My view has not changed at all today.

Advice 3: The company's management must be excellent

Our positions in the three stocks we will always hold—Capital Cities/ABC, GEICO, and The Washington Post—remain unchanged. Similarly unchanged is our unparalleled admiration for the management of these companies: Tom Murphy and Dan Burke of Capital Cities, Bill Snyder and Lou Simpson of GEICO, Kay Graham and Dick Simmons of The Washington Post. Charlie Munger and I greatly appreciate their outstanding abilities and high moral standards in company management.

Advice 4: The company's business operations must be easy to understand

An excellent rider on a good horse can gallop swiftly, but even the best rider on a weak old horse will be powerless. The textile business of Berkshire Hathaway is managed by capable and honest managers. Likewise, if these managers were managing a company with good economic prospects, they would surely achieve very excellent results; but if they manage a company with poor development prospects, it is like running in quicksand, where no matter how much effort they put in, they cannot take a single step forward.

Advice 5: The company must have an economic moat

In an article Buffett wrote for Fortune magazine in 1999, he stated that the key to investment is to see whether the enterprise has competitive advantages and whether these advantages are sustainable. Only if the products and services provided by the enterprise have strong competitive advantages can satisfactory returns be brought to investors. As for the influence of the industry in which the company operates on society and the growth of the entire industry, these are not the most critical factors. After all, you are investing in a specific listed company, not the entire industry.

Advice 6: The company's debt ratio must be low

Buffett said in a letter to shareholders in the 1987 annual report of Berkshire Hathaway that practice shows that listed companies that can create new profit records now operate in ways that are not much different from those 5 or even 10 years ago. Although the company's management should understand the principle of lifelong learning, they should not blindly change things, otherwise it may easily increase the chances of making mistakes. Buffett compared this to: It is unlikely to build a fortress on unstable ground.

Advice 7: Free cash flow must be consistently abundant

One of the miracles is in Aesop's Fables. That time-tested but incomplete investment concept is "A bird in hand is worth two in the bush." To further interpret this principle, you need to answer three questions: How do you know there are birds in the bushes? When will they appear and how many will there be? What is the risk-free cost of capital (here we assume the rate of U.S. long-term government bonds)? If you can answer these three questions, then you will know the highest value of the bushes and how many birds you might possess.

Advice 8: Return on equity indicator must be high

Buffett said in a letter to shareholders in the 1983 annual report of Berkshire Hathaway: "We never take single-year performance figures too seriously. After all, why should we assume that the time required for business operations to generate profits is the same as the time it takes for the Earth to orbit the Sun? Instead, we suggest examining the long-term operating performance of businesses over at least five years. If a company's average earnings over five years are significantly lower than the overall average of U.S. industries, alarms should sound."

Advice 9: The stock trading price must be reasonable

Buffett said in a letter to shareholders in the 1994 annual report of Berkshire Hathaway that when buying shares, Berkshire Hathaway focuses only on price, not timing. Specifically, it is the stock price relative to intrinsic value. He believes that it is a very foolish thing to abandon buying a stock with a good long-term prospect due to concerns about short-term factors. Why give up a good stock just because of unpredictable short-term factors?

Advice 10: View stock price fluctuations as a good thing

Buffett said in a letter to shareholders in the 1993 annual report of Berkshire Hathaway that the academic definition of investment risk is really a bit off the mark, even absurd. For example, according to the beta theory, if a stock's price falls more than the market, it is considered riskier than when the stock price was higher, isn’t that ridiculous? Just like when Berkshire Hathaway bought The Washington Post Company in 1973, imagine if one day someone is willing to sell you the whole company at a very low price, would you reject it because you think the risk is too high?!
