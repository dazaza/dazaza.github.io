Dianping: The Bone That Giants Can't Chew, Living in the Zone of Discomfort

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:38:03

Dianping CEO Zhang Tao

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Dianping's CEO Zhang Tao has once again postponed the company's IPO timeline.

In 2011, Dianping received a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, at which point the company was already valued at $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an opportune time to go public within the following one to two years, but it didn’t. Instead, in 2012, Dianping raised another $60 million in Series D funding.

Given Dianping’s scale and development momentum, going public wouldn't be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that listing now would be too costly. He breaks down the large internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point offers the potential for a giant company to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services represents the fourth potential giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been working hard to accumulate resources in this area, and finally, with the explosion of mobile internet, smartphones have bridged the gap between "location" and "services." Following Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more suited to become the fourth giant than Dianping.

However, the outside world doesn't see it that way. In recent months, with BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent) frequently acquiring companies and expanding their layouts, Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition candidate; under any of the BAT names, it could help them achieve great success early on. On the other hand, Dianping's size is far smaller than BAT's, and it is still too early to declare it as a future giant. Moreover, whether it’s Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, all these companies are directly targeting Dianping's territory—local life services focused on food, drink, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two points, Dianping's IPO would essentially be a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs some time to prove that what Dianping does now is something BAT cannot do. Second, Dianping is about to enter the final battle of group buying. If it secures the top position, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." For speculation and rumors, Zhang Tao turns a deaf ear. He is very clear: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is more suitable for development within the ecosystem of a giant. For example, maps and emails can easily attract a large number of users but struggle to find independent business models. Others, like browsers, can make money, but the amount earned through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money maintaining an email product because it helps with branding and user stickiness, but there aren’t many successful standalone email companies," he says. The second type involves industry consolidation, such as in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type consists of companies facing financial difficulties, selling themselves for survival.

Now, Meituan has already joined Alibaba's ranks. Theoretically, Dianping should quickly find a "backer" to avoid being defeated by competitors who gain capital and rapidly expand. However, Zhang Tao says, "The giants offer us limited help. We do need users, but execution is even more important. Acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities and risk marginalization within a larger company." Furthermore, he aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would be akin to "taking sides," making Dianping an enemy of the others.

Thus, logically speaking, acquiring Dianping becomes a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why would we take their money? We don't lack funds; what we need are resources. If a giant were willing to take a small stake and provide us with many resources, I could consider it. But would they? If a giant only holds a small percentage of shares, would they give you resources?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In the mobile internet sector, opportunities for companies focusing solely on pure internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities arise from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that the main aspect of life services is food, drink, entertainment, and leisure. Other细分 markets, like ride-hailing, may form good business models, but it is difficult for any of them to grow into giants.

For food, drink, entertainment, and leisure, user needs are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, newcomers will find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship race, and in 2013, Dianping will battle Meituan. This is a winner-takes-all market. As long as there are two strong players coexisting, the industry's gross profit margin will be pulled down, preventing stable profitability.

A visible future lies in reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but this must be built on Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop is difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao says, "Currently, feedback from users and merchants is good, but if reservations were done separately, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services also present some heavier opportunities. Takeout, for example, is a rigid demand for users and can help merchants increase sales. In the past one or two years, takeout companies like "To Home Gourmet Society" have flourished. However, the core of takeout is delivery, meaning it must be delivered within a short time frame, along with issues like soup spilling. The logistics requirements are even higher than those of fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao says, "If executed well, takeout still has potential, but it's harder dirty work, and Dianping is already doing enough dirty and hard work."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant information and user reviews, which Zhang Tao calls "dirty and hard work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but the pain of the past has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, aside from Meituan, Dianping has no real rivals in the life services field.

Nevertheless, he still needs to be wary of BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, and users can use the map to search for nearby dining and other life services. Tencent, considered to have secured a ticket to the mobile internet, has launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and expanding goods from physical items to services is a natural progression, especially since it has included Meituan and AutoNavi in its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there hasn't been any explosive growth in reality or data," Zhang Tao says. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to eat, drink, entertain, or relax.

Moreover, in the vast realm of life services, competition among giants will inevitably overlap, making it impossible for anyone to completely monopolize the field. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao believes this makes Dianping safer instead. "In segmented fields like car rental and business travel, the massive user base directed by the giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in AutoNavi, wanting to invade Dianping's territory isn't so simple. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can always be categorized no matter how peculiar. A user's experience of a meal depends on various factors like the day's environment, waitstaff, and chefs. Managing merchants cannot rely on systems and tools like Alibaba does; it requires building a 'ground team' to manage with people.

"All three giants want to walk this path, and there is room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it is a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing life services requires deploying ground teams at various levels, which BAT lacks the genes and capability to manage offline teams. Second, they lack the willingness; life services require dedicated effort, not just sending a small team, and profits come slowly. "A company finds it hard to excel at two important things simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate its advantage in its original field."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Turn Easily

In 2011, when Dianping received $100 million in financing, Zhang Tao told the media that Dianping is "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change fast, and before Dianping went public, his comparison targets had all fallen from grace.

He wasn't particularly anxious about this. "They didn't become big companies because they lacked offline teams, and now they all want to become America's Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang Tao has gradually figured out himself. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform, one deal per day, just like the Americans did. But as orders increased, he realized that group buying had the characteristics of a trading platform. Thus, he foresaw Dianping's future as a life services trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used to transform Dianping from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this "genetic transformation," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth seat among the giants today. But genetic transformation sounds simple, yet in practice, it's equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process extremely painful.

First, compared to selling advertisements alone, a trading platform has lower gross margins and demands stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, a trading platform requires higher cross-departmental communication skills.

Previously, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and the sales team were completely separate. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments—important clients couldn't modify information on the platform for sales purposes, leading to some mutual hostility between content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication. Creating a good marketing plan requires not just planning but also editorial cooperation. He says, "Maintaining information quality remains important, but there's no need to reinforce enmity. The firewall needs to be dismantled."

Though editors may logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and combined across the entire company, the inertia becomes even greater. Zhang Tao specially held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. After a few months, an editorial manager casually told a sales manager, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needs genetic transformation. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team a dual identity. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants—not just selling products but helping merchants develop marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants act as the company's buyers. Dianping is like a supermarket where not only must the product quality be good, but the variety must be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Of course, this dual identity will inevitably generate a series of complex issues. Frontline staff will find this quite challenging, needing to balance the scale themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual identity" is very similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to the frontlines allows the company to respond faster but increases management difficulty. Likewise, compared to frontline employees, the management team suffers more after Dianping's genetic transformation.

Every year, Zhang Tao decomposes Dianping's company goals into various departments, then executives further break them down layer by layer, but decomposition isn't a simple arithmetic operation. Li Jing, Dianping's co-founder responsible for the sales team, sometimes encounters situations where unresolved merchant demands affect sales. Therefore, Li Jing introduces a new role, setting up communication specialists who daily collect information from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change, and everyone feels uncomfortable when encountering changes. All employees, including myself, must go through uncomfortable phases."

Numbers illustrate the weight of Dianping's execution team. Currently, out of more than 3,000 employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground force is responsible for directly communicating with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group-buying deals, and bundled marketing solutions.

For this core team capable of resisting BAT's influence, Dianping has developed a unique management evaluation system.

They are not called "salespeople"; from the first day, their business cards print "marketing planning consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the stage of watching news and chatting on QQ, at least three to five years behind Taobao merchants in understanding the internet. Marketing planning consultants need to tell merchants what online users' needs are and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing says, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing consultants wouldn't recommend group-buying deals for abalone or shark fins; perhaps other dishes, because online users and offline users have different needs."

An excellent marketing consultant must be a versatile player. They need to explain to restaurant owners or hair salon owners the fundamental difference between discounts and group-buying deals; among different types of discounts, which one better fits their positioning. If facing a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant also needs to know whether it's off-season or peak season, what product costs rise in this season, what costs decrease in another season, and propose a group-buying menu that both merchants and users like.

This places high demands on marketing consultants, requiring them to be not only internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing says, "I must admit, it's impossible for every consultant to be a double expert, so we set star ratings for consultants." Like hotels, marketing consultants range from one-star to five-star, with the best being Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it difficult for them to understand internet products. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and related cases that both new and old employees can browse. If they encounter new problems, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer them.

A large number of knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experiences. Dianping has set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, directly linking the results to promotions.

Compared to internet knowledge, industry knowledge is harder to acquire. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after years of industry experience. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that hair salons shouldn't offer shampoo-cut-blow-dry package deals, because male customers lack motivation to buy, while female customers prefer blow-drying without the full package. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never have thought of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are divided not only by city but also by industries such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is even subdivided into hotpot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, making merchants less resistant when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group must hold its own case-sharing sessions: What difficulties did a certain type of merchant encounter, what marketing needs did they have, what product combinations did the company use, and what were the results? Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneurial Winner." In the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing their experiences. If they are responsible for hot pot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Similar to graduation, employees defend their plans on-site, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, with the company offering rewards.

From a strategic perspective, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, what they need to do is quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with basic industry knowledge, and their spoken words are almost identical. "The era where success could be achieved merely by storytelling and concept creation has passed. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
