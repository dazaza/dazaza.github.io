"I had not had that pleasure until he called here, sir. But it appears that Mr. Sipperley, a fellow student of whom Mr. Fink-Nottle had been at the university, recommended him to place his affairs in my hands." The mystery had been solved. I understood everything. As you probably know, Jeeves's reputation as a counselor has long been established among those in the know, and whenever anyone in my small circle finds themselves in any kind of trouble, their first move is always to turn to him for help. And when he gets someone out of a difficult situation, that person refers another to him. Then, after he helps the second person, they send along a third. And so on, if you catch my drift. That's how large consulting practices like Jeeves' grow. Old Sippy, I knew, had been deeply impressed by the man’s efforts on his behalf when he was trying to get engaged to Elizabeth Moon, so it was no surprise that he advised Gussie to seek Jeeves' assistance. It was all part of the routine, you might say.

"Ah, so you're representing him?"

"Yes, sir."

"Now I understand. Now I follow. And what is Gussie's problem?"

"Interestingly enough, sir, it's exactly the same issue that Mr. Sipperley faced when I was able to assist him. No doubt you recall Mr. Sipperley's predicament, sir. Although deeply in love with Miss Moon, he suffered from an overwhelming shyness that made it impossible for him to express himself."

I nodded. "I remember. Yes, I recall the Sipperley case. He couldn’t bring himself to make a move. There was a marked coldness of the feet, wasn't there? I remember you saying something about cats being involved somehow."

"Letting 'I dare not' wait upon 'I would', sir."

"That's right. But what about the cats?"

"Like the poor cat in the adage, sir."

"Exactly. It amazes me how you come up with these analogies. And Gussie, you say, is in the same position?"

"Yes, sir. Every time he tries to propose marriage, his courage fails him."

"And yet, if he wants this woman to be his wife, he has to say so, right? I mean, it's only polite to mention it."

"Precisely, sir."

I reflected. "Well, I suppose this was inevitable, Jeeves. I wouldn't have thought that this Fink-Nottle would ever fall victim to the divine _p_, but if he has, it's no wonder he finds things difficult."

"Yes, sir."

"Look at the life he's led."

"Yes, sir."

"I don't suppose he's spoken to a girl in years. What a lesson this is for us, Jeeves: not to shut ourselves up in country houses and stare into glass tanks. You can't be the dominant male if you do that kind of thing."

In this life, you have two choices. You can either shut yourself up in a country house and stare into tanks, or you can be a socialite with the opposite sex. You can't do both.

"No, sir."

I reflected once more. Gussie and I, as I said, had somewhat lost touch, but still, I was concerned about the poor chap, as I am about all my friends, close or distant, who find themselves slipping on Life's banana skins. It seemed to me that he was in a tough spot. I recalled the last time I had seen him. It had been about two years ago. I had dropped by his place during a motor trip, and he had completely ruined my appetite by bringing a couple of leggy green creatures to the lunch table, crooning over them like a young mother and eventually losing one of them in the salad. That image, rising before my eyes, didn't give me much confidence in the unfortunate goof's ability to woo and win, I must say. Especially if the girl he had set his sights on was one of these modern tough cookies, all lipstick and cool, hard, sardonic eyes, as she probably was.

"Tell me, Jeeves," I said, wanting to know the worst, "what kind of girl is this girl of Gussie's?"

"I haven't met the young lady, sir. Mr. Fink-Nottle speaks highly of her attractions."

"He seems to like her, does he?"
