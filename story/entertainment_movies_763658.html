Synopsis:

Andy Anderson (Kate Hudson), a blonde beauty, is a columnist for the women's magazine "Composure." She has recently been assigned a task: to write an article about how women can ditch men in ten days. This is extremely difficult for Andy because she has no such experience. In order to find inspiration and quickly complete her assignment, Andy decides to experiment herself by finding a man, making him fall in love with her within 10 days, and then dumping him...

The man Andy chooses is Benjamin (Matthew McConaughey), a playboy from an advertising company. He is handsome, humorous, and has just made a bet with his boss that he can make a woman fall completely in love with him in ten days...

A beautiful pair with ulterior motives starts playing a game of love competition. Who will win?

Behind-the-Scenes Production:

Love has always been like a needle in cotton. In today's era of insincere, excessive, and unfeeling relationships, loyalty has become a thing of the past. People can only sigh and lament while continuing their flirtations. I give my love to you but my body to him. You can helplessly say or sing about pure feelings, then act otherwise. Picasso once said to be faithful to love but not to lovers. After the extinction of fidelity and before the truth of love becomes known, love at this point seems worthy of ridicule, because the more serious you are, the closer you get to being hurt. When you're fake, the world is real; when you're real, the world is fake. "How to Lose a Guy in 10 Days" subtly hints at modern people's instinctive self-protection in the game of love through its story. The two protagonists seem tender on the surface but are actually estranged. Beyond the absurd humor, their distorted views of love leave one uneasy.

In director Donald Petrie's view, the primary standard for an interesting script is one that makes him laugh out loud, and this film fits the bill. He said: "The story is full of unexpected twists and turns, allowing the film to stand out among similar works." Besides the witty and humorous script, the opportunity to collaborate with Golden Globe winner Kate Hudson was also an important reason for Petrie to join the project. He said: "Kate displayed amazing acting talent in 'Almost Famous,' and in this film, she will showcase incredible humor. Every shot of Kate is unique, and her performances are always surprising."

All exterior scenes of "How to Lose a Guy in 10 Days" were filmed in New York. The dazzling images depict New York as the most romantic city in the world. Producer Linda Obst, who produced a series of successful romantic comedies including "Sleepless in Seattle," said: "New York is a star in this movie. It is unique on Earth. After 9/11, when you return to this city, you'll see newly renovated restaurants and revitalized people, and this special vitality runs throughout the entire film."

After completing two weeks of filming in New York, the crew moved to Canada to shoot interior scenes. In the grand and luxurious party scenes, the main actors wore Harry Winston diamond jewelry worth a total of $15 million. There were dedicated security personnel on set responsible for protecting these dazzling and valuable jewels, dressed similarly to the actors playing security guards, making it impossible to tell who was real and who wasn't.

Since both protagonists and most supporting characters work in fashion or advertising, the production team paid meticulous attention to the actors' costumes, striving to reflect contemporary professional styles through clothing. Costume designer Karen Patch, who worked on "The Royal Tenenbaums," went to Paris with producer Obst and Kate Hudson to carefully purchase outfits. She said: "Kate has extraordinary fashion awareness and a perfect figure suited for most clothes. Her new look in the film is fantastic, including an 87-carat yellow diamond pendant specially made for her by Harry Winston."

Art director Deidre Despain, who participated in the shooting of "Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas," considered the set design of the "Composure" magazine office the most challenging. Before becoming a production designer, she worked in the advertising department of a magazine. To create a believable set, she visited the offices of "Interview" and "Elle" magazines to gather design inspiration.
