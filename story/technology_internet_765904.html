Another side of Yahoo CEO Mayer: habit of being late for meetings

by anonymous on 2013-08-29 16:29:55

Many people are curious about Yahoo's CEO Marissa Mayer. Despite calling herself a tech geek, Mayer is actually quite unlike a typical tech person. An interview on the news website Business Insider revealed many lesser-known aspects of Mayer’s personality.

Mayer has a beautiful face like a celebrity and always wears dresses by her favorite designer, Oscar de la Renta, attending banquets, mingling at parties, and gracing the covers of fashion magazines. However, as a child, she was extremely introverted and shy, even disliking social interactions, immersing herself in things that interested her, and thus was often mistakenly thought to be arrogant.

Mayer originally aspired to become a neurosurgeon but found the courses dull and unstimulating for her thinking abilities. She decisively switched to "Symbolic Systems," a discipline spanning linguistics, philosophy, cognitive psychology, and information technology, which became her major. Later, she obtained a master’s degree in information technology.

From Google to Yahoo, Mayer has become a role model for many women. She and Facebook's Chief Operating Officer Sheryl Sandberg are completely different types. At the very least, feminism is not a topic of concern for Mayer.

In an article written by Nicholas Carlson, Deputy Editor-in-Chief of the news website Business Insider, and over 23,000 words long, it reveals many lesser-known aspects of Mayer. Below are some excerpts of the highlights.

From McKinsey Consultant to Google Engineer

Even before officially graduating from Stanford's Information Technology Institute, Mayer already had 12 job offers. She had initially accepted an offer from McKinsey. But one Friday evening, while sitting in front of her computer eating pizza and deleting emails from other job opportunities, she accidentally pressed the space bar, opening one email with the subject line “Are you interested in working at Google?” She remembered a professor who served as her mentor suggesting she try Google, so she immediately replied.

This accidental key press completely changed Mayer's career path.

The Unspoken Relationship with Co-founder Larry Page

In 1999, Mayer joined Google. On her second night at work, at 11 PM, she met Larry Page, then serving as CEO, standing in a corner of the company kitchen. At the time, Google had signed a cooperation agreement with Netscape, where Google would assist in handling search functions on Netscape's website.

Google only had 300 computers responsible for search, so they asked Netscape to divert a small portion of its traffic to Google. Unexpectedly, Netscape directed all its traffic to Google's servers, causing Google's search engine to crash. Finally, Mayer and her colleagues spent several hours restoring everything to normal, and Mayer didn’t leave the office until 3 AM that day.

For Mayer, this was nothing out of the ordinary. In her first two years at Google, she worked over 100 hours per week, sleeping only four hours a day. Spending so much time in the office, she grew closer to Page, naturally becoming a couple.

A Data-Obsessed Tech Geek Concerned with User Experience

In 2005, Mayer was promoted to management, responsible for managing the appearance and interface design of Google's important products. To truly understand user experience, she mimicked user habits: before most American households had broadband installed, she refused to install broadband at home; although Google internally used Android phones, she insisted on using an iPhone to stay close to the majority of consumer experiences.

On the other hand, she relied heavily on data analysis for decision-making, requiring data support for even the smallest decisions. For example, once unable to choose between two different shades of blue, Mayer asked engineers to test 41 different shadow effects to see which shade of blue worked best. However, this approach irritated many internal engineers and caused Google to lose a lot of talent.

From Google Celebrity to Being Benched

In October 2010, Mayer was transferred to the Google Maps department and no longer served as the head of the Search department. Although structurally this was a lateral move, and Mayer still retained her Vice President title, it was clearly a demotion. At the time, the Search department was Google's largest department, and the Maps department wasn't as important as it is now.

By December, Page returned to take charge of Google again. In April 2011, Page dissolved the Operations Committee established during former CEO Eric Schmidt's tenure and formed a new Senior Management Committee. However, Mayer, who had been a member of the Operations Committee, was not on this new list. Worse still, what was once within Mayer's direct control—getting whatever she wanted—now had another layer of management above her. Page appointed Jeff Huber as the head of the Maps and Location Services department.

Speculation arose that the past romantic relationship between Mayer and Page might have contributed to her demotion. However, the bigger problem probably lay with Mayer herself.

Only Interested in Product Design, Indifferent to Business Strategy

Mayer had immense passion for product design but showed no interest in discussions about business strategy, often choosing to ignore and skip many strategy meetings. However, for someone in a senior management position, this was a fatal flaw.

Another issue was her management style, which angered many people. Her attitude was strong, and she spoke quickly, leaving no room for others to argue. Even more intolerable was her requirement that anyone wanting to discuss matters with her must schedule an appointment, each session lasting only five minutes. This applied not just to her subordinates but also to her peers. However, she was also a manager who disliked delegating authority, making every decision herself, which significantly slowed down the decision-making process.

Finally, the engineers couldn’t take it anymore and confronted Page directly, “Choose: her or us?”

It seemed that the relationship between Mayer and Google had reached its end.

Ruthless Cleanup, Generous Employee Benefits

The first thing Mayer did upon entering Yahoo was to interview senior executives. Without any pleasantries, as soon as they sat down, Mayer cut straight to the chase, interrogating everyone’s background like conducting a personal investigation: “Where did you graduate from?” “Where are you from?” “What do you do in the company?” Then she focused on taking notes, rarely looking up at the person.

During her pregnancy, Mayer worked tirelessly, cleaning house everywhere, dismissing unsuitable senior executives and rehiring her own team. After giving birth, Mayer took only two weeks of maternity leave before rushing back to the office to work. This time, she put aside her earlier coldness of replacing managers abruptly and started winning people over: providing free breakfasts, gifting smartphones to all employees, holding weekly meetings with employees to allow them face-to-face communication with her. These new measures successfully united the hearts of Yahoo employees and bought their support for her.

Being Late to Meetings Became a Known Habit

For the executives reporting directly to her, life probably wasn’t so easy. The most frustrating aspect was Mayer's habit of being late. She required Yahoo executives from around the world to attend video conferences, but she herself was often late by 45 minutes. European executives frequently found themselves ending meetings at 3 AM.

She often canceled these weekly one-on-one meetings with the executives, sometimes going several weeks without meeting with any of them individually. Many executives once suspected they were being sidelined or possibly fired, but when they later discovered that everyone else’s meetings had also been canceled, they felt relieved.

Since Mayer entered Yahoo, the changes she brought were evident. Besides the major shakeup in management, the popularity of Yahoo's products began to rise significantly: Yahoo Mail traffic increased by 120%, Yahoo Weather by 150%, and Yahoo News by 55%.

For employees, the most direct feeling was the change in work atmosphere and morale, which was indeed very different from the past. Previously, the company parking lot wouldn’t be full until 10 AM and would clear out by 4 PM. Now, it’s full by 8 AM and still not empty by 6:30 PM.
