Facebook Should Enter Venture Capital Business to Prevent Talent Drain

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:13:30

As Facebook gradually "ages" after its IPO, many executives and employees have left to engage in venture capital or start new businesses. The US tech news website TechCrunch recently analyzed that Facebook should learn from mature tech companies like Google and Intel by establishing an independent venture capital (VC) arm within the company. This could help prevent talent loss and allow Facebook to benefit from the VC market.

Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg has expressed that he is most concerned about talent loss after the company went public. Indeed, over the past year, Facebook has experienced a wave of talent and executive departures, with most leaving to enter the tech VC industry or start second ventures.

In the last two weeks alone, Facebook product manager Justin-Shaffe resigned, with rumors suggesting he will set up a VC fund. Additionally, Greg-Badro, responsible for engineering and products, also announced his resignation and may enter the VC field. Chamath-Palihapitiya, another former high-ranking executive at Facebook, started a VC firm after leaving and is now recruiting talent from Facebook to assist the companies he has invested in. There are numerous other examples showing that many talents leaving Facebook have transitioned into the venture capital sector.

This raises the question: why doesn't Facebook establish its own VC business or company to retain some employees who wish to work in this industry or continue maintaining their ties with Facebook?

Traditionally, Facebook has been the recipient of investments, but stepping into the role of an investor would have benefits far beyond just retaining talent. Facebook has every possibility to achieve the same success as Google Ventures and build its brand in the VC market.

Today, the relationship between VC firms and the companies they invest in is much closer. Facebook possesses vast amounts of data, advertising resources, and product development expertise, all of which can help new companies grow. Moreover, Facebook has an abundance of talent. Previously, Facebook had already formed a tech ecosystem around itself, and many of the new companies within this ecosystem were founded by ex-Facebook employees.

In fact, Facebook has already made tentative steps into the VC market by participating in funds raised by other VC firms. For instance, in 2010, Facebook participated in a $250 million fund established by Kleiner-Perkins, a veteran in the VC world, aimed at supporting social startups. Facebook was also involved in managing the FbFund ($10 million), which supports startups on the Facebook platform.

According to statistics, from 2006 to the first half of 2012, companies founded by ex-Facebook employees received $270 million in venture capital. This indicates a significant investment demand within Facebook's ecosystem.

In reality, in the tech industry, most large companies have their own VC arms, such as Amazon, Google Ventures, Intel Capital, and Microsoft Ventures. However, Apple is an exception (it owns a fund for equity investments). Apple's investment strategy focuses on acquiring the technology, talent, and companies it needs, and its closed technical model leads to relatively conservative investments in external tech companies.
