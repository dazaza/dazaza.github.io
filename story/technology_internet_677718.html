The clinical onset age of periarthritis of the shoulder is usually around 50 years old. The symptoms of periarthritis of the shoulder are mainly pain and restricted movement of the shoulder joint, with a relatively long course of disease and slow recovery. During this process, pain, especially severe pain and limited shoulder joint activity, can seriously affect daily life and work such as dressing, washing, and sleeping, thereby causing or aggravating the mental burden on patients, who may subsequently develop psychological symptoms such as anxiety and depression. For patients with periarthritis of the shoulder, psychological imbalance itself may be one of the inducements of the disease. At the same time, female patients may have more obvious psychological symptoms due to the coexistence of menopausal syndrome. In addition, social and economic factors may also cause psychological symptoms in patients with periarthritis of the shoulder. Therefore, patients with periarthritis of the shoulder may have some psychological symptoms to a certain extent (clinically or potentially). While clinically treating periarthritis of the shoulder, it is also necessary to conduct psychological rehabilitation treatment based on the possible psychological symptoms of the patient. In this process, the first step is for the treating physician to carefully understand the patient's condition, especially to observe and analyze from the pain symptoms which are physical and which are psychological, such as using the McGill Pain Questionnaire and other pain scales for evaluation and differentiation. For patients who may have psychological symptoms, psychological measurement methods can also be used, such as Beck Depression Inventory, Self-Rating Depression Scale, Depression Status Questionnaire, Hamilton Depression Rating Scale, Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale, etc., for psychological evaluation, so as to carry out targeted psychological treatment.

Prevention and treatment tips for periarthritis of the shoulder include:

1. Using a towel to prevent periarthritis of the shoulder. Take a long towel, hold both ends with each hand, place them behind your back, one hand up and one hand down, like bathing, first pull up and down, then pull horizontally, repeat, each time for 15 minutes. At the beginning, there may be some restrictions on movement, but you should proceed gradually, with movements starting small and slow, and gradually increasing in size and speed. Do this three times a day: morning, noon, and night. As long as you persist, the symptoms of periarthritis of the shoulder will be controlled and improved.

2. Shaking a fan to prevent periarthritis of the shoulder. Elderly people often suffer from periarthritis of the shoulder caused by exposure to fans or air conditioners, while shaking a fan allows them to stay away from fans and air conditioners and exercise the shoulder joint. Shaking a fan is an upper limb exercise that requires coordination between the fingers, wrists, and local joint muscles. Frequently shaking a fan during hot weather is exactly an exercise for the upper limb joints and muscles, promoting muscle blood circulation, enhancing muscle strength, and increasing the flexibility of joint coordination. These are all effective ways to prevent and treat periarthritis of the shoulder, which middle-aged and elderly people can practice regularly.
