Synopsis:

A 10-year-old girl named Coraline has an innate sense of curiosity and always seeks answers to everything. However, her parents are very strict with her, so despite her inquisitive nature, she obediently follows their rules. When her parents decide to move to a new house, Coraline finds herself in a completely new environment that greatly stimulates her curiosity. She begins to feel intrigued by her neighbors: there's an old man whose greatest joy is training his own circus at home, where the clowns are actually adorable mice.

After playing for a while, Coraline gradually becomes familiar with her new surroundings and starts to feel a bit bored. She then begins looking for entertainment within her home and discovers a big secret: there’s a door in the living room she has never entered before! Carefully counting the doors in her house, she finds there are 13, and this 14th door leads where? One night, Coraline musters up the courage to open the door...

Behind the door lies a world similar to Coraline’s, with someone claiming to be her "other mother" with button eyes (perhaps a more perfect mom). But things start getting stranger. It turns out that the "other mother" is a terrifying monster who intends to keep Coraline in this world forever. She kidnaps Coraline's real parents and even plans to sew buttons over her eyes! With the help of a haughty black cat and three ghosts, Coraline must find the courage...

Behind-the-scenes Production:

An Old-school Director Who Loves Nostalgia

In today's world dominated by computer 3D animations, director Henry Selick, famous for "The Nightmare Before Christmas," remains one of the few "nostalgic" old-school directors whose works have a deep sense of nostalgia. Regarding this new work, Henry Selick said: "The style of this movie still maintains consistency with my past works. I think we need to first solve the problem of character design. You should be able to see that the characters in this movie are still quite unique. I really like these doll-like character images. Some viewers have asked me how long I will continue with this retro animation attempt. I think as long as it satisfies the audience, I will keep doing it. We first created doll versions of each character, and for each character, we made several expressions, poses, and costumes, especially for our protagonist, little Coraline, for whom we made over 100 doll models. Then, the computer engineers scanned these images into the computer for motion simulation. I've seen some puppet animation films from decades ago, and even without computers, they were still able to create very realistic effects, which I admire greatly. I once also considered whether this movie should maintain such a style, but finally abandoned this idea because if we didn’t use computers to simulate character movements at all, the cost would significantly increase, and it would require a lot of rehearsal and planning, which is now very difficult to achieve."

Regarding the story of "Coraline," Henry Selick, who is also the screenwriter of this movie, said: "Based on adapting the original novel, I once discussed the direction of the screenplay with the author Neil Gaiman. There were several options in front of us: one was to write the movie script according to the original without making any major changes, but the original was too dark, and the movie company thought it wasn't entirely suitable for an animated movie; another was to weaken some of the plot elements in the original, making the entire movie look more 'bright' and 'healthy.' I think both approaches are feasible, but unfortunately, we only have one movie (laughs). During filming, we mainly focused on the latter because we considered the limited acceptance ability of children and didn't want to scare them. However, for some classic scenes in the original, such as when Coraline first sees her other mother, we kept them. Parents should explain these scenes to their children, as I think the children might not fully understand them. I believe adults watching this movie will have more insights than children. Coraline is a lone hero, a child saving adults, which sounds incredible, but such things do exist in our society. This movie just presents such a story in an exaggerated and imaginative way."

A Little Girl's Adventure

Through "War of the Worlds," child star Dakota Fanning has gradually become known to more and more viewers. Although her acting career was affected by the controversy surrounding her role in "Hounddog," Dakota Fanning has begun to show her versatility upon returning to the screen. In "Coraline," Dakota Fanning voices the protagonist Coraline. About this role, Dakota Fanning said: "This novel surprised me greatly. The author has an amazing imagination, and this fairy tale also has a scientific basis, allowing adults to view it as a rigorous science fiction story. I think if this novel could be adapted into a live-action movie, it would be great. So when the movie company approached me, I thought I would be starring in this movie, but unexpectedly, I was only doing voice acting, and the audience could only hear my voice. I then told the director to make a live-action movie next, and he agreed with me. I hope he wasn't just saying it. My character is named Coraline, and the movie tells the story of her adventure. She faces herself in a strange environment, encounters sinister villains, and meets many helpful partners. The movie has many rich storylines, and during the dubbing process, I felt as if I was reliving the story, which was quite exhilarating."

As the author of the original novel, Neil Gaiman praised the movie highly: "I participated in the entire filming process of this movie. Every staff member was very dedicated, especially the visual style created by the director, which perfectly matched the atmosphere of the novel. The realism of the entire picture is clearly different from other 3D animations, looking more like a steam-age style opera. During the writing of the novel, I also thought about what kind of effect this novel would have if it were turned into a movie, but when it became a reality, it made me feel unbelievable. Henry Selick's creation of the character images and scenes for this movie left me very satisfied. The story of 'Coraline' revolves entirely around this little girl. The details of Coraline in the movie are outstanding, such as a scene where Coraline runs quickly, covering a distance of about 300 to 400 meters and lasting over a minute. The cinematography in this scene is incredibly well done, and I was completely stunned the first time I saw it."

Trivia:

- The original author of "Coraline," Neil Gaiman, announced the remake of the animated movie on March 21, 2007, at a reader's meeting in Germany and informed the readers that the music for the movie would use "They Will Become Giants."

- The trailer showcased the image of Coraline's "other father" wearing monkey bone slippers. In fact, this item comes from another movie directed by Henry Selick in 2001.

- The moving company used by Coraline's family in the movie actually exists in real life, and director Henry Selick hired this company when shooting "The Nightmare Before Christmas."
