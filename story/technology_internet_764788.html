12 stunning facts about how Amazon is transforming the U.S. economy

by anonymous on 2013-08-06 18:45:43

U.S. President Barack Obama delivered the second of seven key economic policy speeches this summer on Tuesday at Amazon's logistics center in Chattanooga, Tennessee. Just a day earlier, Amazon had announced that it would hire an additional 7,000 employees, with 5,000 of them to be deployed at the company's logistics centers. As the world’s largest online retailer, Amazon clearly has significant economic influence in the United States.

In fact, considering that Amazon does create thousands of jobs (reaching tens of thousands during the holiday shopping season), it serves as an obvious example for President Obama over the past five years when he focuses on how the government and private enterprises can work together to address job creation issues.

Here are 12 insane examples of why Amazon has become a major force in the U.S. economy:

One, this Amazon fulfillment center in Phoenix, Arizona, could fit 28 football fields.

Two, during last year’s "Cyber Monday," Amazon shipped more than 200 items per second.

Three, Amazon hired 50,000 workers for the holiday shopping season last year.

Four, this employee is picking an item from a shelf. She was one of 70,000 employees working at Amazon fulfillment centers during last year's holiday shopping season.

Five, in a fulfillment center covering 1.2 million square feet, two items may be miles apart.

Six, Amazon employees ride tricycles to retrieve items located far away.

Seven, the average hourly wage for Amazon fulfillment center employees is between $11 and $14.

Eight, this female employee was one of 2,000 Amazon employees who shipped items on last year's "Cyber Monday."

Nine, the weekly record for the number of times a single item was moved from the shelves to trucks at Amazon is 2,086,548.

Ten, this conveyor belt moves items through the fulfillment center at 20 miles per hour.

Eleven, Amazon accounts for 10% of the North American e-commerce market. Office Depot, Staples, Apple, Dell, Walmart, Sears, and Liberty combined account for another 10%.

Twelve, last year, when Amazon's website was down for 49 minutes, the company lost nearly $5.7 million in sales.
