The film is too boring, and I miss Sydney Pollack - "Made of Honor" Blu-ray Brief Review

by diglog on 2010-10-07 05:15:06

The first question after finishing the movie is "how to evaluate this film"? It seems that more than an hour has passed watching a movie, but it feels like wasting time, with no significant impact. *Made of Honor* is precisely one of those dispensable films; the story is indeed clichéd, with hardly any highlights. The only part that left an impression on me was Patrick Dempsey's performance in the restaurant scene—intending to confess his love, only to see his beloved bringing her fiancé. Although exaggerated, that scene did have some comedic elements—for me, it was the only one. Another highlight was the magnificent scenery of Scotland, captured through aerial shots, which provided a few seconds of refreshing visuals before returning to the monotony of the rest of the film.

However, when hesitating about purchasing this film, a name suddenly came to mind, prompting me to take out my wallet—Sydney Pollack. Films like *Out of Africa* and *The Interpreter* are familiar to many; Pollack was an excellent director skilled in social and humanistic themes, and he is no longer with us. *Made of Honor* is one of the few films that can serve as a final tribute to him.

The picture quality of the Blu-ray disc naturally surpasses DVD by several multiples, but even by HD standards, there are differences in quality. Unfortunately, the image quality of this disc is not entirely satisfactory. While there are no obvious digital enhancement traces, the overall picture appears soft compared to other BDs, lacking sufficient detail. The tepid style mirrors the content of the film, leaving little good impression. The contrast performance of the picture is acceptable; the opening gala looks relatively realistic, with no loss of dark areas. Some carefully shot cinematography adds color to the otherwise boring film and mediocre picture quality, albeit sparingly.

The disc features both English PCM 5.1 lossless surround sound and English Dolby 5.1 TrueHD surround sound, both of which are more than sufficient for a dialogue-heavy movie. After ensuring clear center-channel dialogue, both audio tracks effectively enhance the film's atmosphere—music. Each channel performs its role well, with the film's score and mixing giving new surprises to familiar songs. Environmental details are well-presented, sometimes making you feel like the streets of New York are right behind you. The bass doesn't have much room to shine, but considering the nature of the film, I don't think it's too big of an issue.

Extras include a featurette on the making of the movie, a behind-the-scenes short, and deleted scenes—all in 1080i HD. These standard extras don't evoke much emotion, but since I didn't have overwhelming affection for the film, this amount of bonus material wasn't too disappointing. The movie has slight edits, with subtitles available in Chinese and English; the translation is somewhat lackluster but doesn't affect the viewing experience. You could easily borrow this Blu-ray to pass the time for 100 minutes.
