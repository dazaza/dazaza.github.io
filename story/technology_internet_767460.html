What I Learned From Getting My Side Project Acquired

I started the project Asterank in May 2012. Earlier that week, Planetary Resources had announced their plan to mine water and valuable materials from asteroids. Like many others, I was intrigued. It was an inspiring, long-term vision with a very low probability of success.

My project began as an experiment of an idea: how many asteroids are actually worth exploring? The media releases were rough estimates with little scientific basis. No one had methodically cataloged the contents and value of asteroids. So on a lazy weekend afternoon at a coffee shop in Mountain View's downtown, I wrote the first version.

Thirteen months later, when Asterank was acquired by Planetary Resources, it had evolved beyond just being an asteroid valuation calculator. It became a complete asteroid toolkit including web scrapers, a data pipeline, powerful visualizations, and even the ability to discover new asteroids.

I still have no idea how I did it, but along the way, I learned a few things:

Lesson One: "Harass" People

Consistently reach out to people who can give you substantive feedback or help.

The key is patience and not giving up. If you have short-term engagement, follow up every two weeks; if not, once a month.

Who to Email

Cast a wide net. Contact anyone who can provide expertise, advice, or publicity, including:

Contact Planetary Resources Reach out to other space companies or organizations Scientists at research institutions NASA scientists Space bloggers Professors in astronomy course introductions Technologists who find my data visualizations interesting

Initial Email Guidelines

Your email should be as brief as possible:

Briefly describe what you've done and your accomplishments (users, press mentions, etc.) Tell them your goals Ask for something specific that would help you achieve your goals

Keep it to no more than two or three short paragraphs. Subsequent follow-up emails should be even shorter:

Project updates – recent achievements, features, etc. (Skip this if they've already committed to updates) Ask for something else

Don't Take It Personally

A large portion of the emails you send will go unread. This might feel insulting or discouraging. Keep going and don't take it personally.

Boomerang for Gmail is a great tool for email reminders. I use the free version to send monthly emails to people I'm interested in contacting.

After persisting for a while, they stopped ignoring me and started reaching out. My network grew significantly. Persistent email outreach was the single biggest factor contributing to Asterank's success.

Lesson Two: Social Content is Useful, But a Time Trap

The only self-promotion I ever did was a post on Hacker News that garnered only 2 points. (As a bad clickbait artist, I deserved it.)

Luckily, someone picked it up, and Asterank got featured on Universe Today, a popular space blog. Afterwards, a few people, including leadership at Planetary Resources, reached out to me. Traffic stabilized, with occasional spikes from social aggregation, press mentions, etc.

This led to my site crashing over Christmas.

If no one notices your project but it's genuinely interesting, keep blogging until someone does. I posted Asterank Discover on Hacker News and got 5 points. Then I wrote a blog post about it which made it to the front page.

It's worth noting that traffic from social networks decays quickly and most people aren't actually interested in your product. It helped me get started, but the marginal benefit decreases rapidly. Don't get stuck in it.

Lesson Three: A Simple Feedback Form is Essential

Some of my best contacts came through the About page of Asterank. I provided my email address and added a feedback form. I recommend adding both (the feedback form is more indirect, but some people prefer direct communication).

It took only a few minutes to add a basic form.

I also added a "subscribe for updates," but in reality, it just sent their email addresses to me. I used it as an interest indicator. Building an email list before knowing if anyone would use my project didn't make sense.

However you do it, having easily findable contact information is essential. It can bring in job offers, conference invitations, and media interviews.

Lesson Four: Your Analytics Might Reveal Clues

Occasionally scan your analytics, especially referral sources. I established a valuable contact just by noticing a link from a company email. Reaching out directly through referral sources makes it easier to connect with people you know are already interested.

When I send emails, I sometimes track them via links like

http://asterank.com/?f=n

where n is a unique string. This way, even if they don't reply, I can see who clicked the link out of interest.

Lesson Five: LinkedIn Can Be Useful Sometimes

For software engineers, LinkedIn can be frustrating, but if you're dealing with an industry outside of tech, it's extremely important.

A downside is that LinkedIn can generate a lot of spammy messages.

I know many software engineers who question the value of LinkedIn. It may cost you some sanity, but maintaining a basic, up-to-date profile is worth it.

Lesson Six: Open Source Everything You Can

Most people were surprised when I told them Asterank was almost entirely open source. It tends to create a transparent atmosphere and invite collaborators. Eventually, the project received useful feedback and insights.

Posting technical challenges on your blog is a good way to get exposure within the open source community. Technologists are often interested in how specific technologies are used in various applications. Asterank became known in the WebGL community due to its visualization techniques. By showcasing interesting technical applications, you can attract smart and communicative people.

Lesson Seven: Persevere

I have 5+ side projects. I wanted all of them to become commercial ventures, but I usually lost interest after a few weeks. Asterank was the only project I stuck with for over a year, and even without a clear monetization path, it paid off.

As a side project, it's hard to predict what will be valuable. For hobbies, working on what you're most passionate about is the best way to get rewarded. Otherwise, it will be difficult to sustain.

Lesson Eight: Learn to Appreciate

I made some tough decisions, passing up several excellent opportunities to end the project. I was lucky to have choices and support from friends, family, and collaborators.

I accepted a position as a software engineer at Planetary Resources and started in November.

Good luck!
