3 Reasons Snapchat Was Right to Turn Down Facebook's $3 Billion Offer

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:14:29

　　English Original: Why Would Snapchat Turn Down a $3 Billion Cash Buyout?

　　Facebook seems determined to acquire the "disappearing-message" app Snapchat. According to the Wall Street Journal, after its previous $1 billion acquisition offer was rejected, Facebook recently made another irresistible offer— at least $3 billion in cash. However, Snapchat, with high aspirations, gave the same answer: not for sale.

　　Why not sell? A company founded only two years ago, with a 23-year-old CEO, why would it reject a $3 billion check?

　　One opinion is that Evan Spiegel is foolish. Snapchat has yet to find its own profit model, and whether their users will pay for premium accounts remains unknown. It's worth noting that most of their users are teenagers with relatively low purchasing power. Moreover, there are numerous IM applications on the market competing with Snapchat, many of which have over a hundred million users, such as Facebook's Messenger service.

　　Of course, Spiegel isn't stupid. The reason he rejected Facebook's $3 billion acquisition offer is because he believes he can achieve great success with Snapchat. There might be three reasons why Snapchat dared to refuse Facebook's acquisition:

　　Rising user engagement

　　Sources revealed that not only is Snapchat's user base growing, but so is user engagement. Snapchat has always been reluctant to disclose various figures, but recently they broke from tradition by announcing that users share 350 million images daily via Snapchat. This figure was 200 million in June and 150 million in April.

　　Snapchat's investor Institutional Ventures Partners blogged in June this year: "Snapchat's user engagement is extremely high."

　　Unique mechanism

　　Snapchat is one of the pioneers of the "disappearing message" model. Users can send pictures and videos to friends via Snapchat, along with text. After a few seconds of viewing, these contents automatically disappear. Recently, Snapchat launched a potentially Next Big Thing feature called Story, allowing users to tell stories to their circle of friends; photos taken on the day can be replayed within 24 hours, no longer disappearing immediately after being viewed.

　　The "disappearing message" model holds great appeal for many users, allowing them to better protect their privacy. Many users hesitate when sending photos to Twitter, Instagram, and other social media platforms, because Twitter displays users' photos globally, and if users forget to delete them, Twitter keeps the photos on their pages indefinitely. Snapchat's "disappearing message" mode effectively addresses user concerns, especially among teenage users.

　　This Tuesday, Bill Gurley, a partner at Benchmark Capital, one of Snapchat's investors, said: "30% of university admissions officers browse admission applications online. They first check the applicant's average grades, then go look at their Twitter accounts." If your Twitter contains some "inappropriate content," the admissions officer may very well see it.

　　Market significance

　　For Facebook, Snapchat carries some crucial keywords: mobile, messaging, images, young users. Although Snapchat isn't the largest provider of mobile text and image information services, for companies like Facebook, Snapchat is undoubtedly a dark horse, worth taking a bet on and investing in.
