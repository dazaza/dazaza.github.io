Must-read for people who spend 4-6 hours a day in front of a computer

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 12:06:56

Authors: Albert_JIAO, Xiao Ji

An article titled "Computer Radiation - A Must-Read for People Who Sit in Front of a Computer for 4-6 Hours a Day" has been circulating online for a long time. Despite the continued popularity of cacti and anti-radiation clothing, let's revisit some old claims, gather together the rumors about computer radiation and so-called "quick fixes," and debunk them all at once!

Rumor One: Remember to turn off your computer when you sleep at noon because just turning off the screen won't stop the radiation. Moreover, we usually sleep with our heads directly facing the computer. If one day we get Alzheimer's disease or a brain tumor, it will be too late! Radiation is really terrifying!! Be careful~ Health comes first!

It is true that turning off only the screen without shutting down the computer still results in radiation because various components inside the computer case also emit radiation. However, there is currently no definitive evidence linking the electromagnetic radiation produced by computer components to Alzheimer's disease or brain tumors.

Rumor Two: The Killer of Computer Users - Thoracic Outlet Syndrome. Are you someone who often sits in front of a computer desk for hours with poor posture, frequently feeling inexplicable shoulder and neck pain, even losing focus on work? Now, please take a small test. Look to your left side, then slowly bend your head down at a 45-degree angle. At this point, do you feel abnormal soreness in your neck and shoulders? If you experience these symptoms, beware! You may likely be a victim of modern computer civilization disease, 'Thoracic Outlet Syndrome.'

Thoracic outlet syndrome is a relatively rare condition, primarily caused by congenital anatomical abnormalities (such as an extra cervical rib). Additionally, people with longer necks or sunken shoulders are more prone to this condition. Although poor posture is related to the disease—long-term slouching may exacerbate symptoms—it is not necessarily linked to using a computer. Even without a computer, poor living habits can still cause pain. Therefore, maintaining good posture is important rather than blaming the computer.

In addition, regardless of whether it is thoracic outlet syndrome, while using a computer, it is essential to maintain proper posture, avoid slouching, and don't sit in front of the computer for too long. If you experience numbness, pain, or weakness in your shoulders, neck, elbows, or hands, consult a professional clinician instead of self-diagnosing or trying unverified remedies, therapies, or treatments. The small test mentioned in the rumor has little diagnostic value for this condition (the condition can occur on either the left or right side, so simply turning your neck to the left is not useful).

Rumor Three: The Killer Turns Out to Be "Computers." You, a computer user, might wonder why you often feel lower back and neck pain, weakening physical resistance, and difficulty concentrating. You would never imagine that the reason lies in "computers." The radiation emitted by computers is often overlooked. According to international MPRⅡ anti-radiation safety regulations, the radiation exposure must be ≤25V/m within a 50cm distance. But do you know how much radiation your computer emits? Let me tell you — I. Keyboard 1000V/m; II. Mouse 450V/m; III. Screen 218V/m; IV. Host 170V/m; V. Notebook 2500V/m.

Fact: The MPRII standard does indeed exist. It is a Swedish technical approval authority standard for electromagnetic radiation from computer monitors. However, it doesn't apply to other computer components. The 25V/m radiation exposure limit is also correct (at a 50cm position, not within 50cm; limited to lower frequency radiation between 5Hz and 2000Hz). This value is based on existing research on electromagnetic radiation and health, setting a strict limit. If a computer monitor's radiation level is within the standard, its safety is very reassuring. Even if the radiation approaches or slightly exceeds the standard, it doesn't mean significant harm to the human body, only some minor risks. Such safety standards are set with "just in case" in mind.

As for the radiation values of keyboards, mice, and other computer components, they are completely unreliable. According to a measurement experiment conducted by the China Academy of Metrology, the actual radiation levels of these computer components are mostly less than 1V/m, and only 6V/m at 5cm away from a notebook computer screen, far from being exaggerated. An electric field intensity of 2500V/m would only be reached near high-voltage lines, rarely found in daily household appliances.

First Anti-Radiation Tip: Place a few pots of cactus next to your computer; it can effectively absorb radiation;

Fact: The electromagnetic radiation or electromagnetic waves emitted by a computer in all directions are mainly determined by the computer itself, and cactus cannot shield or adsorb them. More importantly, the electromagnetic radiation level of a computer does not pose any health hazard, making shielding unnecessary. For more detailed information, refer to "Washing Face + Cactus, Preventing Computer Radiation?"

Second Tip: For people living under stress and busyness, the simplest way to guard against computer radiation is to drink 2 to 3 cups of green tea and eat an orange every morning. Tea contains abundant vitamin A precursors, which convert into vitamin A after being absorbed by the human body. Vitamin A not only synthesizes rhodopsin but also makes eyes see clearer in dim light. Therefore, green tea not only eliminates the hazards of computer radiation but also protects and enhances vision. If you don't like green tea, chrysanthemum tea can also resist computer radiation and regulate bodily functions. Spirulina and sea buckthorn oil also have anti-radiation effects;

Firstly, there is no evidence showing that the radiation emitted by computers adversely affects health, so special protection is unnecessary.

Green tea does contain rich β-carotene (a precursor of vitamin A), but carotenoids are fat-soluble substances, and the amount dissolved in tea is minimal. Thus, green tea is not the best source of vitamin A supplementation. Generally speaking, normal daily diets provide sufficient vitamin A or β-carotene. A deficiency in vitamin A causes night blindness, but additional supplementation of β-carotene does not provide any special benefits, and excessive intake of vitamin A can lead to poisoning. In short, the key to healthy eating is balanced nutrition, and consuming large amounts of single foods over a short period provides no special health benefits. The claims about the anti-radiation effects of chrysanthemum tea, spirulina, and sea buckthorn oil are equally baseless.

Third Tip: Before going online, prepare your skin with protective isolation measures, such as using pearl cream. Its unique "Southern Pearl Green Membrane" forms a layer of 0.001mm pearl film on the skin, effectively preventing pollution and radiation; secondly, after using the computer, your face will attract many electromagnetic radiation particles, and washing your face with clean water will reduce the radiation by more than 70%!

The electromagnetic radiation from computers mainly originates from the circuits inside the computer and does not produce particles similar to radioactive substances. When sitting in front of a computer with it turned on, the body begins to receive electromagnetic radiation, and once the computer is turned off, the electromagnetic radiation stops immediately. There will be no "residual particles" on your face, so there is no need to wash them off. Traditional CRT monitors may attract some dust due to static electricity, but these dust particles themselves do not generate electromagnetic radiation, nor do they cause radiation damage when they fall on your face.

In short, cleaning your face is necessary, but it has nothing to do with preventing radiation.

Fourth Tip: When operating a computer, it is best to install a dedicated computer filter plate on the display to reduce the hazards of radiation. Avoid placing miscellaneous metal objects indoors to prevent secondary electromagnetic wave emissions. Adjust the brightness of the screen properly when using the computer. Generally speaking, the brighter the screen, the stronger the electromagnetic radiation, and vice versa. However, it should not be adjusted too dimly, as it may affect effectiveness and easily cause eye fatigue.

A filter plate can only filter out some visible light, while the radiation we refer to from computers is generally radio waves or radiofrequency electromagnetic waves, for which the filter plate is ineffective. Placing metal objects around can influence the distribution of the electromagnetic field, but often negligibly. As for the relationship between screen brightness and total computer radiation, it matters little since each component of the computer generates radiation, and the CPU and other elements inside the case are the main sources of radiation.

Fifth Tip: Try to buy new computers and avoid using old ones. Old computers generally emit stronger radiation, usually 1-2 times that of new computers under the same distance and model conditions.

This sounds like an advertisement for selling computers. The main difference between new and old computers lies in performance aspects. There is no basis for the claim that "old computers emit stronger radiation, usually 1-2 times that of new computers." To reiterate, the level of electromagnetic radiation from computers does not pose a health hazard.

Sixth Tip: The placement of the computer is crucial. Avoid positioning the back of the screen toward areas where people are present because the strongest computer radiation comes from the back, followed by the sides, while the front of the screen emits the weakest radiation. Maintain a distance of at least 50 to 75 centimeters to see the text clearly, which can reduce the harm of electromagnetic radiation.

As mentioned earlier, the computer display, as a component of the computer, produces limited radiation. The amount of radiation is not solely determined by the position of the display but also by the location of the case. Choosing to stay farther from the computer makes sense because the intensity of electromagnetic radiation decreases with the square of the distance. Staying farther reduces radiation significantly, but it remains within safe standards, so the significance is minimal. Distance and protecting vision are separate topics.

Seventh Tip: Pay attention to indoor ventilation. Scientific research confirms that computer screens can produce a carcinogenic substance called brominated dibenzofuran. Therefore, it's best to install a ventilator in the room where the computer is placed. If not, pay particular attention to ventilation while using the internet.

Brominated dibenzofuran is a product obtained through oxidative coupling of the plastic flame retardant polybrominated diphenyl ethers (PBDEs). This oxidation process occurs more commonly during plastic combustion and rarely during regular use. Actually, PBDEs themselves are pollutants of concern, found in many products beyond just computers. They accumulate in the environment due to their slow metabolism, contaminating water and food sources. Improper disposal of plastic waste is a significant pathway for the release of PBDEs and the production of brominated dibenzofuran.

This claim likely stems from early suspicions about television plastic casings and was later applied to computer screens. Of course, paying attention to ventilation indoors is always a good idea.

Eighth Tip: Pay attention to eating more foods rich in vitamins A, C, and proteins, such as carrots, bean sprouts, tomatoes, lean meat, animal liver, etc., and drinking green tea regularly.

Refer to the analysis of the second tip. Nutritional balance in food consumption is essential, combining both meat and vegetables. Focusing on eating certain foods while neglecting other nutrients is counterproductive. All nutrient intakes should be moderate—not too little and not too much. Specifically, vitamin A intake exceeding 3×10^5 μg retinol equivalents (approximately 6 kg of pork liver) in adults or 9×10^4 μg (approximately 1.8 kg of pork liver) in children can cause acute poisoning. Long-term daily intake of 2.25-3×10^4 μg retinol equivalents (approximately 450-600 g of pork liver) in adults or 1.5-3×10^4 μg (approximately 300-600 g of pork liver) in children for over six months can result in chronic poisoning. Therefore, excessive intake of vitamin A-rich foods is harmful.

Ninth Tip: People who frequently work in front of computers often experience dry and painful eyes. Hence, placing a few bananas on the computer desk is necessary. The potassium in bananas helps the body excrete excess salt, balancing potassium and sodium levels, relieving discomfort in the eyes. Furthermore, bananas contain large amounts of β-carotene. When the body lacks this substance, the eyes become painful, dry, lack luster, and appear dehydrated and dull. Eating more bananas not only alleviates these symptoms but also eases eye fatigue and prevents premature aging of the eyes to a certain extent.

Refer to the analysis of the second tip. Supplementing β-carotene benefits those with vitamin A deficiency, but it offers no extra benefits to those who are not deficient. General eye dryness caused by prolonged computer use is due to visual fatigue, requiring periodic breaks every 45-60 minutes, lasting about 15 minutes, to avoid excessive eye strain. Bananas are indeed rich in potassium, and appropriately increasing potassium intake while reducing sodium intake benefits hypertensive patients. However, calculating the daily intake of any nutrient is essential, and relying on a single food for a specific nutrient is inappropriate and impossible.

Conclusion: People who use computers for long periods should indeed maintain proper posture, take appropriate breaks, and relieve eye fatigue. However, the widely publicized radiation hazards are baseless, and relying on eating certain foods to prevent radiation is equally unreliable. A balanced diet with proper nutritional combinations is sufficient, and moderation is key.
