I hope the iWatch can do these 11 things.

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 10:50:17

After the launch of the iPhone 5s, we may have been eagerly awaiting another device: the much-rumored iWatch. Apple stated that the M7 processor can support highly complex human motion tracking, understanding whether you are walking, running, or even driving. It sounds like this processor has the potential to power a biometric-focused smartwatch.

Apple needs such a processor. Tim Cook commented at the AllThingsD conference that excellent wearable devices on the market today only excel in one aspect, while multi-functional devices cannot perform well across all areas. This hints at Apple's primary goal: a device that performs well in every aspect.

But what are these various "aspects"? For the iWatch, we categorize them into three major categories: optimizing life, hands-free functionality, and aesthetics.

Optimizing Life

Most products on the market today aim to optimize users' lives by tracking health trends. Nike's Fuelband, Jawbone's Up, Fitbit, Misfit's Shine, Basis, and others operate under the premise that our behaviors need to be quantified, identifying trends and adjusting our lives to move more and sleep better.

Durability throughout the day. To optimize life, the first rule is that users must live with this device—whether sleeping, working, bathing, watching TV, exercising, reading, washing dishes, or taking out the trash, it will always be with you. I don’t want to worry about removing my iWatch. Removing it means two things: one, I am no longer tracking my behavior, so even if I engage in various activities, I won't get credit for them; two, I might forget to put it back on. Living with it means the iWatch needs to be durable, waterproof, and comfortable enough (not interfering with sleep). Additionally, the device's battery life should be strong, lasting several days on a single charge.

Biometrics. Wearable devices on the market today promise to help us identify patterns in our behavior and improve upon them. I hope the iWatch can track all of my activities, just like Jawbone Up, Fitbit, Fuelband, and other products. However, I also hope it goes further than other devices. Basis showcases advanced biometrics, tracking sweat and pulse rates, and counting aerobic exercises—things many other wearable wristbands cannot do. The iWatch should also track my sleep patterns: light sleep, deep sleep, when I fall asleep and wake up during the night.

Smart activity tracking. One of the biggest shortcomings of current wearable devices is the lack of intelligence. They track how many steps we take, extrapolate that into a points system, and calculate distance traveled and calories burned. However, a smart device could understand my behavior, helping me not only identify how to save time but also recognize which behaviors can improve my life.

First, the device should know when I go to bed without manual input (Jawbone Up and Misfit Shine both require this). The device should know when I am watching TV or reading, how long I spend eating breakfast, lunch, and dinner, how long I spend commuting by train, working, and showering. The device should be able to differentiate between running, walking, and cycling. In short, the device should provide me with a detailed overview of how I live and how each behavior affects my overall health. If running burns calories more effectively than cycling, I should know that. If I watch TV for five hours, the device should recognize this trend.

Going beyond simple behavioral tracking means the device understands user activity at an unprecedented level, providing opportunities for users to not only move more but also save time.

Smart notifications. Alongside intelligent tracking, there should also be intelligent notification features. If I spend too much time watching TV, stay up too late, or wake up late in the morning, I should be notified. Additionally, by tracking my sleep habits and schedule, my iWatch should wake me up at the right time.

However, the iWatch can go even further.

For example, I commute daily by train to Boston for work. This train departs and arrives on time every day, with only a few minutes delay a few times a month. But those few minutes are critical because I always arrive at the last minute. Therefore, I hope the iWatch can help remind me so I don’t have to rush.

My iWatch should know how long it takes to get to the train station (about 15 minutes), what time it is now, when the train is expected to arrive, when I should leave, and adjust immediately when the train is delayed.

Smart notifications should add more rationality to my busy life and not just identify trends through the creation of new, better habits. Apple has already touched on this with the Frequent Locations feature in iOS 7, and their recent acquisition of the personal assistant app Cue provides additional support.

Hands-Free Functionality

Given the screen size, Big Brother iPhone has some functions that the iWatch cannot achieve. However, the iPhone has some tasks that can be replicated on the iWatch, allowing us to avoid reaching into our pockets or wallets for our phones.

Payment. Achieving a reasonable level of security, perhaps even fingerprint scanning like on the iPhone 5s, the iWatch could become a perfect payment device. Tap, scan, or simply approach a specific payment device, confirm the purchase, and then leave without needing to take out your phone. Apps like LevelUp can run smoothly on the iWatch (MasterCard's Watch2Pay has already launched and is operating in the UK).

Ticketing. Whether it’s an MFA exhibition or a Red Sox game, Amtrak or JetBlue, all ticketing can be completed via the iWatch. I shouldn’t have to pull out my wallet or phone to enter an exhibition or transportation. Apple's Passport app is already moving in this direction and is perfectly suited for use on the iWatch.

Lists. There are many great apps that can help me with grocery shopping. However, checking this list on the iPhone is cumbersome. I need to take my phone out of my pocket, enter a security code, and open the app. If the list could be displayed on the iWatch, despite the limited screen size, it would still be a good experience.

Siri and Maps. Although Siri may not be everyone's favorite personal assistant, I hope to use the app on the iWatch. With limited screen space, there won’t be many opportunities for searching or browsing. If I need to reply to a message directly on the iWatch, Siri will definitely be convenient. I hope Siri can also connect to maps, allowing me to have a super simple map on my wrist when I walk somewhere, without needing to take out my phone, enter a password (or fingerprint), and check the map. I hope to see arrows, street names, and the time of the next turn.

Smart connectivity. One of the most notable features of the iWatch is the ability to control the iPhone from the wrist. Adjusting music volume, seeing who's calling, reading messages, accepting calendar invitations—all of these can be done. This is the least appealing feature of the iWatch to me, but it is a necessary part of the software experience.

Aesthetics

Style. There are already many beautiful wearable devices on the market. Jawbone Up is a stunning, flashy wristband. Misfit Shine looks like some kind of futuristic jewelry. Basis resembles a watch from the 1980s. Jawbone's Up, Nike's Fuelband, and Samsung's Gear Watch come in multiple colors, offering various options to the market. However, watches are very personalized items that can reflect your status, so the iWatch needs to go beyond color and at least offer separate models for men and women.

Price? Considering that most wearable wristbands are in the $90 to $300 range, I hope the iWatch falls within this range.

Apple faces the challenge that the traditional starting price of the iPhone has been $199, which has formed a habit in many people's minds. An iWatch priced higher than the iPhone might seem inappropriate to some. However, since the starting price of the iPhone 5c is $99, this price comparison might change.

In any case, for the iWatch, $199 would be a huge win and make it easily accessible for purchase.
