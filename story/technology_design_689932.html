Title: Complete Guide to Free Wireless Internet Access

Author: Unknown

When you arrive with your laptop at an unfamiliar place without wired internet access, how can you get online? Do you have to buy an expensive "EasyConnect" wireless card? Actually, there are many places with free "hotspots"! So, how do you find out which hotspots are nearby? How do you check the connection speed of a wireless network? Many times we rely on experience, for example, every Starbucks offers free WiFi. But this choice is too narrow; you can't go to Starbucks every time you want to use the internet while outside. Next, we will tell you how to find these "hotspots"!

Tip: What is a "hotspot"?

A "hotspot," or Hotspot, refers to places that offer free or paid WiFi services. Essentially, these places have installed wireless routers and provide wireless internet signals. Generally, airports, Starbucks, McDonald's, KFC, hotels, and other recreational venues have hotspots.

1. Quickly Find Free Wireless Networks

Hoju.cn (http://www.hoju.cn) is a community website specifically for hotspot searches and sharing. You can find which places in your area offer hotspots from here. Open http://www.hoju.cn/v1/redianfenbu.php,www.fzfp2012.com, select your region and venue, then click "Show Hotspots" to find results matching your criteria. (See Figure 1)

Additionally, this site uses Google Maps to create a nationwide hotspot distribution map. Enter http://map.hoju.cn/, input the city, business district, or hotspot name in the bottom right corner of the page to search for results. Select one of the results, and the map will display the hotspot's location and mark it. Clicking on the marker will show detailed information about the hotspot's address and contact number. (See Figure 2)

2. Wireless Should Be High-Speed!

Using Hoju.cn can help you find some hotspots, but if you're already somewhere and want to know if there are any nearby hotspots, it's actually quite simple. Go to http://www.skycn.com/soft/16456.html and download/install Netstumbler. Once you expand "Channels," Netstumbler starts detecting and searching networks, and after a short wait, you'll see the results.

The results include SSID, MAC address, network speed (Speed), network access type (Type), and whether WEP encryption (Encryption) is enabled. Through the "Speed" field, you can see the wireless bandwidth of the hotspot (see Figure 3). At this point, all you need to do is connect your wireless card to the appropriate AP, and you can start browsing the web. It’s worth noting that NetStumbler can also display wireless APs with hidden SSIDs, indicated by a flashing green light in the software interface.

Fun Fact: What is an SSID?

SSID (Service Set Identifier) is used to differentiate between various clients in a wireless network. When setting up a wireless access point/router or network adapter, this name is created. This name is also the name of the wireless network you wish to connect to. All wireless devices attempting to communicate must share the same SSID.

3. Is the Signal Strong Enough Here?

Besides scanning wireless access points, you can also use Netstumbler to detect the strength of the wireless signal at different locations. For example, at home, if you place the wireless AP in the study, you might want to know the coverage and signal strength in the living room, bedroom, kitchen, etc. In NetStumbler, select the wireless AP and observe the signal stability over a period of time. As time passes, you can intuitively understand the signal strength. (See Figure 4)

Figure 3

Through this method, you can determine where the signal is better and how to place the wireless AP so that the areas where you need mobile internet have sufficient coverage.

4. There's No Such Thing as "Encryption" in My Dictionary

For ordinary software, when encountering WEP encryption, you cannot see the structure of the wireless network. However, a tool called AiroPeek can achieve this. AiroPeek is not just a pure wireless AP detection tool; it has packet sniffing and analysis capabilities similar to Sniffer software, decoding 802.11a/b/g protocols and displaying management packets, control packets, and data packets.

Figure 4

Tip:

AiroPeek supports decoding WEP-encrypted data streams of varying lengths and can perform air decoding through multiple key naming methods. It includes a convenient command line to decode captured encrypted packet files, so even WEP-encrypted wireless signals cannot escape AiroPeek's "fingers."

Download and run AiroPeek, and you can see all the IP addresses and data packet transmission statistics of nodes in the wireless network. AiroPeek displays the network topology in an intuitive way, allowing you to clearly understand the network structure (see Figure 5). The data packets contain various information. Besides seeing the source (Source) and destination (Destination) addresses, the protocol (Protocol) allows us to sort and see all packets related to 802.11. If needed, you can further analyze the content of the data packets.
