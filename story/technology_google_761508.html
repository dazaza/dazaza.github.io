Can an Android phone lock on to a sniper?

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 14:43:22

American law allows citizens to legally own guns, but this also leads to frequent shooting incidents across the U.S. The military has long possessed high-tech equipment that can determine the location of a sniper's shot through microphones carried by soldiers or fixed installations. Recently, a team of computer engineers from Vanderbilt University in the U.S. has developed a compact, affordable civilian-grade sniper tracking system compatible with Android smartphones.The research team from Vanderbilt University introduced this system at the 12th ACM/IEEE Conference on Information Processing in Sensor Networks: the sensor module is about the size of a deck of cards. When a gunshot occurs, the sensor records the sound and exact time of the gunshot, then transmits the data to a smartphone via Bluetooth. If there are other smartphones equipped with these modules in the local wireless network, the exact location of the sniper's shot can be immediately determined.This system comes in two versions: one uses a single microphone in each sensor module, but requires at least six independent phones to accurately calculate the location of the shot; the other is equipped with four microphones (slightly larger in size) in each sensor module, but only needs two phones to lock onto the shooting position.This system utilizes the principle that two distinct sounds are produced during a gunshot: one is the airflow shockwave from the gun muzzle, which spreads out in all directions as a spherical wave; the other is the sonic boom generated by the bullet's motion, forming a cone-shaped wave with the bullet as the apex. Based on the time it takes for these two sounds to reach the microphone, combined with data received from microphones in different positions, the location of the shot can be determined.Relevant researchers stated that they initially considered using the built-in microphones of smartphones, but found the sampling rate to be less than ideal. Moreover, most phone manufacturers apply noise reduction features to improve call quality in noisy environments, which could also affect the use of the aforementioned gunshot tracking system.This system is applicable for both official and civilian security work. Researchers also mentioned that the U.S. Secret Service has shown great interest in the system, even requesting specific solutions from them.
