"War Horse": If Spielberg were replaced by a Chinese director

By Ma Qingyun

Last night, I watched the movie "War Horse." After finishing it, I asked the woman beside me how she felt about it. She said, "It's not as good as you expected. If Spielberg were replaced by a Chinese director, this play would definitely fail." The movie "War Horse" tells the story of a boy's affection for a war horse, and their relationship is set against the backdrop of World War I. The focus is on the bond between man and horse, while the anti-war elements are few and far between. In China, there is also a movie that deals with the emotional connection between humans and large animals, called "Walking Together," adapted from the novel "Double Donkey Story."

From my personal perspective, the artistic value of "Walking Together" may be greater, whereas "War Horse" has less to offer in terms of depth or complexity. "War Horse" might simply be seen as a superficial children's tale wrapped around a coming-of-age experience. "War Horse" is a movie better suited for people in their early twenties, while "Walking Together" appeals more to those in their forties.

As the lady mentioned at the beginning, if "War Horse" were to be directed by a mainland Chinese director, it would undoubtedly fail. The success of "War Horse" can largely be attributed to the "Spielberg" label. Spielberg himself has numerous successful works under his belt, giving him the capital to produce such a film centered around the innocence of childhood during the backdrop of World War I. I venture to guess that the bond between man and horse could very well be something Spielberg himself experienced, otherwise why would he choose such a simple story to make into a movie? Director Li Yufeng is one of the young directors I am familiar with. His style leans towards Western cinema, with little attention paid to local Chinese themes. Many of the narrative shots in "War Horse" are similar to those often used by Li Yufeng. But what if "War Horse" were to be directed by a new talent like Li Yufeng?

Firstly, would you dare to attempt a long story lasting over 140 minutes?

Most Chinese films cater to fast-food consumption, and very few directors dare to make movies exceeding 100 minutes. Those who dare to make movies over 120 minutes fall into two categories: either they are truly talented like Jiang Wen and Zhang Yimou, or they are pretenders posing as top-tier directors. Apart from Zhang Yimou, perhaps only Jiang Wen and Wang Quanan, the director of "White Deer Plain," would dare to attempt such a lengthy film. In the Chinese film market, aside from the four major directors (Zhang, Chen, Feng, and Jiang), no one else has the "qualification" to make long films. A newcomer like Li Yufeng would have his script rejected outright if it were too long.

Secondly, would you dare to pitch such a slow-paced script to investors?

According to the fast-food consumption habits of mainland China, "War Horse" appears to have a redundant and slow-moving plot. Many scenes are overly detailed, and there are numerous emotional sequences that lack clarity and conciseness. For instance, to portray the bond between man and horse, the film spends over 40 minutes focusing on the English countryside, slowly narrating how the mare gives birth, how the foal grows up, its subsequent auction, the father's purchase of the horse, the boy's bonding with the horse, and the horse's initial attempts at plowing. This slow build-up is characteristic of many Republican-era novels, which, though tedious, provides a clear background that enhances the credibility of the story as it progresses. However, contemporary Chinese films often do not spend much time on setting up the story background, usually resolving everything within ten minutes. As an audience, we are lazy, preferring quick meals to patiently simmering soups. From this perspective, even someone like Li Yufeng, skilled in Western styles, would struggle to secure funding for a script like "War Horse."

Thirdly, would Li Yufeng receive criticism for directing such a loose and disorganized story?

Chinese narratives tend to follow a web-like structure, with multiple threads weaving together seamlessly. "War Horse," on the other hand, is a linear story. Any segment removed would disrupt the entire narrative flow. Some critics point out that the entire story hinges on a single horse that connects various characters—British youth, British officers, German soldiers, German farmers, and British military forces. This design is clever but lacks an overall framework, proceeding step-by-step without much planning. Since it's a linear story, as long as the horse keeps changing hands, the story continues. You could replace the owners dozens of times and turn it into a TV series. Without adhering to the web-like narrative structure common in Chinese films, "War Horse" seems loosely constructed and fabricated.

Following this narrative pattern, one could imagine a Chinese version of the story involving a red horse named Chi Tu. Initially owned by a farmer's child for plowing fields, the horse later gets conscripted by Dong Zhuo, then follows Lü Bu, and finally ends up with Guan Yu after Lü Bu's death. The horse then accompanies Diaochan when Guan Yu captures her. Later, Diaochan has an affair with one of Guan Yu's attendants, leading to Guan Yu's betrayal. The horse then falls into Eastern Wu's possession, but fearing retaliation from Shu, the horse is sent to Wei. On the way, the horse encounters its original farmer owner, now a soldier searching for Chi Tu. Once reunited, the farmer returns home, and he and the horse resume their peaceful life of farming together. This story also portrays the close bond between the farmer's child and the horse Chi Tu.

The above story is a deconstruction of "War Horse." If such a movie were made in China, it would be considered absurd, yet Spielberg manages to pull it off, probably because foreign monks know how to chant scriptures better!

Fourthly, would Li Yufeng's version of "War Horse" dare to include several minutes of agricultural scenes?

The answer is definite. Which Chinese director has ever included agricultural scenes in their work? These seemingly insignificant details are what Chinese directors dare not confront—of course, there are two reasons for this. First, the short-sightedness of Chinese film investors who reject such topics, and second, Chinese audiences simply do not watch rural-themed films, unwilling to spend money to see scenes of plowing fields, even though these scenes can be quite captivating! In last year's film "Red Cliff," despite its mafia-style love triangle theme, there was a scene where Cao Cao and Yun Chang harvested wheat, but it was done poorly. Directors, please go out and harvest some wheat yourselves before filming such scenes! This is the current state—Chinese directors dare not and cannot create agricultural scenes like those in "War Horse."

Fifthly, would Li Yufeng dare to direct such a shallow play?

In the third point, I deconstructed "War Horse." This play is essentially just a story about the unbroken bond between man and horse, a child's tale, with Spielberg adding elements of war unnecessarily. Such emotional dramas are viewed as lacking depth by Chinese investors and critics. Similar to Zhang Yimou's "Under the Hawthorn Tree," we all think Old Zhang is pretending to be pure, but when we reflect, isn't Spielberg also pretending to be pure? A Chinese director attempting to direct such a "pure" drama would face criticism, so why don't we criticize Spielberg, the old foreigner, in the same way?

Sixthly, isn't "War Horse" also a politically charged play?

Does Spielberg avoid politics? Does Hollywood avoid politics? Carefully examine "War Horse"—all the good deeds are done by the British army, spending lots of money to buy the boy's war horse, writing letters to the boy, and keeping records of the horse's condition. On the German side, the Germans are portrayed as stealing money, food, and women, forcibly conscripting horses, and executing deserters. Don't assume these portrayals are unintentional; they are deliberate choices made by Spielberg's team. The German army during World War I wasn't much better, but neither was the British army significantly superior. If a Chinese director like Li Yufeng or Zhang Yimou were to create a similar story set against the backdrop of fighting Japanese invaders, portraying the Nationalist army buying horses and sending updates to children while depicting the Japanese army as stealing money, food, and women, Hollywood would likely criticize them for being overly political. In reality, Hollywood films are equally political. Regardless of who directs them, carrying such political undertones is inappropriate, and we shouldn't ignore this issue just because the director is named Spielberg.

Finally, I want to say that if this movie were directed by a Chinese director, it would face at least six major challenges. Setting these issues aside, the most important point I want to make is that Spielberg's "War Horse" might be funded by the Freemasons. Whether it's World War I or II, in Europe, these wars were often fought between non-Freemason countries and those controlled by the Freemasons—or their predecessors. Most of the time, the Freemason-controlled countries emerged victorious. Therefore, in historical accounts, Germany is often depicted negatively, and Nazism is portrayed as evil. When everyone says it's bad, there's something worth examining. I believe killing is wrong, and based on this, we should condemn Nazism. However, the scale of Nazi atrocities shouldn't prevent us from questioning the reasons behind them. We should investigate further to uncover what lies behind the scenes. Movies like "War Horse" frequently depict the German army as evil and the armies of Freemason-controlled countries as good. It’s worth reflecting on this aspect, perhaps as a small curiosity outside the realm of academic study.
