Apple Has $200 Billion In Cash: Where's The Next Opportunity?

by anonymous on 2013-08-20 15:58:51

Introduction: Silicon Valley entrepreneur Mark Rogowsky published an article on Forbes titled "Apple Has $200 Billion To Spend, It's Time To Go Big," suggesting that based on its current growth rate, Apple will soon have $200 billion in cash reserves. The company should now consider how to leverage this substantial amount of capital to expand its business.

Summary:

Looking at Apple’s competitors, each possesses tangible and intangible assets that make them seemingly indestructible.

Take Amazon for example, which continuously invests in e-commerce infrastructure with the expectation of eventual high returns. Google dominates the global search engine market, supporting a remarkable keyword advertising business, while long-term creative projects like self-driving cars assure its longevity. Samsung leads in smartphone market share and boasts near-perfect vertical integration in smartphone components. Despite this, Apple generates far more profit through its iPhone model than all these companies combined.

After launching a series of great products such as the iPod, iPhone, MacBook Air, and iPad, Apple risks becoming complacent, while consumers frequently expect even more magical products from the brand. Critics naturally argue that due to lower consumer upgrade frequencies, Apple's product profit margins will eventually decline. Furthermore, without another groundbreaking product, profits are also expected to fall.

However, these negative comments overlook the significant opportunities Apple has: converting the world's most loyal users into a sustainable revenue stream. The key question is whether Apple can seize these opportunities before they vanish.

In comparison to Microsoft, Apple holds several advantages that Microsoft lacks. First, Apple has a great retail platform and network app store but hasn't yet sold many products or services to users. Second, tablet and smartphone businesses are still in their infancy, offering Apple numerous revenue-generating opportunities as it helps mature these markets. Third, compared to Microsoft's market capitalization in 1999 and the entire stock market, Apple's valuation remains relatively low. Fourth, with over $140 billion in cash and profits, averaging $10 billion per quarter in profits for the first three quarters of this year, Apple will easily accumulate $200 billion in cash within the next five years to contemplate the company's future.

Apple has proactive policies regarding debt issuance or using shares for mergers and acquisitions, and it could easily double this amount in the future. Excluding regulatory concerns and the fact that Google founders wouldn’t agree to sell, Apple has enough money to acquire Google.

Where should this money be allocated?

1. Mobile Advertising

Apple launched iTunes Radio to strengthen its advertising offerings. While many apps already include advertisements, iAd, Apple's standalone ad product, has performed poorly. I've previously stated that the chances of iAd achieving significant success are slim. To develop a mobile advertising business, Apple needs to acquire rather than develop internally; Millennial Media's mobile ad platform is quite affordable. As Facebook earns substantial profits by selling ads to promote apps, Apple currently has zero income opportunities in this area. A conservative estimate places this as a potential $1 billion business.

2. Supply Chain Integration

In the era of the iPod and the original iPhone, Apple procured the best components at optimal prices, but now its biggest competitor, Samsung, starts producing most of its components for itself. Although Apple designs its own chips, it cannot manufacture them independently and must outsource production to Samsung.

Besides, Apple relies on Qualcomm for wireless components and multiple suppliers for displays, none of which fully meet Apple's requirements. Why? Because the best display manufacturer is Samsung. To gain access to more advanced IGZO display technology, Apple waited two years, ultimately relying on nearly bankrupt Sharp and perpetually lagging LG. This situation shouldn't have arisen because engineering problems could be overcome with financial investment.

3. Creating Markets

The combination of the iPod with iTunes or Kindle devices with bookstores represents classic examples of creating markets. If you want to sell music or e-books online, invent an electronic bookstore or e-reader. What if better smartphones were sold in India to consumers who wouldn't buy 3G or 4G data services due to cost?

Acquiring a national mobile carrier would suffice, then customizing Indian iPhones and special data plans could make Indian consumers dependent on Apple devices for long-term profitability. Currently, India accounts for 3% of the global smartphone market, and Apple only holds 2% of India's smartphone market.

With just a few billion dollars in investment, Apple could create an entirely different business model in India than in the U.S., making it an undoubtedly exciting venture given the massive scale of the Indian market.

4. Television

Apple CEO Tim Cook's relationship with television mirrors our relationship with the weather – he constantly talks about TV but never actually does much related to it. If Apple enters this market, it could sell TVs to its highly loyal customers, which would be a good business. Apple TVs might even feature new apps from HBO or ESPN networks, rivaling Roku streaming set-top boxes.

Rumors suggest Apple's TV will rethink user experience, replacing the poor channel guides provided by most cable companies; integrating content from Netflix, cable providers, and iTunes; and offering new channel bundles that allow users to receive limited TV-like services for less.

All of these possibilities seem unlikely. If Apple is serious, it may follow Google's approach. More aggressively, it might even acquire NFL programming packages and put them online. A non-exclusive agreement to sell web-based programming could cost just $250 million annually, a trivial expense for Apple.

5. Payment Systems

Apple often mentions having 575 million accounts in the iTunes Music Store but hasn't truly utilized the payment information stored in these accounts. I don't understand why card numbers must always be re-entered on iPhones or online. Developing a service akin to PayPal would be a huge transaction engine benefiting both Apple and its users.

We should already have such a service, but instead, we have half-baked solutions like Square and Stripe. Similarly, Apple would need to acquire its way into this market. Perhaps the rumored fingerprint sensor integration in the iPhone 5S is the start.

6. Software and Services

Apple's software, app, and media business amounts to approximately $15 billion annually. Though considerable, it could grow further. The upcoming Pandora-like radio service is a start, but that's all. I’ve wondered multiple times why Apple doesn’t acquire Netflix? Both in terms of talent and culture, Netflix fits well with Apple. Although acquiring Netflix has become expensive, it's still feasible.

Additionally, Apple excels in developing alternatives to Microsoft Office but lags in sales. Currently, Apple earns $40 per iTunes account annually. For Apple, increasing this figure to $50 while doubling user numbers through market share growth is achievable.

7. More Frequent Product Releases or More SKUs

37, 35, 26, 27, 48, 37, and 31 — these numbers represent iPhone sales in millions for each of the last seven quarters. This "boom-and-bust" cycle is unsettling, especially since the iPhone constitutes half of Apple's business.

There are various viable solutions: one simple option is to release new products twice a year — a March release for S-type upgrades with minor changes and a September release for true next-generation products.

Moreover, introducing larger-screen phones is another choice. Currently, Apple loses those customers who prefer phones with screen sizes over four inches.

8. Corporate Market

Another thing Apple likes to discuss is how many Fortune 500 companies use iPads or iPhones, but this hasn't significantly boosted its enterprise sales. Instead, Apple should introduce more excellent asset management tools like MobileIron.

Additionally, Apple should have dedicated salespeople dealing with the Global 2000 companies, generating more revenue. If this means acquisitions, Apple should not hesitate to proceed.

Currently, many talented salespeople come from HP and Dell. Also, there are numerous small IT companies available for acquisition. Enterprises want to transition from PCs to tablets and cloud services, and Apple should assist them actively, not just applaud from the sidelines.
