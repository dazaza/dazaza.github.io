A few months ago, Aunt Dong, who suffered from hypertension, diabetes, and osteopetrosis, felt numbness and weakness in her limbs. It wasn't until recently, when she had difficulty holding chopsticks and walking, that she realized the severity of the problem and went to a hospital specializing in spinal diseases. Although the doctors here had treated various complex cervical spondylosis patients, her condition still shocked the doctors: her cervical spondylosis was very severe, and any carelessness could lead to paralysis. She also had complications such as hypertension and diabetes, which could result in heart problems or serious infections during or after surgery. But the most difficult issue was osteopetrosis, a rare genetic disease where the structure of all bones lacks normal bone composition, being as hard as stone, making it difficult for fractures to heal. According to previous surgical methods, there might be difficulties in spinal cord decompression, or accidental spinal cord injury leading to paralysis during surgery, or non-healing after surgery resulting in secondary paralysis. After consulting experts in the province, they had not dealt with patients with such complex lesions. After extensive research, the doctors decided on a surgical procedure of posterior cervical single-door decompression plus lateral mass internal fixation, and made detailed contingency plans. The surgery was successfully completed, and the postoperative recovery was also smooth.

Cervical spondylosis surgery, carefully operated by doctors and well-coordinated by patients, rarely leads to complications. Common complications include vagus nerve damage, recurrent laryngeal nerve damage, and superior laryngeal nerve damage. These damages can easily cause:

1. Choking on water, swallowing difficulties, hoarseness, and unclear speech.

2. Thyroid middle artery and thyroid upper artery damage may occur, which are often accompanied by the recurrent laryngeal nerve and superior laryngeal nerve to supply blood to the thyroid, and also participate in the blood supply to the cervical spine. Therefore, their damage may lead to abnormal function of the thyroid and spinal cord, thereby causing a series of clinical manifestations.

3. Vertebral artery damage, especially when removing bone spurs, often causes massive bleeding and reduced blood supply to the brain, sometimes also affecting the blood circulation of the cervical spine.

4. Nerve root and spinal cord damage can occur when using a ring saw to cut the intervertebral disc and scrape the posterior bone spurs of the vertebral body.

If you experience choking, swallowing difficulties, hoarseness, or voice difficulties after surgery, do not panic or fear, because this function is innervated by both sides of the nerves. The nerve function on the opposite side is normal, and generally, such nerve injuries are traction injuries without nerve rupture, so there is a possibility of recovery. With appropriate training, swallowing difficulties will gradually improve, and hoarseness will gradually disappear. If vertebral artery damage results in uncontrollable massive bleeding, a ligation surgery can be performed. Although it temporarily affects the blood flow to the brain and spinal cord, the opposite side will gradually compensate, and eventually, all damaged functions will gradually recover. As for the chance of spinal cord injury, it is also very low, and timely use of high-dose hormones during the operation will reduce spinal cord damage and prevent further aggravation of neurological symptoms after the operation.

Cervical spondylosis refers to the degenerative changes of the cervical disc, hypertrophy of the cervical vertebrae, and neck injuries that cause osteophyte formation of the cervical vertebrae, or protrusion of the intervertebral disc, thickening of the ligament, stimulating or compressing the cervical spinal cord, cervical nerves, and blood vessels, thereby producing a series of symptoms of clinical syndrome. The main symptoms include neck and shoulder pain, dizziness, headache, arm numbness, muscle atrophy, and in severe cases, spasticity of the lower limbs, difficulty walking, even limb numbness, urinary and fecal dysfunction, leading to paralysis. It is more common in middle-aged and elderly people, and the incidence rate in men is higher than in women.
