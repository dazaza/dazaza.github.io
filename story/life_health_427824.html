What is Colitis? It refers to the inflammation of the colon segment caused by specific or unknown causes, and in clinical practice, it can generally be divided into two types: acute colitis and chronic colitis. Patients with colitis often present symptoms such as diarrhea, mucus bloody stools, abdominal pain, and a sense of urgency after defecation. The disease course is long, with varying severity, and frequent recurrence. Patients with colitis have more bowel movements than usual, difficulty in defecation, loose stools, mostly thin or thick paste-like stools, with mucus or pus and blood in the stool, often accompanied by abdominal pain, bloating, and a sense of urgency after defecation. Colitis is also known as non-specific ulcerative colitis, commonly referred to as "ulcerative colitis" or "chronic non-specific colitis" in China. Ulcers are the main feature of colitis, with inflammation mainly located in the mucosal layer. Most cases of colitis develop slowly, with repeated episodes that gradually worsen, while some cases may occur acutely. The disease course can be continuous, chronic, or alternating between chronic and acute phases, occasionally presenting as an acute outbreak. Therefore, the treatment of colitis also requires a longer period. Many patients do not take colitis seriously and are unwilling to seek active treatment. As a chronic disease, the serious consequence of colitis is its development into colon cancer, so patients should highly value it and actively treat it to avoid delaying the condition.

Clinical manifestations of colitis:

● Diarrhea: Mucoid stools and bloody stools; mild colitis patients experience 3-4 bowel movements per day, severe cases may have dozens of bowel movements or alternate between diarrhea and constipation.

● Abdominal pain: Mild colitis patients may have no abdominal pain or only slight discomfort. Generally, they have mild to moderate abdominal pain, which is intermittent in the lower left abdomen or lower abdomen, involving the entire abdomen with a pattern of pain, urgency for defecation, and relief after defecation.

● Constipation: Colitis patients may have constipation with bowel movements every 4-5 days, with stool resembling sheep droppings, sometimes unable to defecate without laxatives.

● Other symptoms: Bloating, weight loss, fatigue, intestinal sounds, insomnia, excessive dreaming, and cold intolerance.

Common complications of colitis include:

● Massive rectal bleeding: Rectal bleeding is one of the main clinical manifestations of colitis, and the amount of bleeding is also an indicator of the severity of the condition.

● Intestinal stenosis: This often occurs in patients with extensive lesions lasting 5-25 years or more, commonly seen in the left half of the colon, sigmoid colon, or rectum. Clinically, there are usually no symptoms, but severe cases can cause intestinal obstruction. When colitis leads to intestinal stenosis, one must be vigilant for tumors and differentiate between benign and malignant conditions.

● Intestinal perforation: This is often a complication of toxic megacolon and can also occur in severe cases, most commonly in the left half of the colon. The use of corticosteroids is considered a risk factor for intestinal perforation.

● Toxic megacolon: This is a serious complication of colitis, most common in patients with pancolitis, with a mortality rate as high as 44%. Clinical manifestations include rapid deterioration of the condition, obvious toxic symptoms, accompanied by diarrhea, abdominal tenderness, and rebound tenderness, weakened or absent bowel sounds, increased white blood cell count, and easy occurrence of intestinal perforation.

● Colon cancer: About 5% of colitis patients develop cancer, especially those who started from childhood and have a history of more than 10 years.
