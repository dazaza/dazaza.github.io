Common sense of on-site packing operations for containerized cargo

by jintaimqr on 2012-02-22 16:14:38

The on-site packing operation of container goods [http://www.jt077.cn/index.php?m=wlps] usually has three methods: A, all manual packing; B, using forklifts to move the goods into the container and then manually stack them inside; C, fully mechanical packing, such as pallet goods stacked inside the container with a forklift. Among these three methods, the third one is the most ideal, with the highest loading and unloading efficiency and the least cargo damage accidents. However, even if all mechanical packing is used, cargo damage accidents can still occur during loading if the characteristics and packaging status of the goods are ignored or due to improper operation. Especially for containers loaded in inland areas, since the packers do not understand the state of the containers during maritime transportation, their loading methods often do not meet the requirements of maritime transportation, leading to cargo damage accidents. There are many examples of this. Below are some points to be noted when packing containerized cargo for reference.

1. In any case, the total weight of the goods packed in the container must not exceed the maximum loading capacity of the container. The maximum loading weight of the container is obtained by subtracting the self-weight of the container from the total weight; the total weight and self-weight are generally marked on the container door.

2. The unit volume weight of each container is fixed. Therefore, if a single type of cargo is loaded in the container, its density can determine whether it is heavy or light cargo. Goods with a density greater than the container's unit volume weight are considered heavy cargo, and the loading quantity is calculated based on weight. Conversely, goods with a density less than the container's unit volume weight are considered light cargo, and the loading quantity is calculated based on volume. Distinguishing between these two different situations is crucial for improving packing efficiency.

3. When loading, ensure that the load on the container floor is balanced, and the load in the container is not concentrated at one end or one side. Especially, strictly prohibit the situation where the center of gravity is biased toward one end.

4. Avoid concentrated loads. For example, when loading heavy machinery, lay wooden boards or other padding materials on the container bottom to distribute the load as much as possible. The standard safe load per unit area on the bottom surface of a container is approximately 20 feet container: 1330x9.8N/m², 40 feet container: 980x9.8N/m².

5. When manually loading goods, pay attention to whether there are handling instructions such as "do not invert", "place flat", "place upright" on the packaging. Use the correct loading tools. Do not use hand hooks for bundled goods. The goods loaded in the container should be neatly arranged and tightly stacked. For easily scattered bundles and fragile packaged goods, use padding or insert plywood between the goods to prevent movement within the container.

6. When loading palletized goods, accurately grasp the internal dimensions of the container and the external dimensions of the goods packaging to calculate the number of pieces loaded, aiming to minimize abandoned space and maximize the amount of goods loaded.

7. When using a forklift to load the container, the free lift height and mast height of the machine will be limited. If conditions allow, a forklift can load two layers at once. If double-layer loading is not possible, when loading the second layer inside the container, consider the free lift height and the possible lifting height (h) of the forklift mast. At this time, the mast lifting height should be the height of the first layer of goods minus the free lift height, allowing the second layer of goods to be placed on top of the first layer. Generally, a common forklift with a lifting capacity of 2 tons has a free lift height of about 50 cm. However, there is also a forklift with full free lift height. This kind of machinery is not affected by the mast lifting height as long as the height inside the container allows, making it convenient to stack two layers of goods. Additionally, note that there should be padding under the goods to facilitate smooth removal of the forks.

8. When mixed loading LCL (Less-Than-Container-Load) goods, pay attention to the following points:

1) Light goods should be placed on top of heavy goods.

2) Goods with weak packaging strength should be placed on top of goods with strong packaging strength.

3) Goods of different shapes and packaging should be avoided being loaded together as much as possible.

4) Liquid and clean goods should be placed under other goods as much as possible.

5) Goods that may leak dust, liquid, moisture, odor, etc., from the packaging should not be mixed with other goods if possible. If mixing is necessary, separate them with canvas, plastic film, or other padding materials.

6) Goods with sharp corners or protruding parts should protect these parts to avoid damaging other goods.

9. When loading refrigerated goods, pay attention to the following matters:

1) During the loading process, the refrigeration unit of the frozen container must stop running.

2) Before loading, the padding wood and other padding materials used inside the frozen container should be pre-cooled. Clean and hygienic padding materials should be selected to avoid contaminating the goods.

3) Do not use paper, boards, or other materials as padding to avoid blocking ventilation pipes and vents.

4) After loading, there must be a gap between the top of the container and the top of the goods to enable effective cold air circulation.

5) Note that refrigerated goods are more prone to sliding and breaking compared to general cargo, so the goods should be secured. Use nets or other padding materials when securing the goods to avoid affecting the circulation of cold air.

6) Strictly prohibit goods that have lost freshness or have deteriorated and become smelly from being loaded into the container to avoid damaging other normal goods.

10. When loading dangerous goods, pay attention to the following matters:

1) Before loading the goods, investigate the characteristics, disaster prevention measures, and handling methods after danger occurs for the type of dangerous goods. The work site should be chosen in a location avoiding direct sunlight, isolated from heat sources and fire sources, and well-ventilated.

2) The work site should have sufficient area and necessary equipment to effectively handle accidents if they occur.

3) Operations should be carried out according to relevant rules. Operators should wear protective work clothes, face masks, and rubber gloves.

4) Before loading, check the strength and structure of the container used to prevent the use of containers that do not meet the loading requirements.

5) Before loading explosives and oxidizing substances, carefully clean the container to prevent fires or explosions caused by residual dust, garbage, or other debris inside the container.

6) Check whether the container, packaging, and markings of dangerous goods are intact and consistent with the content recorded in the transport documents. Do not load dangerous goods with damaged packaging or leaking containers into the container.

7) When using materials to secure dangerous goods, pay attention to fire prevention requirements and ensure sufficient safety factors and strength.

8) No part of dangerous goods should protrude outside the container, and the container doors should be able to close normally after loading.

9) Some dangerous goods packaged in paper bags, fiberboard, and fiber drums can cause chemical reactions upon contact with water, leading to spontaneous combustion, heat generation, or toxic gas production. Therefore, strict waterproof checks should be conducted.

10) Different countries have different regulations regarding the mixed loading of dangerous goods. For example, Japan and the United States stipulate that dangerous goods prohibited from being loaded in the same area or cannot be mixed in the same package cannot be mixed in the same container. The UK stipulates that dangerous goods of different categories cannot be mixed in the same container. In actual loading operations, try to avoid mixing different types of dangerous goods in the same container.

11) When dangerous goods are mixed with other goods, try to place the dangerous goods near the container door.

12) Strictly prohibit dangerous goods from being mixed with instrument-type goods.

13) During loading, do not use methods such as throwing, dropping, flipping, or dragging to avoid impact and friction between goods.
