The Eight Management Skills that Make You Omnipotent_8071

by alockt40 on 2011-10-11 09:21:50

Drucker believed: governance will have some differences in different organizations. Since the mission determines the vision, and the vision determines the structure. Of course, there are differences between governing Walmart and governing the Roman Catholic Church, and these differences lie in the distinct terminologies (languages) used by each organization. The other differences are mainly in application rather than in principle. All managers of organizations face decision-making and personnel decisions, and human resource issues are almost the same. All organizational managers encounter communication problems, and managers need to spend a great deal of time communicating with their superiors and subordinates. In all organizations, about 90% of the problems are common, and only 10% are different. Only this 10% needs to adapt to the specific mission, specific culture, and specific language of the organization. In other words, a successful business leader can also effectively lead a non-profit organization, and vice versa.

Governance does not have a single answer; under the current circumstances, the essence of governance is decision-making. Governance seeks efficiency and effectiveness; governance is the combination of doing the right things and doing things right. Managers at all levels should possess conceptual skills, interpersonal relationship techniques, and technical skills, but the proportion of skills mastered varies depending on the level of personnel. As a manager, one should master eight basic management skills:

**First Skill: Planning**

1. The source of morale is unity, and the most important source is performance.

2. The achievement of performance (goals) is the source of boosting morale.

3. How to set goals is the first step in planning. We should know:

- What is the department's task?

- Who are the department's customers?

- What do these customers need? How do we satisfy them?

- Whom or which departments will our actions affect? Which departments or people will affect us?

- What are our strengths?

- What needs improvement?

- What do we need to become?

4. The benefits of planning include team coordination, better use of resources, increased team efficiency, and enhanced operational control.

5. Seven components of planning: objectives, time, location, personnel, procedures, evaluation, and communication.

**Second Skill: Action and Inspection**

1. Keep your superior informed about progress in real-time, treat your superior as the first resource, leverage their strengths, and align their work with your own thinking.

2. Thorough planning - effective action - strict periodic inspection - positive assessment handling.

3. Planning must be implemented effectively.

4. Outstanding planners must consider creating a schedule before executing plans.

5. During the implementation of the plan, various feedback information must be monitored to rigorously inspect the plan.

6. When handling inspections, summarize exceptional issues promptly and try to convert them into routine management.

7. Inspection skills: Inspect based on the plan established according to its objectives, compare and evaluate based on expected results, and make adjustments.

**Third Skill: Delegation**

1. You cannot handle everything yourself; you must change roles and delegate tasks to others.

2. First, delegate work or businesses that employees can easily get started with and that leaders are familiar with.

3. Full delegation does not mean ignoring: delegation without supervision is ineffective.

4. Trust is the prerequisite for reasonable delegation; only by fully trusting your subordinates can you gain excellent talent.

5. Leadership lifecycle theory.

6. Benefits of delegation: more effective use of time, helping employee growth, improving team efficiency, aiding employees in mastering decision-making skills, unleashing creativity, enhancing employees' problem-solving abilities, and making optimal use of human capital. However, delegation is not suitable when time is tight, inappropriate personnel are involved, or the cost is too high.

7. Delegation process: decide on delegation items, choose suitable personnel, explain the delegated items, let the delegated person create a plan, discuss monitoring methods and key points, and supervise the delegation.

8. Delegation principles: equal authority and responsibility, complete delegation, hierarchical delegation, provide appropriate assistance, involve the delegatee directly, avoid reverse delegation, and maintain control over delegation.

9. How to delegate effectively: grant authority, delegate based on expected outcomes, choose suitable employees, communicate effectively, increase transparency, follow up, and recognize employee performance.

**Fourth Skill: Coaching**

1. Leaders must train employees' skills and abilities while adjusting their personal conditions; the biggest mistake in guiding subordinates in the workplace is lack of planning.

2. Coaching plans need to appropriately respond to benefits and meet employees' needs to be valuable.

3. Participatory management and positive reinforcement help create a good external environment for coaching.

4. Leaders should not become the ceiling for their subordinates.

5. Four C coaching methods: clear (clear), concise (concise), complete (complete), considerate (carefully considered).

6. Steps to provide coaching: think about how to give guidance, conduct coaching, obtain feedback, provide ongoing assistance to employees, follow up, and recognize employee performance.
