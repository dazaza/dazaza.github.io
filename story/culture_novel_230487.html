On the Techniques of Character Image Creation in "Romance of the Three Kingdoms"

by one on 2009-08-29 17:21:58

The pioneering work of Chinese classical episodic novels, "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," is also one of the most influential Chinese novels in the history of world fiction. It vividly depicts a historical panorama spanning nearly a hundred years during the late Han and Jin dynasties, focusing on the political, military, and diplomatic struggles between various ruling groups. For centuries, its concise and brisk language, rich and intricate plot, and lifelike characters have attracted generation after generation of readers, making it widely recited among the common people. The greatest artistic achievement of "Romance of the Three Kingdoms" lies in Luo Guanzhong's successful creation of vivid and distinct character portrayals. The book features over a thousand characters, each with clear personality traits: Liu Bei's benevolence, Cao Cao's treachery, Guan Yu's loyalty and righteousness, Zhang Fei's bravery, Zhuge Liang's wisdom, Zhou Yu's jealousy of talent, Sun Quan's willingness to compromise, and Yuan Shao's indecisiveness.

In terms of character portrayal, Luo Guanzhong excels at capturing individual personalities through physical appearance, expressions, and dialogue, using techniques such as contrast, embellishment, and exaggeration to present vivid and distinctive characters, demonstrating his profound skills as a literary master.

One, Using plain description methods like appearance, expression, and dialogue to create character images

In Chinese classical novels, authors typically use various artistic means to depict characters from multiple angles, including descriptions of appearance, expressions, dialogue, and behavior. These not only reveal external characteristics such as looks, speech, gestures, and clothing but also delve into internal activities like psychology and emotions, creating full and successful character images. As a literary master, Luo Guanzhong was no exception. He used plain description techniques, relying on characters' own words and actions to convey their essence, achieving both form and spirit, thus sketching out lively character images.

For instance, in the first chapter, Zhang Fei's entrance is described: "Xuande turned back to look at this person, who was eight feet tall, with a leopard head and round eyes, a swallow-like jaw and tiger-like whiskers, his voice like thunder, his momentum like a galloping horse." This depiction immediately conveys Zhang Fei's imposing figure, while "voice like thunder, momentum like a galloping horse" vividly presents his impulsive nature. Another example is Zhuge Liang's entrance in the twenty-eighth chapter: "Eight feet tall, face like jade, wearing a silk turban, draped in a crane-feather cloak, he had an air of a celestial being," which vividly complements his later depictions of intelligence. Similarly, Guan Yu's image is perfectly presented in just thirty words: "Nine feet tall, with a beard two feet long; face like a deep red date, lips as if coated with rouge; phoenix eyes, sleeping silkworm eyebrows, dignified and awe-inspiring," allowing readers to sense the heroic aura approaching them.

Secondly, through detailed descriptions of language and expressions, the unique personality traits of characters are highlighted. In the scene where Zhang Fei beats the inspector, the text reads: "Zhang Fei became furious, opening his round eyes wide, grinding his teeth, dismounting from his horse, rushing into the inn where no one could stop him, directly entering the rear hall to see the inspector sitting on the hall, binding the county official to the ground. Fei roared: 'Harmful villain! Do you recognize me?' The inspector had no time to speak before Zhang Fei grabbed his hair, dragging him out of the inn, tying him to the horse post outside the county office; pulling down willow branches, he vigorously whipped the inspector's legs, breaking more than ten branches." Through this passage, readers can clearly understand Zhang Fei's straightforward and fiery nature. Another example is Guan Yu's portrayal: "Yun Chang said: 'I can ride alone amidst thousands of spears and swords, stones and arrows flying all around, moving freely as if there were no one else. How could I worry about the rats of Jiangdong?'" This line from Chapter Sixty-Six shows Guan Yu's pride and grandeur. Furthermore, Guan Yu's loyal and righteous image is established in another passage: "Guan Gong solemnly said: 'I am merely a warrior from Jie Liang, treated by my lord as a brother. How could I betray him and join the enemy country? If the city falls, I would rather die. Jade may be shattered but cannot lose its whiteness, bamboo may be burned but cannot destroy its integrity. Even if I perish, my name can live forever in the annals.' Reading this text often brings tears to the eyes, moved by Guan Yu's demeanor."

Two, Highlighting character images through contrast

Throughout "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," the author deliberately sets up many contrasting scenarios to highlight the towering figures of heroes.

For instance, there are two instances of drawing lots:

The first is in Chapter Twenty-Two with Liu Dai and Wang Zhong drawing lots. "Suddenly, Cao Cao sent someone to urge Liu Dai and Wang Zhong to advance. They discussed in the camp. Liu Dai said: 'Prime Minister urged us to attack the city, you should go first.' Wang Zhong said: 'Prime Minister sent you first.' Liu Dai said: 'I am the main general, how can I go first?' Wang Zhong said: 'We will both lead troops together.' Liu Dai said: 'Let's draw lots, whoever gets it goes first.' Wang Zhong drew the 'first' character, so he had to take half of the army to attack Xuzhou." This reflects the two generals shirking responsibility and fearing battle.

The second is in Chapter Seventy-One with Huang Zhong and Zhao Yun drawing lots. "Zhong said: 'Let me go first, what do you think?' Yun said: 'Let me go first.' Zhong said: 'I am the main general, you are the deputy general, why should we compete?' Yun said: 'We both serve our lord equally, why bother calculating? Let's draw lots, whoever gets it goes first.' Zhong agreed. At that moment, Zhong drew the 'first' character. Yun said: 'Since General goes first, I will assist. Let's agree on a time. If General returns on time, I will hold my troops still; if General returns late, I will send troops to support.' Zhong said: 'Your words are correct.'" This highlights the two competing for the vanguard position. Comparing these two passages, readers can discern praise and criticism. The former gives a sense of negativity and despondency; the latter conveys determination and the self-confidence of the Shuhan group. Despite the same act of drawing lots, the characters portrayed by the author are already very clear.

In "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," comparison is a primary method of character portrayal. Sometimes it occurs through dialogue, sometimes in intense warfare or conflicts, and sometimes through the actions of two characters within the same scene.

Dialogue comparisons are recorded multiple times in "Romance of the Three Kingdoms." For example, Guo Jia's analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of Yuan Shao and Cao Cao includes a brilliant comparison: "Jia said: 'Liu Bang and Xiang Yu's inability to match each other is something you know. Gaozu triumphed through intelligence, Xiang Yu, though strong, was eventually captured. Now Yuan Shao has ten failures, and you have ten victories. Yuan Shao's army, though large, is nothing to fear: Yuan Shao follows elaborate rituals, while you act naturally, this is a victory in Dao; Yuan Shao acts against justice, while you follow righteousness, this is a moral victory; since Emperor Huan and Ling, governance has been too lenient, Yuan Shao compensates with leniency, while you correct with strictness, this is a governance victory; Yuan Shao appears lenient but inwardly suspicious, favoring relatives, while you appear simple but inwardly wise, choosing talent based on merit, this is a degree of victory; Yuan Shao plans much but decides little, while you implement strategies decisively, this is a strategic victory; Yuan Shao collects fame, while you treat people sincerely, this is a virtue victory; Yuan Shao cares for those nearby but neglects those far away, while your consideration leaves nothing unattended, this is a benevolence victory; Yuan Shao listens to slander and becomes confused, while your influence remains unchecked, this is a clarity victory; Yuan Shao mixes right and wrong, while your laws are strict and clear, this is a cultural victory; Yuan Shao likes to create false appearances, unaware of military essentials, while you defeat the many with the few, using troops like a god, this is a martial victory. With these ten victories, defeating Yuan Shao should be easy.'" Cao Cao laughed and said: "As you say, how am I worthy enough?" This "ten victories and ten defeats" theory is one of the classics in "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," comprehensively and deeply comparing the images of Cao Cao and Yuan Shao.

In the intense struggle and conflict of the "Battle of Guandu," the images of Cao Cao and Yuan Shao are further compared. Faced with Yuan Shao, who was stronger than himself, Cao Cao first adopted Liu Ye's design of the "thunder cart" to counter Yuan Shao's "digging soldiers"; then barefoot welcomed Xu You, burning Wuchao according to Xu You's plan; subsequently, he used Cheng Yu's "ten ambushes" strategy to greatly defeat Yuan Benchu at Cangting. This fully demonstrates Cao Cao's deep planning, respect for talent, and great strategy. On the other hand, Yuan Shao initially rejected Ju Shou's suggestion of "delaying defense"; Xu You suggested raiding Xuchang, which he rashly dismissed; later, he listened to Feng Ji and Guo Tu's slanders, causing Xu You, Zhang He, and others to defect to Cao Cao; after the Battle of Guandu, Yuan Shao regretted not listening to Tian Feng's advice but, embarrassed, executed Tian Feng in prison. Through this contrast, Yuan Shao's indecision, lack of strategy, inability to distinguish loyalty from betrayal, and narrow-mindedness are fully exposed.

Contrasts within the same scene vividly express praise and criticism. The classic example is the scene after Guan Yu flooded the seven armies. "Guan Yu said: 'How dare you resist me?' (Yu) Jin said: 'Ordered by superiors, I have no choice. I beg Lord Guan for mercy, vowing to repay with death.' Guan Yu stroked his beard and laughed: 'Killing you is like killing a dog or pig, defiling my blade!' He ordered him bound and sent to the great prison in Jingzhou: 'Wait for my return, then deal with you appropriately.' After dealing with Jin, Guan Yu ordered Pang De brought forward. De opened his eyebrows and glared, standing without kneeling, Guan Yu said: 'Your brother is now in Hanzhong; your former lord Ma Chao is also a general in Shu. Why didn't you surrender earlier?' De became angry and said: 'I'd rather die under the sword than surrender to you!' Continuously cursing." Such a scene of surrender vividly contrasts the cowardly, opportunistic Yu Jin with the brave and righteous Pang De.

Contrast is indeed a classic arrangement in "Romance of the Three Kingdoms." By using this technique, Luo Guanzhong permeates the characters' images and his own preferences, achieving the effect of adding the finishing touch.

Three, Using secondary characters to accompany and highlight the main characters' images

Mao Zonggang, when evaluating "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," mentioned its "wonderful way of highlighting the main through the secondary." What this means in terms of character portrayal is the use of accompanying and highlighting techniques. Secondary characters are used to enhance the main roles, even though the focus is on the supporting characters, the ultimate aim is on the protagonists, which is a prominent feature in shaping main characters in "Romance of the Three Kingdoms."

In "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," Zhuge Liang is the most respected and beloved character by millions of readers, embodying "loyalty" and "wisdom," knowledgeable in astronomy and geography, foreseeing events accurately, and possessing outstanding talents. The author spares no effort in portraying Zhuge Liang's image, extensively promoting it. His introduction alone spans three chapters, using a series of preludes to highlight his presence.

First, Sima Hui, the Water Mirror Master, recommended the recluse Longzhong to Liu Bei while belittling Liu Bei's advisors to contrast the abilities of "Longzhong" and "Phoenix Chick." Then Xu Shu appeared, showcasing his military prowess through several battles against Cao Cao. Later, Xu Shu was deceived by Cao Cao's scheme, prompting the author to highlight again as Xu Shu quickly recommended Zhuge Liang, saying, "Comparing me to him is like comparing a slow horse to a unicorn, or a cold crow to a phoenix," elevating Zhuge Liang's talent. Subsequently, Liu Bei visited Zhuge Liang thrice in his straw hut, encountering various individuals related to Zhuge Liang, such as Cui Zhouping, Shi Guangping, Meng Gongwei, Zhuge Jun, and Huang Chengyan. Their talents and noble virtues essentially served to highlight Zhuge Liang. Although Zhuge Liang hadn't appeared yet, his character and virtues were already highlighted through these individuals.

In the main body of "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," to multi-dimensionally highlight Zhuge Liang's image, the author intentionally arranged Zhou Yu, Cao Cao, and Sima Yi to accompany him.

In the "Battle of Red Cliffs," Zhou Yu continuously used stratagems such as the double agent, bitter meat, and feigned surrender tactics that Cao Cao found hard to counter. However, these schemes, although deceiving Cao Cao, were all seen through by Zhuge Liang, fitting perfectly within his predictions. Zhou Yu attempted to kill Zhuge Liang by forcing him to make arrows, but Zhuge Liang, knowing his intentions, did not retaliate against Zhou Yu but cleverly borrowed ten thousand arrows from Cao Cao instead. Later, Zhou Yu launched a military campaign against Liu Bei to reclaim Jingzhou, but in every confrontation with Zhuge Liang, Zhou Yu was at a disadvantage, his schemes repeatedly uncovered by Zhuge Liang, ultimately leading to his lament, "If Zhou Yu exists, why must Zhuge Liang exist?" Luo Guanzhong's extensive portrayal of Zhou Yu's image not only reveals Zhuge Liang's superior military talent, highlighting his wisdom and divine calculations, but also contrasts Zhuge Liang's magnanimity and ability to see the bigger picture against Zhou Yu's jealousy of talent and narrow-mindedness.

Cao Cao's portrayal of Zhuge Liang's image is also very distinct. After the defeat at Red Cliffs, Cao Cao fled to Wulin, Hulu Mouth, and Huayong Road, laughing, "People all say Zhou Yu and Zhuge Liang are wise strategists, but in my view, they are ultimately incompetent. If there were an army stationed here, we would all be captured." But before his laughter ceased, Zhao Yun, Zhang Fei, and Guan Yu emerged respectively from the three locations. This vividly portrays Cao Cao's cunning and deceitfulness, thereby highlighting Zhuge Liang's wisdom. Additionally, both served as Prime Ministers overseeing state affairs, but through the portrayal of Cao Cao in "Hunting in Xuchang," his "treachery" contrasts sharply with Zhuge Liang's "loyalty" as depicted in "Tosaka Entrustment." Just as the proverb says, "Cao Cao and Zhuge Liang - different temperaments," Cao Cao, as a negative archetype meticulously crafted by the author, serves as a valuable "green leaf" to highlight Zhuge Liang's image.

In the later stages, Sima Yi became Zhuge Liang's main opponent, further enhancing Zhuge Liang's image through Sima Yi's portrayal. For example, in the "Empty City Stratagem" episode, Sima Yi led his troops to the city gates and saw Zhuge Liang sitting atop the open gates playing a qin. Instead of attacking, he thought, "Zhuge Liang is cautious by nature and does not take risks. With the gates wide open, there must be an ambush. If we advance, we fall into his trap," and retreated with his troops. Sima Yi possessed remarkable military talent, deep strategic insight, and excellent command skills, but his overly suspicious and timid nature served to highlight Zhuge Liang's adaptability and ingenious strategies.

Through the above numerous character portrayals, Zhuge Liang's artistic image is fully and vividly displayed, appearing more genuine and complete. In "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," similar treatments abound, such as "Warming Wine and Beheading Hua Xiong." First, several generals from various feudal lords fought but were defeated by Hua Xiong within a few rounds, showing Hua Xiong's ferocity. After layered buildup, the author describes Guan Yu's entrance: "The feudal lords heard loud drumming and shouting outside the pass, as if heaven were collapsing and mountains shaking, everyone startled. Just as they were about to investigate, the sound of bells rang out, and a horse arrived at the center of the camp. Guan Yu held Hua Xiong's head, threw it to the ground. His wine was still warm." Here, the author skillfully uses indirect and atmospheric enhancement techniques, without any direct description of Guan Yu's valor or how Hua Xiong was killed, effectively using sidekick characters like Hua Xiong and other generals to bring a towering and valiant image of Guan Yu to life.

In summary, in "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," the appropriate use of artistic techniques such as highlighting and enhancement often achieves twice the artistic effect with half the effort.

Four, Using exaggeration techniques to portray character images

Luo Guanzhong's most unique technique in "Romance of the Three Kingdoms" is through artistic exaggeration, carving out deeper character images.

In "Romance of the Three Kingdoms," the portrayal of Zhang Fei extensively employs exaggeration. For example, in Chapter Forty-Two, "Zhang Yide Creates Chaos at Changban Bridge," "(Zhang) Fei loudly shouted: 'I am Zhang Yide from Yan! Who dares to fight me to the death?' His voice was like thunder. Upon hearing this, Cao's army trembled with fear. Cao Cao urgently ordered his umbrella removed, turning to his aides and saying: 'I once heard Yun Chang say: Yide takes the head of a general from a million troops as easily as taking something from a pocket. Today we meet, do not underestimate him.' Before he finished speaking, Zhang Fei opened his eyes wide and shouted again: 'I am Zhang Yide from Yan! Who dares to fight me to the death?' Cao Cao, seeing Zhang Fei's bearing, began to retreat. Zhang Fei noticed the movement of Cao Cao's rear guard and thrust his spear forward, shouting: 'Fight or not, retreat or not, what's the matter?' Before his shout ended, Xia Houjie beside Cao Cao was so frightened that his liver and gallbladder split, falling off his horse." Three exaggerated shouts vividly present Zhang Fei's bold and fearless nature. Similarly, many characters in the novel are shaped using exaggeration, such as Sun Ce squeezing a man to death and scaring Fan Neng to death, highlighting the "Little Overlord" image; Zhao Yun's seven entries and exits at Changban Slope, cutting generals and seizing flags, rescuing the lord alone, becoming a timeless legend; Zhang Liao's terrifying reputation at Xiaoyao Ford making children in Jiangdong afraid to cry at night, etc.

Another type of exaggeration involves fictional storylines to imagine and exaggerate characters, such as the portrayal of Zhuge Liang's image in the novel, which extensively uses fictional and exaggerated plot elements, such as borrowing arrows with straw boats, borrowing the east wind, the empty city stratagem, and praying for five zhang stars, none of which actually occurred in historical records. The "Records of the Three Kingdoms" documents that the stories of "borrowing arrows" and the "empty city stratagem" were attributed to Sun Quan and Wen Ping respectively. Placing these stories on Sun Quan and Wen Ping would have little significance in the overall structure of the book, but exaggerating them onto Zhuge Liang highlights his extraordinary wisdom. The author uses such fictional exaggerations to portray Zhuge Liang as the embodiment of wisdom and elevate him to a semi-divine status. Therefore, Mr. Lu Xun commented: "Describing Zhuge Liang's intelligence verges on the supernatural."

While using exaggeration, Luo Guanzhong also paid attention to the reasonableness and continuity of the plot, applying exaggeration in arranging character stories and describing language. Thus, the author successfully achieved artistic prominence in character portrayal, making their artistic images full and vivid, giving the impression of leaping off the page.

In conclusion, the portrayal of characters in "Romance of the Three Kingdoms" mainly relies on the aforementioned aspects. Although these characters' personalities lack development and change, belonging to typified images, it cannot be denied that "Romance of the Three Kingdoms" is the highest-achieving and most influential work of ancient Chinese historical fiction. The techniques used to shape characters not only leave behind numerous beautiful, vivid, distinct, and complete character images but also showcase the author's outstanding literary talent, exerting a significant and lasting impact on the portrayal of characters in subsequent classical novels.
