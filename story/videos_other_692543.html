Futures - Rise in agricultural products prices has limited impact on CPI

by qihuo07fz on 2012-03-06 10:14:27

□ Beijing Mid-term Wang Jun Ma Bing After the Spring Festival, the performance of the agricultural futures market has been quite eye-catching. Influenced by macro factors, weather factors, and capital factors, there has been a noticeable increase in futures prices, attracting futures investment analysis. Among these, the grain market's performance stands out particularly. First, the corn market led a cross-year uptrend, followed by an increase in early indica rice futures prices driven by stockpiling policies. After the early indica rice price stabilized slightly, wheat futures took over the baton of rising prices, with the main contract breaking through the 2600 integer barrier under the impetus of funds and showing a continued upward trend. The rise in the grain market has been fluctuating continuously, worthy of attention from market investors.

From the perspective of the downward trend in prices, the domestic CPI in February will show a decline, and food prices will also fall back, but it does not have a strong guiding effect on the current trend of agricultural futures prices. On the other hand, whether the small spring-like trend in agricultural futures prices after the festival can continue is questionable, especially since spot prices have not seen much increase, making it difficult to exert pressure on the inflation situation in the first quarter.

The basis for the above view primarily stems from our judgment on the domestic and international economic situation in 2012. In the first quarter, the risk of Eurozone debt default is relatively high, with March being the peak month for concentrated Eurozone debt maturity. The issue of Eurozone debt default still exerts significant pressure on the entire commodity market including agricultural products. Any slight disturbance can lead to a sharp reaction in the market, with one day's retracement potentially wiping out several weeks of gains. However, this may also be an opportunity: due to the slight growth of the US economy this year, commodities have a relatively solid bottom support. Combined with the soft landing of the domestic economy, when most commodities experience a pullback, it is the time for us to build long positions and replenish our holdings.

In January 2012, the overall level of consumer prices nationwide increased by 4.5% year-on-year and 1.5% month-on-month; the producer price index for industrial products nationwide increased by 0.7% year-on-year and decreased by 0.1% month-on-month. The purchase price index for industrial producers increased by 2.0% year-on-year and decreased by 0.3% month-on-month. The following information can be derived from the aforementioned domestic price data: First, affected by seasonal factors and the Spring Festival effect, food prices in January increased by 10.5% year-on-year, influencing the overall level of consumer prices to increase by approximately 3.29 percentage points year-on-year. Second, the carry-over factor in January was about 3.03%, which then quickly declined. The push effect of the carry-over factor on the CPI throughout the year is limited, and the inflationary pressure in the first half of the year will show a gradual downward trend, as per futures investment analysis. Third, food prices showed an overall upward trend throughout January, but at the end of the month, prices began to fall as suppliers returned to the market after the holiday. Overall, the domestic CPI data in January was significantly higher than the market's expected midpoint of 4%, but this does not represent a restart of inflationary pressure. In February, with post-holiday price declines and a significant drop in the carry-over factor, the CPI will show a marked decline.

Secondly, from the market trend perspective, after nearly a month of volatile increases, the original grains in most southern producing areas began to stabilize after rising, indicating that the short-term policy effects brought about by the adjustment of support prices have been basically released. Without fundamental support, a downward fluctuation is likely. From the perspective of the declining price trend, the domestic CPI in February will decrease, and food prices will also retreat, but it does not have a strong guiding influence on the trend of agricultural futures prices.

Moreover, the performance of other agricultural products is sufficient to reassure those worried about the resurgence of inflationary pressures. Looking at the charts, there has been some divergence in recent trends in agricultural products. Compared to the relatively strong pattern of grains, oilseed products have performed relatively weakly. Although there has been a certain degree of increase after the festival, the amplitude has been limited, mainly due to the sluggish trading in the spot market. Traders' enthusiasm for chasing higher prices is not high, limiting the rise in futures prices, causing the price difference between domestic and international futures markets for soybean products to gradually widen.
