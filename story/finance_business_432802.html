www.92971.com Friendship and years in my memory....

by cool76615 on 2011-05-18 14:33:08

When I was a child, there were always playmates around me. I vaguely remember that when I was young, it was very popular to become sworn brothers by kowtowing. I grew up in a rural area in Northeast China, so I don't know much about whether this practice was also popular in other places. However, every time I think about those days, finding a temple or a tree, and then several friends kowtowing together made us sworn brothers. Every time I recall those moments, I can't help but smile happily. It was such a simple and pure time, wasn't it? Of course, this was all influenced by the popular martial arts dramas back then. I still remember clearly how we would spend our days catching frogs, playing war games, stealing birds' eggs, wrestling, fighting with wooden swords, and playing pranks... The sky was always a deep blue. Although we didn't pay attention at the time, looking back now, the flowers smelled so sweet, the wind was so gentle, the clouds were light, and the sky was vast, giving us freedom. Or perhaps the world has always been so beautiful, but when we were young, we had eyes that were untouched by the dust of the world. Back then, we were clean and innocent, saying what we wanted without hesitation, never knowing the strategy of feigned retreat. We did what we wanted without calculating the gains and losses, like running freely on the grassland or lying together in the snow during winter, watching time pass (it really wasn't cold). At that time, we possessed youth, happiness, and all the beauty of life, preserved in those special years. Back then, we were playmates!

As we grew a little older, we left that pure world behind. Then, "Young and Dangerous" became popular, and thus began the formation of gangs. Our best friends started carrying knives in their desks (costing 5 yuan each from a hardware store at the entrance of the Qinggang County farmers' market). Whenever we had conflicts with others, the first thing on our lips was whether it would be a one-on-one fight or a group brawl. Our playmates started wearing denim jackets and bell-bottom pants. They began trying to smoke, lighting cigarettes clumsily. They started dyeing their hair and wearing sunglasses, all taking pride in flirting with girls. Back then, calling a girlfriend "mazi" was considered quite cool. Back then, we loved spending time in video game arcades, playing "The King of Fighters," betting on horse machines, and going for big wins. Back then, the song "Single Love Song" was very popular, as were stockings and shapely legs, and we all loved coming out at night to make noise. Back then, we secretly smoked, let our hair grow long, and most looked forward to gang fights and chasing idols... Back then, we were brothers.

When I grew a little older, I left my friends or brothers behind, moving from the county to the city. From having at least a dozen good brothers to being completely alone and clueless in the city, the difference was immense. I still remember vividly standing in the city school for the first time. My first impression was: wow, this school is really freaking huge! There were five or six teaching buildings and three playgrounds, at least five times bigger than the school in our county. Along with this came a deep sense of confusion and inferiority. Looking back now, I realize how silly I looked back then. Haha, I remember the first day of school, I wore a red shirt with an extremely large collar and white bell-bottom pants with a bow tie. Most notably, my hairstyle back then: Here's a picture, not taken at that time, but it gives you a general idea. It was even longer, covering half of my face. Haha, and that was also the darkest period for me. With my hair covering my face, for a long time, no one knew what I really looked like. In short, I didn't look like a student or a good person. A friend once described me humorously as looking like a reformed prisoner. But at the time, I truly thought I looked pretty cool.

Haha, in a daze, I started my short two-year school life in Daqing. Here, both the times and space changed more dramatically. Here, I only made four friends. Here, I was exposed to more things, learned more, started thinking, and thus began to feel confused. At this point, I started trying to understand what the word "friend" really meant.

At this time, pop music was extremely popular. Singers like Andy Lau, Jacky Cheung, Karen Mok, Wakin Chau, Nicholas Tse, Richie Jen, Huang Lei, Jacky Wong, Show Lo, Jay Chou, and Fish Leong... countless songs. Back then, copying lyrics was our favorite activity in class. During this time, we started learning how to groom ourselves (don't say that the bell-bottom pants and long hair I mentioned earlier were ways of dressing oneself nicely). I remember spending half an hour every day combing my hair in front of the mirror. Back then, there were only five of us. We started getting in touch with computers, chatting on QQ, and playing Half-Life. Back then, the clouds were also pure, and we were still somewhat innocent. Back then, we loved going to parks and squares. Later, we started staying overnight in internet cafes to play Legend. Back then, we were happy. We drank together, gathered on the roof of the gymnasium, drank cold drinks, walked the streets. We spent nights sitting in the square singing songs. Back then, we argued with each other, and we wrote apology letters. Brothers? Do you remember that snowy winter square? We bought the wrong liquor, getting two bottles of deer antler wine meant for male enhancement, but we drank them anyway, bare-chested, mixing snow with cola. We sang our songs under the flying snowflakes lit by the square lights, and I still have those scenes vividly in mind today. Back then, we were both brothers and friends.

Later, I left school and started heading towards the so-called freedom of breaking out of the cage that I had chosen. Then, I realized that outside the cage was a bigger cage, a more cruel one. Thus, I started my first job. Haha, it sounds funny now, but my first job was as an electrician apprentice, and I worked for a whole year. By the end of the year, my amusing master told me, "This whole year, I haven't even taught you how to connect wires. It's embarrassing to even say." Indeed, at the time, there was no chance to teach me. We were responsible for power maintenance, mostly just resetting breakers when they tripped. I was too young then, and my father didn't want me to do hard work. Being an electrician was the easiest, so I just followed along for a year without planning to become one. However, we did develop a skill - stripping wires. We could turn a 50-meter copper wire into pure copper in just one minute and five seconds. Back then, the price of copper was 55 yuan per catty, and every industry has its unwritten rules, which you understand. Haha, but my first master and I got along very well. You could say we were intergenerational friends. He was around 37 years old, from Liaoning Province. He had only been out working for a few years. His values were very detached. He didn't smoke, he didn't drink excessively, and occasionally, he would drink a little with me (never more than one bottle). He never overindulged in alcohol, and everything we earned from stripping copper was spent immediately. He neither frequented prostitutes nor gambled. Every month, apart from buying toothpaste, toothbrushes, and daily necessities, he had almost no other expenses. Fifty yuan a month was enough for him. He lived a very simple life. On his mountain back home, he had contracted ten acres of land, and he planned to go back after finishing his work for the next couple of years to focus on planting fruit trees and spend the rest of his life guarding his orchard. I remember asking him back then if such a life wouldn't be lonely. He said, "I've eaten well, worn well, played well, drank fine wine, smoked good cigarettes, and flirted with women before. Now, I don't need any of that anymore. I don't feel like I'm lacking anything mentally. A simple life feels fulfilling." Later, I stayed in contact with him, originally planning to visit his orchard, but never got the chance. This is a regret. Though we were colleagues, we were also intergenerational friends. From his attitude towards life and his simple way of living, you can probably infer what kind of person he was. I won't elaborate further here. Just a small wish sent afar: May the clear wind and bright moon bring tranquility, and the setting sun and falling flowers bring peace to my friend.

Immediately following, in the second year, I started learning welding, which was undoubtedly arduous. Since I wasn't an official employee of the oilfield, I was working for Oil Construction Company No.15, and the daily working hours were at least 12 hours. We woke up at 5 in the morning, ate breakfast, and started work at 6. We finished at noon at 12, ate lunch, and resumed work at 12:30. If you ate quickly, you could rest for about 10 minutes. As for the evening, it was uncertain. The earliest we finished was 7 PM, but sometimes it extended to 8 or 9 PM, and the latest was midnight. We worked overtime without overtime pay, receiving at most two packets of instant noodles and a sausage, while the pickles had to be bought by myself. For accommodation, we lived in iron sheet houses, which I won't explain further since you might not have seen them. In short, they were cold in winter and hot in summer. Throughout the entire year, I mainly worked. I met a colleague named Xiao Wu, who was from Jiangsu Province and could be considered my only friend. He was around 35 years old, relatively young compared to everyone else except me. He had a strong personality. He was decisive in his actions and spoke little. His one notable trait was his love for gambling, though he rarely lost and excelled at whatever form of gambling he participated in. While I didn't admire this aspect of him, he was exceptionally hardworking. Even in the coldest weather of minus 30 degrees Celsius, he would climb a 50-meter steel structure rooftop where the walkable area was only 20 centimeters wide, consisting of a series of I-beams forming the overall steel frame. The surface was icy, slippery, and lacked safety measures. It was incredibly cold, and none of his team dared to go up due to the cold and the slipperiness. But when it came to him, without any excuses, he would simply go up without hesitation whenever the task needed to be done. Moreover, he was exceptionally genuine. During our first conversation, he openly admitted to being selfish, stating that people from Jiangsu became particularly selfish after marriage, showing little affection even for family members. Indeed, people are selfish, but relatively speaking, I truly believe that people from Jiangsu are more selfish than those from other regions, which is directly related to their lifestyle and environment. In summary, that year was tough and exhausting, yet undeniably fulfilling. This sole friend I made that year eventually lost contact with me, and I no longer know where he is in the vast world. However, I still remember that this colleague could also be considered a friend.
