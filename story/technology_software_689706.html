Chinese Concept Stocks' Coming-of-Age: Navigating Through the "Short Selling" Assault

by anonymous on 2012-03-04 14:16:00

Hou Jiayong - Everything that happened over the past three days, like scenes from a movie, replayed in the mind of James Ji, Vice President of Focus Media (Nasdaq: FMCN), as if it had just occurred.

On November 22, Ji received a call from Jianchun Jiang, Chairman and CEO of Focus Media, instructing him to devise a counterattack strategy against Muddy Waters. That day, Focus Media's stock price plummeted nearly 40% due to Muddy Waters' research report. However, immediate responses including official rebuttals and stock buybacks helped the company’s shares rebound the next day.

Unlike Southeast融通 and other Chinese concept stocks that were previously shorted and subsequently severely hit, Focus Media, along with previous targets such as Qihoo 360 and Spreadtrum Communications, chose to strongly retaliate when faced with doubts.

"Short" attack on Chinese concept stocks

Ji said: Behind the "short" attacks on Chinese concept stocks lies a complete chain of interests. The typical pattern is: some institutions or individuals collaborate with investigation agencies to borrow stocks from certain institutions at high prices before releasing short reports. After the short-selling institution releases its report, the target company's share price drops, allowing participating institutions or individuals to purchase the stock at a low price and return the borrowed stock, thereby profiting from the price difference.

Wang Ran of EK Capital said: Short selling is relatively common in the U.S. capital market. Of course, short selling also carries risks; hunters can sometimes get their eyes pecked out by eagles. An investment banker revealed that a friend who studied in the U.S. later took up short selling as a career. A few years ago, he learned through relevant channels that a major Chinese internet giant might face negative information exposure, so he quickly borrowed stocks from an institution. Unexpectedly, the anticipated negative information did not come as scheduled, and during this period, the internet company's stock price rose, forcing him to buy back the stocks at a high price to return them to the institution, resulting in significant losses.

Regarding this, Ji said: This is the result of "hearing the right message but miscalculating the timing."

In this round of "short" attacks, if the short-selling institutions had timed it correctly, they should have made substantial profits because, so far, all the targeted Chinese internet concept stocks have seen their share prices fall, including Focus Media, Qihoo 360, and Spreadtrum Communications.

Internet commentator Fang Xingdong believed: The killing effect of these "short" attacks was so great due to information asymmetry. Companies like Qihoo 360 and Focus Media do not have similar business models in the U.S., making it difficult for American investors to understand them, which leads to the significant impact of Muddy Waters' short reports.

Fang Xingdong believed that "currently, the short reports issued by short-selling institutions against mainstream companies are riddled with flaws."

Muddy Waters claimed that Focus Media overvalued its LCD screens by about 50%. Ji explained: Focus Media's screens include two parts, namely LCD electronic displays and building advertisements. In first-tier cities, there are 29,707 LCD screens and 16,118 building posters, totaling 45,825 units; in second-tier cities, there are 56,301 LCD screens and 45,979 building posters, totaling 102,280 units; in third- and fourth-tier cities, there are 30,018 LCD screens and 259 building posters, totaling 30,277 units, summing up to 178,382 units. However, Muddy Waters only counted the number of LCD screens, causing confusion.

Chairman of Qihoo 360, Hongyi Zhou, told our reporter that Citron's previous accusations against Qihoo 360 were "seemingly plausible but actually false." Citron released a report claiming that Qihoo 360 exaggerated its company performance, lowering its target stock price to $5, which was 75% lower than the then-current stock price of $18. Qihoo 360 announced that it had "more than 300 million active users," "over 80% penetration rate," and "is China's third-largest internet company."

Citron cited data from Google's DoubleClick Ad Planner, stating that Qihoo 360 only covered 10% of internet users, predicting that Qihoo 360 had only 30 to 40 million users. The reality, however, was that DoubleClick Ad Planner only counted the traffic of 360.cn. Fang Xingdong believed that the short-selling companies were "pretending to be ignorant while maliciously attacking."

"Revaluation" of the value of the U.S. stock market

Compared to the treatment they once received, the situation facing Chinese internet concept stocks can be described as "gloomy." On the day Focus Media was shorted and its stock price fell, all Chinese concept stocks dropped across the board. Alex An of Bosin Capital believed that under the current market environment, the PE values of many overseas-listed companies have even fallen below their net asset values.

On one side, investigative agencies are shorting; on the other, capital markets are treating them coldly. What should Chinese concept stocks do? Wang Ran believed there are two choices: one is to say goodbye to the U.S. capital market, go private, and seek more ideal financing environments; the other is to adjust one's mindset and accept the game rules.

Just as Focus Media's stock was being shorted, Shanda Network announced a privatization transaction agreement, where the parent company controlled by the Chen Tianqiao family would acquire all remaining shares of Shanda Network at $41.35 per share.

Chen Tianqiao's dissatisfaction with the U.S. capital market has long been apparent; he believed that "Wall Street doesn't understand Shanda," and he didn't want to "follow Wall Street step by step."

There were rumors that Shanda Network would return to the A-share market. Shanda refused to comment on this.

Regarding Shanda's privatization, Wang Ran, CEO of EK Capital, believed: "No matter which path Chen Tianqiao chooses, he is paving the way for the future of all Chinese internet companies listed overseas."

Sharing the same view as Chen Tianqiao is Robin Li, Chairman of Sina Corporation. At the latest quarterly earnings conference of Sina, Robin Li told our reporter that "Wall Street is temporarily blind." He emotionally said: "The popularity of '鹿鼎记' seems unnoticed, Sina Video is closing in on the leader (referring to Youku), and Sougou is in an explosive growth stage; these seem not to be given the corresponding market value."

NetEase CEO William Ding has also repeatedly expressed dissatisfaction with Wall Street, saying, "Going public in the U.S. means the company must operate transparently, such as NetEase having to publish detailed financial data every quarter, which often puts us in a passive position." William Ding even questioned institutional analysts at a financial results meeting, asking, "You don't understand NetEase's business, yet you ask more questions than my wife does. Why?"

Baidu CFO Jennifer Li called for the introduction of international board-related details to facilitate Baidu's return to the A-share market.

In fact, by October of this year, there have been several cases of overseas-listed Chinese enterprises going private, including Kangpeng Chemistry, Dalian Fu Group, Taifu Electric, Zhongxia'an, and XinDa Technology listed in Singapore.

Alex An believes that some overseas-listed Chinese enterprises seeking privatization may have encountered problems, but low valuations are the main reason driving privatization.

Coming-of-age ceremony for Chinese concept stocks

The victory of short-selling institutions does not necessarily mean the failure of the targeted companies. Among the five companies currently attacked by short sellers, the share prices of Spreadtrum, Qihoo 360, and Focus Media returned to normal after a short-term drop.

Compared to the predicaments of Southeast融通 and Skai after being shorted, the situations of Spreadtrum, Qihoo 360, and Focus Media are entirely different. Wang Ran believed: The reason is that these companies have solid performances.

Before being attacked, Focus Media had just released its third-quarter financial report, showing total net revenue of $210.7 million, an increase of 53.3% year-on-year; net profit was $62.2 million, an increase of 45% compared to the previous quarter's $42.8 million.

On November 23, Fosun International announced in a filing on the Hong Kong Stock Exchange that the company spent $10.35 million purchasing 602,700 ADSs of Focus Media in the open market on November 22 U.S. time, with each ADS priced at approximately $17. After this round of increases, Fosun International holds a total of 22.2156 million Focus Media ADSs, equivalent to 16.42% of Focus Media's total issued share capital.

Two weeks after encountering the "short" attack, Qihoo 360 released its performance, with record-breaking operating revenue reaching $47.49 million, an increase of about 207% year-on-year; net profit was $10.9 million, an increase of about 186% year-on-year.

Against the backdrop of a collective decline in Chinese concept stocks, the post-short-attack performance of Focus Media and Qihoo 360 was better than the overall performance of Chinese concept stocks, without the so-called "avalanche" phenomenon occurring.

How should Chinese concept stocks respond to short sellers? Hongyi Zhou told our reporter: Since entering the U.S. capital market, one must accept the game rules. Ji also stated: This is relatively normal in the U.S., and Focus Media will rationally handle the matter; others' choices will not affect Focus Media.

Lubowang, President of Zhengwang Consulting, believed that Chinese concept stocks have two options when facing short sellers: either repurchase and privatize to list domestically, or strongly counterattack and speak with performance, with performance being fundamental.

Lubowang believed: Besides Chinese concept stocks being collectively undervalued and some companies facing short attacks, the delay or cancellation of new rounds of internet companies going public in the U.S. proves that "the U.S. operational model, U.S. venture capital, and rapid listing Silicon Valley model has failed;" Chinese internet needs to return to fundamentals and convince investors with performance, which is the "coming-of-age ceremony" for Chinese concept stocks.
