Introduction: Laboratories in various industries should design their layouts according to the characteristics of their own industry's inspection work when designing a laboratory. As the leading person in charge of a laboratory, one should first propose a layout design plan, then submit it to the highest management personnel or the principal leader of the unit or enterprise for discussion and finalization of the plan, then contact relevant professional companies for on-site measurements, have the professional company draw up the design drawings, consider the feasibility of the plan, and finally let the highest manager make the final decision. The following is an overview of the basic requirements for laboratory design, mainly aimed at providing a theoretical reference for colleagues in the same industry and serving as a starting point for further discussion.

Basic Requirements for Laboratory Design

The purpose of laboratory design is to establish an efficient, fully functional, and well-considered laboratory. When designing a laboratory, factors that affect its efficiency and safety should be fully considered, such as space, workbenches, storage cabinets, ventilation facilities, lighting, etc. Special laboratories should be designed according to relevant national standards.

1. Rational Space Allocation in Laboratory Design

Laboratory design should consider the needs of experimental function modules and equipment placement to determine a reasonable layout. At the same time, the size of the laboratory space should be determined with a forward-looking perspective. Many factors influence laboratory space design, such as the number of staff, the size of analytical methods and instruments. The laboratory should be flexible, making the staff feel comfortable without causing waste. The size of the working space should ensure that the maximum number of staff can work simultaneously. The effective space should be divided into clean areas (offices, rest rooms, study rooms), buffer zones (storage areas, supply areas, corridors), and contaminated areas (work areas, washing areas, sample storage areas).

The basic principle of laboratory design: human flow, material flow, air flow must be smooth; clean areas, buffer zones, and contaminated areas must be separated.

In designated experimental areas, the number of staff and transport personnel should be controlled. While controlling access to the laboratory, some preparatory areas should also be set up, such as receiving samples or specimens, allowing entry into the laboratory for personnel and visitors. Samples or specimens should be transported through staff, automatic transmission, pneumatic systems, or other automated systems. An internal communication system and alarm should be fully considered to notify or alert (such as disasters, fire alarms, sample arrival, or part of the laboratory seeking help). The expandability of laboratory space should also be considered, designing the laboratory to be extendable outward or mobile to meet future development needs. Transportation and computer network systems are used respectively for transporting samples or specimens within the laboratory and between the laboratory and departments within the unit. National laws and regulations (including national standards and industry standards) significantly impact laboratory design and should be proposed by architects during the entire laboratory design process.

Before formulating a spatial allocation plan, comprehensive analysis of instrument equipment, number of staff, workload, and experimental methods should be conducted, along with an assessment of spatial standard requirements, and calculation of net area and gross area. The allocation of special function areas depends on their functions and activities.

2. Layout Design of the Laboratory

Considerations for laboratory layout include the following points: Since each laboratory has different working natures, it's impossible to establish a unified general design scheme applicable to all laboratories. However, two principles—principle-based and flexibility—should be considered.

1. Sample transfer and personnel movement: Laboratory areas should be allocated considering workflow, sample transfer and circulation, biosafety factors, etc.

2. Flexibility: Whether the laboratory design can adapt to future developmental changes is extremely important.

3. Safety: Laboratory design and size should consider safety, meeting building rules for emergency clearance and evacuation exits, and equipping safety devices according to each laboratory's situation. Within 30 meters of hazardous chemical reagents, there should be an emergency eyewash station and shower room. All laboratories and places directly in contact with contaminants should be equipped with handwashing sinks placed near exits. Handwashing sinks should be independent and dedicated, not shared with contaminant processing or experiments.

4. Installation of fume hoods and safety facilities: Any safety hood should be placed as far away from exits as possible, adhering to the principle of keeping harmful experiments away from main corridors.

5. Special Laboratory Design and Layout: Special laboratories here mainly refer to microbiological and molecular biology laboratories, which should generally follow the "General Guidelines for Biosafety in Microbiological and Biomedical Laboratories." Gene amplification laboratories should have sufficient space and be designed and laid out according to standard requirements to avoid laboratory contamination. Microbiological laboratories typically deal with harmful microorganisms and are usually divided into clean areas, semi-contaminated areas, and contaminated areas. Biological safety cabinets should be used in contaminated areas to protect the health of the staff. Modern microbiological laboratories must also be equipped with air conditioning and filtration equipment.

3. Laboratory Ventilation Design

For laboratory safety, if conditions permit, a central air handling system must be installed. Avoid using fans that cause airflow in microbiological laboratories, spreading infectious diseases. Proper ventilation not only removes harmful odors and toxic gases from the laboratory but also ensures the normal operation of equipment. Currently, air exchange numbers advocate 12 times per hour in general laboratories and 16 times per hour in areas using steam and biochemical hazardous agents.

4. Power Supply and Communication Design

Power layout should adequately consider and analyze the power needs of the laboratory, paying attention to the following:

1. The power requirements and quantity of electric sockets needed for all instruments in the laboratory should be reasonably distributed for safe and convenient use.

2. Electric sockets can be three-hole or two-hole.

3. Electric sockets should be distributed in various places to ensure safe and convenient use.

4. Instruments' voltage requirements (220V or 380V) and power consumption.

5. Sufficient consideration should be given to computer power sockets.

6. The number of lighting fixtures required depends on the type of work, the color of the workbench, the color of the ceiling and walls in the studio, the distance between fixed lighting and the workbench, and the size of the space requiring illumination.

7. Position of lighting fixtures: Lighting fixtures should be installed perpendicular or diagonally to the workbench to eliminate shadows caused by object obstructions.

8. Special lighting fixtures: If the laboratory is used for separating microorganisms and conducting molecular biology experiments, they should effectively protect both the staff and the samples from contamination. Ultraviolet lamps are the most commonly used disinfection devices. Fixed ultraviolet lamps should be no more than 2.1 meters above the ground, and the number of UV lamps should be determined based on the laboratory's space. When using UV lighting, it must be ensured that the surfaces (e.g., wall coatings, workbench surfaces) can withstand the bleaching effect of UV light.

When designing power supply, apart from meeting current usage needs, enough expansion capacity should be provided to satisfy future laboratory needs. Communication plays a crucial role in achieving informatization and networking in laboratories, greatly enhancing management quality and work efficiency. During laboratory design, communication lines should be meticulously planned to fully meet current demands while having extra capacity to accommodate instrument increases and movements.

Building a fully functional laboratory requires professional technical personnel for specialized design, taking into account all aspects comprehensively, following the principle of putting people first, constructing standardized laboratories to achieve optimal usage effects.

If you are knowledgeable, you can create a floor plan based on the laboratory's size, functionality, and aesthetics, then ask manufacturers to provide CAD renderings. Alternatively, you can request several manufacturers to prepare multiple plans, compare them, and select a practical and aesthetically pleasing solution. Ventilation is very important. Many laboratories install ventilation after decoration, resulting in costly renovations of ventilation ducts, or settling for substandard ventilation performance. Therefore, ventilation ducts, fans, air volume, and pressure should be considered during the decoration phase.

First, reasonably design the laboratory water supply system:

1. Use DG15PP materials, PVC materials, Kaitai pipes for water supply lines, ensuring water pressure is no less than 2.5 MPa. For drainage, use DG50 PP materials, PVC, or ceramics, with a minimum slope of no less than 5 degrees, and U-shaped anti-backflow bends. Non-metallic materials are recommended for water supply and drainage pipelines. Outdoor pipeline laying can be separately negotiated.

2. Toxic and harmful liquids produced by experiments should be designed with secondary water retention devices, discharging only after disinfection and purification meet emission standards.

3. Laboratory drainage pipelines should be designed with independent circuits and should not be connected to other drainage systems like bathrooms.

Second, reasonably design the laboratory electrical system:

1. Chinese voltage standards: AC three-phase five-wire power source 380V, 50HZ (red A, green B, yellow C, black 0, dual-color protective ground). AC single-phase three-wire power source 220V, 50HZ (red live, black 0, dual-color protective ground).

2. Reasonably design laboratory electricity, wiring using copper-core BVR, BV wires, wire diameter, and switch sizes calculated based on power capacity.

3. Larger load appliances should have separate circuits and corresponding automatic protection switches.

4. Valuable instruments and precision instrument power supplies should be designed with AC stabilizing devices or isolation power supplies to ensure safe and reliable operation of the instruments.

5. All outlets and appliance housings should be properly grounded to ensure personal safety.

6. Reasonably design air conditioning and lighting. Reasonably design electric heating devices to achieve safe and reliable usage purposes.

Third, reasonably design the laboratory gas system:

1. To ensure safety, various gases used in experiments should be stored in explosion-proof rooms away from work areas if conditions allow, otherwise, gas cylinder safety cabinets with automatic alarm functions should be used.

2. Harmful gases generated by experiments should be designed with negative-pressure exhaust systems to prevent indoor leakage.

3. Corresponding numbers of exhaust fans with reverse wind valves should be designed according to the room size ratio to ensure smooth airflow and maintain cleanliness.

4. Each room should have ventilating holes with filtering devices. Rooms with indoor corridors can also have louver windows on doors and windows, with dimensions calculated according to the exhaust volume ratio relationship.

Fourth, reasonably design the laboratory exhaust system:

1. The exhaust system engineering is the key to laboratory construction. The effectiveness of fume hoods mainly depends on the exhaust system design.

2. The material of the exhaust pipe should primarily be FRP inorganic resin material, or PVC or PP materials. The inner walls of the ducts should be made rough to reduce airflow noise. Circular or rectangular shapes can be used, with diameters ranging from 250-500mm, sized according to exhaust volume requirements.

3. Types of FRP corrosion-resistant fans: (1) Axial fans, suitable for single fume hoods or single atomic absorption hoods with smaller exhaust volumes. (2) Oblique-flow fans, suitable for dual fume hoods or dual atomic absorption hoods with slightly larger exhaust volumes. (3) Centrifugal fans, suitable for multiple fume hoods or situations requiring larger exhaust volumes. (4) Roof fans (same as above) can be selected.

4. Corrosion-resistant fans should be installed on outdoor roofs, with rainproof and birdproof covers, vibration isolators, reverse wind valves, and silencers.

5. If the laboratory is an air-conditioned room with installed fume hoods, a proper makeup air system should be designed to avoid wasting energy.

Fifth, reasonably design laboratory furniture to maximize space utilization:

1. Make full use of existing space to play its maximum role, ensuring a rational layout of laboratory furniture that conforms to the regularity of experimental operations, achieving the best scientific use of space.

2. Laboratory furniture conforms to ergonomics, with length, width, and height standards suitable for humanized design concepts.

3. Laboratory furniture design should suit the process flow of experimental work, ensuring smooth work routes and unobstructed passages.

4. Laboratory furniture design should not obstruct light or heat sources, avoiding energy wastage.

5. Laboratory furniture should be made of environmentally friendly, flame-retardant, corrosion-resistant, high-strength professional materials, ensuring durability.

6. Different types of basic furniture should be designed for biological, genetic, physical, and chemical laboratories to ensure safety, economy, and practicality.

Sixth, laboratory renovation and construction:

1. Laboratory renovation differs from general renovation. In addition to being aesthetically pleasing and comfortable, it must achieve fireproofing, moisture-proofing, and corrosion resistance, adding ventilation, purification, disinfection, and sterile functions to reach environmental protection, safety, reliability, and long-term durability.

2. Main considerations for selecting renovation materials are environmental friendliness and corrosion resistance.

3. Using 12MM-19MM thick glass partitions is a widely promoted design solution for current laboratory construction. It provides spacious, bright, scientific, and modern spaces.

4. Toxic and harmful gases and liquids generated by experiments should undergo secondary treatment before discharge, reaching emission standards.

Laboratory Countertops:

Laboratory countertops are becoming increasingly diverse, including materials like Wilsonart (good physicochemical properties), Formica (suitable density, smooth and easy to clean surface), epoxy resin boards (heat-resistant and repairable), among others. Several domestic brands are also available, and using genuine products should pose no problems. In terms of price, imported epoxy resin boards rank first, followed by Formica, Wilsonart, and then domestic boards. Commonly used domestically are these types, plus economical laminated physicochemical boards. If water usage is minimal, laminated panels can meet the physicochemical requirements of solid core panels, though sink areas should use solid core panels.

Cabinet Boards for Laboratory Counters:

Cabinet boards generally use 18mm melamine-coated medium-density boards, with few domestic options for high-density. Some factories now use 16mm boards.

Steel Frames for Laboratory Counters:

Steel frames for laboratory counters typically come in aluminum alloy or steel structures. Aluminum-wood structures are less common in northern regions, where 1.2mm profiles are predominantly used. Some claim to use 2-3mm quenched materials, but 1.5mm thickness is rarely used. Our company uses 1.2mm, and I believe this isn't an issue as instruments won't crush them. Key considerations in laboratories are acid and alkali resistance and corrosion resistance, which depend on whether the steel frame has undergone a series of acid washes, phosphating, and epoxy resin spraying processes.
