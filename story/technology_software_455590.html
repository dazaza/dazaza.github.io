The level of compatibility between employees and their direct supervisors is the most critical factor affecting whether employees are happy at work. Studies have shown that in 90% of employee resignation cases, the issue lies in a mismatch between the employee and their supervisor. So, how should employees and supervisors be matched? Or, as a direct supervisor, how can one ensure good cooperation with subordinates?

1. Respect Employee Individuality

Supervisors should first ensure that the individuality of employees is respected. Human beings come in all sorts of temperaments, each with distinct characteristics, so no two people are exactly alike. However, human personalities do share common traits, such as impatience versus patience, optimism versus pessimism, self-focus versus other-focus. From a work perspective, individuals generally prefer to collaborate with someone whose style aligns with their own. We often see situations where an impatient supervisor encounters a patient employee. In such scenarios, it is crucial for the supervisor to control their emotions and adjust their working methods. Each person's personality is shaped by genetic factors and environmental influences. There is no such thing as a bad temperament or poor personality style; rather, it is about whether the personality suits the nature of the work or matches collaborators.

Supervisors should avoid attacking subordinates based on their personality traits, as this can severely damage the employee’s confidence and affect team cooperation. Wise supervisors should regulate the behavior of subordinates whose personalities do not match their own. This can be achieved through specific work-related requirements, gradually changing the subordinate's characteristics. For instance, when dealing with a slow-paced subordinate, you must clearly specify meeting times and implement appropriate penalties for tardiness (such as requiring latecomers to contribute to a charity fund, followed by enjoying ice cream together after the meeting). With enough patience, employees and supervisors can gradually achieve compatibility.

2. Review Your Leadership Style

Famous psychologist Kurt Lewin from the University of Iowa discovered that group task leaders do not exhibit their leadership roles in the same way. Leaders typically use different leadership styles, which have varying impacts on the group members' performance and job satisfaction. They focused on three types of leadership behaviors/styles: authoritarian, democratic, and laissez-faire.

Authoritarian leaders focus solely on work objectives, only caring about work tasks and efficiency, but showing insufficient concern for team members. There is a significant psychological distance between employees and leaders, with the latter lacking sensitivity towards employees, who may develop suspicion and hostility towards their leader, leading to frustration and mechanistic behaviors among group members. Democratic leaders emphasize encouraging and assisting group members in their work, caring for and satisfying the needs of group members, creating a democratic and equal atmosphere. The psychological distance between democratic leaders and employees is relatively small. Under democratic leadership, employees have strong work motivation and a strong sense of responsibility. Subordinates decide their own work methods and progress, resulting in high efficiency. Laissez-faire leaders adopt an anarchic leadership method, showing little regard for work and group member needs, lacking rules, requirements, and evaluations, leading to low work efficiency and indifferent interpersonal relationships.

Lewin initially believed that democratic leadership seemed to bring about excellent work quality and quantity, with higher job satisfaction among group members. Therefore, democratic leadership might be the most effective leadership style. However, later studies revealed more complex results. Democratic leadership sometimes produces better work performance than authoritarian leadership, while in other cases, the work performance under democratic leadership may be lower or merely equivalent to that under authoritarian leadership. Employee satisfaction surveys show that under democratic leadership, members generally report higher job satisfaction than under authoritarian leadership. However, in real-world business management, there are rarely extreme forms of leadership. Most leaders are a mix of authoritarian, democratic, and laissez-faire styles. Lewin's theory focuses on the leader's personal style but does not adequately consider the situational factors that influence the leader, as the effectiveness of leadership actions depends not only on the leader's style but also on the followers and surrounding circumstances.

Leaders must continuously self-assess their leadership style while considering whether their style is acceptable to employees or which management actions employees can accept and which might cause psychological harm. "The observer sees more than the participant," diagnosing one's own leadership style is challenging, so leaders must effectively utilize diagnostic tools like employee satisfaction surveys.

3. Acknowledge Employee Achievements

Acknowledging employee achievements has become a treasure for excellent leaders to achieve management goals. When an employee completes a task, they most need recognition from their supervisor. The supervisor's acknowledgment is the greatest affirmation of their work achievement. Former GE CEO Jack Welch said, "My management philosophy is to make everyone feel their contributions are visible, tangible, and countable." A supervisor's acknowledgment can make employees feel important, fostering a sense of responsibility and duty within them, reinforcing their attitude of hard work and positive advancement. The timing of acknowledgment is most crucial. If used too frequently, its value diminishes, whereas using it sparingly in special occasions enhances its value. Methods include sending an email, making a private congratulatory phone call, or shaking hands publicly and expressing appreciation.

4. Maintain Effective Communication

Communication is the primary tool for supervisors and employees to exchange work and emotions. Effective communication is always two-way. As the saying goes, "One hand cannot clap alone." Only through verbal exchanges and emotional expressions can the other party understand your concepts, opinions, and thoughts, achieving the expected communication goal. Maintaining effective communication requires high communication skills from supervisors. Supervisors must continuously improve their communication skills to maintain smooth communication with employees of various temperaments. In practice, we find that outstanding leaders possess much higher communication abilities than average leaders. What are the standout communication behaviors of excellent leaders?

1) First, excellent supervisors have proactive communication awareness. They believe effective communication unifies thoughts, aligns goals, resolves conflicts, and eliminates misunderstandings. They are willing to solve existing problems or eliminate potential risks through communication.

2) Outstanding leaders excel at listening to others' ideas, allowing employees to fully express their opinions without rudely interrupting, making the other party feel respected and more willing to express genuine thoughts and feelings.

3) Excellent supervisors provide active feedback on employees' opinions. Generally, employees' expressed ideas, opinions, or suggestions receive quick responses rather than being ignored. Even if the suggestions are immature, employees receive acknowledgment and useful guidance, quickly refining their ideas.

4) Experienced supervisors pay special attention to communication timing, choosing moments when employees are emotionally stable to discuss less pleasant topics. They avoid public criticism that could hurt employee self-esteem and never scold employees on the spot.

5) Finally, excellent leaders choose appropriate wording and control reasonable speaking speed based on the context. For example, during year-end summary meetings, supervisors often use complimentary words to affirm employees' achievements, using passionate speeches to boost morale. Even when discussing mistakes, they take responsibility sincerely and express their views patiently.
