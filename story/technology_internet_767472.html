Parent who spent 400,000 yuan to change phones for all students in the school: I am not a tycoon

by anonymous on 2013-10-16 17:32:23

Because his mother is from Chongqing, 16 years ago, Liu Jiazheng (a pseudonym), who had been living in the north, came to Chongqing and lived peacefully with his family. However, in early October this year, his willingness to donate 400,000 yuan to purchase non-smartphones for students at Chongqing Railway Middle School, where his son studies, plunged him into a vortex of doubts.

Because of his very simple initial intention, Liu Jiazheng never intended to respond directly to these doubts. To avoid being misunderstood as hype, he even refused face-to-face interviews. Yesterday, after much consideration, Liu Jiazheng decided to accept a telephone interview from the reporter.

1. Has your child used a smartphone?

Liu Jiazheng: Yes, he has. Last summer vacation, I spent more than 2000 yuan to buy my child a smartphone, and soon problems arose.

The phone was always with him; he played with it while eating, doing homework, and especially loved playing small games. Not long after the start of school, his eyesight began to decline. He changed glasses twice in one semester, and his prescription kept increasing. Not only did his eyesight decline, but his sleep also worsened. The last thing he did before sleeping and the first thing he did after waking up were to play on the phone.

At this point, I felt that giving my child a smartphone was like gambling, and I didn't want to stake his health. This summer, I discussed with my child to stop using smartphones. He was extremely unwilling, but we eventually switched to non-smartphones.

There was some effect; his eyesight improved somewhat, and he no longer just stared at the phone, ignoring others. Once, he came back and told me about something he saw at school: a classmate fell down the stairs because they were too engrossed in their phone. I felt that I needed to do something to save more children, so I sent a text message to the principal.

2. Is the reason you chose Railway Middle School for the pilot program because your child studies there?

Liu Jiazheng: That's part of the reason, but mainly it's because of Principal Huang. I've heard him speak at parent-teacher meetings before; he speaks slowly and methodically, and seems rigorous. I feel that he has ideals and passion for education. Initially, we communicated via text messages, and after my idea matured, I had a face-to-face discussion with him and other school leaders. At that time, the school made a commitment to protect my privacy, which is why I agreed to do this.

3. Where were the donated phones purchased?

Liu Jiazheng: They were bought in Chongqing. I first looked online to see which phones would be suitable. After selecting some options, I even called the customer service of the phones. In the end, I chose a model that has calling and texting functions, can access QQ, but cannot browse the web. The QQ function is considered for the convenience of communication among children. If there are any notifications in the class group, they can be promptly informed. Relying solely on sending text messages would definitely increase costs.

4. 400,000 yuan is quite a substantial amount.

Liu Jiazheng: 400,000 yuan is just my annual income, and doing this will not lower our quality of life. I haven't signed any agreements with the school, but I won't unilaterally terminate the arrangement. After the class trial begins, as long as the school requires it, I will continue donating in batches.

5. How are these phones distributed?

Liu Jiazheng: That's something the school needs to consider. Previously, I communicated with the school authorities; the phones must be collectively collected by the class as a unit, rather than being privately collected by individual students. Why is this rule necessary? We cannot isolate even one child. For example, if most classmates use smartphones and only a few use regular phones, it could create an unhealthy atmosphere. Therefore, the entire class must switch together.

6. What do your family and friends think?

Liu Jiazheng: My friends don't know about this matter at all; I have remained anonymous. Only my wife knows within the family, and she supports me, believing that I am doing a good deed. My son still doesn't know, and I plan to tell him after he enters university. Before then, I will have him continue using a regular phone.

7. How many media calls have you received recently?

Liu Jiazheng: I've lost count. Every time I receive an interview call, I feel anxious, not because I'm afraid, but because some questions come with strong doubts and are highly aggressive, completely deviating from my original intentions.

8. What specific questions are there?

Liu Jiazheng: Some privacy-related questions. Some ask if the donation is legal? Why not donate to the Red Cross first and then transfer it to the school? Is there any personal motive? Am I selling phones? Why not donate to students in impoverished mountainous areas... All sorts of questions.

Another kind of statement suggests that experts and scholars recommend considering another approach. I replied that what they (experts and scholars) want are papers, and what we need are methods. By the time discussions are over, the children will be grown up. Do you have a better method? When faced with such questions, I usually counter-question them.

9. What do you think about the doubts online?

Liu Jiazheng: I'm not asking children to abandon smartphones entirely, just to put them aside temporarily during adolescence. The current putting aside is for the future ability to use them better. Why choose middle schools for the pilot program? It's because children at this age have the strongest curiosity, and it is also a critical period for intellectual and physical development. Even if using regular phones may not necessarily help with learning, as long as we protect their eyes and necks, it is worth it.

I am not a "tycoon"; I am just a father who cares about children. Someone said (changing to regular phones) is a step backward in history, and I can't bear these comments. My original intention was very simple; I just wanted to do something for the health of the children, but now it has become complicated.

10. Is there anything that has made you particularly unhappy recently?

Liu Jiazheng: Yes. A couple of days ago, Principal Huang told me that a telecommunications company approached the school wanting all the phones to use their provided SIM cards, but Principal Huang drove them away.

If I were there, I would have driven them away too. What makes me angriest are these commercial behaviors.

We are just providing phones; the children will continue to use their previous SIM cards. Any messy commercial behavior should stay far away from the children.
