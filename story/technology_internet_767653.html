Google Engineering Director Ray Kurzweil: We Are a Human-Machine Civilization

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 11:52:24

Not long ago, Google founded a biotech company called Calico and invited Arthur Levinson, the former chairman of Genetech's board, to serve as its CEO. This company is part of Google’s Moon Shot plan, and it is also a controversial topic. Through the development of artificial intelligence, Google has already begun building something akin to the "Skynet" from science fiction movies. However, the project aimed at combating aging and death raises even more concerns. Birth, aging, illness, and death are natural laws, and they are also necessary for societal renewal. Once this rule is overturned, who knows what kind of energy will be released?

However, it is not surprising that Google has ventured into the health field. Prior to this, Google had hired futurist Ray Kurzweil to serve as its engineering director, a man who proposed the singularity theory and believes that human thought can achieve immortality. Although we are not yet clear on how Ray Kurzweil relates to Calico, his interest in bioscience is clearly strong. Recently, Ray Kurzweil was interviewed by the website macleans, discussing many aspects of bioscience, and perhaps we can also glimpse Google's future direction from this.

Kurzweil compares the mechanisms of life to the operation of software, and the software running in the human body is outdated. The insulin receptor gene in our bodies makes us store every bit of heat, which may have been applicable ten thousand years ago because people consumed fewer calories back then, so it was necessary to store them in fat cells. It is no longer necessary now. At the Joslin Diabetes Center, experimental mice with this gene turned off ate a lot but did not become obese. Their lifespans increased by 20%. Currently, the center is working with pharmaceutical companies to bring this product to market.

By planning health and medicine as software, human lifespan will continue to extend. “We treat biology, and by extension health and medicine, as information technology. Intuitively, we think progress unfolds linearly, but information technology exhibits exponential growth.”

Kurzweil views immortality as a continuously advancing process, “I cannot say, ‘I am done, I will live forever’, because there is no such thing as 'forever'. What we truly discuss is the path towards the next node.” Currently, he takes 150 supplements daily, and all indicators are normal, but this practice is just the first bridge. The second bridge is a revolution in biotechnology, where humans can stay far away from diseases. The third bridge is a revolution in nanotechnology. By that time, people can use nanorobots to enhance their immune systems. These robots can recognize all diseases and combat new ones.

Just like the evolution of mobile phones, this technology will initially be very expensive, only available to a few people, and not very user-friendly, but it will gradually become accessible to the masses and reach a highly functional state by then. Technology is a double-edged sword. If bioterrorists create new viruses, we can quickly detect and reverse engineer them. “It took five years to sequence HIV's genome, and SARS took only 31 days. Nowadays, we can sequence a virus's genome within a day. Within a few days, we can detect new viruses, sequence their genomes, manufacture resistance drugs, and deploy them.”

Currently, Kurzweil's main work at Google is related to artificial intelligence. He mentioned IBM's super robot Watson. Watson demonstrates powerful intelligence because it can read a large number of natural language documents. “Its ability to read documents is not as good as ours, but its extensive reading compensates for its lack of comprehension — we cannot read 200 million documents. This is an example of artificial intelligence technology. What we do at Google is similar. We want machines to understand the meaning of documents, just like Watson does.” In this way, machines will be able to search and answer questions better.

In Kurzweil's view, intellectual capacity in human civilization is experiencing exponential growth. Technology is a part of civilization. People will discuss conflicts between machines and humans, but “the machines we create are not invaders from Mars; we create tools to expand our own abilities.”

“We are a human-machine civilization. Machines are part of our intellect.” The machine's intellectual part will grow rapidly, leading to the arrival of the singularity, and human history will change. At that time, humans will be wealthier and live longer. Kurzweil said he pays more attention to the aspect of wealth. Wealth is not just financial; technology will enrich our lives, “by expanding our intellect, all forms of human expression, art, technology, etc., will be expanded.”
