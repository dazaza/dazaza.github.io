Sina Weibo, how did it change from a chess game to a pawn?

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 19:03:59

Rumors of Alibaba's stake in Sina Weibo were finally confirmed during the May Day holiday. Thus, the short-term answer to Sina Weibo's commercialization challenges has been set: not advertising, not gaming, but rather e-commerce with the support of Alibaba. Looking back two years ago, Weibo users were growing exponentially, Sina's stock price soared over $140 under the impetus of Weibo, and marketing professionals invariably discussed Weibo marketing. Now, Weibo's prominence has faded, and Sina must pragmatically seek ways to monetize its services. If Alibaba gains control in the future, whether or not Weibo can independently go public will depend on Alibaba's decisions. For Sina, partnering with Alibaba is a lifeline, while for Alibaba, although Weibo is important, it is merely one piece in Jack Ma's empire.Why hasn't Weibo achieved a greater scale? In retrospect, it seems almost inevitable that Sina Weibo would reach this point. I attribute this outcome to two reasons: lack of self-reliance and lack of confidence.First, lack of self-reliance. The product and technical capabilities are insufficient to develop a platform-level product.Although Weibo still possesses media attributes, compared to the portal sites Sina has long dominated, Weibo places higher demands on product and technology. Sina’s consistently pragmatic, or perhaps shortsighted, behavior patterns have rendered it incapable of developing a platform-level product. Apart from internet advertising, Sina has dabbled in virtually all mature forms of internet products and made significant investments, but all except blogging have failed. Gaming (Sina LeGu), e-commerce (Yahoo's YiPai partnership), instant messaging (Sina UC), search (Sina iAsk) — each market was much larger than portal advertising, yet Sina withdrew from all of them without success. Only blogging, which is low in technological complexity and high in media attributes, succeeded due to Sina’s strong operational capabilities.In the leading areas of portals and display advertising where Sina excels, it has not led industry developments through either technology or business models. The layout of Sina's portal site has remained largely unchanged for over a decade, and its April 2013 redesign was simply following suit with Tencent and NetEase's updates. Sina pioneered the Chinese internet advertising CPM (cost per day) model, making it the primary sales model for Chinese portal sites. However, this model, akin to selling ad space in print media, remains Sina’s main source of income. It wasn’t until 2013 that there began some scaled explorations into precise advertising. By then, Tencent's precise advertising platform was already relatively mature and generating substantial revenue.Regardless of whether it is due to an inherent DNA theory or the overly dispersed shareholding structure causing Sina to be inconsistent in product development, Sina's weak technical and product capabilities are widely acknowledged within the industry. Given the resources at its disposal, Sina did well to bring Weibo to its current state. It would be unrealistic to expect Sina to develop competitive platform-level products like Facebook or Twitter.Second, lack of confidence, wavering between social media and social networking, missing out on opportunities to further develop its media attributes.From both the perspective of Sina as a company with media attributes and the nature of the Weibo product itself, Weibo is fundamentally a media platform—a socialized media platform. During the middle phase of Weibo’s development, encouraged by its promising growth, Sina’s senior management aimed to create a social networking platform rather than a social media platform. One major reason was the dominance of Facebook at the time; it was widely believed that social networks like Facebook were far more valuable than social media platforms like Twitter. Sina emphasized Weibo's social network attributes in its external communications and strengthened functions related to strong connections in its product design. For example, Sina launched Mifriend, mimicking Path, and WeFriend based on private messages. However, these strategies to enhance strong relationships were unclear. When chatting with people inside Sina, they themselves were unsure about the differences in positioning between Mifriend and WeFriend.This leads to another mistake by Sina—attempting to strengthen social networking attributes while neglecting social media functionality. Sina also introduced several media-oriented products, such as MicroJournal, MicroBar, and LightBlog, but neither the user experience nor the operations received sufficient attention. None of these products became core offerings capable of significantly enhancing user media experiences and engagement.When WeChat developed and its Moments provided a sharing platform for users to connect with close friends and family, Weibo faced an awkward situation of being hollowed out. Users' strong relationships shifted to WeChat; you may have noticed that posting photos during holidays is livelier on WeChat than on Weibo. On Weibo, only opinion leaders, media outlets, and businesses maintain weak connections with users, leading to more Weibo users lurking rather than sharing content. Due to the aforementioned reasons, the experience of weak connections on Weibo has not improved substantially over the years, resulting in user experience fatigue.Looking at Twitter, which Sina once emulated, it has consistently adhered to its social media positioning, continuously strengthening its media functions and layouts. Recently, it launched rmusic music services and acquired BluefinLabs, which might be integrated into its social TV products in the future. Meanwhile, Facebook has also enhanced its media characteristics in certain features. Competitive internet products are not about addressing every weakness but about leveraging their strengths better than others. Sina Weibo lost its identity by wavering between social networking and social media.Alibaba's investment in Sina Weibo means that Sina has completely lost the opportunity to change China's internet landscape from BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent) to BATS (adding Sina), turning it into just another piece in Alibaba's grand chessboard.To play its role effectively as this piece, Sina still has a long way to go. With closer collaboration with Alibaba, Sina Weibo's technology and product capabilities should improve. The positioning of Sina Weibo still rests in the hands of the people at Sina.
