Getting 4 points is pretty good/www.zp-nmg.com

by pmakerx7 on 2011-06-20 10:49:02

The evaluation of employees' achievements is one of the main tasks of a manager. At HP, whether it's an old employee or a new recruit, everyone will have a very clear job responsibility statement.

The job responsibility statement is formulated based on the job description. At HP, the job description is a framework document designed for a certain type of work, while the content of the job responsibility statement is more specific to a particular person and what they need to do in a specific department in the coming year. For example, all market engineers have the same job description, but when it comes to a specific engineer, their job responsibilities differ depending on which department they are in, what specific tasks they perform, and what the department manager expects them to focus on in the coming year. This is the job responsibility statement. In general, the former is generic, and the latter is specific.

The composition of the evaluators determines who the employees are responsible to. If an employee’s evaluator is only their direct superior, then they just need to please this superior. However, at HP, it's not like that. The evaluators consist of superiors, subordinates, and relevant peers from different departments. For instance, in employee satisfaction ratings, which are assessed by subordinates evaluating their superiors, there are around ten questions related to leadership, such as whether your superior treats their subordinates fairly and impartially, and whether your superior maintains good communication with you...

With such a job responsibility statement, employees immediately understand: they cannot just be responsible to their superior; they must also be responsible to many others. Under such institutional constraints, everyone gradually realizes: helping others is actually helping oneself, because you never know which cloud will rain. This way, team cooperation becomes an employee's voluntary action. Everyone will cooperate well and get every task done properly. Only by doing so can HP employees gain recognition from others and achieve high scores during evaluations, avoiding distractions and speculative pursuits.

To say the least, comprehensive performance evaluation is not just about operational performance. For newly hired and regularized employees, their evaluations are conducted every six months according to the job responsibility statement. After one year, evaluations are conducted annually. The specific evaluation process involves taking out the employee's job responsibility statement and having the evaluators score each item. The highest score is 5 points, and the lowest is 1 point. A final weighted average score is calculated, with a total score of 5 indicating excellence. However, the proportion of people scoring 5 across the entire company cannot exceed 5%.

I have worked at HP for nearly 15 years, and I have only received a score of 5 twice. More often, I received scores of 4 and 3, because once promoted to a new level, one usually returns to a score of 2 or 3. The criteria for getting a 5 vary at different levels, and higher levels come with higher requirements. This scoring method continuously motivates outstanding employees. When an employee is promoted to a front-line manager or a front-line manager is promoted to a second-line manager, it is quite difficult to get a 5 within the next two years; getting a 4 is already good. If an employee performs well and gets promoted every two years, the chance of getting a 5 is very small. New positions come with new, higher demands, which is normal: if the company pays higher salaries, employees must demonstrate corresponding levels of performance. If they fail to do so, their scores will drop. As a result, employees must continuously enrich themselves, enhance various abilities, and perform at the required level in their new position; otherwise, even if their rank goes up, their overall evaluation score may go down, and their salary might not increase.

Furthermore, making employees clearly understand their job responsibilities is a significant task for HP managers, and conducting annual performance evaluations based on job responsibility statements is the duty of managers.

After the comprehensive performance evaluation is completed, each employee receives a written document - the Performance Evaluation Report (performance evaluation). It is written by the employee's direct superior. The company allows each manager to take one day per year to specifically write a performance evaluation report for a subordinate. If a manager oversees 10 subordinates, they can spend 10 days in a year specifically writing these 10 reports, either in the office or at home. This is a very important task for managers at all levels of HP, requiring dedicated time. In HP's view, if a manager does not seriously write this report, employees may feel neglected or deceived, leading them to believe their superior is merely going through the motions.

When managers write evaluations for their subordinates, they cannot use ambiguous language but must provide concrete examples to prove that the employee has demonstrated a certain skill, attitude, or enthusiasm. For example, a specific event where an employee performed on a particular date, showing a certain level of competence; or another event where an employee fell short in some aspect.

If a superior does not observe the employee closely, it would be difficult to write the evaluation report in a single day, or even if it is written, the employee may feel that the report lacks specificity - it doesn't highlight their strengths or address their weaknesses, failing to convince them fully. Therefore, at HP, managers need to be aware of much of what employees do daily. They should note both commendable actions and mistakes, pointing them out clearly while also recording them as material for future evaluations.

Thus, as a manager, one must carefully observe and meticulously record events regularly. When it comes time to complete the evaluations, it won’t be difficult; otherwise, scratching heads for a whole day still wouldn’t yield results. Over time, HP managers have developed the habit of observing employee behavior and documenting it regularly.
