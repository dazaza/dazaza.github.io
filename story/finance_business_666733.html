Your beloved car occasionally "cries" - A brief discussion on water entering the headlights.

Many drivers have encountered the situation where mist forms inside the headlights. At such times, the headlights look like big, watery eyes that evoke sympathy. What exactly is going on? Why has it become so upset? Is it due to poor sealing of the headlights, allowing water to enter inside, or is it because the lens is damaged, allowing moisture to seep into the headlights?

In terms of the overall structure of the headlights, whether they are ordinary halogen headlights, xenon headlights, or those with LED light groups, there is a ventilation rubber tube located at the rear cover. When the headlights are turned on and during use, a large amount of heat is generated. The function of the ventilation tube is to discharge as much of this heat as possible outside the headlights in order to maintain their normal operating temperature and ensure stable usage.

It is through this ventilation tube that moisture from the air can enter the headlights and adhere to the lens. As water vapor accumulates, droplets form and flow down along the lens. This situation is mostly caused by significant temperature differences and is most likely to occur during winter and rainy seasons. In such cases, drivers need not worry too much. After the headlights have been on for some time, the mist will be discharged outside the headlights along with the hot air through the ventilation tube. This generally does not damage the headlights or electrical circuits. In this scenario, drivers should not act rashly, such as attempting to bake the headlights, which could easily cause damage since the outer surface of the headlights is made of plastic material. Additional heat can easily melt the lens, and such damage is often irreparable.

Aside from weather changes, human factors can also make the headlights appear "teary," such as driving through water or washing the car. When driving through water, since the engine and exhaust system themselves are relatively large heat sources, rainwater falling on them generates a large amount of steam. Some of this steam enters the headlights through the ventilation tube.

Washing the car is even more direct. Some car owners like to use high-pressure water guns to clean the engine compartment, enjoying the satisfaction of watching mud and leaves being washed away one by one. However, after cleaning, if the accumulated water in the engine compartment is not promptly dealt with and the engine compartment cover is closed, the water vapor cannot quickly dissipate outside the car. The water vapor trapped inside the engine compartment may then enter the headlights.

For cleaning the engine compartment, we should primarily use cotton threads or cloth to wipe, or use high-pressure air to blow, minimizing the exposure of the engine area to moisture as much as possible.

All the above-mentioned situations belong to relatively mild cases of water entering the headlights. However, if the accumulation of water inside your car's headlights is sufficient to keep fish, then you need to dismantle them, open the lens, dry them, and check for any damage or potential leaks on the surface of the headlights. If no abnormalities are found, we generally recommend replacing the rear cover seal strip and ventilation tube of the headlights.

During winter and rainy seasons, car owners should ideally develop the habit of regularly checking the headlights. For example, checking after driving through water is necessary. Early detection and prompt remediation can effectively eliminate faults at an early stage, which is the best approach to vehicle maintenance.
