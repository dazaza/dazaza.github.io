Woman sues social security bureau after being rejected for nearly 20 years of social security contributions after resignation

by jeffbcl5644 on 2010-06-23 21:19:10

BEIJING - After nearly 20 years of working abroad, Ms Zhang returned to China and demanded her former employer pay her social security contributions. When they refused, she took the Chaoyang District Human Resources and Social Security Bureau (hereinafter referred to as Chaoyang HRSSB) to court. The administrative lawsuit was heard in Chaoyang Court yesterday morning.

Ms Zhang is now 55 years old and was originally a telephone repairer at the Civil Aviation General Hospital. In 1987, she self-funded her study in Italy and applied for a one-year unpaid leave. The following year, due to incomplete studies, Ms Zhang entrusted her husband to write a written application agreeing to resign according to the hospital's regulations.

In 2005, unexpectedly, Ms Zhang suddenly commissioned a lawyer to go to the Civil Aviation General Hospital, demanding that they pay her social security contributions. After being rejected, she sued the Civil Aviation General Hospital in court claiming damages of one million yuan. After mediation, the Civil Aviation General Hospital agreed to have Ms Zhang fund her own social security contributions.Good ManGood Man

In February this year, when the Civil Aviation General Hospital attempted to process Ms Zhang’s social security, the Chaoyang HRSSB rejected it on the grounds that Ms Zhang did not meet the continuous service requirements and had already resigned voluntarily. More than two months later, Ms Zhang claimed that the Chaoyang HRSSB failed to act administratively, taking them to court demanding that they handle her pension and medical insurance.

Yesterday morning, the Chaoyang HRSSB stated that they strictly reviewed according to national regulations and determined that Ms Zhang did not meet the conditions for supplementary social security contributions, hence refusing to enroll her.

Ms Zhang’s attorney argued that since she did not formally resign, she should still be considered an employee of the Civil Aviation General Hospital and her length of service should be calculated accordingly.

The representative from the HRSSB questioned: "Do you believe that after almost 20 years of working abroad without providing any labor to the unit, her length of service should still be counted? Would it be fair to other employees if the Civil Aviation General Hospital were to pay her social security contributions?"

In response, Ms Zhang’s lawyer insisted that since her file was never withdrawn, her length of service should be counted.

The Civil Aviation General Hospital participated in the litigation as a third party. Their representative stated that the Chaoyang HRSSB found a letter in Ms Zhang’s file indicating her "voluntary resignation." Her lawyer had requested the Civil Aviation General Hospital to revoke it, but they refused.

Chaoyang Court did not announce the verdict during the hearing.
