Zinc oxide (ZnO), commonly known as zinc white, is an oxide of zinc. It is insoluble in water but soluble in acids and strong alkalis. Appearance: white or pale yellow powder or hexagonal crystals. No odor. Bitter taste. Can sublime under normal pressure. Can absorb carbon dioxide from the air. Heating to 300℃ causes color change to yellow, but it returns to white upon cooling. Soluble in dilute acetic acid, mineral acids, ammonia water, ammonium carbonate, and hydroxide alkali solutions, almost insoluble in water. Relative density 5.67 (hexagonal crystal), (d204) 5.607. Melting point above 1800℃. Refractive index (nD) 2.0041(2.0203). Storage: Sealed cool storage, avoiding damage, moisture, contamination, and contact with acids. Mechanical properties: The hardness of zinc oxide is approximately 4.5, making it a relatively soft material. The elastic constants of zinc oxide are smaller than those of III-V semiconductor materials like gallium nitride. Zinc oxide has good thermal stability and thermal conductivity, high boiling point, and low thermal expansion coefficient, making it useful in ceramic materials. Among various semiconductors with tetrahedral structures, zinc oxide has the highest piezoelectric tensor. This characteristic makes zinc oxide an important material for mechanical-electrical coupling. Electrical properties: At room temperature, the band gap of zinc oxide is approximately 3.3 eV, thus pure zinc oxide is colorless and transparent. A high band gap gives zinc oxide advantages such as high breakdown voltage, strong electric field holding ability, low electronic noise, and high power endurance. Mixing zinc oxide with a certain proportion of magnesium oxide or cadmium oxide can vary the band gap between 3-4 eV. Even without any other substances added, zinc oxide exhibits N-type semiconductor characteristics. These characteristics were once thought to be related to the non-stoichiometry of compound atoms, but research on impure zinc oxide serves as a counterexample. Using elements from Group IIIA such as aluminum, gallium, indium, or halogens like chlorine, iodine can adjust its N-type semiconductor properties. Making zinc oxide into a P-type semiconductor presents some difficulty. Available additives include alkali metals such as lithium, sodium, potassium, Group VA elements like nitrogen, phosphorus, arsenic, and metals such as copper, silver, but they require special conditions to be effective. Preparation methods: Zinc oxide exists naturally in red zinc ore but with low purity. Industrially used zinc oxide is typically obtained by burning zinc or roasting sphalerite. The global annual output of zinc oxide is around 10 million tons, produced by several methods. Indirect method: The raw material for the indirect method is metal zinc ingots or zinc slag obtained through smelting. Zinc is converted into zinc vapor in a graphite crucible at a high temperature of 1000°C, then oxidized by the air blown in to form zinc oxide, which is collected after passing through a cooling tube as zinc oxide particles. The indirect method was promoted by French scientist LeClaire in 1844, hence it is also called the French method. The indirect method for producing zinc oxide has simple technology, but costs are greatly influenced by raw materials. The particle diameter of zinc oxide produced by the indirect method is around 0.1-10 micrometers, with purity between 99.5%-99.7%. In terms of total production, the indirect method is the most important way to produce zinc oxide.
