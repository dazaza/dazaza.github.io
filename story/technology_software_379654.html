Baidu Xiaosheng: The Android app I'm using

by xdash on 2011-01-24 10:25:20

(The author of this article, Xiaosheng, is currently a product manager at Baidu Mobile Department and a co-founder of Mobile Design China.)

As professionals in the mobile internet industry, we must study all kinds of popular Android/iOS applications. Especially those products similar to our own projects, we need to understand them from aspects such as visual design, interaction design, information architecture, and operation. But as a Moto Milestone user, I also have to use various applications in daily life. Since I graduated with a degree in industrial design, I am fond of simplicity, like MIJI, IKEA, 1984, and Douban. Therefore, I have higher requirements for the visual interface when choosing mobile applications, just like how programmers love cutting-edge technology. Professional background can even affect the way we choose products in daily life.

Invited by XDash to talk about commonly used applications, there's no need to mention familiar ones like Gmail, Gtalk, and browsers. So, I'll pick four lesser-known "daily essentials."

**Jorte**

Jorte Notebook is like a simplified version of Google Calendar, with simple and easy-to-use main functions. Less frequently used features are placed in the settings. The first page after opening shows the calendar, allowing quick viewing of schedule arrangements. You can choose different visual styles and arrangement methods, dynamic effects for view transitions, and swipe left or right to flip pages. It can sync with Jorte Calendar or Google Calendar, supports data export and import, and friends who love syncing will enjoy it.

The functions can basically meet most users' needs for calendars quickly and efficiently. It supports up to 16 different styles of desktop widgets, more than any other application, which can satisfy any screen display space requirement.

When clicking on a date, a dialog box pops up to create a new event, and you can also select "previous day" and "next day." These two options are actually unnecessary. The resolution of the Moto Milestone is 480*854 pixels, and even with a horizontal resolution of 320 pixels, each date's clickable area can reach 45 pixels. After converting precision, it already meets the thumb-clicking area for adult users, so the possibility of accidental clicks is minimal.

Clicking on a single task allows you to view the task details, set status, and edit. The underline style for "show details" is a web design standard, but personally, I find such designs in client-side apps quite annoying. This might be related to Android's own control design. Hiding some less-used "advanced" options in the page still aligns well with user operation habits.

Compared to Jorte, the design of the sixth-ranked 365 Calendar in the Android Market is slightly more complex, with more information on the page and more functions. 365 Calendar is a typical example of completely transplanting iOS design to Android, with unresponsive physical back key operations, making me feel awkward using it.

365 Calendar has lunar terms, fitting the local context. The schedule and calendar are just different views, with significant overlap of information between adjacent views. When I first used it, the default display was Beijing weather, assuming that I could modify it under "more" or "settings," but failed twice before realizing that it's in the "tools" weather section, which doesn't support automatic positioning. This is a common problem when adding too many features. Its desktop widget combines with time, making its usability higher than Jorte's, occupying the user's main screen position.

**Baidu Around**

Just released in December, Baidu Around already has a WAP version and an Android version. The polar bear on the startup screen is quite cute and looks very friendly. Personally, I think the interface design of the Dianping app I previously used needed major changes, and after six months of use, the interface design had hardly changed. New LBS products like Baidu Around, Shanda Check-in, and QQ Food are all more aesthetically pleasing. However, currently, Dianping has a clear advantage in data.

For LBS products, I personally don't care much about check-in features, but rather what kind of convenient information services they provide. After asking a few colleagues in the LBS field about improvements to user check-ins, the answer was that as long as users like to check in, they would post their current location and activities. Personally, I think this idea is a bit simplistic; it makes the product very pure, but there are still many designs around check-ins or user geographical locations that can be done.

For Baidu Around, I focus on how to quickly discover nearby merchants, obtain information to help me determine my action goals, and what I can do upon reaching my destination. The interface details of Baidu Around still need some fine-tuning. Personally, seeing so many "＞" on the homepage drives me crazy. What is displayed on the main interface of a mobile product reflects what the product design focuses on. For example, the main page of Dianping displays business districts and channels, while the main page of Jiapang shows friends' check-in information.

If you often watch movies, Mtime is indispensable. Last year, I worked on a mobile product for querying movies and selecting seats, so I paid more attention to Mtime, and indeed, I have a real need to check movie information.
