The "key" to opening the network world

by shooter on 2007-06-22 14:30:06

             Currently, browsers have become the "key" for enterprises to open up the network world. One can say that all kinds of browsers dazzle you. While people stick to IE, I believe they also secretly pay attention to others like FIREFOX, maxthon, TheWorld, etc.....

           The storm in the browser field rises again in China. In 2007, Mozilla Foundation's Firefox browser announced its official entry into China and truly gained a foothold there due to its open-source characteristics. However, the journey is bumpy with the "squeeze" from international IT giant Microsoft ahead and the "encirclement" by domestic browsers behind. Whether the Firefox browser market can remain calm and dominate the mainland market is worth looking forward to or observing critically.

           In the vast browser field, although Microsoft IE's leading position is self-evident, I believe that Mozilla Foundation's Firefox browser is not the only rival. But Firefox is IE's biggest opponent.

           Related news: At the beginning of this year, Chris Beard, vice president of the Mozilla Foundation, announced to the public that the Firefox browser would officially enter the Chinese market and set up a company or office in China, with research and development work in China soon to follow. Liu Zhiyong, secretary-general of Mozilla China Center, confirmed this matter to reporters: "The preparatory work for setting up an office in China by the Mozilla Foundation is underway, and it is expected to yield results within two months." This move has drawn attention from industry insiders: what kind of situation will the Firefox browser, which holds a 30% share in Europe, create upon entering China? Will it be resisted by Chinese local culture?

           Firefox vs Microsoft?

           The browser competition between Microsoft and Firefox started as early as 1998 when a group of volunteers publicly released the source code of the Firefox browser as a bargaining chip against Microsoft, achieving remarkable "results": On the day the Firefox 1.0 version was launched, global downloads exceeded one million people; By October 2006 when version 2.0 was launched, the first-day download volume reached two million people, surpassing the simultaneously released Microsoft IE 7.0 version. "Choosing Firefox is choosing freedom," Liu Zhiyong explained to reporters the reason for Firefox's beautiful opening. At the same time, he emphasized, "The biggest advantage of Firefox over other browsers is being open-source, allowing programming experts and network enthusiasts worldwide to participate in the development of Firefox and customize it personally." The humanized concept is reassuring.

           Related information: Although the competition between the two parties in the United States is fierce, Firefox's entry into China remains relatively "low-key." When asked whether Firefox could threaten Microsoft IE's "leading" position in China, relevant personnel stressed: "We are not competing with Microsoft. Firefox enters China to provide more choices for Chinese netizens to freely download the Firefox browser. The key point is to improve everyone's internet usage concept." However, "Firefox will subtly influence China's internet and bring a fresh breeze to China's internet."

           Besides facing Microsoft's challenge, Firefox's entry into China must also deal with many domestic browser manufacturers such as TT and Maxthon. Currently, the leaders of domestic browsers have not made too many public statements, and they are all quietly observing Firefox's direction.

           Firefox browser in China is a non-profit free software. Due to different survival methods, Firefox browser cannot be compared with domestic software enterprises. It is understood that Firefox's income sources mainly come from two aspects: one is that every time a user downloads and installs the Firefox browser with the Google toolbar, Google pays one dollar fee to Mozilla Corporation; In addition, donations to the Mozilla Foundation.   "Chinese understand Chinese better, from this perspective, Firefox's entry into China has little impact on Tencent." Tencent browser, which has a 4.7% market share domestically, has a different view. "Tencent has unique advantages in customer resources. Tencent browser is based on Tencent QQ, and its large user base is no less than the total number of users of Microsoft IE in China."

           Actually, in attracting users, Firefox has its own calculations. Due to the large student population in China who easily accept new things and prefer personalized, humanized browsers, we target the student population as our main promotion space. But this is also Tencent's main user base, so both sides will inevitably engage in a battle for students. However, Tencent personnel emphasize that Tencent's platform strategy is to build a living online platform to meet netizens' needs, not just a software company based on browser operations. What problems does Firefox face in China? Initially, when Google, Yahoo! and other foreign IT giants "set up camp" in China, they all encountered issues of cultural resistance, preventing them from increasing their market share. Firefox's journey in China faces similar problems.

           Although Firefox quickly increased its market share abroad due to its advantages, China's internet environment is very different from abroad. Although China has at least 130 million internet users, a large portion are still at the beginner level. The style of the Firefox browser is more suitable for advanced users, so Firefox has its own limitations in competing in China's internet. Moreover, Microsoft is deeply rooted in the minds of the Chinese people, having an allegedly unshakable status because Microsoft's IE browser comes pre-installed with the system, satisfying the internet needs of most beginner users in China. However, as people's knowledge of the internet grows, I believe it won't take long before people try different browsers to experience the internet. Personally, I've also tried switching from TT to Maxthon and then to Firefox.

           Regarding adapting to the Chinese market, Firefox itself has some situations unsuitable for the Chinese market. First, there are issues displaying Chinese web pages. Some fonts display normally in IE but appear strange in Firefox, affecting the aesthetics of Chinese web pages. Second, the extensive use of plugins can lead to compatibility issues between plugins themselves and between plugins and the browser. Third, code compatibility issues. Some web pages display correctly in IE but encounter many problems in Firefox. As everyone knows, no software is perfect, and no browser is an exception. Based on browser services, Firefox will develop according to the needs of Chinese users, better support Chinese language, and provide more comprehensive technical support and services to users in the Chinese region, making it more suitable for Chinese people to use. This is what Firefox needs to do in the future, and must do. At least, I personally think so.
