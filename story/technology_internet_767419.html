Windows Phone is still a child at age three

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:02:46

Recently, the head of Microsoft's Windows Phone department announced via Twitter: Windows Phone turns three!

Windows Phone has turned three. Before we know it, from its release on October 11, 2010, until now, three years have passed. In these three years, Android has rapidly become the leader in market share for smartphone operating systems. Although iOS cannot compare with Android in terms of market share, who can deny iOS's status in the global smartphone operating system landscape? Meanwhile, Windows Phone seems to still be like a child learning to walk, every step filled with uncertainty and slowness.

In the three years since its release, Windows Phone has undergone three major updates, from Windows Phone 7 (version number 7.0.7004) — Windows Phone 7.5 (version number 7.10.7720) — Windows Phone 7.8 (version number 7.10.8858) to Windows Phone 8 (version number 8.0.9900). Over the entire three-year period, Windows Phone has only upgraded one major version number. In contrast, iOS developed from iOS 4 in 2010 to the current iOS 7, and Android progressed from version 2.0 in 2010 to the current Android 4.4 version. While we cannot summarize the development speed of mobile operating systems purely through numbers, it can be inferred that, at least in terms of system update enthusiasm, Microsoft lags behind Apple and Google.

The lack of functionality is the primary issue Windows Phone needs to address. Take a simple example: the screen rotation switch, a very basic function for modern smartphones, is absent on Windows Phone. Users waited for three years, and even today, after three years, there is still no sign of it. The world of Microsoft is something you will never understand. Speaking of the ecosystem, when Windows Phone 7 was first released, Apple and Google had not yet perfected their respective systems or app stores. At that time, the release of Windows Phone 7 was not late. Windows Phone could have occupied an important position between iOS and Android. However, due to its inherent shortcomings, Windows Phone lost out right from the start. By the time iOS and Android were moving forward quickly or even running, Windows Phone was still progressing slowly. Both Apple and Google have already established a sufficiently complete ecosystem in the mobile operating system field, whether it's hardware or applications, from the system itself to the app store, tightly locking in users. With iOS and Android being more difficult to beat, how can Windows Phone snatch customers from the iOS and Android camps? Surprisingly, Eugene Gavrilov, the design integration director of the Windows Phone department, once stated in 2012 that actually Windows Phone 8 was already three years old, even older than Windows Phone 7. However, due to various factors and variables, things did not go as planned. So they initially launched a system that was relatively restricted in many aspects, hoping to rely more on the suggestions and support of Windows Phone 7 users to improve and develop Windows Phone 8. This is equivalent to admitting that Windows Phone 7 was just a Beta version released by Microsoft, and the large number of Windows Phone 7 users were Microsoft's guinea pigs. We can only sigh: the world of Microsoft is something you will never understand.

Among the initial partners standing firm with Microsoft in the Windows Phone lineup, Dell, Samsung, HTC, Acer, ZTE, and Huawei have either left or scattered. Recently, there was even news that HTC, due to poor financial conditions, plans to exit the Windows Phone camp. Microsoft offered to allow HTC to install both Android and Windows Phone dual systems on the phones they produce in order to retain HTC... And what about Nokia? Ironically, Nokia's phone division has been acquired by Microsoft for $7.2 billion. In fact, the only company truly persisting with Windows Phone is Microsoft itself. One can't help but ask: are the partners producing Windows Phone phones with Microsoft making a profit or suffering a loss?

For Windows Phone and Microsoft, we feel more frustration over their inability to succeed. The reason Windows Phone hasn't died like BlackBerry and Palm is because it has the backing of the Microsoft tree. We don't want to see iOS and Android dominate alone; we simply hope that Microsoft will stand up from now on!
