"Full of debts, being pursued like Yang Bailao, how can one focus on running a university?" On the morning of February 20th, Governor Jiang Daming, while participating in a joint discussion with the science and technology sector of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference (CPPCC), once again mentioned the issue of university debts. He expressed that to run a university well, the first step is to resolve the debt to a reasonable extent.

During the 2011 Two Sessions, the long-standing problem of "university debt resolution" became a hot topic. And this year at the Two Sessions, "university debt" has once again become a widely discussed subject. Many committee members believe that only by properly resolving the debt can universities move forward unburdened.

"Now it's not educators who are running schools, but real estate developers who are running universities," Governor Jiang Daming had asked during last year's CPPCC education group discussion on university debt: "Without a library, what kind of university are you running?" On February 20th, Jiang Daming reiterated the issues of university debt resolution and building world-class universities. "University debts significantly affect the development of universities," said Jiang Daming. "Now many university presidents are burdened with heavy debts, their minds preoccupied with nothing but debt, being chased around like Yang Bailao. How can they have the mental space to think about running a school?"

The province has proposed four measures to resolve university debts: increasing per-student funding, land swaps, incentive policies, and cost-cutting. In October 2011, the provincial finance department and the provincial education department reviewed and summarized the debt resolution plans of 78 public universities, locking in a total of 19.93 billion yuan in bank loans across all universities in the province as of the end of 2009. The provincial finance department and the provincial education department, representing the provincial government, signed target responsibility agreements with the first batch of 49 universities with bank loans. Currently, the debt resolution work of each university is progressing actively.

This year, the government work report once again delivered a "heavy punch" regarding university debt resolution: the per-student fiscal allocation for provincial undergraduate universities will be increased from 9,500 yuan to 12,000 yuan, achieving a goal of resolving 12 billion yuan worth of debts for public general universities within the year.

When discussing the use of land swaps to resolve the debt crisis, Jiang Daming said, "Now it's not educators running schools, but real estate developers running universities." He stated that there is currently a policy from the central government, and the first step should be to take advantage of this opportunity to repay the debt. "After resolving these 12 billion yuan, university debts will reach a reasonable level. Not having any debt is also unrealistic; as long as it stays within a reasonable range, it is still acceptable. Therefore, we must quickly pay off the debt and move forward unburdened. Otherwise, no matter how much you shout, no one will listen, even if you change the president, it will be the same."

Currently, the Shandong Provincial Government has issued a debt resolution reward and subsidy policy for higher education institutions, greatly motivating the various universities to resolve their debts.

Amending Debt Verification Methods to Support More Indebted Universities

Although the government's reward and subsidy policy is quite clear, some universities, due to not meeting the debt verification methods, find it difficult to receive support from government special funds.

"For instance, in the reward and subsidy policy, the debt verification is defined as 'obtained from financial institutions, verifiable through bank credit reports, and unpaid bank loans,' with 'the balance of bank loans as of the end of 2009 as the basis.' This regulation is clearly unfair to universities that do not meet the debt verification method," suggested CPPCC member Tian Youqin, director of the Computer Department at Shandong Labor Vocational Technology College. She recommended revising the debt verification method based on the actual situation of universities so that indebted universities can truly benefit from government support.

Some committee members also offered different interpretations of resolving the university debt crisis. "It depends on the purpose and direction of the university loans, which requires relevant departments to carefully verify and clarify," said CPPCC member Tan Jiwen, dean of the Mechanical Engineering College at Qingdao University of Technology. He pointed out that many universities' debts often result from expanding enrollment scales, borrowing money at all costs to acquire land, expand new campuses, purchase teaching instruments and equipment, and improve infrastructure.

"Although our province has been increasing per-student funding for universities year after year, considering the factor of inflation, the allocated funds are barely enough to maintain the operation of the school, leaving little ability to repay loans," said Tan Jiwen. He believed that if investigations prove that university loans were indeed used for expanding campuses, purchasing instruments, and other state-owned fixed assets, then the loan debts generated from these reasonable uses should be repaid by the government.

An undeniable fact is that government investment in education is increasing year by year. "Our province has proposed that educational investment account for 20% of the entire fiscal budget. The policy support is gradually increasing, and I hope that Shandong will have more and more world-class universities," Jiang Daming hoped.
