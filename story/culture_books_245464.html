*The Old Man and the Sea* by Hemingway

"The Old Man and the Sea," by Ernest Miller Hemingway (1899 - 1961)

"The Old Man and the Sea" is a representative work of American writer Ernest Hemingway, and also a symbolic novel. The protagonist Santiago is an old fisherman who, after overcoming numerous difficulties, catches "a marlin weighing more than fifteen hundred pounds," but the marlin is eaten up by sharks, leaving only its skeleton for Santiago to drag back.

Ernest Hemingway (1899-1961) was an American novelist and winner of the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1954, awarded for "The Old Man and the Sea." This novel was based on the real-life experiences of a Cuban fisherman. It records Santiago's entire fishing journey with cinematographic realism, portraying an image of an elderly man who remains elegant under pressure and is spiritually invincible. The book set an unprecedented record in publishing history by selling 5.3 million copies in 48 hours! When he was announced as the recipient of that year's Pulitzer Prize, critics universally praised his work. "A man can be destroyed but not defeated," a phrase from "The Old Man and the Sea," moved readers and impressed critics alike. In 1954, Hemingway won the Nobel Prize in Literature "for his mastery of the art of narrative, most recently demonstrated in 'The Old Man and the Sea,' and also for his influence on contemporary style." One of Hemingway's writing tenets was "to write each sentence and paragraph as concisely as possible," which led to more adaptations of his works into films than any other Nobel laureate.

The novel realistically depicts the graceful demeanor of the fishing elder Santiago under pressure, making him one of the most famous "tough guy" images in literary history. For "The Old Man and the Sea," translated into dozens of languages, Hemingway himself believed it was "the best work he could produce in his lifetime."

This book tells the story of an old Cuban fisherman named Santiago who went out to sea alone to fish. After 84 days without catching anything, he finally hooked an enormous marlin. This was the largest fish Santiago had ever seen or heard of, two feet longer than his boat. The fish was so strong that it dragged the small boat for two whole days and nights. During these two days and nights, the old man endured trials he had never experienced before, eventually killing the marlin and tying it to the front of his boat. However, he then encountered sharks and fought them to the death. In the end, the great marlin was eaten by the sharks, leaving only a bare skeleton and wounds all over his body when he returned home. Despite this, he received people's admiration.

On July 2, 1961, the world-renowned Hemingway ended his life with his own hunting rifle. The entire world was shocked, lamenting the tragedy of this giant. The American people mourned the passing of this important American writer.

Ernest Miller Hemingway (1899-1961), an American novelist, was born on July 21, 1899, in Oak Park, a suburb of Chicago, Illinois, USA, into a doctor's family. His father loved outdoor activities like hunting and fishing, while his mother enjoyed literature, both of which influenced Hemingway's later life and career significantly. After graduating from high school, Hemingway worked as an intern reporter for six months at the Kansas City Star, where he received excellent training in concise and clear news reporting.

After recovering, Hemingway became a correspondent for the Toronto Star in Paris. He was deeply interested in writing, working as both a journalist and novelist. His work was encouraged and guided by renowned novelists of the time. In nearly a decade, he published many works, among which "The Sun Also Rises" was the most famous. "The Sun Also Rises" was Hemingway's first important novel, depicting a group of American expatriates in France similar to Hemingway himself. They were lost after World War I, suffering immense physical and psychological damage from the war. They felt very empty, distressed, and depressed. They wanted to achieve something, but the war left them mentally confused, and they were repulsed by the deceitful society around them. They could only drift through life, leading Stein to call them "the Lost Generation." This novel was a true reflection of Hemingway's own life path and worldview. Hemingway and the literary movement he represented were thus called "the Lost Generation."

After the outbreak of World War I, Hemingway, eager to experience the battlefield, joined the American Red Cross battlefield service team and participated in the Italian front.

After the war, Hemingway was awarded the Italian Military Cross, silver medal, and medal of valor, gaining the rank of lieutenant. Accompanying these honors were 237 scars on his body (from shrapnel) and haunting war memories he couldn't shake off.

In 1929, Hemingway's novels "The Sun Also Rises" and "A Farewell to Arms" were the best works of the "Lost Generation" literature. The protagonist Henry is a young American who voluntarily joins the Italian battlefield. While injured, he falls in love with British nurse Catherine. Henry works hard, but during a retreat, he is mistakenly thought to be a German spy and almost gets shot. He escapes by jumping into the river and decides to leave the war. To avoid capture by military police, Henry and Catherine escape to neutral Switzerland. There, they enjoy a period of happy and peaceful life. Soon, however, Catherine dies during childbirth, and the baby suffocates to death. Henry is left alone in the world, devastated and tearless. Against the backdrop of war, the novel describes the love between Henry and Catherine, profoundly pointing out that their happiness and love were driven to the brink of destruction by the war.

In 1928, Hemingway left Paris and lived in Florida, USA, and Cuba, enjoying a tranquil rural life. He often hunted, fished, and watched bullfights. But soon, World War II broke out, preventing Hemingway from continuing his tranquil life. From 1937 to 1938, he worked as a war correspondent covering the Spanish Civil War. During World War II, he accompanied the army as a journalist and participated in the liberation of Paris.

After the outbreak of the Pacific War at the end of 1941, Hemingway immediately converted his yacht into a patrol boat to detect German submarines and provide intelligence for their elimination. In 1944, Hemingway followed the U.S. Army to Europe as a journalist, surviving a plane crash but still continued to report from behind enemy lines. After World War II, he was awarded a bronze medal.

In 1940, Hemingway published the anti-fascist novel "For Whom the Bell Tolls," set against the backdrop of the Spanish Civil War. The story describes the protagonist, young American Jordan, who volunteers to fight in the Spanish people's anti-fascist struggle. He is ordered to blow up a strategically significant bridge with the help of a mountain guerrilla unit at a specified time. Jordan blows up the bridge and, seriously wounded, fights the enemies alone, awaiting death. Jordan has a strong sense of justice and responsibility, feeling honored and proud to sacrifice for the anti-fascist struggle. This work is one of the strongest ideological pieces in Hemingway's mid-career, largely overcoming and escaping feelings of loneliness, confusion, and sorrow, integrating individuals into society, and showcasing a noble spirit of dedication to just causes.

In 1952, Hemingway published the novella "The Old Man and the Sea": Fisherman Santiago has gone 84 consecutive days without catching a fish. Initially, a boy named Manolin went out to sea with him, but after 40 days without success, the child was sent to another boat by his parents because they believed the old man brought bad luck. On the 85th day, the old man sailed far out early in the morning and unexpectedly caught a marlin larger than his boat. Santiago wrestled with the fish for two days and finally speared it. However, the wounded fish left a bloody trail in the sea, attracting countless sharks. The old man fought fiercely with the sharks, but when he returned to the harbor, only a massive skeleton of the marlin remained. Exhausted, Santiago collapsed on land. The boy came to see the old man, believing Santiago was not defeated. That afternoon, Santiago fell asleep in his shack, dreaming of lions. "A man is not made for defeat. You can destroy him, but you cannot beat him." This was Santiago's life belief and also the idea Hemingway intended to convey in "The Old Man and the Sea." Through Santiago's character, the author warmly praised the indomitable spiritual strength humans show in the face of hardship. The boy prepares to go out to sea again with the old man, learning all of Santiago's "skills," symbolizing that this "undefeatable" spirit will be passed down from generation to generation.

Santiago is a symbol of the perfect person Hemingway admired: strong, kind, compassionate, full of love. Even if he fails on the arena of life, facing irreversible fate, he remains a spiritual victor, a "tough guy." The "tough guy" is a recurring theme in Hemingway's works and a common character in them. Under immense external pressure and misfortune, they remain unbowed, moving forward bravely, even viewing death with equanimity. Although they may fail, they maintain human dignity and courage, showing the grace of victors.

Hemingway devoted himself to diligent creation throughout his life. The first thing he did upon waking up was to write. He had a habit uncommon to others: standing while writing, sometimes on one foot. He said, "I stand and write, and do so on one foot, putting myself in a state of tension, forcing me to express my thoughts as briefly as possible."

In the 1930s and 1940s, he created characters who shed their confusion and pessimism, fighting heroically for the people's interests and fearlessly sacrificing themselves in "The Fifth Column" and the novel "For Whom the Bell Tolls." In the 1950s, he created the "hard man" image epitomized by Santiago—"he can be destroyed but not defeated" (represented by "The Old Man and the Sea" in 1950). Hemingway stands as a monument to the spirit of the American nation.

In the 1920s, Hemingway's early literary works included "In Our Time," "Spring Tide," "Men Without Women," and the novels "The Sun Also Rises" and "A Farewell to Arms." This period coincided with the Western world's descent into the wasteland era described by Eliot behind the collapse of society. The novel "The Sun Also Rises" depicts the lives of a group of expatriates in Europe after the war and the profound changes in their spiritual worlds. The protagonist, Jack Barnes, is an American journalist whose sexual ability was destroyed by the war. He falls in love with a British nurse, Brett Ashley, who reciprocates his feelings, but they cannot be together.

An American writer, Robert Cohen—a man filled with vain and romantic fantasies—also falls in love with Brett, but she does not like him. This group of war-weary youths drifts across the European continent after the war, spending their days aimlessly drinking, arguing, or brawling. The war took away their loved ones, leaving them with physical and mental wounds. They intensely despise war, question justice and traditional values, and feel weary, lost, and disheartened about life. The novel condemns war from a unique perspective and has an anti-war tone. As it captures the disillusionment of a generation, it became a representative work of the "Lost Generation" literary movement.

"A Farewell to Arms" (also translated as "War Is Kind") is one of Hemingway's masterpieces. Opposing imperialist wars, it reveals the historical reasons for the emergence of the "Lost Generation," denouncing how war destroys people's ideals and happiness, harms their spirits, and results in the deaths of millions of innocents. This work showcases the basic characteristics of Hemingway's prose style and "modern narrative art." The plot is simple yet evokes pure emotion, the language is plain, sentences are short and concise, and the environmental descriptions blend scenes with emotions.

In the 1940s, based on his experiences and impressions in Africa, he wrote "Green Hills of Africa" and "The Snows of Kilimanjaro," and published "The Short Happy Life of Francis Macomber." In 1932, he published "Death in the Afternoon," following the maxim of American architect Ludwig that "the less, the more," making his works more concise, reducing the distance between the work and the reader, and proposing the "Iceberg Principle"—showing only one-eighth of what's there, making the work rich, implicit, and thought-provoking.

After World War II, Hemingway entered the late stage of his creative career, with "The Old Man and the Sea" as his representative work. Due to the novel's portrayal of "courage in a violent and death-filled reality," he won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1954. The award citation stated: "for his mastery of the art of narrative, most recently demonstrated in 'The Old Man and the Sea,' and also for his influence on contemporary style." This praise was well-deserved. Hemingway's lifelong creations have left a brilliant page in modern literary history. By exposing the hypocrisy of those in power and the cruelty of reality, he depicted the disillusionment of the American youth, filled his works with love for the working class, and explored new paths for artistic creation, giving realism new luster through open and inclusive innovation!

After receiving the award, Hemingway suffered from multiple diseases, causing him immense physical and mental pain, preventing him from creating influential works. This led to depression, forming a negative and pessimistic mood, and ultimately ending his life by suicide, perhaps a pursuit of the "tough guy spirit."

Regarding his writing, Hemingway had a famous metaphor: An iceberg typically has seven-eighths submerged underwater; the writer should depict the one-eighth visible above water, leaving the rest for the reader to think about and imagine.

Born on July 21, 1899, Hemingway was the second of six children in his family. His mother taught him to play the cello; his father taught him fishing and shooting. His childhood seemed untraumatized. He graduated from high school in 1917, a passionate, competitive standard American boy: good grades, well-rounded in sports (swimming, football, shooting, and secretly learning boxing at the local gym), participating in debates, playing the cello in the school band, editing the school newspaper "The Trapeze," and contributing to the literary magazine "Tabula," writing short stories (already showing signs of his mature style) and poetry. Sometimes he hitchhiked, traveling. Once, he hunted herons in a restricted area and hid afterward to avoid legal trouble. Some critics believe Hemingway's travels away from home indicate a normal childhood; others see it as a symbol of his early rebellion against Oak Park's lifestyle, reflecting tense relationships within his family.
