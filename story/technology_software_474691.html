Agave (Agave americana), also known as Century Plant or American Aloe, belongs to the Agave family and genus. It is a perennial evergreen large herbaceous plant native to Mexico. Due to its sturdy leaves and year-round greenery, it is one of the main materials used in southern garden landscaping. In regions along the Yangtze River and northward, it is commonly potted indoors. It prefers well-drained, fertile sandy soil. In its natural habitat, it usually takes several decades to bloom, after which the mother plant dies. Cross-pollination is required for robust growth.

**Plant Morphology**

Agave americana is a perennial evergreen plant from the Agave family. The plant is tall with gray-green or bluish-gray leaves that can reach up to 1.7 meters in length and 20 cm in width, arranged in a rosette pattern at the base. The leaf edges have spines that start brown and turn gray-white, with terminal spines reaching up to 3 cm long. The flower stalk emerges from the core of the rosette, bearing yellow-green flowers. It thrives in warm environments with sufficient sunlight, growing optimally between 15°C to 25°C. It is highly drought-resistant and requires loose, well-draining soil.

In its native habitat, it typically takes ten to several decades to bloom, producing massive inflorescences up to 7-8 meters tall, one of the longest in the world. After blooming, the parent plant dies. Agave is often referred to as the "Century Plant."

**Toxicity Warning**

The species is listed in the Chinese Plant Spectra Database as toxic. Its leaf sap contains toxins that can irritate the skin, causing burning sensations. Oral administration of 100ml of leaf sap daily to rabbits resulted in symptoms like loss of appetite, reduced activity, and limb numbness within three days, potentially leading to death if untreated. Autopsies revealed gastric mucosal congestion and hepatic ischemia. Sheep may exhibit symptoms such as otitis media, cyanosis, respiratory difficulty, and increased heart rate after ingestion. The plant can also be used to poison fish.

**Chemical Composition**

The leaves of Agave contain saponins, which upon hydrolysis yield hecodgenoin and 9-dehydrohecodgenoin. The original species' leaves contain various steroidal saponins, including hecodgenoin, 9-dehydrohecodgenoin, helogenin, mannojirimycin, tigogenin, diosgenin, logogenin, and 12-epi-logogenin. Seeds contain new tigogenin, hecodgenoin, and camogenin. Flowers contain helogenin (0.5%), kaempferol-3-glucoside (isoflavone glycoside), and kaempferol-3-rutinoside (rutin).

**Cultivation**

Agave prefers full sun and dry conditions. It tolerates cold moderately, grows well in shade, and is extremely drought-resistant. It requires well-drained, fertile sandy soil. Winter temperatures should not fall below 5°C. The rope-like fibers contract violently when wet, making them useful as ropes.

**Propagation Methods**

Common methods include division and seed propagation. Division is performed in early spring during repotting, separating offsets from the mother plant for independent planting. Seeds require cross-pollination to produce fruit, and seeds are sown in April-May, germinating within two weeks. Seedlings grow slowly but mature plants grow rapidly, with flowering occurring in plants over 10 years old.

**Cultivation Management**

- **Sunlight**: Agave thrives in full sun. Insufficient light can lead to poor growth.

- **Temperature**: Hardy and adaptable, it can tolerate harsh conditions. Optimal growth occurs at temperatures above 7°C. Indoor cultivation is recommended during cold winters.

- **Watering**: Requires adequate water during the growing season but minimal watering during winter dormancy to avoid root rot.

- **Fertilization**: Benefits from annual fertilization. Over-fertilization can cause damage.

- **Potting Soil**: Prefers sandy, well-draining soil. Repotting is unnecessary due to slow growth.

**Uses**

- **Environmental Beautification**: With its sturdy, evergreen leaves, Agave is widely used for ornamental purposes in gardens and indoor settings.

- **Air Purification**: Can remove up to 70% of benzene, 50% of formaldehyde, and 24% of trichloroethylene in a 10 square meter room.

- **Tequila Production**: The heart of the Agave plant is used to produce tequila, a popular Mexican alcoholic beverage.

**Differences Between Agave and Aloe Vera**

While both are succulent perennials with short stems, white powder on leaves, and spines, they differ significantly:

- **Family and Genus**: Agave belongs to the Agavaceae family and Agave genus, while Aloe belongs to the Liliaceae family and Aloe genus.

- **Leaves**: Agave leaves contain fibrous veins and do not exude sap when broken, whereas Aloe leaves release a clear, sticky sap containing yellow pigments.

- **Growth Pattern**: Agave leaves grow in a cone shape, layer by layer, while Aloe leaves grow independently from the center.

- **Spines**: Agave has hard, hooked spines along the edges and a dark-brown spine at the tip, while Aloe's spines are softer and grow outward, becoming smaller toward the tip.

- **Flowers and Fruits**: Agave produces conical inflorescences with pale yellow-green flowers and ovoid or spherical fruits, while Aloe has racemose inflorescences with densely packed orange-yellow flowers and triangular fruits.

Agave is toxic and should not be consumed. While some Aloe species, such as Woodfordia fruticosa and Nongda Leaf Aloe, are edible, most are ornamental and some are toxic, posing health risks if ingested.
