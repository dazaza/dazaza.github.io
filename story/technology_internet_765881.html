Why has Microsoft been on the back foot for the past decade?

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:38:09

Out of jealousy or not, Paul Allen, the co-founder of Microsoft, hastily concluded that Ballmer was not fit to be the head of Microsoft.

Microsoft is probably the company most closely related to people's daily lives in the world, but it has been tormented over the past 10 years by governments, competitors, and even internally, missing great opportunities to enter fields such as internet search, smartphones, social networks, and digital media sales.

In "I Changed the World with Microsoft: Allen Memoirs," published at the beginning of 2012, out of jealousy or not, Paul Allen, the co-founder of Microsoft, hastily concluded that Ballmer was not fit to be the head of Microsoft. Then, he believed that Microsoft's difficulties were related to his absence. Allen wrote: "Bill would also miss my ability to predict the direction of technological trends and my ability to meet these trends with excellent and original ideas."

After reading this book, one can roughly summarize the three reasons why Microsoft has repeatedly been in a passive position over the past 10 years.

Reason 1: Poor Management

Seventeen years ago, Bill Gates said in an interview with a journalist: "The technology industry is very frightening. Once you fall behind in technology, no matter how much glory you have achieved, it cannot guarantee future leading advantages." Unbeknownst to him, a few years later, Microsoft found itself in a predicament where it could not maintain its leading advantage, lasting for 10 years without breaking free from the quagmire.

Apple has now become the supplier of "cool" devices; Google uses its network-based "cloud computing" to surpass Microsoft in the search field; Facebook is undoubtedly the leader in the social networking field... How did a former technical flag-bearer end up like this?

Microsoft is plagued by institutional bloat. In the eyes of the co-founder Allen, today's Microsoft's momentum continues to weaken. When Ballmer joined, he was the 24th employee, which was in the 6th year of the company's establishment. When Allen left in 1983, there were 500 people at Microsoft. By around 2000, when the monopoly lawsuit was nearing its conclusion, there were about 40,000 people, and currently, there are more than 90,000. To avoid mediocrity among employees, it is necessary to ruthlessly remove those who perform poorly, but Microsoft clearly hasn't done enough in this regard. A senior executive once told Allen that they hoped to lay off one-quarter of the workforce.

In 2009 and 2010, Allen had lunch with more than a dozen employees who had recently left Microsoft. They all talked about the same thing: too many managers lack real talent, and there are too many power struggles and political infighting in the main product lines.

Windows Vista was a warning bell. This operating system launched in 2007 has now become a joke among experts and a target of mockery in Apple's advertising campaigns. How did Microsoft let its flagship product end up in such a state? Kai-Fu Lee once said: "Microsoft employees have higher IQs than anyone else, but our efficiency is worse than anyone else's. In other companies, employees' IQs add up, but ours actually subtract."

Reason 2: Obsolete Patterns Not Suitable for the Times

Leaders like to stick to established successful patterns. For Gates, the most feasible pattern was super-aggressiveness combined with super-competitiveness. The antitrust case sounded a warning bell for Microsoft, and the fleeting market environment made Microsoft taste the bitterness of outdated success patterns.

Allen recalled that in these markets and many others, Microsoft grew strong as a quick "follower," following this pattern from MS-DOS to Windows to Word and Excel. However, the world changed again. As content shifted to the web, the pace of innovation accelerated. Compared to disk-based software of old, the follower strategy became increasingly difficult to implement.

Over time, Microsoft's operation model dominated by enterprise customers began to stiffen, and what was once swift following became slow and sluggish. Five years after Apple launched the first iPod, Microsoft released Zune. Microsoft's first attempt to compete with Google Search, "Bing," wasn't introduced until 2009. Fourteen months later, Microsoft's MSN, WindowsLive, and Bing search only held a 14% share of the domestic search engine market, ranking second but far behind Google's 65%. Since the second half of 2010, Microsoft has fallen to fourth place in the high-end smartphone market, trailing Apple, BlackBerry, and Google.

Kai-Fu Lee also briefly pointed out this disadvantage of Microsoft in "World Because of You." Lee recounted: All employees believe that Microsoft's most magical moments always occur when it learns, catches up, and crushes NO.1 as NO.2. However, once a product team loses its imagined enemy, it becomes lax, and Gates and Ballmer withdraw their investment and support. For example, after Internet Explorer defeated Netscape, Microsoft reduced its investment, causing its browser to stagnate for many years until the appearance of Firefox, this "enemy," spurred it back into action.

Reason 3: Antitrust Case Dealt a Heavy Blow

In June 2000, the federal appeals court overturned the ruling to split Microsoft, ultimately fining Microsoft relatively mildly. However, this case dealt a severe blow to Microsoft. Allen believed that the antitrust case consumed too much of Gates' time and energy.

Kai-Fu Lee, former vice president of Microsoft, also mentioned in his autobiography "World Because of You" that before 2000, Microsoft was like a self-centered, proud, and mischievous child. It was competitive everywhere, and patience and humility were not in its dictionary, but the antitrust case completely changed Microsoft.

"In a centralized decision-making technology company, the negative impact of a CEO embroiled in lawsuits and distracted is highly damaging." Allen commented.

Additionally, serving enterprise clients while being distant from end consumers is one of Microsoft's chronic problems. Allen said: "Although Microsoft has never stopped trying in the past 30 years, designing products that make consumers' hearts race has never been the company's strength." Allen cited The New York Times' "pipeline equipment" metaphor, saying that once you become the largest "pipeline equipment" wholesaler, the spirit of innovation disappears from the company's business. "You may want to innovate, but that's as hard as fighting gravity."

Arrogance and complacency are both Gates' flaws and Microsoft's long-standing issues. Before Google became the giant it is today, Microsoft had the opportunity to acquire Google, but Gates didn't take the then-insignificant small competitor seriously, always saying: "In six months, we can beat it!" Complacency eventually led to bitter consequences.
