The soaring oil prices are bearish for the dollar.

by ikonfx123456 on 2012-02-28 14:23:08

The US government and think tank experts generally believe that the main factors leading to this round of oil price increases are the continued tension in the Middle East and the rapid growth of energy demand in emerging economies. Affected by multiple factors, international oil prices have recently been on the rise. Some analysts believe that the continuous increase in oil prices is detrimental to the US economy and increases the risk of the Federal Reserve (FED) implementing a third round of quantitative easing measures (QE3), which would be bearish for the dollar.

Marc Chandler, Chief Currency Strategist at Brown Brothers Harriman, pointed out that the recent rise in oil prices is expected to have a negative impact on the dollar.

Chandler believes there are many factors contributing to the recent rise in oil prices. For example, due to political instability, Sudan, Syria, and Yemen have seen a sharp drop in oil supply; meanwhile, Libya and Iran's oil exports have already decreased; Japan is increasing its oil purchases to replace nuclear energy; additionally, the unusually cold weather in Europe has also stimulated oil demand.

Chandler stated that these factors all pose a bearish outlook for the dollar, as rising oil prices may prompt a policy response from Federal Reserve Chairman Ben Bernanke.

Chandler remarked that with the continuous rise in recent crude oil prices, the risk of the Federal Reserve implementing a new round of quantitative easing measures seems to be increasing, which will put downward pressure on interest rates and similarly affect the dollar. The rise in oil prices might lead to the emergence of the "Bernanke Put."

The term "Bernanke Put" evolved from the "Greenspan Put," a market slang originally referring to actions taken by former Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan aimed at preventing significant and sustained market declines.

Another analyst pointed out that Federal Reserve Chairman Ben Bernanke will testify before Congress on Wednesday (February 29) and Thursday (March 1), where he may face numerous questions about the rise in oil prices, and investors will closely monitor the situation.

NYMEX crude oil futures have been continuously rising recently and once approached $110 per barrel, while Brent crude oil futures rose to around $126 per barrel. As of last Friday (February 24), NYMEX crude oil futures had risen for seven consecutive trading days. Despite a decline on Monday (February 27) due to profit-taking by investors, analysts generally expect oil prices to remain strong in the short term.

Since the beginning of 2012, the United States, together with the EU and other allied countries, has continuously intensified economic sanctions on Iran, attempting to prevent its development of nuclear weapons by reducing Iran's oil exports. In response, Iran has threatened to cut off oil supplies to the West. Meanwhile, rumors about Israel's plan to militarily strike Iran's nuclear facilities have drawn high attention and concern from the international community and the oil market.

On the other hand, the White House of the United States indicated that the continuous growth in energy demand from emerging major powers such as China and India is a long-term factor driving up international oil prices.

The tense situation in Iran continues to push up international oil prices, and US gasoline prices have also risen accordingly. According to the latest data released by the American Automobile Association on Sunday (February 26), the national average gasoline price in the US reached $3.688 per gallon, an increase of nearly $0.30 compared to a month ago.

Some experts predict that due to sanctions on Iran and the harsh winter in Europe, the gasoline price in the US could rise to $4.5 per gallon in the summer of 2012. This would break the historical record of $4.11 per gallon set on July 14, 2008.

It is well known that the US is a "nation on wheels." Statistics show that gasoline consumption accounts for about 8% of household consumption in the US. Analysts indicated that given the special importance of gasoline in American society, the rise in oil prices will hinder the fragile economic recovery in the US. It is estimated that every $20 increase in the price of a barrel of crude oil would reduce the US GDP growth rate by 0.4%.

As of 10:47 Beijing Time, the dollar index was reported at 78.43.
