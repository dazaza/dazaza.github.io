Common causes of insomnia in elderly people at home. Insomnia has a serious psychological impact on people for a long time, increasing their mental burden. Treating insomnia requires dedication; we cannot help but give more attention to insomnia. What are the reasons for insomnia? As people age, the incidence of insomnia increases, and insomnia becomes a problem that troubles the lives of the elderly. Sleep disorders in the elderly mainly manifest as prolonged sleep onset time, unstable sleep, easy awakening, increased number of awakenings, making sleep segmented, and reducing deep sleep time. The main causes of insomnia in the elderly can be summarized as follows:

(1) Physiological factors: The older the age, the less sleep is needed, which is well-known. Nerve cells decrease with age, and sleep is an activity of the brain. Due to the reduction of nerve cells in the elderly, sleep disorders naturally occur, and insomnia is the most common symptom.

(2) Organic brain diseases: With increasing age, the degree of cerebral arteriosclerosis in the elderly gradually worsens, or they may have hypertension, cerebral hemorrhage, cerebral infarction, dementia, Parkinson's disease, etc. These diseases can all reduce cerebral blood flow, causing metabolic disorders in the brain and producing symptoms of insomnia.

(3) Systemic diseases: Entering old age, the incidence of systemic diseases increases. Many elderly people suffer from cardiovascular diseases, respiratory system diseases, and other degenerative diseases such as cervical spondylosis, rheumatoid arthritis, numbness in the limbs, etc. These diseases can affect sleep due to the disease itself or accompanying symptoms, aggravating insomnia in the elderly.

(4) Mental illness: Relevant data statistics show that the proportion of depression and depressive tendencies in the elderly is significantly higher than in young people. Depression often has symptoms such as insomnia, constipation, palpitations, etc., and its sleep disorder mainly manifests as early waking and reduced deep sleep. As the patient's age increases, sleep disorders in the second half of the night become increasingly severe, with complaints mainly being early waking and difficulty returning to sleep after waking.

(5) Psychosocial factors: Various psychosocial factors can cause the elderly to think, feel uneasy, nostalgic, sad, troubled, anxious, and painful, all of which can lead to insomnia. The main characteristic is difficulty falling asleep, with thoughts lingering in the mind, making it hard to fall asleep even after lying in bed for a long time, tossing and turning. Or just after falling asleep, they are awakened by surrounding sounds or nightmares, making it difficult to fall back asleep.

(6) Environmental factors: This is also a reason for the elderly having difficulty falling asleep and experiencing restless sleep. For example, living near a busy street, noisy neighbors, or a chaotic environment can make it difficult for the elderly to fall asleep. A messy environment can easily wake up elderly people who sleep lightly, preventing them from falling back asleep.

(7) Medication factors: Taking drugs that stimulate the nervous system before bedtime, such as isoniazid for tuberculosis treatment, ephedrine and theophylline for asthma treatment, can easily produce excitement and make it difficult to fall asleep. Additionally, levodopa and phenytoin sodium can cause insomnia in the elderly. Levodopa can cause insomnia and also nightmares, disrupting sleep. Taking diuretics at night increases nighttime urination, making it difficult to fall back asleep.

(8) Fear of sleeping: Some elderly people have a fear of sleep, worrying about not waking up once they fall asleep. Once they encounter insomnia, they become very tense. Tension affects sleep, and sometimes even if they fall asleep, they experience continuous nightmares, forming a vicious cycle. Conversely, some elderly people believe they sleep too little. With nothing to do in the evening and feeling bored, they go to bed early as soon as it gets dark. By 3-4 o'clock in the morning, they have already slept for 7-8 hours. After waking up, they think about various things, making it difficult for them to fall back asleep.

(9) Excessive daytime sleep: Elderly people don't have much to do during the day, so excessive napping during the day is also one of the reasons affecting nighttime sleep in the elderly. Elderly people generally nap a lot, and in quiet environments with nothing to do, daytime naps can increase easily. Appropriately controlling daytime sleep can significantly improve nighttime sleep quality.

(10) Increased nighttime urination: Increased nighttime urination is a common phenomenon among the elderly. In addition to diuretics increasing nighttime urination, dysfunction of the detrusor muscle and prostate enlargement in the elderly, along with increased residual urine in the bladder, can also lead to increased nighttime urination, thus disrupting sleep.

Experts remind that there are many more symptoms of insomnia in the elderly. According to data, the proportion of insomnia is still rising.
