Wubi word root: a method that beginners of Wubi should master

by mennei on 2009-11-25 01:35:28

If you are a beginner learning Wubi input method, or if you have already basically mastered its usage but cannot increase your speed to a satisfactory level, then please carefully read this post. This is my own experience from learning Wubi over the past ten years, using it, and teaching it. I believe that it will greatly help with your Wubi typing skills and boost your confidence.

**Confidence Section**

Let's start by boosting your confidence. Any kind of learning needs to be targeted, meaning you need to first know what you're going to use, and then learn accordingly based on your needs. To become a qualified or even excellent Wubi user and proficiently use Wubi, how many characters should you focus on practicing? What characters should you practice?

Any language with a long history and cultural tradition has a large vocabulary. English and Russian both have millions of words. However, Shakespeare and Pushkin only used more than 10,000. I don't know how many Chinese characters there are in total, but it is said that there are one million in total. GB2312-80 includes a total of 6763 characters, divided into two levels: Level One includes 3755 commonly used characters, and Level Two includes 3008 less commonly used characters, as well as Wubi radicals. The usage frequency of Level One characters reaches a combined total of 99.7%, which means that in an article of ten thousand characters, 9970 are commonly used Level One characters. Among these, 2475 characters have a usage rate of 99%, 1375 characters have a usage rate of 95%, 950 characters have a usage rate of 90%, 560 characters have a usage rate of 80%, 365 characters have a usage rate of 70%, 244 characters have a usage rate of 60%, 163 characters have a usage rate of 50%, 104 characters have a usage rate of 40%, 59 characters have a usage rate of 30%, 28 characters have a usage rate of 30%, and 8 characters have a usage rate of 10%.

From the above introduction and statistical data, we can see that in order to quickly master and achieve a high typing speed, mastering and using the most common 1375 characters with the highest frequency is the goal. Because these characters appear at a frequency of 95% in general articles. In actual operation, if 95% of the characters can be typed smoothly, then you will have a fairly fast speed and accuracy. Typing eighty to one hundred characters per minute? That can only be considered barely passing. Among the last batch of students I taught, one person had a normal workday typing speed between 130 to 180 characters per minute. And during tests, they broke through two hundred characters per minute several times. Of course, this requires extraordinary practice, as well as memorizing a large number of four-character or longer word entries. More importantly, a certain level of talent is required. However, typing one hundred to one hundred twenty characters per minute is just a very normal speed, a level that ordinary people can reach.

How about it? Are you confident now? 1375 characters really isn't that many.

**Misconceptions Section**

The worst thing when learning a skill is falling into a misconception. Not only does it not help with learning, but worse still, it severely damages a person's interest and confidence in learning, eventually leading to giving up.

Of course, learning Wubi also has its misconceptions. First and foremost is the learning of radicals. That is, do you need to memorize the radicals, and how should you memorize them?

I just counted the radical chart, and the 86 series Wubi actually has two hundred radicals (I didn’t count it twice, so there might be one or two discrepancies). I want to clarify that you must distinguish between radicals and the radical chart concept. When Mr. Wang Yongmin invented Wubi, he provided a mnemonic rhyme for learners to memorize radicals. Actually, this was counterproductive for beginners. The purpose of that rhyme is to let you know what radicals are included on a key. But everyone who uses Wubi knows that the thought process of using Wubi is: character - radical - keystroke. What we practice is knowing which key a radical is on when we see it, rather than knowing what radicals are on a particular key. Therefore, what I want to tell everyone is, don’t bother memorizing the radical chart and that ridiculous rhyme. Instead, reverse the process, memorize the key positions through the radicals, and combine this step with actual practice. Let your brain memory gradually shift to muscle memory in your fingers, rather than sitting in a study room with a notebook trying to force yourself to memorize. As for how to practice, I will mention it below.

The second misconception is about key names and the twenty-six English letters. I’ve encountered many people who already have a good foundation in typing English letters, but this actually doesn’t help much with initially learning Wubi, and may even cause some trouble. Because they can never forget the positions of the English letters, and when hitting the radical keys, they always use English letters as a medium. That is, their first reaction upon seeing a radical is not which key to hit, but which English letter the radical belongs to, and then they make the keystroke reaction. This slows down the speed and increases mental labor. Therefore, in the early stages of learning Wubi, you should completely separate the English letter keys from the Wubi key names. Moreover, the twenty-five key positions of Wubi themselves have their own names... gold person moon white grain speech stand water fire... etc., all of these are. And they themselves are also Wubi radicals.
