As long as you are happy, does anything else matter?

by coffeelovetea2 on 2012-02-27 15:43:48

You and I attended the same high school, were from the same hometown, and lived very close to each other. In the mid-1980s, our hometown was still very poor and the transportation conditions were also poor. The school was more than forty miles away, and we had to walk back and forth. At seventeen or eighteen years old, our strength had not yet fully developed. Every time we went to school, we had to bring a week's or even two weeks' worth of food, spare clothes, books, homemade pickled vegetables, etc., carrying large and small packages by hand or on our shoulders, which was very inconvenient. You deeply understood the hardship. After you graduated, you often sent me to school. I remember one time, after a heavy rain, the roads were muddy, you carried the grain bag on your back and insisted on sending me. We found a small road that extended along the foot of the mountain, waded through rivers, passed through villages, and you insisted on sending me to a place only three or four miles away from the school [http://www.zhongnanpaiju.com]. Then, you returned home, covered in mud and water.

Later, you farmed at home while I continued my studies outside. Due to the lack of labor force at home, you often helped my family with work. My parents always spoke of you with gratitude. We often exchanged letters, and the topic we discussed the most was literature. You loved reading and writing poetry, subscribed to a lot of literary books, and even enrolled in a correspondence course for literature. This was perhaps a common characteristic of educated young people in rural areas during that era. Sometimes, you also confided in me about your own troubles, feeling out of place and having no common language with others at home. I understood your thoughts and didn't fail to counsel you. During holidays when I returned home, we often spent time together, talking about heaven and earth [http://www.zhongnanpaiju.com], and it was very enjoyable. One summer vacation, in your melon garden, we picked and chose carelessly, leaving the melon field in a mess, and you wished I could eat all the melons in the field at once. We depended on and supported each other spiritually. Regarding my marriage, you firmly supported my choice despite strong opposition, encouraging me to achieve a happy union with the person I loved. You must understand, due to outdated ideas, many people did not understand my actions, and I was under immense pressure. Wanting to love who I loved but fearing to hurt others and disappoint their expectations, your support made me stronger, allowing me to resolutely stick to my choice. Now, many people know about my happy marriage and family life, but how many know how difficult it was for me back then? But you do!

Later, after graduating from a teacher training institute, I taught at a high school and served as the principal. You also taught at the village elementary school. At that time, I was obsessed with work, young and ambitious, always wanting to accomplish something to be worthy of the trust placed in me by my leaders and colleagues. Therefore, I rarely went home. You often visited my house, chatting with my parents to reduce their loneliness. At the time, I was full of confidence and couldn't appreciate your state of mind and situation. Sometimes, I thought you were too dispirited and self-centered, secretly worried, but didn't know what to say. As a result, we talked less and less. I remember one time when I visited your house, after a random chat, I felt there was already a barrier in our thoughts and words, so I got up to leave. When you saw me out of the village, we happened to meet a young man from your village. He invited me to drink alcohol, so I drank; after drinking at his house, I continued drinking at another person's house in your village. In my opinion, these people were good friends and enjoyed lively gatherings, being from our hometown, even if they lacked knowledge, meeting them and having some fun wasn't wrong. A person shouldn't be too serious, living isn't easy for anyone, and everyone needs encouragement and recognition from others, sometimes requiring acting a part. But later, I realized that these behaviors might change your view of me, or even hurt your self-respect.

Later, I increasingly felt that communication between us was difficult, and I couldn't explain it, fearing that saying too much or too little would hurt you, and we occasionally met with just a few hurried words. Once, you came to my school, and someone introduced you to a girlfriend, asking for my opinion. I knew this girl quite well. Although she was excellent in some aspects, she was somewhat frivolous and vain, unsuitable for you. Therefore, I strongly opposed it. But I could see that you were very eager for this marriage and liked this girl. I advised you not to stay at home, to go out, and even said some rude words. Later, I learned that I once again hurt your self-respect. Soon, you joined the army, and I didn't know your exact departure time, so I didn't come to see you off. In those few years, I didn't follow your example, nor did I think it necessary to visit your parents frequently to comfort them. Even when I met your elder brother studying in school, I showed little human warmth. Always feeling unable to give genuine help, however warm-hearted I pretended to be, it would be futile. It seemed that once, when you returned from the army on leave, we met. You felt uncertain about your future, and I could only offer comfort and advice. Fate is hard to control, and it's better to go with the flow. Even if you can't really get into military school, when you return, if it doesn't work out, you can come here, we brothers will be reunited, things won't always be like this, life will get better. In fact, these were heartfelt words from me, and all I could do was this. But why did we lose touch later? You must know that I actually cared a lot about any news about you.

The last time we met was after you became an officer in the army. Hearing that you had returned, I went to your house to see you, only to learn about your mother's passing and the formation of your own family. You talked about the people who saw you off when you joined the army, those who handled your mother's funeral, and also about your own future, appearing for the first time full of ambition. I was very happy, but also felt guilty, and faintly realized that my rudeness once again hurt you, and also sensed your innocence about worldly affairs. Seeing how much you had changed, and having experienced some of life's difficulties myself, I knew we probably wouldn't be able to reunite again.
