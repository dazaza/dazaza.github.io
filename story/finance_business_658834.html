The western-style education and training of students is not about producing graduates who only excel in western-style academics. Out of a total of 13 students taking the global unified college entrance examination, at least three achieved excellent grades with a minimum of three A’s. Eighty percent of these students pursued admission to Rukangnaer University, one of the top 100 universities globally. Additionally, among 23 sophomores taking the first global unified college entrance examination, many scored at least three or four A's, achieving outstanding results that matched the standards for admission into U.S. Ivy League schools like Harvard, Yale, Oxford in England, and Cambridge — all world-class super universities.

What kind of culture allows students from outside Britain to have such a high acceptance rate at elite schools? Teacher Jiang, who has been involved in admissions from outside Britain for over a decade, explained that 80% of graduates from outside Britain are admitted to some of the world’s top 100 universities, gaining recognition both inside and outside the province from parents and students alike. The school focuses on individual training based on student interests. In the past two years, while maintaining its strong application advantages for top elites, the school continues to attract a large number of overseas high-quality full-time foreign teachers, creating a wide range of professional courses for students.

Besides English, mathematics, physics, and chemistry, the curriculum outside Britain in A-LEVEL courses also includes economics, business, accounting, world history, applications, and humanities. Biology and ICT computer skills courses are also offered. If you were to interview a Western teacher outside Britain, you’d find that their understanding of Western courses is structured and theoretical. The hard impact of the first line of the world’s elite students is the most direct and realistic idea.

The reporter also interviewed several graduates and students reading at schools outside Britain to explore how students aspire to reach elite status. Li Yuan, an introverted and not very talkative student at first glance, initiated and organized numerous inter-school activities within her three years at school. Her materials for prestigious university applications were sincere, accurate, and detailed, highlighting bits and pieces of her volunteer work and personal views, making her resume stand out and better impressing admissions officers. It seems that Li Yuan's path to Cornell, one of the world’s first Ivy League schools, really makes sense.

Cao Yang, known by his peers as a student with fully upgraded learning ability and knowledge comprehension, stands out. Not only that, the reporter found that he shares a common trait with science students—his expression lacks flamboyance but is strictly logical. The small class counseling and large class lectures in A-LEVEL courses, combined with a relatively looser curriculum compared to Chinese high schools, allow students to take full advantage of after-school time for independent learning, exercising self-learning ability and time management skills. This leads to a comprehensive and in-depth upgrade in core subject knowledge, paving the way for dreams at world-class elite schools through A-LEVEL courses.

As a sophomore taking the world’s top exams for the first time outside Britain, Cao Yang achieved remarkable results: scoring 100 in three subjects and 99 in one, a rare feat. Despite leading from the start, the pressure was clearly not small. On one hand, adhering to traditional exam-oriented educational viewpoints, he had to cope with the heavy academic burden of preparing for college entrance exams; on the other hand, he had to maintain his leading position to ensure it wouldn't be surpassed. Zhao Beibei, however, showed no concern for any of these pressures, providing a dose of calm reassurance.
