The portal revolution: Sina may become the loser

by anonymous on 2013-08-06 18:46:40

Although Zhang Lin was not on the long list of layoffs at Sina, she still felt low in spirits. She received a group email from her editor-in-chief, which stated that most of the reimbursable expenses for business trips in the past would no longer be reimbursed.

In April, she did not receive her due allowance as scheduled. That month, Sina made a comprehensive adjustment to the portal model that had been followed for 15 years. For users, this only manifested as more Weibo traces on the homepage of the portal, but for Zhang Lin and her colleagues, the biggest change was that sub-channels such as travel, sports, and finance were transformed into business units. This meant that these channels, which were originally only responsible for producing content, began to bear their own profits and losses.

Sina classified the channels into three categories: the first category was commercial channels, such as travel and automobiles; the second category was comprehensive channels, such as technology and finance; the third category was media channels, such as news centers. The first and second categories were responsible for sales tasks, while the third category was not.

"It has quite a big impact on me personally. The budget just disappeared," Zhang Lin said.

The employee evaluation method also changed, with fixed salaries turning into KPI evaluations. For example, Zhang Lin's monthly salary used to be a fixed 9,000 yuan, but now her basic salary is 1,000 yuan, with 8,000 yuan being performance-based pay. "Journalists and editors have traffic evaluations, while editors-in-chief and directors carry advertising tasks," Zhang Lin said.

At the same time, Sina started its largest-scale layoff in recent years, with the laid-off employees accounting for 10% to 15% of the total number of portal staff, mainly involving video, reading, and other channels.

Sina was not an isolated case. In the past two years, all four major portals have undergone strategic adjustments. They increasingly appear on mobile phones, are more interactive, speak with more attitude, and are more like distinct personalities. If people from the peak period of portals in 2008 were to travel to today, they would definitely be shocked by the current image of portals.

Weibo, WeChat, news apps, and other mobile internet products with social elements have completely changed the unidirectional mode of information dissemination. The old portals' mission of "meeting all the public's information needs" has been forcibly ended.

From the Q1 2013 financial reports of the five major portals—Tencent, Sina, Sohu, NetEase, and Phoenix News—it can be seen that although they all maintained year-on-year growth, their quarter-over-quarter data were declining. Tencent, ranked first, even showed a decline for three consecutive quarters.

"A few years ago, we still considered ourselves new media, but now we've become old media." A high-level portal executive revealed that last year, the main portals failed to complete their advertising targets, and each portal faced the challenge of self-transformation.

Left and Right Boxing

Ironically, Sina, which fired the first shot in the portal revolution with Weibo, was the last to make active changes.

On the revamped Sina homepage, the most prominent position is "Weibo Search." Its mission is to better connect Weibo with the portal, allowing Weibo users to flow back to the portal. This is the core of Sina's revamp—to make the portal more personalized, localized, socialized, and mobile-friendly. The actual effect of this strategy is yet to be seen. However, many inside Sina attribute the rapid decline of the portal to Weibo's drag.

Once upon a time, "Weibo illuminates Sina" was a truth believed by many. This product, hailed as a "star" by Sina, carried the hopes of Sina in the mobile internet era.

Sina CEO Charles Chao once publicly stated: "Inevitably, there will be overlaps between Sina's portal, blogs, and Weibo. New modes will impact existing ones, but if we believe a product represents the future trend, even if it means revolutionizing ourselves, we must develop in that field."

For a time, all of Sina's resources began tilting towards Weibo. Privately, Charles Chao once told former General Manager of the Weibo Business Unit Peng Shaobin: "Anyone you want, you can use freely."

Initially, Sina employees were excited by Weibo's rise, but soon they realized, "It's scary how popular it became. It felt like the portal people were being phased out." Said a former channel editor-in-chief who has since left.

Sina is a company with limited resources, unlike Tencent with QQ, NetEase, and Sohu with games, it only has the portal (portal ads account for 75% of revenue). When Weibo's workforce grew to 1,500 people, other businesses and products inevitably suffered.

Between Weibo and the portal, there were frequent "conflicts." Taking a recent incident as an example, a movie called "Detective B" reached a promotional agreement with Weibo. Since Weibo collaborates with various channels, once Weibo recommended something, it would sync to relevant channels. As a result, the entertainment channel staff was very upset because the portal's cooperation with the film party had not been finalized, and the Weibo department had essentially "stolen the order."

This incident concluded with an apology from the Weibo department.

A sense of imbalance quietly brewed among portal employees. After all, when Weibo launched, many celebrity resources were brought over by portal employees, and now Weibo enjoys the fruits of victory for free.

More importantly, Sina's massive investment in Weibo directly affected the normal operation of the Sina portal. In 2012, Sina invested about 1 billion yuan in Weibo, generating 410 million yuan in revenue. However, with the rise of WeChat, the activity level of Sina Weibo dropped by over 30% that year, with daily active users reaching only 13.86 million. Although Sina Weibo recently received a $586 million investment from Alibaba, the commercialization path of Weibo remains unclear.

The decline in portal performance and the massive investment in Weibo have brought unprecedented operational pressure to Sina. One day, Zhang Lin discovered that her desk trash can was missing. Walking around the office, she found that half of the trash cans had been removed. The front desk replied: "This saves cleaning fees." More alarming than cutting administrative costs was Sina's significant reduction in the scale and expenses of its regional reporters.

This was seen as Sina's "self-destruction." Despite being surpassed by Tencent in both portal ad revenue and traffic in 2012, from a content influence perspective, Sina has always been recognized as the top portal. This is thanks to its cost-insensitive reporting investments, such as its expensive US-based correspondents stationed in Washington, New York, and Silicon Valley, focusing on original coverage of politics, economics, sports, and technology in the United States.

To some extent, the competition among the four major portals resembled the Star Wars between the US and the Soviet Union. In this "military competition," speed and comprehensiveness were key. Portals once gave newspapers a fatal blow with this strategy, but after the emergence of Weibo and other social media, the rules of the game are changing.

In the vanguard land of new media in the United States, a new type of social media website emerged. For instance, a site called Business Insider adopted the layout and personal expression style of a blog. Its topics often resemble "Why everything you know about Facebook is wrong" or "The ten most disgusting yet addictive games on App." Another site called BuzzFeed specializes in cute cat news, often related to celebrities and business figures. These highly viral, seemingly gossipy but carefully edited pieces have disrupted the traditional portal playbook.

This perhaps suggests another possibility for portal content production. Last year, Tencent took the lead in experimenting with technology and finance. Besides a fresh and simple page design, it required articles to be meticulously edited, aiming to be engaging, concise, exclusive, and informative, suitable for placement on the portal, video, and mobile ends simultaneously.

The person leading this experiment was Yang Fu, the director of the Technology and Finance Channels. Before joining Tencent in May 2012, he was the executive editor of Global Entrepreneur magazine. In an interview with All Things Finance last year, he stated: "Independent, distinctive, and premium articles should be the most accurate description of Tencent Tech today." This positioning clearly requires a larger investment. For example, the technology channel has a content team of over 60 people, which was unimaginable in the past.

After seeing initial results, Tencent revamped the entertainment, women's, and fashion sections. Adjustments to the news center were also included in the schedule. "News is a bit more cautious. It's the second wave after the first round of revamps," said Chen Jihong, the chief editor of Tencent.com.

Chen Jihong does not want to simply categorize Tencent as a "portal," preferring to call it a "media." The difference may lie in the latter having a more independent style and brand image, unlike the simple aggregation nature of traditional portals.

Sina similarly saw this direction. The tech section opened a blog focused on Apple coverage called "Apple Collection" and another blog dedicated to domestic and international tech startups called "Startup Notes." However, constrained by Sina's system and resources, these products often fell silent after a brief promotion using Sina's platform advantages.

Delayed Attempts

Zhang Lin's channel was categorized as the second type within Sina, requiring it to shoulder part of the sales responsibility. Some original program teams within the channel had to independently seek sponsorship. While employees like Zhang Lin do not need to directly solicit advertisements, they need to cooperate with the marketing department.

In the past, all of Sina's sales were uniformly handled by the sales department, but now, Sina subdivides the unified sales targets among various channels.

In fact, this "business unit system" split is not a new concept for portals. As early as 2008, Sohu began similar experiments, transforming channels like cars and travel into self-sufficient business units. For Sohu, splitting off a vertical sector and listing it separately maximizes portal resources; Changyou in gaming and Sougou in search have already proven successful.

As for Sina, apart from automobiles and real estate, it has shown some hesitation and conservatism in channel verticalization.

Taking the entertainment channel as an example, Chen Tong, who came from a network editing background, always hoped that entertainment would be a user-retaining news center. Thus, for a long time, he transformed the channel into a one-stop information service website offering movie schedules, TV program previews, theater ticketing services, etc.

Retaining users through content was also the direction Chen Tong hoped the portal reform would take. However, the people in the entertainment channel wanted to mix in some commercial elements. Despite multiple objections from Chen Tong and rejections from the market department, discussions about commercialization continued internally within the entertainment channel. The idea was that the entertainment channel could solicit its own advertisements and sponsorships, as advertisers had indeed approached them regarding this matter. Eventually, these ideas were rejected by Chen Tong. With the complexity of the website content, if commercialization were to occur, some black holes might emerge, such as secretly publishing certain articles.

In the eyes of industry insiders, this conservatism caused Sina to miss some opportunities.

Sina Sports was once undoubtedly the leader in the sports news domain, ranking first in both traffic and influence. However, a vertical website called Hupu is surpassing Sina Sports.

This website started with basketball, relying on a combination of special correspondents and UGC (user-generated content) to translate and interpret the latest basketball information from abroad. After cultivating the first batch of basketball fans, Hupu quickly expanded this low-cost information production method to other competitive fields.

In June 2011, Hupu's weekly user visits exceeded those of Sina Sports for the first time, making it China's largest online sports media. In 2012, Hupu's revenue surpassed 100 million yuan, with annual growth rates consistently exceeding 50%.

"Now small products are like special forces, flexible and combat-effective, while large companies, due to their past achievements, find it hard to revolutionize themselves," said Zhao Yiming, who joined a startup after leaving Sina.

Moving Portal

In the eyes of many who have left Sina, the rise of Weibo goes beyond just tilting funds and manpower toward Weibo. The biggest impact is that under the shade of Weibo, Sina lost its past innovation capabilities that led the industry. This prevented Chen Tong from timely recognizing trends such as personalization and verticalization after the portal model became obsolete.

If Weibo could dominate the future media landscape alone, then this sacrifice might seem worthwhile. However, the problem lies in the unexpected impoverishment and radicalization of Weibo content, which has led to a rapid decline in Weibo's activity levels. Meanwhile, with over 500 million smartphone users in China, news apps on mobile devices have suddenly become the top priority for portal transformations.

Due to constraints imposed by Weibo, Sina's portal lags behind in mobile layouts.

Among the smaller brothers in the portal world, NetEase was the first to push forward in the news app space. According to NetEase's Q1 2023 financial report, the total number of users of NetEase's news app has exceeded 75 million, with over 29 million daily active users.

This company has always been relatively conservative in its investment in portals and information, but it never lacks an experimental spirit.

"NetEase didn't initially know what to do, but later it placed great emphasis on it," Wu Maolin, Director of NetEase's New Media Center, told All Things Finance Weekly. He said that the client has become the most important strategy for NetEase's portal, with all resources beginning to tilt toward mobile. "News clients are in the stage of grabbing territory, first grab users, then discuss other things," he said.

Although this product belongs to NetEase's portal, it doesn't entirely depend on the traditional model. Besides replicating the comment feature representative of NetEase's portal tradition onto the client, this small team has also created some original products that cannot be seen on PCs, such as "Today's Light Moment." This small column reaches up to 6 million PVs per day, with over 10,000 comments.

Like Yang Fu, Wu Maolin also comes from traditional media and has an experimental enthusiasm for internet products. "I want to conduct such an experiment, combining UGC with traditional media content organically. To achieve this, I specifically hired several editors to string together jokes from Weibo under a single theme, and users love it. Although it's only about a thousand words per day, preparing the material requires tens of thousands of words. Therefore, copying us isn't easy," he said.

In Wu Maolin's view, the core competitiveness of NetEase's news client lies in optimizing the presentation of information according to the characteristics of mobile interconnection—prohibiting editors from writing lengthy essays, limiting each paragraph to no more than 100 words, and putting a lot of effort into abstracts and titles, so readers can understand just by reading the title and summary.

Wu Maolin admitted that even traditional channel editors within NetEase have begun to prioritize the client over the PC portal for browsing news. NetEase's news client has also recently achieved revenue, with the price of the splash screen advertisement reaching 700,000 yuan per day, higher than the unit price of portal advertisements.

Only at this point did Chen Tong realize that the news client itself could also serve as an entry point.

On April 26, 2013, Sina announced that it would rename its news client Zhanzhong Sina to Sina News and change the single way of presenting news, incorporating social reading elements such as blogs and Weibo. Chen Tong required portal employees to actively share news read on the client to Sina Weibo.

In the eyes of a high-level portal executive, Sina's actions in mobile, like others under the shadow of Weibo, were slow.

Tencent also felt it had lagged behind in the client area. Chen Jihong believed that if the portal had started reforms when smartphones began gaining popularity in 2007, it would have been the best timing.

The adjusted news center of Tencent will be more applicable to mobile releases, avoiding large and numerous topics on the portal, focusing on fine-tuning the most important information. At the same time, the evaluation methods of all channels in Tencent will change, including directors, editors-in-chief, and frontline employees, with KPI evaluations being 60% for mobile clients.

"We hope good content can be distributed across different platforms," Chen Jihong told All Things Finance Weekly.

"In mobile, content quality and distribution methods are more emphasized," said an anonymous insider. According to his view, in the news client field, it is a competition between Tencent and NetEase, while Sina basically follows the rigid approach of moving PC content to mobile. Sohu relies on pre-installing apps through mobile phone manufacturers, increasing installations, but the active user base is not impressive.

The Future of Portals

Regarding this adjustment of Sina's portal, although there are some praises, more people expressed concern about Sina—excessive focus on advertisements and sales might turn the company into a "public relations firm," and the establishment of business units means that if Sina firmly believes in Weibo's potential, it could diversify its remaining influence into various monetization channels.

A widely circulated story in the industry is that during this year's Two Sessions, Wahaha initially approached several major portals hoping for more representation of Zong Qinghou in their Two Sessions special features. Only Sina accepted this collaboration, opening a section titled "Zong Views the Two Sessions" in the upper right corner of its special feature.

"This was COO Du Hong's decision; Chen Tong definitely wouldn't agree with or want such a topic," said an informant. But facing sales pressure, Chen Tong often chooses to compromise.

However, most frontline employees are not overly worried about the future of portals. "Advertisers are now studying Weibo and WeChat, but without finding a good way, they will still invest in portals. Because portals provide guarantees, in terms of traffic and users," said Li Xiang from Sina's advertising department.

According to Fu Jiren, General Manager of Phoenix News' National Marketing Center, due to the lack of sufficiently strong and high-quality media brands in China, portals remain the primary channel for mass internet information consumption.

In a sense, Phoenix News is very similar to Sina, both being typical content-driven companies heavily reliant on online advertising. In the first quarter of this year, Phoenix News' advertising revenue was 166.4 million yuan, while NetEase's advertising revenue was 166 million yuan, meaning Phoenix News has overtaken NetEase in terms of income and joined the first tier.

According to Fu Jiren, Phoenix News' growth mainly comes from third- and fourth-tier cities, a market that has been overlooked by major portals. These groups, besides watching satellite TVs, CCTV, and local newspapers, mostly browse portals when obtaining national information.

After Tencent revamped its portal, the advertising customer department quickly upgraded and optimized related aspects for advertisers, including more social elements, more video content, and integration between mobile internet and traditional internet. "Portal advertising continues to evolve. For example, integrating social genes has become a necessary direction for interactive advertising evolution, as the audience of future interactive advertising will no longer be silent and mediocre masses, but rather distinct individuals. This aligns with the goal of channel revamps," said Luan Na, General Manager of Tencent Network Media Group's Advertising Customer Department, to All Things Finance Weekly.

"Portals have become traditional media and need to retell their stories," said Li Xiang from Sina's advertising department. This direction is also what Sina is currently doing.

Recently, the operations department where Li Xiang works began to re-integrate Sina's existing products and package them for presentation to advertisers. In his plan, images are a key product to recommend to advertisers.

Chen Tong hopes that each channel selects more high-definition images. Channels like sports and entertainment are hiring photojournalists. According to his plan, the focus of Sina's revamp is personalization, localization, socialization, and mobilization. Personalization refers to targeted recommendations based on the content users view daily. Localization involves leveraging the role of local stations to provide users with city-specific news. Mobilization emphasizes mobile clients for phones and iPads, while socialization aims to expand the portal boundaries using Weibo.

"Old soldiers never die, they just fade away," said Zhao Yiming. As a veteran employee who spent five years at Sina, he hopes Sina can overcome its difficulties.

(At the request of the interviewees, Zhang Lin, Li Xiang, and Zhao Yiming are all pseudonyms.)
