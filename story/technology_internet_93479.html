Jack Trout - the person who has had the greatest influence on American marketing.

by yilbi on 2008-09-20 11:53:50

Jack Trout, President of Trout & Partners

He introduced the most revolutionary marketing concept in human history - positioning, and over the following decades, continuously injected the freshest ideas into the marketing world. He is widely recognized as "the person who has had the greatest influence on American marketing in history."

Reference article: "The Person Who Has Had the Greatest Influence on American Marketing in History"

On March 4, 2002, Forbes reported that Southwest Airlines was named one of the "Most Admired Companies in America." Southwest Airlines ranked first in the aviation industry and second among all industrial groups. Prior to this, the company had been honored four times consecutively. When discussing why the company's marketing was so successful, Don Valentine, Vice President of Southwest Airlines, said: "The first rule of marketing is to read the book 'Positioning.' Its core seems simple but is actually filled with power and has already been widely applied in various fields."

The person who discovered the eternal laws of marketing. At the end of the last century, Rowan Gibson, a renowned European management consultant, visited 15 corporate thought leaders including Peter Senge, Michael Porter, and Stephen Covey to ask them to envision the future for 21st-century businesses. The compilation of these interviews became the book "Prethinking the Future." For the reflection and prediction on "marketing," three masters were invited to lead the discussion: one was Philip Kotler, the pioneer of marketing management theory, and the other two were "the world's top practical marketing strategists" — Jack Trout and Al Ries. Mr. Trout and Mr. Ries are the people who discovered the eternal laws of marketing, and it was fitting for them to predict the marketing trends of the new century. Trout was the instigator and practitioner of the latest marketing concepts in the United States since the 1960s, profoundly influencing the development of the American marketing industry. In the late 1960s, he first proposed the concept of "positioning" in marketing communication. In the mid-1980s, Trout and Ries put forward the concept and theory of "marketing wars." In 1988, they co-authored the book "Marketing Revolution," providing scientific processes and templates for companies to formulate and implement marketing strategies. In 1993, after two decades of professional research and practical experience, the two masters summarized the patterns of success and failure in business history and proposed 22 immutable eternal rules of marketing — "The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing."

The growth and development of American enterprises over the past 30 years have been greatly influenced by Trout's marketing ideas. He was the first to point out that Coca-Cola's success was due to its representation of the word "cola" in consumers' minds; Coca-Cola's brand-building should emphasize being the authentic cola. Pepsi, on the other hand, occupied the word "young" in the consumer's mind, and the brand should focus on youthfulness. The results proved that consumers indeed viewed the two brands this way, and after understanding their own positioning, both Coca-Cola and Pepsi gradually adjusted their respective promotional strategies and developed side by side. Meanwhile, Seven-Up followed Trout's advice and emphasized its characteristic of being "caffeine-free," strengthening its position as a "non-cola" in people's minds, becoming the third brand in the soda market. Here, American companies learned the "Focus Law" from "The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing": the most powerful marketing strategy is to occupy a single word in the consumer's mind.

The computer market is no different. Trout pointed out that IBM occupies the word "computer (mainframe)," while Microsoft's word is "software." Therefore, IBM should not aggressively enter the software industry, whereas Microsoft can continue to grow strongly. IBM suffered significant losses in the software field due to not following this principle. Trout positioned Lotus Notes as "groupware (Groupware)" which made it stand out in the software industry, and eventually, Lotus Corporation successfully sold for $3.5 billion based on this positioning. Before this, Lotus Corporation was in peril because its leading product, the spreadsheet (Lotus 1-2-3), was defeated by Microsoft Excel.
