A comedy film not suitable for elementary school students to watch

by hoouk260 on 2012-03-03 20:08:27

A Film Not Suitable for Elementary School Students - Reflections on "Confucius" by Chen Miao

The school organized a free outdoor screening of the movie "Confucius," with the stipulation that parents must accompany their children to watch it. Since I had some unfinished work that evening, I asked my wife to go with our child instead. After watching the movie, the teacher assigned homework: write a reflection. Thus, an unspoken division of labor among us parents emerged: those who accompanied didn't have to write, while those who didn't accompany had to write the essay.

To complete the teacher's glorious assignment, I watched this movie online intermittently over several days and ultimately concluded that, frankly speaking, this is not a film suitable for children. The reasons are as follows:

Firstly, the characters' dialogues in the movie are heavily derived from classical Confucian texts such as "The Analects," filled with ancient Chinese phrases, mixing literary and vernacular language, and full of ostentatious bookishness... This sounds like celestial scripture to children, and even to most parents who dislike reading classical Chinese, it's already quite headache-inducing. If one can't understand the dialogue, how is the student supposed to comprehend the movie? From this perspective, it's clear that the filmmakers never intended to target children; this is a movie aimed at adults, specifically those who are passionate about traditional Chinese learning. Therefore, showing it to elementary school students is akin to a "knowingly difficult" arrangement—admirable in spirit but questionable in effect.

Secondly, the mindset of the director while filming this blockbuster appears extremely contradictory, with chaotic focus. This directly results in the movie lacking a good perspective and narrative style, making it hard to move the audience, including children. A blockbuster is a large production, which can be either a painstaking artistic film or a commercially-driven spectacle. "Confucius" seems to be caught between these two extremes, eventually leaning more towards a commercial blockbuster. As such, casting high-profile actors with hefty salaries, such as Hong Kong star Chow Yun-fat and renowned actress Zhou Xun, to boost box office appeal makes perfect sense within the framework of a commercial film. Based on this commercial mindset, the movie lavishly depicts scenes of military confrontation and warfare, hoping to create an audio-visual spectacle. However, these heavily emphasized segments have little relevance to portraying Confucius's character, serving rather as superfluous embellishments. Confucius represents "culture" (wen), not "military might" (wu). Using "wu" to shape him, even if historically plausible, strays far from the point and can be considered irrelevant. "Wu" should at most serve as a historical backdrop, while depicting inner logical truths should be the film's main focus. If the director had a more upright mindset and decisive approach, then roles like Nanzǐ portrayed by Zhou Xun wouldn’t need to exist so provocatively. War and beauty are basic elements of many vulgar commercial films, attempting to "piggyback" on "Confucius" to attract attention, revealing the director's inner weakness. Ultimately, the film’s multiple objectives intertwine, leading its production away from its original intent—to portray a kind, respectable, and genuinely moving Confucius.

Thirdly, the portrayal of Confucius lacks convincing plot design. Given the commercial demands, this is understandable. The emphasis is on surface-level excitement and visual appeal, with logic being secondary, as mentioned earlier. Many parts of the film's storyline feel awkward, such as the death of Confucius's disciple Yan Hui. Familiar records state he died of starvation, but the movie shows him dying after falling into an ice hole while rescuing bamboo slips. This is a forced plot device, reminiscent of the "tall, grand, complete" era of revolutionary heroism imagery. In summary, many of the plots surrounding Confucius leave viewers with the impression of artificiality and unreliability. This reveals the director's mediocre overall quality and insufficient directing skills. Based on my personal viewing experience, well-made and profound Chinese films often adapt from excellent novels. Scripts written directly by screenwriters without solid source material usually result in movies that are hard to endure. Therefore, if adults find the film unbelievable, why show it to children?

I saw Han Han use extreme words to evaluate "Confucius." While I don't fully agree, I still resonate with his critique. He said, "This movie 'Confucius,' whether from the perspective of shooting significance, commercial profit, artistic pursuit, cinematic exploration, educational enlightenment, warning and touching the world, audio-visual impact, entertainment relaxation, or recording history, has no reason to exist. It is a movie that could have been completely erased." Though harsh, his words hit the nail on the head regarding the film's hybrid nature.

However, from an educational standpoint, encouraging children to engage more with traditional culture from a young age is commendable. Unfortunately, many of the things we do have the right direction but lack clarity in execution. This is indeed worth more reflection after watching. Whether our education system was misled by the commercial tactics of the film company is rather secondary.

Note: The child's school organized a free screening of the movie "Confucius," and the teacher required both the child and the parent to submit a reflection. This is the reflection submitted by the parent. The child, however, couldn't manage to write one.
