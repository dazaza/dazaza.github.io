The 1% Rule of Network Interactive Sites

by chn-blogbeta-com on 2006-07-21 00:18:13

Original Address?

Charles Arthur, July 20, 2006

What is the 1% Rule?

It's a rough rule of thumb that has emerged from statistics: In any online community of 100 people, one person will create content, ten will interact with it (by discussing it or making suggestions) and the remaining 89 will simply observe.

You can see this on YouTube, the famous video-on-demand site which took only 18 months to grab 60% of the online video market. The numbers there are stark: 100 million downloads a day, against 65,000 uploads (according to Antony Mayfield), or one upload for every 1,538 downloads. There are 20 million unique visitors a month.

The proportion of uploaders to downloaders is thus 0.5%; though note that this is at an early stage in the site's development - not everyone knows about YouTube yet (and anyway, it's easier to download than upload since you can embed links to YouTube videos on any webpage).

Another example of interactive content creation in a community form comes from Wikipedia: 50% of wiki edits are done by 0.7% of users; more than 70% of articles have had input from less than 1.8% of users (according to Church of the Customer).

Early figures suggested that 80% of the content on sites was created by 20% of the users, but that ratio is now falling. That's something Web 2.0 companies need to think about. A site which places too much emphasis on interactivity and user-created content may find that nine out of every ten people who visit are just passing through.

Bradley Horowitz of Yahoo pointed out the same issue exists there: In Yahoo Groups, 1% of the users create the groups; 10% of them actively discuss and initiate new topics; and 100% of them gain information from the active few.
