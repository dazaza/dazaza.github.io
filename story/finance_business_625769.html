Woman wants to preserve her boyfriend's sperm after his unexpected death

by makemsuper on 2011-12-15 16:55:49

By reporters of this newspaper Gong Baiwei, Wang Haiqi and Liu Shaolong, Changsha report - Yesterday at noon, Ah Long (a pseudonym), who was installing aluminum alloy windows on the 25th floor of a building in Changsha's Xingsha district, accidentally fell to his death. After her sorrow, Yan Yan (a pseudonym), Ah Long's girlfriend, began seeking help everywhere, hoping to extract sperm from her deceased boyfriend’s body to preserve his lineage. The reporter helped her contact the vice president of the中信Xiangya Reproductive and Genetic Hospital. The vice president stated that due to Ah Long being dead for over 10 hours, it would be difficult for the sperm to survive, making Yan Yan's wish unlikely to come true.

A six-month romance ends with an accidental death

At 9:30 PM last night, the reporter met Yan Yan in front of Xiangya Grand Hotel. There were no tears on Yan Yan's face. When she saw the reporter, she almost knelt down, crying incoherently: "Please help me! What I need now is time!"

After a few minutes, Yan Yan's emotions slowly calmed down. She said that both her and her boyfriend Ah Long are from Jiangxi. They were classmates in junior high school. At the end of last year, they confirmed their relationship after returning home. After the Spring Festival, they both came to Changsha, where Ah Long worked installing aluminum alloy windows. This year during the Dragon Boat Festival, Ah Long's mother also came to Changsha.

"Being with him these past six months has been the happiest time of my life," Yan Yan choked up, sobbing intermittently as she recounted the moment when happiness abruptly ended. Yan Yan said that around noon yesterday, Ah Long was installing aluminum alloy windows in a building in Xingsha, Changsha. His mother had prepared lunch, and the family was waiting for him to return. However, while working on the exterior wall of the 25th floor, Ah Long suddenly fell from the building. When Yan Yan saw him again, Ah Long was already bloodied and mangled, with no hope of survival.

She wanted to preserve her boyfriend's lineage

After crying, Yan Yan had an idea: to keep Ah Long's sperm and have his child, "I am willing to never marry and raise the child." As for how to handle future birth certificates and other documents, Yan Yan said she didn't have time to think about it.

"You must help me quickly. If I can't leave him with a lineage, I will go with him..." Yan Yan cried so hard she couldn't straighten up. Ah Long's mother said Yan Yan's actions moved her deeply. They agreed with Yan Yan's decision and urgently needed a doctor to extract and preserve Ah Long's sperm. Around 8 PM, Ah Long's mother accompanied Yan Yan to the中信Xiangya Reproductive and Genetic Hospital. Although the hospital had a sperm bank, it lacked a urology department and could not perform the extraction.

Yan Yan called our newspaper for help, and the reporter helped her contact the hospital. At 10 PM, the vice president of中信Xiangya arrived at Xiangya Grand Hotel. The vice president told Yan Yan that it had been 10 hours since Ah Long's death. After more than 8 hours, the sperm survival rate was not high. Despair showed in Yan Yan's eyes.

[Doctor's Statement]

This kind of surgery is a legal vacuum in medical regulations

Director Zhu of the sperm bank at中信Xiangya Hospital said that starting from the moment of death, sperm generally loses vitality after 24 hours. Extracting sperm from a deceased person requires surgically removing the male reproductive system, but the hospital does not have the qualification to perform such an external surgical procedure. "Today, I received more than 20 calls regarding this matter, showing how anxious the woman is. We can only preserve sperm, but we cannot perform the surgery," Director Zhu said.

To quickly remove the male reproductive system, the reporter contacted several hospitals. A hospital medical affairs director stated that the hospital had not encountered such a case before. Removing the reproductive organs of a deceased person is still a legal vacuum in medical regulations. "It's not that we don't want to help, but there's nothing we can do. If there is proof from the police, recognition from legal experts, and consent from the deceased's family, doctors might possibly proceed, but basically, there are still no surgeons willing to perform such a surgery."
