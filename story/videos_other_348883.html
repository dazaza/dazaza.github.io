"In Baudelaire's work, Paris became the subject of lyric poetry for the first time. His poems were not local folk songs; this allegorical poet gazed at the city of Paris with a sense of alienation. This is the gaze of the flâneur."1 This description by German thinker Walter Benjamin in the 1930s about French poet Charles Baudelaire, who was born 70 years earlier, fits perfectly with his contemporary—Chinese filmmaker Sun Yu—all that is needed is to replace Paris with Shanghai. In Chinese cinema, Sun Yu was also hailed as a poet, only he wrote his poems on film reels. In 1934, Sun Yu wrote an article titled "Can I Accept the 'Poet’s Laurel Wreath'?" in which he said, "If that laurel wreath is prepared to be awarded to an 'ideal poet,' whose eyes are wide open and look forward, then his poetry—his films—are full of vitality, unafraid of hardship, unafraid of ridicule, dedicated to instilling an upward spirit into the hearts of those who are decadent and suffering... I long for that 'poet’s laurel wreath' and am willing to cherish it forever!"2

Chinese cinema was born in 1905, and for its first 20 years, it developed in a haphazard manner, focusing on supernatural martial arts and domestic dramas. The modern urban landscape (primarily referring to Shanghai) emerged in the cracks of the global capitalist economic crisis of the 1920s and just surfaced in the early 1930s. Against this backdrop, Sun Yu's profound gaze on the modern metropolis of Shanghai through works like *Wild Rose* (1932), *Dawn* (1933), and *The Sports Queen* (1934) was one of the earliest poetic explorations of Shanghai as a lyrical subject. Considering the increasing leftward tilt of the film industry and the rising prominence of war themes after the January 28th Shanghai War, Sun Yu's gaze on Shanghai becomes even more significant. These urban films, which had yet to transition to class or war themes, provide the best specimens for studying issues of Chinese modernity. They record the initial arrival of modernity in China and document the complex attitudes of Chinese intellectuals towards modernity.

1. City, Film, and Shock: Representing Modernity in a Modern Way

As an academic discourse, "modernity" has been discussed in China for over a decade, though defining this seemingly simple but highly complex concept remains difficult. However, after heated discussions, the academic community reached the following consensus: "Modernity, from the West to the East, from the modern era to the contemporary, is an open concept characterized by 'family resemblance.' It is the focus of contradictions and conflicts across political, economic, social, and cultural dimensions during the process of modernization." In other words, modernity is the "overall characteristic of the historical concept of modern times and the social-historical process of modernization."

What is the biggest distinction between modernity and antiquity? Or, what is the greatest hallmark of modernization? There may be many answers, but no one would deny the importance of "cities and urbanization" in this context. In a certain sense, modernization is urbanization, and cities are the largest and most prominent symbols of modern society! Cities are the foundation of modern democratic politics and nation-states, the condition and product of modern capitalist market economies, the breeding ground and arena for modern instrumental rationality, and the exhibition hall and distribution center for so-called modern civilization. In short, cities are the concentrated manifestation of contradictions and conflicts across political, economic, social, and cultural dimensions during the process of modernization. Therefore, when Shanghai, the largest city in China, suddenly rose on the western shore of the Pacific Ocean in the 1930s, modernity came bundled with it.

For individuals entering or living in cities, "shock" could be described as the greatest experience provided by the behemoth of modernity that is the city. Etymologically, "shock" or "jolt" is an old, ordinary term that gained special significance thanks to Freud. In his book *Beyond the Pleasure Principle* (also translated as *Beyond the Principle of Pleasure*), Freud mentioned a "traumatic neurosis," identifying "mainly the factors of astonishment and jolt" as its causes. "Jolt" refers to "a situation where a person finds themselves in danger without any mental preparation."4 "Jolt," or "shock," a medical term borrowed by Benjamin and applied to Baudelaire, was considered "one of the important experiences that had a decisive influence on Baudelaire's personality," placing "the experience of shock at the center of his artistic works."5 But soon, Benjamin realized that Baudelaire's "shock" experience was not personal—it was a universal experience of people in modern cities. Benjamin believed that all innovations in the mid-19th century shared a common feature: sudden movements initiated by a single touch. He gave examples like the telephone and camera, arguing that "the camera endows moments with a nostalgic shock" and that "this tactile experience combined with visual experience, much like the sensation given by newspaper advertisements or urban traffic"—what kind of sensation? "Navigating through the flow of vehicles and pedestrians pulls individuals into a series of panic and collisions. The nervous stimulation rapidly passes through the body one after another, just like energy in a battery."6 There is no doubt that Benjamin's description of life in 1860s Paris integrated his own feelings about modern cities, feelings that apply equally well to 1930s Shanghai, perhaps explaining why Shanghai was called the "Paris of the East" at the time.

Of course, cities don't just shock people with their bustling streets; we see in Sun Yu's films his deliberate display of towering skyscrapers, flashing neon lights, rapidly rotating machinery in factories, and large chimneys on ships... Interestingly, these displays are often placed before newcomers to the city, such as Xiao Feng in *Wild Rose*, Lingling in *Dawn*, and Lin Ying in *The Sports Queen*. Thus, the "gaze" of these newcomers expresses this shock. In *Dawn*, Lingling and her companions escape from the countryside to the city. After arriving, the film uses nearly ten minutes of footage to depict the following scenes: (1) the tall chimneys and whirring machines of ships; (2) garbage collection and transportation in the city (an interesting detail used to represent urban civilization); (3) the surging crowds of workers going to and from factories; (4) Lingling and her companions carefully crossing the street as cars rush past them; (5) they walk along today's Dongdaming Road beside the Huangpu River, pointing and looking, with skyscrapers appearing in the background or as subjective shots; (6) they visit their cousin's house, feeling fresh and curious about the structure of the building; (7) their fat brother-in-law takes them to the Yu Garden for sightseeing, where they excitedly squeeze through crowds and Lingling personally experiences some amusement rides; (8) even more interestingly, the film sets aside a group of "night in the city" shots, showcasing flashing neon lights, spinning light columns, dragon-like streetlights, and, of course, street-side prostitutes after a specific title card appears. Similar images appear in *The Sports Queen*, where the first shot shows the female protagonist Lin Ying's ship arriving at the Bund dock against the backdrop of the Bund buildings after departing from Zhejiang. Then, the audience follows the characters in the film as they begin searching for the missing protagonist. Where did the young lady go? Following the eyes of a little dog, Lin Ying appears in the frame, perched atop a high chimney. This shot not only depicts Lin Ying's liveliness and playfulness but also emphasizes the size of the modern ship's chimney in contrast to Lin Ying. Subsequently, Lin Ying lands and gets into an open-top car, driving through the Bund buildings. At this moment, the director uses a moving upward angle shot, presenting the audience with the subjective perspective of passing several-story-high buildings swiftly and oppressively, offering the best presentation of the newcomer's shock upon entering the modern city.

The shock experienced by outsiders is not limited to these external urban landscapes; modern urban life and fashion have similar effects. These mostly relate to people and human bodies, including wavy hairstyles, red lips, especially women's thighs—whether wearing silk stockings or naturally exposed, they become images frequently captured by the movie camera. A special mention must be made of *The Sports Queen*, which is less a story film and more a documentary of modern student life. The film not only showcases drills of female students, track and field events such as sprints, high jumps, shot put, javelin (especially the detailed depiction of sprint starts, finishing techniques, starting commands, and specialized running shoes), basketball, football, and modern competitive sports—including a modern sports meet—but also presents numerous details of student life, such as brushing teeth, taking showers, attending music classes, etc. Presenting these shorts-and-tank-top-clad female bodies to the audience and showing the spirited athletic performances of these girls would surely create a shocking effect for Chinese audiences in the early 1930s.

Just as Baudelaire's shock experience of 19th-century Paris was not solely personal, neither was the shock experience of 1930s Shanghai limited to Sun Yu or expressed only in film. Literature also features classic descriptions by urban masters Mao Dun and Mu Shiying. Take the opening passage of *Midnight*: "The twilight mingled with thin fog enveloped the towering steel framework of the Waibaidu Bridge. When trams passed, the electric wires under the steel frame occasionally burst forth with emerald sparks. Looking eastward from the bridge, one could see the foreign warehouses in Pudong like monstrous beasts squatting in the dusk, their countless lights resembling thousands of small eyes. Looking westward, what startled one was the extraordinarily large neon advertisement installed on top of a Western-style building, radiating fiery red and phosphorescent green flames: Light, Heat, Power!" Iron bridges, electric trams, and lights are the very symbols of the city, while what startles is the enormous neon advertisement on the rooftop of a Western-style building, together displaying the three primary colors of the city—"light-heat-power."

The most classic description of the shock of the city to an outsider is undoubtedly found in *Midnight*’s portrayal of Old Master Wu's first arrival in Shanghai: "The car sped crazily forward. Old Master Wu looked ahead. My God! Hundreds of windows lit up like hundreds of strange eyes. Towering skyscrapers soared toward the heavens, rushing toward Old Master Wu and suddenly disappearing; bare poles standing upright on flat land endlessly approached Old Master Wu's face and then vanished again; a string of black monsters, each with a pair of bright eyes emitting dazzling strong light, roared—pou—pou—and rushed forward like lightning, directly heading toward the small box in which Old Master Wu sat! Closer! Closer! Old Master Wu closed his eyes, trembling all over. He felt his head spinning on his neck; his vision was filled with red, yellow, green, black, luminous, cubic, conical shapes—a chaotic mass jumping and spinning; his ears were filled with rumbling, rumbling, rumbling! Rolling, rolling, rolling!"

Of course, what shocked Old Master Wu also included the flesh we mentioned. "Even though it was still May, the sudden heat caused Second Miss to wear summer clothes completely; her slim, healthy body was tightly wrapped in pale blue thin silk, a pair of full breasts clearly protruding, the sleeves rolled above the elbow, exposing half an arm white as snow. An indescribable aversion suddenly filled Old Master Wu's chest. He quickly turned his face away, only to find himself staring at another semi-nude woman dressed in shiny silk vests, her skin visible through the fabric, sitting high on a rickshaw, lifting her naked white leg as if she wasn't wearing pants." Ultimately, "the mechanical noise, the foul smell of car exhaust, the fragrance of women's bodies, the red glow of neon lights—all the ghostly essences of the city mercilessly pressed down on Old Master Wu's aging soul until he only saw stars, heard ringing, and felt dizzy!" Poor Old Master Wu could not withstand this stimulation of modernity and died.7

Beyond literature, pictorials were another carrier of modernity. Flipping through the *Liangyou* pictorial, which started publication in 1925, a large portion of its diverse content consists of charming urban scenes—department stores, cinemas, race tracks, dance halls, public trams, and various modern concepts of life and daily necessities, naturally including fashion and the human body. By presenting these symbols of modernity to readers, *Liangyou* also brought the shock of modernity to its readers.

This is a collective presentation, a natural reaction of various media to the arrival of modernity. Among all forms of media, however, film is the most direct, vivid, and authentic in conveying the shock effect, making shock gradually transform into the core aesthetic feature of film. "At some point, a new urgent need for stimulation discovered film. In a film, shock as a form of perception has been established as a formal principle. What determines the rhythm of production on the conveyor belt is also the basis of the rhythm people feel in films."8 Thus, both film and the city become symbols of modernity, sharing the ability to give people a "shock experience." What makes film particularly intriguing is that it not only achieves objective shock through the presentation of shock but often consciously pursues a formal shock effect—for instance, some non-subjective shots of buildings and human bodies frequently appearing in Sun Yu's films. *Dawn* altered the conventional black-background white-letter subtitles, choosing instead a bright and deep tunnel of light as the subtitle background. Shock becomes the core aesthetic feature of this modern art form and, in turn, becomes an element constituting the shock effect of the city. The city and film, when serving as signs of modernity, become intertwined—you in me, me in you.

Thus, when Sun Yu focuses on portraying emerging cities through the medium of emerging film, he creates a meta-modern entity that is consistent inside and out, complementing each other beautifully. And when someone enters the luxurious movie palaces of 1930s Shanghai to watch such a film, they almost find themselves surrounded by modernity in three layers.

2. Artistic Stance vs Class Stance: Countering Modernity with Modern Concepts

The worthiness of attention regarding modernity lies not primarily in its complex forms of expression but in the attitudes of individuals and groups encountering it. It is well known that Chinese intellectuals in the 1990s predominantly held reflective and critical stances. Interestingly, even at the dawn of modernity in the 1930s, Sun Yu stood on its opposite side. However, Sun Yu's critical stance is not singular but compound, combining two dimensions: art and class. These are expressed through two opposing relationships in Sun Yu's films: city versus countryside and capitalists versus workers.

Matei Calinescu, in his book *Five Faces of Modernity*, explicitly distinguishes between two types of modernity. He points out: "At some moment in the first half of the nineteenth century, an irreparable split occurred between modernity as a stage in the history of Western civilization and modernity as an aesthetic concept." The former includes doctrines of progress, belief in the potential of science and technology to benefit humanity, concern for time (measurable time, a time that can be bought and sold and thus possesses a calculable price), worship of reason, free thought defined within an abstract humanist framework, and pragmatism and orientation toward action and success—all of which are linked to varying degrees with the struggle toward modernity and remain vibrant and promoted as core values in the victorious civilization established by the middle class. For this concept of modernity, Calinescu explicitly names it "the bourgeois concept of modernity." Conversely, "another modernity, which would lead to the emergence of avant-garde movements, from its romantic beginnings inclined toward radical anti-bourgeois attitudes. It despises middle-class values and expresses this disdain through a variety of means, ranging from rebellion, anarchism, apocalypticism to self-exile. Therefore, cultural modernity is better indicated by its public rejection of bourgeois modernity and its intense negation passion than by its positive aspirations (which often differ)."9

The rejection of bourgeois modernity by aesthetic (cultural) modernity is concentrated in their contrasting attitudes toward cities and the countryside. In the former's view, the city is a gathering place for falsehood, rigidity, coldness, and deception, while the countryside is idealized as a natural, simple pastoral poem. This attitude is perfectly embodied in Sun Yu's early films. The country girl Xiaofeng in *Wild Rose* can be seen as China's Carmen and Adala—she is beautiful, helpful, and wildly spirited, like a thorny wild rose. Her counterparts—the father of Jiangbo and the city girl pursuing Jiangbo—are selfish, indifferent, short-sighted, and conservative. The detail of Xiaofeng taking off her shoes in the Jiang family's living room vividly expresses the opposition between urban and rural cultures. Accustomed to running barefoot in shorts in the countryside, Xiaofeng feels extremely uncomfortable in silk stockings and heels that distort her nature, leading her to secretly throw the shoes aside. This act is seen as improper by the upper class but represents a natural expression of her innate nature, conforming to nature. Xiaofeng supports Jiangbo, who has left home, and later saves him from prison by agreeing never to see him again, asking his father for help. In the eyes of director Sun Yu, this country girl is akin to Jiangbo's praise in the film—"My wild rose, how beautiful you are! How great you are!"

Similarly clear binary oppositions are evident in *Dawn*. The beautiful country girl Lingling comes to the city full of hope, but the city turns out to be a den of iniquity. She is first raped by the young boss, then bullied by the foreman, forced outside in a panic, and finally sold to a brothel, becoming a prostitute. The city is everywhere a trap. Yet, compared to the city's evils, Lingling retains her inherent kindness—she still helps the poor even as a courtesan and ultimately sacrifices herself to protect her revolutionary lover from the warlord's gun. More meaningfully, the director specially arranges two flashbacks during Lingling's misfortunes—one shows Lingling and her lover boating and picking lotus flowers in a pond of lotuses, and the other shows them chasing and playing by a stream. Sun Yu is clearly using these idyllic rural scenes to contrast with the ugly, cruel city, thereby negating and criticizing the latter.

Aesthetic modernity's critique of bourgeois modernity often targets "philistinism," because "this is a typical form of middle-class hypocrisy." Calinescu mentions that the best example of critiquing philistine mentality is pre-Romantic and Romantic Germany, which once became the core of cultural life at the time. Later, this critique was inherited by young radical groups in the 1830s and 1840s, developing into two clearly opposed types: revolutionaries and philistines. Both Marx and Engels criticized Goethe as both a rebel and a philistine. The aesthetic resistance to the bourgeois mindset via the concept of philistinism developed in Germany into an ideological and political critique, which contemporary Chinese thinker Wang Hui calls "anti-modernity modernity," where Marxism plays a core role.

In 1930, the Chinese Left-Wing Dramatic League was founded, and in 1931, the party began actively intervening in the film industry. After the September 18th and January 28th incidents, the left-wing cultural movement widely arose. As a progressive director, Sun Yu in the early 1930s had already become a conscious worker in the left-wing film movement. Therefore, his critique of modernity at this time gained another weapon—Marxist class theory. Two new opposing elements were introduced into the plot: capitalists versus workers, revolutionaries versus counter-revolutionaries. Thus, in *Dawn*, we see that the city not only harbors "philistinism" but also ruthless capitalists exploiting workers and militarists oppressing the people. Lingling's lover, her cousin, goes to Guangdong, the birthplace of the revolution, joins the revolutionary army, and participates in the Northern Expedition. Scenes of war and smoke are incorporated into the film, serving dual functions of creating visual shock and expressing political opposition. Lingling, upon escaping the brothel and witnessing her cousin's death, passionately declares, "Heaven... despite such darkness! ... Oppression... despite such increase! Only thus does revolution come! Only thus does dawn come!"

3. Ambiguous Modernity: The Ambiguous City in the Eyes of the Flâneur

Just as vast cities hide filth and gather excellence, modernity itself, from its inner meaning to its outer scope, is entangled and complex, sequentially bringing about the observer's attitude of welcoming and rejecting, rejecting and welcoming. Cities, modernity, and the stance of intellectuals facing modernity can be summarized in two words: "ambiguous."

Sun Yu's ambiguous attitude towards modernity is mainly manifested in the handling of the relationship between city and countryside. When Sun Yu critiques modernity, the relationship between the city (the embodiment of modernity) and the countryside manifests as an opposition between a place of sin and degeneration and a place of purity and natural pastoral beauty. Simultaneously, there exists another dimension of observation between them—that is, the difference between modern civilization and the world outside civilization. Although Sun Yu maintains his imagination of the pastoral countryside and does not display its backwardness and ignorance, he at least positively portrays the advanced and civilized aspects of the city. This portrayal mainly refers not to the richness of urban transportation, housing, clothing, and food or the immense power of modern machine production, but rather to the civility and health of modern urban life. In *The Sports Queen*, the female students of the sports school naturally showcase their healthy bodies, engage in rich and varied study and exercise, and cultivate good and civilized living habits. In *Wild Rose*, Xiaofeng consciously imitates Jiangbo's coffee-drinking gestures, and although the dress she once found uncomfortable was eventually pawned due to financial difficulties, the plastic rose flower decorating the dress was carefully preserved by her. In Sun Yu's other films, such as *Little Toy*, Zhu'er's desire for mechanized toy production and Luo Ming's aspiration to drive a road roller in *The Great Road* reflect the director's affirmation of the advanced aspect of modernity.

The advancement of the city, according to Sun Yu, manifests in another way: enhancing the revolutionary consciousness of workers. In *Dawn*, Lingling laments during her last meeting with her lover, "Isn't it funny, we came from the countryside to Shanghai not even two years ago, and now we seem like different people!" In less than two years, although Lingling was forced to become a courtesan, she now understands, "It's not you who made me suffer, nor one or two people." Meanwhile, her lover, her cousin, has become a sailor and joined the revolution, becoming a revolutionary soldier. This is the role of the city as a melting pot, a byproduct of modernity.

The ambiguity of cities in Sun Yu's eyes stems from both subjective and objective reasons. Subjectively, it lies in their position in the city. Left-wing intellectuals in 1930s Shanghai mostly occupied the fringes of society, belonging to Baudelaire's "flâneurs." Flâneurs (writers, free artists, professional conspirators) are the offspring of big cities, as only big cities provide them with space for survival. Although they can enjoy the convenience of big cities, they do not possess them. They wander through the arcades and cafes of big cities, residing in cramped attic rooms, surviving by selling their labor in big cities. Their status and living conditions resemble those of Xiao Li, the painter of advertisements played by Zheng Junli in *Wild Rose*. They have a natural closeness to the lower classes, while bosses and capitalists are both their breadwinners and relentless adversaries. The ambiguous identity of flâneurs determines their ambiguous attitude towards cities and modernity.

Objectively, this stems from the unique circumstances of China as a late-developing country. In the impoverished and backward China of the 1930s, the country was at a critical juncture of national survival. To avoid extinction, modernization—that is, industrialization and urbanization—had to be achieved. Therefore, whether Mao Dun or Sun Yu, left-wing intellectuals, while criticizing capitalists from the standpoint of the working class, generally express admiration and approval for large-scale capitalist production.

Of course, regardless of the reasons, the drawbacks of modernity brought about by industrialization and urbanization cannot be overlooked. These drawbacks all point to the alienation of humans, including the alienation of interpersonal relationships, behavior patterns, and emotional patterns. Simmel stated that "the characteristic of interpersonal relationships in large cities is the emphasis on the use of eyes over ears, which mainly helps public communication systems," but in modern large cities, "those who can see but not hear experience more troubles than those who can hear but not see," because before the complete establishment of public buses, trams, and trolleybuses in the 19th century, "people have never been placed in such a situation where they could stare at each other for minutes or even hours without exchanging a word."10
