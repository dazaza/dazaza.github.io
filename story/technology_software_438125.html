Trust me, I am an eagle.

▲

A person grabbed an eaglet from an eagle's nest on the peak of a deep valley, and brought it home to raise in a chicken coop. The eaglet ate, played, and rested with chickens. It thought it was a chicken. As the eagle grew up, its wings became full. The owner wanted to train it into a hunting eagle, but because it had always been with chickens, it had become just like them and basically had no desire to fly. The owner tried various measures, all to no avail. Finally, he took it to the top of the mountain and threw it out. The eagle fell like a stone. In the panic, it flapped its wings desperately, and finally, it flew!

Secret 1: Hardship calls forth the power of success.

▲ Five Gold Coins ▲

There was a man named Abag living on the grasslands of Inner Mongolia. Once, young Abag and his father got lost on the grassland. Abag was tired and scared, and eventually almost couldn't walk anymore. His father took five coins out of his pocket, buried one in the grass, and put the other four in Abag's hand, saying, "Life has five gold coins, each for childhood, adolescence, youth, middle age, and old age. You have only used one, which is the one buried in the grass. You cannot throw all five in the grassland; you must use them bit by bit, each time using them differently, so life is not wasted. Today we must get out of the grassland, and in the future, you must also leave the grassland. The world is vast, and while alive, you should travel more places, see more, and not waste your coins." Encouraged by his father, that day Abag walked out of the grassland. When he grew up, Abag left his hometown and became an excellent captain.

Secret 2: Cherish life, and you will walk out of the swamps of setbacks.

▲ Sweep Sunshine ▲

There were two brothers, no older than four or five years. Because their bedroom window was always closed, they thought the room was too dark. Seeing the bright sunshine outside, they envied it very much. The two brothers discussed, "We can sweep some sunshine inside together." So, the two brothers took brooms and dustpans and went to the balcony to sweep sunshine. When they brought the dustpan into the room, the sunshine disappeared. They swept many times like this, but there was still no sunshine in the house. Their mother, busy in the kitchen, saw their strange actions and asked, "What are you doing?" They replied, "The room is too dark. We want to sweep some sunshine inside." The mother laughed and said, "Just open the window, and the sunshine will naturally come in. Why bother sweeping?"

Secret 3: Open the closed heart door, and the successful sunshine will drive away the darkness of failure.

▲ A Spider and Three People ▲

After the rain, a spider struggled to climb towards the already broken web on the wall. Because the wall was wet, it would fall off when it climbed to a certain height. It kept climbing again and again, falling again and again... The first person saw it and sighed, muttering, "My whole life is just like this spider, working hard but getting nothing." Thus, he gradually became depressed. The second person saw it and said, "This spider is really foolish. Why doesn't it go around the dry place? I won't be as stupid as it in the future." Thus, he became smarter. The third person saw it and was immediately moved by the spider's spirit of perseverance after repeated failures. Thus, he became stronger.

Secret 4: Those with a successful mindset can perceive the power of success everywhere.

▲ Save Yourself ▲

A person was sheltering under the eaves from the rain and saw Guanyin walking by with an umbrella. This person said, "Guanyin Bodhisattva, please save sentient beings. Can you take me along?" Guanyin said, "I am in the rain, and you are under the eaves. There is no rain under the eaves, so you don't need me to save you." This person immediately jumped out from under the eaves and stood in the rain: "Now I am in the rain too. Will you save me now?" Guanyin said, "You are in the rain, and I am in the rain. I am not getting wet because I have an umbrella; you are getting wet because you don't have one. So it's not me saving myself, but the umbrella saving me. If you want to be saved, don't find me; find your own umbrella!" After saying this, she left. The next day, this person encountered difficulties and went to the temple to pray to Guanyin. When he entered the temple, he found someone praying in front of Guanyin's statue, and this person looked exactly like Guanyin. This person asked, "Are you Guanyin?" That person replied, "I am Guanyin." This person asked again, "Then why do you worship yourself?" Guanyin laughed and said, "I also encounter difficulties, but I know, asking others is not as good as asking oneself."

Secret 5: Successful people save themselves.

▲ Make Losses Desirable ▲

An elderly person on a fast-moving train accidentally dropped a newly bought shoe out of the window. The people around him felt sorry, but unexpectedly, the elder immediately threw the second shoe out of the window as well. This action shocked everyone even more. The elder explained, "No matter how expensive this shoe is, it is useless to me now. If someone can pick up a pair of shoes, maybe they can wear them!"

Secret 6: Successful people are good at letting go and seeing value in loss.

▲ Don't Open the Wrong Window ▲

A little girl was lying on the windowsill, watching people outside bury her cute little dog. She couldn't help but burst into tears and feel extremely sad. Her grandfather noticed and immediately led her to another window, allowing her to admire his rose garden. Indeed, the little girl's mood brightened instantly. The old man lifted his granddaughter's chin and said, "Child, you opened the wrong window."

Secret 7: Open the window beside failure, and perhaps you will see hope.

▲ Secrets of Life ▲

Thirty years ago, a young man left his hometown to start discovering his future. His first stop was to visit the patriarch of his clan to seek guidance. The old patriarch was practicing calligraphy when he heard that a descendant of his clan was about to embark on his life journey. He wrote three words: "Don't be afraid." Then he looked up and said to the young man, "Child, the secret of life consists of six words. Today I will tell you three, for your benefit for half a lifetime." Thirty years later, the former young man, now middle-aged, had achieved some success but also added many sorrows. On a long journey back home, he visited the patriarch again. When he arrived at the patriarch's house, he learned that the elder had passed away a few years ago. The family handed him a sealed envelope and said, "This is what the patriarch left for you. He said one day you would come back." The returning traveler then remembered that thirty years ago he heard half the secret of life here. Opening the envelope, it clearly read another three big words: "Don't regret."

Secret 8: Before middle age, don't be afraid; after middle age, don't regret.

▲ Driver Test ▲

A certain company planned to hire a car driver at high pay. After multiple rounds of screening and exams, only three most skilled competitors remained. The examiner asked them, "On the edge of a cliff, there is a piece of gold. How close do you think you can drive and still not fall off?" "Two meters," the first one said. "Half a meter," the second one confidently said. "I will keep as far away from the cliff as possible," the third one said. As a result, the company hired the third one.

Secret 9: Don't compete with temptation, but stay as far away as possible.

▲ Lion and Antelope Education ▲

Every day, when the sun rises, the animals on the African savanna begin to run. The lioness was educating her cub: "Child, you must run faster, faster. If you cannot run faster than the slowest antelope, you will starve to death." At another place, the antelope mother was also educating her cub: "Child, you must run faster, faster. If you cannot run faster than the fastest lion, you will definitely be eaten by them."

Secret 10: Remember, you may run fast, but others may run faster.

It is said that in the mountains, there is a kind of monkey that likes to steal corn. Local farmers fix a gourd-shaped bottle, tie it to a big tree, and put corn that the monkeys like to eat inside. At night, when the monkeys see the corn in the bottle, they are very happy and reach in to grab it. As a result, they catch the corn, but their hands cannot be pulled out. By the next morning, when the farmer catches them, they still refuse to let go.

You would certainly laugh at the monkey's greed. This is called "being able to pick it up," but "not being able to let go." Actually, not just monkeys, but people also often experience "being able to pick it up" but "not being able to let go." In daily life, it is easy to "pick it up," gritting teeth, enduring hardships, and managing to "pick it up and get through." But to willingly "let go" is not so easy.

What is meant by "letting go" is a psychological state, a philosophy of dealing with the world, which means when faced with overwhelming pressure, one can unload the mental burden and feel light and carefree. Renowned scholar Ji Xianlin's method of health preservation is the "three no-principles," one of which is "not to fuss," meaning "letting go." Professor Wu Jieping, at eighty years old, when talking about mental health preservation, said, "Not to carry sorrow in one's heart," which is also a form of "letting go."

However, for ordinary people, there are too many things "they cannot let go of." Children's schooling, employment, and even marriage and career establishment make parents unable to let go; personal work dissatisfaction, encountering setbacks, losing face, making mistakes, fearing criticism from superiors and ridicule from colleagues, having unresolved issues, unable to let go. In short, sometimes this cannot be let go, that cannot be let go, thinking about this and that, worrying about this and that, endless worries, knotted intestines, if prolonged, it will inevitably lead to psychological fatigue, easily causing psychological barriers. A psychologist said, "People who are overly fatigued are chasing death." Some people live too tiredly, always looking down, aging before their time, habitually carrying everything in their hearts without letting go. Carving "wrinkles" in their hearts, torturing their hearts both laboriously and prematurely. So, how can one truly "let go"?

Do not be enslaved by material possessions. Especially avoid being solely profit-driven, calculating every penny, and harboring resentment. One should learn from the broad-mindedness of the poet Li Bai, "Born with talent, I am bound to be useful, and even if I spend all my money, it will return." Letting go of wealth is a graceful "letting go."

Do not be weighed down by fame. Especially for some high-IQ intellectual types, who usually strive to be the best, fear of losing or gaining, and view status very seriously, even loving fame as life itself. Despite being full of knowledge, they die for fame, truly starting confusion with literacy. Being able to let go is a transcendent "letting go."

Do not be disturbed by emotions. The world cannot clearly explain anything better than the word "emotion." Some people get caught up in emotional entanglements, lose control psychologically, cut endlessly, mess untidily, suffering unbearably every day. If one can let go, it is a rational "letting go."

Do not be hurt by worries. There are too many troubling things in life, but one should face them calmly and optimistically. Many people "just wipe off their brows, but troubles rise in their hearts," being overly sensitive, melancholic, depression becoming a common and frequent disease, which greatly harms physical and mental health. The great writer Rabindranath Tagore said, "The best way to deal with the affairs of the world is to laugh, not to wash them away with tears." Quietly putting worries aside is a joyful "letting go."

As the ancients said well, "Unmoved by honor or disgrace, watch the flowers bloom and fade before the courtyard; indifferent to coming and going, look at the clouds roll and scatter in the sky." Being good at "letting go," leaving space for the mind, and bringing blessings to life.

(1) Everyone who has been to a temple knows that upon entering the temple gate, the first thing you see is Maitreya Buddha, smiling to welcome guests, and to his north is the fierce-looking Vajrapani. But according to legend, they did not originally reside in the same temple but separately presided over different temples.

Maitreya Buddha was cheerful and welcoming, so many people came to visit, but he didn't care about anything, dropping things here and there, and did not manage accounts properly, leading to financial deficits. On the other hand, although Vajrapani was good at accounting, he always wore a grim face, being too strict, driving away more and more people until the incense offerings ceased.

When the Buddha checked the incense offerings, he discovered this problem and placed them in the same temple. Maitreya Buddha handled public relations, warmly welcoming guests from all directions, thus greatly increasing the incense offerings. Meanwhile, Vajrapani, with his stern demeanor and meticulous attention to detail, was responsible for finance, strictly monitoring expenditures. Through their division of labor and cooperation, the temple flourished.

In the eyes of a true master of employing people, there are no useless individuals. Just as martial arts experts do not need precious swords, plucking flowers or flying leaves can cause harm, the key lies in how they are utilized.

(2) A person went to buy a parrot and saw one labeled: "This parrot speaks two languages, priced at 200 yuan." Another was labeled: "This parrot speaks four languages, priced at 400 yuan." Which one should he buy? Both were brightly colored and lively. This person paced back and forth, unable to decide.

Suddenly, he noticed an old, toothless parrot with dull feathers, priced at 800 yuan.

This person quickly called the shopkeeper: "Does this parrot speak eight languages?"

The shopkeeper replied: "No."

This person was puzzled: "Then why is it so old, ugly, and incapable yet worth this price?"

The shopkeeper answered: "Because the other two parrots call this one 'boss.'"

This story tells us that a true leader does not necessarily have to be exceptionally capable themselves, as long as they understand trust, delegation, and care, they can unite forces stronger than themselves, thereby enhancing their own value. On the contrary, many highly capable people fail due to perfectionism, taking everything on themselves, believing no one can match them, and end up being the best salespeople or negotiators but never excellent leaders.

(3) A works as a white-collar employee in a joint venture, feeling that his ambitions are not appreciated by his superior. He often thinks, "If one day I could meet the boss and have a chance to showcase my talents!"

A's colleague B has the same idea but takes it further by finding out the boss's schedule and calculating when he would enter the elevator. He also goes to the elevator at that time, hoping to meet the boss and exchange greetings.

Their colleague C goes even further. He thoroughly researches the boss's career path, learns about the school he graduated from, his interpersonal style, and concerns. He carefully designs a few simple but impactful opening lines and enters the elevator at the calculated time. After exchanging greetings several times with the boss, he eventually has a long conversation one day and soon secures a better position.

Fools miss opportunities, wise ones seize them, and successful ones create them. Opportunities only come to those who are prepared, and preparation is not just talk.

(4) A psychology professor visits a mental hospital to understand the lives of the patients. Throughout the day, he finds their behavior unpredictable and gains new insights.

Unexpectedly, when preparing to leave, he discovers that someone has removed the tire of his car. "It must be one of these lunatics!" the professor angrily thinks and starts to take out the spare tire to replace it.

The situation worsens. The person who removed the tire also took the screws. Without screws, the spare tire can't be mounted! The professor is at a loss. In his anxiety, a patient jumps over and approaches him, singing an unknown happy tune. He notices the professor's predicament and stops to ask what happened. The professor reluctantly explains the situation.

The patient laughs loudly and says, "I have a solution!" He removes a screw from each of the tires, thus obtaining three screws to mount the spare tire. Surprised and grateful, the professor asks curiously, "How did you come up with this idea?"

The patient grins and replies, "I'm crazy, but I'm not stupid!"

In fact, there are many people in the world who, having discovered the joy in their work, exhibit a passion unmatched by ordinary people, making it hard for others to understand. While many laugh at them as lunatics, others might be laughing at them as fools.

Being a bit naive in conduct but smart in dealings is especially advantageous in China.

(5) A Ph.D. joins a research institute, becoming the highest-educated individual there. One day, he goes fishing by a small pond behind the unit. Coincidentally, the director and deputy director are on either side of him, also fishing. He merely nods slightly, thinking, "These undergraduates, what's there to talk about?"

Soon, the director puts down his fishing rod, stretches lazily, and walks across the water surface like flying to the other side to use the restroom. The Ph.D.'s eyes nearly pop out. Walking on water? Impossible! This is a pond!

When the director returns from the restroom, he once again walks across the water surface like flying. What's going on? The Ph.D. doesn't dare to ask, considering himself a Ph.D.

After a while, the deputy director also stands up, takes a few steps, and similarly walks across the water surface to use the restroom. This shocks the Ph.D. almost unconscious: Could it be a gathering place for martial arts masters?

The Ph.D. urgently needs to use the restroom. There are walls on both sides of the pond, and it would take ten minutes to walk around to the other side. Going back to the unit is too far. What to do?

The Ph.D. doesn't want to ask the two directors, so after holding it for a while, he steps into the water: "I don't believe that what undergraduates can cross, I, a Ph.D., cannot."

Splash! The Ph.D. falls into the water.

The two directors pull him out and ask why he stepped into the water. He asks, "Why can you walk across?"

The two directors glance at each other and smile: "There are two rows of wooden stakes in this pond. Due to recent rains, the water level has risen just above the stakes. We know the positions of these stakes, so we can step on them to cross. Why didn't you ask?"

Education represents the past, but only learning ability represents the future. Respecting experience allows one to take fewer detours. A good team should also be a learning-oriented team.

(6) A says to B, "I want to leave this company. I hate this company!"

B suggests, "I fully agree you should retaliate!! The company must be given a lesson. However, leaving now isn't the best timing."

A asks, "???"

B says, "If you leave now, the company's loss wouldn't be significant. You should take advantage of your time in the company to win over some clients, becoming a key figure. Then, suddenly leave with these clients. Only then will the company suffer a major loss and become very passive."

A finds B's advice very reasonable. Thus, he works hard, achieving his goals, and after half a year of effort, he has many loyal clients.

Meeting again, B asks A, "Now is the time. Jump when you're ready!"

A smiles indifferently and says, "The boss has talked to me, planning to promote me as the assistant to the general manager. I don't plan to leave anytime soon."

In fact, this was also B's intention. A person's work is always just for their resume. Only when contributions exceed gains, and the boss truly sees your abilities surpassing your position, will more opportunities arise for creating more profits for him.
