Analysis of the Pros and Cons of Property Management from an Economic Perspective and Research on Countermeasures

Property management is closely related to the lives and work of the general public. In recent years, conflicts between property management companies and homeowners have occurred frequently, making property management a hotly debated social issue.

Keywords: Property Management, Homeowners, Operational Mechanism

It is generally believed that property management does not involve high technology or complex management issues but is merely a labor-intensive service industry. The question is why such a common industry has generated such widespread conflicts in its operations (intense conflicts between homeowners and property management companies have erupted in major cities like Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou)? The fundamental reason lies in the fact that despite its seemingly simple nature, due to the lag in theoretical research, there is a lack of profound understanding of property management. As of now, a set of effective operational mechanisms suitable for national conditions has yet to be found.

From a historical perspective, the emergence of property management in urban areas of our country is a product of housing system reform. Under the traditional housing system in urban areas of our country, residents' housing was almost entirely constructed and allocated by the state or their employers, with low-rent leasing. Correspondingly, the maintenance and management of housing were entirely undertaken by government housing management offices and employer housing management departments. With the continuous advancement of housing reform, more than 80% of salable public housing stock in urban areas of our country has been sold to individuals; after the cessation of physical housing allocation, the proportion of individuals purchasing new commercial housing has reached 95%. By the end of 2002, the total floor area of self-owned (private) residential buildings in urban areas nationwide was 5.683 billion square meters, with a self-ownership rate of 72.86%. Due to changes in ownership entities, this has objectively triggered adjustments in property management relationships, which are mainly reflected in two aspects: changes in the supply entities of property (service) management and changes in the fee-paying entities. Under the traditional system, the supply entities of property management were government-affiliated housing management offices (these housing management offices generally belonged to the municipal governments' housing bureaus and were established as public institutions) and employer housing management departments. Residents almost did not bear property fees; the required property fees were listed under the rents collected by housing management offices and employer housing management departments. If the funds were insufficient, housing management offices would apply for subsidies from public finances, and employer housing management departments would apply for subsidies from their respective units.

Residents directly purchasing newly built commercial housing did not initially involve housing management offices or employer housing management departments as property supply entities. However, as housing is both a consumer good and the primary asset and form of wealth accumulation for households, residents have objective service needs regarding the use, maintenance, and cleanliness of their homes. To meet these needs, since 1981, China has gradually developed property management services provided by enterprises as supply entities. By the end of 2002, the total number of property management companies nationwide exceeded 20,000, with more than 2.3 million employees.

Since property management companies adopt a service-provision model where they charge homeowners (housing owners), many conflicts have arisen in reality. In some places, there have even been incidents where property management company staff physically assaulted homeowners, leading to social tensions and instability. Conflicts between property management companies and homeowners during the transition period mainly manifest in the following aspects: 1. The contradiction between homeowners choosing property service consumption and property management companies forcing homeowners to consume. When the behavior of consumers accepting services does not constitute a threat to public interests, the acceptance of services is based on the voluntary principle of consumers. A homeowner's decision not to choose property service consumption does not pose a threat to public interests. Therefore, property management companies cannot impose services on homeowners. In reality, developers often establish or select a property management company while developing commercial housing, resulting in mandatory property management services for homeowners upon moving in. If the property management company provides good service, this provider-recipient relationship can continue; if the service quality is poor, it leads to conflicts; 2. Homeowners believe that property management companies charge excessively high fees or do not provide services commensurate with the price, while property management companies argue that low fees lead to corporate losses and inability to survive; 3. Improper custody and capital preservation methods for public repair funds result in harm to homeowners' interests.

This article attempts to conduct an in-depth analysis of the three main issues causing conflicts between homeowners and property management companies from an economic perspective, aiming to find solutions to these problems at the mechanism level and promote the healthy development of property management.

I. The Complexity of Ownership and the Selection of Property Management Organizational Models

Despite the seemingly simple property management issue, no effective solution has been explored over the past 20 years. The key lies in the oversight of the complexity of property ownership by both the academic community and operational departments.

The complexity of ownership in property management manifests in three aspects:

Firstly, the material complexity of ownership. Most (over 80%, over 90% in Beijing) urban residential properties in our country are multi-story (below six floors) and high-rise (above six floors) buildings. Their construction forms are essentially collective. The rights homeowners have over their houses include separately defined individual ownership of the house (the part inside the homeowner's door) and jointly owned spaces (the parts outside the doors of single buildings), shared facilities (common water supply and drainage pipelines, downspouts, water tanks, pressure pumps, elevators, antennas, power lines, lighting, boilers, heating pipelines, gas pipelines, fire protection facilities, non-commercial parking lots and garages, etc.), as well as joint land ownership. Due to the coexistence of individual ownership and joint ownership, the exercise of ownership by homeowners affects the rights of associated homeowners and is constrained by neighboring homeowners' rights.

Secondly, the complexity of the ownership system for existing public housing. Before public housing was sold, the ownership belonged to the government or the unit. After the start of public housing sales, situations arose where government ownership, unit ownership, and personal ownership coexisted in certain areas. Different entities have inconsistent preferences for property services. Representatives of government ownership hope that the housing management office will manage the property, representatives of unit ownership prefer the unit’s housing department to manage it, while representatives of personal ownership do not want paid property management and still wish to free-ride, or they hope to choose a property management company for management.

Thirdly, the complexity brought about by the process of ownership conversion. Our country is currently in a period of rapid economic takeoff and urbanization. To improve housing conditions and provide housing for people entering cities, a large amount of new housing must be built every year. Many residents purchase pre-sale properties, and property management companies face both the developer as the property owner and the post-purchase homeowner. The former selects property management for better sales, while the latter chooses it for comfortable and safe living. These two often conflict in selecting property management companies.

Due to the complexity of ownership, homeowners cannot individually decide on property service consumption; instead, they need to collectively decide through relevant homeowners to choose property management service consumption. There are basically four available consumption provision methods: First, continuing to have property services provided by housing management offices and unit housing departments, but this model no longer meets the needs of housing commoditization and socialization and has been proven outdated in practice; Second, having the government designate property management companies to provide services, which may work in specific times and places but is not suitable for comprehensive implementation according to government functional positioning; Third, allowing developers to select property management companies, which facilitates the connection between development and property management but is not conducive to providing homeowners with cost-effective services; Fourth, letting homeowners hire property management companies, which respects and protects homeowners' property rights. The "Property Management Regulations" announced by the State Council on June 8, 2003, takes the homeowners' property rights as the foundation of property management, clearly stipulating that all homeowners in the property management area form a homeowners' assembly, which hires and dismisses property management companies.

To safeguard common interests, due to the complexity of ownership, homeowners need to establish democratic consultation and self-management mechanisms through homeowners' assemblies. However, in specific implementations, this will encounter issues of high transaction costs, increasing operational difficulties. In a newly completed residential neighborhood in Beijing, with 14 high-rise buildings and 4,500 housing units, assuming each resident purchases one unit, there are 4,500 homeowners. Organizing a homeowners' assembly for 4,500 people requires finding a specific meeting venue and incurs high meeting expenses. Among the current 3,300 residential projects in Beijing, about 60% implement property management, and less than 300 homeowners' committees have been established, accounting for approximately 10% of those that should be formed. If 3,000 homeowners' committees need to be established and maintained in daily operations, the transaction costs will be quite high.

Additionally, according to the "Property Management Regulations," the homeowners' assembly elects a homeowners' committee as its executive body, responsible for six duties: convening homeowners' assembly meetings; reporting the implementation status of property management; representing homeowners in signing property service contracts with property management companies hired by the homeowners' assembly; promptly understanding homeowners' opinions and suggestions; supervising and assisting property management companies in implementing property management; mediating disputes among homeowners and between homeowners and property management companies.
