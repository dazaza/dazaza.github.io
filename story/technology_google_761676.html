Google Glass Creates New Business Model: Risks Shifted to Developers

by geekzhang on 2013-05-06 15:38:16

Introduction: A Forbes article published Friday and written by Haydn Shaughnessy claims that Google has pioneered a new business model with its Google Glass project. Through measures such as roping in investment companies for cooperative investment, selling developer edition devices at high prices, and attracting developers to create applications via crowdsourcing, Google has transferred the financial and development risks of the project to investors and developers.

Here is the full text of the article:

Google Glass is full of surprises. Beyond the novelty of being a wearable device, it has also spawned an entirely new business model. Google Glass was created through a collaboration between Google Ventures, Google's investment arm, and two other venture capital firms, KPCB and Andreesen Horowitz.

Prior to this, there were several existing commercial ecosystems in the market. Apple is a relatively typical example of such an ecosystem, with hundreds of thousands of developers creating apps for its iconic iPhone and iPad products. However, Apple's iOS ecosystem was not born overnight. Nine months after the release of the first-generation iPhone, in May 2008, Steve Jobs unveiled the software development kit (SDK) to third-party developers. At that time, the iPhone had already sold well and achieved initial success.

However, for the Google Glass project, there are currently no commercialized products on the market, so it is difficult to say that Google Glass is entirely the result of Google's independent innovation.

Exploring the uses of Google Glass through crowdsourcing

There are already similar wearable devices on the market, with Vuzix's glasses-style display being one example. This product, which looks quite good, is making every effort to avoid being seen as a pair of glasses. In addition, another competing product, Telepathy, is expected to hit the market before Google Glass.

However, Google Glass has so far only provided a developer version priced at a steep $1,500, which even for some developers is a relatively high entry threshold.

Nevertheless, Google does not seem to be in a hurry to launch a commercial version of Google Glass but is instead focusing on preparatory work. Google first launched a developer program and then held some competitions, such as the Google Glass Explorer competition. These moves by Google, on the one hand, acknowledge that they do not know what uses this wearable device resembling glasses may have, and on the other hand, aim to explore the application possibilities of the device through crowdsourcing.

Most of the crowdsourcing projects we have seen before already have ready-made products and markets, such as Starbucks' My Starbucks Idea and Dell's Ideastorm. In these projects, the organizers encourage public participation to improve and enhance their products or services. Google, however, gets people involved at the invention stage of the product, so if Google Glass can gain widespread recognition, its commercialization will naturally follow.

Google has skillfully and meticulously planned this model, and so far, it appears to be progressing well. Two months ago, Sergey Brin, one of Google's founders, still doubted whether Google Glass would eventually be launched, but today, Google Glass has become a hot topic in tech circles.

A new business model akin to lean startup

Now let us examine the various components of this new business model of the Google Glass project to see if it can become a universally applicable new innovation model.

1. Product concept - consumer head-mounted display. Is this professional head-mounted display suitable for the consumer market?

2. Verification - after a great deal of high-profile publicity, find out the uses of these products through crowdsourced competitions.

3. Rapid evaluation of ideas while maintaining product momentum.

4. Ecosystem construction - establish a developer community and build an ecosystem.

5. Financing - research and development funds are in place (these funds come from venture capital).

6. Announce tentative release dates - continue product development and continuously verify product usage.

Google's move is quite bold and could become a classic case of how to minimize financial expenditure and development risks to advance innovative projects with huge potential benefits. It is somewhat similar to the lean startup method, but because of its large scale, it cannot be classified under the lean startup category.

Transferring financial and development risks

The Google Glass project has done an excellent job in avoiding risks. Even after the invention phase, Google's own investment remains relatively low because developers (who pay $1,500 for the development board) actually bear part of the risk.

The main risk of the Google Glass project does not lie in the inability to develop suitable products leading to the failure of the project, nor in financial risks, but rather in Google's own reputation risk. However, Google has been prepared for this point, consistently telling the outside world that this is an attempt, wanting to explore various possibilities and develop an exciting product. Indeed, that is the truth. Therefore, even if the project fails, who would truly suffer losses?

General Electric (GE) adopted a similar mode to the Google Glass project in its Healthymagination initiative. In this project, GE pledged to invest $1 billion in health research within an open innovation environment. The first phase selected five teams to study new methods for cancer diagnosis, care, and treatment.

However, the scale of GE's Healthymagination and the Google Glass project differs. Google has already provided approximately 2,000 developer editions of Google Glass and is about to provide 8,000 to 10,000 pairs of Google Glass to winners of the "If I Had Glass" competition. Importantly, Google does not need to invest $1 billion in this project.
