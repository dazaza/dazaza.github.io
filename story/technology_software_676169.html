"Shade of Heaven" Novel Recommendation "At Eighteen, I Set Off on a Journey"

by sznstejx3q3 on 2012-02-25 19:55:40

The undulating asphalt road was like being attached to the waves of the sea. I walked on this mountain road, and I was like a boat. This year I am eighteen years old, and the few yellow beards under my chin fluttered in the wind. They were the first batch to settle here, so I cherished them especially. I walked on this road all day long, and I had seen many mountains and clouds. All the mountains and clouds made me think of familiar people. I called out their nicknames to them, so even though I walked all day, I wasn't tired at all. Thus, I went through the morning, now into the end of the afternoon, and I even saw the hair of dusk. But I hadn't entered an inn yet.

I met quite a few people on the road, but none of them knew where ahead was or if there was an inn ahead. They all told me: "You go and see." I thought what they said was too good. I indeed was going to see by walking forward. But I still hadn't entered an inn. I felt that I should worry about finding an inn.

I was surprised that I encountered a car only once after walking all day. That was at noon, just when I started thinking about hitchhiking. But at that moment, it was just a fleeting thought. At that time, I wasn't worried about finding an inn, I just thought that hitching a ride would be very impressive. I waved at the car from the side of the road, trying to wave as casually as possible. But the driver didn't even look at me, nor did the car. It flashed past me right before my eyes. I chased after the car for a while, but I did it just for fun because I wasn't worried about finding an inn yet. I kept chasing until the car disappeared, then I laughed heartily at myself, but I immediately realized that laughing too hard would affect my breathing, so I stopped laughing right away. Then I continued walking with great enthusiasm, but I began to regret not having put a big stone in my hand while waving casually.

Now I really want to hitch a ride because dusk is coming, but the inn is still nowhere to be found. However, I didn’t see another car throughout the entire afternoon. If I could stop a car now, I believe I surely would be able to. I would lie down in the middle of the road, and I'm sure all the cars would screech to a halt right before my ears. But now, I can't even hear the sound of a car engine. Now I can only walk forward and see, this saying is correct, walk forward and see."

The road rose and fell, and those high places always tempted me, tempting me to run up desperately to see the inn, but each time I only saw another high place, separated by a frustrating arc. Despite this, I ran up to the high places again and again, each time running desperately. Now I was running up to another high place. This time, I saw something, but it wasn't an inn—it was a car. The car was parked facing my direction, mysteriously parked in the lower part of the road. I saw the driver's high-up buttocks, illuminated by the sunset. I couldn't see the driver's head, which was stuck inside the car hood. The car hood was tilted up, like a flipped lip. Inside the car, baskets were piled high, and I imagined they must be filled with fruit. Preferably bananas. I thought his cab should also have some, so as soon as I sat in, I could grab one and eat it. Although the car was going to drive off in the direction I came from, I no longer cared about the direction. What I needed now was an inn, if there was no inn, then a car would do, and the car was right in front of me.

With great enthusiasm, I ran over and greeted the driver: "Hey, buddy, how are you?"

The driver seemed not to hear, still fiddling with something.

"Hey, buddy," I continued, "do you smoke?"

At this point, he exerted himself, pulling his head out, and extended a blackened hand to pinch the cigarette I offered. I quickly lit it for him. After puffing on the cigarette a few times, he stuffed his head back in.

So, I was reassured. As long as he accepted my cigarette, he would let me ride in his car. I started circling around the car, inspecting the contents of the baskets. But I couldn't see clearly, so I used my nose to sniff and smelled apples. Apples weren't bad either, I thought.

Soon, he fixed the car, closed the hood, and jumped down. I quickly approached and said, "Hey, buddy, I want to hitch a ride."

Unexpectedly, he pushed me with his blackened hand and rudely said, "Get lost."

I was so angry I couldn't speak. He leisurely opened the car door and climbed in, then the engine roared to life. I knew if I missed this chance, I wouldn't get another. I knew I had to take a risk now. So, I ran to the other side, opened the door, and climbed in. I was ready to fight with him in the cab. As soon as I got in, I barked at him, "You're still smoking my cigarette!" By this time, the car had already started moving.

However, he looked at me with a grin, showing unexpected friendliness. He asked, "Where are you going?"

"Anywhere," I replied.

He kindly asked again, "Do you want to eat an apple?" He was still looking at me.

"Do I need to ask?" I retorted.

"Go grab one from the back," he said.

Could I dare crawl out of the cab with the car speeding so fast? So, I said, "Forget it."

"Go grab one," he insisted, his eyes still on me.

"I said, don't look, there's no road on my face," I said.

Only then did he turn his head to look at the road.

The car drove toward the direction I came from, and I comfortably sat in the seat, looking out the window, chatting with the driver. Now, we had become friends. I had already learned that he was doing individual trading. The car was his, and the apples were his. I also heard the jingling of coins in his pocket. I asked him, "Where are you going?"

"Drive forward and see," he replied.

This statement was like something my brother would say, so intimate. I felt even closer to him. Everything outside the car window should be familiar to me, those mountains, those clouds reminded me of another group of familiar people, so I called out another batch of nicknames.

Now, I didn't care about any inn at all. This car, this driver, this seat made me feel completely at ease. I didn't know where the car was heading, and neither did he. Anyway, wherever it was ahead didn't matter to us. We just wanted the car to keep driving, so we'd drive forward and see.

But the car broke down. By that time, we were already the best of friends. I had my hand on his shoulder, and he had his hand on mine. He was telling me about his love story, just about to tell me the feeling of his first embrace of a woman, when the car broke down. The car broke down while climbing a slope. At that moment, the car suddenly stopped making noise, like a dead pig, and it stopped moving altogether. So, he climbed onto the hood again, flipped up the upper lip, and stuck his head inside. I was sitting in the cab, knowing his butt must be sticking up high at this moment, but the upper lip blocked my view. I couldn't see his butt, but I could hear the sound of him fixing the car.

After a while, he pulled his head out and closed the hood. His hands were even dirtier then. He wiped them repeatedly on his clothes, then jumped down to the ground and walked over.

"Fixed?" I asked.

"Finished, can't fix it," he said.

I thought it was over. "What should we do now?" I asked.

"Just wait and see," he said indifferently.

I was still sitting in the car, not knowing what to do. At that moment, I thought about inns again. The sun was about to set, and the evening glow was rising like steam. The idea of an inn re-entered my mind and gradually expanded, filling my brain in no time. My iron head was gone, replaced by an inn in its place.

At this moment, the driver was doing radio calisthenics in the middle of the road, from the first movement to the last, doing them very seriously. After finishing, he jogged around the car. Maybe the driver had been in the cab for too long and now needed to exercise. Watching him move outside, I couldn't sit still inside anymore, so I opened the car door and jumped out. But I didn't do the exercises or jog. I was thinking about inns and inns.

At this time, I saw five people riding bicycles down the slope. Each bicycle had two large baskets tied with a bamboo pole behind the seat. I thought they might be farmers nearby, probably returning from selling vegetables. Seeing someone coming down, I was extremely happy and shouted, "Hey, buddy, how are you?"

When the five riders reached me, they got off their bikes. I happily went up and asked, "Is there an inn nearby?"

They didn't answer but instead asked me, "What's in the car?"

"Apples," I said.

The five of them pushed their bikes to the car, two climbed onto the car, and then ten baskets of apples were thrown down. The three below opened the basket lids and poured the apples into their own baskets. For a moment, I didn't know what was happening, and the scene left me stunned. When I realized, I rushed forward and accused, "What are you doing?"

None of them paid attention to me and continued pouring apples. I grabbed one of their hands and shouted, "Someone is stealing apples!" At that moment, a fist struck my nose violently, throwing me several meters away. When I got up and touched my nose, it was soft and not stuck but hanging on my face. Blood flowed like sorrowful tears. But when I saw the strong man who hit me, the five of them had already ridden off.

At this moment, the driver was slowly strolling, panting heavily with his lips flapping. He must have been exhausted from running earlier. He seemed completely unaware of what just happened. I shouted at him, "Your apples have been stolen!" But he didn't pay attention to what I was shouting and continued strolling slowly. I really wanted to punch him in the face, letting his nose hang too. I ran over and shouted loudly into his ear, "Your apples have been stolen!" Only then did he turn and look at me. I noticed his expression getting happier and realized he was looking at my nose.

At this moment, more people rode bicycles down the slope, each bike carrying two large baskets. Some children were among the riders. They swarmed over, immediately surrounding the car. Many people jumped onto the car, and baskets of apples fell down one after another. Apples spilled out of some broken baskets, flowing like my nosebleed. They all frantically filled their own baskets with apples. In an instant, all the apples on the car ended up on the ground. At that moment, several tractors rumbled down the slope, stopping beside the car. A group of strong men jumped out and started loading apples onto the tractors. The empty baskets were thrown out one by one. By then, apples were scattered everywhere, and everyone squatted like frogs picking up apples.

It was at this moment that I threw myself in without hesitation, shouting loudly, "Thieves!" Countless fists and feet came towards me, and almost every part of my body got beaten simultaneously. As I struggled to get up from the ground, some children threw apples at me. The apples hit my head and broke, but my head didn't. I was about to rush over and beat those kids when a foot kicked my waist fiercely. I wanted to scream, but when I opened my mouth, no sound came out. I fell onto the ground and couldn't get up anymore, could only watch them plunder the apples. I started searching for the driver with my eyes. This guy was standing far away laughing at me, and I knew my appearance must be even more spectacular than my nose earlier.

At that moment, I didn't even have the strength to be angry. I could only watch with my eyes everything that made me furious. What angered me most was the driver.

More tractors and bicycles came down the slope, joining in this great disaster. I saw fewer and fewer apples on the ground, and people leaving and arriving. Those who arrived late started messing with the car, unloading the windows, the tires, and prying off the wooden boards. The car, with its tires removed, looked particularly deflated, lying there like it was crawling. Some children picked up the baskets that were thrown out earlier. I watched the ground becoming increasingly clean, and fewer people remaining. But I could only watch now, because I didn't even have the strength to be angry. Sitting on the ground unable to get up, I could only let my gaze wander.

Now, the surroundings were empty except for one tractor still parked next to the fallen car. A few people were looking around the car, checking if there was anything else they could take. After looking for a while, they climbed onto the tractor one by one, and the tractor started moving.

At this moment, I saw the driver jump onto the tractor too. He sat down in the back and was still laughing at me. I saw that he was holding my red backpack. He stole my backpack! Inside were my clothes, my money, food, and books, but he stole my backpack!

I watched the tractor climb up the slope and then disappear, but I could still hear its sound, but not for long. Soon, everything became silent, and the sky started getting dark. I was still sitting on the ground, cold and hungry, but I had nothing left now.

I sat there for a long time, then slowly struggled to get up. It was difficult because every movement caused intense pain all over my body, but I managed to stand up. I limped over to the car. The car looked terrible, battered and bruised, and I knew I was battered and bruised too.

It was completely dark now, with nothing around except the battered and bruised car and me. I looked at the car with infinite sadness, and the car looked at me with infinite sadness too. I reached out to touch it. It was icy cold. At that moment, the wind started blowing strongly, and the sound of the leaves rustling on the mountain was like the sound of waves, which terrified me, making me as cold as the car.

I opened the car door and crawled inside. The seats weren't pried off, giving me a little comfort. I lay down in the driver's seat. I smelled the gasoline leaking out, and it smelled like the blood flowing out of my body. Outside, the wind grew stronger, but lying on the seat, I started feeling a bit warmer. I felt that although the car was battered and bruised, its heart was still intact and warm. I knew my heart was warm too. I had been searching for an inn, never expecting to find it here.

Lying in the heart of the car, I remembered a bright, warm noon. The sunlight was beautiful. I remembered playing happily outside for half a day, then going home. Through the window, I saw my father tidying a red backpack inside the house. I leaned against the window and asked, "Dad, are you going out?"

Father turned around gently and said, "No, it's your turn to go out."

"Me going out?"

"Yes, you're eighteen now, it's time for you to go out and recognize the world."

Later, I carried that beautiful red backpack. Father patted me on the head, like patting a horse's ass. So, I cheerfully ran out of the house, galloping happily like a horse.

November 16, 1986, Beijing

(Selected from "Beijing Literature", Issue 1, 1987)

"The Journey Out at Eighteen" was written by Yu Hua, born in 1960 in Haiyan, Zhejiang Province. He started literary creation in 1984, writing dozens of short and medium-length novels such as "The Journey Out at Eighteen", "Reality One Kind", "World Affairs Like Smoke", "Mistakes by the River", "Crimson Plum Blossoms", etc. He has published collections of stories such as "Accidental Events" and full-length novels such as "Calling in the Misty Rain" and "Living". He is one of the representative writers of the new wave literature in recent years. Yu Hua's novels, from their narrative style, can roughly be divided into two categories: one mainly uses traditional realistic techniques but differs from traditional story novels. The other borrows various modernist expressions, disrupts the boundaries of time and space, creates mysterious atmospheres, restores the agitation of desires, represents psychological abnormalities, and expresses all sorts of strange feelings due to consciousness confusion. But whether focusing on realism or restoring sensations, Yu Hua mercilessly exposes the dark, ugly, cruel aspects of human nature. He writes about inexplicable premonitions, chilling death scenes, twisted, grotesque, bizarre human natures - all these plus the indifferent, cold narrative attitude suggest that Yu Hua has pushed the cold tide stirred up in the mid-1980s Chinese literary circle to a cold and ruthless depth.

Mo Yan once referred to Yu Hua as a "first clear dream-teller in contemporary literature", considering "The Journey Out at Eighteen" to be a "logically coherent imitation-dream novel". Indeed, the novel is filled with various uncertain, incomprehensible scenarios from beginning to end. The opening description conveys a misty, strange, floating sensation, making it seem as if in a dream. As the novel progresses, the dreamlike elements grow stronger: the sudden appearance of the car, followed by its abrupt breakdown; the locals rushing to steal apples, "me" getting beaten up trying to protect the apples, while the driver not only ignores everything happening but laughs joyfully at "me". The whole process is like something occurring in a dream, full of absurdity and incredibility. The brilliance of the novel lies in its accurate depiction of everything logically, yet effectively dismantling the meaning of the story itself, allowing the reader to experience a beauty akin to a dream created by paradoxical logical relationships and clear, precise actions.

Yu Hua once said, "Human superficiality comes from the limitations of experience and the alienation from the essence of spirit. Only by breaking free from common sense, rejecting the order and logic provided by the existing world, can we freely approach truth." This explanation undoubtedly serves as an apt interpretation of "The Journey Out at Eighteen", where we can also see the influence of Western modern literature and philosophical trends on the work. Absurd playwright Eugene Ionesco once said, "Absurdity means no purpose - humans feel lost. All their actions become meaningless, absurd, and useless." Yu Hua vividly reveals the absurd unpredictability of the world and the profound bewilderment of young people facing such absurd lives using an extreme "imitation-dream" method. The subtle moods dissected in the work during the protagonist's initial journey reflect Heidegger's existentialist theory that mood is the fundamental existence of humanity, and after being thrown into the world, humans first produce various moods. Artistically revealing the proposition of human existence. The stark contrast between the cheerful clarity of the initial journey and the dark ugliness of the absurd world in the novel gives it strong aesthetic tension.

Of course, the contradictions and paradoxes revealed in "The Journey Out at Eighteen" undoubtedly carry distinct national imprints, and the fluctuations in the youth's mood depicted in the work vividly reflect the psychological trajectory of the growth journey of contemporary Chinese youth. Meanwhile, the pure language and ingenious narrative techniques make the novel an independent aesthetic entity, and its intrinsic spirit exudes Eastern wisdom-style serene introspection.
