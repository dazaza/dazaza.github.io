Microsoft's Mobile Patent War Tactics: Scare Tactics, No Real Lawsuits

by geekzhang on 2013-05-10 15:02:17

In the technology industry, patent battles within the same field are not uncommon, but very few companies can completely destroy their rivals or establish a dominant position through patent litigation. Once involved in prolonged litigation, both plaintiffs and defendants will expend large amounts of time, money, and manpower, making even the final victory seem insignificant. Perhaps the only winners are the lawyers who earn substantial daily fees.Jobs once believed that Google had infringed on Apple's patents, threatening to launch a thermonuclear war against Google. In fact, after many years of patent disputes with Samsung and Google, Apple won a few lawsuits, but compared to the compensation received and legal expenses incurred, it was negligible. Companies like Microsoft, Qualcomm, and ARM rarely go to court but have used the patents in their possession to collect substantial licensing fees and consolidate their own positions, achieving victory without fighting.In the development of human society, the more frequent commercial activities become and the more advanced technology gets, the less likely wars become. Because in any ecosystem, double-loss or zero-sum games are discouraged. When the cost of conflict is too high, society naturally develops other ways to resolve issues, and the tech industry is no exception. Microsoft precisely uses patent strategies to avoid zero-sum games. (Note: Zero-sum game is a concept in game theory, where in strict competition, one party's gain necessarily means another party's loss, and the total sum of gains and losses for all parties involved is always zero.)Suing competitors is not worth itIn the past few years, the mobile industry has been filled with numerous patent lawsuits and counter-suits, seemingly as if all companies are suing each other. According to The Wall Street Journal report, apart from huge judicial costs, there is almost nothing in this industry that can be shown to us in terms of patent litigation.Not only are courts increasingly inclined to dismiss patent lawsuits, but even the companies in the mobile industry themselves admit that patent litigation lasts for a long time and often ends in settlement, or even the related judgments bring far less benefits than expected to the plaintiffs. In the technology sector, it often takes several years for litigation cases to reach a verdict, and regardless of what the court ultimately decides, it is usually irrelevant, unable to provide the expected returns to the plaintiffs. By the time the court makes its decision, the rapidly developing technology industry has already moved forward significantly.Therefore, although Apple tried hard to strangle Android in its infancy, the open-source mobile operating system developed by Google now still holds a dominant position in the industry, with its main licensee Samsung surpassing Apple in market share.Stanford University law professor Mark Lemley pointed out: buying up some patents and then trying to profit from them through litigation is an unreliable path. For this reason, Microsoft adopted a different strategy: threaten, but don't sue.Threaten and then profitMicrosoft has never been known as a company that frequently initiates patent lawsuits. Throughout Microsoft's long history, the number of times this company has gone to court can be counted on one hand, while Microsoft itself has had to pay more than $9 billion in compensation. Perhaps because Microsoft has suffered many defeats in court, the company has adopted a different strategy towards Google's Android. According to Reuters, Microsoft's revenue from Android patent fees currently far exceeds the patent fee revenue it obtains from its own Windows Phone OS. In fact, it is estimated that Microsoft's Android patent fee revenue in 2013 will reach $3.4 billion and will exceed $8.8 billion in the next few years.Simply sending a lawyer to threaten that Microsoft will initiate litigation using its patent portfolio and then earning substantial patent fees is indeed a good method.Microsoft claims that 80% of all Android devices sold in the current market pay patent fees to the company. Regarding this, Google spokesperson Matt Kallman stated: Microsoft has consistently implemented this strategy, this company does not engage in the development of great new products, but rather attacks competitors, attempting to increase the consumer price of Android devices.Who violated whose patent?A fact about patents is: all tech companies infringe on other companies' patents. For a long time, the US Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) has been granting patents for technologies of questionable quality, so much so that it is no longer just speculation: patents are to Microsoft as gifts are to Santa Claus.It is likely for this reason that Microsoft prefers to conduct its patent litigation in the meeting rooms of LG, ZTE, Asus, and other Android licensees. Behind closed doors, Microsoft can convince these licensees that they have infringed on the company's patents (which is quite possible), and the court will uphold justice for Microsoft (which is unlikely). For these licensees, the risk of possibly being sued by Microsoft is enough to make them sign patent fee contracts obediently, allowing Microsoft to quietly make a fortune without having to actually initiate litigation.Microsoft: We love peaceOf course, Microsoft's description of its own strategy differs greatly from Google's perspective. Microsoft Corporate Vice President and Deputy General Counsel Horacio Gutierrez said that Microsoft is the victim.He stated: In the so-called smartphone patent wars, many of the existing lawsuits could have been avoided if both sides were willing to fairly recognize the value of each other's patents. At Microsoft, experience tells us that respecting intellectual property is a two-way street. We are always prepared to respect others' intellectual property rights and have consistently sought respect for our own intellectual property. This is why we paid more than $4 billion in patent fees to other companies over the past decade; by paying these fees, we acquired intellectual property for the products we provide to users.It is questionable whether the $4 billion Microsoft pays compares to the patent fees extracted from the Android market, and it is also unlikely that Microsoft can obtain as much in patent fees through courtroom victories.Bravely resisting MicrosoftIn the server domain, Microsoft has also adopted the same strategy, attempting to extort enterprise IT and enterprise software providers. Although Microsoft claimed that Linux infringed on 235 of its patents, this strategy largely failed except for Novell, its partner. Over the past few years, Microsoft has constantly boasted about Novell, showing the world how responsible companies cooperate with Microsoft.Luckily, almost no one in this domain falls for Microsoft's tricks, and the entire industry's stance remains firm. Linux founder Linus Torvalds asked Microsoft to specify which patents were infringed upon and called on Microsoft to stop manufacturing noise privately. In the end, Microsoft had to compete using Windows Server instead of relying on patent litigation to generate income. The result is that Microsoft's Windows Server performs exceptionally well and does not need to rely on patent litigation to compete.If Microsoft could do this in the mobile domain, it would undoubtedly be a good thing; or at least, the mobile industry should bravely stand up and resist Microsoft.Going to court is indeed risky; however, from recent court rulings, this risk has been continuously decreasing. Courts dislike the strategy of competing through litigation; especially when the litigation is based on seemingly plausible claims and dubious patents. Moreover, Microsoft has proven that the company is not incapable of competing without patents. It is time to give Microsoft a chance to prove its ability in the mobile domain, rather than surviving solely on collecting patent fees.
