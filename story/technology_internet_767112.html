Jobs said: Let there be iPhone, and then the world changed - written on the second anniversary of Jobs' death

by anonymous on 2013-10-08 18:17:10

Editor's note: The master craftsman of an era has passed. It’s been two years since he left on the 5th. Under his leadership, Apple experienced some very glorious moments — every product launch event sent the company stock soaring, with Apple's share price peaking at $702 per share, making it the most valuable company in the world. The iPhone and iPad that he created not only transformed consumer trends but also reshaped the cultural landscape behind human-machine interaction, prompting us to rethink how humans and machines should coexist.

The New York Times published an article titled “And Then Steve Said, ‘Let There Be an iPhone,’” detailing the historic moments surrounding the release of the first-generation iPhone: frazzled engineers, rehearsals that never went smoothly, and even with only a hundred iPhones produced, each one had different issues... How did Jobs turn things around and bring this groundbreaking product to life?

The 55-mile commute from Campbell to San Francisco is arguably one of the most pleasant commutes in the world — nestled along the eastern edge of the Santa Cruz Mountains, stretching forward along the grand and spacious Interstate 280 (Junipero Serra Freeway). It's also an ideal place for a Silicon Valley entrepreneur to test the performance of their Ferrari. However, the downside is that it's difficult to get a good cell signal there, perhaps one of the worst places for reception. For Andy Grignon, however, it became the perfect place to be alone with his thoughts.

In fact, the route from Campbell to San Francisco wasn't Grignon's usual commute. He was a senior engineer at Apple, so he worked at Apple's headquarters in Cupertino, which is west of Campbell. Typically, he would drive just 15 minutes to reach the office in Cupertino. But today was an exception — he had to go see his boss, Steve Jobs, make history.

It was in San Francisco, where Apple's Macworld conference was about to take place. Prior to this, Apple fans had been pleading with Jobs for years to add calling functionality to the iPod, so they wouldn’t have to carry two devices when going out. Grignon knew that Jobs would fulfill this wish today. So he spent the night in a hotel near the venue with a group of colleagues and, at around 10 o'clock the next morning, witnessed alongside the rest of the world as Jobs unveiled the first iPhone.

However, as Grignon drove north, he felt neither excitement nor joy, only an unshakable sense of fear. He thought, weren't all product launches in Silicon Valley pre-recorded? Why would Jobs choose to risk having a historic moment ruined by poor network conditions and weak phone signals, instead of opting for a live broadcast?

But for Jobs, this was a non-negotiable choice. Live broadcasts of product launches were part of his charm — almost no one could find any minor flaws during his product demos, and Jobs was incredibly successful at this. What people didn't know was how much psychological pressure the backstage staff like Grignon endured.

Grignon was the senior manager responsible for the iPhone's baseband portion, a massive responsibility. Even though today's phones can have all sorts of dazzling features, their fundamental function is still to receive calls, and a phone is essentially a two-way communication radio device. Grignon's control over the baseband made the iPhone a real phone. If the iPhone couldn't make calls, or connect to Bluetooth or Wi-Fi, then Grignon would bear full responsibility for the errors. As one of the earliest engineers on the iPhone project, Grignon had already spent two and a half years — usually working seven days a week — on the iPhone project.

Grignon was also a member of the rehearsal team for the iPhone launch, but he rarely saw Jobs flawlessly complete a 90-minute rehearsal. Various problems always occurred, and even after five days of rehearsals, on the last day, the iPhone kept dropping connections, losing its network connection, and even crashing.

“In the early days, I thought it was so cool to participate in the rehearsals, like getting a medal of honor,” Grignon said, because only a very small number of people were allowed to participate in the rehearsals. “But soon the excitement faded, and everything became arduous — I rarely saw Jobs completely lose his temper, but when it happened, he would scream at me, 'You're ruining my company!' or 'If the launch fails, it's all your fault!'"

Looking back, Grignon said that Jobs was really nervous at the time, and he himself felt utterly embarrassed. He and the rest of the staff knew that if something went wrong during the live demo, Jobs would never take the blame for it himself. “At the time, it was like we had rehearsed the demo hundreds of times, but there were always issues, and it felt terrible,” Grignon said.

Both the launch and the rehearsals were top secrets at Apple. From Thursday onwards until the following weeks, Apple rented out the entire Moscone Center. Behind the stage, a more than four-square-meter electronics lab was set up to place and test the iPhone, and next to it was a lounge where Jobs could rest on a sofa. Apple placed security personnel at almost every door in the building, guarding the secrets inside 24 hours a day. Without Jobs' personally approved staff ID card, it was impossible to enter the building. Jobs was so concerned about leaks that he even wanted the staff entering the building, from setting up the booth to handling sound effects, to sleep inside without going out until the launch was over. His assistant stopped him.

Grignon knew that this iPhone launch wasn't just an ordinary product launch, but it might be hard for anyone to foresee that this launch would mark the beginning of a brilliant future for Apple — in the subsequent seven years, several generations of iPhones and iPads almost became the most important inventions in Silicon Valley's history. They transformed the mundane mobile phone industry and provided a huge profit platform for the software industry, generating over $10 billion in revenue from apps starting in 2008. They also changed the multi-billion-dollar personal computer industry; if iPad sales were included in notebook computer sales rankings, Apple would already be the largest P.C. manufacturer in the world. Last year, the combined shipments of iPhones and iPads reached 200 million units, more than twice the global car sales volume.

More importantly, the impact of these two products extended beyond economics; they even transformed the cultural landscape. Apple's innovations prompted the entire industry to rethink the possibilities of human-machine interaction, not just replacing the mouse with fingers but extending smartphones as extensions of the human brain. They also changed the way people receive and process information, cramming books, newspapers, phones, tape recorders, cameras, video recorders, compasses, TVs, VCRs, DVDs, PCs, games into people's pockets. Our ways of studying in school, doctors treating patients, travelers traveling, have all been transformed by them. Finally, the media and entertainment industries are exploring new paths.

However, the Apple before us today is surrounded by ten-sided ambushes. After Google released the Android system in 2007, it successfully defeated the iPhone and achieved its goal of becoming the dominant force in smartphones and mobile devices — by 2010, Android had become wildly popular, capturing 80% of the global smartphone market, while Apple's smartphone market share fell below 20%. A similar trend occurred with the iPad: In 2010, the iPad held 90% of the tablet market share, but now 60% belongs to Android.

What worries Apple fans the most is not that the company cannot provide answers about its future direction, as Tim Cook took on the heavy burden of Apple after Jobs' death in October 2011. People aren't worried that Cook can't be a qualified successor, but rather that no one can be a successor. When Jobs was still at Apple, it was like an innovation machine, producing countless revolutionary products every three to four years. He once told his biographer Walter Isaacson that his next revolution was coming — he wanted to change traditional television, but Cook hasn't made this vision come true, and even Apple's investors have started to lose confidence significantly.

In the past, every Apple product launch could send Apple's stock soaring, but after the release of the iPhone 5c and 5s, Apple's stock price dropped by ten percentage points. A year ago, Apple's stock price peaked at $702 per share, making it the most valuable company in the world. But now, the stock value has fallen 25% from its peak.

Actually, comparing anyone to Jobs is unfair. Over the two years since Cook became Apple's CEO, he has consistently tried to make Jobs understand that he cannot take Apple in the direction Jobs hoped for, but only in the direction Cook can lead it. This point doesn't need to be said — when you know how the iPhone became the iPhone, you will realize that it created user needs that didn't even exist, and such creators cannot be imitated.

In fact, the period surrounding the release of the first iPhone was fraught with twists and turns. It's hard to believe that Jobs in January 2007 actually took such a big gamble, a bet with little chance of success. Jobs wasn't just unveiling a new type of phone that Apple had never attempted before; in the past few months, the phone in his laboratory barely functioned properly. But he thought that even if this phone couldn't sell for six months, he still wanted the world to know that phones could be made this way, creating anticipation for such a phone.

To make this phone more refined, Jobs still had a lot to do — a complete production line hadn't been established yet, and the currently produced hundred iPhones varied greatly in quality. Some iPhones had noticeable gaps between the screen and the plastic edges, while others had obvious scratches on the screen. The software running on the iPhone was riddled with bugs. It could play some music or videos, but it couldn't stream them smoothly. You could easily send an email and browse the web, but if you reversed the order, the device might completely crash.

Finally, after endless trials and errors, the engineers on the iPhone team created a "golden path," jargon for engineers meaning a very specific set of instructions and channels to complete a series of predetermined tasks, making the iPhone look like it was running smoothly during the launch. But Jobs ordered his engineers not to use this method until the last moment, hoping that the iPhone could eventually run normally like a regular phone.

But even on the day of the launch, the software portion of the baseband that Grignon was responsible for was still glitching, and the software controlling the iPhone's memory was also malfunctioning. And no one knew whether the additional electronic equipment that Jobs requested would make the above issues worse.

This additional equipment was added because Jobs wanted the changes on the demo phone's screen to be projected onto a large screen behind him while he demonstrated it on stage. Other companies might have simply projected from above the demonstrator's head, but Jobs couldn't tolerate the poor results that would bring — his fingers would appear on the screen, and he thought it would ruin the entire demonstration. So he had the engineers spend several weeks installing extra circuit boards and video cables on the back of the demo phone. These cables connected to a projector, so when Jobs clicked on the calendar app icon on the iPhone, his fingers wouldn't appear on the screen, but the large screen would show what the calendar app looked like after opening. This amazed the audience because it seemed like each viewer was holding an iPhone themselves. However, to make this additional requirement work smoothly, the iPhone had many more problems, and it was hard to determine the root cause.

Meanwhile, the software portion of the baseband used for receiving WiFi in the iPhone was also very unstable. Eventually, Grignon and his team had no choice but to connect the phone to internal wiring on the stage to extend the phone's antenna, so the wireless signal wouldn't have to travel too far. But there was another problem: they had to avoid the audience competing for the same WiFi source, otherwise the demo phone's speed would be too slow. “Even if the WiFi ID could be hidden and not exposed when the audience's laptops searched for WiFi,” Grignon said, “there were still 5000 nerds in the room who knew how to hack into this channel.” Grignon finally modified the AirPort software to make it appear as if it was being used in Japan rather than the U.S., because the frequency used for WiFi in Japan is banned in the U.S.

The thing Grignon and his team were least confident about was ensuring that Jobs could make a call smoothly on stage. All they could do was ensure the signal was strong enough, and then pray. They got Jobs' approval to let the iPhone display five bars of signal regardless of the actual strength on-site. Although the probability of the baseband crashing while Jobs used the demo phone to make a call was low, it was difficult to guarantee that the baseband wouldn't crash during the entire 90-minute presentation. “Since we didn't want the audience to notice the baseband crashing and restarting, we had to code it to display five bars of signal,” Grignon said.

But none of these fixes solved the iPhone's biggest issue — once it ran multiple processes simultaneously, it would deplete its memory and need to be restarted to continue demonstrating. To prevent this, Jobs brought a stack of demo units on stage. If one machine's memory slowed down too much, he would switch to the next one, giving the first one time to restart. But when Grignon learned how many demonstrations Jobs planned, he began to worry that the possibility of failure due to too many items being shown was too high. Even if all the previous demonstrations went smoothly, it would certainly crash during the final spectacular scene — because Jobs planned to run all the iPhone's top functions on one unit simultaneously. He would play music, make a call, dial another call while on the first, email a photo to the listener of the second call, search the internet for something for the first caller, and then return to playing music.

“My colleagues and I were all anxious about the final part of the demonstration,” Grignon said, “the phones only had 128MB of memory, and since all the running software was incomplete, the apps were bloated.”

As everyone knows, Jobs rarely put himself in such a precarious situation. He was known for solving problems and pushing employees to accomplish seemingly impossible tasks. And he always had a backup plan, a Plan B, to steer back to safety before unexpected situations arose.

However, for Apple, the iPhone was their first entirely new creation, and everyone in the company revolved around this project. There was no possibility of a Plan B. “The outside world guessed that this time it might be either Apple TV or iPhone,” Grignon said, “if Jobs just brought the Apple TV to the Macworld conference and released a device that could run iTunes on TV, the whole world would say, 'What the hell is this?'”

Thinking about how his career-defining moment might end in disaster, Grignon's stomach began to hurt. Until 2007, Grignon's visible career had been spent at Apple or in Apple-related companies. In 1993, while studying at the University of Iowa, he and his friend Jeremy Wyld rewrote the Newton MessagePad program to enable it to connect wirelessly to the internet. Although Newton never became a mature product, it was still considered the first generation of mainstream portable handheld computers, and their achievement caused a sensation in the industry — they both got jobs at Apple because of it. Wyld joined the Newton team, while Grignon worked in Apple's famous R.&D. laboratory (Advanced Technology Group), researching video conferencing technology.

In 2000, Grignon left Apple to join Pixo, a company founded by former Apple software developers, to build operating systems for phones and other small devices. When Pixo's software was eventually built into the iPod in 2001, Grignon found himself back at Apple again.

Thanks to his experience at Pixo, Grignon mastered two skills outside of video conferencing technology: one related to computer baseband transmission equipment (Wi-Fi and Bluetooth), and the other related to running software on small electronic devices like phones. Grignon started moving towards a world vastly different from that of most software engineers in Silicon Valley. He thought that these engineers never considered why their code occupied so much disk space or overloaded the chip.

In the world of laptops and desktops, hardware is powerful, replaceable, and cheap, hard drives and processors can be upgraded inexpensively, and computer power can be plugged into outlets. But in the embedded software field that Grignon worked in, all hardware was irreplaceable, and if the code was too large, the software wouldn't run. Meanwhile, the batteries in the devices were small, supporting a laptop for only a few minutes. Therefore, when the iPhone project began in late 2004, Grignon had no doubt about his ability to participate in the project.

And at that moment, just before the release of the iPhone, he was exhausted deep down. He gained 50 pounds (about 22 kilograms) because of this project and was even facing marital stress. The entire iPhone team discovered that developing an iPhone was completely different from developing a personal computer or an iPod. “This was so exciting,” Grignon said, “the idea that it would be Apple's next big thing entered everyone's mind. That's why Apple gathered so many smart people in a crowded, narrow corner, letting them create miracles under immense pressure and various crazy circumstances.”

Notably, Jobs decided to create a phone only under "public pressure." Starting roughly from the release of the iPod in 2001, Jobs discussed this topic with Apple's core executives. The reasons were obvious: consumers wanted emails, phone calls, music, etc., and who would want to carry multiple devices if they could just carry one? But every time Jobs and the executive team delved deeper into the details of this idea, they found it extremely challenging, almost impossible. At that time, the chips and bandwidth in phones were insufficient to support people using them to access the internet or download music and videos. Email was a great feature on phones, but unfortunately, RIM's BlackBerry was quickly dominating that market.

More importantly, Jobs didn't intend to cooperate with any carrier. At the time, since the networks were controlled by carriers, phone manufacturers inevitably had to collaborate with them. But Jobs, the famous control freak, couldn't possibly follow the carriers' orders. Apple even considered acquiring Motorola in 2003, but the executives soon concluded that the acquisition would be too large for Apple. (A couple of years later, the two companies had an unsuccessful collaboration.)

By the fall of 2004, cooperation between phone manufacturers and carriers had become easier. Sprint started considering selling its wireless bandwidth wholesale. This meant that Apple itself could act as a carrier, known as a "mobile virtual network operator." Apple could develop its own phone while largely avoiding dealing with carriers. Disney, where Jobs served as a director, was already negotiating with Sprint regarding this business. Jobs seized the opportunity to ask many questions while contemplating whether Apple should adopt the same approach. By 2006, after over a year of negotiations, Apple finally signed a contract with Cingular (later acquired by AT&T). Compared to the over a year of negotiations, creating the iPhone was a far greater challenge.

Many executives and engineers initially believed that making a phone was equivalent to making a mini-Macintosh. However, between 2005 and 2006, Apple designed and built three early versions of the iPhone. An employee involved in the project stated that Apple developed six prototypes of the iPhone, each with its own hardware, software solution, and design tweaks. Because the development process was so grueling, some exhausted members of the team left the company shortly after the iPhone's release. "It was like the first moon landing," said Tony Fadell, one of the key executives on the project. "Not knowing some things in a project is normal for me. But during the iPhone development, the amount of new things you had to figure out was astonishing."
