TaylorMade TMX Stand Bag - "Most players won’t experience a dramatic reduction in spin from fairway lies, but will see a decrease in spin by up to 50 percent from the rough," said Wahl about the new conforming grooves. "All the more reason to keep the clubface of your wedge fresh and at peak performance." Normal wear and tear reduces the effectiveness of grooves, especially their ability to grip the ball and impart spin on it, making it harder to control finesse shots from the sand and the rough.

Exchangeable Face Technology, which we call xFT, is a system that allows you to replace a worn face with a new one using just a few twists of a wrench. The face is held in place by two screws on the back of the clubhead. To insert a new face, simply loosen the two xFT screws, remove the old clubface, insert the replacement face, tighten the screws with the R9 torque wrench, and presto! We know that most PGA Tour players frequently exchange their wedges to ensure fresh grooves on their clubs. The average recreational golfer might replace his or her wedges every few years, which makes those tough greenside shots even more difficult. Senior director of iron, wedge, and putter development for TaylorMade Golf, Bret Wahl, tells us that it's like rubbing a piece of sandpaper on the clubface when you explode from the sand with your wedge.

The TP xFT wedge with Z groove face will be available online and in stores starting December 1st, but only in the 56-degree version (with 12 degrees of bounce). It will retail for $129. Nine more combinations will follow in mid-February 2010, with lofts ranging from 50 to 64 degrees. Each wedge comes equipped with a slightly heavier than normal KBS High-Rev shaft, designed to increase spin due to its weighting.

The groove rule is, of course, the highly publicized USGA rule limiting the volume (cross-sectional area) and edge sharpness of the current "bigger" grooves. A Condition of Competition was adopted prohibiting high-level professional golfers (PGA Tour, LPGA Tour) from using the older grooves beginning January 1, 2010. Recreational golfers have until 2024 before they have to change, although all clubs manufactured in 2011 and beyond must have the conforming grooves. Before, if you wanted to replace your grooves, you had to go out and buy another wedge for $125. That was reason enough to make you hang onto your sand wedge for another season. But now, thanks to new technology and rules that allow for clubface adjustability (before the round), you don’t have to wait. Dirt, grass, moisture, and other debris accumulate over time and combine to wear down the face surface and volume of each groove, making that 25-yard pitch shot over a bunker to a tight pin play seemingly impossible.

So professionals recommend that the average golfer replace their wedges every 15-20 rounds, or about 1,000 shots. The xFT offers two different types of faces – a ZTP groove face, which conforms to the new USGA rule, and a Z groove face which conforms to the pre-2010 rule and is in play for recreational golfers until 2024. The ZTP groove is designed to provide maximum spin under the new USGA regulations, while the Z groove is said to generate green-stopping spin.
