Dianping: A Bone Too Tough for Giants to Crack, Living in the Zone of Discomfort

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:39:06

CEO of Dianping, Zhang Tao

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping, has postponed the company's IPO once again.

In 2011, after securing a $100 million Series C financing round from Sequoia Capital and others, Dianping’s valuation reached $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an appropriate time to go public within the next one to two years, but it didn’t. Instead, in 2012, it secured another $60 million in Series D funding.

Given Dianping’s scale and development momentum, going public would not be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that listing now would be too costly. He deconstructs the big internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point presents an opportunity for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is the seat for the fourth giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been working hard to accumulate data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where smartphones merge "location" with "services." Following Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping to occupy the imagination of the fourth giant.

But outsiders don't think so. In recent months, with BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent) frequently acquiring and laying out strategies, Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; regardless of which family name among BAT it adopts, it could help them achieve great success early on. On the other hand, Dianping's size is much smaller compared to BAT, and it's still too early to predict whether it can become a giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after its investment in Gaode and Meituan, all are directly targeting Dianping's territory—local life services centered around eating, drinking, and entertainment.

Before proving the following two things, Dianping's IPO would equal a discounted sale. First, Zhang Tao needs some time to prove that what Dianping does now, BAT cannot do. Second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "year of decisive battles." For speculation and rumors, Zhang Tao turns a deaf ear. He is very clear: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great integration, but Dianping will never be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is better suited to develop within a giant's ecosystem, such as maps and emails. These products can easily attract large numbers of users but find it difficult to establish independent business models. There are also browsers, which can make money, but the revenue earned through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money maintaining an email product because it helps with brand and user stickiness, but I haven't heard of any standalone email company succeeding." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, using acquisitions to expand market share. The third type consists of companies facing financial difficulties, selling themselves to survive.

Now Meituan has already been brought under Alibaba's wing. Theoretically, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated by its opponent's rapid expansion due to capital. Zhang Tao, however, says, "The giants can offer us limited help. We do indeed need users, but execution is even more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities, and we might even be marginalized by a large company." Furthermore, he wants Dianping to maintain the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would be akin to "choosing sides," making Dianping an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically, the acquisition of Dianping becomes a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why would we take their money? We're not short on funds. What we need are resources. If a giant is willing to invest a small amount and give us many resources, I could consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only hold a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for companies doing purely internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities come from those that intersect with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that life services mainly revolve around eating, drinking, and entertainment. Other细分 markets, such as ride-hailing, can form decent business models, but it's hard for them to grow into giants.

For eating, drinking, and entertainment, user needs are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, newcomers find it difficult to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Now group buying has entered the championship race, and Dianping will face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as there are two strong players, the industry's gross margin will be pulled down, making it impossible to form stable profits.

Another foreseeable future is reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but reservations must be based on Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts; otherwise, creating a closed loop will be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, both users and merchants provide good feedback, but if we were to do reservations alone, it would be hard to solve the chicken-and-egg problem."

Of course, life services present some heavier opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid demand for users, and merchants can increase sales. In the past couple of years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet" have done well. However, the core of food delivery is delivery—it must be delivered within a short time frame, and issues like soup spilling must be resolved. The logistics requirements are even higher than fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If food delivery is executed well, there is still opportunity, but this is harder dirty and hard work, and what Dianping is already doing is enough dirty and hard work."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has diligently accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, which Zhang Tao calls "dirty and hard work." This primitive growth model once restricted its speed, but past hardships have become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the life services field.

However, he still needs to be wary of BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps has already surpassed 100 million users, and users can use the map to search for nearby catering and other life services. Tencent, considered to have obtained a ticket to mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and expanding goods from physical items to services is a natural progression, especially since it has incorporated Meituan and Gaode into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there hasn't been an explosive growth in usage or data yet," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to eat, drink, and entertain.

Moreover, in the life services sector, which has immense imaginative potential, competition among giants will inevitably cross over, and no one can completely monopolize the field. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that this makes Dianping safer instead. "In细分 sectors like car rental and business travel, if giants redirect their massive users, they could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which invested in Gaode, wanting to encroach on Dianping's territory isn't that simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized no matter how peculiar. A user's experience of a meal depends on factors such as the day's environment, waitstaff, and chef. Managing merchants cannot rely solely on systems and tools like Alibaba uses; it must establish a 'ground force' managed by people.

"The three giants all want to walk this path, and there is imaginative space, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing life services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, something BAT lacks the genes and capability to manage offline teams. Second, they lack the willingness; life services require focused effort, not something a small team can accomplish, and the profits are slow. "A company finds it hard to do two important things well simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate their advantages in their original fields."

Weapon Two: Small Ships Turn Easily

When Dianping raised $100 million in 2011, Zhang Tao told the outside world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change quickly, and before Dianping went public, his analogies fell from grace.

He wasn't particularly troubled by this. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they want to be America's Dianping."

The current path was painstakingly explored step by step by Zhang Tao. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying lit up his eyes. Initially, he understood group buying as Americans did—as a marketing platform, one deal per day—but as orders increased, he realized group buying had transactional platform characteristics. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a life services transaction platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used to transform Dianping from an information platform to a transaction platform.

Without this transformation, Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence today to compete for the fourth giant's position. But the transformation sounds simple, yet in reality, it is equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, an extremely painful process.

First, compared to selling ads separately, transaction platforms lower gross margins and require stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, transaction platforms demand higher cross-department communication skills.

To maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and the sales team were completely separated. Zhang Tao even deliberately erected a "firewall" between departments—no matter how important the client, the information on the platform could not be modified for sales, which once led to mutual hostility between the content and sales teams. Now, as a transaction platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication to achieve a good marketing plan. Besides planning, editorials need to cooperate. He said, "Maintaining information quality remains important, but there's no need to reinforce enmity anymore; we need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors may logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and combined across the entire company, the inertia becomes even greater. Zhang Tao specially held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. After a few months, an editorial supervisor casually chatted with a sales supervisor saying, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needed transformation. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team a dual identity. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing plans. Second, for the company, marketing consultants are the company's buyers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety of goods must be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users buy group-buying products. Of course, dual identities will inevitably lead to a series of complex problems. For frontline personnel, this is quite headache-inducing, requiring them to balance the scales themselves.

Substantially speaking, Dianping's "dual identity" is very similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. By decentralizing decision-making power to the front line, the company can respond faster, but management difficulty increases. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's post-transformation management team suffers more.

Each year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, then executives further break them down layer by layer. However, decomposition is not simple addition or subtraction. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping, responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds that unmet merchant demands are due to sales issues but affect sales. Therefore, Li Jing creates a new position, setting up communication specialists who gather information daily from marketing consultants and submit it to other departments while tracking feedback. He often tells his team, embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change, and everyone feels uncomfortable when encountering changes. "All employees must go through discomfort zones, including myself."

Numbers reflect the heaviness of Dianping's execution team. Currently, among more than 3000 employees, over 2000 work offline. The ground force is responsible for directly communicating with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and bundled marketing solutions.

For this core team resisting BAT's clutches, Dianping has a unique management evaluation system.

They aren't called "sales;" from day one, their business cards bear the title "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the stage of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in their understanding of the internet. Marketing planning consultants must inform merchants of online users' needs and teach them how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing advisors wouldn't recommend a fish maw and shark fin group-buying package, perhaps switching to other dishes, because online users and offline users have different needs."

An outstanding marketing advisor must be an all-rounder. They must explain to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the fundamental differences between discounts and group buying and which discount aligns better with their positioning. If facing a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing advisor should know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise in this season, which fall, and propose a group-buying menu liked by both merchants and users.

This places high demands on marketing advisors, who must not only be internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every advisor to be a double expert, so we've set star ratings for advisors." Similar to hotels, marketing advisors range from one-star to five-star, with the best being Super Star.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing advisors, containing knowledge points and relevant cases that both new and old employees can browse. If encountering a new problem, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, and thousands of consulting advisors nationwide will help answer them.

A large number of knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees combining practical experiences. Dianping has set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base; employees who help others the most each quarter receive rewards. Each quarter, Dianping also conducts skill assessments for employees, with results directly linked to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to obtain than internet knowledge. Some experiences are only summarized by traditional merchants after years of experience in the industry. A Super Star marketing advisor told Li Jing that the beauty industry should avoid hair wash-cut-blow packages because boys lack motivation to buy, and girls prefer blow-drying without washing. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing advisors are divided not only by city but also by industries such as catering, beauty, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hotpot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing advisor responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing advisors can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, making merchants less resistant when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a certain type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, what product combinations are used in the solution, and what the effect is. Outstanding cases are reported upward, and Dianping officially shares one case per week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneurial Winner." Within the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential customer, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If they are responsible for hotpot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a commercial plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hotpot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees defend their presentations on-site, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, with the company offering certain rewards.

Strategically speaking, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, the tasks they need to accomplish are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with basic industry knowledge, and the words spoken are almost identical. "The era where storytelling and concept creation could ensure success is long gone. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
