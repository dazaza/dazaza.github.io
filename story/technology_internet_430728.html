Ignoring the effect of a small soft pillow for a long time will lead to fatigue.

by 13819310671 on 2011-05-13 19:24:34

Non-precious metal porcelain-fused-to-metal (PFM) crowns are a mature restoration technology, with a longer clinical usage history and relatively ideal restoration effects. Currently, non-precious metal PFM crowns are mainly used in China; however, over time, they may lead to neglect of the fatigue effect of small soft pillows. The disadvantage is that the marginal fit is slightly poorer, and a bluish or gray line may appear near the gumline, slightly affecting aesthetics. Occasionally, chipping of the porcelain may occur.

After using nickel-chromium alloy PFM crowns, some people experience daily neck pain. Over time, most individuals may develop blackened gingival margins (due to metal ion penetration), redness, swelling, bleeding, and shrinkage of the gums, affecting both appearance and dental health. Some people may also experience allergic reactions, impacting overall health. This type of material was widely used in China when PFM crowns were first introduced but is typically banned in developed countries. Therefore, we do not recommend young people or those needing anterior tooth restorations to choose this option. For posterior teeth, caution should be exercised. If economic conditions limit the choice to this type of PFM crown, it is important to select a reputable dental clinic and an experienced dentist.

1. Nickel-Chromium Alloy PFM Crowns: Using this metal as the base for PFM crowns, like cervical spondylosis, has poor biocompatibility. A portion of users may experience allergic reactions due to the slow release of nickel and beryllium elements, which can cause gum discoloration and oral toxicity, affecting human health. This material is typically banned in developed countries. The advantage is its low cost. Therefore, experts from the Wuzhou Digital PFM Crown Center at Shenzhen Dental Hospital do not highly recommend this type of crown.

2. Cobalt-Chromium Metal PFM Crowns: Physical properties are good. Since it does not contain nickel, biocompatibility is significantly improved, reducing the probability of gum discoloration. Cobalt-chromium alloys from Germany’s BEGO company are considered ideal, providing thorough treatment results. For patients with lower requirements, the restoration effect is still acceptable.

3. Titanium and Titanium Alloy PFM Crowns: Titanium has excellent biocompatibility, is non-toxic to the human body, and has good corrosion resistance. It is currently one of the better-developed metal materials. However, due to high production difficulty, further research is needed for clinical application development.

Cast ceramic full crowns, also known as all-ceramic crowns, are new full crown restorations made of castable ceramic materials. In terms of appearance and biological performance, they are superior to metal PFM crowns. The biggest difference lies in the inner crown. The inner crown of cast ceramic crowns is ceramic, a transparent skeleton, unlike metal-based crowns.

1. Transparency: This is an impossible task for PFM crowns, as the opacity of the metal inner crown requires opaque porcelain to cover the metal base, affecting the transparency of the restoration, making the PFM cosmetic result appear chalky, pale, and false, with a "black line" at the neck of the tooth affecting aesthetics. Cast ceramics solve this cosmetic problem with excellent transparency and refractive index, allowing for good aesthetic results even at the neck of the tooth.

2. Gum Health: Nickel-chromium metal, also known as base metal, severely irritates the gums. Some people (especially women) are allergic to it, causing gum swelling and bleeding. Some patients are even allergic to precious metals. Cast ceramics have not been found to cause sensitivity cases.

4. Inner and Outer Crown Bonding: The bond between metal and porcelain is weak, often leading to separation of porcelain and metal, exposing the gray-black metal. Cast ceramics, since both inner and outer crowns are ceramic, ensure the best bonding between porcelain and porcelain.

Zirconia all-ceramic crowns, due to their outstanding properties, are considered the new favorite among all-ceramic crowns. They are increasingly becoming the preferred material for people restoring PFM crowns. What advantages does zirconia offer?

Because of its excellent light transmission, similar to real teeth, and customizable according to individual patient needs, the color of the zirconia ceramic base crown is tooth white, so it will not turn black, dark, or bluish after being embedded for a period of time, solving the hardest problem of metal PFM crowns.

The production of zirconia all-ceramic crowns involves computer-aided design, laser scanning, and computer-controlled grinding, ensuring the precision of the crown and excellent marginal closure, making the porcelain crown fit well with the patient's abutment tooth, greatly reducing the incidence of post-restoration root inflammation.

Zirconia is an excellent high-tech biocompatible material, better than various metal alloys, including gold. Zirconia does not irritate the gums or cause allergic reactions, making it suitable for oral applications. Its thermal conductivity is extremely low, only one-seventeenth that of gold, providing better pulp protection. It is lightweight, with a density only one-fourth that of gold, making it more comfortable for patients to wear.

It has no metallic odor, and when exposed to air, water, or any electrolyte, it quickly forms a micro-oxidation film, not only preventing metal corrosion but also stopping the generation of metallic odors. Additionally, non-metallic zirconia does not block X-rays, meaning that if zirconia PFM crowns are embedded, there is no need to remove the false teeth for future head X-rays, CT scans, or MRI examinations, saving much trouble.
