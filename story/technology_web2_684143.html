The survival status of Russian billionaires

by anonymous on 2012-02-29 18:21:26

The watch worn by Alek Perov looked very ordinary, and there were no diamond cufflinks on his shirt cuffs. If he had any extravagant hobbies, they were out of the public eye. He lived comfortably but quietly. Like all billionaires, Alek Perov traveled in an armored Mercedes car with bodyguards nearby. But the private jet he took belonged to LUKOIL Company, the standard was the same as the chairman of a global oil company. What about Russia's LUKOIL Company in 1989? The answer is simple: like other companies evolved from the former Soviet Union industry, Russia's LUKOIL Company is the property of the Yeltsin family and the core family of Yeltsin. Now, Alek Perov's emotions are somewhat excited, telling stories: "I once worked there, that was my first job in the oil industry. When I graduated from university, it was an honor to become a petroleum worker. You can feel this in Baku, a city of petroleum workers, where there are things related to oil everywhere. My mission is to prove to the world that LUKOIL is as important as other Western major oil companies, equally significant in the global market, and this is my life-long responsibility." If Alek Perov's son gets emotional after Alek Perov's death, he might tell a story like this: "In 2030, Russia's LUKOIL will be as important as other Western major oil companies, equally significant in the global market, and this is my life-long responsibility." In July 2000, not long after coming to power, Russian President Putin convened a famous meeting at the Kremlin. He told the Russian oligarchs of the Yeltsin era that they could keep their existing wealth, regardless of how they obtained it, but they should not interfere in politics anymore. Most of the oligarchs agreed, after all, they were still very rich and still influential in all levels of government.

But some people openly opposed him. Among those who confronted Putin, the most famous one was Mikhail Khodorkovsky, the former president of Russia's largest oil company, Yukos Oil Corporation. Khodorkovsky can be called the paragon of the "new generation of Russians". He relied on speculation to dig up his "first pot of gold", and in the mid-1990s, under Yeltsin's tacit approval, he acquired Russia's oil gem - Yukos - for $3 billion through the "stock-for-property" method. The company and its affiliated industries had a market value of up to $32 billion in 2005, employing more than ten thousand people. As the fourth-largest oil company in the world, Yukos owned 14 to 21 billion barrels of oil reserves out of Russia's total of 49 billion barrels, ranking first among Russian oil companies.
