Breast hyperplasia is a common disease among women, and it is a benign lesion. Although some people believe that benign breast diseases may increase the risk of breast cancer, the probability of breast hyperplasia turning into cancer is generally very low. The probability of simple breast hyperplasia becoming cancerous is less than 1%, while the probability of cystic hyperplasia becoming cancerous is 2%-4%.

Clinically, breast lobular hyperplasia is closely related to the menstrual cycle. The prominent clinical manifestation is breast distension pain, stabbing pain, or hidden pain about one week before the menstrual cycle, which alleviates or disappears after menstruation. The pain worsens with breast movement or upper limb activity, and can radiate to the shoulder and back. It can occur in one breast or both breasts simultaneously.

The breast pain of cystic breast hyperplasia is not as intense as that of simple breast hyperplasia, but the duration of pain is longer, and the nature of pain varies. Generally, it manifests as distension pain, stabbing pain, or dull pain, irregular pain, which may be accompanied by nipple discharge. The discharge is serous, serum-like or bloody fluid. There are single or multiple cystic lumps of varying sizes in the breast, sometimes with gravel-like particles or membrane-like nodules, with clear boundaries and no adhesion to the skin.

Adenoid lobular hyperplasia is characterized by breast pain, with the same nature of pain as cystic breast hyperplasia. On physical examination, localized, tough, rubber-like hardness lumps can be palpated in the upper outer quadrant of one or both breasts, with clear boundaries and a painful sensation upon touch.

In summary, breast hyperplasia has three main characteristics: pain, tenderness, and nodules.
