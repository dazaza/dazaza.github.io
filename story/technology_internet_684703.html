Application of Pilz products in the packaging industry

by maipaishiye on 2012-03-01 09:49:04

With the development of automation technology and the increasing social demand, the requirement for the degree of automation in product packaging is becoming higher. Any automated packaging line inherently carries a certain level of danger. Therefore, it is necessary to implement appropriate safety solutions tailored to these automated packaging lines in order to reduce the risks associated with these machines, ensuring the safety of on-site operators as well as the safety of factory equipment.

In particular, in the food packaging industry, many factories have not only specialized product manufacturing lines but also dedicated packaging lines. Typically, the packaging process consists of three steps: 1. Filling or adding — filling finished product materials into plastic bags, cans, glass bottles, etc., such as bottle fillers, liquid fillers, bagging machines, etc. 2. Packaging or wrapping — packaging or wrapping plastic bags, cans, glass bottles containing product materials in cardboard boxes and applying labels. 3. Transporting and stacking — transporting cardboard boxes containing plastic bags, cans, glass bottles, etc., placing them in specified locations, and arranging them neatly.

Here’s an example of a chocolate production and packaging line. Chocolate is transported via a conveyor belt from the production line to a collection point (Stock). The chocolates pass through a channel where they are arranged neatly, for instance, in a 4x6 pattern. They then go through a tube bagging machine that wraps each piece of chocolate in a sealed plastic bag. Next, a robotic gripper places the individually wrapped chocolates into a carton packaging machine, which packages the sealed plastic bags containing chocolates into hard cardboard boxes of 20 bags per box. After passing through a series of channels, the boxed chocolates are transported to a palletizer, which stacks them into crates (100 boxes per crate), followed by a stretch wrapper that applies a protective film around the crates before they are stored in the warehouse.

The production speed of this chocolate packaging line needs to reach 100 cardboard packs per minute, which means the operation runs at a relatively high speed. Thus, the mechanical parts of the packaging line require 1-2 protective covers to ensure closed-loop operation, preventing injuries such as impact, entrapment, or cutting to on-site personnel. The robot and palletizing areas generally do not allow people to enter, but during maintenance, technicians may need to enter these areas for inspection and maintenance. In such cases, safety light curtains and safety doors are used to detect whether anyone is approaching these hazardous areas.

In the filling and bagging sections, there are potential dangers such as shearing, impact, and entrapment for on-site personnel. A risk assessment indicates that these sections should meet EN954-1 Category 2 or 3 safety standards. In these sections, we typically use 2-4 safety enclosures for protection, along with a certain number of emergency stop buttons.

In the hard box packaging section, pneumatic and electric actuators are used to lift and fold cardboard sheets into open cardboard boxes. This section usually requires 4-8 safety doors and several emergency stop buttons for protection, with a safety rating that meets EN954-1 Category 2 or 3. In some larger machines, safety light curtains are also used. Then, pneumatic and electric actuators load the plastic bags, cans, or glass bottles containing product materials into the cardboard boxes, wrap them, and apply labels. Similarly, this section often requires 4-8 safety doors and several emergency stop buttons for protection, with a safety rating that meets EN954-1 Category 2 or 3.

These sections pose risks such as shearing, impact, and entrapment for on-site personnel, requiring a risk assessment that meets EN954-1 Category 2 or 3. In these sections, we typically use 2-4 safety enclosures for protection, along with a certain number of emergency stop buttons.

In the palletizer and robot units, there are risks of collision and impact for operators. These sections are more dangerous, with a risk level reaching EN954-1 Category 3 or 4. Safety interlock doors, emergency stop buttons, and safety light curtains are required for protection in these sections.
