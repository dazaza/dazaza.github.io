Mobile payment will bring about earth-shaking changes in the way consumers pay.

by huiming588 on 2012-02-28 10:31:59

This week, we reviewed the five major trends in the first half of 2011. So far, we have explained four trends: online privacy, group texting, HTML5, and second-screen applications. In the final part of this series, we will discuss mobile payments. In 2011, this is a very busy market, with credit card companies, banks, telecom operators, Google, eBay, and a bunch of startups all vying to secure a place in this market.

One of the defining features of the next generation of smartphones will be a technology called Near Field Communication (NFC), and this technology has already started to make an impact on the market. When shopping at stores, you can simply tap or wave your phone using NFC technology to pay without having to take out your wallet. This technology has significant implications for banking institutions and the four major payment networks in the U.S.: Visa, Mastercard, American Express, and Discover. All parties involved are actively preparing for the revolutionary changes that are coming to consumer payment methods.

Sarah Perez, an editor from a U.S. website, wrote a series of reports describing the impact NFC technology would have in 2011. Below is her introduction to the technology:

NFC is a relatively new wireless technology that allows devices to connect over short distances. Data transfer between devices occurs via one of two methods: through short-range radio waves or, more commonly, by touching or tapping.

Some well-known phones already offer or are about to launch NFC functionality, including the Google Nexus S, Samsung Galaxy II (certain models), Nokia C7, Nokia C7-00, and Samsung Wave 578.

Credit Card Companies

All major credit card companies have been busy rolling out NFC functionality.

In May, Visa announced the launch of the next-generation electronic wallet service. This platform will allow consumers to create a digital version of their wallet. Consumers can load all their bank cards into this wallet, regardless of whether they are Visa cards or not. The wallet even supports merchants' membership cards.

Additionally, Visa partnered with Samsung this year to introduce NFC mobile payment at the 2012 London Olympics. Any user with an NFC-enabled phone will be able to make purchases at over 60,000 locations across London.

In April, we reviewed the latest Mastercard. They are working on SIM card-based solutions, embedded solutions, and continuing to deploy NFC tags.

American Express launched a digital payment and commerce platform called Serve in March this year and recently announced its first carrier transaction. Their new partnership with U.S. carrier Sprint will enable users of specific Android phones on the Sprint network to use Serve's mobile wallet app.

Mobile Banking

Banks are also accelerating the mobile payment revolution.

At the end of March, Wells Fargo launched an NFC mobile payment pilot program in San Francisco. This program uses Visa’s In2Pay microSD solution. Of course, other banks in the U.S. are also launching NFC-based mobile wallet services, including Bank of America, Chase, and US Bank.

Square and Startups

Like banks and credit card companies, startups such as Square (a mobile payment solution founded by Twitter co-founder Jack Dorsey) are also taking advantage of the mobile payment opportunities this year.

Square offers a device similar to a USB dongle that connects to the user's phone for payment purposes. However, unlike the other solutions discussed earlier, Square does not use NFC technology. This year, Square received investment from Visa and then in May, the company announced the launch of Square Register. The purpose of this service is to make real-world shopping as convenient as "one-click" payment on Amazon or iTunes.

As of May this year, Square had sold 500,000 smart card readers. Just in May alone, it completed one million transactions with a daily transaction volume of approximately $30,000. The company's goal is to reach $10 billion in transactions within a year.

Other products in this field include: Card.io (which connects credit cards to mobile phones for payment), Zoosh (which uses ultrasound instead of NFC), Zong (acquired by eBay in June, acting as an intermediary between merchants and telephone companies), Payfone (a company invested in by American Express), and Sage (a mobile payment platform for small businesses).

Telecom Operators and Google

The operator-led mobile commerce platform Isis is another heavyweight in mobile payments. It has established partnerships with Visa, Mastercard, American Express, and Discover. Isis uses NFC technology, allowing consumers to simply tap or wave their phone to pay at point-of-sale terminals.

If Google were not involved, it wouldn't be a true internet-related battle. Google also launched Google Wallet and its associated Google Offers service in May this year.
