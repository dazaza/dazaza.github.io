If you want to become a successful salesperson, if you want to become an outstanding leader, you need to become an excellent communicator. You need to be good at communicating with your customers and with your superior leaders. In fact, the reason customers are willing to buy your products or become your business partners is because they like you. Being good at communication allows you to quickly build rapport and friendship with customers.

If you lack communication skills, you will not be able to achieve great success. A person's ability to communicate determines the quality of their career, and someone who cannot communicate well will struggle to succeed.

Whether or not communication is done well is one of the key factors in determining whether people do things well or poorly. Lack of communication or poor communication can lead to many inconsistencies, conflicts, and difficulties. Good communication can effectively solve 99% of the problems in a career.

To become an outstanding communicator, the following six communication strategies can help you:

1. Make the customer feel important.

People generally want to feel significant and have a sense of high status. Customers want you to respect them, so make them feel like they are important individuals.

Customers prefer to talk about themselves rather than others. When you communicate one-on-one with a customer, to make them feel important, you should talk more about them and less about yourself. Customers are more concerned with their own success rather than how successful you are.

In communication, if you value the other person, the emotional distance between you and them will shorten significantly.

The great man Mao Zedong achieved remarkable success in his communication with others.

In the 1970s, Tang Youzhi became Mao Zedong's personal doctor for the first time, and during their initial meeting, he was quite nervous. Unexpectedly, Mao Zedong looked at Tang Youzhi and repeatedly pondered his name: "Youzhi, Youzhi..." and asked, "Is your name from the Analects of Confucius 'Guan Ke Shi You Zhi, Bu Ke Shi Zhi Zhi'?" He then said, "You shouldn't follow Confucius' 'You Zhi', but instead be passionate like Chen when flowers bloom and fall." Mao Zedong's witty remarks made Tang laugh heartily, and they began a harmonious conversation.

There was once a customer who bought a suit at a department store in Taipei. After wearing it, he was disappointed because the suit faded and dyed the collar of his red shirt black. So, he returned to the store and sought out the saleswoman who had sold him the suit, intending to inform her of the situation. Unfortunately, before he could fully explain himself, the saleswoman interrupted by saying, "I've sold dozens of these suits, and this is the first time I've heard anyone complain." Her implication was that the customer was intentionally causing trouble with dishonest means. During the argument, another saleswoman joined in and said, "All black clothes fade a bit at first; there's nothing we can do about it. For this price, the clothing won't be very good; it's due to the dye." Hearing this, the customer felt increasingly dissatisfied, even angry. He thought, "The first saleswoman doubts my honesty, and the second implies that I bought inferior goods." Feeling upset, he was about to confront them when the department manager arrived. The manager resolved the issue within ten minutes and turned the customer's anger into satisfaction. How did the department manager achieve this? Through three steps:

First, the manager listened attentively as the customer recounted the entire incident without interruption.

Second, after the customer finished speaking, the manager allowed the saleswomen to express their opinions and then argued on behalf of the customer, stating not only that the customer's shirt collar should not have been stained but also that the store should not make customers dissatisfied.

Third, the manager acknowledged the problem with the suit and expressed willingness to resolve it, concluding with, "How would you like us to handle this suit? We will certainly comply."

The department manager proposed a solution, allowing the customer to try the suit for a week. If still unsatisfied, the customer could return it for a refund or exchange.

After hearing the department manager's words and attitude towards resolving the matter, the customer's emotions calmed down. He agreed to try the suit for a week, and after a week, the suit indeed did not fade further.

2. Let the customer talk more and listen more yourself.

The most crucial step in sales is identifying the customer's needs or problems to be solved. If you don't understand where the customer's needs lie or what urgent issues require resolution, your sales efforts cannot proceed. To make a horse drink water, you must first make it thirsty; to make it thirsty, you must first feed it salt.

Understanding the customer’s needs involves not only observation but also asking questions, encouraging the customer to articulate their needs or problems. Encourage the customer to speak more; the more they say, the more information they provide, and the more control you have over the customer's information, the easier it is to succeed in sales.

As a direct salesperson, you may sometimes notice that when a customer leaves your home, they might tell their spouse, “I like this person; he is the most understanding person I’ve ever met.” Why? Because throughout the evening, the customer might have done most of the talking while you remained mostly silent. You were an exceptionally skilled listener, and it was your listening that won the customer's favor.

3. Talk more about “you” and less about “me.”

In the process of pursuing the infinite cause, especially in communicating with customers, as a salesperson, you should always play the role of a supporting actor or assistant, not the main character or president. Your customer is the protagonist and the president. You should be willing to be a green leaf to highlight the customer as the red flower, content to stay behind the scenes and let the customer enjoy the applause and accolades.

In the communication process, you should talk more about “you” and less about “me,” meaning more about matters related to the customer and less about yourself; empathize more with the customer's inner feelings and less with your own.

If you talk more about “me” and less about “you,” you may win a debate but lose the sale.

In the sales process, I have found that many salespeople commit the following offenses:

(1) Overusing sentences starting with “I” and excessively emphasizing the importance of the word “I.” For example, “I think the price is not high at all” or “I think the product suits you perfectly.” These statements clearly indicate that your opinion is more important and does not allow others to dissent.

(2) Habitually boasting about oneself. For example, “My product is top-notch. My product has never had quality issues.”

(3) Drawing conclusions based on personal experience. “This absolutely won’t work,” said with the air of an authority figure, making judgments based solely on personal experience while completely ignoring others' experiences.

(4) Confronting customers with threatening tones. “Are you kidding! You still think the price is too high at such a low cost.” This tone is intimidating, turning what should be a friendly inquiry into an accusatory confrontation.

(5) Making demands with commanding tones. “You’d better buy this set of products” or “You’d better sign the contract yesterday.” These words represent the speaker's desire to control the other party.

(6) Offering coercive suggestions. “You should do this” or “You must do that.” Such coercive language puts a lot of pressure on customers, forcing them into awkward situations where they have no choice. These words also reduce the listener's opportunity to judge and decide for themselves.

(7) Complaining and blaming others. For example, “Your spending concept has a problem,” “If you could arrive 10 minutes earlier, I could finish more tasks,” “Your attitude is entirely wrong.” Such generalized definitive statements severely damage the customer's feelings.

When talking to customers, unless the customer asks you to talk about yourself, avoid focusing on “yourself” and using phrases like “not far from me.” Instead, care more about the customer and their affairs, discussing topics relevant to them or those they find interesting. When discussing issues, listen more to the customer's opinions and avoid constantly saying “I think.” Even if you disagree with the customer’s opinion, use a consultative tone as much as possible, with phrases like “I estimate it might be like this” or “Do you think my idea is correct?”

For the above seven offenses, the correct way to phrase them should be:

(1) “What do you think?” “What do you consider appropriate?” “What kind of situation do you hope for?” Start with “you” instead of “I.”

(2) “Many people have used our products and feel they are good. What do you think?” Fully respect the customer’s feelings without imposing your own.

(3) “This method may not work. I believe you can see that too.” Ask for the other person’s opinion in a consultative tone.

(4) “Why do you think our product is expensive? I trust there must be a reason for your statement.”

(5) “If you buy this product today, we can offer you a more favorable price. If you sign today, we’ll give you a gift.”

(6) “If you have no objections, I suggest you…”

(7) “Sorry, if you temporarily don’t approve of our product, it must be because I haven’t explained it clearly enough.” “If I had scheduled you 10 minutes earlier, we would have had more time.” “If you express this attitude, it must be because there’s a problem with my communication style.” Take responsibility for the problem or mistake without blaming the customer.

4. Listen to what the customer is saying.

In studying the art of communication, I discovered that one of the essential foundations of good communication is showing genuine interest in others. Without this foundation, communication cannot proceed orderly, nor can harmonious interpersonal relationships be established to achieve sales goals. Listening attentively to others expresses your interest better than anything else you can do.

Most of us tend to make a common mistake, which is talking too much. In reality, your success in the Infinite Cause and your performance in sales depend more on how well you listen than on how well you speak. Listening is more important than speaking because speaking shows you are interested in yourself, while listening shows you are interested in the other person. Human nature dictates that if you value someone by one measure, they will value you tenfold. Many salespeople, while communicating with customers, fail to carefully listen but instead quietly wait for their turn to speak, thinking about what they should say next.

A person may appear to be communicating with someone else while actually thinking about something else entirely. Therefore, although they are hearing the other person speak, they are not truly listening to what is being said.

The following example illustrates this point well.

This story is about President Franklin D. Roosevelt. Even as the leader of a nation, President Roosevelt complained that no one truly listened to him carefully. One day, at a reception, President Roosevelt decided to conduct an experiment. As each guest approached, he muttered, "This morning, I killed my grandmother." All the guests responded, "That's wonderful! Please continue." "We're proud of you." "God bless you, sir." Only when the president reached the end of the line did Ambassador Paul Vibia truly hear the statement. The ambassador, feeling uneasy, leaned in and whispered, "I think she deserved it."

Now let's look at the four steps of listening:

(1) Stop. Whether visiting a customer or having a customer visit you, regardless of whether a sales action is involved, when communicating with a customer, stop what you're doing and give them your full attention. Even brief attention makes the customer feel that you are listening carefully and that you value them.

Someone once said, "Listening to others talk is like tuning into a radio station. To get good reception, you can only listen to one station at a time." I think this analogy is very vivid. Imagine trying to listen to two stations at once; all you'll hear is static. Reading books or materials while listening to someone talk is like trying to tune into two stations simultaneously, leaving you frustrated. Before listening, decide where to focus your attention. If you choose to listen to the other person, move anything that might distract you aside.

(2) Look. Face the person while communicating, maintain eye contact, and engage in eye exchanges. Appropriate facial expressions help encourage the other person to share their true feelings and thoughts with you.

(3) Listen. Pay close attention to the other person's words and tone, clarify the explicit and implicit meanings, and note keywords and concepts that help you better understand them.

(4) Speak. After stopping, looking, and listening, you should respond. An active response involves making appropriate comments or asking questions based on what the other person has said, guiding them to find solutions themselves.

Active listening should not just be passive silence but an active and cooperative engagement. While conversing with a customer, continuously assess their intent and use verbal or non-verbal cues to show that you are actively participating in the dialogue. God gave us one mouth and two ears for a reason. People who talk incessantly without listening are not well-liked.

There are various ways to listen. Some categorize listening into four levels:

(1) Listening with one ear. Listening with one ear sends two negative messages to the speaker:

① It indicates you are not fully concentrating, appearing distracted and inattentive, making the speaker feel you are not interested.

② It suggests you let information go in one ear and out the other, showing you don't value what the speaker is saying.

(2) Listening with a blank expression. Staring passively at the speaker without any non-verbal encouragement creates a dull and oppressive atmosphere, making it difficult for the speaker to continue.

(3) Appreciative listening. Nodding and responding with continuous affirmations like "uh-huh" lets the speaker know you are following their train of thought and encourages them to continue. However, mechanical listening without reflecting on and digesting the speaker's content or effectively feeding back your understanding diminishes its effectiveness.

(4) Active listening. Active listening is like dancing the social dance, where both parties are fully engaged and cooperate seamlessly. Active listening represents the highest level of listening. It provides more information, deeper understanding, and better communication results. Proper exchanges occur through active listening.

Active listening stimulates inspiration in both the speaker and listener, engaging both actively in the exchange. First, it requires the listener to actively comprehend the speaker's content and feed this understanding back to the speaker. Second, feedback from active listening helps the speaker clarify their thoughts, making the exchange more accurate.

Active listening conveys that the listener has clearly understood the speaker's content. Active listening requires coordination between the listener's body language and the speaker's body language.

Pay attention to the content you feedback, whether it is your understanding of their speech or their feelings. Regardless of the type of understanding, it should be expressed at the appropriate moment.

Below are five strategies for effective listening:

(1) Identify the key points. Repeating the key points of what the customer says ensures clarity. For instance, “I want to buy a house with three sunny sides.” Here, the key point is a house with three sunny sides. You should restate this in your own words and note it down.

(2) Acknowledge strengths. Praise the customer's preferences reflected in their statements. For example, “I want to buy a house with a larger study.” Here, you might praise the customer by saying, “It’s clear that you are a knowledgeable person who loves books.”

(3) Address weaknesses. When recognizing weaknesses reflected in the customer's statements, avoid using them against the customer but instead empathize and seek ways to assist them in resolving issues.

(4) Identify inconsistencies. If you detect inconsistencies in the customer's statements, use them as a breakthrough to persuade effectively.

(5) Sense emotions. People speak differently depending on their emotional state. In communication, guide the customer's emotions appropriately. In fact, customer emotions can be guided, and persuasion itself is a process of guiding emotions.

5. Provide appropriate self-feedback.

Communication doesn’t have right or wrong distinctions; it only has “effective” or “ineffective” distinctions. In the communication process, expressing ideas clearly and accurately is important, but more importantly, the information you convey must be received by the customer and elicit a response. If the customer doesn’t understand your message and doesn’t respond, the communication is meaningless. You control the communication effect, but the customer decides it.

An idea or statement can be expressed in many ways. When the customer fully receives and responds to your message, the communication is effective. Changing bad methods of expression can change the listening effect.

Conversely, when the customer speaks, what is important is not what they say or how much they say, but whether you are truly listening and responding appropriately.

Hearing what the customer says through physiological organs is one thing, but receiving and digesting the meaning of their words is another. Not only must you listen, but you must internalize what you hear, and not only internalize it, but also digest it to provide an appropriate response to the customer.

Effective communication occurs only when you can appropriately respond to the information received.

What constitutes an appropriate response?

(1) Nodding. Continuously nodding appropriately while listening shows that you are paying attention.

(2) Smiling. Maintain a smile while listening. Sincere smiles enhance your likability. Sometimes, a smile is more persuasive than words. Of course, your smile should adapt to the customer's mood and the content of their speech. When the customer talks about sad or painful things, you should not smile.

(3) Body language. Leaning forward toward the customer, mimicking their movements, etc., are good response methods. In terms of communication effects, sometimes body language is more important than words.

(4) Asking questions. You can ask the customer questions related to their conversation, indicating that you are actively listening.

(5) Explaining. Explain the content of what the customer has said in your own words to test whether you have understood it correctly.

(6) Reflecting feelings. When the customer expresses certain emotions or feelings, reflect similar feelings or emotions.

(7) Feedback meaning. Summarize the content and facts briefly in your own words.

(8) Bold imagination. From the customer's perspective, boldly imagine scenarios.

6. Praise the customer more.

The most beautiful language in the world is praise. Genuine praise can help you build good interpersonal relationships and pave the way for smooth sailing in your career. In a sense, praise is an effective emotional investment. Naturally, where there is giving, there is receiving. Praising your leader can make them feel pleased and pay more attention to you; praising your colleagues can foster harmony and strengthen unity, making competition more enjoyable; praising your customers can lead to more sales or cooperation opportunities, thus earning more profits.

A brilliant clothing dealer would often say, “Madam, you have excellent taste; this is the latest fashionable design here. Wearing it, you will surely look even more beautiful.” Such a few sentences can make the madam immediately open her wallet. American businesswoman Barbara Corcoran once said, “Praising your customers is more important than praising your products. Making your customers happy means you have succeeded halfway.”

There is a praise club abroad. This praise club has no administrators, no office, and no formal meetings. The main activity of its members is voluntarily giving sincere praise to others three times a day. They don’t wait for others to do something extraordinary or encounter a perfect person to praise; instead, they strive to find praiseworthy aspects in others' daily lives, even the smallest personal advantages and characteristics. In reality, everyone has their strengths and characteristics.

This method of deliberately seeking beauty in others' ordinariness and offering praise has produced miraculous effects on the club members. It shifts attention from oneself to others, reducing selfishness and arrogance. They become more perfect and humane, and their intelligence is fully utilized.

Giving roses to others leaves fragrance in your hands. Praise brings joy and a sense of achievement to others, while also benefiting oneself. Dr. Cran, the creator of the praise club, said that this simple method has worked wonders for people in distress, fear, and oppression.

Praise is the lubricant of your life and work. As a supervisor, if you need to criticize your subordinate, direct criticism may make them feel hurt and possibly resentful. However, if you praise first before criticizing, the effect will be different.

If you are a subordinate and need to advise your superior, direct advice may anger them. But if you praise first before advising, the effect will be different.
