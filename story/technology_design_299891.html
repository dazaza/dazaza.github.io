Relevant knowledge about cervical spondylosis

by jiznbecdsw on 2009-12-09 11:22:36

Office; Cervical spondylosis is a relatively common disease nowadays. It is no longer highly prevalent among middle-aged and elderly people, but rather more inclined towards younger age groups. Staff who are engaged in professions such as accounting, writing, editing, typing, and secretarial work for a long time fall into this category of office workers.

Cervical Spondylosis; The main symptoms of cervical spondylosis manifest as soreness in the head, neck, shoulder, back, and arms, stiffness and limited mobility in the neck. Cervical spondylosis can be divided into three stages:

(1) Early Stage: In the early stage of cervical spondylosis, there are mainly symptoms such as frequent stiffness and hardness in the neck, pain (as if suddenly pricked by a needle), and inflexibility. During this stage, the degree to which blood vessels and spinal nerves in the cervical area are "compressed" is very mild.

(2) Middle Stage: In the middle stage of cervical spondylosis, symptoms like dizziness, blurred vision, numb hands, palpitations, irritability, and weak legs may appear. At this point, the "compression" on the blood vessels and spinal nerves has become quite severe, and it could progress to the later stage at any time.

(3) Late Stage: In the late stage of cervical spondylosis, blood vessels may be "flattened," and the spinal nerves may be "cut off," leading to dangerous situations such as stroke, fainting, paralysis, or even death.

You can judge whether you have cervical spondylosis based on the descriptions of the above three stages, and determine which stage you are currently in.

So, how can we avoid cervical spondylosis?

For cervical spondylosis, during its mild phase, it can be prevented and treated through changing bad sitting postures and working habits, as well as strengthening exercises for the muscles of the neck and waist. Proper sitting posture should involve pulling closer to the worktable, adjusting the height of the table and chair to the best state suitable for your height; while sitting, keep your waist straight, shoulders relaxed, and try to avoid excessive forward or backward tilting of the head and neck. Your buttocks should fully contact the chair surface. Maintain an appropriate distance between your computer screen and eyes; when looking at the computer screen, avoid looking up, preferably look straight ahead, and slightly downward if necessary.

When focusing on close-range tasks for a long time, especially in a lowered head position, it not only affects the cervical spine but also easily causes visual fatigue, and may even trigger refractive errors. Therefore, after prolonged deskwork, you should raise your head and look into the distance for about half a minute. This not only eliminates fatigue but also benefits the health of the cervical spine.

During work breaks, frequently perform shoulder-lifting movements. Every 5-10 minutes, lift your head and rest by leaning back for a moment. Perform head and neck exercises 1-2 times daily, including forward flexion, backward extension, and left-right rotational movements. Every 2 hours, leave your desk briefly, stand naturally, place your interlocked hands on your neck, push forward with your hands while resisting backward with your neck. Each movement lasts 3-5 seconds.

Through the above introduction, you must have gained some understanding of cervical spondylosis. Since the cervical spine is located in an important part of the human nervous system, treatment is crucial. If you find yourself experiencing the aforementioned symptoms, seek active treatment, otherwise, severe cases can lead to blindness.
