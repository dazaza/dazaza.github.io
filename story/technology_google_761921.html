Google Teams Up with Universal and Sony to Take on Spotify Head-On

by geekzhang on 2013-05-16 16:58:46

In recent years, as music streaming services have improved, fewer and fewer users are willing to purchase records themselves. Applications like Pandora and Spotify have promising futures.According to Engadget, Google has signed licensing agreements with Universal Music Group and Sony Music Entertainment. Together, they will launch a subscription-based music service at the I/O Conference to directly compete with the rapidly growing Spotify.This information comes from a highly credible anonymous tipster. The tipster claims that Google plans to create a separate music subscription service within the Google Play store and YouTube, with an expected launch date as early as this week. This service will operate on a paid subscription model, allowing paying users unlimited access to a specific song library.It is understood that Apple has been negotiating with record distributors over music licensing issues, planning to launch a service similar to Pandora. Amazon is also considering entering the market. Currently, Google, Apple, and Amazon all sell digital music singles and albums.As far back as 2011, Google launched its own music service, which was part of Google Play. The service content only allowed users to purchase singles or albums. In terms of functionality, Google's new music subscription service is similar to Spotify, offering on-demand services with songs streamed to user devices. In comparison, Apple's online music streaming service is more akin to a traditional radio playback mode.Looking at Spotify and Pandora, Spotify charges subscribers in North America $9.99 per month and also offers free services supported by advertising business; Pandora charges subscribers $3.99 per month and also offers similar free services.Additionally, YouTube has been working hard to obtain licenses from record companies to launch a music video subscription service.It is reported that Universal Music's digital music sales have surpassed physical record sales, accounting for 54% and 46% respectively. Prior to finalizing agreements with Sony Music and Universal Music Group, Google also signed a music subscription agreement with Warner Music Group, the smallest of the world's three major record companies, earlier this year.With the collaboration of Universal Music and Sony, Google can utilize the resources of the world's two largest record companies, including music from singers such as Bob Dylan, Alicia Keys, Lil Wayne, Rihanna, and Jay-Z.Sony and Universal have both declined to comment on this matter.At last year's Google I/O conference, the Nexus Q priced at $299 was unveiled. Google originally planned to produce and release this product in the United States, but due to lukewarm user feedback, the release was postponed. It is rumored that Google may still be working on the Nexus Q project internally, but it is unlikely to be released this week.
