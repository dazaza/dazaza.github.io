"The domain name merchants in the 'Domain Name Capital' dare to act, buying and selling website domain names, there are many capable people in Xiamen."

by xwkj0983 on 2012-02-23 13:50:22

"huoche", "55", "tianmao"... To the average person, these are just ordinary combinations of letters or numbers. However, to some people, these letter or number combinations are worth several million, or even tens of millions of yuan, and they harbor immense business opportunities. The key is whether you have the "discerning eye" to uncover the value in these things.

For a period of time, the domain name of NBA rising star Jeremy Lin was preemptively registered, sparking a fierce battle over the trademark and domain name of Apple China's iPad, which led to a rush registration of more than 60 trademarks and domain names on Facebook. Subsequently, three major travel websites—eLong, Ctrip, and Tongcheng—all purchased double-pinyin domain names, creating a series of hot events that unveiled the domain name scramble of the Year of the Dragon. With the rapid development of e-commerce, preemptive registration of website domain names has become a trend, and has even become a new tool for making money.

Xiamen, one of the three most advanced internet cities alongside Beijing and Shanghai, enjoys the reputation of being the "Domain Capital." Xiamen leads the nation in per capita domain ownership, with domains registered in Xiamen accounting for more than half of the national market. Many ordinary citizens are also enthusiastic about investing in website domain names.

The reason why Xiamen, located in the southeastern coast, can become the "Domain Capital" is closely related to a group of renowned domain merchants nationwide who dare to explore and strive.

Cai Wensheng

Making Money from Domain Trading: He Considers Himself the Best

"There aren't more than 100 people in all of China who have truly made money from buying and selling domain names, and I should be considered the best." This is what Mr. Cai Wensheng, widely respected by webmasters as the godfather of personal websites and a famous Chinese angel investor, said.

He has the confidence. In China and even globally, when it comes to domain registration, Cai Wensheng cannot be overlooked. In his Sina Weibo account, his personal profile reads: "Born in the 70s," dropped out of high school and worked in various industries. "The 'piazza.com' domain was sold at a high price by Cai Wensheng," "Wholesale Network spent a large sum to buy the 'seller.cn' domain from Cai Wensheng," "80% of good city-related domains are in Cai Wensheng's hands"... A search of news related to Cai Wensheng reveals such messages everywhere.

At the age of 42, Cai Wensheng was born in Shishi, Quanzhou. Like many people from southern Fujian, he started his career in clothing business before finishing high school, and began doing domain trading in 1999, becoming one of the most successful domain investors in China.

In fact, Cai Wensheng's understanding of the internet could be described as late-blooming, but in front of the market, he was prescient. In 2000, he accidentally saw a news report stating that the 'business.com' domain was sold for $7.5 million. This immediately sparked his interest, leading him to an unstoppable passion.

Cai Wensheng is familiar with almost every story behind the domain names of all well-known Chinese websites, and many of these domains are closely related to him. For instance, 'g.cn' was sold by Cai Wensheng to Google China, reportedly for a price as high as several million dollars. Additionally, he has collected domain names for hundreds of local cities in China, enough to form a local portal alliance.

Zhuang Liangji

The Domain Merchant Who Sold 'Tmall': Formerly a Book-Loving Detective

"Universal Pinyin Domain Era" — this is Zhuang Liangji's QQ signature. Zhuang Liangji claims that most of the domain names he has handled transactions for are full pinyin, making him the "King of Full Pinyin Domains." Recently, one of the highest-value domain transactions, such as the sale of the double-pinyin domain 'tianmao.com' to Alibaba, was done by him.

Zhuang Liangji, a well-known domain merchant who has handled numerous domain names worth hundreds of thousands of yuan, hails from Jinjiang, Quanzhou. He used to be a detective. After ten years as a detective, Zhuang Liangji chose to enter the entrepreneurial world of the internet.

During his time as a detective, Zhuang Liangji had a great love for reading and "playing" with computers. In the 1990s, when computers were not as widespread as they are today, Zhuang Liangji was already a computer expert. His reading interests covered a wide range of topics, including philosophy, business management, archaeology, geography, history, education, psychology, and more.

"To make a living off domain names, one must have a very broad knowledge base and be involved in various fields. Otherwise, even if there's a domain name worth tens of millions of yuan right in front of you, you wouldn't know its value," Zhuang Liangji explained. For example, what does 'bharat' mean? Very few people know. Actually, 'bharat' is another name for India, similar to how 'sino' is another name for China. Most of India's largest state-owned companies start with 'bharat', so the domain 'bharat.com' is worth several million dollars, and this domain is currently in Cai Wensheng's possession.

Cai Liwen

The "Post-80s" Company CEO Who Loves Short Domain Names

"If you own hundreds of domain names, it's actually embarrassing to say it in front of professionals in Xiamen because there are quite a few professionals in Xiamen who own tens of thousands of domain names, and these domain names are all quite valuable." Cai Liwen is from Zhangzhou. Although he is only a "post-80s," he founded his own company back in 1999.

In Cai Liwen's view, a good domain name should first be short, because the shorter it is, the easier it is to remember, and thus the more valuable it becomes. Second, it should ideally overlap with words or trademarks, which would increase its value. The domain names that Cai Liwen is most proud of indeed meet his ideal standards for domain names, such as the domain '55.la' (Wula Wang) and '3366.com.cn' (Click Network). Wula Wang has become one of the largest free image creation platforms in China. "If the domain '55' had a '.com' suffix instead, its value would be unimaginable," Cai Liwen remarked.

He believes: "A domain name is like the engine of a car; its value depends on where the engine is installed—on a Hummer or a tractor. Therefore, a key factor in determining the value of a domain name is who the buyer is. The level of the buyer determines the value level of the domain name."

Extension

Domain Investment Is Not for Everyone

"Grassroots civilians trying to preemptively register domain names now probably isn't a good time anymore, because most good domain names have already been registered. You can only look for domain names with potential future market value, then buy them from their current owners and wait for their appreciation." Cai Liwen pointed out that some domain names may seem insignificant now, but as times change, these domain names might suddenly become popular.

Zhuang Liangji once owned a domain name called 'huoche' (train), and in 2003, someone approached him to negotiate purchasing the domain. Zhuang Liangji asked for 30,000 yuan, but the buyer rejected it. The next year, he raised the price to 50,000 yuan, and still no one accepted it. Then he increased the asking price to hundreds of thousands of yuan, yet still, no one took it. Eventually, 'huoche' was acquired by a large travel company for a price as high as over a million dollars.

Because of the perceived low investment and lack of too much technical content, many ordinary people are enthusiastic about investing in personal website domain names, hoping that one day the domain names they own will skyrocket in value and sell for a good price. "Actually, investing in domain names carries significant risks and requires substantial capital, which is not something everyone can afford," Zhuang Liangji stated.

On a domain registrar's website, the reporter found that registering a domain ending with .com, .net, or .org costs 100 yuan per year, while registering a domain ending with .cn costs 80 yuan per year. For example, Cai Wensheng owns more than 300,000 domains. Assuming each domain costs 100 yuan annually, maintaining 300,000 domains would require an annual fee of 30 million yuan, not including the cost of acquiring the domains. Such expenses are beyond the reach of ordinary people.

Mr. Xiao from Quanzhou invested in many domains a few years ago, but these domains have remained unsold. Mr. Xiao pays tens of thousands of yuan in fees each year without seeing any hope of cashing out in the future. Consequently, Mr. Xiao had to painfully let go of these domains.
