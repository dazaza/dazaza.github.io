"Magic Stones" revives our folk sentiment memories

by tianxiangyi on 2012-02-25 15:58:22

Watching the community fire, attending the ancestral worship ceremony, eating farmhouse meals, and visiting ancient villages. A group of nearly a hundred tourists traveled through the Stone Village, with many visitors exclaiming that it was "worth the trip." Amidst the lively atmosphere, some traditional folk customs and history gradually revived in the minds of us urban dwellers, and childhood memories became increasingly clear.

**Watching the Community Fire: Experiencing Heart-stopping Historical Legends**

At 10 a.m. yesterday, the tourists arrived at the Yu Family Stone Village. The Yu Family Stone Village community fire team, stationed in front of the large stage at the village entrance, had already prepared. As soon as the tourists arrived, the gongs and drums resounded. In an instant, those scenes that seemed to have disappeared in the city returned to people's eyes. A vividly colored rural painting unfolded before them.

Amidst the deafening sound of gongs and drums, four men dressed in black clothes with yellow hats and painted with black and white patterns jumped into the center of the stage, dancing rhythmically with the drumbeats, wielding spears and staffs, looking quite heroic. However, when the bearded man "Guan Gong" dressed in green robes, painted with red face paint, and sporting flowing black whiskers entered the scene, these four were no match for him. "Guan Gong," holding a large knife, fought one against four, engaging in combat with the black-clad men. His movements resembled a martial arts opera but were rougher than such performances. Several times, the spear tips of the four black-clad men almost reached "Lord Guan's" red face, yet "Lord Guan" did not flinch, maintaining a composed expression, truly embodying the phrase "I remain steadfast." However, "Lord Guan" was not waiting to be captured; after remaining steadfast, he would glance sideways at his "enemies," dodge, and continue fighting.

The community fire opened the eyes of the tourists from the city. Museum Director Ma proudly said, "Many villages in our Jingxing County celebrate the New Year with community fires. What you saw today is the painted-face community fire, also known as the red-face community fire. Now, the painted-face community fire from Taolinping Village in Jingxing County has successfully been declared a national intangible cultural heritage."

**Watching the Lahu Dance: Tasting Shy Love on the Road to Famine**

After watching the community fire, it was time to watch the Lahu dance. The Lahu dance performance troupe of the Yu Family Stone Village was urgently formed two months ago, with teachers from the county cultural center dispatched to teach the villagers step by step. Villagers who usually farm and work now hold fans and handkerchiefs and perform the Lahu dance. Their movements are slightly clumsy, but their shoulder shakes, waist twists, and umbrella lifts are done very seriously. After one segment of the Lahu dance, a story of rural love unfolds on the stage.

"The young man" holds an umbrella and fans, while "the girl" holds fans and handkerchiefs. The umbrella symbolizes favorable weather, the handkerchief symbolizes love, and the fan symbolizes peace throughout the four seasons. They gather or disperse, shaking the umbrella tassels into lines and waving bamboo fans into mountains, shaking shoulders, flipping wrists, twisting arms, and splaying feet. Their steps are graceful, blending strength and gentleness. Suddenly, the girls shake their fans to cover their faces, which Mr. Ma explained represents shyness in love. Then, the young men and women support each other, lifting their legs and splaying their feet, simulating climbing mountains, representing the rugged roads and arduous journeys during famine migrations. With every glance, movement, and smile, they vividly portray the shyness of young girls and the bravery and passion of young men. Although life is difficult, love always endures.

**Tasting Farmhouse Meals: "Minxu and Pancakes Are So Delicious"**

It was lunchtime, and more than 90 tourists were divided among three households. As soon as the group I was with entered the courtyard, we smelled the delicious aroma coming from the kitchen. "Wow, it smells so good!" one tourist's comment made everyone laugh.

The female host had just stir-fried sour cabbage and tofu, and the aroma was the scent of scallions being stir-fried in oil. Now, she set up a large iron pot and began making Minxu, a famous local snack. We watched her use a grater-like tool placed over the pot. She put the dough (a mix of black beans, corn, and wheat flour) on the grater and used a small iron spatula with a 90-degree angle to press the dough forward. The dough was squeezed through the small holes of the grater into the pot, forming short strips about an inch long and uneven in thickness. Once the pot boiled again, she scooped it out into bowls, poured the sour cabbage and tofu sauce over it, and served a steaming hot, fragrant, and smooth bowl of Minxu.

When the Minxu was served, it was greatly welcomed by the tourists. I tried making it myself and found it quite labor-intensive. The dough was soft but elastic, making it strenuous to grate. Most importantly, it couldn't be made ahead of time; it had to be freshly grated when eaten. By the time twenty or thirty tourists had eaten the Minxu, the female host was already drenched in sweat.

The people of Yujia Village were incredibly hospitable. Just after making Minxu, they brought out pancakes for everyone to taste. Thin, crispy, and salty pancakes were another characteristic local snack. The host also served eight dishes per table, including scrambled eggs with chives, sesame salted soybeans, stir-fried celery, and braised tofu. Tourists only paid 10 yuan to enjoy "four cold and four hot dishes," along with three types of staple foods: rice cakes, pancakes, and Minxu. Seeing the tall pile of mixed soybeans and the endless stack of pancakes, the tourists remarked, "The people of Yujia Village are really generous!"

**Visiting Ancient Villages: Experiencing Pure Rural Charm**

The Stone Village has six streets, seven alleys, eighteen lanes, and twelve narrow paths. This neatly arranged ancient village immerses you completely in a world of stone. Streets run east-west, and alleys north-south, but none of the streets are uniformly paved. Each family built their house and simultaneously organized the stone path in front of their door. Over time, these paths naturally connected. The houses on both sides cannot be dated precisely, but the moss at the base of the walls tells their age. On the walls, the well-preserved horse-tethering posts and stepping stones in front of doors speak of the status of the house owners. Among the tourists, some local villagers moved freely through the crowd, not deliberately performing but still living in these old houses. Tourists immersed in bustling cities showed great enthusiasm for the environment here. Several young tourists bounced around in the crowd, touching the walls and sitting on stone doorsteps, their adorable actions drawing laughter from the adults. "This place truly has winding paths leading to secluded areas," Mr. Wang said admiringly as he walked.

**Seeing Qingliang Pavilion: A Single Person’s Effort, A Naturally Perfect Miracle**

Qingliang Pavilion is the landmark building of the Stone Village. When the guide led everyone to the pavilion, the tourists vied to select angles and snap photos. The Qingliang Pavilion has a history of three hundred years and was completed solely by Yu Xichun, a descendant of the Yu family. What is astonishing is that there are over eighty stones weighing more than a thousand catties in the pavilion, and how he managed to build it remains a mystery to this day. The guide introduced several valuable features of the pavilion: First, it was built without a foundation, rising directly from the stone road. Second, it was constructed using dry stacking, with no mud between the stones, relying entirely on friction. Third, under the circumstances at the time, the precise positioning in all four directions demonstrated the wisdom of the builder in its rugged and wild style. Below the pavilion, there is a three-hundred-year-old bloodstone. It is said that the builder dropped blood from an injured finger onto the stone during construction. When the tourists saw the bloodstone, they noticed that the stone was indeed red, and no amount of scrubbing could remove the color. "The ancients were truly great, able to overcome various difficulties in harsh environments to complete such a grand project," one tourist said to the reporter after viewing it.

**[Fun Fact] Reporter Serves as Interpreter for Foreign Tourists**

During the visit to the Yu Family Ancestral Hall to observe the ancestral worship ceremony, amidst the resonant sounds of gongs and drums, the reporter noticed two foreign guests in the corner. They were led by a Chinese friend to participate in the grand ceremony. "Hello, sir. Where are you from?" the reporter asked as he pushed through the crowd. "Holland," one of the friends replied. "Oh, I know, Holland's football is great!" the reporter's words drew smiles and thanks from the two foreign friends.

During the visit to the Qingliang Pavilion, the reporter met two American tourists who marveled endlessly at the stone-built pavilion, taking photos continuously with their cameras and showing great interest in the murals inside the pavilion. The reporter told them that the pavilion was a one-man project, with a history of three hundred years, and was the landmark building of the Yu Family Stone Village. Hanging inside the pavilion were iron bells, and the reporter pointed to the bells and said, "You can try." One of the American tourists struck the iron bell, producing a crisp "ding dong" sound, drawing the attention of tourists below. "Interesting!" the two foreigners happily said.
