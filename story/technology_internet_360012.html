Jiang Jiasi: Building low-carbon cities should prioritize social benefits

by ditan360 on 2010-07-06 14:33:49

Exclusive report of Low Carbon Network: With the Copenhagen Conference and the two sessions of China, a wave of building low-carbon cities quickly swept across the country, expanding rapidly from so-called international metropolises to remote towns in the west. But what is a low-carbon city, and how should it be built? Many people disagree, and the means are varied.

The Sino-US Low-Carbon City Cooperation Project has a demonstration effect

Professor Jiang Jiashi, this Chinese scientist who was personally received by Premier Wen Jiabao, the first to bring environmental protection concepts into China, currently serves as the chairman of the Sustainable Development Technology Foundation and the vice chairman of the Global Urban Development Association. He has unique insights on how to build low-carbon cities. In face-to-face conversations with Professor Jiang, his vigorous spirit and sharp thinking made the reporter forget multiple times that the environmental celebrity sitting opposite was already approaching seventy.

Talking about low-carbon cities, Professor Jiang Jiashi emphasized that we cannot blindly follow trends; we must think carefully before building. Currently, the Sustainable Development Technology Foundation where he is located is actively promoting the long-term cooperation demonstration project between Sino-US low-carbon cities. This project was signed on October 23, which sufficiently demonstrates the importance the governments of both countries place on this project.

Jiang Jiashi explained that both China and the United States are among the world's largest greenhouse gas emitters and bear common but differentiated international responsibilities. As the world's largest developed and developing countries, both have an extremely significant impact on the global economy, and their economic development models are widely representative globally. Therefore, the success of both countries in developing a low-carbon economy will inevitably bring strong demonstration effects, accumulating experience for the global promotion of a low-carbon economy and laying a solid foundation for formulating practical standards.

Professor Jiang emphasized that the cooperation between Sino-US low-carbon cities is not a collaboration between two cities but the formation of a city alliance, primarily starting with medium and small cities. The project will begin with the organization of mayor capacity building, instilling cutting-edge concepts of low-carbon city construction into decision-makers. Once a city voluntarily participates, the project will organize training for local officials and experts at the executive level, understand the different needs and characteristics of various places, collaborate locally to formulate location-specific low-carbon implementation plans, and assist in executing specific projects to achieve measurable energy conservation and emissions reduction. The results report of the first batch of demonstration projects will be held before the Kyoto negotiations in 2012, hoping to showcase the impact of the Sino-US low-carbon city cooperation project on climate change and economic development during the post-Kyoto negotiations.

When asked why medium and small cities were chosen as demonstration cities, Jiang Jiashi explained that most development projects are concentrated in large cities, and the emission reduction effects are often difficult to quantify clearly due to the complexity of large cities. The reason why the proposed Sino-US low-carbon city cooperation demonstration project suggests starting with medium and small cities or specific regions is that it can more effectively quantify the emission reduction effects and formulate emission reduction standards. Through the establishment of Sino-US low-carbon city demonstration zones, innovation bases mainly focused on low-carbon economic development can be formed, attracting more talents, funds, and technologies, gradually realizing a new economic development model centered on knowledge. Low-carbon new energy products will also be rapidly promoted worldwide due to the cooperation of Sino-US low-carbon cities. Long-term cooperation in the development of Sino-US low-carbon cities will not only benefit the two countries responsible for 40% of global emissions but also send a strong signal to the world, setting an example. Through Sino-US low-carbon city cooperation, low-carbon city development in the post-Kyoto era is an effective way for sustainable economic development, reducing people's negative attitudes towards climate change negotiations and providing references for post-Kyoto negotiations.

The Sustainable Development Technology Foundation and the Energy Research Institute of the National Development and Reform Commission will host the second "Low Carbon Urban Development Forum" and the "Second Sino-US Low Carbon City Mayor Seminar" from July 16-18, 2010. This will become a long-term cooperative exchange network platform for cities, enterprises, and academia in Sino-US and cross-strait low-carbon city construction. In September, the organization will continue to host the "Sino-US Clean Energy Practical Cooperation Strategy Forum" to further promote cooperation between both sides.

New energy must be combined with traditional industries

Jiang Jiashi believes that new energy sources like wind and solar power are actually more expensive than traditional energy sources, so how to combine traditional energy and traditional technology with new energy and new technology is a question worth deep thought.

For instance, the solar energy industry is one of the short-term effective measures for low-carbon city construction and economic recovery. Solar energy is the most promising new energy source, but the cost of solar technology mainly based on monocrystalline and polycrystalline silicon remains very high, and the extraction of raw materials severely pollutes the environment, somewhat contradicting the original intention of developing new energy. China is an agricultural powerhouse, so if solar energy is combined with agriculture, it can not only fully utilize new energy but also solve employment issues, increase farmers' income, address the three rural issues, thereby bringing social stability, thus generating many social benefits.

For example, in Yanqing, there is an ecological park covering over a thousand acres. We can use this to build solar organic agricultural greenhouses, using solar photovoltaic power generation to heat and warm the greenhouses, further increasing vegetable production and extending the production cycle. At the same time, it can also be used to solve lighting, irrigation, pest control, and other issues, significantly increasing farmers' income. Preliminary estimates show that using solar power generation for heating can double the yield and benefits of vegetable greenhouses, ensuring year-round production and supply, which is crucial for increasing farmers' income and solving urban residents' food basket problems.

Additionally, most solar industry competitions focus on the upstream market, and national economic aid plans are limited to integrated solar power generation and construction, ignoring the importance of downstream product development driven by solar energy. Developing the downstream solar industry will bring sustainable economic benefits to the solar industry, supporting the development of the upstream industry, especially the commercialization of solar film technology in downstream markets such as streetlights, bicycles, wheelchairs, water carts, and water pumps, alleviating the high cost pressure of upstream product development and injecting vitality into the solar industry.

For example, currently, when people visit the Great Wall at Badaling, they usually return on the same day. If LED is used to create a night view of the Great Wall, some visitors may choose to stay overnight locally, thus boosting local tourism, increasing farmers' income, and promoting the tertiary industry.

Building a low-carbon city follows four elements

Jiang Jiashi believes that China needs to transition from an economy dominated by manufacturing to one dominated by knowledge. In the process of China's economic transformation, low-carbon urbanization could become a common strategic choice for China and the United States in the future. A low-carbon city must have transparency, innovation, intellectual property rights, and compliance with laws and regulations, all of which are necessary factors. However, constructing all these elements takes a long time and cannot be achieved overnight.

Simply put, continuous innovation is essential because in the past 30 years, China has been the world's manufacturing hub, but many intellectual properties belong to foreign entities, and many Chinese manufacturing industries lack innovation. Without innovation, it is difficult to develop. To respect innovation, you must respect intellectual property rights; otherwise, there will be no innovation. To have intellectual property rights, there must be a legal system, and transparency must be guaranteed. These are key points of a knowledge-based economy, possessing them can drive new civilizations. Without innovation, it is hard to promote China's manufacturing industry, and the climate crisis will become increasingly severe.

Professor Jiang Jiashi served as the founding dean of Peking University's Environmental College until 2006 and as the chairman of Peking University's Environmental Fund until 2009. During his 30 years in the U.S., he served as a member of the U.S. National Academy of Sciences' "Global Climate Change (China)" and "30-Year Review of American Atmospheric Management" committees and was the chairman of the Department of Geophysics at Georgia Tech from 1981 to 1988. From 1991 to 1993, he founded and served as the director of the Environmental Science, Technology, and Policy Research Center at Georgia Tech.

Professor Jiang Jiashi is the chairman of the Sustainable Development Technology Foundation and the vice chairman of the Global Urban Development Association, a member of the International Advisory Committee of the Board of Directors of the King Abdullah Petroleum Studies and Research Center in Saudi Arabia, a World Future Parliamentarian, a member of the Global Agenda Council on Climate Change, Water Resources, and Clean Energy of the World Economic Forum, and an advisor to several international non-governmental organizations and companies such as the East-West Center and The Elders.
