Zhu Ziqing (1898-1948), originally named Zi Hua, with the style name Qiu Shi, later changed his name to Zi Qing, and his courtesy name was Pei Xian; a native of Shaoxing, Zhejiang Province; he was born in Donghai, Jiangsu Province; a modern famous essayist, poet, scholar, and democratic fighter; his essays are characterized by simplicity and meticulousness, clear elegance and solemnity, refined language, elegant writing style, and rich sincerity. With his unique artistic style of prose, Zhu Ziqing added a splendid color to Chinese modern prose. To establish a completely new aesthetic feature of Chinese modern prose and create a prose system with Chinese national characteristics, his main works include "Searching for the Morning", "Traces", "The Back View", "European Travel Notes", "You and I", "Guidance Examples for Intensive Reading", "Guidance Examples for Extensive Reading", "Chinese Language Teaching", "Differentiation of Poetic Expression", "New Poetry Miscellaneous Talks", "Standards and Scales", "On the Appreciation of Elegance and Vulgarity".

Person Introduction

Mr. Zhu Ziqing

Zhu Ziqing (November 22, 1898 - August 12, 1948), Han nationality, a modern Chinese essayist, poet, literary researcher, democratic fighter, Chinese language educator, and scholar; originally named Zhu Zi Hua, style name Qiu Shi, courtesy name Pei Xian, later changed his name to Zhu Zi Qing; pen names included Yu Jie, Bai Shui, Bo Xiang, Zhi Bai, etc.

Born on November 22, 1898, in Donghai (now Haizhou District, Lianyungang City, Jiangsu Province); as his grandfather and father had long settled in Yangzhou, he referred to himself as "a person from Yangzhou" (actually from Shaoxing, Zhejiang).

Zhu Ziqing graduated from the high school department of Jiangsu No. 8 Middle School in Yangzhou (now Yangzhou Middle School) and became a teacher in Yangzhou (his grandson now resides in Yangzhou and is engaged in cultural work); he also taught at Taizhou Middle School in Zhejiang Province, where he personally planted wisteria flowers that later became the school flower.

Zhu Ziqing's grandfather Zhu Ze Yu, styled Ju Po, originally surnamed Yu, but adopted the Zhu surname; he was cautious and strict, serving as an adjudicator in Donghai County, Jiangsu Province, for more than ten years during the Qing Guangxu period; Zhu Ziqing spent thirteen years of his life in Haizhou Ancient City, passing through his childhood and adolescence there—his feelings about this part of his life were subtle and complex. Perhaps due to the dullness of life, he later said that his childhood memories only left behind "thin shadows," "as if washed away by great water, lonely to an alarming degree!" However, on the long winding road of life, childhood remains the first "station."

Father named Hong Jun, courtesy name Xiao Po, married Zhou family, was a scholar; in 1901 (the 27th year of Guangxu), Zhu Hong Jun went to Shaobo Town under the jurisdiction of Yangzhou Prefecture. Two years later, the whole family moved to Yangzhou City, thus settling down in Yangzhou.

Young Zhu Ziqing studied in private schools, deeply influenced by traditional Chinese culture; in 1912, he entered higher elementary school, and in 1916 successfully passed the entrance examination to enter the preparatory department of Peking University, publishing his maiden poetry collection "Sleep Well, Little One" in February 1919, graduating from the philosophy department of Peking University in 1920.

In August 1931, Zhu Ziqing studied abroad in the UK, studying linguistics and British literature; later he traveled around five European countries; in July 1932, he returned to China and served as the head of the Chinese Literature Department at Tsinghua University, working together with Wen Yiduo.

Published in 1934, "European Travel Notes" and published in 1943, "London Miscellany," are two travelogues written in an impressionistic style. In 1935, he edited "Poetry Collection of The New Literature Series of Modern China" and wrote the "Introduction." The next year, the essay collection "You and I" was published, among which, "To the Deceased Wife" reminisces about various past events of the deceased wife Wu Zhongqian, sincere and heartfelt, touching and moving. During this period, although the sentimentality of Zhu Ziqing's essays was slightly inferior to the earlier ones, the delicacy of conception and the sincerity of attitude remained consistent, while the oralization of literature became even more natural and refined. Ye Shengtao once pointed out: when teaching Chinese literature or compiling modern literature history, "in terms of the perfection of the genre, the complete use of written language, Mr. Zhu should be the first one mentioned" ("Mr. Zhu Peixian").

After the outbreak of the Anti-Japanese War, Zhu Ziqing followed Tsinghua University south to Changsha, arriving in Kunming in March 1938, serving as the head of the Chinese Literature Department of the merged National Southwest Associated University formed by Peking University, Tsinghua University, and Nankai University, and was elected as a member of the board of directors of the All-China Federation of Literary and Art Circles Against the Enemy. During the arduous years of the Anti-Japanese War, despite living in poverty, he engaged in teaching and literary research with seriousness and rigor, co-authoring books such as "Chinese Language Teaching" with Ye Shengtao.

After the victory of the Anti-Japanese War, the Kuomintang government launched a civil war and suppressed the democratic movement, especially the successive murders of Zhu Ziqing's close friends Li Gongpu and Wen Yiduo in July 1946, which shocked and enraged him—he disregarded his personal safety and attended the Chengdu mourning assembly for the Li-Wen Tragedy, delivering a speech on the life and achievements of Wen Yiduo.

In October 1946, he returned to Beiping from Sichuan and served as the convener of the "Committee for Organizing Wen Yiduo's Posthumous Works." After a long and winding journey, educated by the dark reality and propelled by the patriotic democratic movement, he eventually became a resolute revolutionary democrat. In the practical struggle against hunger and civil war, suffering from severe illness, he still signed the "Protest Against U.S. Policy of Supporting Japan and Refusal to Accept American Relief Flour Declaration," instructing his family not to buy rationed flour, always maintaining the noble integrity and precious character of an upright patriotic intellectual.

On August 24, 1948, Zhu Ziqing tragically passed away due to severe gastric disease (serious gastric ulcer leading to gastric perforation). Before his death, Zhu Ziqing weakly held his wife Chen Zhu Yin's hand and intermittently said: "Remember, I signed the declaration rejecting American relief flour, our family will not buy the American flour of the Kuomintang." Zhu Ziqing demonstrated the dignity and integrity of an outstanding Chinese intellectual in refusing this "soul-buying nature" charity, praised by Mao Zedong as showing "our nation's heroic spirit."

Zhu Ziqing's works can be divided into three categories: 1) those criticizing social life and dark realities as their theme; 2) mainly describing personal and family life, expressing human relationships between fathers and sons, husbands and wives, and friends; 3) those primarily depicting natural scenery and expressing emotions through landscapes.

Zhu Ziqing has 27 works, totaling approximately 1.9 million words, including poetry, essays, literary criticism, academic research, etc. Most of these works have been included in the four-volume "Complete Works of Zhu Ziqing" published by Kai Ming Bookstore in 1953. In 1988, Jiangsu Education Press re-collected and organized Zhu Ziqing's works, publishing a six-volume "Complete Works of Zhu Ziqing."

Although Zhu Ziqing started writing new poetry after the May Fourth Movement, his 1923 publication of "The Qinhuai River in the Sound of Oars and Lantern Light" showcased his talent in essay writing. From then on, he focused on essay creation, achieving remarkable success—works like "The Back View" and "Lotus Pond under Moonlight" written in 1927 became widely acclaimed masterpieces.

The documentary essay "The Back View," published in 1928, made Zhu Ziqing a renowned essayist of the time. His famous poetry collection is "Traces," and his essay collections include "The Back View," "You and I," "Lotus Pond under Moonlight," "Hastily," etc., all widely acclaimed masterpieces. Literary critiques include "Differentiation of Poetic Expression" and "On the Appreciation of Elegance and Vulgarity," among others. There are also "Green" and "Spring."

Zhu Ziqing's essays prefer narrative and emotional expression. Narrative essays mainly depict social realities; emotional essays include those represented by "The Back View" and "Children," describing personal and family life with a strong sense of humanity; another category consists of short pieces focusing on natural landscapes, expressing personal emotions, such as "Green," "Spring," "Lotus Pond under Moonlight," "The Qinhuai River in the Sound of Oars and Lantern Light," etc. Regarding borrowing scenery to express emotions, Zhu Ziqing has an excellent piece recalling old times and ancient landscapes, expressing deep nostalgia for departed friends, titled "Baima Lake."

Biography

Zhu Ziqing, born in 1898, registered as a resident of Shaoxing, Zhejiang, but was born in Donghai, Jiangsu, as his family had long resided in Yangzhou, so he also called himself a Yangzhou person;

In 1917, at the age of 20, Zhu Ziqing, "due to poor family economic conditions, in order to warn myself not to follow the俗world, changed my name to Zi Qing." The name "Zi Qing" comes from "Chuci·Bu Ju": "Would rather be clean and upright to keep oneself pure?" meaning to remain clean and upright to maintain purity.

His courtesy name "Pei Xian" was chosen because Zhu Ziqing considered his temperament slow, inspired by a phrase in "Han Feizi": "Dong An Yu's nature was slow, so he wore a string to urge himself," hence his courtesy name "Pei Xian" served as a self-alerting measure.

Zhu Ziqing studied in private schools during his childhood, deeply influenced by traditional Chinese culture.

He entered middle school in 1912; in 1916, he was admitted to the preparatory department of Peking University, the following year, he entered the philosophy department, graduating in advance in 1920; during his time at Peking University, Zhu Ziqing actively participated in the May Fourth patriotic activities and later joined the Plain Clothes Education Lecture Group organized by Peking University students to spread new ideas. After graduation, he taught at several middle schools in Zhejiang and Jiangsu, continuing to participate in the new literary movement, becoming an early member of the Literary Research Association. Additionally, he participated in organizing the first poetry group in the history of new literature, "China New Poetry Society," and launching the first poetry magazine, "Poetry" Monthly, supporting the activities of the lakeside poetry society and the dawn literature society composed of young students, contributing hard labor to opening up the path of new poetry. Zhu Ziqing began publishing poetry at the end of 1919, as one of the initial poets of the new literary movement, he gained recognition for his fresh and brisk poems.

In 1922, the Commercial Press published the first anthology of eight poets from the Literary Research Association, "Snow Morning," which included 19 of Zhu Ziqing's poems.

In 1923, Zhu Ziqing published a nearly 300-line lyric poem, "Annihilation," expressing his serious contemplation of life and his relentless and progressive attitude towards leaving deep footprints step by step on the soil, which had a significant impact at the time.

In 1924, the poetry and prose collection "Traces" was published. His poems, though few in number, presented a pure, simple, and novel style in both thought and artistry. Among them, "Light," "New Year," "Coal," "Sending Han Bo Hua to Russia," "Sheep Flock," "Modern Times in a Small Cabin," and others either passionately sought light and looked forward to the future or forcefully lashed out against the dark world and revealed the blood-stained reality, filled with anti-imperialist and anti-feudal revolutionary spirit, making them rare works in the early stages of new poetry.

In 1925, Zhu Ziqing became a professor in the Chinese Department of Tsinghua University, beginning his literary research and shifting his creative focus to essays.

In 1928, his first essay collection, "Back View," was published, containing personal true experiences and unique insights, forming his own style with plain, simple, yet fresh and exquisite writing.

During this period, Zhu Ziqing's essays focused on exposing societal darkness, warlord atrocities, and imperialist crimes, filled with love and sympathy for the oppressed and victimized, demonstrating his anti-imperialist and anti-feudal democratic thoughts, patriotic passion, humanitarian spirit, and upright and honest character. Zhu Ziqing wrote more and was most praised for his landscape-emotion essays. This type of essay exhibited a diverse yet unified artistic style. "Paddle Sounds and Lantern Shadows on the Qinhuai River," depicting the Qinhuai River scenery, and "Lotus Pond under Moonlight," expressing solitude while strolling by the pond at night, were emotionally rich, popular masterpieces. These poetic essays, through novel observations and graceful descriptions, conveyed the melancholy and sorrow he felt in the face of the dark reality after the failure of the Great Revolution. "Back View" used plain language and sincere emotion to vividly describe a touching scene of family changes, with the father sending off his son at the station, "achieving both form and substance, winning entirely by genuine feeling and character" (Ye Shengtao, "Preface to Zhu Ziqing's Selected Works"). Zhu Ziqing displayed the artistic vitality of new literature with his proficient and meticulous essay skills and thorough and detailed style, being recognized as an outstanding essayist in the new literary movement.

In August 1931, Zhu Ziqing studied abroad in the UK, furthering his studies in linguistics and British literature; afterward, he traveled through five European countries.

In July 1932, he returned to China and served as the head of the Chinese Literature Department at Tsinghua University, collaborating with Wen Yiduo.

In 1934, "European Travel Notes" was published, and in 1943, "London Miscellany," both written in an impressionistic style.

In 1935, he compiled "Poetry Collection of The New Literature Series of Modern China" and wrote the "Introduction." The next year, the essay collection "You and I" was published, among which, "To the Deceased Wife" reminisced about various past events of the deceased wife Wu Zhongqian, sincere and heartfelt, touching and moving.
