However, whether this check package is necessary still exists certain controversy in the medical field. The reason is that mothers do not necessarily lead to fetal malformation or abortion after being infected with these viruses. In places like Beijing and Hong Kong, obstetricians generally will not actively suggest that women planning to conceive and those in early pregnancy undergo this test. However, local obstetrics experts believe that the "four pre-birth checks" are necessary because if a mother gets infected during pregnancy, the impact on the child may not be discovered at birth but gradually manifest later.

The "four pre-birth checks" mainly examine whether there are infection signs of four viruses in the mother's body: toxoplasma virus, rubella virus, cytomegalovirus, and herpes simplex virus. In the English and American medical fields, based on the first one or two letters of the English names of these four viruses, they form the word TORCH, hence this test is also known as the TORCH test overseas.

1. Toxoplasma Virus - Higher infection rate for pet owners

Among the general population, 60% to 80% have been infected with the toxoplasma virus, and the infection rate is even higher for those who keep cats and dogs at home. If a woman becomes pregnant without treatment after being infected, the chances of repeated miscarriages are higher than normal. In the 1980s, a domestic survey found that the miscarriage rate among pregnant women infected with the toxoplasma virus was around 10%, and it could potentially cause issues such as hydrocephalus, microcephaly, and poor development of the central nervous system in fetuses.

2. Rubella Virus - Infection rate among women of childbearing age reaches 84%

Rubella virus spreads through the respiratory tract. In Guangzhou region, the overall infection rate of rubella virus among women of childbearing age is as high as 84%, and the annual infection rate among pregnant women is 3.6%. Adults infected with the rubella virus often do not feel unwell or only exhibit non-specific symptoms similar to a cold, making it quite hidden. Moreover, since rubella virus infections are more likely to occur during cold seasons, even if some discomfort appears, it is easily mistaken for a common cold. If the fetus is infected, it may result in congenital cataracts, eye defects, ear defects, deafness, microcephaly, and other congenital developmental abnormalities. Chen Dunjin introduced that the United States once tracked over 30,000 pregnant women infected with the rubella virus, finding that 20,000 gave birth to children with congenital deformities. Guangzhou once administered rubella virus vaccines to a group of junior high school girls, hoping they would develop immunity before their first sexual activity, but the protection rate was found to be only 85%-90%, unable to completely prevent reinfection during pregnancy.

3. Cytomegalovirus - Can spread through urine and feces

Cytomegalovirus is the most complex virus discovered by humans so far, and there is no corresponding vaccine yet. The infection incidence rate among Chinese people is as high as 95%, and clinical findings show that the proportion of active cytomegalovirus infections during pregnancy is around 5%.

"This might be related to the fact that Chinese people only pay attention to 'intake' and not 'output'. Cytomegalovirus mainly spreads through the respiratory tract, and it also exists in the urine and feces of infected individuals. If you touch something containing the virus and then touch your mouth or nose, you may get infected."

Clinically, it is found that about 5% of pregnant women have active cytomegalovirus infections, of which one-third will infect newborns, and one-third of the infected newborns will experience developmental problems in the brain, central nervous system, retina, etc.

4. Herpes Simplex Virus - Can cause miscarriage, premature birth, and deformities

Herpes simplex is divided into type I and type II. Seven to eight out of ten adults have been infected with the herpes simplex virus. Most people are infected with type I, which affects areas outside the reproductive organs and has little teratogenic effect on fetuses; however, type II herpes simplex virus infections in the reproductive organs can significantly affect the fetus, causing miscarriage, premature birth, stillbirth, and deformities.

Neonatal herpes simplex virus infection has a high mortality rate, and survivors often have sequelae.

Controversy: Is the "four pre-birth checks" necessary?

Counterpoint: Pregnancy infections do not necessarily infect the fetus

Chen Dunjin, Director of the Guangzhou Critical Pregnant Women Treatment Center and Chief of Obstetrics at the Third Affiliated Hospital of Guangzhou Medical College, stated that theoretically, all women planning to conceive should undergo the "four pre-birth checks", but currently, there is still controversy among obstetrics experts regarding whether it is necessary to conduct this test in clinical practice.

"Pregnant women getting infected does not necessarily mean the fetus will be malformed."

"As far as I know, hospitals in Beijing and Hong Kong do not actively recommend this test to women planning to conceive or those in late pregnancy, while in Guangzhou, most hospitals perform this test."

One of the reasons for this controversy is that there is no definite relationship between pregnant women getting infected and the fetus getting infected, nor is there a definite relationship between fetal infection and fetal malformation, stillbirth, or miscarriage.

"For example, only 1/9 of pregnant women infected with cytomegalovirus will give birth to deformed children, and 40% of pregnant women infected with toxoplasma virus will infect the fetus." Moreover, there are currently no specific treatments available in the medical field for these four infections.

Therefore, some experts believe that the "four pre-birth checks" are not very meaningful and prenatal diagnosis is more reliable for excluding congenital deformities.

Proponent: Infection effects may appear when the child grows up

Despite this, Chen Dunjin believes that conducting the "four pre-birth checks" before planning to conceive is necessary, and currently, both the United States and the United Kingdom provide such tests for women. If an infection is detected, treating it beforehand aligns better with the principles of eugenics.

Higher incidence of ADHD

"We have already found that some children who were infected but showed no obvious abnormalities at birth gradually developed issues during their development process. For instance, our follow-up observations of newborns revealed that children whose mothers had cytomegalovirus infections during pregnancy have a higher incidence of ADHD and learning difficulties compared to regular children."

Experts believe that performing the "four pre-birth checks" within the first three months of pregnancy helps detect maternal infections early, allowing for checking whether the fetus is infected and receiving targeted treatment or terminating the pregnancy accordingly.

If the fetus is not infected, the mother can receive treatment to prevent fetal infection; if the fetus is already infected and internal organs have shown abnormalities, doctors usually recommend terminating the pregnancy; if the fetus is infected but no internal organ abnormalities are detected, the mother can receive standardized treatment at the eugenics clinic and possibly give birth to a healthy baby. However, the impact of viral infections may gradually manifest as the child grows up.

Chen Dunjin also reminds that besides undergoing necessary tests, women planning to conceive should also pay attention to personal hygiene, avoid risky behaviors, limit excessive sexual partners, and try to avoid crowded and poorly ventilated places from planning conception until the first three months of pregnancy.
