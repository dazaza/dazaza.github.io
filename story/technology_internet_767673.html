Windows 8.1 Proves Microsoft's Online Investment Strategy Is Paying Off

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 12:01:05

Microsoft has long been criticized for its investment in online services. Hedge fund manager David Einhorn complained back in 2011 that Microsoft wasted billions of dollars on Bing in a futile attempt to catch up with Google.

Einhorn's complaint was not without merit, as since the first quarter of 2005, Microsoft's Online Services Division (mainly centered around Bing) had lost nearly $11 billion. Despite this massive expenditure, Microsoft still lagged far behind Google in the search domain.

However, now, Microsoft’s investment in online services is finally paying off. As Apple has done over the past few years, Microsoft is also beginning to integrate online services with its operating system. The Windows 8.1 system it released on Thursday integrates many of Microsoft's online services.

Microsoft finds a new way through its operating system

Apple, Google, and Microsoft—these three major tech companies each offer two different operating systems. Google's Android and Apple's iOS are specifically tailored for tablets and smartphones, while Google's Chrome OS and Apple's OS X target traditional PCs.

But Microsoft has adopted a different strategy. It provides the Windows Phone system specifically for mobile phones, whereas the Windows 8 system works across both tablets and PCs. On Thursday, Microsoft released the first significant upgrade to the Windows 8 system. Although it remains a hybrid operating system based on the Metro interface, there have been many improvements. Most notably, it integrates some of Microsoft's key online services, such as SkyDrive and Bing.

Comparison between SkyDrive, Google Drive, and iCloud

Although Microsoft's SkyDrive, Google's Google Drive, and Apple's iCloud are all services for storing data online, their working principles differ greatly. Google Drive and iCloud stand at opposite ends of the spectrum, with Microsoft's SkyDrive falling somewhere in the middle.

iCloud runs quietly in the background. If you own both an iPhone and an iPad, iCloud ensures that your photos, documents, and music are shared seamlessly between these devices. This is an elegant and easy-to-use solution but lacks flexibility.

In contrast, Google Drive is essentially akin to a portable hard drive. It can easily upload, download, and share files with other Google users, but it does not provide the effortless background system like Apple's iCloud.

SkyDrive used to be more similar to Google Drive, but with the advent of Windows 8.1, it has moved closer to Apple's iCloud in terms of functionality. Like iCloud, it automatically syncs settings, images, and documents across all of the user's Windows devices, while also retaining features akin to Google Drive, such as basic file search capabilities.

Bing has become integral to Windows 8

SkyDrive is integrated with Microsoft's search engine Bing, which itself is deeply embedded within Windows 8.1. With the removal of the "Start" menu, the simplest and fastest way to access a file, app, or setting in Windows 8.1 is through search.

In the first version of Windows 8, searches were limited to the local PC, but in Windows 8.1, the search scope, aided by Bing, extends to the web and SkyDrive. Users can even search for text within images, such as taking a photo of a document and then searching for text within it. This is a unique feature of Microsoft's SkyDrive; neither Apple's iCloud nor Google's Google Drive offers a similar function yet.

The restructuring of Microsoft begins to take shape

Bing, once viewed as a loss-making project, is finally stepping into the spotlight of Microsoft's ecosystem, becoming the backbone of the Windows 8 system and acting as the glue connecting various Microsoft products.

SkyDrive will also become increasingly indispensable. Through the integration of SkyDrive with Windows, Microsoft will create a shared experience across all Windows devices. This strategy has been perfected by Apple in recent years and has proven highly effective.

From this perspective, Microsoft's upgrade to Windows 8 perfectly embodies its new "One Microsoft" strategy, integrating all of Microsoft's devices and services through the Windows 8 platform.
