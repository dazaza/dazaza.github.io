Giordano and Uniqlo

by wgyuvs8a on 2012-02-29 13:10:29

From a businessman to an entrepreneur, this is the transformation that Mr. Yanjing has never completed. When Yanjing Zheng decided to take over the seal given by his father, this mission of inheritance had already begun - Uniqlo. In fact, Yanjing Zheng never intended to become a rule-abiding businessman; he wanted to be a disruptor.

It is well known that traditional clothing retailers usually select and purchase goods from orders placed with clothing stores and wholesalers, adopting a consignment sales method. The advantage of this business model is that the risk for the retailer is small, as unsold clothes can be returned to the supplier. However, there is also a flaw: retailers have no room for free pricing, the gross profit margin on goods is low, and since production is not in their own hands, there is a lack of consistency and quality control. This is why, during the first ten years of entrepreneurship, Yanjing Zheng was constantly expanding but always short of money, and why he was always facing various quality issues.

Indeed, with the increase in Uniqlo stores, its bargaining power with manufacturers greatly increased, allowing Yanjing Zheng to enter the upstream of the supply chain, such as commissioning manufacturers to develop their own products, buyout-style production, and increasing profit margins. However, since the clothing factories are overseas, the risks are enormous. Once there is a misjudgment in product planning and ordering, the loss could be fatal.

At this time, after experiencing the great economic development of the 1980s, Japan's middle class had become the mainstream of society, relatively stable and mature. They began to pursue natural and comfortable clothing. It was at this time that the American company GAP entered the Japanese market. Founded in 1969, GAP designed its own products and sent people to manage the production process on-site. Both styles and quality were guaranteed, making affordable, high-quality products possible. With strong terminal data collection and logistics distribution, GAP could quickly adjust designs and production, rapidly updating its inventory. This caused a huge impact on Uniqlo.

A conversation with the founder of "Giordano" and GAP China, Jimmy Lai, ignited Yanjing Zheng's fiery passion for a second entrepreneurial endeavor. He decided to transform Uniqlo into fast fashion, and the idealized production chain model gradually began to take shape. That is: in New York, collecting fashion information and conducting product planning, specific design of new products by Osaka's product design office and the headquarters in Yamaguchi, then entrusting overseas factories like those in China to process and produce them.

However, the path of replication was still full of twists and turns.

In December 1994, "Impression New York Company" was established. In the following autumn, the products it created were fully launched into the market. What Yanjing Zheng did not expect was that due to the separation of design, planning, and production across three locations, communication problems arose. The market did not accept the dull, single-tone styles they introduced, resulting in a complete failure of the series in Japan.

When summarizing the lessons learned, Yanjing Zheng realized that to become a fast-fashion brand, establishing a communication system and information feedback mechanism that could respond quickly was very important. Uniqlo must comprehensively reform its organizational system, processes, and meeting systems. "At that time, I thought about changing the organizational structure every day. Without extraordinary speed in decision-making and execution, it would only remain a pipe dream," said Yanjing Zheng.

Thus, in the following four years, people saw the abandonment of the "dictatorial," order-giving single-channel management style. "Not treating the president's words as gospel" became Uniqlo's new creed. Meetings were no longer dominated solely by Yanjing Zheng; he started delegating decision-making power to stores and subordinates. The most advanced IT and POS terminal collection technologies were introduced. Uniqlo began transitioning towards an internet-based organizational structure.

This reform proved effective. Communication between design, product planning, production, and logistics no longer faced obstacles. The fleece (Fleece) garments were the result of this close cooperation. Fleece refers to polyester fiber fabric with raised loops on both sides. In October 1998, Uniqlo launched fleece garments. This material was lightweight, warm, and quick-drying, and the key point was that the price was only 1990 yen, corresponding to about 15,000 yen, the average daily salary of Japanese salaried workers, making it a good value for money. That year, this product sold 2 million pieces, 8.5 million pieces the next year, and 26 million pieces in 2000, becoming a cultural phenomenon.
