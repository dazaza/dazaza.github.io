What is Instant Messaging (IM)?

Instant Messaging can go by many names. For instance, "chat software" is a more common term. But remember, IM isn't just Tencent QQ. There are many excellent IM software options worldwide. The high recognition of QQ among Chinese netizens stems from its absolute market share in the domestic IM market.

Currently, the more popular IM software in China includes: QQ, MSN Messenger (Windows Live Messenger), Baidu Hi, Feixin, Skype, Sina UC, NetEase Bubble, TM, Google Talk, Aliwangwang, etc.

The biggest difference between instant messaging and email is the lack of waiting time; there's no need to check "send and receive" every two minutes. As long as both parties are online simultaneously, it functions like a multimedia phone, allowing the transmission of text, files, sound, and images to each other. As long as there’s a network connection, distance doesn’t matter. Although email plays a crucial role in internet applications, due to its "instant" nature, instant messaging has gradually replaced email in many fields.

The Origin of Instant Messaging (IM)

Instant Messaging software was born in 1996 when four Israelis invented ICQ, which stands for "I SEEK YOU (I find you)." ICQ supports chatting, sending messages, and transferring files on the Internet. Once launched, it gained global attention, leading to the establishment of Mirabilis Company, which provided instant messaging services to registered users. Within six months, ICQ became the world's most popular instant messaging software. In the seventh month, it reached one million official users. In 1998, ICQ was acquired by AOL for $287 million, at which point it had over ten million users. With an unprecedentedly creative idea, ICQ quickly amassed a large number of users worldwide. Even in Asia, where the internet wasn't as developed back then, it held 70% of the market, and domestically, it captured 80%.

The Development of Instant Messaging (IM) in China

Back in 2000, what was trending online? That was the golden age of China's internet. It seemed that if you planted any seed on the internet, it would grow into a towering tree in the near future. At that time, there weren't many popular applications on the internet, unlike now, where there are plenty of fun places to explore. The concept of "playing online" hadn't yet formed. Everyone knew the internet was a good thing, but they didn't quite know how to utilize it. The most popular applications at the time were undoubtedly email and online chat rooms. Emails were introduced in previous chapters, while many people got to know the internet through chat rooms.

I remember my first time online. I pulled out a piece of paper, opened the browser, and nervously typed in the copied chat room address in the address bar. With great reverence, I registered an account and started chatting. The moment I clicked to enter, I felt an explosion of joy. A brand-new world unfolded before me. The colorful fonts made me dizzy for a while, but eventually, I found my way.

As time went on, I noticed a problem: meeting like-minded netizens was rare and unpredictable. There was no way to save friends like in a contact list. Later, I started trying something new called OICQ Network Pager. Thus, I registered an OICQ number, which is now known as QQ, though it wasn't called QQ back then. With this tool, our communication with netizens became much smoother.

Initially, OICQ was modeled after ICQ because ICQ didn't have a Chinese version at the time, so very few people used it in China. Only Ma Huateng himself knows exactly when OICQ was born. Its initial positioning was as a "wireless network paging system." Therefore, the early OICQ had a pretty cool feature: sending messages to pagers.

At that time, aside from this, there weren't any other instant messaging software available. With media hype and promotion, no one expected OICQ's registered user count to skyrocket so rapidly.
