On August 10, our newspaper's "Report-Internet Interaction" reported on Zhang Bin, a teacher at Tietai Primary School in Geliang Town, Changshou District, who left his post for over a decade to start a business but was still receiving "empty salaries" from the school. The day after the report was published, the reporter learned that Zhang Bin had already sent a resignation letter to the school, and the Changshou District Education Commission also began investigating the matter, holding accountable those involved.

Parties involved want to retain their public positions.

How does the law define the "empty salary phenomenon"? Professor Liu Xin of China University of Political Science and Law said that there are no specific penalty provisions for this problem in the Civil Servant Law. However, such situations definitely indicate neglect in school management. If the responsible person used this behavior to receive bribes (small amounts) or benefit the unit, disciplinary responsibility can be pursued; if the amount of bribery reaches the level of a crime, criminal responsibility must be pursued.

Lawyer He Zhiqiang from Chongqing Yu Bo Lawyer Call Center said that first, it should be determined whether this was a mistake made by relevant school personnel or intentional action. If it was the former, Zhang Bin would only be considered unjust enrichment, requiring him to return the money. If relevant personnel knowingly continued to pay salaries to absentees, as Tietai Primary School in Changshou is a public school funded by national finances, both the relevant personnel and Zhang Bin would be suspected of embezzling state property and should face corresponding punishments.
