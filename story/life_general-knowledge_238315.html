The scientific way to arrange plants

by longersing on 2009-09-15 14:32:20

The Scientific Method of Plant Placement: During the days at home, the presence of flowers and plants makes the atmosphere particularly warm and inviting. Plants such as Araucaria, Cycas, Red Mulberry, Variegated Wood, and Lily, among others, enhance our living spaces. Although keeping and placing green plants at home has many benefits, it is not true that the more plants you have indoors, the better. Placing green plants indoors and cultivating them on the balcony are two different things.

On the balcony, as long as there is enough space, having more green plants doesn’t hurt. However, indoors, one must consider both the space available and the fact that plants undergo photosynthesis and respiration even inside. During the day, plants release oxygen and absorb carbon dioxide; at night, they emit carbon dioxide and consume oxygen. Therefore, when purchasing green plants, it's important to understand that having too many plants indoors isn't necessarily good, especially in bedrooms which are generally more enclosed. The number of plants should be kept to a minimum in these areas, while 2-3 pots in the living room would suffice. Some families have experienced thinner indoor air due to an overabundance of oxygen-consuming plants, significantly affecting their quality of life. Clearly, plant placement requires both science and skill.

Plant Purchasing Tips

When purchasing plants, one should choose green plants suitable for personal cultivation based on factors like occupation, interests, and hobbies. For example, individuals with busy schedules can opt for low-maintenance plants like spider plants. On the other hand, those who enjoy nurturing plants and have more time to care for them can select plants that require higher maintenance or sun-loving plants (which can be moved to the balcony periodically for sunlight), such as Azalea. Additionally, people with a more refined personality might prefer orchids, asparagus ferns, or some bonsai for indoor decoration.

When choosing plants, it's essential to consider whether they can thrive in your living environment, taking into account factors like lighting, temperature and humidity, and ventilation. It's also important to ensure harmony between the plants and the surrounding space, adjusting according to the size of your living area. For larger spaces with good lighting, taller and sun-loving plants can be selected. Taller green plants can be placed in living rooms and entrance halls. In front of windows where sunlight is abundant, a variety of colorful and shaped plants such as Araucaria, Pineapple, and African Violet can be chosen. Bedrooms should feature smaller plants with limited quantities. Bathrooms can house hanging plants, and children's rooms can incorporate vibrant-colored plants, but avoid cacti or spiky plants that could harm children.

Experts suggest considering the "complementary" function of plants when placing them indoors. Most plants release carbon dioxide at night and absorb oxygen, whereas cacti release oxygen and absorb carbon dioxide at night. Combining these complementary plants in one room can balance the levels of oxygen and carbon dioxide, maintaining fresh indoor air.

Suitable Indoor Plants

Many leafy plants effectively absorb harmful gases from the air, such as spider plants, African daisies, Golden Pothos, and Aloe Vera, which are excellent at absorbing formaldehyde. However, many people hang spider plants on balconies without fully utilizing their potential. Ivy, Ironwood, and Chrysanthemums can break down formaldehyde and xylene, while evergreen plants like Aglaonema can eliminate various harmful gases. Currently, chrysanthemums are in high demand, and they can last for about a month at home. Here, we recommend a few common indoor plants available in the market.

Peace Lily

The Peace Lily is a popular leafy plant loved by consumers, known for its water-dripping characteristic, earning it the nickname "Dripping Goddess." It thrives in warm, humid, and semi-shaded environments, intolerant to cold. When placed indoors, it effectively absorbs formaldehyde, though the plant is toxic and should never be ingested. Prices range from 60 to 280 yuan.

Camellia, Azalea

Camellias can resist harmful substances like sulfur dioxide, hydrogen chloride, and nitric acid fumes and absorb gases like hydrogen sulfide, hydrogen fluoride, benzene, ethylbenzene, and ether, although inhaling these may cause discomfort. Azaleas are ideal flowers for resisting pollutants like sulfur dioxide. These flowers are available in Sanchengxiang, so careful identification is necessary due to their similar appearances.

Hibiscus, Crape Myrtle

Hibiscus, also known as "morning open evening fall flower," originates from China and India. It prefers warm and humid climates, thriving in full sunlight but tolerating slight shade, drought, wetness, poor soil, and cold. It can absorb harmful gases like sulfur dioxide, chlorine, and hydrogen chloride. Crape Myrtles, also called "hundred-day red," "full hall red," and "itchy tree," exhibit strong resistance to harmful gases like sulfur dioxide, hydrogen chloride, chlorine, and hydrogen fluoride.

Mimosa, Osmanthus

Mimosa can absorb sulfur dioxide and chlorine from the air.
