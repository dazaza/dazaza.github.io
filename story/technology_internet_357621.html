The following is a summary of the article:

Although mobile communication services in China still primarily focus on voice calls and text messages, the popularization of 3G applications and smartphones has prompted various users to transform their phones into Internet devices, e-readers, music players, gaming consoles, and even e-commerce and financial trading platforms.

This development has not only given rise to numerous small and medium-sized start-up companies, providing convenient services for China's market which boasts the most Internet and mobile phone users globally, but is also driving large publicly-traded companies with substantial capital to change their previous观望attitude, seeking various investment opportunities in the confined world of smartphones.

"Investors and company managers are facing very difficult decisions: if they invest (in mobile Internet), they may not necessarily find good projects, but if they don't invest, they will certainly miss out on the biggest opportunity of the next decade," said Yu Yongfu, CEO of UCWeb.

In China, there are over 700 million people who own a mobile phone, approximately twice the number of computer users, providing this industry with unlimited imagination space. The continuous decline in smartphone prices and the popularization of broadband have further fueled this trend.

"The business model of mobile Internet is much better than that of early Internet business models," said Tian Suning, chairman of Broadband Capital.

The former president of China Netcom established a Chinese private equity investment institution, which recently announced a joint investment of $1 billion in China, together with the Blackberry Partner Fund, aimed at investing in the communications industry. "Mobile Internet and e-commerce are our key areas of focus," he said.

In Yu Yongfu's view, over the past 30 years, the IT (information technology) industry has experienced three dynasties: the hardware dynasty created by IBM, the software dynasty created by Microsoft, and the Internet dynasty created by Google.

"In the next decade, the IT industry will experience its fourth dynasty, and its keyword is mobile Internet," he said. Phones will no longer simply be terminals used for communication; future phones will definitely be Internet phones.

UCWeb provides free mobile browser products to users, already boasting over 100 million users. Yu Yongfu, who had six years of experience working in venture capital institutions before founding the company, said that the reason for creating UCWeb was to not miss out on the next wave of IT industry development.

According to data from the market research firm Analysys International, the user base of China's mobile Internet reached 206 million in the first quarter, representing a year-on-year increase of 48.4%.

Large companies are no longer observing, each finding breakthroughs

Compared to small and medium-sized companies that make flexible decisions and act quickly, the reactions of large companies are usually much slower. However, as the market saturates and the growth rate of existing businesses slows down, large publicly-traded companies are also turning their attention to mobile Internet.

Lenovo Group, the world's fourth-largest personal computer manufacturer, recently began selling the LePhone smartphone, claiming to challenge Apple's iPhone. Nokia, the world's largest mobile phone manufacturer, is also testing the waters of mobile Internet services and applications by starting with downloadable music services.

"In the future, the majority of Internet traffic will come from phones, rather than computers," Douglas Raymond, Google's product manager, said at a forum in Shanghai.

An increasing number of users are beginning to transfer or parallel some of their online activities to their phones, inevitably giving birth to some emerging fields.

Tan Qunzhao, chairman of Shanda Games, previously stated that they have been looking for investment and acquisition opportunities, including in mobile game businesses. In terms of revenue, Shanda is China's second-largest online game company.

Analysys International predicts that China's mobile game market size will reach RMB 4.208 billion in 2011, with a compound annual growth rate of 45.54% from 2008 to 2011.

Tencent replaced Shanda last year to become the top-ranked online game enterprise domestically. However, Tencent's layout in mobile business extends far beyond games. In February this year, Tencent launched the mobile payment function of its payment platform Tenpay, allowing users to purchase movie tickets, air tickets, and pay utility bills via their phones.

A Tencent insider revealed that Tencent is currently developing mobile payment programs based on Apple's iPhone and Google's Android systems, expected to be launched within the year.

Taobao.com, Asia's largest online retailer, stated that the daily visits to its mobile version website have exceeded 30 million, with more than 100,000 transactions per day, mostly completed through Alipay, China's largest third-party payment tool.

Sharks vs. Small Fish: The Competition Has Just Begun

Over the past ten plus years, dozens of Internet companies have gone public overseas in mainland China, collectively amassing hundreds of billions of US dollars in market value. In many fields, it is very common for one large company to dominate half of the market: Sina's news, Baidu's search, Alibaba's e-commerce, Tencent's instant messaging tools, etc.

However, in mobile Internet, the competition has just begun, with almost no company having established an advantage that competitors cannot shake.

"Large companies may have 10,000 people working on 200 products, averaging 50 people per product; we only have 600 people, but we only do one product. We are more focused, thus our chances of success are greater," said Yu Yongfu.

He also mentioned that the company's valuation has reached at least 200 million US dollars and has successively received multiple rounds of financing from venture capital firms Morningside Venture Capital, Legend Capital, as well as Alibaba Group and Nokia's venture investment fund.

"This market will see the entry of many sharks, but there will always be some small fry that can survive and eventually grow into sharks one day," he said.
