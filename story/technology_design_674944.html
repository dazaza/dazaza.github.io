Backbone - "Grassroots" tycoons create business miracles

by tianxiangyi on 2012-02-24 16:39:39

The starting points of entrepreneurship for most well-known Zhejiang businessmen were very humble: Feng GenSheng, Shen AiQin, Zong QingHou, Qiu JiBao, Shi JiXing, Nan CunHui, Xu WenRong, Xu GuanJu, Lu GuanQiu, and Lou ZhongFu... Among these veteran Zhejiang businessmen, only one, Shi JiXing, who was from a state-owned enterprise, had a university degree. Seven of them had junior high school education or below, and seven were originally farmers.

Thirty years ago, most Zhejiang businessmen were farmers, laid-off workers, or even unemployed vagrants. Under the incentive of basic survival pressure, they began their adventurous journey from the fringes and cracks of the system. Therefore, in Zhejiang businessmen, one can find the characteristics of five types of entrepreneurs: adventurers, capitalists, reformers, operators, and managers.

In the annual Forbes and Hurun lists of mainland Chinese billionaires, the number of wealthy people from Zhejiang always ranks first. As a result, some say that Zhejiang, which has long been known as the "land of fish and rice, and cultural heritage," should now add the new title of "hometown of bosses."

The backbone force driving Zhejiang Province's economy is not monopolistic state-owned enterprises or multinational companies but private enterprises. A large number of national-level entrepreneurs have emerged from small villages in Zhejiang. On this land of Zhejiang, one of the most creative internet companies in the world, Alibaba, was born. Ma Yun, Ling LanFang, Xu GuanJu, Zou GuoYing, Zong QingHou... Analyzing their experiences, it is found that most of them originated from the "grassroots." In the early stages of their entrepreneurship, they did not receive help from any "important person" and their start-up funds were extremely limited. It was precisely these "grassroots" entrepreneurs who transformed Zhejiang, with its per capita resource composite index ranking third to last nationwide, from a "resource-poor province" into a wealth-rich province with GDP ranking fourth nationwide.

Nearly nine out of ten Zhejiang businessmen are "grassroots"-born.

In a beautifully decorated hall at the headquarters of Transfar Group, a coarse porcelain jar stands out particularly, serving as the group's "mascot." In 1986, when Xu ChuanHua, aged 51, worked at a small phosphate fertilizer factory that went bankrupt, he became unemployed. At the same time, his 25-year-old son Xu GuanJu suddenly contracted a terrible blood disease—hemolytic anemia—and within two months, accumulated medical expenses of over 20,000 yuan. For an ordinary farming family, this was an astronomical sum. To pay off debts, father and son decided to go into business with a do-or-die attitude. Xu ChuanHua saw potential in the liquid soap market, borrowed 2,000 yuan, and together with his son, manually mixed liquid soap in a coarse porcelain jar, setting up a small family workshop for producing liquid soap. Initially, due to lack of funds, the father and son could only produce at night and sell door-to-door during the day on bicycles loaded with liquid soap. They used the money earned from selling soap to buy raw materials for further production. With the diligence of both father and son, within a year, the sales of liquid soap reached 330,000 yuan, with a profit of 30,000 yuan.

In 2007, Transfar Group's total annual revenue was 10.37 billion yuan, with a profit of 947 million yuan, ranking 139th among the top 500 competitive large enterprise groups in China. The group chairman, Xu GuanJu, was elected vice chairman of the Zhejiang Provincial Committee of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference.

If we were to design a trademark for the Zhejiang businessman community, "grassroots" would probably be the most vivid symbol. Lu GuanQiu, who started by working in iron forging and only completed junior high school; Zong QingHou, chairman of Wahaha Group, who at the age of 43 was still delivering goods on a tricycle, selling simple student supplies like erasers costing 2 fen and exercise books costing 6 fen; Nan CunHui, who at the age of 15 started wandering through streets and alleys as a shoemaker; Qiu JiBao, also a contemporary of Nan CunHui, who traveled far to Northeast China to mend shoes; Feng GenSheng, who after finishing primary school, entered Hu QingYu Tang as an apprentice at the age of 14; Pang QingNian, who raised cattle for over 20 years; Chen AiLian, who drove a tractor; Zhou ChengJian, who sold goods at street stalls; Zheng ShengTao, who pulled carts; Wang ZhenTao, who worked as a carpenter.

These former "grassroots" individuals are now, like Xu ChuanHua and Xu GuanJu, successful in their careers. Having been in business for nearly 40 years, Lu GuanQiu is undoubtedly a perennial evergreen tree, aiming for a daily profit of 10 million yuan in 2009. The ZhengTai Group operated by Nan CunHui has already ranked among the top five in the list of the top 500 private enterprises nationwide...

Before starting their businesses, "grassroots" Zhejiang businessmen might not have been able to find a decent book at home. Not to mention "Western education," they didn't even have much "traditional education." However, they possessed the most resilient character, capable of cutting a bloody path through thorns in the toughest times.

Zhejiang businessmen originating from the "grassroots" inherently have the simplest "integrity" sentiment: if you promise something, you must deliver. In modern enterprises, integrity is precisely the longest-term investment, bringing valuable credibility to a brand and reaping the richest rewards. It seems that the simplest is often the best.

Ma Yun, the "grassroots" Zhejiang businessman, played to his "grassroots" strengths, daring to imagine what others dared not and doing what others dared not. When the market was initially a "desert," planting a big tree directly wouldn't survive; grass was the species most suitable for the environment. Ma Yun accurately identified the commercial opportunities in this "desert," without attaching himself to rich and powerful "big trees," but instead appropriately supported the growth of "grasses," serving small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs). Eventually, under the protection of Alibaba's wings, SMEs sold "Made in China" products all over the world. Conversely, SMEs also made Alibaba successful.

In the autumn of 2007, Alibaba Network Limited successfully IPOed in Hong Kong, creating the biggest miracle in China's internet history. The small network workshop founded by 18 people with 500,000 yuan of capital turned into the world's largest online B2B trading website eight years later; the ordinary scholar who promoted "China Yellow Pages" everywhere became a globally renowned entrepreneur. Alibaba became the "Chinese experience" in the internet industry, written into many famous business schools' textbooks as a unique case study: succeeding by supporting SMEs, just Alibaba's business model alone draws worldwide attention.

The world's largest small commodity distribution center, Yiwu Small Commodity City, and the world's largest e-commerce website, Alibaba, are both in Zhejiang and operated by Zhejiang businessmen. The success of Ma Yun and Alibaba is essentially no different from the success of those "grassroots" peddlers who set up stalls all over Yiwu back then, except that Ma Yun and Alibaba have more modern flair.

For Zhejiang businessmen, the thirty years of reform and opening-up are their thirty years of development history, condensing and concentrating the three-hundred-year history of Western enterprise development. Thirty years ago, most of them were farmers, laid-off workers, or even unemployed vagrants. Under the incentive of basic survival pressure, they began their adventurous journey from the fringes and cracks of the system. Therefore, in Zhejiang businessmen, one can simultaneously find the characteristics of five types of entrepreneurs: adventurers, capitalists, reformers, operators, and managers, making them "all-round entrepreneurs."
