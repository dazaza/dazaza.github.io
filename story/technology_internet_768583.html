Qizhen, Chief Architect of Intel's Data Center Software Department: Intel Hadoop Boosts the Big Data Society

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 19:55:30

Qi Chen, Chief Architect of Intel's Data Center Software Department

"Intel Hadoop is an open-source technology. Intel uses its own capabilities to improve the version, and these improvements will be placed in the open-source community. We hope that this mutual open structure not only contributes to programmers but also to the entire society. A data-driven society requires interaction, not closure."

Transcript of Qi Chen's speech:

Everyone is familiar with Intel. When people talk about Intel, they first think of CPUs, which are mainly based on an open architecture. After listening to my presentation, you might remember Intel Hadoop. Intel does not just make CPUs or hardware; especially in the field of big data, we want to establish a Hadoop infrastructure called Intel Hadoop. The concepts like smart cities, Internet of Things (IoT), cloud computing, etc., all generate massive amounts of data. How to store these data, how to mine the information you need from them, and ultimately how to solve these problems are what our infrastructure today can help achieve. We've discussed various technological evolutions before, and they result in creating different kinds of value.

My topic is Hadoop. Some people may not be familiar with Hadoop. Others have already mentioned it earlier. I won't talk about what Hadoop does now, but rather whether Intel can add its label in this field. We've done this successfully domestically.

The theme of this conference is a data-driven society. It’s less about how much data you have and more about big data—how these data are stored and utilized. Previously, it was simple data storage, but now, can these data be mined? Can your data predict the future—not necessarily predicting your destiny, but using Intel's infrastructure and technology to help you accomplish what you want. In our country, urbanization is discussed, and it's not just about how much land or how far the city expands—it's more about building smart cities.

Smart cities also produce various types of data. As I mentioned earlier, smart cities and cloud computing cannot exist without data. One guest mentioned, for example, if you work in a factory and cameras constantly monitor you, these cameras produce large amounts of data and videos. You can't rely on dozens of people watching screens 24/7 without blinking. This is where Intel Hadoop helps you complete real-time analysis and warnings. Last time I visited a large semiconductor company, they were concerned about employees leaking data to third parties, which required good data mining and our open architecture. In our country, whether it's intelligent transportation or urban safety monitoring, we do well in hardware because we install many cameras. How these camera data are utilized concerns the government, and citizens also hope to benefit from the convenience of traffic and smart management. This is where Intel Hadoop comes into play.

I gave an example before: the telecommunications industry. China has a very large population, and generally, telecom companies generate 30TB of records monthly. Many people use 3G internet, and how your mobile data is calculated is something young people care about. They worry about going abroad and getting a bill for ten thousand yuan. If you deal with large companies, they provide convenient ways for you to know your mobile data usage anytime and anywhere. Simultaneously, companies understand user habits and preferences, creating a win-win situation. This is what we are helping these companies achieve, effectively utilizing our open Hadoop platform. Their data volume is significant; it exists there, but what can these data do for you? We provide various tools, most importantly, a foundation. This foundation is an open-source architecture, an open architecture called Intel Hadoop. With the rise of the internet, when people think of big data, they often think of an important framework: Hadoop.

Hadoop operates on an open platform, an open distributed system, using relatively inexpensive standard formats instead of expensive ones. Three years ago, when I talked about Hadoop to many large enterprises, they didn't know what Hadoop was. But in recent years, including banks, telecommunications, and manufacturing companies, they specifically requested this technology. Big data has become a strategic decision in our country. Of course, to respond to this strategy, we need a fundamental infrastructure, an open platform. This open platform isn’t the previous database platform; it's more of a Hadoop platform.

Hadoop runs on Intel CPUs, but since it's a newly emerging technology, Intel hopes to make it better and allow its hardware to perform better. This is a prediction chart showing the number of Intel Xeon processors running Hadoop by 2017. Here we discuss the basic infrastructure of Intel Hadoop.

When considering data storage, security must be taken into account. In this aspect, Intel has its unique features, making significant improvements on the Hadoop platform, as well as improvements in data management, etc. Then there’s the acceleration of architecture, and Intel advocates open architecture. Now when people mention big data, they often think of Hadoop, which is a practical infrastructure. Unlike cloud computing, which lacks a unified framework and is somewhat ambiguous, Hadoop is indeed a solid standard open architecture. Intel also hopes to play its role within this practical architecture. Intel offers various big data solutions, not just leveraging its hardware advantages in terms of infrastructure but also utilizing its software and hardware strengths at deeper and broader levels to help us analyze big data better, faster, and more accurately.

What are the results of Intel's contributions and improvements? First, Intel dominates in CPUs, using Intel's CPU can enhance performance by 50%. Intel has its own technology combining hardware with software technology. Therefore, using Intel Hadoop can increase performance by 50%. Additionally, Intel accelerates storage. Another point is Intel's improvement in big data storage and management, particularly utilizing Intel hardware to enhance performance. There are various technologies in the market today, and when discussing big data, Hadoop is the standard technology that comes to mind. Hadoop is an open platform, but the original platform did not fully utilize the characteristics of hardware. Intel has unique advantages in hardware characteristics and has improved the existing platform combined with hardware. For instance, processing 1TB of data takes about four hours using the original open Hadoop platform, but if you use Intel Hadoop, it only takes a few minutes. This is Intel's contribution to this open platform, adding Intel's label to the Hadoop platform. Intel Hadoop is based on an open architecture, and Intel uses its own strength to make improvements. These improvements will be placed in the open-source community. We hope that this mutual open structure not only contributes to programmers but also to the entire society. A data-driven society needs interaction, not closure. This is Intel's most widely used base in the field of big data, especially in enterprise applications. Intel stands out in hardware, and Intel Hadoop is also widely used domestically in open architectures. Moreover, Intel combines software and hardware to ensure reliable operation. Because these large enterprises are not internet companies; they first consider data security and reliability, followed by performance improvement.

Intel is a company that advocates open architecture. Of course, Intel is not just a software company but also a hardware company. Intel hopes to become a better open-source software company, combining with open-source software. In this diagram, there are various companies collaborating with Intel. Intel doesn't rely solely on its own strength but more on its partners. When these partners develop some big data technologies or apply these big data tools, they will first think of Intel Hadoop.

That's basically what I've presented today. Since it's not technical, I mainly want everyone to know that when mentioning big data, they think of Hadoop technology, and when thinking of Hadoop technology, they think of Intel Hadoop. Intel has its leading advantages in this area, improving Hadoop technology well, and gradually dominating the Hadoop market. So, as Hadoop applications become more widespread in the future, the Intel version of Hadoop will be the most widely used. Our activity and application scope are already very extensive. Thank you all!
