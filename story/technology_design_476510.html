Centipede Breeding Techniques:

1. Wild Land Breeding Method: This method is simple and easy to implement. In winter, dig a small trench of unlimited length and width in places where centipedes are known to appear. Place garbage, broken bricks, animal residues, bones, intestines, fish bones, chicken, duck, and goose feathers inside. Centipedes, attracted by the smell of feathers, will gather there to rest and reproduce. They can be caught in spring. Generally, every 1-2 days, the trench can be opened to catch centipedes. After catching them, replenish fresh food and cover with fine soil for continued capture. The best season for catching centipedes is around Qingming. Additionally, iron rakes can be used to catch centipedes in rock piles, tree roots, wastelands, and beaches. This method is especially suitable for beginners in centipede breeding, solving the problem of seed sources, and is particularly appropriate for those without professional breeding conditions.

2. Full Indoor Artificial Breeding Method:

Indoor breeding can utilize cement tanks with a bottom area preferably measuring 50x40 cm. Stack the cement tanks in a "pin" shape 2-3 layers high, using glass plates as tank covers. Inside each tank, lay soft fine soil with thickness varying according to the season; generally 10 cm in spring and autumn, 5 cm in summer, and about 20 cm in winter. Place broken tiles, small stones, or wooden blocks on the upper layer; water channels can be set up at the four corners of the tank using glass or water containers to supply drinking water for the centipedes. The density of breeding depends on the size of individuals, different growth stages, and bait situations. Typically, each tank can hold 200 young centipedes aged 1-2, 75 three-year-old centipedes, or 40-50 centipedes aged 4 and above. Each tank should have about 4 male adult centipedes. Other aspects can refer to outdoor semi-natural state artificial pool breeding methods.

3. Outdoor Natural State Pool Breeding Method:

(1) Build the centipede pool in a relatively cool, humid, shady, windproof, and quiet hillside area. Construct the pool walls with bricks, stones, and cement materials, about 80-100 cm high. Use cement to seal the seams. The size of the pool depends on the number of centipedes being bred. Large pools should be divided into smaller "grid" sections for separate breeding. Install drainage ditches around the pool. The pool bottom should not be cemented but instead covered with about 6 cm of fine soil, then topped with fine stones or broken tiles with gaps. Plant grasses and trees inside the pool and place water trays for clean drinking water. Try to create a natural ecological environment suitable for centipedes. Surround the pool mouth with a 15 cm wide glass strip forming a right angle "inner eave" to prevent centipedes from escaping or harmful animals from entering.

(2) The first batch of centipedes can come from wild collection or purchased seedlings. Typically, select sexually mature centipedes aged 3-5, requiring them to be strong and active, with a glossy black back. A ratio of 10 females to 1 male is ideal. However, during the egg-laying period, all male centipedes should be separated and placed elsewhere. The breeding density for pool-raised centipedes is approximately 350-450 per square meter. The humidity inside the pool should ideally be 60-70%, not exceeding 75-90%. If the humidity is too high, centipedes are prone to bacterial infections and damage, leading to difficulties in shedding and eating, hindering normal development and causing physiological diseases, possibly resulting in non-reproduction or low reproduction rates. The temperature inside the pool should be maintained between 26-29°C. In summer, when temperatures rise to 40-42°C, the rapid evaporation of moisture in centipedes requires constant attention to cooling. A common method is to frequently sprinkle water inside the pool to reduce temperature and maintain humidity. In winter, rice straw should be thrown into the pool to maintain warmth, creating a damp, warm, and feed-abundant environment suitable for centipede growth.

(3) Centipedes are carnivorous animals that prefer various insects like locusts, crickets, cicadas, grasshoppers, dragonflies, spiders, moths, etc. Their feed should mainly consist of poultry and livestock meat, including loaches, eels, earthworms, snails, bats, rodents, geckos, lizards, snakes, fly larvae, termites, stinkbugs, and other leftover animal parts like chicken blood, bones, intestines, feathers, fish, shrimp, and small miscellaneous items. Occasionally, they may also eat fruits, carrots, watermelon peels, and tender plant grasses, so it's appropriate to mix in 25-35% green feed like lotus grass and leafy greens. During the summer and autumn evenings, lights can be used to attract insects and moths for centipedes to hunt, and some insects can be appropriately released into the grass in the pool for centipedes to prey on. However, centipedes can withstand hunger for several days without dying, but extreme hunger or severe disturbances may cause them to cannibalize their own kind. Although centipedes can go days without food, they cannot survive a day without water, so water dishes must be set up in the breeding pool to provide ample drinking water.

Especially, centipedes are particular about their food and won't eat rotten or smelly items, even if they are insects. Feeding amounts should vary according to the season; centipedes are more active in spring and summer, so more food should be provided, while less is needed in late autumn and early winter. Generally, an adult centipede consumes about 1 gram of food per feeding. Juvenile centipedes can be fed every 2-3 days, whereas adult centipedes need daily feeding. Evening is the best time for feeding. Leftover food from the previous day should be removed first, and larger food items should be cut into pieces and spread out evenly.
