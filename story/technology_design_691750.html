John Calipari isn't gaming the system—he's maximizing it.

by xiaojiaozygb on 2012-03-05 17:36:14

GAINESVILLE, Fla. ― John Calipari went on a rant Sunday. And when Calipari rants, it's worth a listen. The topic was the way he conducts business at Kentucky. Right now, business is very, very good. Guard Marquis Teague is one of several UK freshmen with NBA potential. (AP Photo) The top-ranked Wildcats beat No. 16 Florida 74-59 at the O’Connell Center on Sunday afternoon to finish the regular season 30-1. They are just a buzzer-beater at Indiana away from perfection. Go ahead and pencil the Wildcats into the Final Four and being favored to win it all. Then wait to see if the NCAA vacates the title after finding Anthony Davis sat in the same seat Derrick Rose did when he took his SAT test.

I don’t know if Calipari is the recruiting scofflaw he’s made out to be. I do know that he’s wrongly convicted of running a mercenary program at Kentucky. “Do you really want me to start on that?” he said Sunday. He’d just been asked about the "one-and-done" approach to college basketball. Since the NBA no longer drafts high schoolers, studs like Davis have to play a year in college before bolting for the pros. And nobody bolts like the Wildcats. So Kentucky is seen as the great pirate ship of college hoops, winning SEC titles and plundering educational values. Even worse for Wildcats fans, losing three or four freshmen a year is no way to build long-term success. Right?

“Let me say this,” Calipari said. “When we lost five guys in the first round, (critics said) ‘You can’t do this. He’s taking too many good players.’ The next year, we went to the Final Four. We lost four guys off that team to the NBA. This year, we’re doing OK. We’re hanging on.” If this is hanging on, Billy Donovan wants to find the rope. Kentucky built a 16-point lead Sunday and then saw it dwindle to two as the Senior Day sellout crowd roared. Davis then showed why he likely will be the national player of the year. He finished with 22 points, 12 rebounds and six blocks. The chances he’ll ever attend Senior Day at Kentucky are approximately zero. Can you blame him? The first pick in the NBA Draft will make $4.2 million a year. The last pick in the first round makes $850,000. If any other college student could make that kind of salary by dropping out and going to work, we’d call it an American success story. With college basketball players, we call it an affront to higher education. As if the world would be better off if John Wall still were working on his "general studies" degree at Kentucky instead of starting for the Wizards.

Calipari is a little self-righteous when he says it’s all about doing what’s best for the kids. But what’s best for the kids is not always best for the school or coach. If Calipari thinks players are NBA-ready, he tells them to go. That’s more admirable than a coach advising a stud to stay, just to enhance the basketball well-being of the school and its fans. “If a kid got hurt, I couldn’t live with myself,” Calipari said. The other option, he ranted, is to recruit players that aren’t good enough to face the NBA quandary. When Calipari tossed that one out, he looked toward Kentucky athletics director Mitch Barnhart. “Keep doing what you’re doing,” Barnhart laughed. He has to. Kentucky has three freshmen starters, and all likely will turn pro after the NCAA Tournament. No problem. Kentucky’s 2012 recruiting class is No. 1 or 2 in the country, depending on what service you subscribe to. Next year’s team will look nothing like this year’s that went unbeaten in the league. But is there any doubt the Wildcats again will be favored to win the SEC, if not more?

Furthering his defense, Calipari notes that two of last year’s freshmen starters did not turn pro. The one who did, Brandon Knight, had a 4.0 GPA and 60 credit hours. This year’s team actually has two seniors. It’s why Calipari gets defensive when Lexington, Ky., is called the "one-and-done capital of America." He really shouldn’t. Calipari didn’t construct the system. He just operates it better than anybody else. “It’s not my rule,” he said. The rule is players have to stay only one year in college. Here’s where Calipari really got on his soapbox. He’d like to see more incentives for players to stay in college ― things like stipends, and easier access to insurance and loans. He wants the NBA to alter its contract rules so that each year a player stays in college will take a year off the first (and cheapest) contract they must sign. “I don’t like the rule,” Calipari said, “but I’m dealing with the rule that’s here.” As Sunday’s performance showed, he’s dealing with them quite well. The '12 Wildcats might be the one that gets it done, the one that gets Calipari the NCAA championship he so covets. And Calipari showed that when he speaks, it’s not always with a forked tongue.
