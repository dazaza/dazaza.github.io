Management and Feed Formula of Yellow Mealworms

Provided by Jindi Agricultural Yellow Mealworm Breeding Base:

Yellow mealworms, commonly known as bread beetles, are juicy soft-bodied insects. They contain 30% fat and over 50% protein. Additionally, they are rich in phosphorus, potassium, iron, sodium, aluminum, and various trace elements, as well as the 16 amino acids essential for animal growth. Every 100 grams of dried yellow mealworm contains up to 847.91 milligrams of amino acids, with its nutritional components ranking first among all types of feed.

Management of Yellow Mealworm Breeding:

1. Temperature Requirements for Yellow Mealworms:

- The optimal breeding temperature for adult insects is 26-28℃.

- Eggs require a temperature of 19-26℃.

- Larvae and pupae thrive best at 26-30℃.

- The most suitable relative humidity is 75-85%.

In summer, ventilation should be paid attention to, while in winter, measures should be taken against cold and to keep warm.

The eggs of yellow mealworms can hatch within 3-5 days at 25℃; lower temperatures delay hatching. Egg-laying is closely related to temperature. Yellow mealworms adapt to a wide range of temperatures, generally between 50-80%. When the insect's body water content approaches suitability, low humidity can inhibit metabolism and delay development, whereas high humidity accelerates development.

During the pupal stage, although the pupa does not eat or move, it still breathes, so it needs to be kept in a ventilated, dry environment with maintained humidity. It should not be sealed or overly damp to avoid the pupa rotting into a yellowish-black color. In the hot southern summers, dead pupae tend to dry out easily, so they should be turned occasionally, and a little mist spray can help keep the pupa skin moist.

2. Water Requirements for Yellow Mealworms:

Yellow mealworms have a drought-resistant habit, but normal physiological activities cannot proceed without water. There are three ways yellow mealworms obtain water from the outside world:

- One is through food, so vegetables like melon rinds, fruit peels, and vegetable leaves must be provided regularly.

- Another way is through absorbing moisture from the air via their skin. In the hot southern season, a few drops of water can be sprayed inside the breeding box to create a humid microclimate.

- Lastly, yellow mealworms can convert water from organic substances within their bodies since the final oxidation of organic matter produces water and carbon dioxide.

3. Light Requirements for Yellow Mealworms:

Yellow mealworms naturally fear light, are active, and move around both day and night, indicating that they do not need sunlight. Female adult insects lay more eggs in darker places than under strong light.

4. Breeding Density of Yellow Mealworms:

For newly hatched larvae, approximately 500,000 can fit in one square meter. For every 500 grams of yellow mealworms, there are about 300,000 first to second instar larvae, 150,000 third instar larvae, 60,000 fourth instar larvae, 30,000 fifth instar larvae, 10,000 sixth instar larvae, 8,000 seventh instar larvae, and 5,000 eighth instar larvae.

A one-square-meter insect box can house about 5 kilograms of yellow mealworms. To breed 500 grams of yellow mealworms, 1.25 kilograms of wheat bran is needed. An individual using leisure time can manage 10 square meters. Calculating each adult reproducing 3000 offspring, at least four generations can be bred annually. After deducting costs, one can produce 200 kilograms of yellow mealworms per year.

When changing paper, spread wheat bran on top, allowing the insects to burrow down and eat. Mix fine feed into balls and scatter green feed on top, letting them consume freely. Adding sufficient wheat bran is enough for larvae. Feeding green feed depends on the temperature. Uneaten feed should be promptly cleared away. Feeding time is usually in the evening when they are more active and voracious.

5. Methods for Breeding Yellow Mealworms:

The quality of breeding methods directly affects the survival rate and yield of yellow mealworms. Disease and pest control during breeding is also crucial. Although yellow mealworms rarely get sick during breeding, one thing to note is that male adults often die after mating. Dead males should be removed promptly to prevent decay and bacterial infections that could harm other insects.

Enemies of yellow mealworms include poultry, livestock, rats, frogs, ants, etc., which should be managed carefully. As long as the above measures are followed, successful breeding is possible.

Feed Formula for Yellow Mealworms:

Requirements for Yellow Mealworm Feed:

In nature, yellow mealworms have a broad range of food sources, so artificial feeds do not need to be overly selective. However, to quickly produce yellow mealworms, feeds such as wheat bran, rice bran, cake powder, cornmeal, grass, carrots, cabbage, etc., can be used. Quail composite feed can also be fed to increase nutrition, with 50% wheat bran being ideal. Both larvae and adults require feeding, typically once every 3-7 days. Although yellow mealworms rarely suffer from diseases, moldy substances should not be fed.

Yellow mealworm feed has a wide and economical source, mainly consisting of wheat bran and various miscellaneous foods, such as discarded fruits, vegetables, leaves, wild grasses, etc. These food sources include the following aspects: When breeding yellow mealworms on a large scale, fermented feed can be used, utilizing materials like wheat straw, leaves, weeds, etc., which are fermented before feeding. Fermented feed not only reduces production costs but is also nutritious, making it an ideal feed for yellow mealworms.

High-fiber agricultural by-products, such as sawdust, wheat straw, rice straw, five-grain stalks, leaves, etc., can be used after fermentation treatment to feed yellow mealworms. The digestive tract of yellow mealworms contains cellulase, allowing them to gradually adapt to digesting woody fibers when fed on sawdust over a long period. Using feed containing woody fibers not only reduces breeding costs but also converts waste agricultural and forestry by-products into high-quality animal protein, avoiding competition with livestock and poultry for feed. This also provides new feed sources for the development and utilization of yellow mealworms.

The processing of yellow mealworm feed differs from that of chicken, pig, and cattle feed. During the process of feeding yellow mealworms, the insect droppings in the insect box often mix with the feed, and yellow mealworms live in such an environment. Therefore, feed hygiene is extremely important. The most critical factor in maintaining good feed quality is the water content of the feed.

When producing yellow mealworms on a factory scale, processing the feed into granules is ideal. Granulated feed has moderate water content and undergoes instantaneous high-temperature treatment during puffing, which serves to disinfect, sterilize, and kill pests. It also causes starch in the feed to saccharify, making it easier for yellow mealworms to digest and absorb. When processing granulated feed, it's best to separately process the feed for small larvae, large larvae, and adults. The diameter of the granules for small larvae should ideally be less than 0.5 mm, while for large larvae and adults, the diameter should be around 1-5 mm. The particle size should facilitate yellow mealworm feeding. Moreover, the hardness of the feed should suit the feeding requirements of different larval stages. Since yellow mealworms have chewing mouthparts, overly hard feed is unsuitable, especially for small larvae, which require softer feed.

For raw materials that cannot be processed into puffed granular feed due to lack of conditions or unsuitability, various feed ingredients and additives can be mixed uniformly, with 10% clear water added (complex vitamins can be dissolved in water and stirred evenly). After mixing, let it dry in the sun for later use.

Feeds with higher starch content can be scalded with 15% boiling water before mixing with other feeds, then dried for storage (vitamins cannot be scalded with boiling water).

Moldy or infested feeds should be promptly aired or placed in drying cabinets or ovens at around 50℃ for 30 minutes until dry. Such treatment prevents mold and pest infestation and kills other pest eggs in the feed.

If freezing conditions are available, infested feed can be sealed in plastic bags and placed in refrigerators or freezers at -10℃ or below for 3-5 hours to kill pests. After freezing, the feed can be dried in the sun for later use.

Feed Formulas:

To enable yellow mealworms to grow and reproduce normally, several feed formulas are provided for reference.

1. Formula No. 1 Yellow Mealworm Feed: Wheat bran 75%, fish powder 4%, cornmeal 15%, sugar 4%, feed-grade complex vitamins 0.8%, mixed salt 1.2%. Processing method is the same as formula No. 1. Primarily used for feeding adult insects during the egg-laying period. Feeding this feed can increase egg production and extend the lifespan of adult insects.

2. Formula No. 2 Yellow Mealworm Feed: Wheat bran 70%, cornmeal 25%, soybeans 4.5%, feed-grade complex vitamins 0.5%. Mix all components uniformly, puff them into granules using a feed pellet machine, or mix with 16% boiling water into lumps, press them into small cakes, and dry them for use. This feed formula is primarily used for feeding production yellow mealworm larvae.

3. Formula No. 3 Feed: Wheat bran 40%, cornmeal 40%, soybean meal 18%, feed-grade complex vitamins 0.5%, feed-grade mixed salt 1.5%. Processing method is the same as formula No. 1. Used for feeding both adult insects and larvae.

4. Formula No. 4 Yellow Mealworm Feed: Pure wheat flour (made from poor-quality wheat and sprouts, containing wheat bran) 95%, sugar 2%, royal jelly 0.2%, feed-grade complex vitamins 0.4%, feed-grade mixed salt 2.4%. Processing method is the same as formula No. 1. Primarily used for feeding adult insects for reproduction and breeding.

5. Formula No. 5 Yellow Mealworm Feed, also called wheat bran feed, involves feeding solely with wheat bran. In winter, wheat bran can be supplemented with appropriate amounts of cornmeal.

Yellow mealworms have diverse diets. Besides the aforementioned feeds, vegetables or fruit peels should be supplemented in appropriate quantities to provide water and vitamin C. During breeding, adjustments to the feed composition ratio can be made based on local feed resources, referring to the above formulas.
