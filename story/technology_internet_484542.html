Five Key Factors: Influencing Customer Loyalty_23429

by tkjmlj12 on 2011-09-02 09:17:49

For financial retail and other service-oriented enterprises, customer loyalty is the foundation for maintaining long-term profitability and winning competitive advantages. Customer loyalty is directly related to five key influencing factors that customers perceive in their service quality, service experience, relationship interaction, concept recognition, and value-added perception.

Service Quality

Service quality is not judged by the company but perceived by the customer. The service quality perceived by the customer is the intrinsic value of the products and services they receive, including product quality, service level, and delivery capability, which are the three most important aspects.

Product quality is just one aspect of service quality. For example, the durability of Miss Shi's water heater represents the static manifestation of the value that customers receive from product quality.

For industries with relatively homogeneous products like financial services, telecommunications, and transportation, service level and delivery capability become even more important.

Service level and delivery capability refer to the process design and action performance of how a company delivers value to its customers. Airlines need to provide comfortable cabin environments and pleasant service experiences for first-class passengers, while courier companies need to have the promised speed and accurate delivery capabilities.

Service Experience

Service experience refers to the psychological feelings and satisfaction that customers have while receiving products and services from a company.

As information technology advances bring about diversified customer touchpoints, the influence of service experience on customer loyalty behavior is increasing. In other words, customer experience has never been as important in influencing customer loyalty as it is now.

A bank’s wealth management client might often receive services at the wealth management center and may also use the bank’s online banking software regularly. Even if the client finds the online banking software provided by the bank very user-friendly, if the client perceives poor service experience during on-site services, it will create an unfavorable impression of the bank's overall service, possibly leading to the loss of the client.

The way information is provided can significantly impact the experience of financial clients. Securities trading clients enjoy both the trading software provided by securities firms and the information services offered. Even if the trading software interface is friendly and operations are convenient, if crucial information is not communicated to the client in a timely manner, it could result in missed investment opportunities, potentially affecting the client's loyalty.

Relationship Interaction

To maintain customer loyalty, interaction cannot be overlooked. Relationship interaction is the two-way communication process between the enterprise and the customer.

Since it is an interactive process, it involves actions initiated by both the enterprise and the customer. On one hand, it includes service communications and relationship maintenance activities proactively initiated by the enterprise towards the customer, such as birthday greetings sent to the customer. On the other hand, it also includes service communication requests proactively initiated by the customer, or the customer actively responding to marketing activities proposed by the enterprise, or the customer voluntarily providing feedback and opinions to the enterprise.

Of course, mere interaction alone cannot guarantee the maintenance of a loyal customer relationship; what is more important is whether such interactions are based on mutually beneficial principles and a service-oriented mindset.

Concept Recognition

Customer loyalty is also related to the conceptual alignment between the customer and the enterprise, especially for service-oriented companies. This conceptual alignment is not only about the design and production philosophy of the company's products but more importantly, the recognition of the company's brand and service philosophy.

Appropriate dissemination of the company's philosophy helps establish a sense of recognition with the customer. This is why many companies provide membership publications to important customers. On one hand, it offers valuable product knowledge and information to the customer, and on the other hand, it allows the customer to better understand and experience the company's service culture and product philosophy.

High-level customer loyalty often stems from the customer's recognition of the company at the conceptual level. Customers tend to support a company due to their recognition of its philosophy. For example, Google successfully achieved ideological recognition through the effective communication of its core values. Apple's philosophy influences every Apple fan through the legend of Steve Jobs and the classic Apple products.

Value-Added Perception

No added value, no loyalty. Just having good service is not enough; it is essential to make the customer feel a long-term sense of added value. Enabling customers to truly feel value-added is a decisive factor for companies to achieve optimal pricing strategies.

Many service companies have very successful lessons and practices in this area. For instance, the frequent flyer membership reward program commonly used in the international aviation industry continues to improve upon the basic flight mileage reward model, establishing a richer and more comprehensive mileage accumulation reward system based on product types and pricing tiers. For frequent flyers, airlines skillfully use this tiered reward system to offer more valuable rewards, continuously stimulating them psychologically, effectively enhancing the perceived value of flight mileage rewards, thereby achieving the expected outcome of encouraging frequent flyers to prioritize choosing their airline for the next trip.
