The Tesla Owner Who Had His Car Catch Fire, Can't Wait To Buy Another

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:37:01

Tesla's electric sports car, the Model S, caught fire after a collision on November 6th, U.S. time. This was the third accident in six weeks since October 1st.

The first fire incident involving the Model S occurred on October 1st in Kent, a suburb of Seattle, Washington, when a black Model S hit an unknown metal object by the roadside, causing the battery to catch fire. The second incident happened on October 18th when a white Model S driven by a drunk driver in Mexico crashed into a concrete wall and eventually hit a tree. The battery pack caught fire, destroying the entire vehicle. The third incident occurred on Wednesday afternoon this week on Interstate 24 in Tennessee, where a Model S collided with a trailer hitch in the middle of the road, damaging the undercarriage and causing the vehicle to catch fire.

All three accidents were caused by the electric vehicle's battery pack being impacted and catching fire — Tesla uses Panasonic's 18650 cobalt lithium batteries, which have a higher explosion probability compared to the lithium iron phosphate batteries used by other mainstream electric vehicles (but offer a higher energy density). However, the outcomes were handled well: the owners expressed understanding and claimed they were still Tesla fans, even stating they would buy another Model S in the future. Of course, the capital market did not show the same leniency (or perhaps they took the opportunity to adjust the bubble that had accumulated over the past 12 months for Tesla), and Tesla's stock price has fallen from $193 to the current $137, a drop of 30%.

The Tennessee accident has also been properly addressed. Tesla's PR department contacted the accident owner and invited him, Juris Shibayama, to write an article on the official Tesla blog detailing the incident. From his account, Tesla once again successfully turned a misfortune into a blessing, promoting the safety of the Model S electric vehicle.

… About 30-45 seconds later, a warning appeared on the console display: "The car needs repair, the car cannot be restarted." I continued driving, hoping to get home quickly. About a minute later, the information on the control screen sounded again: "Please safely pull the car to the side of the road. The car is stopping." Throughout the process, I could fully control the vehicle and safely pulled over to the left side of the road. I got out of the car and started taking all my belongings. About 5-10 seconds after leaving the car, smoke began to emerge from the bottom front of the car. After leaving the car about 100 yards, more smoke began to emerge from the bottom of the car. About two minutes after leaving the car, the front of the car caught fire.

This owner thanked Tesla because his car did not penetrate upon hitting the hitch (the Model S battery pack is protected by a 0.25-inch steel plate between it and the body of the car, while Musk says traditional cars are just a "thin sheet of metal"). Moreover, the car gave the owner five minutes to escape before catching fire. Juris believed the Model S "performed perfectly." He eagerly hoped to buy another Tesla ("I would buy another one in a heartbeat").

In fact, after the first collision and fire incident on October 1st, Tesla CEO Elon Musk explained why their electric cars catch fire after collisions.

If a traditional gasoline car encountered the same thing at high speed, the result could be worse. Traditional gasoline cars only have a thin sheet of metal protecting the bottom, so the fuel supply area or tank is easily damaged, leading to gasoline leaking everywhere, often resulting in the entire car burning. In contrast, our battery pack has only 10% of the combustion energy of gasoline, distributed across 16 modules, with fireproofing between them. Therefore, the probability of a Tesla catching fire is only 1% of that of a traditional gasoline car.

National driving data supports this point: according to statistics from the National Fire Protection Association (NFPA), there are 150,000 car fires annually in the U.S., while traffic department data shows Americans drive 3 trillion miles per year. That equates to one car fire every 20 million miles, whereas Tesla has driven 100 million miles before a single fire incident. This means the likelihood of your traditional gasoline car catching fire is five times greater than a Tesla!

However, even though Musk and Tesla's PR team effectively handled the three collision and fire incidents, technical issues that Tesla should address remain unresolved, specifically how to better manage power safety. For instance, can the 8,000+ cobalt lithium batteries on the car’s underside be effectively isolated in groups after physical impact, so that damage or fire in one group does not affect others? Or perhaps rapid cooling mechanisms could be implemented. Additionally, fires in Tesla electric vehicles are difficult to extinguish and need to be addressed.

In summary, whether through Musk's public relations statements or the clever operations of the PR team, after experiencing three collision and fire incidents, all Tesla owners managed to escape safely without loss of life. This value outweighs even the $6 billion drop in market capitalization. Those who criticize Tesla or electric vehicles have yet to find a truly fatal weapon.
