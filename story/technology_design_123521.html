Introduction:

After witnessing Voldemort's resurrection, Harry spends the longest and loneliest summer of his life. Of course, his aunt and uncle still treat him like a pest, and his cousin Dudley uses him as a punching bag. Even two Dementors escaped from the control of the Ministry of Magic and attacked Harry on Privet Drive. Fortunately, Harry drove away the Dementors with the Patronus Charm.

The sinister Ministry of Magic attempted to accuse Harry of illegally using magic outside school through the Dementor incident, violating the "Reasonable Restraint Act for Underage Wizards" - prohibiting underage wizards from abusing magic outside school, intending to expel Harry. Harry went to the Ministry of Magic for trial, with Dumbledore testifying for him, eventually helping him escape the charges.

Poor Harry, after experiencing a series of incidents during the summer, finally waited for the start of school... Deputy Minister Umbridge became the new teacher for Defense Against the Dark Arts this year. She represents the Ministry of Magic to correct the "bad habits" at Hogwarts. On the first day of class, Harry got into a conflict with her because he couldn't control himself, ultimately being punished with detention. If you think the "detention" mentioned by Professor Umbridge is just copying text, then you're wrong, because she gave Harry a magical pen, using his blood as ink, deeply etching every word onto Harry's hand.

Since Voldemort's resurrection, Harry has been plagued by more and more nightmares. The scar on his forehead created a connection between Harry and Voldemort's thoughts. In one dream, Harry witnessed Ron's father Arthur being bitten by a snake, promptly issuing a warning that saved Arthur's life.

For Harry, Valentine's Day became meaningful for the first time because he had agreed to go to Hogsmeade Village with Cho Chang. However, the date didn't go well; the typical little woman-like Cho needed to be coaxed and cared for, but Harry was clumsy in such matters, resulting in their unpleasant breakup.

Due to betrayal within the DA, Umbridge eventually discovered that Harry was secretly teaching everyone Defense Against the Dark Arts behind her back. Everyone scrambled to escape, but only Harry was caught because he stayed behind – the informant turned out to be Cho Chang, and Dumbledore took the blame for Harry, being expelled from Hogwarts.

It turns out Voldemort had always wanted the prophecy orb hidden in it concerning his future fate... He infiltrated Harry's mind, learning that Sirius Black was the most important person in his life, luring Harry to the Ministry of Magic with a false image. A fierce battle ensued, but what Voldemort didn't expect was that although the DA members were just teenagers, they fought the Death Eaters evenly until the Order of the Phoenix arrived as reinforcements. In this battle, both the Death Eaters and the Order of the Phoenix suffered casualties, but none compared to Harry's pain - his last remaining relative also left him – farewell, Sirius Black.

Behind the Scenes:

[New Director, New Attempt]

In his fifth year at Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry, it was a crucial year for Harry Potter because he was no longer a child but learning to face the choices and challenges adults must make: Harry not only dealt with Voldemort's return but also endured the pain of losing his friend Cedric Diggory. Everything happening to him forced him to mature mentally faster than other peers of his age, as he bore unexpected major responsibilities... To find an entry point into Harry Potter's magical world, director David Yates did a lot of preparation before shooting, thoroughly analyzing the background of the entire Harry Potter series: "This episode plays a very important role in the whole novel series because by this point, our young protagonist is gradually growing up, and the surrounding situations are becoming increasingly complex. They begin to discover the bumps in this world and experience 'the troubles of growing up.'"

As the producer of the Harry Potter series, David Heyman expressed that it was precisely the unique characteristics of this story that led him to ultimately choose David Yates to direct the film: "Yates excels at handling fantasy themes and has demonstrated the ability to address political issues in a highly entertaining way. Of course, while Harry Potter 5 is not a politically heavy film, it does extensively portray the politics followed in the magical world. One could say that Yates joined with boundless passion, and watching his process of building trust with the children reveals that Yates is indeed an expert at motivating actors. He understands that the young actors who have grown up alongside the series know their roles better than anyone else, so Yates often encourages them to boldly express their thoughts and inject their true personalities into their characters."

Daniel Radcliffe, who portrayed Harry Potter for the fifth consecutive time, said: "I enjoy working with David Yates and appreciate his gentle demeanor. However, the fifth installment was the hardest movie I've filmed partly due to the uniqueness of this chapter and partly because of the new director Yates. He always pushes me to analyze the character of Harry more deeply, and I found this approach necessary. He truly is a talented genius director."

Rupert Grint, who played Ron Weasley, agrees: "David Yates' directing style differs greatly from previous directors, but he achieves great results in the easiest way possible." Emma Watson, who portrayed Harry's ever-loyal friend Hermione Granger, added: "What makes Yates adorable is how seriously he listens when we talk about our characters. He sincerely admires that we can continuously portray the same character in five films. He values the relationship between myself, Daniel Radcliffe, and Rupert Grint because it enhances the authenticity of the friendship between our characters in the film."

Besides David Yates, screenwriter Michael Goldenberg is also new to the Harry Potter series: "When David Heyman approached me, I was thrilled to the core. My statement might sound clichéd, but joining this magical series and bringing it to life on the big screen through your efforts is an indescribable feeling. Although Yates gave me ample creative space, he had one requirement for the script: every moment of the story must be as authentic as possible. Clearly, the primary task of the script is to preserve the essence and soul of the original novel. One could say that the fifth episode is the most critical part of the entire series. Harry transforms from a child into a confused teenager, beginning to realize that nothing is absolute: bad people have good sides, and good people have evil sides... He discovers that the adult world isn't as perfect as imagined and has flaws – these are the themes we need to highlight in the film."

[Magic Ministry]

Before Harry faces Voldemort's challenge, there are other issues to resolve, such as escaping the ordeal of being summoned to the Ministry of Magic for trial... Here, the Ministry of Magic's hall is most vividly depicted, where art designer Stuart Craig used a large poster of Minister Fudge with a Soviet feel to cover an entire wall. Craig also pointed out that despite wizards in the Ministry flying instead of walking and using paper airplanes instead of phones, it cannot hide the fact that it is essentially a purely bureaucratic institution: "In the UK, government buildings have distinct 19th-century Victorian architectural features, adorned with numerous decorations. Since the Ministry of Magic is set underground, our first step was to visit all the oldest subway stations in London to feel their cold atmosphere and design styles. We noticed that many subway stations use large amounts of colorful tiles for decoration – combining this feature, we invented an underground world – the Ministry of Magic. After covering each wall with beautiful and solemn black tiles, the grandeur immediately became apparent. However, this posed a significant challenge for cinematographer Slawomir Idziak, as he had to consider the reflection of the tiles while filming."

Throughout all the Harry Potter films, the art design of the Ministry of Magic is arguably the largest set piece, exceeding 200 feet long, 120 feet wide, and 30 feet high, with at least 30,000 tiles – all manually glued. Moreover, in the film, this hall will be digitally enhanced to appear even larger.

Harry entered the Ministry of Magic accompanied by Mr. Weasley through the "Visitor Entrance" – looking like an ordinary phone booth on a London street, Stuart Craig laughed: "I believe such a setting makes the Ministry of Magic located directly below the Muggle government building more interesting, so we chose a phone booth near the Ministry of Defense… Muggles would surely be shocked to learn that the Ministry of Magic is beneath the British Ministry of Defense." David Yates also stated: "Perhaps the most intriguing storyline of the Harry Potter series is showcasing how the magical world 'hides' within Muggle lives. In fact, these two worlds are intertwined, just unnoticed by Muggles."

[pink represents a new kind of "dark force"]

In the new semester, a new Defense Against the Dark Arts teacher arrives at the school – Professor Umbridge, portrayed by Imelda Staunton. Wrapped in pink from head to toe, Professor Umbridge possesses a practiced fake smile and a sickly sweet voice, but her nature is completely the opposite. David Yates expressed: "Fudge believed Dumbledore was coveting the position of Minister of Magic, so he sent his most trusted deputy, Umbridge, to investigate the truth at Hogwarts. But Umbridge believes her mission is to clear all potential threats for Fudge, causing chaos at Hogwarts, making her a wolf in sheep's clothing." Staunton agreed: "Many people are like Umbridge, charming on the surface but full of malice inside, so playing such a character is a challenge I am willing to accept."

Although it was to remain consistent with the original novel, Imelda Staunton sometimes felt annoyed playing the role of Umbridge: "In the book, she is described as very ugly, resembling a repulsive toad, so when someone told me 'you are the most suitable person for this role,' it didn't feel comfortable."

Imelda Staunton is the actor closest to costume designer Jany Temime among all the actors because designing the overall image of Umbridge was one of Temime's most challenging tasks in the entire film, Staunton said: "We came up with many interesting ideas for this slightly plump lady, I didn't want her clothes' silhouette to be too sharp – although her first impression is gentleness and kindness, her heart is not really that way."

To display Professor Umbridge's "gentleness" externally, Jany Temime stated: "We stuffed a lot of things into Imelda Staunton's costumes because she is particularly slim." Temime also used many soft fabrics on the clothes to enhance the sense of gentleness in her character. However, the color of the clothes was already determined in the novel: pink, pinker, and pinkest. Temime continued: "Every time we see her, her pink clothes are different in style, except for the color. As she gradually displays her privileges at Hogwarts, the brightness of the colors increases, becoming extremely glaring, eventually turning into the darkest cherry pink."

The pink theme extends to Professor Umbridge's office, decorated with various pink items by art director Stuart Craig and his design team, including lacey, velvet-like curtains and various cute little ornaments around the room. The furniture style originates from France, described by Craig as "very linear" – hinting subtly from all aspects that the owner of this office has a strong personality. However, the most distinctive feature of this office lies in the 200+ plates on the walls with kittens printed on them, suggesting Umbridge's feline-like, unpredictable nature. In contrast, the classroom used by Professor Umbridge appears serious and simple, reflecting her strict teaching style – allowing students only to sit quietly and study her designated textbooks. Rupert Grint said: "Students find Professor Umbridge's Defense Against the Dark Arts class unfamiliar, but she firmly believes we must study theory without any practical application, which is absurd in a magic school."

[First Kiss under Mistletoe]

When Hogwarts enters the holiday season due to the upcoming Christmas, Harry's DA study group must take a break for a while. After the last class, all DA members left in pairs, except for Cho Chang. Harry had liked her since last year, but their relationship became somewhat complicated due to the murder of Cedric Diggory. It seems the all-knowing Room of Requirement anticipated their hidden feelings and decorated the scene with mistletoe during the last DA gathering, finally bringing the moment all Harry fans eagerly awaited – Harry's first kiss.

Daniel Radcliffe recalled the filming of this part: "I was a bit uneasy because I noticed Katie Leung seemed nervous. This wasn't just a kiss but also hinted at the complex relationship between Cho and Harry. After trying several times, we realized it wasn't that scary and was quite fun." Katie Leung continued: "I was especially nervous because it was my first kiss on the big screen, fortunately, director David Yates was very professional. He told us what effect he wanted, so we didn't feel this scene was difficult to shoot. At first, it was a bit awkward, but Daniel Radcliffe was great at leading the scene, making the shooting simple, and he is an excellent kisser."

The director's approach may have alleviated the concerns of the two young actors but overlooked the equally nervous staff nearby because many of them had watched Daniel Radcliffe grow up with the Harry Potter series. Producer David Heyman said: "Many of us knew Radcliffe when he was ten years old, and we've watched him grow up... So we care about him and want to protect him. Watching him experience his 'screen first kiss' here feels strange, I kept telling myself not to look, but I couldn't help it. Luckily, this scene was shot perfectly, and I believe the audience will definitely feel the tenderness and sweetness."

[Giant Brother Grawp]

Knowing he would eventually be expelled by Umbridge from Hogwarts, gamekeeper Hagrid assigned Harry, Ron, and Hermione a special task: help take care of his half-brother Grawp – who happens to be a 16-foot tall giant.

To bring Grawp to life on the big screen, this combined the collective wisdom of manual design, motion capture, computer effects, and the brilliant performance of an actor named Tony Maudsley. David Heyman said: "In our assumption, Grawp is a very innocent giant, just a bit hyperactive and unable to concentrate for long periods. Maudsley and David Yates spent a long time discussing some performance details because Grawp is a digital character requiring 'motion capture' technology." Yates praised Maudsley's performance: "He fully immersed himself, evident from every subtle performance detail. Even though the character is almost entirely generated by digital technology, Maudsley gave Grawp life and soul."

When Grawp is with Hermione, you can see from their relationship that Grawp is not just a monstrous giant, Emma Watson said: "Grawp has a cute side; in front of Hermione, he is as docile as a kitten, and Hermione is the only one who can calm him down... I know Grawp is a digital giant made by computers, but the staff have made him seem real. He has puppy-dog eyes, and I love him so much."

Biological effects artist Nick Dudman said they also made a full-size head of Grawp for scenes with other actors, Rupert Grint excitedly said: "Filming with Grawp was fantastic; there were huge heads and shoulders on set, so realistic that we forgot we were only dealing with part of his body. My favorite part was when Grawp liked Hermione so much that he lifted her up. Ron got jealous and tried to make Grawp put Hermione down, attempting to be the hero by hitting the giant... You can guess the result; Ron was immediately thrown off by Grawp."

[Five Cuts]

Since Harry Potter and the Order of the Phoenix is the book with the most pages in the seven-book series, reaching 870 pages – the page count for the seventh book, Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows, has been set at 784 pages. Therefore, the screenwriter of the film could only painfully cut out parts of the novel that were "insignificant." There are now five confirmed cuts in content.

1. All scenes related to Quidditch... This isn't hard to guess, as Harry was mostly confined to detention by Professor Umbridge in Harry Potter 5, not only missing Quidditch training but also skipping several important matches. However, this is undoubtedly bad news for Rupert Grint, who portrays Ron, as Ron will participate in the Gryffindor Quidditch team for the first time in this episode. Grint can only regretfully say: "Hope for next year." Since there are no Quidditch matches, we won't hear the song "Weasley Is Our King" related to it.

2. Disputes between Mrs. Weasley and Sirius Black regarding Harry at the Order of the Phoenix headquarters... Cut.

3. House-elf Dobby no longer appears... In Harry Potter 4, all of Dobby's parts were replaced by Neville, so it's not surprising why this character missed out on the fifth episode. Perhaps the only one unhappy about this is Hermione because it clearly violates the "Society for the Promotion of Elfish Welfare" (S.P.E.W.) she founded.

4. Neville's parents... They were once brave Aurors who went insane due to Voldemort's torture and are now recuperating at St. Mungo's Hospital for Magical Maladies and Injuries. This is truly unfortunate news because as Neville's role becomes increasingly important in each episode, his parents should have received corresponding "respect," rather than being mercilessly cut out.

5. Harry and others cleaning up the Black family home, the headquarters of the Order of the Phoenix... Very regrettable because we won't get to see the magical creatures there come to life.
