Zinc oxide (ZnO), commonly known as zinc white, is an oxide of zinc. It is sparingly soluble in water but dissolves in acids and strong bases.

### Properties

- **Appearance**: White to light yellow powder or hexagonal crystal.

- **Odor**: Odorless.

- **Taste**: Bitter.

- **Behavior under pressure**: Sublimes under anomalous pressure.

- **Reactivity with air**: Absorbs carbon dioxide from the air.

- **Thermal behavior**: Turns yellow when heated to 300°C, but returns to white upon cooling.

- **Solubility**: Soluble in dilute acetic acid, mineral acids, ammonia water, ammonium carbonate, and alkaline hydroxide solutions. Practically insoluble in water.

- **Density**: Relative density 5.67 (hexagonal crystal), (d204) 5.607.

- **Melting point**: Over 1800°C.

- **Refractive index (nD)**: 2.0041 (2.0203).

### Storage

Sealed storage in a cool place is recommended. Avoid damage, moisture, contamination, and contact with acids.

### Mechanical Properties

The hardness of zinc oxide is approximately 4.5, making it a relatively soft material. Its elastic constants are smaller than those of III-V semiconductor materials like gallium nitride. Zinc oxide has good thermal stability and conductivity, with a high boiling point and low thermal expansion coefficient, making it useful in ceramics.

Among various semiconductors with tetrahedral structures, zinc oxide has the highest piezoelectric tensor, making it a key material for mechanical-electrical coupling.

### Electrical Properties

At room temperature, zinc oxide has a band gap of about 3.3 eV, making pure zinc oxide colorless and transparent. The high band gap provides advantages such as high breakdown voltage, strong electric field retention, low electronic noise, and high power tolerance. Incorporating magnesium oxide or cadmium oxide can vary the band gap between 3-4 eV.

Even without doping, zinc oxide exhibits n-type semiconductor characteristics, which were previously thought to be related to non-stoichiometry in compound atoms. Research on doped zinc oxide serves as a counterexample. N-type semiconductor properties can be adjusted using elements like aluminum, gallium, indium, chlorine, or iodine. Making zinc oxide into a p-type semiconductor is more challenging. Useful dopants include alkali metals like lithium, sodium, potassium, group V elements like nitrogen, phosphorus, arsenic, and metals like copper or silver, though they require special conditions to be effective.

### Preparation Methods

Zinc oxide exists naturally in red zinc ore but with low purity. Industrially produced zinc oxide is typically obtained by burning zinc or roasting sphalerite. Global annual production is around 10 million tons, with several production methods:

#### Indirect Method

The raw material for the indirect method is metallic zinc ingots or zinc slag obtained through smelting. Zinc is converted into zinc vapor at 1000°C in a graphite crucible, then oxidized by air to form zinc oxide particles, which are collected after cooling. This method was popularized by French scientist LeClaire in 1844, hence its alternative name, the French method. The process is simple but costs are heavily influenced by raw material prices.

Zinc oxide particles produced by this method have diameters ranging from 0.1 to 10 micrometers and a purity of 99.5%-99.7%. By total production volume, the indirect method is the primary way to produce zinc oxide. It is used in industries such as rubber, varistors, and paints. The heavy metal content of zinc ingots or slag directly affects the impurity content of the product. Low heavy metal content products can be used in livestock feed, pharmaceuticals, and healthcare.
