There is a cross-over fold.

by zlockw02 on 2011-06-13 11:30:18

Enterprises that participated in the survey are optimistic about the future trend of economic prosperity. More than half of the enterprises believe that it is very likely to achieve performance for the year 2009.

After a year since the crisis, the actions taken by enterprises in response to the financial crisis have not changed fundamentally. Among the cost reduction measures, human resource costs have become one of the three main methods adopted by enterprises. Wage freeze and reducing salary growth rate have become the mainstream of human resource policies.

61% of enterprises have reduced promotion scope; 33% of enterprises have cut training budgets, leading to dual challenges in career development and capability enhancement.

However, senior professional/technical talents as the backbone force of enterprises continue to be favored. The crisis has also prompted a decrease in the resignation rate of excellent employees, resulting in an overall improvement in employee competence levels.

Affected by the international financial crisis, the macro environment for enterprise survival and growth in our country continues to deteriorate. Many enterprises have had to adopt various cost-saving measures due to business contraction. Among these measures, reducing human capital investment and cutting human resource management-related costs have become the primary choices for most enterprises. Salary cuts, layoffs, and welfare reductions have become buzzwords during the crisis period, directly impacting the strategic planning and development of enterprise human resources.

In such a severe situation, how to respond to changes in human resource policies and how to uncover hidden talent opportunities within the crisis are the most pressing concerns for every HR department. As a leading human resource management company, GZ Consulting conducted continuous tracking surveys on human resource policies under special circumstances for 123 enterprises across 26 industries nationwide. Through the analysis of corporate data related to compensation, incentive plans, welfare plans, talent development, and employee relations, GZ Consulting delved into the challenges and opportunities faced by human resources.

According to GZ Consulting's survey on Chinese human resource policies under special economic conditions in 2009, Chinese enterprises have shown an increasingly optimistic attitude towards future economic performance. Reflecting on their own performance over the past year of the economic crisis, 44% of surveyed enterprises gave relatively satisfactory evaluations, believing that their market performance was comparable to competitors. Additionally, 27% of enterprises were highly satisfied with their performance, showing better sales revenue and profit growth compared to competitors. On the other hand, 9% of enterprises believed that the crisis significantly impacted their performance, even falling behind competitors.

Regarding whether the crisis presents more opportunities or challenges for enterprise growth, 62% of enterprises believe that opportunities outweigh challenges, while 16% hold the opposite view, believing that challenges are greater.

Expectations for 2009 performance and economic recovery: Optimistic or pessimistic?

Compared to September 2008 when the financial crisis erupted, the proportion of organizations holding an optimistic view of economic prospects (measured by expectations of economic recovery) increased to 65%. Moreover, 15% of surveyed organizations believed that the economic crisis did not affect them. Organizations adopting a cautious stance accounted for 12%, while the proportion of观望enterprises dropped significantly to 9%.

Actions taken by organizations in response to the financial crisis: How to take action?

One year after the financial crisis, compared to the outbreak period, there was no significant change in the actions taken by organizations to respond to the crisis. Facing uncertain market growth prospects, maintaining continuity in the actions taken during the crisis period is necessary. Notably, the ranking of consolidating existing market positions rose from fourth place a year ago to second place now, indicating that although developing new markets is important, maintaining existing market positions without decline may be crucial for sustaining competitiveness. Meanwhile, the importance of encouraging innovation slightly decreased, while the importance of improving service satisfaction slightly increased, reflecting the "defensive strategy" adopted by organizations in response to economic uncertainty.

Major cost-cutting measures: Is the reduction of human resource-related costs necessary?

The major cost-cutting measures at the outbreak of the financial crisis and one year later showed no significant change in importance;

The most likely cost-saving measure, reducing human resource-related investments, rose from fourth place originally to third place;

Salary increase rates reflect enterprises' confidence in future economic prospects: Salary adjustment amplitude correlates with the enterprise lifecycle.

To address the impact of the financial crisis, enterprises adopted salary freezes and reduced salary increase rates to ease labor cost pressures.

For start-up enterprises, 43% of surveyed enterprises implemented salary freezes, significantly higher than the market average of 37%; meanwhile, 14% of enterprises chose to increase salaries, demonstrating their confidence in being unaffected by the crisis. Although growing enterprises used salary freezes less frequently than the market average, reducing salaries remains a common practice, reaching 41% among surveyed growing enterprises.

Impact of the financial crisis on short-term incentive plans: How do enterprises adjust short-term incentive plans to respond to the crisis?

Among surveyed enterprises, more than half (55%) clearly stated that they would not change their existing bonus calculation and distribution policies due to the financial crisis. 41% of enterprises planned to reduce total bonus pool amounts to address the "burden" of labor costs on business operations.

For enterprises that maintained total bonus pool amounts, 16% and 20% respectively adopted measures to stop salary increases and reduce salary growth rates to save on human resource expenses.

Changes in welfare plans: How to understand the role of welfare in motivating employees?

An economic crisis can lead to short-sighted actions by enterprises, which may cut long-term incentive measures beneficial for sustained corporate growth in order to get through difficult times. In reality, some excellent companies choose to retain long-term incentives during crises, sending a positive signal to employees that the company will not relax its efforts in discovering, cultivating, and incentivizing talents because talent development requires time. If key talents are not retained during a crisis, when the crisis ends and the company needs a large number of talents, it might miss growth opportunities due to a lack of talent.

Talent cultivation and development policies: Should talent cultivation and development be halted during a crisis?

Promotions: Survey results show that 61% of surveyed enterprises reduced the scope of promotions. It is well-known that promotions generally come with salary increases. According to "GZ Consulting," the level of salary increase for promotions is approximately twice that of regular salary adjustments. Therefore, it is clear that reducing promotions is a commonly used tactic by enterprises to effectively prevent rapid increases in labor costs.

Succession planning: Half (51%) of respondents did not alter their company's succession plans, while 30% of companies were considering revising established succession plans.

Talent retention: Only 16% of surveyed enterprises adjusted their talent retention plans, and 60% of enterprises did not plan to change existing strategies. As mentioned earlier, even if an enterprise chooses to reduce labor costs during a crisis, talent remains an object that the organization should retain to prepare talent resources for business expansion when the economy recovers.

Training budget: Compared to promotions, succession planning, and talent retention policies, organizations showed three types of differences in training budget expenditures with similar proportions. 33% of surveyed enterprises decided to reduce training budgets. According to a study by "GZ Consulting," 70% of training costs are spent on external training, which is often the first part to be canceled by enterprises. Notably, 23% of enterprises still increased their training budgets. This is because some enterprises hope to use the relatively "idle" internal management time during the crisis period to enhance employee capabilities and prepare for the upcoming economic recovery.

Training time: Training time generally has a positive correlation with training budgets, so the changes are basically similar.

Labor market activity: Who can remain undefeated in the game of layoffs versus recruitment?

Survey results show that only 11% of surveyed enterprises adopted "layoffs" to deal with the crisis; meanwhile, 10% of enterprises temporarily stopped recruitment activities;

15% of surveyed enterprises were not affected by the financial crisis, and recruitment continued according to original plans; survey results also showed that 15% of enterprises emphasized strengthening recruitment work.

Nearly half of the enterprises (49%) said that although the crisis affected recruitment plans, they still focused on scarce and high-quality talents in demand in the market. Since the crisis began, due to enterprise downsizing, stopping promotions, and other policies, a group of outstanding talents encountered bottlenecks in career development, temporarily halting their internal career advancement opportunities within the enterprise. Therefore, some outstanding talents sought new opportunities in the market, providing opportunities for other enterprises to attract top talents.

Who can remain undefeated in the game of layoffs versus recruitment?

According to "GZ Consulting" experience, whether it is general organizations or knowledge-based organizations, the group represented by professional and technical knowledge, known as "senior professionals," are "scarce" resources within organizations. They mainly exhibit the following characteristics: they are the backbone of technical/professional expertise within the organization, a reservoir for promoting outstanding talents, and simultaneously the group most significantly affected by career development bottlenecks with relatively higher resignation rates.

During layoff periods, enterprises, in order to ensure stable business levels and anticipate the need for talent during economic recovery, will not easily abandon groups of senior professionals with rich professional/technical experience, especially during crises when maintaining existing customers and preserving current technical standards become important means for enterprises to protect their existing market positions. Senior professionals are precisely the critical forces playing roles in this context. Therefore, compared to other occupational classes, they face relatively lighter impacts from layoffs during crises.

Additionally, senior professionals in organizations encounter "ceiling effects" in career development, with their own career advancement paths limited. Coupled with high favoritism from external markets towards this group, they become tight and relatively scarce resources in the talent market. For the reasons mentioned above, if this group encounters career development bottlenecks within the organization, they can easily find new career advancement opportunities in the external market.

In summary, senior professional talents are a group in high demand in the labor market, with their growth opportunities relatively less affected by external market fluctuations.

Impact of changes in human resource policies: Are the policies appropriate? Do they bring positive or negative impacts to the enterprise? Are there any potential crises?

Employee complaints:

30% of surveyed enterprises reported that complaints caused by related human resource policies increased compared to usual.

Work enthusiasm:

27% of surveyed enterprises reported a decline in employee work enthusiasm.

Retention of top talents:

Although 44% of surveyed enterprises reported a decrease in the resignation rate of top talents, 12% still faced the reality of an increased resignation rate of top talents.

Overall employee skill levels:

Survey results showed that 37% of surveyed enterprises believed that the overall skill levels of employees improved during the crisis, which is a positive sign. On one hand, enterprises utilized the unfavorable external market conditions to provide internal training, saving relatively abundant time and enhancing skills; on the other hand, the crisis tested the employee team, allowing truly talented top talents to stand out.
