"Food and accommodation provided, 58,000 yuan a year, plus an additional 900 yuan pocket money each month for such 'foreigners'"

by ab8v34t565 on 2012-03-07 22:53:13

Mobile phone My Sohu Kaixin.com Renren.com Douban Sohu Weibo

Times News: 21,000 yuan for three months, 35,000 yuan for six months, and 58,000 yuan for twelve months. Would you be willing to spend this amount of money to hire a "foreign tutor" for your child? This is a project launched by an educational institution in Hangzhou.

This program, known as the Au Pair scheme, involves young people from abroad living with a family, taking care of and educating the children, as well as performing light household chores. In return, the family provides free accommodation and a small allowance. In other words, it's a "foreign tutor."

Providing a single room and pocket money

Yesterday afternoon, the educational institution held a briefing on the au pair project. According to Xie Zhenyu, the general manager of the educational institution, his organization collaborates with foreign educational institutions, which select au pairs based on certain basic criteria. For example, they must be between 18-29 years old, in good physical and mental health, have a high school diploma or above, no criminal record, fluent English skills, and a strong interest and basic understanding of Chinese culture and language.

At the same time, Chinese families can also make requests for their "foreign tutors." The organization allows Chinese families to select candidate "foreign tutors" and communicate with them via the internet and telephone. If both parties agree, the "foreign tutor" will come to China, undergo short-term training, and then move into the Chinese family. The Chinese family needs to provide an independent room for the "foreign tutor." If the "foreign tutor" stays for three months, the Chinese family needs to pay 21,000 yuan to the organization; for six months, it's 35,000 yuan, and for twelve months, it's 58,000 yuan. Additionally, the family must give the "foreign tutor" 900 yuan per month as pocket money.

Parents are most concerned about communication issues

At the briefing, Allen, the "foreign tutor" from a pilot family in Hangzhou, answered questions from parents on-site. Allen is Dutch and a university graduate. She started working with Mr. Wu's family last September as a "foreign tutor" for his seven-year-old son.

Parents attending the au pair project briefing generally worry about being unable to communicate with foreigners. Some parents don't know any English, and some families include elderly members. Allen frankly admitted that the biggest challenge she faced after four months in the family was communication barriers. She said that once, she told the parents she would be coming home late at night, but they couldn't understand what she meant, resulting in them worrying when she returned late. Xie Zhenyu said that indeed, communication requires adjustment from both sides. Ideally, one parent in the family should have good English skills. If no one speaks English, the organization will introduce "foreign tutors" with basic Chinese skills to these families.

A parent surnamed Zhang, whose son is three years old, is concerned that the "foreign tutor" may stay too short a period. Although the child's English might improve during this time, after one or two years, the child will return to a fully Chinese environment, diminishing the effect of the "foreign tutor." Allen said that actually, while the "foreign tutor" is with the child, they will cultivate the child's interest in English, making them feel proud of speaking English. Watching English cartoons, etc., can help the child develop the habit of self-studying English.

Additionally, some parents are worried about whether special meals need to be prepared for the "foreign tutor," fearing they won't be accustomed to Chinese food. Allen expressed that she really likes Chinese food and even learns how to cook Chinese dishes from the female head of her host family. Moreover, occasionally, she prepares a Western feast for the Chinese family.

"Foreign tutors" remain in a gray area

Hiring foreign teachers for schools or offering foreign teacher courses at training institutions has become commonplace, but hiring foreigners as private tutors is still a novelty. Besides the concerns parents have about communication problems and the uncertainty of learning outcomes, relevant departments in Hangzhou stated that such operations by this educational institution are illegal.

This responsible person explained that since the educational institution charges a fee, it essentially acts as a mediator, introducing foreigners to families, which falls under labor dispatch. Labor dispatch requires signing labor contracts, but according to Article 5 of the "Regulations on the Administration of Employment of Foreigners in China," employers hiring foreigners must apply for employment permits for those foreigners. After approval and obtaining the "People's Republic of China Foreigner Employment Permit Certificate," they can hire foreigners. The positions foreigners are hired for should meet special needs, lack appropriate domestic candidates, and not violate national regulations. In other words, foreigners who can work in China are generally high-end talents. Clearly, the position of "foreign tutor" does not fall under the high-end category, so these "foreign tutors" cannot obtain employment permits. Even if these "foreign tutors" come to China as interns, they cannot become real "foreign tutors" because Article 34 of the "Regulations on the Administration of Employment of Foreigners in China" prohibits individual economic organizations and citizens from hiring foreigners.

Moreover, this responsible person also pointed out that hiring a "foreign tutor" does not necessarily improve a child's English level. She mentioned that in a northern city, there were cases where local-accented foreign teachers gave a group of children American western accents in their spoken English. She reminded parents to assess the teaching ability and purity of the foreigner's English accent before letting their children take classes with foreign teachers or hiring "foreign tutors."
