From collaborative coding to wedding invitations: GitHub is going mainstream

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 16:02:20

　　From Collaborative Coding to Wedding Invitations: GitHub Is Going Mainstream

　　Bubby Rayber's wedding day was coming too fast. It was already mid-April, and in less than a month, he would be hosting a grand thousand-person celebration in southern India. He needed a quick way to spread the news. So the 28-year-old senior systems engineer at LinkedIn turned to a site he knew well. Rayber sent out invitations to his friends via GitHub, the social network beloved by programmers.

　　In fact, sending wedding invitations through GitHub is not very common. Founded in 2008, GitHub is a software engineering collaboration platform primarily designed to save your work and track all changes comprehensively. Its content is shared worldwide and open to various comments, which programmers find fascinating. Recently, however, its scope has started to expand beyond its original purpose.

　　As mentioned earlier, wedding invitations have more in common with open-source software projects than you might think. In Rayber's case, shortly after he uploaded his wedding invitation, one guest corrected a typographical error, while another added a charming congratulatory message. Employees at GitHub believe this collaborative approach represents the future: anyone can contribute suggestions to any project, and discussions about solutions are as simple and convenient as posting on Facebook.

　　GitHub co-founder and CEO Tom Preston-Werner said:

We have created an open collaboration process for software development that is so enticing that even non-software projects with high collaboration potential are drawn to it.

　　Last year, Benjamin Lee, Twitter's chief lawyer, drafted a new patent license agreement for engineers on GitHub. Soon after, GitHub users corrected many small grammatical errors. Later, Trishan Arul, the director of operations at Evan Williams' incubator startup, suggested adding some text, which Lee accepted.

　　In a completely different field, Adam Wood, the music director of a Episcopal church in Texas, is trying to upload an outline of Gregorian chants to GitHub. He believes that for choir directors, it is the best platform for sharing and improving various types of music.

　　GitHub, a five-year-old website with 3.4 million users, has become the hottest community in the hacker world. Programmers enjoy sharing project prototypes and crowdsourcing improvements on it. However, the company itself has bigger ambitions: to change how people work. First and foremost, it targets software developers, followed by anyone involved in text editing, such as lawyers, authors, and even civil servants, who will embrace GitHub's collaborative style.

　　For newcomers, GitHub is like a version of Facebook where videos and photos are abstracted into code snippets. But many underlying concepts are similar. You can "follow" other hackers to see what they're working on. You can comment on their code just like commenting on photos on Facebook. You can even "star" projects to express your liking, similar to marking "favorites" on Twitter.

　　But it’s much more than just a social network. People use it to discover interesting new projects, debug and modify them, and try out new ideas. Then with a push button, updates are merged into the project. You can also "fork" a project, which is GitHub's term for copying it. By copying a project, you can modify it and create your own independent version.

　　In reality, people did more than just suggest modifications to Benjamin Lee's Twitter patent. That patent was forked 53 times, including by Arul, a computer science student in Portland, and a bicycle designer in Belgium. These forks evolve separately and may eventually merge back into Lee's original version. This experiment inspired top Silicon Valley law firm Fenwick & West (of which GitHub is a client) to publish a 30-page standard document for startups on GitHub early this year. Ted Wang, one of the firm's partners, said:

I predict that in the future, GitHub will become an actual tool for legal practitioners.

　　This is what Tom Preston-Werner and his GitHub team are building. Anyone can set up free open-source projects on this site, and most users do exactly that. However, through paid services, companies can keep their software and other projects private. Even by installing their own version of GitHub, they can keep project contents confidential. Depending on the project, fees range from a few dollars per month to tens of thousands of dollars per year.

　　Preston-Werner, 34, says he sells better collaboration to his clients, akin to the quality under Jack Welch at General Electric. Preston-Werner said:

General Electric has the Six Sigma philosophy. They adhere to these principles to ensure product quality and build teams. We have the GitHub way. And we believe many people will benefit from it, producing more and better results while enjoying the process.

　　Last year, the company received a staggering $100 million investment from venture capital firm Andreessen Horowitz. The large investment reflects greater ambitions. Andreessen Horowitz partner Peter Levine said:

We firmly believe this is an absolutely special business. What GitHub does for source code can definitely be applied to other fields. Whether GitHub develops it themselves or other companies develop based on it, it will undoubtedly be a very interesting collaboration platform.

　　As an investor, he sees GitHub starting with programmers and believes this model applies to any industry that needs to store, modify, and discuss text, such as law firms, hospitals, banks, and design industries.

　　Many people have tried to sell web-based collaboration tools to large enterprises. Although it's unclear whether companies that bought Microsoft's Yammer product or IBM's Connections software can adapt to GitHub's model, GitHub indeed has advantages that others lack: a large number of loyal enthusiasts who have already set up private projects on GitHub. Through their trials, they will become the fundamental driving force behind encouraging their units to install GitHub for daily work.

　　The Origin of GitHub

　　Beer:30 - half past beer o'clock — is the name of GitHub's weekly two-hour employee meeting. It's also a TED talk. Nearly half of GitHub's employees participate. Outside the window, Giants fans cheered enthusiastically in the April sunshine after defeating the St. Louis Cardinals. Inside, GitHub employees either cradled MacBooks or held beers. On stage, four cameras were locked onto Preston-Werner, dressed in jeans and a black t-shirt behind the podium. He was criticizing the Bay Area's subway system BART, stemming from his unpleasant experience a few weeks ago when he took the subway with his wife and young son to go to the farmers' market. He said:

This is how GitHub came about — finding a bad solution and then trying to create a better one to replace it.

　　Six years ago, Preston-Werner, along with his co-founders Chris Wanstrath and PJ Hyett, were typical Bay Area programmers. Microsoft was trying to keep Preston-Werner from the search startup PowerSet that they had acquired, but failed. Wanstrath and Hyett had already quit their jobs at CNet and founded a website called FarmSpam. Everything hadn't begun yet.

　　Working for big companies left them exhausted both physically and mentally. They hated inefficiency and bureaucracy, as well as the lack of autonomy. Preston-Werner said:

You work somewhere for two years, bored and frustrated to the extreme, and you end up quitting.

　　They met at a sports bar called Zeke and decided to start GitHub as a side project. The "bad solution" they wanted to fix was Linus Torvalds' Git software, the creator of the Linux operating system, which was designed to track changes.

　　Git was great for geeky programmers, but most people encountered many difficulties when installing and using it. Therefore, Preston-Werner and Wanstrath built a website that made it easy to share code managed by Git. They privately released a beta version to their friends in 2008.

　　Within three months, GitHub had 6,000 users. Many of them shared code from their projects. They appointed Hyett as the Chief Operating Officer. Industry companies appreciated the service so much that they were willing to pay for it. Thus, a business plan was born. (Geoffrey Grosenbach, founder of PeepCode, even wrote in an email, "Using it for free makes me uncomfortable. Can I just write you a check?") GitHub's enterprise customers made the site one of the rare social networks that didn't need to flatter advertisers.

　　For those who don't program, browsing the GitHub site feels somewhat confusing. It includes many code repositories, program branches, large blocks of code text, and small snippets called Gists. For non-technical users, these are almost like hieroglyphs, but for engineers, they are full of benefits.

　　GitHub's greatest innovation lies in pull requests. After forking and copying something, GitHub sends a document to the software developer saying, "Hey, I'm doing secondary development on your project. You can see what improvements I've made. Press this button, and these changes will be incorporated into your project." Pull requests make it easy for anyone to correct typos in documents, bugs in software programs, or add new suggestions for legal texts.

　　You can learn a lot from Preston-Werner's GitHub page. GitHub's CEO has established over 40 code repositories, including a very popular blog engine for hackers called Jekyll. Jekyll has been starred over 12,000 times by users and forked over 2,000 times. By GitHub's standards, these numbers are impressive.

　　If you continue exploring, you'll find that Preston-Werner writes far more code than the average CEO. But there are also other interests. One project includes custom-made beer fridge photos from GitHub, which have been forked three times. He has another repository containing his favorite San Francisco vegetarian restaurants, which has been forked 37 times. Most of the changes come from vegetarians correcting typos and adding their own favorite restaurants, bars, and ice cream shops.

　　Preston-Werner hopes his employees can think about problems like redesigning the subway, because he doesn't want them to forget their mission: reshaping how people work. In this regard, he has spent $4 million improving the tools used by employees. The only acquisition the company made was finalized before receiving the $100 million investment from Andreessen Horowitz. It was a five-person company specializing in web developer tools and software, whose products help people share slides online.

　　A $100 million investment from a venture capital firm for a company making source code management software would seem unreachable. But GitHub's social networking attributes make it unique. Jason Pressman, managing director of Shasta Ventures in California, said:

Programmers are the rock stars of the new era. Who could have imagined in the 1950s that rock stars would have such immense commercial value? Programmers are gaining such influence, and therefore GitHub, as the hub for programmers, holds infinite value.

　　GitHub at the White House

　　Nowadays, GitHub has many paying users: Blizzard Entertainment, Etsy, and major cloud service provider Rackspace, among others. Even Microsoft has a public GitHub page among the most important tech companies. GitHub also has many admirers. The company itself shows sharp business acumen, creating a series of themed hoodies, cups, and stickers featuring their five-legged octopus logo.

　　But how exactly GitHub will achieve its goal of changing the way people work is still unclear. Preston-Werner says product roadmaps go against GitHub's corporate culture. He said:

I think the idea of making roadmaps is outdated, and the risk of promising too much and not delivering is very high. So we don't talk about new features until they are released.

　　However, investors Levine and Pressman believe GitHub's strength lies in being a platform. With over three million users, it can enter enterprises in ways similar to Linux or PCs, becoming the shared platform behind custom software. Pressman said:

GitHub can absolutely penetrate large enterprises.

　　Just like how GitHub entered the White House. A year ago, White House CIO Steven VanRoekel began formulating a plan to make government data more accessible. Federal agencies are treasure troves of information, from court records to data on private radio stations held by the Federal Communications Commission. But this data was difficult for software developers to access.

　　VanRoekel worked at Microsoft for 15 years before coming to Washington, tasked with solving this problem. Last summer, his team developed a series of software tools and policy documents as guides for officials.

　　This project, called Open Data, was developed and coded based on GitHub. Initially, the entire project was stored in a private repository, accessible only within the working group under VanRoekel's jurisdiction. When anyone needed to make significant changes, they didn't need to send emails or make phone calls. All they had to do was submit a pull request.

　　In May, VanRoekel's office released the software necessary for developers to access their databases publicly on GitHub. These programs, along with the policy documents of the Open Data project, were all published openly on GitHub. VanRoekel said:

This is the first time the White House has released policies via a GitHub repository. So if you think you're smarter than the officials and want to improve the definition of 'open licenses,' go ahead. Copy the original, improve it, and submit a pull request to the White House.

　　VanRoekel is modernizing the federal government through GitHub, but there is another group of designers attempting to collaborate on 3D printing through GitHub, which could become a new growth point for GitHub. Duane Johnson, a software developer from Salt Lake City, Utah, recently open-sourced his house via GitHub. He is publishing small home improvement 3D print design projects on GitHub, such as an aerator for the sink, a thin carpet fixation sheet for the living room, or a dish rack.

　　Johnson's designs are dense and complex. They aren't as easy to modify as source code or restaurant listings, but Preston-Werner believes his company can improve GitHub using the tools favored by the 3D printing circle. He said:

The revolution in 3D printing technology is ripe, and we are excited to be part of this revolution.

　　In the egg-shaped house (a 1960s retro-style cooling spot with many spherical chairs) at GitHub, there is a 3D printer. The company encourages employees to print good creative ideas into finished products. Last December, employee Yossef Mendelssohn injured his foot and struggled to walk with both medical crutches and regular canes simultaneously. So he designed and printed a mounting device that helped him attach the regular cane to the medical crutches. His code is available for anyone to view.

　　Will Mendelssohn's mounting device become popular? No. But playing with 3D printers is crucial to discovering useful open-source designs. It might take five years to reach conclusions, but once 3D designers and hardware hackers are ready for the open-source revolution, GitHub will be waiting. Preston-Werner said:

If we can make it so that any ordinary person finds 3D printing convenient, then the open-source movement will affect the hardware world just as it has the software world. And that's exactly what I want to do.
