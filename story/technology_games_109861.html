Mei! Playing online games pollutes the environment - a character consumes 4.8 kilowatt-hours of electricity per day - Age1983

by hexiaoyao on 2008-12-08 23:13:06

Next to the computer at the business center of a five-star hotel in Europe, where free internet access is provided, there was a small sign that read: "Please protect the environment." This was an indirect reminder for people not to use the internet without limits, as it consumes electricity generated by power plants. These power plants burn fuel (coal for thermal power plants, uranium for nuclear power plants), leading to the emission of large amounts of carbon dioxide, waste heat, and corresponding nuclear waste.

Rarely do people think while surfing the web, "Am I polluting the environment?" Clicking continuously to send and receive emails, post on forums, write blogs, search for information, send photos, transfer blueprints, play online games, or enter a "virtual world" to experience a "second life"... Exploring the virtual realm, the electricity consumed by the computer itself is relatively insignificant. The largest energy consumer is the server (including air conditioning equipment to maintain constant temperature) that enables these activities. An American science writer discovered that each virtual character consumes as much electricity as a Brazilian individual (Brazil being very hot, with many cooling devices in use). Each virtual character consumes approximately 4.8 kilowatt-hours of electricity daily. To ensure smooth gameplay worldwide, around 4,000 servers must be operational.

To leverage the internet's capabilities, numerous devices are required (computers, data links, routers, servers, etc.), all of which consume electricity, especially servers, which operate 24 hours a day. The faster the server works, the faster the processor runs, and the more heat the data center generates, necessitating air conditioning. It’s estimated that Google has 450,000 servers. A large data center consumes as much electricity as a residential area inhabited by tens of thousands of people, which could be considered a small city in the West. Some rough estimates suggest that to ensure the internet's operation, 14 power plants, each generating 1,000 megawatts, are needed worldwide.

The issue of environmental pollution caused by the internet, which has not received sufficient attention, if unresolved, will make the internet responsible for 18.5 million tons of carbon dioxide and 27 tons of highly radioactive atomic waste by 2010. To address this, people are researching whether "clean energy" can be used, meaning no reliance on nuclear or thermal power stations but instead using clean energy from "Greenpeace Energy" (originating from the Greenpeace organization). There are also calls for establishing an "eco-web" and advocating for energy-saving practices in other devices, such as not necessarily using processors with unlimited high power (which is also beneficial for laptop batteries).

Until now, the automobile and aviation industries have been the main targets regarding environmental pollution. Now, computers and communications have become the new focus. However, humanity in the "E" era will not abandon digitization. New measures are constantly emerging. For example, if Google's homepage had a dark background, it could save the electricity output of one power plant, as bright backgrounds consume 74 watts of power compared to 59 watts for dark backgrounds. Experts from the Irish Microcomputer Company and the University of Limerick have jointly developed a new desktop computer equipped with an Intel dual-core processor, a 400G hard drive, Windows Vista operating system, wooden keyboard, and ecological mouse, meeting the EU's "Eco-Flower" environmental standard. This is an extremely modern PC yet highly energy-efficient (only 4 watts).
