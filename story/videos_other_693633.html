[Repost] Criticism is actually a kind of well-meaning encouragement

by koucai4472 on 2012-03-06 17:11:11

There is an ancient Chinese saying: "Criticism makes people progress." Many people have continuously improved themselves through criticism, growing amidst others' doubts, and eventually becoming successful. In fact, many people fear being criticized, which is understandable—no one really wants to be criticized. However, in life, we are bound to make mistakes. When we err, we should be criticized; otherwise, how would we know our own shortcomings?

Actually, we should have learned this in high school politics class—we should view things with a dialectical perspective. Everything has two different effects, it all depends on how you understand it. Everyone views problems from different angles, so the meaning you assign to it will also differ, and thus its significance will naturally vary as well.

For example, being criticized can have two different meanings. An optimistic person believes that others criticize them to remind them where they still need improvement. It's just that the way the other party reminds them may differ. As long as they improve, there will naturally be no more criticism. On the other hand, a pessimistic person might think the other party is just trying to cause trouble for them. They believe they've done everything perfectly and that there shouldn't be so many problems. This ultimately leads to a gradual estrangement in interpersonal relationships. If this person is part of a team, their communication within the team will surely be a complete mess. In fact, it all comes down to how you choose to understand it. As long as you're willing to think positively, the outcome will naturally be good, and vice versa. Today, Koucai Training Network will share with everyone the purpose and significance of criticism.

1. Gold Is Not Pure, No One Is Perfect

An ancient saying goes: "Gold is not pure, no one is perfect." In life, who can avoid making mistakes? There is nothing or event in the world that is truly perfect. Therefore, everyone will make errors in behavior. Koucai Training Network believes that if one has made a mistake, even if they are self-aware and don't cover up their faults, their analysis and reflection on the nature, harm, and root causes of their mistakes will never be as clear as those of numerous onlookers. We need sincere praise, but we also need kind criticism. Praise is encouragement, and criticism is supervision. Praise is like sunshine, and criticism is like dew; both are indispensable. When you understand this point, you will naturally know the significance of criticism.

2. "Good Medicine" Doesn't Have to Be Bitter

The phrases "good medicine tastes bitter" and "honest advice is hard to hear" are often used to warn people to humbly accept criticism without caring about the method of delivery. The method, however, should not be something the criticized person dwells on, but something the criticizer should research and refine. With the development of medical science, many "good medicines" are now coated with sugar or honey-fried, making them no longer bitter. Similarly, with the development of language science, by paying attention to the method and art of criticism, one can achieve "honest advice that doesn't hurt to hear," making it enjoyable for people of all ages. How can we reach this level? Criticism should be kind, respectful, understanding, and trusting towards the criticized person. Focus on the issue rather than the individual, convincing with reason. Address only the flaws and mistakes, not generalizing to negate someone's entire work or history. Further analyze the motive and effect. If the motive is good but the result is poor, first affirm the good intention, then criticize the inappropriate aspects, and finally teach the correct method. Avoid getting angry or blaming before fully investigating the situation, and never mock, ridicule, or insult. Doing so would only create or exacerbate antagonism, leading the other party to resist, pretend agreement while remaining unchanged internally, or go through the motions of a false confession. Such criticism may seem fiery, but its actual effectiveness is minimal.

3. Clever Use of Humor

Generally speaking, when criticism begins, the criticized person's psychology is often tense and oppressed, especially when superiors criticize subordinates or elders criticize juniors. These situations may manifest as anxiety, fear, opposition, resistance, dejection, or discouragement... These abnormal psychological states become obstacles to the exchange of ideas and feelings between the two parties, greatly reducing the practical effectiveness of criticism. If humor is cleverly used, the criticizer can smile while discussing truths and reasoning, and the criticized person can blush slightly while laughing, feeling touched rather than stimulated from within, happily accepting education. Isn't that wonderful? Humor differs from satire, though both have the effect of making people laugh. Satire induces sharp, stimulating laughter, making people feel awkward; humor induces light, warm laughter, subtle and profound, sometimes containing deep wisdom and philosophy, prompting deep thought and introspection.

Clever use of humor in criticism often involves presenting issues in a semi-joking, semi-serious manner, breaking the deadlock before moving on to substantive issues. Even if the other party cannot immediately accept it, it does not damage harmony or make the other party lose face. Therefore, out of kindness, humorous criticism differs from sharp sarcasm or mockery, determined by the criticizer's starting point and attitude. Humorous criticism is not vulgar or mechanical, with healthy thoughts and vivid language, simple yet profound, decided by the criticizer's ideological and cultural cultivation. Chen Yi, serving as the first mayor of Shanghai in New China, often used humorous criticism to properly handle various contradictions within and outside the Party, strengthening the unity of the revolutionary ranks and the solidarity of all social strata, promoting the development of the revolutionary cause.

Humorous criticism in social activities, after all, is different from performing stand-up comedy or telling jokes. While it contains humorous elements, it still maintains a serious and earnest tone. The atmosphere can be relaxed and lively, but must never be frivolous, otherwise it would dilute the necessary seriousness of criticism, affecting its effectiveness. Similarly, you should also be aware of etiquette. Therefore, exaggeration, contrast, homophones, and humor all need to be kept within certain limits. The criticizer can smile, but should not burst into laughter, and must absolutely avoid pointing fingers or gesticulating wildly.
