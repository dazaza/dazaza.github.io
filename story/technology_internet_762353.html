Why Tencent and Chery are fighting for the QQ trademark: neither can afford to lose

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:05:02

Does Tencent produce cars? The answer to this question is no. However, as early as 2005, the company applied to register the "QQ" trademark for goods such as cars in Class 12 and was approved in 2008. But this move did not sit well with Chery Automobile Co., Ltd., a car manufacturer. In 2009, Chery filed an application with the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board (TRAB) to revoke Tencent's automobile-related trademarks on grounds of identical or similar trademarks for similar goods, and received support. Following the revocation of its trademark, Tencent took the TRAB of the State Administration for Industry and Commerce to court. Yesterday, the case was heard at the First Intermediate People's Court, where plaintiff Tencent, defendant TRAB, and third party Chery faced each other in court.

It is well known that in China, companies associated with "QQ" are none other than Tencent Technology and Chery Automobile, which belong to different sectors. Tencent has established itself in the internet sector, while Chery's QQ cars have cumulatively sold over a million units. From the chronological use of "QQ," Tencent launched its instant messaging software QQ in 1999, whereas Chery introduced its microcar, Chery QQ, in 2003. The issue of who "QQ" belongs to has been a point of contention between these two companies for nearly a decade.

On May 19, 2005, Tencent applied to register the "QQ" trademark for goods such as cars in Class 12 and was granted registration on March 7, 2008. On November 26, 2009, Chery cited violations of trademark law, including the copying and imitation of famous trademarks, identical or similar trademarks for similar goods, and infringement of prior rights, to file an application with the TRAB to revoke the disputed trademark. After review, the TRAB ruled that the registration of the disputed trademark constituted the preemptive registration of another party’s already used and influential trademark under Article 31 of the Trademark Law, and thus revoked Tencent's registered trademark.

Tencent argued that the contested ruling made by the TRAB contained obvious errors and omissions, stating that the disputed trademark did not violate legal provisions and should be maintained. Its representative introduced that on August 31, 2001, Tencent had applied to register the "QQ" trademark for services in Class 38, such as "message transmission, computer terminal communication." Through extensive publicity and promotion, the number of registered users of QQ communication services grew from 5 million in 2000 to over 200 million in 2003. Therefore, before Chery Automobile used the "QQ" trademark, Tencent's "QQ" trademark had already become a famous trademark. In 2009, Tencent had obtained a certificate of recognition as a famous trademark from relevant national departments, and any further use of the "QQ" trademark by other companies would constitute an infringement.

Chery Automobile, however, believed that Tencent's registration of the "QQ" trademark in the automotive field was malicious. As the third party in this case, Chery's representative stated that the trademark Tencent registered in 2001 was not the "QQ" letters but a graphic. In contrast, Chery Automobile registered the "QQ" letter trademark in the automotive field in 2003. Therefore, the two enterprises were using their respective trademarks in different fields, with Chery's "QQ" trademark in the automotive field predating Tencent's. Moreover, Chery QQ's cumulative sales had surpassed one million units, with advertising costs exceeding 200 million yuan, and deserved protection.

Additionally, Tencent argued that its registration of the "QQ" trademark across multiple domains was protective registration for "QQ." However, Chery Automobile pointed out that from the initial planning of the car name to subsequent promotional campaigns, it relied entirely on the already well-known "QQ" trademark and penguin mascot to achieve its promotional effects. Such infringement could not establish legitimate prior rights.

The verdict for this case will be announced on another day. For both Tencent and Chery, having operated the "QQ" trademark for nearly a decade, neither side can afford to lose this lawsuit.

Is there a third way in the dispute over QQ?

Tencent QQ is the first domestic instant messaging software launched by Tencent in 1999, while Chery QQ is a microcar introduced by Chery in July 2003. Both "QQ"s enjoy high renown in their respective fields.

Journalists found that as early as 2003, when the "QQ" car was introduced, Tencent had raised a trademark infringement dispute with Chery. Tencent explained that due to its busy preparation for going public at the time, it did not sue publicly. Later, Tencent sent a lawyer's letter to Chery, but Chery never provided a substantive response to Tencent.

In late 2005, news about the legal battle between the two parties over the QQ trademark reappeared in the media, but the details of this lawsuit were not disclosed later, leading some media to even speculate it was a publicity stunt.

This time, the trademark dispute between Tencent as the plaintiff, TRAB as the defendant, and Chery as the third party was brought to court. Trademarks are the banners for businesses to stand in the market. For Tencent and Chery, who have cultivated their brands for around ten years, neither side can afford to lose. Some believe that whoever is identified as infringing in this case will face substantial compensation. Is there only a win-lose situation in this trademark dispute without a third way? Tang Guangliang, an intellectual property expert at the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, told reporters that Tencent and Chery could fully collaborate to achieve a win-win situation.

Tang Guangliang said that the role of trademarks is to attract consumer attention, acting as a link between merchants and consumers, and also as the image of merchants in the minds of consumers. Trademarks themselves are tools rather than goals, and how to use these tools depends on each merchant's consideration. In recent years, China's trademark protection has matured, and whether or not a trademark constitutes infringement does not solely depend on the similarity of the two marks; the key lies in whether consumers would confuse them. If there is no confusion, then allowing two or more merchants to use the same mark could expand the influence of the trademark, benefiting all users.

"For example, if consumers buy Chery's QQ car because they think it is produced by Tencent, or if consumers use the QQ chat tool because they like Chery QQ cars, then confusion exists," Tang Guangliang said. If there is no such situation, then both sides do not need to compete. Because regarding trademarks, it is not necessarily owned by whichever merchant claims it, and even if it is owned, seeing another use it does not mean you must eliminate it. As long as the trademark is not maliciously tarnished or the enterprise loses its market due to it, it might even have a promoting effect, allowing both parties to coexist peacefully and share benefits.

Furthermore, Tang Guangliang said that from a rational market perspective, Tencent and Chery have no conflict. Chatting tools are primarily for young people, and QQ cars are also popular among young people. Both sides can fully cooperate, operate mutually, share the market, and achieve a win-win situation without the need to fight over trademark ownership in the absence of harm.
