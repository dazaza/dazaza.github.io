1. Aluminum pigments are used in coatings primarily for their appearance and ability to provide special metallic effects. The pigments often appear flake-like, with a particle diameter much larger than that of general pigments.

2. In most cases, the properties of metal pigments are determined by the production process.

Aluminum pigment production process:

1. Aluminum ingots are melted and sprayed to obtain irregular spray powder, which is then ground into flake particles under solvent and lubricant conditions through ball milling. The resulting pigment suspension is sieved, filtered by a press filter, and mixed to produce a product with 65-70% solid content and 35-30% solvent.

2. Aluminum ingots can also be rolled into aluminum foil, crushed, ball milled, sieved, filtered, and mixed to produce the final product. This product can be directly used after adding specific solvents or special additives in the filter cake. (The grinding parameters and types of lubricants added depend on the final application.)

3. Wetting performance: Lubricants must be added during processing to assist in various production processes. This has a unique effect on the wetting performance of metal pigments. There is an obvious difference between floating and non-floating pigments (wetting performance).

(1) Floating pigments: Due to their high surface tension, floating pigments cannot be wetted by the binder, so they remain on the surface of the wet coating film. This effect is achieved by using stearic acid as a lubricant. In paint systems containing strong polar solvents or binders, floating pigments may become unstable, being wetted by the system and transforming into non-floating pigments. Therefore, this possibility must be considered when designing paint formulations.

(2) Non-floating pigments can be prepared by adding highly polar substances or drying agents to floating pigments, or by directly adding special lubricants (such as oleic acid) during the grinding process. Non-floating pigments can be completely wetted and therefore evenly distributed throughout the entire coating film.

Characteristics of floating aluminum pigments:

1. Extremely high reflectivity (visible light, infrared rays (heat), ultraviolet rays) applied in general industrial coatings, roof coatings, such as tank coatings.

2. Long diffusion pathways for shielding protection, applied in anti-corrosion coatings.

3. Chrome plating effect, applied in decorative coatings.

4. Floatability related to solvent and binder polarity, acidity, and water content. Disadvantages: Coatings can be wiped off, making recoating difficult.

Characteristics and applications of non-floating aluminum pigments:

1. Can be oriented in coatings, applied in primer paints.

2. Completely resistant to wiping, applied in can coatings.

3. Can be topped with a finish coat, applied in coil coatings.

4. Can be combined with colored pigments to achieve multi-color effects in paints and inks, applied in patterned paints, three-dimensional coatings, automotive paints, "flash" effect coatings.

Particle shape, particle size, and particle size distribution:

The aluminum used in production is ductile, so during ball milling, irregular powders formed by atomization can be ground into flake particles with a shape factor (thickness/diameter ratio) of 1:50 to 1:500. Particle shapes can be irregular (pearl flower type) or completely round (silver dollar type). The particle size distribution of metal pigments has a very important influence on optical characteristics during use, so it must be measured during quality control.

Optical characteristics (aluminum pigments):

Aluminum pigments produce a special effect through the specular reflection and diffuse reflection of light on the pigment surface. The following factors directly determine the metallic effect of aluminum pigments:

1. Particle size distribution.

2. Particle shape (pearl flower, silver dollar).

3. Shape factor (thickness/diameter ratio).

4. Surface smoothness (substrate).

5. Parallel orientation degree of pigments relative to the substrate surface.

6. Wetting property (floating/non-floating).

Sometimes it is difficult to distinguish and measure metallic effects directly with the naked eye. Metallic effects mainly depend on the following factors:

Hue Luminance/whiteness Brightness (sparkle and metallic luster) Coloring power (covering power) Color saturation Clarity Angular-dependent color-changing flash.

Hue: Aluminum pigments themselves have no color but can create bright or metallic colors distinct from white and black pigments through light reflection.

Whiteness, brightness, color saturation, angular-dependent color-changing, covering power, and clarity: The reflection and scattering of light on the surface of metal pigments give car paints a metallic flash effect, with these metal pigments oriented and arranged in the coating film. The visual effect depends on the ratio of reflected light to scattered light. The proportion of reflected light increases with the increase in pigment area, while the proportion of scattered light increases with the scattering of light at the edges of the pigments.

The coarser the pigment and the more circular its shape, the higher the proportion of reflected light, making it brighter, whiter, with higher color intensity, and increased angular-dependent color-changing flash (referring to changes in whiteness observed at different angles). Observing the vertical and horizontal surfaces of a car or viewing it from different angles can demonstrate angular-dependent color-changing flash.

The finer the pigment particles and the less regular their structure, the higher the proportion of scattered light, appearing more uniform with stronger light and dark changes, higher covering power, and better clarity. The higher the clarity, the clearer the image on the coating surface, such as houses, clouds in the sky, people, trees, etc. If the whiteness, brightness, angular-dependent color-changing effect, clarity, and covering power are all excellent, undoubtedly this silver slurry will be ideal; however, unfortunately, the relationship between these characteristics and particle size variation is opposite. The successful development of narrowly distributed round aluminum pigments (silver dollar type) largely satisfies the above performance requirements, although further research is needed to improve the covering power. Within a certain range, an aluminum pigment can be produced that simultaneously has good flash, angular-dependent color-changing, brightness, color saturation, and high clarity. (Such as our company's electroplated silver and imitation electroplated silver series)

Pigment orientation: The quality characteristics introduced earlier, such as whiteness, brightness, color saturation, covering power, angular-dependent color-changing flash, and clarity, not only relate to particle size and particle size distribution but also to the orientation of flake aluminum pigments in the coating film. When flake pigments are parallel to the coating surface, the effect is optimal. Poor parallel orientation can cause "haze" or diffuse reflection phenomena. The orientation of flake pigments is related to the formulation and application conditions. The evaporation of solvents causes the shrinkage of the wet coating film, ultimately pressing the aluminum pigments into a horizontal orientation position. The higher the solvent content in the coating, the stronger this effect. This explains the phenomenon of flake pigment orientation, so low-solid-content coatings have better optical properties than high-solid-content coatings. The evaporation of solvents can cause so-called Benard vortices, hindering the parallel orientation of aluminum pigments (causing haze phenomena). At this point, you can use some additives that can fix the flake pigments. You can also use resins to promote the rapid evaporation of solvents (such as cellulose acetate butyrate CAB), but their functions should be tested before application.

Very thin metal pigments are extremely sensitive to high shear forces. Mechanical shear can cause particle deformation or form many fine particles, reducing optical properties. In the worst case, gold glitter powder results from many unprotected metal surfaces leading to chemical reactions, making metal pigments chemically active and able to chemically interact with the paint system. Therefore, the mechanical properties of metal pigments must be taken seriously, especially during the dispersion process, and the same issue should be considered for metallic paints during pumping or circulation pipelines.

Dispersion of metal pigments: To achieve satisfactory special effects, metal pigments must be completely dispersed in the paint system. Due to mechanical limitations, high-shear mixing methods should be avoided when dispersing metal pigments. Metal pigments should first be dispersed in a solvent, which helps with pigment dispersion. Generally, the ratio of aluminum silver paste to solvent is 1:1 to 1:2. The solvent and pigments should be slowly stirred and dispersed, and care should be taken to ensure that the solvent does not contain water. Halogenated hydrocarbons should not be used because chlorinated solvents release HCl under appropriate conditions, causing a chemical reaction with fine aluminum pigments. Resin diluents should be slowly added to the pigments after pigment dispersion, continuously stirring. However, in practice, the opposite is often done: the diluted resin is slowly added to the pre-mixed metal pigments, and this operation must be slow to avoid pigment precipitation.

Preventing decomposition: In the automotive industry, most paints are pumped through a circulation system before spraying, generating high shear forces. Therefore, a so-called "non-decomposing aluminum pigment" has been developed to adapt to high-shear circulation systems. The thicker the flake aluminum pigments, the more mechanically stable they are. However, this geometric change has some impact on their orientation and optical properties.

Chemical properties: The chemical characteristics of aluminum pigments greatly limit the selection of resins and solvents in paint systems.
