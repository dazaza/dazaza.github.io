Flash flower teacher's eyes: college students take photos with mobile phones to take notes for convenience

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 16:01:50

Netizen @Night Meteor 1991: Nowadays, everyone has a smartphone. When teachers show important slides during class, very few people take notes with pens and notebooks anymore. All you hear is the sound of "click click click" as people furiously snap photos, storing everything on their phones.

Reporter follow-up: By definition, taking notes means recording important content with a pen in a notebook. This was how it used to be in classrooms. But now that smartphones are widespread, many college students have switched to taking pictures instead of writing notes.

Seeking convenience through photography or copying slides

Blogger Liu, a third-year student at a university in Hankou, was attending a web design and production class yesterday morning when the teacher said, "Please make some notes." Out of the 42 students, about 30 instinctively took out their phones to photograph the slides. "Handwriting is a bit troublesome; taking a picture is much more convenient," Liu said. However, he also admitted that while he takes pictures, he rarely looks at them until just before exams when he reviews the material.

A fourth-year student, Chen, agreed that taking pictures in class is convenient, but once back in the dormitory, it becomes a hassle to organize them, so often nothing gets done. Sometimes, he simply asks the teacher for a copy of the PPT slides, which is even easier.

Writing by hand helps keep up with the teacher's pace

At 2:30 PM yesterday, Zhou, a journalism student, attended an image processing class where she saw a few classmates using their phones to take pictures of the PPT slides. "Most students still take notes with pens, but using phones to take pictures is mainly to prevent missing information if the teacher flips through slides too quickly," she explained. She believes that taking pictures can distract from listening attentively and disrupts thought processes. As long as one keeps up with the teacher's rhythm, they can still take effective notes.

Another fourth-year student, Wu, adjusts his note-taking method based on the teacher's preferences. "One teacher requires us not to take pictures during lectures but encourages attentive listening, allowing us to copy the slides after class. Another teacher insists we write notes by hand, dictating sentence by sentence, forbidding both photography and slide copying. Some students forget to turn off the flash, which dazzles the teacher, making them reluctant to allow photos."

Teacher: Acceptable if handled calmly

A professor from Wuhan University's history department finds taking notes by photography acceptable, provided the shutter sound and flash are turned off to avoid disrupting classroom order. Since modern teaching often involves multimedia, PPT slides contain a lot of content, and due to copyright issues, not all teachers are willing to let students copy their slides. It’s understandable for students to take pictures and then organize them later.

Netizen "Paris Half Sea" also believes that if one intends to study, there’s no difference between handwritten notes and photographs.
