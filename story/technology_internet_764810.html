Is Google more like Microsoft or more like Apple?

by anonymous on 2013-08-07 18:42:32

The online version of The New York Times published an article today titled "Is Google More Like Microsoft or Apple?" The article states that Google, which rose after Microsoft and Apple, absorbed the strengths of both companies to form its own unique technology-driven culture while giving engineers enough freedom to explore. Therefore, Google is neither like Microsoft nor Apple but more like a company in between the two.

Here's the full text of the article:

In a previous article, I analyzed Microsoft's current predicament as its products shift from focusing on technical features to emphasizing design and simplicity, in other words, somewhat imitating Apple. However, constrained by the speed of product development, Microsoft’s Surface tablet has a kind of "jack-of-all-trades" feel, offering multiple options that confuse consumers.

A reader asked, isn't Google similar to Microsoft, or even worse? No, Google is a company with mysterious labs where its Google X lab seems capable of freely developing products independent of its core business. From self-driving cars to Project Loon (Google Balloons), and Google Glass, these cool creations are all masterpieces from the Google X lab.

Moreover, Google's products don’t appear as complex and chaotic as Microsoft’s. Former Engadget editor Ryan Block believes that the “chaos” in Google’s products exists at another level, characterized by being detached from core business and mostly technology-driven. It appears that Google better grasps the balance between technology and design.

Block said: "Google rose after Microsoft and Apple and could refer to the frameworks of these two companies, absorbing their advantageous corporate cultures. As a result, Google formed its own 'product-driven' and 'engineer-driven' corporate culture."

But Microsoft is different. In my interview with Microsoft's vice president of communications, Frank Shaw, he interpreted Google’s product naming methods as "confusing for consumers." He pointed out: "We chose a better path than Google; at least we wouldn’t name our products Jelly Bean or Coffee Cake, names that leave consumers utterly baffled."

Shaw’s critique was directed at the naming conventions of Google's Android operating system versions. He stated: "Microsoft’s operating system won’t confuse consumers. When they see Windows 8.1, it’s easy for them to understand that it’s an upgraded version of Windows 8."

Shaw is correct; unless you’re an Android engineer or a Google employee, it’s not easy to know the latest Android operating system without some effort.

However, Google’s technology-driven culture does help employees fully utilize their abilities. Besides the Google X lab, Google also established the famous "20% free time" rule, allowing employees to spend 20% of their workday on projects unrelated to their main tasks.

A Google spokesperson explained that since the implementation of the "20% free time" rule, engineers have developed numerous award-winning projects during this "leisure time," including last year's "Popular Science Innovation of the Year Award."

These seemingly insignificant projects may well be decisive factors for the company's future. On one hand, Google streamlined its core business by discontinuing services such as the social network service Google Buzz (the predecessor to Google+), the RSS reader Google Reader, and the LBS service Dodgeball. On the other hand, Google actively expanded into projects "unrelated" to its core business. This is Google's unique style.

Therefore, Google is neither like Microsoft nor Apple but more like a company somewhere in between the two.
