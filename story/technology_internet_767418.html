The 6 Most Failed Acquisitions in Apple's History

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:02:36

　　Introduction: The US tech blog BusinessInsider recently reviewed the six worst acquisition deals in Apple's history, spanning fields such as computer hardware, network systems, and graphics chips.

　　The following is the full text of the article:

　　Some of Apple's greatest successes have come from acquisitions and the integration of acquired technologies. For example, Mac OS X and iOS originated from Apple’s 1997 acquisition of NeXT, while iTunes came from the 2000 acquisition of SoundJam MP, which also helped the iPod outpace its competitors.

　　Of course, not all of Apple's acquisition deals have been stellar. Here are some failures:

　　1. Acquisition of Mac clone company Power Computing Corporation

　　In 1995, Apple granted Power Computing Corporation a license to produce Mac clones, hoping to transition to a business model similar to Microsoft's and counter the impact of Windows 95.

　　However, when Steve Jobs returned to Apple in 1997, he realized it was too late to beat Microsoft, and that Mac clones were harming the high-profit-margin Mac product line. That same year, Apple acquired Power Computing Corporation for $100 million and shut down the Mac clone business.

　　2. Acquisition of Orion Network Systems

　　In 1998, Apple acquired Orion Network Systems with the aim of entering the enterprise market dominated by IBM computers. At the time, IBM held an 80% share of the enterprise market, while Apple had only about 6%.

　　Despite having software that was easier to network, many faults led to poor performance in the enterprise market, including issues with price, software compatibility, and difficulties in training employees.

　　3. Acquisition of graphics chip manufacturer Raycer Graphics

　　When Apple acquired the graphics chip manufacturer Raycer Graphics in 1999, it also hired the company's vice president of engineering operations, Bob Mansfield.

　　Although the technology of this manufacturer wasn't critical to Apple — the chips used in Apple computers came from ATI and nVidia — the acquisition was still considered successful mainly due to the significant role Mansfield later played. After joining Apple, he led the Mac hardware engineering department and launched a series of best-selling products.

　　4. Acquisition of student information system company PowerSchool

　　PowerSchool was a company providing K12 (below high school) student information system services. In 2001, Apple acquired PowerSchool for stock worth $62 million, which seemed like a strange decision.

　　Apple has always tried to enter the education market, but the PowerSchool software wasn’t a good fit. In 2006, Apple sold PowerSchool to the educational publisher Pearson Group.

　　5. Acquisition of map company Placebase

　　In 2009, Apple acquired the map service company Placebase, leading some to speculate that iPhone map services would use this company's technology to break away from Google. Three years later, Apple launched iOS 6 and its own map service, but Apple Maps were riddled with errors and faced heavy criticism.

　　6. Acquisition of NetSelector

　　In 2000, Apple acquired NetSelector, a company that provided an internet monitoring system for children. Unlike other filters, teachers and administrators could manually select websites suitable for children to visit.

　　However, most people thought the system was too restrictive, and most schools still opted for traditional filters. Later, Apple integrated NetSelector's technology into iMac and iBook software.
