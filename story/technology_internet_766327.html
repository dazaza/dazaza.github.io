For programmers, laziness is a virtue worth praising.

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 13:47:42

　　English Original: The Laziest Programmer In The Room

　　There are actually many blog posts explaining why laziness is one of the qualities of a great programmer, but you only notice these blogs once you become a programmer. This article aims to explore how to assess a programmer's laziness and see how it can be either a good or bad thing.

　　If you know a little about programming, you'll soon realize that software development is full of trial and error. You make a change, refresh your browser, and check if it works. It's like rinsing clothes, over and over again. Sometimes, even for an extremely simple issue (if you're developing web applications for IE, you'll understand).

　　The work of a software programmer is filled with repetitive tasks, which is where laziness comes in. A programmer who quickly gets bored with repetitive work will immediately develop tools to automate these tasks. They'll find ways to automatically refresh the browser whenever code is modified. They'll create tools to clear databases, automatically execute file import tests. If a task follows a pattern, they'll figure it out and make it happen automatically.

　　Many times, I've seen clients who actually need software to do their work for them, but they don't realize it's a problem. Instead, they manually perform these repetitive tasks, which is inefficient and wastes time and money.

　　If they find a programmer and explain their workflow, lucky for them, the programmer can help them create convenient tools within a few hours. If it's more complex, it might not be that simple, but from a long-term perspective, it's much better than always using Excel to handle business processes.

　　Ruby on Rails is often the framework of choice for many programmers when developing such business process software. It allows programmers to build simple and easy-to-use automated business processing tools in just a few hours. These tools provide a clearer view of your business. Improving business processes through these tools not only makes things easier for you but also benefits your customers, potentially earning you more money.

　　I often say that the lazier the programmer, the better. This doesn't mean taking shortcuts during development is the right approach. A lazy programmer knows that taking shortcuts can be harmful in the long run. Proper planning upfront ensures peace of mind later. Lazy programmers are like home-run hitters; they think long-term. But at the same time, they know when to take shortcuts. If an idea is still immature and based on assumptions, a lazy programmer would say: let's do a bit first, test it, see the feedback, and then make the next decision based on that. The more you build on hypothetical software, the more manpower, resources, and money you'll waste when you have to redo it later. That's something no one wants.

　　If changes are needed in the future, a lazy programmer can easily implement them because they’ve already planned ahead. On the other hand, programmers who pile on too much technology trap themselves in a box—a box with hundreds or even thousands of features from the start. They may think it's grand, but in reality, it will prove difficult to adapt according to business changes.

　　So, now when you hear that laziness is a virtue, you understand the reasoning behind it. Laziness is no longer a negative term. It’s a compliment.
