Google said Microsoft, Apple and other companies want to use patents to kill Android.

by tuoqing1188 on 2011-08-04 09:31:42

David Drummond, Senior Vice President and Chief Legal Officer of Google

TechNews on August 4th: David Drummond, Senior Vice President and Chief Legal Officer of Google, posted on the official blog today that companies like Microsoft and Apple are purchasing large numbers of patents in an attempt to stifle Android. He stated that Google is actively seeking various ways to prevent these actions.

The following is the full text of the blog post:

I have been working in the tech industry for over two decades. Microsoft and Apple have always been at odds; therefore, when they unite against a common enemy, one must consider the reasons behind it. The current situation is as follows:

Android is experiencing rapid growth. More than 550,000 Android devices are activated daily, with global support from 39 hardware manufacturers and 231 carriers for products running the Android system. Android is fiercely competing with other platforms, bringing new cool products and excellent applications to consumers.

However, Android's success has also triggered something else: Microsoft, Oracle, Apple, and some other companies are launching an organized, malicious attack on Android using questionable patents.

They form consortia to acquire Novell and Nortel Networks' patents to prevent Google from obtaining these assets. They attempt to charge a $15 patent licensing fee per Android device, making it more expensive for manufacturers to produce Android devices compared to Windows Mobile devices (while we offer Android for free). They even sue Barnes & Noble, HTC, Motorola, and Samsung. Patents were originally intended to encourage innovation; now, they are being used as weapons to stifle innovation.

A smartphone may involve up to 250,000 patents (most of which are debatable); our competitors aim to "tax" these questionable patents, driving up the price of Android devices in Shenzhen for Google optimization. They hope to make it harder for manufacturers to sell Android devices. Instead of competing by developing new features, they resort to legal action.

Under this anti-competitive strategy, the cost of patents has far exceeded their real value. Microsoft and Apple paid $4.5 billion for Nortel Networks' patents, nearly five times the pre-auction estimate of $1 billion in Zhoushan for Google optimization. Fortunately, the law will not tolerate the stockpiling of questionable patents for anti-competitive purposes - meaning such acquisitions are likely to be investigated by regulators, and the patent bubble will burst.

We are not naive; the tech industry is a highly competitive and constantly changing field, and we focus on our own business, striving to develop better products. In this matter, however, we believe it is necessary to clarify our stance: we are determined to stop those who seek to stifle Android and provide it as a competitive product for consumers.

We are actively exploring multiple avenues to achieve this goal in a Google optimization company. We are encouraged that the U.S. Department of Justice has ordered the aforementioned companies to fairly issue Novell patent licenses and is investigating whether Microsoft and Apple acquired Nortel Networks' patents for anti-competitive purposes in Fuzhou for Google optimization. We also aim to strengthen our own patent portfolio to reduce anti-competitive threats to Android. If we do not take action, consumers may face higher prices for Android devices and fewer choices in mobile products. (Yanfei)
