Amazon Studios: From Grassroots to Hollywood's Biggest Threat

by anonymous on 2013-11-25 11:34:22

Amazon's TV and film production department was initially established to attract more Prime members and compete with Netflix. However, their ambitions have since expanded, and they now even aim to compete with Hollywood for market share.

In April this year, a group of comedians gathered in Portland to attend the "Bridgetown Comedy Festival." After a night of revelry, many were still groggy, reluctant to get up from their beds or even the hotel lobby.

There were two exceptions: Ben Roy and Adam Cayton-Holland, both members of the Denver-based three-person comedy troupe Grawlix. That morning, they woke up early to stare at their computer screens, waiting for the premiere of "Those Who Can't" on Amazon. They had never been on such a big stage before, and as thousands of users began to comment, they remained glued to their computers. Soon, interview calls started coming in.

"It felt like Christmas morning," Roy said.

This is exactly the scene Amazon Studios envisioned when it was founded three years ago. Its goal was: as outsiders to Hollywood, invest in video projects and let Amazon's vast user base vote on whether to continue production. This concept not only unearthed a lot of talent but also helped Amazon gather a large amount of valuable viewing data.

However, to challenge Netflix, disrupt the traditional film and television production industry, and attract millions of users to Amazon Prime, these relatively unknown actors may not be enough.

Although "Those Who Can't" received the highest user rating among Amazon's pilot projects, the company believed that Prime members might prefer big-name actors over Grawlix. Therefore, Amazon, like HBO and other cable giants, invested heavily in hiring famous actors.

This model of disrupting traditional industries through heavy investment is familiar — Kindle falls into this category. Thus, Amazon's first complete original series, "Alpha House," will star actor John Goodman, with Stephen Colbert and Bill Murray as supporting actors. The scriptwriter is Garry Trudeau, creator of "Doonesbury" and the first cartoonist to win the Pulitzer Prize.

An industry insider said, "Since content is just a small part of Amazon's business, its potential is enough to make competitors wary."

Idealistic Beginnings

When Amazon Studios was founded in November 2010, its founding director Roy Price positioned it as "the movie studio of the future." It hoped to compete with Hollywood's model while fully utilizing Amazon's reach. "Today's film industry is dominated by Hollywood. Through Amazon Studios, we hope to uncover some unheard voices," he said.

From the start, they adopted a crowdsourcing model, inviting members to submit scripts and suggestions, then passing them on to Warner Brothers for selection. If a project was released theatrically, the creator could receive a $200,000 prize, and an additional $400,000 if the box office reached $60 million. To date, Warner Brothers has not chosen any projects, but Amazon still awarded $2.7 million in prizes in 2011.

However, Amazon accumulated a large amount of data throughout the process. For example, they learned through data that the most attractive scripts are comedies. However, Amazon's premise assumption was flawed: their ultimate target audience were people who enjoyed online shopping, but those reading the scripts were just a niche group.

Moreover, many viewers do not decide whether they like a TV show until they see the final product. "Breaking Bad" is a good example; probably few would have liked the show based solely on the script.

Lagging Behind Netflix

At the beginning of 2012, Amazon Studios was significantly behind in the competition for original content. At that time, Netflix had secured the rights to produce "House of Cards" from HBO. Netflix CEO Reed Hastings also revealed to investors that original shows would account for 15% of their content budget.

Amazon's content also began shifting towards professional routes, recruiting professionals from 20th Century Fox and Sony Pictures. "We want to launch an open online submission process while having a robust traditional production process to access top talents and producers," Price said.

This new direction was recognized by industry insiders.

While Price did not disclose the budget for "Alpha House," he did indicate that Amazon's production costs would match all high-end TV shows.

Data-Driven

Despite spending less than Netflix, Amazon's efforts in data may already surpass its rival. The company can not only understand which films users buy through the Amazon website but also owns IMDb, the TV and film review site acquired in 1998.

Price mentioned in an October speech, "Amazon users like 'Breaking Bad' and 'Downton Abbey,' so we should combine noble and drug-dealing elements. It's too simple."

Amazon's reliance on data goes beyond this. In April, Amazon Studios put 14 pilot episodes on its site for users to watch and rate, including "Alpha House" and "Those Who Can't." When making the final decision, they not only used rating data but also analyzed viewing data throughout the process. Joe Lewis, who moved from Fox to Amazon, said the company paid attention to data such as how many people finished watching a pilot episode, how many watched more than once, and how many wrote reviews.

In fact, Amazon is the first company to decide whether to produce a TV series through this evaluation model. Price said it was akin to letting users taste-test new dishes before deciding whether to include them on the menu.

Nevertheless, data analysis firm Sandvine believes that Amazon lags far behind Netflix in many aspects. Netflix's traffic ranks at the top of all North American network assets, peaking at as high as 31%, while Amazon Video service is only 1.61%.

Investment bank BMO Capital Markets analyst Edward Williams thinks one reason is recognition. Among the 12 million Amazon Prime members, most believe the service is just for shipping discounts and are unaware of its video offerings. Meanwhile, Netflix's over 43 million users worldwide enjoy using the service to watch videos.

However, Amazon still threatens industry giants in other ways. New director Jill Soloway believes that compared to traditional film studios, working with Amazon is easier. "Amazon's business model feels fast," she said.

Retail-Focused

The biggest difference between Amazon and its competitors is its identity. Amazon's core positioning is still as a retailer, so to develop its retail business, it is willing to sacrifice profits in other businesses.

Bloomberg Businessweek journalist Brad Stone described in his new book Walmart and Amazon's bidding war for Diapers.com parent company Quidsi. At that time, they launched the "Amazon Mom" service, offering users a year of Prime membership and a 30% discount on diapers.

Quidsi executives said the project cost Amazon about $100 million every three months. An Amazon executive also warned that if Walmart successfully acquired Quidsi, Amazon would reduce diaper prices to zero.

Industry insiders believe that because Amazon is willing to operate at a loss to capture market share in many areas, their entry into a market often causes existing companies to fear. And Wall Street seems to recognize Amazon's long-term strategy.

How does Amazon Studios fit into their long-term strategy? Ultimately, it's still about Prime membership. Analysts estimate that Prime members spend 2 to 4 times more than regular Amazon users. Original content like Amazon Studios can attract more new users. Additionally, unlike Netflix, Amazon doesn't release all episodes of its original series at once but releases them weekly to bring repeat visitors back to the online store.

Amazon also allows everyone to watch original series for free and attracts a significant number of users this way. Now, Amazon has locked its Prime competitor as Netflix, which won three Emmy Awards with "House of Cards," precisely what Amazon is striving for.
