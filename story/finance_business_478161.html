Jobs claimed cheated

As early as 2004, when he was CEO of Google, Eric Schmidt announced that it vowed, as in the United States, Google would become an important player in the mobile era.

Even after the 2005 acquisition of Android, Google continued to clarify over the years its plan to enter the mobile phone market. This strategy was affected until 2008, that summer, Apple CEO Steve Jobs visited Google’s office to see a mobile phone prototype.

Google’s Levi writes, when Steve Jobs finally saw the Google phone prototype, he was upset, but Schmidt said he had never been confused by Jobs.

In this context, Google's $12.5 billion acquisition of Motorola's mobile blockbuster deal with the real motive may have some historical reference value. In interpreting Google’s motive for the deal, it was said that the main reason was to acquire Motorola Mobility's approximately 17,000 patents rather than to seize the opportunity to get involved in the mobile phone market. During a conference call with analysts, Google executives talked about the significant value of Motorola’s mobile patents, with little acknowledgment of the mobile phone business.

Android ally change from rival vendors

If Google’s goal is to acquire Motorola’s portfolio of patents rather than the mobile phone business, which almost makes no sense: the Android operating system has long remained open, including Samsung and HTC, among many mobile phone manufacturers using it. These companies have invested billions of dollars in the Android business to help make the Android mobile operating system more popular than Apple’s. Today, they shift from Google’s partners into competitors.

U.S. investment firm Morgan Keegan Equity Research Analyst Tavis McCourt said: "While Google has made all the gestures, I believe that in a couple of years Google will be looking to bring the Android platform closer, or start building its own unique features on the phone." Meanwhile, Motorola Mobility will increase distribution channels in Europe, compared to Samsung and other manufacturers; Motorola's mobile products sales are weaker there.

Even if Google continues to support Android as a consumer offering, a proprietary Google Android phone will ultimately become a vertically integrated end system, making it more competitive with the Apple iPhone. The reason why the iPhone has achieved great success is because Apple could control the smartphone extensively from hardware to software.

In fact, Apple's primary motivation for the acquisition of BlackBerry-maker RIM over a year before the acquisition of QNX Software Systems (hereinafter referred to as QNX is Harmon International’s software business), RIM said at the time was complex in the acquisition purpose of QNX automotive and infotainment services, and this is precisely the QNX’s strong suit. RIM strongly contended the QXN will become the next dominant operating system, rumors about RIM. Of course, RIM’s statement lacked sincerity: RIM is developing now QNX operating system.

Page's peak

MACAT said: On Monday, Android handset makers all bid support for the deal on the grounds that it will help protect Android against potential patent litigation damage.

This does not mean that Google considers acquiring patents solely based on Motorola’s mobile motivations. Over the past year, Google has been trying to buy cell phone-related patents: Google bid for Nortel Networks' 6000 patents but lost to a consortium led by Apple and Microsoft. An undeniable fact is that Google’s new CEO Larry Page has long supported the development of the mobile phone business, and this acquisition might be his peak.

After all, it's claimed efforts in Page, Google completed the acquisition of Android, Android and he is the most powerful supporter. In recent years, Page also participated in the U.S. federal government's auction of radio spectrum resources, so that Google is still willing to take quick shots at competitors, sometimes even willing to spend more money for these resources. Levy said in an interview: In order to obtain the latter’s patent portfolio, the company can consider another option. Many industry insiders believe that if Google really wanted to buy patents, InterDigital might be a more desirable target, with a market capitalization of only about $3 billion, and Motorola’s mobile handset business isn't a big burden.

MACAT said, if Google is simply to access patents, they can buy InterDigital. On Monday, Google can no longer be InterDigital’s buyers, investors dumped the stock, causing its stock price plummeted 14 percent. (Xuan Chen)
