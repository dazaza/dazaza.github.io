Precarious AFC Champions League journey with "tigers in the front and wolves at the back"; three major problems assault Gao Hongbo

by qqqoo60xs on 2011-08-25 23:01:47

A Hard Road in the AFC Champions League: Surrounded by Tigers in Front and Wolves Behind, Three Problems Confront Gao Hongbo

Two days ago, the draw for the 2008 AFC Champions League group stage was announced. The newly crowned champion of the Chinese Super League (CSL), Changchun Yatai FC, was placed in Group E alongside Pohang Steelers from South Korea, Adelaide United FC from Australia, and Binh Duong FC from Vietnam.

Among these teams, Pohang Steelers are champions of the South Korean league, while Adelaide United FC are runners-up in the Australian league. Both clubs possess strong overall strength. For Changchun Yatai to break out of this challenging group stage, they must overcome the encirclement of the "Korean tigers" and "Australian wolves." As for Binh Duong FC from Vietnam, if Changchun Yatai can't win against them, then perhaps they should focus on domestic CSL instead of embarrassing themselves abroad.

Faced with the disadvantageous situation of having fierce tigers ahead and wild wolves behind, three major problems confront Changchun Yatai's head coach, Gao Hongbo.

Firstly: Changchun Yatai has no experience in the AFC Champions League

The importance of experience for a team goes without saying. Just look at AC Milan, who have become world-renowned under the leadership of their veteran players. Relying on their extremely rich tournament experience, they have consecutively won the UEFA Champions League, UEFA Super Cup, and most recently the FIFA Club World Cup. There’s no doubt about the capabilities of Milan’s players, but in knockout-style cup competitions where teams are evenly matched, experience plays a crucial role.

In contrast to AC Milan's extensive cup competition experience, Changchun Yatai, who first won the Chinese Super League title in 2007 and are appearing for the first time on Asia's football stage, lack any AFC Champions League experience. Compared to the other two stronger teams, Changchun Yatai is already at a disadvantage before even playing a match.

Of course, the issue of experience isn't insurmountable. It depends on how Changchun Yatai chooses their pre-season friendly opponents based on their rivals' characteristics and conducts targeted training before their debut in the Asian arena. This will be a significant challenge for Gao Hongbo, head coach of Yatai.

Secondly: Prioritize the League or the AFC Champions League?

As the new CSL champion, if the management of Changchun Yatai sets its main goal on achieving success in the AFC Champions League, it will inevitably affect their performance in the domestic CSL. Their championship victory in 2007 relied not only on their own strength, luck, and grassroots spirit but also on various objective factors: the decline of Dalian Shide, the joint pain relief of Shanghai Shenhua, the incomplete roster of Shandong Luneng, Beijing Guoan's slim margin of survival, and Tianjin Teda's insufficient depth. One could say that Changchun Yatai's championship wasn't solely due to their overwhelming strength. In the dual-front battles of the new season, as the defending champions, if Changchun Yatai repeatedly stumbles domestically, it would be absolutely unacceptable to both fans and club management.

Moreover, representing the entirety of Chinese football on the Asian stage, Changchun Yatai and Beijing Guoan bear the hopes of the face-conscious Chinese Football Association (CFA) and Chinese fans alike. The CFA and fans cannot tolerate Changchun Yatai prioritizing the domestic league over the AFC Champions League, leading to a humiliating defeat in the Asian tournament. Such an outcome would not only lose public support but also further diminish the already limited credibility of the Chinese FA, which nobody wants to see.

Frankly speaking, at present, Changchun Yatai, or any club in China, does not have the capacity to fight on two fronts within Asia. They cannot abandon their revolutionary base — the CSL — nor can they forsake the face-saving project — the AFC Champions League. Should they prioritize the league or the AFC Champions League? Should they safeguard their domestic reputation or preserve the face of Chinese football across Asia? This is a decision Gao Hongbo must make.

Finally: The Significant Impact of the Chinese National Team's Qualifying Campaign for the 2010 South Africa World Cup

In the 2006 Germany World Cup, Chinese football crashed out early in the group stages, leaving a deep scar in the hearts of Chinese fans. The qualifying campaign for the 2010 South Africa World Cup is currently the top priority for the Chinese FA, and also the top priority for every player and fan. Club matches will undoubtedly give way to national team games. Du Zhenyu, the CSL Footballer of the Year and midfield attacking core of Changchun Yatai, will have to juggle between his club and national team duties, competing on three fronts. If he performs poorly or gets injured, it will make Changchun Yatai's already difficult path in the AFC Champions League, surrounded by fierce competitors, even more arduous.

Lacking experience, unable to afford losses in either the league or the AFC Champions League, and the call-up of players for national team games—these three problems simultaneously confront Gao Hongbo. How well he handles these current issues plaguing Changchun Yatai will determine the club's final results in the new season.
