Qian Zhongshu alluded to Shen Congwen in two works.

by caip2692 on 2012-02-08 22:40:17

Qian Zhongshu and Shen Congwen are two towering figures in modern and contemporary Chinese literature. Qian Zhongshu enjoys the beautiful reputation of "Cultural Kunlun", while Shen Congwen is praised as having "starry writings, and a sincere person". There has been such a saying in intellectual circles: the two literary works most worth reading in the 20th century are the "two cities" - Qian Zhongshu's *Fortress Besieged* and Shen Congwen's *Border Town*. Both works are highly artistic; *Border Town* focuses on melancholy, sadness, and beauty, whereas *Fortress Besieged* uses black humor to expose the myriad facets of society with incisive satire.

What is little known is that Qian Zhongshu once alluded to Shen Congwen in two of his own works. One is the novella *The Cat*: "Cao Shichang, whose actions are refined, speaks in a soft and gentle voice that is pleasing to the ear. When heard from the next room, it is quite enough to make one drunk with admiration. However, when a man speaks so softly in front of you, many people become impatient and wish they could turn him up like a radio to amplify his voice. This gentle scholar loves to give readers an impression of savagery in his works, as if he possesses both the innocence of a primitive man and the ferocity of a superman. His past life is shrouded in mystery. If what he says is true, then he has done everything. He was once a bandit in his hometown, later became a soldier, worked as a junior thug in Shanghai, also performed on stage, served as a waiter in a grand hotel, and had other romantic experiences of wandering. Talking about these can make young people who have only lived in families and schools shake their heads and stretch out their thumbs to say: 'Who would have thought! It's indescribable!' He writes about doing these jobs as if it were more profitable than actually doing them, so he no longer changes careers. In theory, with so many fascinating memories, he should write an autobiography and include them all. But he only writes some autobiographical novels piecemeal; perhaps because writing a real autobiography would not allow for the insertion of various colorful experiences into a life of over thirty years, or perhaps because after finishing an autobiography, it would be inconvenient to make new supplements to the past at any time. He is now famous in the literary world, but he still cannot forget not having attended school properly when he was young, always feeling that those who graduated through formal channels look down on him, and being vigilant against others hurting his dignity. The honey-sweet voice conceals an aggressive attitude..."
