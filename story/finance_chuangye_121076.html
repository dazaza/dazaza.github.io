How to Write Meeting Minutes: Standard Format and Writing Requirements for Meeting Minutes

by longersing on 2009-01-24 00:32:54

I. Concept of Meeting Minutes

Meeting minutes are a type of official document used to record and convey the basic situation or main spirit, as well as the agreed-upon matters discussed in a meeting. They are based on the meeting records, summarizing the key contents and decisions made during the meeting, forming a factual and guiding document for implementation or publication in newspapers.

Meeting minutes can be categorized into two types based on their scope of application, content, and function:

1. Office Meeting Minutes (also known as daily administrative work meeting minutes), mainly used for discussing issues, assigning tasks, and providing guidance and basis for conducting work. Examples include: Meeting Minutes of XX School Work Conference, Meeting Minutes of Minister's Office, and Meeting Minutes of Standing Committee of Municipal Party Committee.

2. Specialized Meeting Minutes (also known as consultative exchange meeting minutes), mainly used for meetings such as exchange conferences, seminars, and discussion sessions, aiming to transmit information, discuss problems, and inspire work. Example: Meeting Minutes of Poverty Alleviation Work Discussion Session in XX County.

II. Format of Meeting Minutes (How to Standardize Meeting Minutes)

Typically, meeting minutes consist of three parts: title, body, and conclusion.

1. Title has three forms:

- The name of the meeting plus "minutes", e.g., "Minutes of Guilin Tourism Conference in China."

- The convening authority plus content plus "minutes," which can also be simplified to authority plus "minutes," e.g., "Minutes of Enterprise Profitability Meeting by Provincial Economic and Trade Commission" or "Minutes of Organization Department Minister's Office Meeting."

- A combination of main and sub-titles, e.g., "Maintaining Financial System and Strengthening Economic Management – Speech Minutes from the XX Department XX Seminar."

The date of composition should be marked below the title, centered and enclosed in parentheses. As an official document, the file number should be indicated in the header section, while the issuing unit and date of composition should be placed at the end with a seal.

2. The body of the meeting minutes generally consists of two parts:

(1) Introduction, referring to the meeting overview, including time, location, name, chairperson, attendees, and basic agenda.

(2) Main body, referring to the essence of the meeting and the agreed-upon items. For standing committee meetings, office meetings, and regular work meetings, the minutes typically include the meeting content and agreed-upon items, sometimes also summarizing the significance of these items. For work conferences, specialized meetings, and discussion sessions, the minutes often also include experiences, practices, and opinions, measures, and requirements for future work.

(3) Conclusion, mainly summarizing the meeting, evaluating speeches, and stating the chairperson's requirements or calls to action. Generally, most meeting minutes do not require a concluding statement, ending after the main body.

III. Writing Methods for Meeting Minutes (How to Write Meeting Minutes)

Depending on the nature, scale, and topics of the meeting, the body part can roughly have the following writing methods:

1. Concentrated Summary Method (Comprehensive Style): This method summarizes the basic situation of the meeting, the main issues discussed, the understanding of the attendees, and the agreed-upon matters (including measures, methods, and requirements for solving problems). It provides a comprehensive explanation and description. This method is commonly used for small meetings with focused and unified topics, making it easier to implement and write shorter documents. If there are multiple topics, they can be listed separately.

2. Item-by-Item Narrative Method (List Style): For large or medium-sized meetings or those with many topics, this method divides the main content of the meeting into several major issues, then adds numbers or sub-titles, addressing each item separately. This method emphasizes horizontal analysis and elaboration, covering relatively comprehensive content and detailing the issues. It often includes analyses of purpose, significance, current status, as well as goals, tasks, policies, and measures. Such minutes are generally used for meetings that require grassroots understanding and deep implementation.

3. Key Points of Speech Method (Excerpt Style): This method organizes and extracts the content and essence of typical and representative speeches, then explains them according to the order of speeches or different contents. This method reflects the opinions of attendees more truthfully. Some meeting minutes, arranged by higher authorities to understand different opinions of attendees, can use this method.

IV. Characteristics of Meeting Minutes

1. Content Authenticity: Meeting minutes truthfully reflect the content of the meeting. They cannot deviate from the actual meeting content, nor can they artificially exaggerate, deepen, or fill gaps. Otherwise, they will lose their objective authenticity, violating the requirement for accuracy.

2. Point-based Expression: Meeting minutes are synthesized based on the meeting situation. Writing meeting minutes should focus on the meeting's main theme and key outcomes, organizing, refining, and summarizing them. The emphasis should be on presenting the meeting's results rather than narrating the process, avoiding overly detailed accounts.

3. Special Address: Meeting minutes usually adopt the third-person writing style. Since they reflect the collective will and intention of the attendees, they often use "the meeting believes," "the meeting points out," "the meeting decides," "the meeting requires," and "the meeting calls for" to express their special address.

V. Differences Between Meeting Minutes and Meeting Records (This is Important, Many People Confuse Them)

First, Nature Difference: Meeting records are verbatim recordings of discussions and belong to administrative documents. Meeting minutes only record key points and are legally recognized administrative official documents.

Second, Function Difference: Meeting records are generally not disclosed and do not need to be conveyed or read, serving only as archival materials; whereas meeting minutes are typically communicated within a certain range or read, requiring implementation.

The format of meeting records mainly includes two parts: one is the organization of the meeting, requiring details such as the name of the meeting, time, place, number of attendees, absentees, guests, chairperson, and recorder. The other part is the content of the meeting, requiring details of speeches, resolutions, and issues, which form the core part of the meeting record.

For speech content, there are two recording methods:

- Detailed specific recording, trying to record the original words, mainly used for important meetings and significant speeches.

- Summary recording, only recording the key points and central content, mostly used for general meetings.

At the end of the meeting, after recording is complete, write "adjourned" on a new line. If there is a break during the meeting, write "recess."
