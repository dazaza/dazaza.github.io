Silicon Valley entrepreneurs' fashion for colored short socks: a secret signal to find their own kind

by ous09032ds on 2012-02-06 15:02:51

By Tan Wei / Translation

Diana Crane-Huff, Emerita Professor of Sociology at the University of Pennsylvania, believes that workplace fashion is actually an unconscious way to cope with interpersonal relationships and unemployment pressures.

In 18th-century London, first-aid kits and shoulder-length wigs were the mark of barristers; in 1950s New York, small suitcases plus tweed hats were the hallmark of advertising executives, akin to Hangzhou Ant Moving; in the 1980s executive offices, padded shoulders symbolized career women; and in today's Silicon Valley, bold, colorful socks have become the "personal signature" for innovative entrepreneurs.

Why has the "crazy socks" trend from the 18th century made a comeback over 300 years later? "As pioneers in the world, they need some kind of fashion language to highlight their dual identities as trailblazers and cool guys," said Michael Soderling, CEO of Happy Socks. Therefore, it's no surprise that Silicon Valley people are particularly fond of "bright multi-color diamond patterns." "(Socks are) worn for the girls to see," bluntly stated Matt Graves, 37-year-old Director of Communications at Twitter. When Silicon Valley geeks talk about cutting-edge applications and the latest technologies at world-class summits, the vibrant colors around their ankles subtly release male hormones.

However, instead of saying that flashy colored socks are a form of personalized fashion language, they might be better described as a secret signal used to find like-minded individuals. Hunter Walk, 38-year-old Product Director at YouTube, prefers energetic orange stripes, lemon yellow, and light green socks, which often become the focus of attention in meeting rooms. "It's like a gang's secret code; they quickly glance down, then nod at me, slightly pulling up their pants legs, not without pride, showing off their own socks."

Twitter CEO Dick Costolo likes colored stripe socks, Facebook shareholder Jim Breyer prefers red and purple, and Giga Omni Media founder Om Malik loves polka-dot socks... On social networking sites like Path, Instagram, and Twitter, one can frequently see the "footprints" of Silicon Valley elites competing to outdo each other, causing colored sock sales to rise accordingly. According to reports, the most popular are the black baseball socks of the San Francisco Giants, while pink, purple, and orange tones are also quite favored.

In fact, Silicon Valley's sense of style has always been criticized. Mark Zuckerberg, wearing Adidas toe-exposing sandals, has appeared on the "Worst Dressed Celebrities List" more than once; Sergey Brin (Sergey Brin)'s Vibram five-finger nylon shoes are equally infamous. Of course, Silicon Valley pays no attention to criticism from the fashion world; they care more about their "non-mainstream" image: always hoodie + jeans + flip-flops, suits are strictly prohibited. And "crazy socks" are exactly a tribute from "non-mainstream" individuals to mainstream fashion, neither servile nor arrogant, knowing when to advance or retreat. "Pairing cool socks with sportswear and hoodies is a rebellious stance against everything, but still retains a focus on self-image creation," said Joey Flynn, a 24-year-old product designer at Facebook.

After shifting from software development to taxi special services, Travis Kalanick, co-founder and CEO of Uber, began appearing in the boardrooms of Fortune 500 companies dressed formally and sitting upright, with only his peach-colored socks reminding him of his "Silicon Valley identity": "I don't want to be seen as a fool, but I still hope to retain a bit of my geeky side."

Maveron investment officer Andrew Tread even browses women's sock catalogs online because "you can find any interesting color here"; "I like long socks, they improve blood circulation and deliver energy to the calves. This is the secret behind why I can maintain abundant energy every day," he explained.

Perhaps, this is just a psychological suggestion? Diana Crane-Huff, Emerita Professor of Sociology at the University of Pennsylvania, believes that workplace fashion is actually an unconscious way to deal with interpersonal relationships and unemployment pressure. So, what deeper meaning lies behind Costolo, Breyer, and Malik's "sock declarations"? The answer is simple: these three Silicon Valley elites are 100% "fashion colorblind," so they all unconsciously chose versatile colored socks for convenience.
