Everyone has teeth, but do you really know your teeth? Before asking for quick remedies to relieve toothache, it's important to first understand your own teeth.

1. Structure of the Teeth: Teeth are the hardest part of the human body and consist of three parts: the crown, neck, and root. They also include enamel (also called the hard outer layer), dentin (the dense bony tissue), and pulp (containing nerves and blood vessels).

2. Growth Periods of the Teeth: The growth of teeth is related to bones, but due to different embryonic origins, the growth of teeth and bones does not always occur in parallel. By the time a baby is born, the milk teeth have already ossified. The tooth buds of milk teeth are hidden in the jawbone and covered by the gums; the ossification of permanent teeth begins during the neonatal period, and by 18-24 months, the third molar has already started to ossify. A person will have two sets of teeth in their lifetime: milk teeth (a total of 20) and permanent teeth (28-32). Milk teeth begin to erupt between 4-10 months after birth. If they haven't erupted within 12 months, it is considered delayed eruption of milk teeth. The order of eruption of milk teeth is generally lower jaw before the upper jaw, from front to back, and by about 2.5 years old, all milk teeth should have erupted. There is significant individual variation in the timing of milk teeth eruption, which is related to genetics, endocrine function, and food texture.

At around 6 years old, the first permanent tooth (the first permanent molar, located behind the second milk molar, also known as the "six-year-old tooth") erupts; between 6-12 years old, the milk teeth are gradually replaced by corresponding permanent teeth. The first and second premolars replace the first and second milk molars, respectively. This stage is called the mixed dentition period. At around 12 years old, the second permanent molar erupts; after approximately 18 years old, the third permanent molar (wisdom tooth) erupts, although some people never experience the eruption of their wisdom teeth.

Teething is a physiological phenomenon. During teething, some infants may experience low-grade fever, increased saliva production leading to drooling, and symptoms such as sleep disturbances and irritability. Healthy tooth growth is related to nutrients like protein, calcium, phosphorus, fluoride, vitamins A, C, D, and thyroid hormones. Chewing food promotes tooth growth. Abnormal tooth growth can be associated with conditions such as poor development of the ectoderm, calcium or fluoride deficiency, and hypothyroidism.

3. Dental Care: To have strong and beautiful teeth, it is necessary to pay attention to dental care and consume foods rich in calcium. It is especially important to choose the right diet during infancy. Parents should give their children vegetables that promote chewing, such as celery, cabbage, spinach, leeks, and seaweed. These help promote the development of the jaw and ensure the alignment of teeth. Eating vegetables can also increase the content of molybdenum in teeth, enhancing their hardness and durability. Studies have shown that children who dislike eating vegetables and meat have lower bone density compared to those who regularly eat these foods. Eating vegetables can also prevent cavities because vegetables contain 90% water and some fibrous substances. When chewing vegetables, the water in them dilutes the sugar in the mouth, making it difficult for bacteria to grow. Fibers can clean and sweep the surface of teeth. Additionally, eating harder foods benefits dental health, such as corn, sorghum, beef, dog meat, and some nuts like acorns, sunflower seeds, walnuts, and hazelnuts.
