The level of alignment between employees and their direct supervisors is the most critical factor affecting whether employees can work happily. Studies have shown that in 90% of cases where employees resign, it is due to a mismatch between the employee and their supervisor. So, how should employees and supervisors align? Or, how should direct supervisors ensure that their subordinates collaborate well with them?

### 1. Respect Employee Individuality

Supervisors should first ensure that the individuality of employees is respected. People have various personalities, each with distinct characteristics, so there are no two completely identical individuals. However, human personality traits also share common features, such as impatience vs. patience, optimism vs. pessimism, self-focus vs. other-focus. From a work perspective, everyone prefers collaborators whose style aligns with their own. We often see situations where an impatient supervisor encounters a patient employee; in such cases, it is crucial for the supervisor to control their emotions and adjust their working methods. Each person's personality is shaped by genetic factors and environmental influences, and there are no inherently bad personality traits or styles. It is simply a matter of whether the personality aligns with the nature of the work or the collaborator.

Supervisors should avoid attacking the personality traits of their subordinates, as this can severely damage the employee's confidence and affect team cooperation. Wise supervisors should regulate the behavior of subordinates whose personalities do not align with their own. This can be achieved through specific job-related requirements, gradually changing the subordinate’s characteristics. For example, when dealing with a slow-paced subordinate, you must clearly specify meeting times and impose appropriate penalties for tardiness (such as requiring latecomers to contribute to a charity fund, followed by enjoying ice cream together after the meeting). With sufficient patience, employees and supervisors can gradually align.

### 2. Review Your Leadership Style

Kurt Lewin, a renowned psychologist from the University of Iowa, found that group task leaders do not express their leadership roles in the same way. Leaders typically use different leadership styles, which have varying impacts on the performance and job satisfaction of group members. They focus on three types of leadership behaviors or styles: authoritarian, democratic, and laissez-faire.

Authoritarian leaders focus only on work objectives, caring solely about work responsibilities and efficiency while neglecting team members. There is a large psychological distance between employees and leaders, with the latter lacking sensitivity towards employees who may harbor suspicion and hostility, leading to frustration and mechanistic behavior tendencies among group members. Democratic leaders encourage and assist group members in their work, care about and satisfy the needs of group members, fostering a democratic and equal atmosphere. The psychological distance between leaders and employees is relatively small. Under democratic leadership, employees have strong motivation and a sense of responsibility. Subordinates decide on their own methods and progress in work, resulting in higher efficiency. Laissez-faire leaders adopt an anarchic leadership approach, showing little concern for work or the needs of group members. There are no rules, requirements, or evaluations, leading to low work efficiency and indifferent interpersonal relationships.

Lewin initially believed that democratic leadership might bring good work quality and quantity, along with high job satisfaction among group members. Thus, democratic leadership seemed to be the most effective style. However, later studies revealed more complex results. In some cases, democratic leadership yields better performance than authoritarian leadership, while in others, the performance under democratic leadership may be lower or merely equivalent to that under authoritarian leadership. Employee satisfaction surveys show that under democratic leadership, job satisfaction tends to be higher than under authoritarian leadership. However, in practical business management, extreme leadership styles are rare. Most leaders exhibit a mixed style somewhere between authoritarian, democratic, and laissez-faire. Lewin's theory focuses on the leader's own style but does not adequately consider the context in which the leader operates, as the effectiveness of leadership actions depends not only on the leader's style but also on the followers and surrounding environmental factors.

Leaders must continuously self-assess their leadership style while considering whether it is acceptable to employees or which management actions employees can accept without causing psychological harm. "A leader, like a spectator, sees the flaws." Diagnosing one's own leadership style is difficult, so leaders must effectively utilize leadership style diagnostic tools—employee satisfaction surveys.

### 3. Acknowledge Employee Achievements

Acknowledging employee achievements has become a valuable tool for excellent leaders to achieve management goals. When an employee completes a task, they need affirmation from their supervisor most. A supervisor's acknowledgment is the greatest confirmation of their work achievements. Former GE CEO Jack Welch said, "My management philosophy is to make everyone feel their contribution, which is visible, tangible, and countable." Supervisor acknowledgment makes employees feel important, which can inspire a sense of responsibility and duty, reinforcing a positive attitude towards hard work and progress. The timeliness of acknowledgment is key. If used too frequently, its value diminishes; if used sparingly in special occasions or for significant achievements, its value increases. Methods include sending an email, making a private congratulatory phone call, or shaking hands in public and expressing appreciation.

### 4. Maintain Effective Communication

Communication is a critical tool for supervisors and employees to exchange work and emotional information. Effective communication is always two-way. As the saying goes, "One hand cannot clap alone." Only through verbal exchanges and emotional expressions that allow the other party to understand your concepts, ideas, and thoughts can the expected communication goal be achieved. Maintaining effective communication with employees requires high communication skills from supervisors. Supervisors must continuously enhance their communication skills to maintain smooth communication with employees of various personality types. In practice, we find that outstanding leaders have much higher communication abilities than average leaders. What are the prominent communication behaviors of excellent leaders?

1) First, excellent supervisors have proactive communication awareness. They believe that effective communication can unify thinking, unify goals, resolve conflicts, and eliminate misunderstandings. They are willing to solve existing problems through communication or eliminate potential risks.

2) Outstanding leaders are good listeners, especially allowing employees to fully express their opinions rather than rudely interrupting them. This makes the other party feel that their opinions are respected, encouraging them to express their true thoughts and feelings.

3) Another common communication behavior of excellent supervisors is providing positive feedback on employees' opinions. Generally, any ideas, opinions, or suggestions expressed by employees receive quick responses rather than being ignored. Even if the opinions and suggestions are immature, they receive effective guidance alongside acknowledgment, helping employees quickly refine their ideas.

4) Experienced supervisors pay special attention to the timing of communication. For example, they choose moments when employees are emotionally stable to discuss less pleasant topics. They avoid making comments that could hurt employee confidence in public and never reprimand employees on the spot.

5) Finally, excellent leaders choose appropriate wording based on the situation and control reasonable speech rates. For instance, during year-end summary meetings, supervisors often use praise to affirm employee achievements and deliver inspiring speeches to boost morale. Even when discussing mistakes, they take responsibility sincerely and express their ideas earnestly.
