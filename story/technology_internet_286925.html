It doesn't matter whether Obama praises the Great Wall or not.

by alexsgxu on 2009-11-30 23:54:59

Xu Shuguang, Chairman of the Board of格林豪泰酒店and 519.com Wine Direct Sales Network

U.S. President Obama, on the afternoon of November 18th, after climbing the Great Wall, remarked: "The Great Wall is so magnificent, it reminded me of China's long history and civilization." This expression of admiration for China’s ancient civilization was not only made during Obama's visit to the Great Wall, but also during his tour of the Forbidden City. However, despite this, some journalists still felt that Obama's praise was too "fleeting."

Of course, China's brilliant civilization over many years is indeed something we should be proud of. Whenever a distinguished foreign guest comes, showcasing the ancient Chinese civilization and splendid culture is a national courtesy, and there is nothing wrong with that. But if we feel that the admiration expressed by visitors is not grand enough, and that it has hurt the pride of the people, then that would be truly tragic. Because, regardless of whether Obama's admiration for the Great Wall or the Forbidden City is high or low, these are all achievements of our ancestors. We do not need to rest on their laurels with self-satisfaction, nor do we need to seek the favor of foreigners for the accomplishments of our forebears. After enduring a century of humiliation in modern times, the goal of the Chinese people should be focused on achieving the great rejuvenation of the Chinese nation.

In the near future, when another distinguished foreign visitor arrives, we will not only be able to show off the Great Wall and the Forbidden City, but also display China's brand-name enterprises, showcase the happy lives of Chinese people today, demonstrate China's top-notch scientific research capabilities, and exhibit China's environmental protection measures. When others express heartfelt admiration for these aspects as well, we can then feel equally gratified. We need the world's recognition, not only that ancient China was great, but also that present-day China is just as formidable and cannot be overlooked.

We have a foundation of five thousand years of brilliant culture, which is both an advantage and a burden and benchmark. The Chinese people need to put aside the arrogance ingrained deep within us, reflect deeply, and ask ourselves: Can we do better than our predecessors? Can we cultivate a few more Nobel Prize winners like we did with the four great inventions? Can we produce a few great individuals who can influence the world? These are all things we need to achieve. How we are doing now, history will judge us accordingly.
