New York Times: Gates should leave Microsoft along with Ballmer

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 11:06:17

Microsoft Chairman Bill Gates and CEO Steve Ballmer

Lead: The U.S. New York Times website published an article on Wednesday titled "It's Time for Bill Gates to Part Ways with Microsoft," which commented that due to the wrong decisions made in recent years under the leadership of Bill Gates and Steve Ballmer, and when Ballmer announced his retirement, as Chairman of Microsoft, Gates was slow to select a successor, so he should be held responsible for Microsoft's current weak state and leave Microsoft along with Ballmer.

The following is the full text of the article:

Bill Gates might need to leave Microsoft together with Steve Ballmer.

Purchasing Nokia just as Ballmer is about to leave will hand over a flawed strategy to Microsoft's next CEO. As the founder and chairman of the company, Gates should take responsibility for this mistake and other previous issues. Since Gates currently holds less than 5% of Microsoft's shares, his Gates Foundation is now far more important to him than Microsoft.

In a sense, letting Gates leave the software giant he founded himself seems too inhumane. Few people understand the technology industry better than him, and his ability is undoubtedly evident since he has established a company worth $270 billion. With approximately $12.4 billion worth of Microsoft shares in his possession, he remains the largest single shareholder of Microsoft. Added to the deep personal feelings built up between him and Microsoft over the long term, it's impossible for him not to care about Microsoft's future.

However, despite his strong abilities, he hasn't been a good chairman. Under his leadership, Microsoft's board of directors has been too accommodating to Ballmer. Now, with Ballmer retiring within a year, Gates still hasn't selected a successor. This is a crucial task for any chairman.

Microsoft's board allowed Ballmer to waste huge amounts of money on consumer markets and hardware while losing $12 billion in online services alone over the past three years. They have also just approved Microsoft's acquisition plan for Nokia's mobile phone business totaling $7.2 billion, forcing Ballmer's successor to develop equipment manufacturing businesses, which are certainly not Microsoft's forte.

Yes, during Ballmer's 13-year tenure, investors received a return of $180 billion just in the last decade, far exceeding all tech companies. Profits have also increased significantly. Nevertheless, although Ballmer continues to show immense passion - last week's annual investor conference was a good example - shareholders seem not to recognize the strategy jointly formulated by Ballmer and Gates.

For ten years, Microsoft's stock price has remained almost stagnant. In contrast, except for HP, the stock prices of Apple Inc., Google, Oracle, IBM have all reached new highs repeatedly. During the same period, the Nasdaq Composite Index has doubled.

Nowadays, Gates' main interest seems to have shifted. Five years ago, he no longer devoted himself entirely to Microsoft's work. The $38 billion he donated to the foundation far exceeds the market value of his Microsoft stocks. In his view, improving education and preventing diseases seem much more appealing than leading a company to develop tablets.

Ballmer's belated decision to retire gives Gates an opportunity. If he devotes all his energy to the foundation, the world can become a better place - and the days for Microsoft investors will improve as well.
