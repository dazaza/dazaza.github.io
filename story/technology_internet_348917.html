I am sure that among those who have read this article, there must be some people more talented than me. But for those who are more eager to receive help, I still hope to write down my experience and share it with you.

I wrote an article called "The Eight Decisive Things for Your Future, How Many Have You Achieved?" Here I'll summarize it, hoping it will be helpful to you all.

One, you must clearly understand what your future goal is

Many people idealize the workplace too much, but it's not actually like that. If you haven't planned well, you will inevitably encounter various problems: feeling unhappy at work; lacking motivation to move forward; finding work not as good as imagined; unable to showcase your talents; seeing old classmates with better treatment and feeling frustrated, wanting to switch jobs urgently; the job you found for survival simply isn't suitable for you...

These are problems almost every newcomer to the workplace will face. And these problems are most evident within the first 1-3 years of entering the workplace (after 3 years, they're less noticeable because you've been worn down by reality for too long, assimilated by various inequalities, and thus accustomed to them. Your original ambitions have been crushed by reality, and suddenly you've transformed from an ambitious young person into an ordinary worker who runs around for a living).

If these problems aren't solved early, then you will waste these 3 years (for some people, it might be even longer), and for a new employee, these 3 years are crucial, the golden 3 years for exercising abilities and accumulating capital! Once missed, it will take more time to start over and make up for it! Worse yet, some might never be able to make up for it! This is absolutely not scaremongering! Therefore, if your career age hasn't exceeded 3 years, congratulations, you still have a chance to change your fate. But once it exceeds 3 years, each of your changes will come at a huge cost!

Why must you be clear about your goal? Because having a goal provides a guideline for action. Knowing what you want to do and like doing is your ultimate driving force. The fundamental reasons for unhappiness at work, insufficient motivation, and blind job-hopping are unclear professional goals. Without a goal, there's no pursuit, and thus all actions are merely for making a living. You haven't considered what kind of livelihood you should aim for in the future.

Perhaps before the age of 30, you still have the capital to indulge, because you're young, and nothing is scary. Even advertisements say, "What's there to fear before the age of 30?!" But once you pass 30, every step you take must be carefully considered because: you need to get married, support your wife and children, pay off the mortgage, support your aging parents, send your children to school when they grow up, and care for your elderly parents... All these responsibilities fall on you. Without planning, if something goes wrong, you'll only end up floundering.

Professional development is like brand building, except that your product is yourself. Your personal development goal is your life's brand goal. With a goal, you need to consider how to proceed. Each step you take should bring you closer to your goal, not further away. Therefore, whenever you make a decision, the sole criterion for judging its correctness is: does it help you get closer to your goal? If not, then stop immediately, because you're deviating from your goal! Continuing down this path will only make you more wrong!

Regarding life goals, it involves career planning issues. This is an old topic, and many people think they understand it thoroughly, but it's far from that. You can read related books, but the biggest problem with such books in China is that they are too preachy and lack practicality! Many people are still confused and at a loss after reading them, and even more confused afterward. My suggestion is to consult specialized institutions.

Note: It's consultation, not evaluation. The difference between the two is that the purpose of consultation is to "provide clients with actionable solutions," while evaluation is limited to "helping you understand yourself." As for how to act to get closer to your goal, they don't know! Because evaluators are experts, and domestic experts focus on research, lacking practical experience, and even unclear about workplace promotion, how could they guide you in practical planning? I recommend doing career planning. If not addressed now, the next 3 years of your life may be wasted, and it might even ruin your entire life!

Remember: Having a plan doesn't guarantee success, but without a plan, success is impossible!

Two, Ability is important, but it doesn't represent everything

Here, ability refers to professional skills. When you enter the workplace, you'll find that ability isn't as important as you thought; it's not the entirety of getting promoted. You'll find someone with average ability becoming your boss just because they've worked in the company for 10 years — this shows that "seniority" is your promotion capital (this phenomenon is particularly evident in state-owned enterprises and government agencies, so those working in SOEs and civil servants should take note); sometimes you'll find someone with average ability but good relations with the boss often getting promoted — this shows that "connections" are also capital. There are two obvious examples in my previous company.

A man with rich work experience jumped ship to our company, highly capable, but he didn't handle relationships with colleagues well. He thought he was very competent and liked to be others' "mentor," frequently criticizing others' work, which his superior disliked. Another woman who entered the company at the same time wasn't particularly strong but was diligent, hardworking, got along well with other colleagues, and could see the bigger picture. At the end of the probation period, the woman was recognized by the company, while the man received a termination notice.

In the workplace, you must have a clear understanding of the workplace, not live in a dream world, not be overly idealistic, not try to stand out unnecessarily, not be arrogant due to talent, and not think that having ability alone allows you to go anywhere. Professional skills are indeed important, but workplace development looks at a person's overall quality. People with high comprehensive qualities are the "potential stocks" for workplace development. These comprehensive qualities include communication skills, interpersonal relationship abilities, teamwork capabilities, management skills, work attitudes, dedication spirit, and various "soft powers."

For some friends, they might look down on these "soft powers." Many people would say, if the boss likes sycophants, do I have to humble myself and learn to flatter? Unfortunately, I would tell you, yes, you must learn to flatter, otherwise you'll suffer losses. But let me clarify: I'm not teaching you deceit. There's a Chinese idiom, "outwardly round but inwardly square," which means when interacting with people, you should learn to properly handle various relationships without losing your inner principles, otherwise it becomes degenerate. Especially in a Chinese society that values personal relationships, this interpersonal interaction ability must be practiced well.

If you have time, read "The Thick Black Book," understand social realities, and avoid losing yourself in the process.

Three, You must have enough "showing your sword" capital

Some college students often tell me, after graduation, I must find a job with a certain monthly salary, aiming for a certain position. At this point, I always patiently listen to their "grand blueprint" and then ask: "What makes you think you can achieve your goal? What is your capital?" Then they are left speechless.

I believe those who read this article will also be like this college student, having grand ambitions, but after four years of university, have you ever thought about what capital you need to achieve these goals? For many already in the workplace, what is your capital for promotion?

In the process of life development, many times you need to unhesitatingly show others your strength, to dare to display and skillfully display, which is "showing your sword." Because these days, even good wine fears a deep alley, gold may not shine. On the contrary, what shines are broken glass and bottles. China lacks everything except people. In a vast sea of humanity, if you remain modestly unknown, then you're completely mistaken.

Since you have the awareness to "show your sword," the next step is to consider what to show: what capital do I have to show others? For those in the workplace, where is your "showing your sword" capital? Has your performance improved? Has your ability improved? How many books beneficial to your profession have you read in a year? How many training sessions have you attended to recharge yourself? If not, how do you expect your boss to give you a raise? By complaining? You will surely be eliminated from the workplace early!

Four, Treat your first job with caution

This viewpoint is especially meaningful for college students.

Nowadays, the whole society, media, experts, etc., all kinds of organizations keep promoting "get employed first, then choose your career." This notion didn't just appear this year; it has existed for many years and keeps reappearing, incessantly. I believe when we graduated from university, many people believed this, right? Regardless, ride a donkey while looking for a horse, find a job to earn money to fill your stomach first!

But based on my personal experience and the actual situation of workplace development, I absolutely disagree with this statement. I believe that many people currently struggling in their careers are mostly related to their first job. There's a saying, "Men fear choosing the wrong industry, women fear marrying the wrong man." Taking the first step wrong leads to frequent mistakes later. One wrong step, and everything goes wrong.

Why is the first job so important? There are three reasons:

1. The influence of the first impression.

Specifically, the first words heard or the first impressions obtained often dominate the mind, making it difficult to accept different opinions later. Take the simplest example: in your mind, Nokia is synonymous with mobile phones. If one day Nokia produces a "Nokia computer," what would you feel? Very uncomfortable, right? This is the first impression effect.

I had a colleague who used to work at Haier. She used a Haier phone, which functioned quite well, but she always said she wanted to change her phone. When I asked her why, she said that carrying a Haier phone felt like carrying a big refrigerator, very uncomfortable. Why? First impression! Don't underestimate this phenomenon; it significantly affects subconsciousness. Haier's IT products like computers and phones sell poorly, partly due to first impressions: people generally perceive Haier as a household appliance brand, not an IT brand.

For college students seeking employment, this concept also influences your future development. Your first job will affect your subsequent jobs. When changing jobs, your new employer will judge whether you can handle the job based on your first work experience. A college graduate once wanted to become a planner and joined an advertising company. Initially, due to good writing skills, he was assigned to copywriting. Simultaneously, another person who couldn't write well was placed in the planning role. Later, he repeatedly tried to switch to planning but was always denied opportunities due to his previous work experience.

2. The workplace doesn't allow starting over.

If a company recruits experienced employees, it usually requires "relevant experience"! This is crucial! Such employees generally don't need training, can start work immediately, and quickly bring maximum benefits to the company. For applicants who plan to switch industries after working for several years, they usually won't be given much attention, as they wouldn't bring direct benefits to the company, and training them is challenging since their thinking is fixed. Instead, it's better to hire fresh graduates, blank slates, moldable, with greater potential for development.

Therefore, after gaining work experience, switching industries and starting over is quite difficult. Unless you know someone high up in the company who can offer you a chance, otherwise, it's hard.

3. Time costs don't allow it.

The first three years in the workplace are the most important and critical period for personal career growth. If during this time you focus on switching industries in a "riding a donkey while looking for a horse" manner, you'll waste your most precious youth, your growth will lag behind others, and many promotion opportunities will be lost.

Therefore, treat your first job with caution. Finding a job is like getting married; it's better not to marry than to marry recklessly.

If you've been in the workplace for less than three years, I advise you to create a career plan soon. It's not too late to mend the fence after the sheep are lost. Once you're married and buying a house, it's too late to remedy!

Five, Maintain an "empty cup" mentality

Let me tell you a story. In ancient times, there was a person with deep knowledge of Buddhism who heard about a venerable old Zen master in a temple, so he went to visit. The disciple who greeted him was haughty, thinking: I have deep knowledge of Buddhism, who are you? Later, the old Zen master received him respectfully and made tea for him. When pouring water, although the cup was already full, the old Zen master continued pouring. Perplexed, he asked: "Master, why pour water when the cup is already full?" The master replied: "That's right, if it's already full, why pour more?"

The master's meaning was, since you already have much knowledge, why come to me for teachings? This is the philosophy of the "empty cup" mentality. Its direct meaning is that a cup filled with water finds it hard to accept new things. To empty the "cup" in your heart, discard many things you value, and conclude and clear your once-glorious past from your mindset. Only by emptying your heart can you let go externally, achieving greater success. This is the most important mindset that anyone wanting to develop in the workplace must possess. It tells us a principle: the prerequisite for doing things is to have a good mindset. If you want to learn more, imagine yourself as "an empty cup" rather than being proud and conceited.

I've met many college graduates who, upon entering a company, often brag everywhere, boasting about their achievements in school, claiming they could have found better jobs, but ended up here reluctantly, as if the company wronged them. Let me remind those about to enter the workplace: this mindset is absolutely unacceptable! Don't think you're extraordinary. Still that line you don't like hearing: China lacks everything except people! Speaking of which, if you're so extraordinary, why didn't you drop out of university like Bill Gates, Dell, or Jobs and start a world-class enterprise? Trains aren't pushed, and boasts aren't blown.

If you're capable, prove it with facts, not constant complaints!

For those already in the workplace, this mindset is equally important. There's a Chinese term, "humble as a valley." We all know that excellent people have extremely high qualities, even humility, possessing a broad-mindedness that includes everything. With such a broad-mindedness, you'll gain respect in the workplace and more development opportunities. Similar terms include "wisdom appears foolish" and "skill appears clumsy," and so on. I won't elaborate; you can ponder them yourself.

Six, Understand a bit about human common sense

The workplace is like a melting pot. In the workplace, it's not just about earning a living, but more importantly, learning the principles of being a decent human being. If you don't even understand the most basic principles, you won't gain respect from others and will lose more development opportunities. Because in China, interpersonal relationships are also productivity. There's an ancient Chinese saying: "Those who follow the righteous path will receive much assistance, while those who stray from it will find themselves alone."

I've seen many people who, despite working for many years, still don't understand even the most basic common sense about being a decent human being. Among the countless principles of being human, the most important ones are the following three:

1. Be strict with yourself and lenient with others.

The workplace differs from daily life. Although relationships between colleagues seem relatively harmonious on the surface, you always feel something is missing. A very obvious point is that everyone is using each other, and conflicts inevitably arise due to interests. At this moment, if you nitpick, it will undoubtedly cause unpleasantness among colleagues.

In such situations, to avoid disputes and win respect from others, you need to learn to be more generous and tolerant. When problems arise, first reflect on whether you made a mistake instead of immediately blaming others (if this continues, your mindset will become imbalanced, making it difficult for you to tolerate others).
