The Little King of Online Store Opening Achieves 80,000 Transactions in Three Months

by longyi on 2008-08-31 14:11:43

At the end of August 2004, after graduating from Shanghai University's advertising art design program, Hong Qiliang did not go job-hunting. Instead, he chose to continue operating his online store. This Shanghai boy, born in 1983, has been online for nine years as of now. In the recently concluded university student e-commerce competition, Hong Qiliang's online store won first place with a transaction amount of 82,090 yuan over three months.

Shanghai is China's most modernized city, and the young people here enjoy exploring new things. In 1995, while still in the first year of junior high school, Hong Qiliang became one of the first internet citizens. Browsing web pages, chatting, listening to music... After becoming familiar with these elements of the internet, Hong Qiliang discovered that many dazzling goods were also sold online, so he wanted to try it out himself. He dug up a pile of unused CD-Rs that his father had bought and wrote a brief introduction which he posted on eBay, along with pictures. For the first three days, there was no interest. Unexpectedly, seven days later, he received a purchase call asking how payment could be made. Hong Qiliang was overjoyed. This was his first successful online transaction, and he didn't lose money.

After this initial success, Hong Qiliang began using his free time to learn operational techniques and converted all his pocket money into working capital. In 2001, he entered the Art School of Shanghai University and also opened his own online shop that same year, naming it "Ruizhishu Digital." "At first, I just did it for fun, then I realized that I could actually make some pocket money," said Hong Qiliang. The first two months of his shop's operation were relatively slow due to low credibility ratings. Later, some visiting customers found that this shopkeeper was trustworthy, leading to more repeat customers and gradually increasing business. Hong Qiliang used the money earned from his online store to pay for all his expenses during his college years. No longer financially dependent on his parents, he felt proud among his peers.

When Hong Qiliang first started getting obsessed with the internet, he faced opposition from his parents, who argued that it would "affect his studies," resulting in his computer being "sealed off." "I had to argue and insist until I finally got my lucky money back from them. My first risk investment should have been me investing in myself," said Hong Qiliang. He believed this was a correct decision in his life, "After I achieved results, my family supported me and provided funding." Hong Qiliang mentioned that later on, his online store had the help of a "family studio." His father worked at a property management company near the post office, often taking care of mailing goods on his behalf. His mother also helped manage the financial accounts of the online store, "My mom is an accountant. Although she hasn't retired yet, handling my little business is a piece of cake for her."

By 2004, the monthly average sales of Hong Qiliang's online store had reached over ten thousand yuan; he said if the business expanded further, he would start a company and hire people to work together. After winning the university student e-commerce competition, Hong Qiliang received a 100,000 yuan prize. When China E-Commerce Association Chairman Song Ling and eBay CEO Greg Gebu presented him with the trophy and a giant check model, Hong Qiliang felt like a "star" at that moment. In the early stages of entrepreneurship, Hong Qiliang had many ideas. He planned to use part of the prize money as working capital to continue expanding the business, and also intended to take some courses in management and e-commerce.
