Types of People Unsuitable for Sales

Author: Source: Internet Update Time: June 23, 2010

There is a saying that as long as someone undergoes rigorous training, anyone can become a qualified salesperson. However, the innate characteristics each person is born with are difficult to change. Sales is one of the most challenging industries, requiring constant adjustments in mindset and actions to adapt. The difficulty of training someone whose personality is unsuited for sales into a qualified salesperson is self-evident. Moreover, companies need their sales teams to deliver results quickly, and the time available for training is limited.

If an employee's personality traits are suitable for sales, they will quickly get up to speed. However, if they are not suited, it will take more time and intensified training. Additionally, due to the recurring thought patterns caused by their personality, training them may require even more time. Corporate training is not like the nine-year compulsory education system; whether a company can bear such time costs is a question. Therefore, it’s best to retain employees whose personalities suit sales and let those who don’t leave. This benefits both individuals and companies in the long run.

Types of People Unsuitable for Sales:

1. Aristocratic Lord Type

People with this type of personality consider themselves superior to others and look down on others, including clients, subconsciously. Their work often shows impatience, irritability, frequent complaints, and severe cases may involve losing control of emotions and provoking others.

This type of person often has the following attitude towards clients: "Fine, if you don't buy, so be it!" They lack patience and understanding for clients, making it hard to win their trust.

2. Lin Daiyu Type (Overly Sensitive Type)

People who are overly sensitive. Changes in weather or the blooming and falling of flowers can affect their mood. In social interactions, they often put all their enthusiasm forward but easily become disheartened and disappointed when faced with setbacks. Such people are overly sensitive to their environment. A single word or small action from a client can hurt them greatly. They often view minor gains and losses as significant issues and dwell on them, causing unnecessary distress.

3. Qu Yuan Type (Melancholic Type)

People who are melancholic and unhappy, believing that "the world is murky, only I am clear," or "the world is drunk, only I am sober." They are self-centered, uninterested in external matters, proud and aloof, dislike socializing, prefer solitude, lack team spirit, and blame fate, feeling everyone else owes them something.

Overly melancholic sales representatives often see themselves as the most unfortunate people in the world. They appear suppressed, gloomy, and rarely smile. They tend to have pessimistic views about things, easily feel inferior and self-blame when encountering setbacks, and make internal attributions. They rarely take proactive market actions and only communicate with clients when absolutely necessary.

Common Misconceptions About Sales Work:

1. Only Extroverts Are Suited for Sales

Extroverted people are talkative, humorous, emotionally expressive, enthusiastic, creative, liven up the work atmosphere, and have a wide circle of friends. However, everything has two sides. Extroverted and lively people often seek novelty, frequently changing their focus, starting strong but lacking follow-through and patience. They never reflect on themselves, never think they make big mistakes, talk too much, listen too little, fail to notice negative reactions from others, and do not pay attention to others' interests and real needs. They lack organization and systematicness in their work, and while they have many friends, their relationships are shallow. On the other hand, introverted people, with their steadiness and empathy, can give people a sense of trustworthiness and often achieve excellent results.

2. Ignoring Differences in Industries and Sales Models

Believing that the quality requirements for salespeople across different industries are the same. In fact, different product types, industries, and sales models require different qualities from salespeople.

Generally speaking, extroverted people are better suited as "hunters" to develop new clients, fitting more into efficiency-based sales like fast-moving consumer goods and insurance. Introverted people are better suited as "farmers," cultivating existing clients, suitable for relationship-based sales such as industrial products and government relations.

If the client is a hospital, the salesperson should ideally be meticulous and expert-like. If the client is the government, the salesperson should ideally be amiable and peace-loving. If the client is extroverted, the salesperson should also be extroverted. When France's Alcatel mobile phone department was acquired by TCL, many salespeople and sales managers left because Alcatel phones were sold through channel distributors, requiring "consultative" salespeople. Later, TCL emphasized direct sales, which required "efficiency-based" salespeople.
