Xie Yong, with his consistent plainness and gentle touch, carefully reads through the first-hand collected materials of recent China, where doubts about ideas gradually emerge. A segment recalling Chen Baozhen's implementation of new policies, an obscure article by Hu Shi introducing a Peking University professor from the U.S., Chen Shiceng's self-written short booklet "A History of Chinese Painting," a less noticed letter by Chu Anping titled "To My Younger Brothers," early translation works on logical positivism by Yin Haiguang, initial explanatory documents on the "Joseph Needham Question," rumors about Mao Zedong's relationship with Xiamen University, Sun Yuesheng's posthumous work on bureaucratic politics, Qian Zhongshu's anecdotes about avoiding the Zhou brothers, the handwritten copies of Chen Yinke's poems among professors at Sun Yat-sen University, a letter defending himself by Fan Wenlan, several directives that influenced Fei Xiaotong's later life... We read history, and the author believes that if we start from the materials, we may sometimes more easily see the real situation, while even the best textbooks are inevitably somewhat misleading. Writing articles and doing research should aim for knowledge increment, adding something to what already exists, be it a piece of historical material, an angle or a clue; having an increment makes it meaningful.

A normal era should be one where there is freedom in reading. One should believe that people have strong selection abilities; let them read what they want, and over time, they will naturally know the good from the bad. A person raised in an environment of free choice will grow up mentally and physically healthy. If a society has lost the freedom to read, that is very frightening. In my personal opinion, among Chinese intellectuals, those born after the 1930s basically haven't read much. It's not that there weren't capable readers, but during their later developmental years, there were no books available to read.

From this piece of historical material, it can be seen that the young Mao Zedong had already entered the vision of China's main intellectuals. Judging from what Shu Xin Cheng said, he intended to introduce Mao Zedong to work at the China Public School, but due to "only graduating from No.1 Normal School," it seemed difficult to arrange. As for the matter of "Xiamen University needing teachers," it originally stemmed from Xiamen University's early days when its president Lin Wenqing wrote to Liang Qichao asking him to help find suitable teachers.
