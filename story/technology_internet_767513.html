Baidu Sues Qihu 360 for Violating the "Robots Protocol"

The case where Baidu sued Qihu 360 for violating the "Robots Protocol" by scraping and copying website content without permission was heard in the Beijing No.1 Intermediate People's Court on the morning of October 16th.

Baidu claims that 360 Search scraped content from Baidu’s websites, including Baidu Zhidao (Baidu Knows), Baidu Baike (Baidu Encyclopedia), and Baidu Tieba (Baidu Forums), without Baidu's consent, violating the widely recognized Robots Protocol within the industry, thereby constituting unfair competition. As a result, Baidu has filed a lawsuit against Qihu 360 demanding compensation of 1 billion yuan.

In August 2012, shortly after 360 Search quietly launched, it began to violate the Robots Protocol by forcibly scraping content from Baidu's websites such as Baidu Zhidao, Baidu Baike, Baidu Tieba, and Baidu Travel. Even before being listed in Baidu’s Robots.txt file to allow its web crawler, 360 violated the protocol by forcibly scraping content from “Baidu Zhidao” and “Baidu Baike.”

Baidu believes that Qihu 360's actions have disregarded internationally recognized industry standards, ignored Baidu's rights statements and technical measures, illegally scraped and copied Baidu website content, directly providing snapshots to internet users, severely infringing upon Baidu's legitimate rights and interests, thus constituting unfair competition. Subsequently, Baidu filed a lawsuit against Qihu 360 at the Beijing No.1 Intermediate People's Court, which was officially accepted on February 23rd of this year.

Guo Biao from Baidu's Public Relations Department stated to the media that the Robots Protocol is one of the internationally recognized norms for protecting website information and netizen privacy, and should be followed by all global internet companies. Disregarding the Robots Protocol could lead to large-scale leakage of netizens' privacy. Previously, due to vulnerabilities in its own site, 360's server captured private data from users, leading to the exposure and dissemination of commercial secrets such as internal procurement documents from Chery Automobile and financial reports from the Three Gorges Group, along with the privacy of 1.4 million netizens. This violation of the Robots Protocol once again exposed 360's disregard for website copyrights and netizen privacy. Consequently, Qihu 360 was severely criticized by authoritative departments such as the National Copyright Administration and the State Administration for Industry and Commerce.

Guo Biao noted that what is most reprehensible about Qihu 360 is not merely its search crawler's deliberate violation of the Robots Protocol, but also its use of 360 Browser and other client applications to forcibly scrape browsing data and information from users and send it to the search server, completely ignoring the Robots Protocol. This practice has already led to the leakage of a large amount of enterprise intranet information. Last year, through a test named "Ghost Festival Ghost Catching," Baidu engineers proved that 360 Browser had the behavior of privately uploading isolated page content and other private content to 360 Search.

Guo Biao said that such practices of uploading information via client applications and placing them on search engines could lead to large-scale leakage of netizens' privacy. For instance, if a securities company employee inadvertently used the 360 browser to view customers' names, bank account numbers, and passwords, then these accounts and passwords might be searched and made public. If a senior executive used the 360 browser to check internal confidential data, then the core trade secrets of the company might be directly searchable by competitors.

Renowned IT commentator Hong Bo pointed out, "If such behavior by 360 is not promptly stopped by legal means and government supervision, all websites could trample on industry rules like 360 does, causing chaos in the entire internet industry."

Prominent intellectual property lawyer Zhao Zhanling believed that 360 uses technical means to bypass the copyright protection measures set up by Baidu, directly scraping and copying related product web pages without permission, suspected of copyright infringement. At the same time, 360 bypasses Baidu's protective measures through hidden access sources and other technologies without permission, involving unfair competition. Chief lawyer Zhao Hu of Beijing Hanzhuo Law Firm also expressed that according to a series of laws and regulations such as the Tort Liability Law and the General Principles of Civil Law, 360's search behavior has been illegal. Since 360's illegal search behavior involves a wide range and many people, it has infringed upon public interests, and government departments cannot turn a blind eye but must conduct investigations and handle the matter.

Regarding Baidu's statement, Tencent Technology contacted 360 Company but did not receive any formal response from the other party.

What is the Robots Protocol?

The Robots Protocol, also known as the Robot Exclusion Standard or simply the爬虫协议, is an agreement between search engines and websites regarding the scope of content scraping. It includes whether a website wishes to be indexed by search engines and what content should not be scraped. Web crawlers accordingly "consciously" scrape or avoid scraping certain webpage content. Since its introduction, the Robots Protocol has become an international convention for websites to protect their sensitive proprietary data and netizen privacy.
