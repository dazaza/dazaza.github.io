The Key to Caring for Esophageal Cancer Patients Lies in Lifestyle and Diet

How should patients with esophageal cancer be cared for, especially in terms of diet?

1. Nutritional Imbalance: Caused by long-term difficulty in eating, leading to insufficient nutrient intake. The main symptoms include weight loss, anemia, dehydration, and hypoproteinemia.

2. Anxiety and Fear: Related to the knowledge of having cancer and fear of surgery.

3. Possible Postoperative Complications

a. Risk of Anastomotic Leak: Related to local infection, circulatory disorders, excessive tension during suturing, and suboptimal suturing techniques. Anastomotic leak is a serious postoperative complication that often leads to patient death.

b. Risk of Electrolyte Imbalance: Preoperative electrolyte imbalance due to fasting and vomiting, and postoperatively related to surgical damage to the thoracic duct and lymphatic vessels causing chylothorax.

4. Dysphagia: Caused by esophageal tumor obstructing the cavity, mainly manifested as progressive dysphagia. At this point, liquid foods can be consumed. Caring for Esophageal Cancer Patients Should Be Scientific and Reasonable

For esophageal cancer patients, external care and support are the source of courage in their battle against the illness. However, we need to note that this care must be moderate, conducted in a way the patient can accept, and aimed at helping the patient recover. Sometimes excessive care or inappropriate methods can lead to opposite results, harming rather than helping the patient.

1. Showing obvious pity and sympathy in front of the patient during visits, even shedding tears together, being overly cautious in conversation, avoiding eye contact, and changing the subject. All these can make the patient feel sad, lonely, and oppressed, leading to feelings of pessimism and despair.

2. Cutting off the patient’s social connections, such as overly strict restrictions on visits from others, limiting reading and studying, even banning newspapers and television, reducing their activity range, etc. This increases the patient’s sense of loneliness, possibly making them feel abandoned by society, and does not help them shift focus or relieve mental pressure.

3. Excessive frequency of visits. Sometimes after an esophageal cancer patient is hospitalized, family members, relatives, friends, colleagues, and old classmates all come to visit. This affects the patient’s rest and creates significant psychological pressure, making them think they don’t have much time left.

4. Excessive indulgence or restriction in daily life. Some family members, regardless of scientific evidence, restrict what the patient can and cannot eat or touch, even depriving them of normal marital rights. Other families believe that since the patient doesn't have much time left, they try to satisfy all their requests, allowing them to smoke if they want, drink if they wish, and eat whatever they desire. However, many of their diseases are caused by long-term unhealthy lifestyles, which do not benefit treatment or recovery, easily leading to nutritional imbalance and psychological disorders.

5. Being overly attentive, even handling trivial tasks like squeezing toothpaste or wringing out towels for the patient, fearing they might get tired. This can easily make esophageal cancer patients feel like they are useless, believing their existence only burdens society and family, thus losing faith in life. On the other hand, some patients, after treatments like radiation and chemotherapy, may become physically weak and develop dependency on others' help, abandoning opportunities for exercise and rehabilitation.

Molecular anti-cancer experts indicate that all of the above practices are incorrect. For family members and friends of patients, it is important to encourage and supervise changes in unhealthy lifestyle and dietary habits, enabling esophageal cancer patients to engage in appropriate exercise and gradually improve their physical condition to fight the disease. Encourage them to actively integrate into society and not interrupt continued cancer treatment just because obstructive symptoms have disappeared post-surgery, as the risk of recurrence, metastasis, and spread is too high. It is recommended to use small molecule targeted anti-cancer drugs like Kang Ai Xin Sheng R-su or combine with other treatments to enhance natural anti-cancer immunity, prevent recurrence, metastasis, and spread, increasing the five-year survival rate and quality of life.
