Danger Signals of Intellectual Disability in Infants and Toddlers

Infants, especially newborns, are too young, so their psychological impairments may sometimes be harder to detect. Parents might even mistakenly think that their child is simply very well-behaved—lying quietly without crying or causing trouble. However, this could actually be one of the manifestations of psychological and behavioral disorders. These infants often show little interest in their surroundings and have poor attention and response capabilities.

Key manifestations of psychological disorders in infants include:

1. Smiling appears much later than usual, they don't pay attention when others speak, accompanied by delayed motor development (e.g., remote control airplanes).

2. Poor eye function development—they don’t focus on their surroundings and are often mistakenly thought to be blind.

3. Lack of response to sound, leading them to be misdiagnosed as deaf.

4. Delayed chewing, resulting in feeding difficulties. When given solid food, they experience swallowing difficulties, which can cause vomiting.

5. Normally, after children learn to walk, their feet no longer bump into each other while walking. In children with slow development, their feet continue to bump even after they start walking; some still exhibit this behavior at 2-3 years old.

6. Persistent fascination with hand movements. Normal children around 3-4 months old frequently lie in bed looking at and playing with their hands. Children with intellectual disabilities may continue this behavior beyond 6 months.

7. During wakefulness, children with intellectual disabilities may display teeth grinding, a behavior not seen in normal children.

8. Sometimes, children with intellectual disabilities require repeated or continuous stimulation to elicit crying. Their cries often produce throaty sounds, sharp cries, or high-pitched screams, and sometimes weak cries. Normal children's cries usually involve tone changes.

9. Lack of interest and poor concentration are two significant characteristics of children with intellectual disabilities. They show little interest in their surroundings and toys, and their reactions are sluggish.

10. Children with intellectual disabilities sometimes sleep excessively or exhibit aimless hyperactivity.

Emotions and feelings develop as children adapt to different situations. For example, completing a task successfully brings a sense of ease and happiness, losing a loved one causes sadness, facing an enemy evokes anger, and encountering danger leads to fear. Emotions and feelings are non-intellectual factors but are crucial for healthy child development.

During the neonatal period, children tend to exhibit more negative emotions due to adapting to new environments. After two months, positive emotions gradually increase. When fed and warm, one can observe more active and smiling expressions. Especially, there is a unique expression toward mothers or close individuals. After 5-6 months, they become particularly interested in colorful or sound-making toys. With proper nurturing, children generally display affirmative emotions after the age of one, enjoying constant activity mainly driven by the pursuit of pleasure.

If movement freedom, appropriate toys, and interaction with adults are lacking—even if physiological needs like being full or warm are met—children may still have poor emotional states, such as being lethargic or prone to crying. This is very detrimental to their physical and mental development.

Besides emotions, children also begin to experience more complex feelings. For instance, they enjoy interacting with familiar people and derive happiness from it. They also develop empathy, showing concern when their mother or other family members are in pain.

It is essential to correctly guide the emotions and feelings of infants and toddlers. If improperly guided, children may develop negative and unhealthy emotions such as jealousy, shyness around strangers, fear of darkness, fear of thunder, and frequent temper tantrums. Therefore, cultivating good and rich emotions from a young age is extremely important.
