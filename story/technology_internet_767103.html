iOS System: A touching metamorphosis process

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:57:01

Time flies; before we know it, iOS has become one of the oldest mobile operating systems still in active use today. How did a platform that once lacked third-party apps, multitasking, and even copy-paste functionality evolve into the feature-rich platform we know today? Let's take a look back at the evolution of iOS.

Since the introduction of the iPhone by Steve Jobs on January 9, 2007, over six years have passed during which the iPhone, iPod touch, and iPad have redefined the entire mobile computing landscape. Time certainly moves quickly; before we know it, iOS has become one of the oldest mobile operating systems still in active use today. Being one of the oldest does not mean it has become outdated or obsolete. On the contrary, with each update, it has grown into one of the most feature-rich and best-supported platforms available.

iOS 6, the current operating system running on Apple's mobile devices, offers new users an easy-to-understand platform, developers a powerful toolset, and maintains a relatively unified ecosystem across multiple devices. Perhaps what is most astonishing about iOS is that fundamentally, the system has not changed since 2007, yet there has been significant enhancement in both depth and quantity of features. Fortunately, iOS has avoided the "stagnation" issues plaguing other older operating systems, consistently maintaining its forward-thinking nature and consistency. The story of iOS will continue with the official release of iOS 7 on September 18.

How did a platform that once lacked third-party apps, multitasking, and even copy-paste functionality evolve into the feature-rich platform we know today? Let's take a look back at the evolution of iOS.

The origins of iOS actually stem from OS X.

At the launch of the first iPhone, Apple claimed that its system was based on a Unix core, much like Mac OS X, using many of the same tools as Mac OS X. However, even then, iOS had enough distinct characteristics to be recognized as an independent system. Originally known as iPhone OS, this name persisted for four years until it was renamed to iOS with the release of iOS 4 in June 2010. For convenience, throughout the following text, it will simply be referred to as iOS.

iOS 1: The Birth of the iPhone

It may be hard to imagine now, but the original iPhone was functionally behind its competitors when it launched. Systems like Windows Mobile, Palm OS, Symbian, and even BlackBerry were already stable platforms in 2007, with a wide range of features. In contrast, the iPhone lacked 3G support, multitasking, third-party apps, copy-paste functionality, the ability to attach files to emails, MMS support, Exchange push email, customizable home screens, voice dialing—essentially, it was a completely closed platform that shut out hackers and developers alike.

These omissions were evident, and Apple was fully aware of them. But instead of competing feature-for-feature, Apple focused on delivering a superior core experience. It concentrated on speed and tight integration between applications, optimizing some functions beyond what existed in 2007. Among the innovative features introduced in iOS 1, three had the most significant impact on the mobile industry:

The Core iOS User Interface. Before iOS, smartphones either lacked touchscreens or relied on stylus input on resistive screens. The capacitive touchscreen of the iPhone revolutionized this norm, but more importantly, Apple designed a simpler and more powerful system that highlighted the hardware’s advantages. By reducing physical buttons to just five, Apple made the touchscreen the primary mode of interaction. Apple also perfected pinch-to-zoom and smooth scrolling, making apps feel more natural and immediate. The speed and immediacy of iOS 1 not only shocked contemporaries but remains impressive even today.

Mobile Safari Browser. When introducing Mobile Safari, Steve Jobs declared it was several years ahead of competitors’ offerings. Yes, it didn’t support Flash, but it was the first mobile browser to offer desktop-like browsing capabilities. At the time, mobile browsers were often limited or offered significantly different experiences compared to their desktop counterparts. Mobile Safari was fully-featured and included advanced zooming and scrolling capabilities that were cutting-edge at the time.

“Wide-Screen” iPod. Apple integrated its wildly popular iTunes and iPod music playback functionalities into iPhone OS. For many, listening to music on a phone might not have been a decisive factor in purchasing, but it was certainly a key selling point for the iPhone, easily understood and appreciated by end-users.

iOS 1.0 also introduced several forward-thinking applications and features:

Google Maps provided a surprisingly better experience on the iPhone than on other platforms. Apple fully utilized the pinch-to-zoom gesture, making Google Maps feel smoother and faster, but more importantly, it felt more intuitive and natural than even the desktop version.

Virtual Voicemail showcased Apple’s ability to negotiate with carriers, signaling that Apple would be the provider of the user experience, not the carrier.

iTunes Sync, while less noticed today, was revolutionary at the time. Anyone who has used Palm’s HotSync or Microsoft’s ActiveSync would appreciate the simplicity of iTunes Sync—a perfect example of how Apple simplified complex functionalities.

The virtual keyboard in iOS 1 was perhaps the first truly usable on-screen keyboard for typing with fingers. While Palm OS's Graffiti and third-party components like FitalyStamp allowed stylus input, iOS introduced a virtual keyboard that appeared when needed and disappeared when not, playing a critical role in the widespread adoption of smartphones.

Of course, being a new system, iOS 1 had its shortcomings, the most notable being the lack of native third-party app support. Apple attempted to compensate with web-based apps, but HTML5 apps in 2007 weren't ready for prime time. While other smartphone platforms offered less-than-ideal multitasking experiences, they at least supported multitasking, whereas iOS 1 only allowed background music playback via iPod. The absence of multitasking was a sore point for users for several years.

iOS 1 also established a new computing paradigm for smartphones by hiding the file system from users. This design choice continues to spark heated discussions today, but it undoubtedly simplified the device’s technical aspects, making it easier for users to understand. However, the inability to change alert tones was frustrating, especially since even the cheapest feature phones could do so.

iOS 1 introduced the Springboard home screen concept, arranging all apps in a grid, though at the time, users couldn’t rearrange app positions. Even today, iOS users cannot place widgets on the home screen, a customization option that Windows Mobile and Symbian supported long ago.

If you compare features item by item, iPhone OS fell short of Windows Mobile. But in terms of actual usability, the longevity of these two systems tells us which one proved more sustainable.

Major Updates to iOS 1

Three months after the launch of the first iPhone, Apple released its first software update, iOS 1.1.1. This was considered a major update for three reasons: First, it came alongside new hardware, the iPod touch; second, it demonstrated Apple's commitment to continuously updating iOS with new versions and features; third, it showed Apple’s effort to ensure as many iOS devices as possible could run the latest software updates. At the time, Apple only had two iOS devices, avoiding fragmentation within the product line, but ensuring every product received software updates equally.

Functionality-wise, 1.1.1 only included an update to the iTunes Wi-Fi Music Store, allowing users to purchase music directly from their phones. From this, we can see the first obstacle Apple faced with carriers—due to AT&T's bandwidth concerns, purchases were restricted to Wi-Fi networks. Similar carrier obstacles arose in subsequent updates.

1.1.1 also added a shortcut operation by double-tapping the Home button, marking the first change to the Home button's functionality and hinting at its evolving responsibilities beyond returning to the home screen.

With iOS 1.1.3, users could finally reorder icons on their home screens. A web shortcut feature called “Web Clips” was added, which was well-received by users at the time but also reminded everyone that iOS still lacked a local App SDK. I vividly remember the mixed feelings people had towards the iPhone at the time—frustration because they could see the untapped potential of iOS, coupled with excitement as Apple began rolling out regular updates. Every iOS update brought cheers of “Finally!” as users knew exactly what features were missing and celebrated when Apple addressed them. Despite still lacking native third-party app support, everyone seemed confident that it would come—and soon.

iOS 2: About Applications

In July 2008, iOS reached its "historic moment"—the App Store. Third-party apps were unfamiliar to new smartphone users, but Apple streamlined the process of development, browsing, and installation.

App Store. Users could browse and install apps directly on their devices or through iTunes, a revolutionary move. Prior to this, third-party apps were sold through websites or third-party stores, with no integration into the device itself. Apple had already built a large base of iTunes music users, giving the App Store instant credibility, enabling users to securely link their credit cards for purchases.

iOS SDK. Another groundbreaking innovation was the iOS Software Development Kit (SDK) provided to developers. Generally, iOS apps were better-functioning, more visually appealing, and technologically advanced compared to apps on other platforms. The synergy between hardware and software put this platform ahead in terms of third-party app support.

However, the App Store wasn't without criticism. Apple enforced strict guidelines, rejecting some apps that were commonly accepted on other platforms.

Unexpectedly, the App Store drove down app prices collectively, reducing the cost of a new mobile app from $40 to $5 or even $0.99. The popularity of app rankings rose, similar to music charts—if your app made the top list, it meant big sales. Initially, this worried developers, but over the years, it became clear that both large companies and independent developers benefited from the App Store.

Microsoft Exchange indicated that iOS 2 started incorporating features already supported by other platforms.

MobileMe—Apple's own cloud service replaced .Mac, integrating email, calendars, and contacts. MobileMe never gained much traction among users, partly due to its $99 annual fee and relative instability at the time.

iOS 2 was launched alongside the iPhone 3G, adding hardware features such as A-GPS and 3G support.

Major Updates to iOS 2

iOS 2 was not a very stable version. Many users experienced severe issues such as rapid battery drain, app crashes, and missed calls. Two months later, Apple quickly released 2.1, addressing these serious problems and fixing numerous bugs, improving iTunes sync speeds.

Two months after that, Apple released iOS 2.2 in November. The Maps app saw its biggest update, adding Google Street View, walking directions, and public transit routes.

iOS 3: About Features

In June 2009, iOS 3 was launched alongside the iPhone 3GS. It didn’t introduce jaw-dropping features—it was more about gradually filling in the functional gaps of iOS, bringing various new small and large features.

Copy-Paste. iOS's copy-paste feature arrived late compared to other systems, where users could previously select text with a stylus. However, the text selection experience in iOS 3 surpassed those of competing platforms. Although many features added in iOS 3 were long overdue, Apple eventually refined its user interface according to its high standards.

Spotlight Search. As iOS content grew, a system-wide search function became necessary. Apple finally incorporated this feature, already common on BlackBerry, Palm OS, WebOS, and Windows Mobile: quick text entry on the home screen for searching. Initially placed on the far-left of the home screen, Spotlight was hidden starting with iOS 7. At the time, Spotlight could search contacts, emails, calendars, notes, and iPod music, expanding to include new search categories later.

Push Notifications for Third-Party Apps. Although promised during the iOS 2 launch, push notifications were fully supported starting with iOS 3. Not an Apple invention, the implementation was still superior to competitors. Push notifications served as a temporary substitute for multitasking, but the intrusive pop-up style quickly annoyed users.

MMS. Apple added support for multimedia messaging, which by mid-2009 had become somewhat redundant.

iOS 3 marked the time when Apple improved existing features found on other platforms, including video recording, landscape keyboards, voice memos, remote wipe, Bluetooth speakers, and network sharing via Bluetooth or USB.

Apple also added voice dialing, not novel for smartphones, but fairly speaking, Apple’s voice control extended beyond dialing, supporting music playback and pausing in multiple languages. The iOS language support expanded accordingly.

While iOS 1 laid the groundwork for the platform, iOS 2 opened the door to applications, and iOS 3 furnished and fine-tuned the platform. In this third version, Apple didn’t settle for merely matching competitors' features but created a consistent operating system in both surface-level functionality and underlying experience. At the time, only a few key features were missing, with multitasking being the most anticipated by users.

Major Updates to iOS 3

Months later, Apple released iOS 3.1, adding more features, including remote locking, ringtones download, Music Genius, and Bluetooth-connected voice control. iOS 3.1.3, released in February 2010, became the final firmware update for the original iPhone and iPod Touch, preventing further upgrades for these early devices.

iOS 3.2: The Birth of iPad

Each hardware update was accompanied by a new iOS version, and the iPad was no exception. Apple released iOS 3.2 primarily to adapt iOS to the iPad's new screen resolution, including landscape home screens and new popup dialog boxes.

UI for Larger Screens. To adapt iOS to the iPad's larger screen, Apple didn’t simply scale up the interface but introduced several changes specifically tailored for larger displays. The most important change was the addition of a sidebar menu on the left. Previously, users had to navigate through lists one-by-one and return to previous screens. Apple placed the list on the left, showing content on the right, eliminating the need to return to the list. In horizontal mode, the content expanded full-screen, with the list becoming a popup. Starting with this version, Apple increasingly used popup dialogs, avoiding the need for iPhone users to switch between menus and content.

Application Redesigns. Apple's core apps were updated for the iPad. Safari featured a row for bookmarks, the App Store adopted CoverFlow-style layouts, the Photos app displayed pictures stacked together, and finger-spreading gestures previewed the stack. The Music app interface was simplified with album art like iTunes, and the Settings app adopted a side-by-side layout. These interface tweaks adapted the local apps to the iPad's 1024x768 screen resolution.

Skeuomorphism. iOS's aesthetic of skeuomorphism originated here. The Notes app resembled leather-bound paper, looking like a notebook. The Calendar and Contacts apps mimicked notebooks, even down to the paper texture. At the time, skeuomorphic designs merely imitated real objects rather than providing interactive elements like page-turning animations. iBooks introduced realistic page-turning effects, setting a precedent for true skeuomorphism. Despite this, skeuomorphic design became a hallmark of iOS and even transitioned to Mac OS X.

For the newly introduced iPad, opinions varied: some viewed it as merely a larger iPhone. Technically, iOS 3.2 retained the core interface of the iPhone, with Apple not designing a separate core operating system for the iPad. This strategy familiarized users with the iPad while offering something new, quickly attracting both existing iPhone users and newcomers to Apple products.

iOS 4: About Multitasking

Released in June 2010, iOS 4 aimed to meet the needs of more demanding users, adding multitasking, app folders, Wi-Fi tethering, autocorrect, customizable Spotlight search (including web and Wikipedia searches), unified mailboxes, and support for multiple Exchange accounts. These updates kept iOS competitive against Android.

The biggest update was multitasking, though iOS 4 did not support true multitasking where apps could run in the background. Instead, Apple provided developers with several multitasking services that could run in the background:

- "Local Notifications" allowed apps to display reminders at set times;

- "Task Completion" let apps continue uploads or other limited tasks after leaving the app interface;

- "Quick App Switching/State Saving" enabled apps to easily save their current state for seamless return;

- "Music Apps" could play in the background;

- "Navigation Apps" maintained location tracking even when users exited the app;

- "VoIP Phone Apps" allowed users to leave the app interface while making calls.

Developers needed to update their apps to support these background functionalities, and many did. At the time, people were curious about the unique aspects of Apple's approach to multitasking.

Apple's multitasking ensured that idle apps in the background consumed less memory, saving power and maintaining performance comparable to the earlier "single-task" iOS.

Multitasking also introduced new operations for the Home button: double-tapping accessed the background, holding an app icon closed it, and music controls were added to the left of the multitasking bar.

FaceTime. With the release of iOS 4 and the iPhone 4, which added a front-facing camera, FaceTime video calling became available. Like many iOS features, video calling was not Apple's invention. However, Apple's video call interface was simpler and offered a smoother user experience. FaceTime eventually expanded to iPhones, iPads, and Macs. Apple initially promised to make FaceTime open-source, but this commitment has yet to be fulfilled.

Folders. Creating folders on the home screen was another awaited feature. Apple elegantly implemented folder creation by dragging one app icon onto another, automatically forming a folder. This interface innovation was only matched by Android with Ice Cream Sandwich. Starting with iOS 4, users could customize their home screen backgrounds.

Retina Display. Beginning with the iPhone 4, Retina displays were introduced. iOS 4 began supporting Retina-quality graphics, and developers could enhance their apps to Retina standards. This upgrade process was straightforward, with Apple providing free tools in the SDK, eliminating the need for developers to rewrite code.

Productivity Features. While iOS 4 did not fully attract die-hard BlackBerry users, Apple began enhancing email functionality. iOS 4 introduced a unified inbox, support for multiple Exchange accounts, continuous email reading, system-wide spell-checking, and the ability to hide specific calendar events.

Significant Updates to iOS 4: Expanding the Ecosystem

AntennaGate

Shortly after the launch of the iPhone 4, AntennaGate emerged, affecting iOS 4 as well. Regarding complaints of weak signal strength, Apple stated that iOS 4 inaccurately displayed the signal bars.

iOS 4.0.1: Released in July 2010, it addressed the issue of incorrect signal display in iOS 4.

iOS 4.1: This was a major update. The introduction of Game Center helped solidify iOS devices' position in mobile gaming, establishing a social platform for iOS gamers. Around this time, Apple also launched Ping, another social platform, which unfortunately failed to gain traction and was discontinued on September 30, 2012.

4.1 also enhanced the Camera app with HDR (High Dynamic Range) functionality, combining three differently exposed photos into one optimal image.

iOS 4.2.1: Notable because iPads began supporting multitasking and folders. AirPlay, a feature with lasting influence, was introduced at this time, connecting iOS devices to Apple TVs. AirPrint also emerged with great potential but less widespread usage compared to AirPlay.

iOS 4.2.5: Accompanying the Verizon iPhone, this version introduced Wi-Fi hotspot functionality, exciting iPhone users.

iOS 5: About Siri and Other Companions

Similar to iOS 3, iOS 5 was introduced alongside the "S" generation iPhone, the iPhone 4S. Also like iOS 3, iOS 5 brought numerous new features. iOS 5 was pivotal for Apple. If the iPhone was Apple's anvil, then iOS 5 was its hammer, shaping the market for the long term.

Siri. Exclusive to the iPhone 4S, Siri replaced the Voice Control feature, arriving as a "virtual assistant." Siri's remarkable feature was her ability to understand natural human language and respond in kind. Simply put, Siri could converse with users. Launched as a beta, it underwent constant refinement until shedding the beta label with iOS 7.

Notification Center. This was Apple's first attempt at presenting information below the home screen in a pull-down format, including recent messages and the ability to clear them by clicking "x." The Notification Center also integrated weather and stock widgets. However, similar UI elements already existed on Android.

Discussions centered on whether Apple could further improve the Notification Center. Regardless, for iOS, this represented a new area outside the home screen, lock screen, and app icons.

iMessage. Apple ventured into a feature BlackBerries once prided themselves on: free messaging between devices on the same platform, even with multimedia files. With internet connectivity and an Apple ID, any iOS device could send free messages. Unlike standalone apps, iMessage was integrated into the Messages app, automatically sending messages as free iMessages if both parties had it enabled. Like BlackBerry's BBM, iMessage's potential was not immediately realized.

Activation Without PC Connection. One of the significant features of iOS 5 was the elimination of the need to connect to a PC for activation. New users were freed from the hassle of connecting via a data cable. This change also allowed Apple store employees to activate devices directly in-store.

iTunes Wi-Fi Sync. Once again demonstrating Apple's patience, this feature was rolled out only when Apple was confident.

OTA Update. Before iOS 5, iOS devices required downloading the entire ROM in iTunes and installing it on the device. iOS 5's OTA wireless update eliminated the need for cables and computers.

iCloud. Replacing MobileMe, iCloud signaled Apple's determination to excel in cloud services. Today, our iOS devices are inseparable from iCloud, especially for multi-device users who can wirelessly synchronize content. iTunes Wi-Fi Sync means you don't need to frequently connect your iOS device to a computer, but iCloud allows you to completely disconnect from a computer, relying solely on a wireless network to sync apps and music across multiple iOS devices.

iOS 5 also introduced features still relied upon by users, such as using the iPhone volume buttons as a camera shutter, Reminders, and iPad multitasking gestures (four-finger swipes). AirPlay mirroring also made its debut.
