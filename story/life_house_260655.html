How to effectively decorate and utilize corners and surrounding areas

by longersing on 2009-11-17 15:33:02

How to effectively decorate and utilize corners and nearby areas? Corner areas are easily forgotten, often piled with unused items, affecting the overall aesthetics. To make use of every bit of space, we also treat corner decoration as a key element.

Home, as a place for people to settle down, has strong personal characteristics and diverse forms of expression. It is not difficult to understand why people always have great enthusiasm and rich imagination for home design. Just like art that emphasizes change and values "golden corners and silver edges," in home space design and layout, there are also so-called "corner spaces" that seem fragmented, becoming opportunities for people to integrate living spaces and express their lifestyle tastes.

These are places where spatial directions change, and their good or bad utilization directly reflects the level of home design. Specifically, such as the space generated by the intersection of walls, or the space produced when walls meet the floor; these spaces belong to those enclosed by planar components and horizontal components. When spaces with different functions intersect, fragmented spaces will also be generated, such as corridors. When you consider how to maximize the value of space, apart from leveraging the existing layout of your home, you can also transform two-dimensional space into three-dimensional space, breaking through the limitations of predetermined spaces. Due to the special position of corner spaces, they connect different spatial interfaces, allowing you to have a panoramic view of "four sides" in home design.

Scientific recreation at wall intersections

The area where walls intersect is often where the maximum utility of a room can be achieved. The general design approach is to utilize the triangle formed by the plane to create storage space that can accommodate more items. Similar ideas can also be applied in the following examples.

Custom-made modular furniture can effectively solve the problem of how to reasonably utilize corners and small areas. An excellent furniture designer can design a 360° rotating corner cabinet or design sliding doors or drawers into unique furniture that best fits usage habits. This not only demonstrates its rationality but also saves space and enhances the beauty of the home.

Organic combination of narrow spaces in the vertical direction

For spaces with limited horizontal area but larger vertical dimensions, the most common method is layered treatment. Rooms are set up in layers, connected by steep stairs or spiral stairs in the middle. This effectively doubles the usable area and makes the space more dynamic.

In living spaces, bookshelves, desks, computer tables, and storage cabinets are designed vertically, making them easy to store and retrieve, fully utilizing the space. Suspended cabinets and beds hanging from your ceiling can meet all your usage needs and enrich your space.

People can also use wood, bamboo, stainless steel, synthetic fibers, and other materials with distinct personalities to create soft suspended walls that can be rolled up. They can be pulled down when needed and rolled up when not needed; they can be opened when required and tightened when unnecessary. The indoor space can be large or small, divided or combined, changing according to the owner's requirements, making home life flexible and convenient.

Rational extension of scattered areas in the horizontal direction

How to reasonably and efficiently utilize incomplete walls and fragmented spaces becomes the key to room arrangement. Shelves, with their varied forms, scientific combinations, and easy installation, fully compensate for deficiencies in space utilization. The placement of shelves effectively develops edge spaces such as corners, making storage more flexible, access more convenient, and visually more dynamic. Moreover, the use of various materials such as glass and metal provides designers with greater room for creativity.

Functional reshaping of inclined walls, floors, and ceilings

If your residence has many inclined walls, they will inevitably form polygonal spaces with the floor and ceiling. Chinese people believe that the layout of a home must be square, often cutting off extra corners. The remaining square spaces may be more convenient in terms of visual appeal and practical use, but the leftover fragmented spaces are indeed difficult to plan. At this time, you can plan the remaining triangular or polygonal irregular spaces as auxiliary spaces adjacent to the main space. For example, an irregular space next to the living room can be used as a simple sitting room or study, while one next to the bedroom can be converted into a dressing room. If the angle of furniture placement can be slightly adjusted according to the use of space, it not only looks less rigid but also enables more effective use of space.

Some architectural pillars that are only decorative and lack actual supporting functions can have their interiors hollowed out for storage. If covered and concealed with flat wooden cabinet doors on the outside, they can well mask structural deficiencies and provide excellent private storage space.
