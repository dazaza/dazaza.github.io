Jewish Wisdom - A School on the Roof

by liqiao359 on 2010-04-20 08:36:31

Compendium of Jewish Wisdom website: http://www.judahcn.com. Long ago, in the homeland of the Jewish ancestors, the Jews began to establish schools. These schools were not only for children but also for adult men, who would bring their own children to school together. I see one of these men, Hillel, working now on the Book of Jewish Wisdom.

Hey! Hey! Hey! His axe is chopping into the wood.

"Hard work, this chopping wood," Hillel said as he put down his axe and wiped his face with a handkerchief. "But I have to support my family and pay my tuition for the Compendium of Jewish Wisdom." Hillel was already an adult, but he continued to study. He worked hard so that he could attend school. The money he earned, half went to his family, and the other half was given to the gatekeeper to be allowed to attend school.

Huff... Huff... The saw moves through the wood. Hillel started sawing wood instead of chopping it, thinking this might be a little easier.

After a few more swings of the axe and pulls of the saw, the day was over. Evening came. Hillel happily went to the school of Shemaya and Avtalyon to learn about Jewish business principles. He forgot the day's hard work. He forgot everything about his family. He forgot that he had to find another new job the next day. Hillel—his heart, his mind, and his body—was completely focused on listening to the teacher’s words.

But even good things come to an end, so school ended quickly too.

The next morning, Hillel set out to find another job. He went door to door, but everyone had already prepared enough firewood for the winter. Hillel was hungry, cold, and worried. Where would he go to earn money to pay the gatekeeper at school? And what about his family?

Finally, the sun began to set. Hillel returned home and ate whatever small amount of food he could find. Then, as usual, he went to school.

When he arrived at the gate, the gatekeeper asked him as usual: “Where is your coin?”

“I couldn’t find any work today,” Hillel muttered, “I’ll pay you tomorrow.”

“Do you have at least a penny?” the gatekeeper asked.

“No, I don’t even have a penny,” Hillel lowered his head. “Then you can't come in today,” the gatekeeper said sternly.

“Alexander has a powerful army.” Another person said.

Hillel looked around. He would figure out a way, and indeed he did. He wouldn’t miss even a single night of class.

Hillel noticed there was a skylight on the roof with a small hole. Hillel quietly climbed up to the roof, lay down near the skylight, and pressed his ear against the small hole. From this position, he listened to the teachings of the Jewish rabbis.

At this point, it began to snow. The snow piled thicker and thicker. But Hillel didn’t even notice. He was too absorbed in listening to the rabbi's teachings. His one foot froze, then the other foot froze, then one arm froze, then the other arm froze, until Hillel became very weak and fainted.

The next morning, when Shemaya and Avtalyon arrived at the classroom, Shemaya said: “Isn’t it too dark here? The sun is shining brightly outside!”

“Yes,” Avtalyon replied, “I think it's strange too.”

At this moment, they both looked up at the skylight.

“What’s going on? It seems like someone is lying there! Is that possible? Impossible!”

They quickly went to the roof. There, buried deep in the snow, Hillel was unconscious, his whole body numb.

They brought him down, rubbed him with warm olive oil until he woke up. Then Hillel told them everything, explaining why he had gone to the roof. From that day on, Hillel was allowed to attend school without paying tuition.

A few years later, with the same determination that made him climb onto the roof on that snowy night, Hillel became a learned and respected elder among the Jews. In fact, he became the principal of that school!
