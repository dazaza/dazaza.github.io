"China Megatrends" - The Eight Pillars of a New Society in China

by jqrxass on 2009-09-20 13:27:42

John Naisbitt is a name that always resonates. Naisbitt is a remarkable figure with global influence who established his solid position as a futurist through two works, "Megatrends" and "Asia: The Next Megatrend." He has consistently demonstrated an ability to perceive everything and keenly sense the future, forever leading among many scholars of his generation. He was the first to propose the theory of the eight pillars of a new society, foreseeing China's future megatrends, giving not only confidence to China but also to the world.

In "China’s Megatrends," standing at a global height, he incisively proposed the "Eight Pillars of a New Chinese Society" theory — emancipation of mind, the combination of "top-down" and "bottom-up," planning the "forest" while allowing "trees" to grow freely, feeling one's way across the river, the stirring of art and academia, integration into the world, freedom and fairness, from Olympic gold medals to Nobel Prizes. From this, he summarized the megatrends of China's development — China is creating a completely new social, economic, and political system. Its new economic model has already elevated China to a leadership position in the global economy; its political model may prove that so-called "the end of history" of capitalism is merely a stage in the course of human history. Today, the rise of China's economy deeply attracts this futurist, whether it be China's GDP, its status in globalization, or its role in the financial crisis, John Naisbitt is paying attention to China's development and outlining its future. He firmly believes that the "Chinese Model" will influence the entire world with an incredible force.

President Jiang had a private meeting with me in Zhongnanhai, Beijing, specifically in the room where Mao used to receive guests. The reason for President Jiang inviting me was due to a book I wrote in 1982: "Megatrends." It analyzed the transformation of America's economy, politics, society, and culture. This book topped the New York Times bestseller list for two years, mostly at number one, and sold well globally. Surprisingly, "Megatrends" even entered China, and I learned that its sales might have reached 20 million copies there, though all were pirated, a common phenomenon at the time.

Pillar One: Emancipation of Mind

May 1978 in China: Deng Xiaoping, a great man of modest stature, issued a call to the whole country: "We must emancipate our minds," thus taking the crucial first step in leading China towards modernization and a market economy.

Deng understood that a centrally controlled society making top-down decisions was not fertile ground for a market economy. Therefore, to achieve the success of economic reform characterized by decentralized power, liberation of thought was essential first.

Pillar Two: Combination of Top-Down and Bottom-Up

The top-down directives from the Chinese government combined with the bottom-up participation of the Chinese people are forming a new political model, which we call "vertical democracy."

The most important, subtle, and critical pillar supporting the long-term stability of the new Chinese society is the balance between top-down and bottom-up forces. This is the key to China's stability and understanding China's unique political philosophy.

Pillar Three: Planning the Forest, Letting Trees Grow Freely

Thirty years ago, China was like a vast forest where all trees had to be the same, with no allowance for innovation. However, it was proven that such a highly uniform forest could not achieve sustainable development. Deng Xiaoping realized this and called for the liberation of thought, allowing diversity, taking a critical step for China's survival and development.

The best approach is to gradually reforest, see which trees can survive and which cannot. After some time, let the trees in the forest grow freely.
