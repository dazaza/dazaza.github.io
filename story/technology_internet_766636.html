LinkedIn's Hoffman: Education reform should completely upend the degree system

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 21:02:28

Reid Hoffman, Co-founder of LinkedIn

American educational startups are reflecting on the meaning of receiving an education. These companies include MOOC (massive open online course) platform providers Coursera and Udacity, online educational training company Knewton, and the Minerva Project, known as the "online Ivy League." However, Silicon Valley star investor and LinkedIn co-founder Reid Hoffman (Reid Hoffman) hopes that entrepreneurs in these educational institutions will go further and completely disrupt the college degree system.

Hoffman wrote in a post on LinkedIn that an effective way to make higher education more efficient and economical is to rethink the meaning of diplomas. He wrote: "To achieve this, we need to use new technology to give diplomas new meaning. We need to abandon the current lifeless document model of diplomas and transform it into a rich, updatable, and more accessible record of people's skills, specialties, and experiences. Then, we need to store this record and make it part of the content of an Internet qualification certificate platform."

Can LinkedIn profiles replace diplomas?

For most employers, a university degree is the bottom line for job hunting. But Hoffman argues that in an era where market-required technologies are constantly changing, career development and continued learning are experiencing explosive growth, making university degrees somewhat outdated.

Given his special relationship with the world's largest professional social networking site, it is unsurprising that Hoffman hopes LinkedIn profiles can replace diplomas.

According to estimates by market research agencies, currently, about half of university students have never used LinkedIn. But LinkedIn announced a major marketing project last month aimed at attracting university educational institutions and students as young as 14 years old to use LinkedIn. American children today grow up in a Facebook environment. In the future, they may take pride in having online educational and professional profiles rather than just a piece of paper hanging on the wall.

Possibly because Hoffman is a member of the board of directors of the non-profit organization Mozilla, he specifically mentioned Mozilla's Open Badges project launched on September 26, 2011, and believed that the company has opened up new avenues in exploring professional qualifications certification. Open Badges is an open network infrastructure that provides open and standardized APIs, offering any organization an interoperable badge certification platform. These badges can be created and evaluated by educational institutions, permanently displayed when users complete specified subjects to show skills and achievements, and users can also share their accomplishments on social media, job-hunting sites, and other websites. This is highly beneficial for job hunting, establishing collaborations, and building professional qualifications.

Hoffman and Mozilla are not the only individuals and organizations looking to break through in this area. Silicon Valley famous investor Fred Wilson (Fred Wilson) also included learning qualifications certification in the business scope of interest for his holding company Union Square Ventures. Meanwhile, Coursera and Udacity are attracting more students to learn online by providing online open courses outside traditional university education. Many entrepreneurs and investors refer to this as the "unbundling" trend in education.

Disrupting the degree system

Many companies are adopting different ways to present potential trends in digital degrees. Lifelong learners and job seekers may acquire many skills outside traditional classroom education, while gamified professional ability testing websites like Smarterer use crowdsourced tests to examine and certify these acquired skills. Job search website Bright provides employers with a quantitative method to assess and rank job seekers' experiences and education levels.

Degreed is a startup founded by a member of the founding team of Zinch, the largest U.S. college application website. Degreed seems to be the company closest to Hoffman's "technology-driven qualification certification" concept. The company's slogan is "disrupting the degree system" (Jailbreaking the Degree), which aligns with Hoffman's "disturbing the degree" (Disturbing the Degree) philosophy. The services developed by Degreed also feature design metrics similar to those proposed by Hoffman.

Degreed has raised a total of $900,000 in funding and has just emerged independently from the TechStars startup incubator. Degreed provides users with an online, upgradeable, and computer-recognizable storage space to track and store all their learning experiences, from education received at Harvard University to online classes on Coursera, to reading educational books and watching educational videos. Degreed also attempts to score users' learning experiences so that users and employers can more intuitively understand a person's skills. Since its establishment last year, Degreed users have uploaded 1.5 million qualification certification records to the site.

However, Hoffman also noted that the transition to a new model of qualification certification will take a long time. Although paper diplomas may be silent, they also possess many unshakable traditional advantages, such as centuries of history, strong educational institutions, and deeply rooted cultural norms.

But the momentum supporting the "reform of the diploma system" seems to be slowly accumulating. People are eagerly awaiting: what role will LinkedIn play in promoting the reform of educational qualification certification?
