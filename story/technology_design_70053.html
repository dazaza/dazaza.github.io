My Experience of Learning PS

by 483351 on 2008-05-22 00:23:54

It has been more than a year since I first touched PS, and I still can't call myself an expert. So, please regard this as my journey of the heart.

My first contact with PS was in the autumn of 2006. The first time I opened PS and used it, everything was fresh. Because I had long known about the power and beauty of PS, I couldn't help but shake my hands and played with it for half a day. To be honest, I was like a blank sheet of paper back then. My first piece took me an entire afternoon to finish, and I got stuck several times. Fortunately, my dear roommate Xiao Hua patiently taught me various knowledge.

That very meaningful picture is lost now. I vaguely remember that the overall color was black-purple, with some strange brushes added, and the border was incomplete. However, the teacher praised it, saying the overall combination was good. Now when I think back, how much courage must that kind teacher have had to say that sentence!

The second PS work was a poster about caring for disabled people. It was produced three days after the first work. At that time, there were only the default brushes and fonts in PS, and I didn't even know about the fill tool. It took a long time to make this poster. However, I am glad that I didn't make flashy fonts and borders like many beginners do. This one also received special praise from the teacher, who said the design was very international. Once again, I realized the unknown hardships of being a teacher.

Before I started learning PS, I was studying CoreDRAW. So I always treated PS like CD, and I even disliked the pen tool in PS because I thought it wasn't as good as the one in CD. Plus, due to my studies and hobbies (some people know how inhumane my major is), I really didn't have much time to study PS, so I put it aside. For various reasons, I set aside PS no less than five times, each time for more than a month. By the time I finally remembered to touch it again, I had already lost a lot of the previous feelings.

Later, on a forum I frequented, I discovered a section for PS exchanges. After browsing through a few collections of excellent works out of curiosity, I realized I was like a frog at the bottom of a well, never truly understanding PS. During that period, I actively searched online for beautiful works to study (because I don't like reading tutorials, I prefer looking at works I like and figuring out how they are made). It happened to be the early summer of 2007, when the European and American style was very popular. Then I also tried to create this style, and it was around this time that I gradually began to find my own positioning. Most people's pictures can be divided into beautiful or not beautiful, but mine were always described as strange, and it was unclear whether they were good or bad.

One day, I saw a video by a PS expert, and I went crazy drawing with the mouse. I spent countless hours drawing continuously like a madman. The result was that I gained a certain degree of control over the mouse, and I understood the importance of layers better. This is purely a personal hobby, not recommended to try.

I was also interested in turning static images into dynamic ones, making some weird stuff to entertain myself. It also exercised my infinite patience with IR, allowing me to make dozens of layers for one image and repeatedly adjust them.

Nowadays, I have developed a certain level of recognizability and am not as naive as before. I also start creating my own materials and writing Photoshop tutorials. Compared to those days, my skills have naturally improved a lot, and I blush a little. I've even been praised for designing, which makes me blush even more.

Thank you, Xiao Hua, without you, I wouldn't have quickly become familiar with Photoshop. Thank you, an unnamed expert, for letting me know that Photoshop can be used for painting. Thank you, someone Q, for letting me understand that images are meant to be edited, not destroyed. Thank you, a young man from a certain Photoshop forum, for letting me understand what ultimate weirdness is. Thank you, Uncle, for letting me know about that young man...

There are still many people to thank, in short, thank you very much!
