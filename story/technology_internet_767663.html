Xiaomi should bid farewell to the "Chinese mitten crab" practice earlier.

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 11:59:23

Xiaomi was all the rage last week, especially on Tuesday when it gained much attention due to its miraculous achievement of selling out 100,000 Xiaomi Phone 3 units in just 86 seconds. At the same time, many scalpers also made a killing. Relying on software and other advantages, they went on a frenzy buying spree, then resold the phones at a markup of 500 to 1000 yuan to "Mi Fans."

In addition, Xiaomi's marketing strategies have been directly adopted by many companies in the industry, using Xiaomi's own tactics against it. For a while, surrounding and attacking Xiaomi became a buzzword.

One of the successful marketing tactics of Xiaomi is scarcity marketing, but now that Xiaomi has become a company valued at 10 billion US dollars, should it still stick to this tactic? Many people believe that Xiaomi should start learning how to be a real hardware company, diligently producing and steadily distributing products. If Xiaomi's previous scarcity marketing strategy was limited by production capacity and capital, now Xiaomi should have the strength to bid farewell to this approach.

Recently, Lei Jun's ten summarized success experiences shared with his good friend Chen Nian have been widely circulated online. The seventh point is the simplest, consisting of only five words: users are friends. If Xiaomi really adheres to this principle, it should understand: even for the best of friends, you shouldn't keep teasing them; otherwise, they will eventually turn their back on you.

Lei Jun's sixth experience is cost price. The sales price is the cost price. First, do the industry's cost price, then achieve a cost price that can destroy the industry. Don't deceive yourself with gross profit margins; relying on current gross profits to make money won't lead to excellence. However, under the current situation where sales volume is restricted and scalping is rampant, most of Xiaomi's "friends" cannot enjoy the "cost price." The scalpers' markup sales are equivalent to restoring the agency system. It's hard to stop consumers who can't buy phones from generating various speculations, such as suspecting that Taobao's scalper stores might have an official background from Xiaomi. They would ask, if Xiaomi also hates scalpers, why doesn't it adopt Apple's effective method to deal with scalpers (i.e., sending verification codes to each reserved phone number)?

Lei Jun is called Lei Fubao by Mi Fans. He is a good student of Steve Jobs in China, almost achieving both form and spirit. This difference may be reflected in supply. Apple also experiences supply shortages, but in the domestic market, new products are generally quickly spread across the entire United States. Xiaomi cannot achieve this; its phones seem to always be in short supply. Xiaomi not only conducts pre-sales and抢purchases but also distributes something called F-codes in advance. It's similar to the popular Yangcheng Lake crab vouchers in autumn. When a new batch of Xiaomi phones is launched, users can choose to buy Xiaomi M3 phones directly with this F-code without participating in the抢purchase.

Of course, Xiaomi will not admit that they are practicing scarcity marketing. The public reason for "tight supply" is generally production capacity issues. However, from September 5 when Xiaomi 3 was released to October 15 when sales began, there was a full forty days. How many phones can be produced in 40 days? Foxconn produces iPhone 5 at full speed with a daily output of 150,000 units. A medium-sized OEM factory, with one production line conservatively estimated to produce 1,500-2,000 units per day, with 10 lines it would be around 20,000 units per day. According to Xiaomi's own data, last year's news reported that Xiaomi's person in charge revealed that apart from Foxconn's production line daily output, Xiaomi's only Yinghua Da daily production capacity would reach around 20,000 units, with a monthly output between 600,000 to 700,000 units. "Planned to reach 16 SMT production lines and 8 assembly lines by the end of the year, with a monthly production capacity exceeding 1 million units," referring to last year's end.

Regardless of whether Xiaomi is actually practicing scarcity marketing or not, the benefits of scarcity marketing are evident. Selling phones like futures not only solves funding problems but also often offsets low costs. For example, a 2000 yuan phone that was barely profitable in March may have a cost of less than 2000 yuan by June when a large number of consumers start to "quench their thirst," because during this period, the manufacturer's hardware procurement costs have decreased.

But I think the above factors are not the main reasons why Xiaomi is enthusiastic about scarcity marketing. Looking back at Xiaomi's success, two factors cannot be ignored: its low price and its successful use of word-of-mouth marketing in the social media era. However, in fact, these two points often cannot coexist. If a consumer buys a phone at a low price, it is difficult for them to have the motivation to spread positive word-of-mouth to their friends. "Scarcity marketing" continuously creates this motivation, making the act of being able to buy a Xiaomi phone itself worth boasting about. From this perspective, "shortage" is Xiaomi's style. Without shortages, Xiaomi could easily become just another cheap product for the common folk.

Being able to sustainably take scarcity marketing to the extreme is what makes Xiaomi worthy of praise and admiration. But this is also like walking on a tightrope. Xiaomi has never encountered a noteworthy opponent. Imagine, if after Xiaomi painstakingly creates scarcity, suddenly a manufacturer starts flooding the market with similar high-performance-to-price ratio products, what would be the consequences? Zhou Hongyi saw through this point early on, so he could mainly rely on verbal virtual threats to spark a counterattack.

Xiaomi is successful, but even in the social media era, a great hardware company ultimately still relies primarily on technology, design, and production. Marketing is indeed important, but at least historically, no hardware manufacturer has become a giant solely through marketing. Of course, in the fast-moving consumer goods sector, such marketing-driven giants abound. But the question is, can Xiaomi turn phones into fast-moving consumer goods?
