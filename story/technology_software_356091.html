Real estate regulation policies are working, speculation is decreasing_Shanghai Y8

by ww0938788 on 2010-05-26 20:57:22

China's economy shows signs of overheating again. In the first quarter of 2010, China’s GDP grew by 11.9%, stimulating inflation and accelerating the rise in real estate prices. In April, China’s inflation rate reached 2.8%. The average real estate price in Shanghai among 70 large and medium-sized cities rose by 12.8% year-on-year, surpassing the 11.7% increase in March. New credit in April amounted to 774 billion yuan, exceeding the 510 billion yuan in March. Under the combined effects of economic overheating, rising inflation rates, low policy interest rates, and rapid expansion of credit scale, China’s real estate market clearly showed signs of a bubble in the fourth quarter of 2009, with the bubble further intensifying in the first quarter of this year. The Thai Farmers Bank Research Center expects that the Chinese government will introduce further measures to control economic overheating and the real estate bubble. Since the beginning of the year, the Chinese government has taken measures from multiple aspects to curb the real estate bubble, including setting minimum down payment ratios and interest rates for housing loans, increasing bank reserve requirements, requiring banks to strictly control credit, implementing tax policies for regulation, and strengthening supervision and penalties on speculative profit-seeking behavior by real estate developers. Although these increasingly stringent regulatory measures began to take effect by the end of April, as evidenced by the decline in housing prices in major Chinese cities during the second half of April, overall real estate prices are still rising. The Thai Farmers Bank Research Center believes that stricter regulatory policies that the Chinese government will implement in the future will affect real estate prices, especially high-end residential property prices in urban areas, with investors seeking profits through speculation being most directly affected. However, due to the enormous actual demand for real estate in China, the mid-to-low-end real estate market will continue to grow. It can be said that the regulatory measures taken by the Chinese government are an important mechanism to curb speculative profit-seeking behavior and avoid serious bubbles in the Chinese economy in the future. Meanwhile, expectations of continued growth in the Chinese economy will weaken the impact of strict regulatory policies on the real estate industry. Additionally, stable growth in the Chinese economy helps drive sustained growth in domestic consumption and investment, which is also beneficial for sustained growth in Sino-Thai trade. Statistics show that Thailand's total exports to China in the first quarter of 2010 reached $5.05 billion, a significant increase of 69.9% compared to last year. China has replaced the United States as Thailand's largest export market. Major Thai products exported to China include computer equipment and components, rubber and its products, etc. Meanwhile, China is Thailand's second-largest source of imports. In the first quarter, Thailand's total imports from China reached $5.244 billion, growing by 55.35% year-on-year. Major products imported by Thailand from China include computer equipment and components, electrical products and components, and machinery and equipment and components, etc.
