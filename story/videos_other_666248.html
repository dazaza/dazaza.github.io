The seemingly harmless ice fishing harbors hidden dangers [with pictures]

Fishing, elegantly called angling, can be done all year round. Spring fishing on the shoals, summer fishing in deep pools, autumn fishing in the shade, winter fishing in the sun—this saying is a proverb that selects the best season for angling based on the habits of fish. Angling in spring, summer, and autumn isn't uncommon, but waiting impatiently for the fish to ascend under the ice after Start of Spring and ice fishing are rare experiences. This is my first time trying it, and I intend to invite friends to come along and have some fun.

Start of Spring has already passed "the first period," yet the north remains freezing cold. Fish under the ice prefer warmth and sunny places. Around two or three o'clock in the afternoon when the sun is at its best, which is known as winter fishing in the sun, is the perfect time for ice fishing. Take out the ice-breaking tools and start chiseling away with "cha cha" sounds to create an ice hole. Clear out the floating broken ice blocks and begin fishing. Ice fishing differs from river or sea fishing; once a fish is hooked, pull the fishing rod with all your might. For larger fish, you may need to maneuver the rod to tire them out before dragging them onto the shore and into the basket. When ice fishing, if you're lucky, a slight movement or dip of the fishing rod means a fish has bitten the hook, and with a gentle pull, the fish is dragged onto the icy surface. Watching carp and crucian carp struggling and flapping on the ice, rolling on the slippery surface, the sense of accomplishment is indescribable—it's exhilarating! Then place the fish in an ice hollow prepared beforehand to keep them fresh and lively.

Ice fishing is indeed full of fun, no wonder literati and poets have composed countless timeless poems about the charm of winter angling. Tang Dynasty poet Sikong Shu's "Rural Matters by the River": "After fishing, return without tying the boat, the moon sets over the riverside village, it’s time to sleep. Even if the wind blows the boat away overnight, it will only be among the reeds in shallow water." It vividly portrays the leisurely lifestyle of winter anglers and the serene beauty of the riverside village. Liu Zongyuan's "River Snow" has a profound and grand atmosphere, being both cold and elegant, with the solitary fishing in the cold river becoming a timeless masterpiece. As I stand amidst the scene of ice fishing, reciting "No birds fly over a thousand mountains, no human traces on ten thousand paths. An old man in a straw cloak, alone he fishes in the cold river snow," Liu Zongyuan was truly talented, his poem depicts a marvelous picture of solitary fishing in the cold river.

Everything has its pros and cons. While ice fishing brings joy and allows one to enjoy the scenery and catch fish, it also brings moments of fear. The warm sunlight on the ice contrasts with the cold water below, where fish, drawn to the warmth, unknowingly face danger as they rise to the surface, possibly becoming the angler's catch and meal. The fish beneath the ice entice anglers like stars lighting lamps, but if the ice hole isn't dug accurately and the water level isn't measured, bad luck may lead to catching nothing but ice chips. A small dog accompanying its owner to stroll on the ice seems to understand human emotions, dozing off on the crystal-clear ice while waiting patiently.

The biggest downside of ice fishing is the hidden danger it harbors. If the ice isn't solid, it can easily become dangerous. Standing on the ice, hearing the 'creeking' sound underfoot as you move should prompt you to stop immediately. Falling into the icy water could result in severe frostbite even if not fatal. Watching others fish, envying their catches, this pastime is essentially a thrilling game on the ice.
