For 15 years, 26 students have taken care of and supported the parents of a classmate who passed away from illness

by xue94fwsh on 2012-03-09 14:31:36

This year marks the 15th anniversary of the passing of Li Baoyuan, a student from Class 93(01) of Hebei Agricultural University's Pomology Department. For 15 years, Li Baoyuan’s 26 classmates have taken on the responsibility that was once his, sending 15 money orders and 56 letters to quietly support and care for Li Baoyuan's parents. An unwritten agreement has fostered a heartfelt connection that has lasted for 15 years.

In June 1997, during the graduation party of Class 93(01), Niu Shuqi wrote down an address in front of all his classmates: "Li Weihè, Wulongji Village, Xiabancheng Town, Chengde County, Chengde City." Li Weihè was the father of Li Baoyuan, who had passed away due to illness in October 1996, leaving behind his grieving parents. His older brother had also died of illness at the age of 21. In the blink of an eye, the family of four was reduced to two elderly people.

"Baoyuan's parents need our help," Niu Shuqi said. "Let's send some money to the elders every Spring Festival. If you're not financially comfortable, just write them a letter."

At that moment, without any grand promises or declarations, all the classmates silently copied the address onto their papers, hands, and into their hearts.

Actually, as early as the beginning of 1996, when Li Baoyuan returned to Chengde on medical leave, his 26 classmates started sending money and writing letters to this suffering family. Unbeknownst to them, this act of kindness would continue for 15 years and counting.

Today, these former classmates are at an age where they have both aging parents and young children. Some have fared well, while others haven't, but sending money to "Baoyuan's parents" has almost become a must-do task for each member of Class 93(01) before the Spring Festival. Every year before the festival, money orders from places like Shijiazhuang, Baoding, Handan, and even Yuanping, Shanxi, would arrive in Chengde for "Baoyuan's parents." Over the past 15 years, as the classmates have spread across the country, so too has this genuine emotion.

When they first graduated, the students earned little, so they sent mostly 50 yuan or 100 yuan to Li Weihè. Gradually, the amounts increased to 200 yuan, 300 yuan, and even 500 yuan. Those with more financial means sent 800 yuan or 1000 yuan. A few years ago, classmate Yang Zhengwu was tight on cash due to a housing loan, so he didn't send money for a couple of years. Now that the loan is paid off, he has resumed sending money. Initially, his wife didn't understand, but after learning about the situation, she now reminds him to send the money every year.

"There's no reminder or urging among us, and even when we meet, no one asks about it," Du Yanmin, the class president of 93(01), told China Youth Daily. Sending money is entirely voluntary.

In 2007, during their 10th reunion, the classmates reminisced about their "good brother" Li Baoyuan. The final activity of the reunion was to send money to "Baoyuan's parents." They collectively sent over 1000 yuan to Li Weihè far away. This was the only time they acted as a group.

"In this world, true feelings are eternal"

In the early part of 1996, Li Baoyuan returned home from school to recuperate. Despite being in pain, reading letters from his classmates was what made him happiest. Tragically, he passed away in October that same year. Knowing the deep bond between her son and his classmates, Li Weihè informed them of this devastating news through a letter. In the letter, the elder told the classmates about his son lying ill in bed and his wife also being severely ill. Yet, despite the distance, the sincere affection and assistance between Baoyuan and his classmates brought warmth to their broken hearts.

In the closing of the letter, the elder specifically asked the children: "Please keep the correspondence going; it is the greatest spiritual comfort to me!"

Soon after, Li Weihè received a letter from all the classmates of Class 93(01). This letter warmed the hearts of two broken individuals. From then on, the communication between the elder and the 26 classmates never ceased.

In a letter written by Song Caixia in 1997, she addressed the two elders as "Dad and Mom."

"Losing a child in old age is one of life's greatest misfortunes, naturally causing immense inner pain. Feel free to share your thoughts with me. I am your daughter, and so are Gaofeng and Yafeng..." Just before leaving school, Song Caixia told the two elders, "Our student life is coming to an end, and the future is uncertain. But wherever we go, we will always remember that in Chengde, we have two dear relatives."

Zhang Shumei said that Li Weihè replied to every letter from the classmates. In the letters, the elder never mentioned the debt incurred from treating his son, instead expressing concern and instructions for the children, "like a father to his son."

In a reply to Li Weihè in 1998, Zhao Gaofeng first told the elder, "I already took out the rice on the afternoon of the 17th." The act of the elder sending rice moved Zhao Gaofeng deeply. "Shipping items is quite troublesome. I imagine you must have made several trips to Xiabancheng and written several registered letters." He advised the two elders, "You really don't need to do this. As long as you two live well, we, as your nieces and nephews, will feel reassured and happy."

Every time the classmates wrote to the elder, it plunged him into painful memories of his son. Thus, they reluctantly reduced the frequency of their letters.

In January 1999, Shi Cuiping wrote in a letter to Li Weihè, "Due to various reasons, we cannot frequently ask about your well-being, but if there's anything that requires our effort, a letter or a phone call, and everyone will come running."

Although the letters became fewer, during the Spring Festival when sending money, the classmates always included warm wishes in the limited space of the remittance form: "Wishing you good health," "Wishing you a happy Spring Festival," "Happy New Year"... Even today, with advanced mobile communication, such correspondence continued until 2011.

Years after graduation, classmate Yang Zhengwu lamented in a letter to the elder, "Time flies, and it has been many years since graduation. Everything changes, but there is one thing that truly remains unchanged: the eternal truth of human emotions."

"Our promise has no deadline"

Through frugality and years of working odd jobs, Li Weihè finally paid off the 70,000+ yuan debt incurred from treating his son earlier this year. At 66 years old, his biggest wish was to see these adorable children again, 15 years after his son's passing. Finally, with the help of kind-hearted people, on March 25th, the elder met Niu Shuqi, Du Yanmin, Li Jinggang, Shi Cuiping, Qi Yefeng, and others once more.

At the lunch gathering after their long separation, the elder personally read a letter he had written to all the classmates of Class 93(01): "My dear children, you all have your own parents, spouses, and children, yet you still remember two elders who have no blood relation to you. What Baoyuan couldn't do, you have done for him. In our hearts, you are our daughters and sons..." At this point, the elder softly called out his son's name, "Baoyuan, did you hear that?"

When media outlets expressed interest in covering this story, the entire class of 93(01) initially opposed it. "We are ordinary people, with ordinary love, living in our ordinary world. We are so ordinary that we don't want to face the spotlight of the media," Niu Shuqi told reporters. More importantly, they didn't want the elder to relive painful memories during interviews.

However, when someone suggested that media attention might ensure a more secure life for the two elders, they all agreed willingly, repeatedly requesting every journalist to "not exaggerate, report honestly."

As they bid farewell to the elder, despite Li Weihè's repeated protests, the classmates, like all filial children, forcibly placed 2000 yuan collected among themselves into Li Weihè's pocket.

"Certainly, we will visit the elders in Chengde this summer," they promised, nearing middle age with responsibilities to both their elders and juniors.

"Our promise has no deadline. As long as the elders are alive, we will take care of them!" Du Yanmin voiced everyone's sentiment.

Reported by Baoding on April 3rd (Editor: SN047)
