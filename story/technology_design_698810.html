College student takes 25 rideshares to go home, says journey proves there are a lot of kind people

by yanp559zxue on 2012-03-09 11:20:28

Hu Beilei completed a stimulating and warm "Spring Festival Travel Rush" journey (Image source: China Newsweekly) Hu Beilei's travel route diagram (Image source: China Newsweekly) Hu Beilei on the trip Hu Beilei took a photo with the driver who gave him a ride.

25 hitchhiked cars, passing through 7 provinces, 3700 kilometers, from Nanjing to Urumqi. From December 25, 2010, to January 6 this year, college senior Hu Beilei spent 13 days completing his journey home using the free hitchhiking method.

With 25 yuan in his socks as emergency money [High-definition images: College student hitchhikes 3700 kilometers home], "How far can my passion take me?" Hu Beilei once asked himself on Weibo. This 24-year-old student at Nanjing Normal University studying electrical engineering and automation gave himself a perfect answer through his actions.

"This time of hitching home was not a spur-of-the-moment adventure, nor was it because I couldn't buy a Spring Festival ticket," said Hu Beilei. He explained that this was a trip he had planned for a long time. His plan originated from the documentary "Hitchhiking to Berlin." The story of two Chinese-American young men who hitchhiked 88 times, traveling over 16,000 kilometers from Beijing to Berlin, inspired him to try this way of traveling before graduation.

A sleeping bag, clothes, a camera, biscuits, a map, postcards, and 60 yuan were all the belongings in Hu Beilei's backpack. Additionally, he kept 100 yuan in his socks. "If I encounter robbery, I will hand over everything I have. The money in my socks will be used as emergency funds," said Hu Beilei.

Proof of kindness during the journey

At 10 am on December 25, 2010, Hu Beilei set off from Nanjing Normal University and took a bus to National Highway 312. After nearly four hours of walking, he hitched a ride on a truck by the roadside that was under repair.

"I didn't have a very detailed plan before leaving; I just knew that following National Highway 312 would lead me home," said Hu Beilei. Initially, he thought hitchhiking wouldn't be difficult—just wave your hand, and you'd definitely get a ride. However, along the way, he faced countless rejections.

The longest time he spent was at a toll station in Datong County, Qinghai Province, where he waved his hand for three whole hours. His persistence moved the toll station staff, who all helped him stop cars. In the world's windiest place, Guazhou, he was blown around on the Gobi Desert. Eventually, a truck driver slammed on the brakes for over 100 meters and pulled him into the cab.

"There were two people in the truck; the driver was pulling the door hard, and the other master was helping me get in," said Hu Beilei. These two drivers deeply touched him. Without asking any questions, they took him on the road. Upon reaching Guazhou, they not only treated him to a meal but also tried to give him 100 yuan.

"Parents' and school education always told us that society is complex and not to talk easily to strangers. Actually, the world isn't that bad. Most people are kind and willing to help others," said Hu Beilei. This was his greatest feeling during the journey.

Never taking night rides or staying in hotels

On the evening of December 31, 2010, after arriving in Lanzhou, Hu Beilei bought sunflower seeds and beer and celebrated the New Year's arrival in a small hotel.

"That night was especially lonely, and I missed home very much, longing for its comfort and warmth. Ultimately, I got drunk and fell asleep," said Hu Beilei. The greatest pain along the way was loneliness, but he never thought about giving up. "At most, when I really couldn't hitch a ride, I considered buying a long-distance bus ticket to continue hitchhiking to the next station."

During the journey, Hu Beilei feared the coming of night, which meant he had to find a place to stay overnight. "I rarely sleep in hotels because I want to save expenses," he said. Many nights, he spent in internet cafes or the driver rest areas of highway service zones. "In the driver service area, I would put together a few chairs, cover myself with clothes, and spend the night."

Hu Beilei never hitched rides at night. "Hitchhiking at night is unsafe, and many drivers increase their vigilance, making it harder to get a ride," he said.

At 8 pm on January 6, Hu Beilei finished his last ride and was dropped off one kilometer away from home.

"Twenty-five cars and countless kind-hearted people, it's you who made me believe I can fly higher and farther in my own sky. If you truly want to do something, the whole world will help you. Don't let your ideas remain just ideas," Hu Beilei summarized in his blog. By reporter Shi Minglei.

"Hitchhiking has three things that work well"

■ Interview

Beijing News: What was the biggest gain from hitchhiking?

Hu Beilei: This journey made me realize the importance of trust: trusting yourself, daring to act, and not giving up; mutual trust between people, hitchhiking itself is a process of building trust between strangers, sincerity and kindness are the best ways to build trust. Some drivers who picked me up expressed that they would be more willing to help future hitchhikers.

Beijing News: What are your hitchhiking techniques?

Hu Beilei: Three things work well: smiles, student ID, sincere communication and interaction. When hitchhiking, having thick skin; strong psychological resilience, not fearing rejection; being able to chat with the driver; I also carry many postcards as gifts for the drivers.

Beijing News: Did your family support your 'adventure' behavior?

Hu Beilei: My girlfriend once scolded me, saying, "Do you think you're an explorer?" My parents were also upset when they learned I hitchhiked home, thinking I was too bold. My younger brother gave me an evaluation: "Brother, you're getting more reckless."

Of course, many classmates supported my actions, saying I helped them fulfill this dream. In fact, many people hope to have such rich and interesting life experiences.

This hitchhiking home wasn't the end of my journey. I will still choose this way of traveling. Of course, it's better not to tell my parents beforehand, even if they support me, they will still worry a lot.
