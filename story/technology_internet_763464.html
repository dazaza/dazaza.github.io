Lenovo Responds to Ban by Five Countries' Intelligence Chiefs: Claims Ignorance of the Matter

by anonymous on 2013-08-02 10:12:20

If in the next James Bond movie, 007 opens a Lenovo computer, then he is probably breaking the rules. Several British media outlets on the 29th cited an internal ban that was just disclosed: for years, intelligence and defense agencies from the U.S., UK, Australia, New Zealand, and Canada have been confidentially prohibiting the use of Lenovo computers due to "numerous tests proving that Lenovo computers have 'backdoor' hardware vulnerabilities that can be easily remotely controlled by intruders."

"We are not aware of this ban, nor have we received any news about it. We cannot respond to this. Lenovo has always maintained a good and stable relationship with our government department clients," Lenovo Group issued a statement on the 30th regarding the hype by multiple foreign media over the "Five Eyes Intelligence Alliance banning Lenovo computers."

In a report titled "Intelligence Agencies Ban Lenovo PCs Over Security Concerns," The Australian Financial Review stated that multiple intelligence and defense sources from the UK and Australia revealed that the Five Eyes Intelligence Alliance roughly issued a written ban in the mid-2000s, prohibiting intelligence and defense agencies in their respective countries at the "confidential" and "top secret" levels from using Lenovo computers due to concerns about malicious hardware vulnerabilities that could be exploited for remote control.

"If the reports are true, the ban on Lenovo computers should have taken effect around 2005, which coincides with Lenovo's acquisition of IBM's PC business," said The Daily Telegraph of the UK. The paper noted that the ban generally applies to top-tier intelligence departments, while ordinary non-classified government departments can still purchase Lenovo computers.

"Forget Huawei, there's a new monster in town, and its name is Lenovo," technology website Quartz wrote under this headline on the 29th. It said that Western governments have long suspected Chinese computer product manufacturers of being "spy factories," and the company most often at the center of these suspicions has been Huawei. However, now people have discovered that the common enemy of Western intelligence has all along been Lenovo. The article also mentioned that what's more important is that previous suspicions about Huawei couldn't be substantiated, whereas this time there is evidence due to British test conclusions regarding Lenovo.

If such evidence truly exists, why isn't it being made public? Information Security Magazine of the UK said that the Australian tech site "Slashdot" was already dissatisfied with The Australian Financial Review's revelations. The site stated that if Lenovo computers were indeed risky from the moment of production and there is solid evidence, then the evidence should be immediately disclosed, which would benefit global commerce and governments. If such evidence does not exist, the media coverage constitutes defamation against another Chinese enterprise. This would raise doubts as to whether this is a security incident or a political-economic event. The site also said that comments made last week by George Plummer, Vice President of Huawei North America, apply equally well to Lenovo: "Some people say they have evidence of a certain threat? Fine, show us the evidence, otherwise shut up."
