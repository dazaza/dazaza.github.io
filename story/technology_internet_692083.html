Research on the Issue of Intellectual Property Rights Protection in International Trade

by mncvty on 2012-03-05 22:00:37

Abstract: With China's accession to the WTO, intellectual property trade has become an important part of international trade. However, the development of trade liberalization has posed new challenges to intellectual property protection, leading to serious conflicts between the parallel imports and intellectual property protection in the context of international trade liberalization. To promote free trade, China should generally support parallel imports but impose certain restrictions on the parallel importation of patents and copyrights at a micro level, so as to achieve harmonious development between the two.

Keywords: International Trade Liberalization, Parallel Imports, Intellectual Property Protection, Legal Conflicts, Legal Harmony

I. Introduction

According to reports in the Science and Technology Daily, one of the top ten trends in contemporary international trade is that the development of intellectual property trade has become an undeniable reality in international trade. Meanwhile, the Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (hereinafter referred to as the TRIPS Agreement) is an important component of the WTO rule system. Its formation aims to fully and effectively protect intellectual property rights in international trade through the establishment and implementation of rules, preventing trade obstacles and distortions caused by the disregard or infringement of intellectual property rights. The impact of the TRIPS Agreement on China's foreign trade, as a set of rules for standardizing and unifying the intellectual property trade behavior of WTO members, was strongly promoted by developed countries. From a long-term development perspective, it will have both positive and negative impacts on developing countries, mainly manifested as follows:

1. Positive impacts include:

(1) Adapting to the trends and requirements of global economic development.

(2) Facilitating the introduction of international investments related to trade.

(3) Promoting the production and export of products with intellectual property rights in our country.

(4) Enhancing intellectual property protection awareness in international trade.

(5) Establishing a channel for resolving intellectual property disputes.

2. Negative impacts mainly include:

(1) Increasing imbalance in intellectual property trade.

(2) Intensifying competition in the intellectual property market.

(3) Affecting the growth of export production.

(4) Increasing trade disputes related to intellectual property protection.

[1] It can be seen that with China's accession to the WTO, although it accelerates the process of international trade liberalization, it also brings about the challenge of protecting intellectual property rights for knowledge products. Therefore, while promoting economic development and advancing international economic and trade liberalization, special attention must be paid to the unique protection of knowledge products in different countries. Consequently, a dilemma arises in international trade regarding intellectual property protection, which we must face and seek solutions for.

II. International Trade Liberalization: Need for Parallel Imports

Parallel imports (referred to as [gray markets] in the U.S.) refer to situations in international trade where the intellectual property rights of a certain right holder are protected in more than two countries. Without the permission of the intellectual property right holder or the exclusive licensee, a third party purchases intellectual property products from a foreign intellectual property right holder and imports them into their own country for sale. [2] For example, if B, the intellectual property right holder in Country Y, grants A the intellectual property rights for a product in Country X, and C holds the same rights in Country Z, then if D in Country X imports the product from Country Z without A’s permission, this constitutes a parallel import. From this, its characteristics can be derived as follows:

(1) Products imported from one country to another are obtained through legal means such as purchase.

(2) It involves two domains; whether the law in each region allows parallel imports is determined by domestic law.

(3) There are two layers of legal relationships: one is the licensing relationship established between the intellectual property right holder and the relevant party through an authorization license contract or the legal protection of intellectual property in two regions, and the other is the import-export behavior of the third party who acquires intellectual property products in good faith in one country and sells them in another country. This leads to the legality issue of parallel imports, i.e., whether parallel imports infringe upon the rights of intellectual property holders, which has been a long-standing controversial issue in the legal community. Existing international conventions on intellectual property protection and those related to intellectual property have not provided a definitive conclusion either affirmatively or negatively. Due to the influence of domestic legal traditions and economic policies, there is no uniform stance among countries' legislation and judicial practices on whether parallel imports constitute infringement, thus placing parallel imports in the "gray area" of rights protection. The main reason for parallel imports lies in the retail price of a particular intellectual property product being higher in the importing country than its wholesale price in a foreign country. Driven by profit, importers buy goods produced and sold abroad and then sell them in the domestic market at prices lower than the normal domestic market prices, thereby creating a situation where importers compete with intellectual property holders or relevant licensees for the same product in the market.
