Postoperative care methods for surrogate mothers in Shanghai after cesarean section

A cesarean section involves making an incision through the abdominal and uterine walls under anesthesia to remove the fetus and its associated structures, followed by suturing each layer of tissue in the uterus and abdomen. Currently, the cesarean section rate in China has reached around 40%, and it is an effective method for relieving critical conditions in both pregnant women and fetuses.

The most widely used technique in clinical practice today is the transverse incision in the lower segment of the uterus, as this area has a thinner muscular layer, leading to less bleeding, and after surgery, it has the lowest probability of uterine rupture during subsequent pregnancies. Additionally, since the incision is located below the "bikini line," mothers who have had a cesarean section can still wear bikinis or fashionable clothing, satisfying their desire for beauty.

A cesarean section is an open-abdomen surgery performed to solve difficult deliveries, which differs from other types of open surgeries due to some special circumstances that require attention in nursing care.

After a cesarean section, as the effects of the anesthetic gradually wear off, the sensation of pain in the lower abdomen of the mother begins to return, and usually, several hours after the surgery, the wound will start to cause severe pain. To allow the mother to rest well, doctors may administer some pain-relieving medication on the day of the surgery or shortly thereafter. Afterward, the use of painkillers should be avoided as it can affect physical health, particularly the recovery of intestinal peristalsis function. Therefore, the mother needs to endure some degree of pain.

Due to the stimulation of the surgery on the intestines and the influence of the anesthetic drugs, all mothers who undergo cesarean sections experience varying degrees of slowed intestinal peristalsis. The faster the intestinal function recovers, the sooner the gas inside the intestines will be expelled, alleviating abdominal distension.

Mothers who have undergone cesarean sections cannot get up and move around like those who have had normal vaginal deliveries within 24 hours after childbirth, so lochia is not easily expelled. If the mother lies in a semi-reclining position and turns over frequently, it will promote the discharge of lochia, preventing the accumulation of lochia in the uterine cavity, which could lead to infection and affect the recovery of uterine function.

Before a cesarean section, a urinary catheter is placed in the mother, typically removed 24 to 48 hours after surgery once the bladder muscles have recovered their contraction and urination functions. After the catheter is removed, the mother should make an effort to urinate naturally as soon as she feels the urge to do so, as retaining the catheter for too long can easily lead to bacterial infections in the urinary tract.
