When is the best time to introduce complementary foods to babies?

Research shows that introducing complementary foods is an important nutritional stage in a baby's growth and development. It can meet various nutritional needs during the rapid growth and development phase, and also promote the early potential development of the baby. However, when to introduce complementary foods should be determined based on the actual developmental status of the baby (individual differences), and should not be blindly followed.

The Importance of Complementary Foods

- Provides the nutrients needed for rapid physical growth in babies, such as iron, calcium, phosphorus, etc.

- At 4-6 months of age, it is the sensitive period for the baby's taste development, and also a critical period for establishing good eating behavior.

- Recognizing the color, aroma, taste, and shape of food can promote the cognitive development of the baby.

- Exercising the baby's chewing ability, promoting the eruption of teeth, the development of head and facial bones and muscles, and the development of pronunciation.

The Best Time to Add Complementary Foods: 4-6 Months

- Generally speaking, 4-6 months is the best time to add complementary foods to babies. Regardless of the type of breastfeeding method, all babies should start adding semi-solid foods after reaching 4-6 months of age, and this should not be later than 8 months.

- When the baby's daily milk intake exceeds 1000 milliliters or each feeding exceeds 200 milliliters but still shows signs of hunger, appropriate complementary foods can be added.

- When the baby shows interest in adult meals and turns their head toward food when needed, suitable complementary foods can be added appropriately.

- Complementary foods can be added appropriately when the baby is physically healthy and has normal digestive function.

- If the baby's weight does not reach the standard even after the mother provides sufficient milk, appropriate complementary foods can be added. Premature babies, obese babies, and sick babies should carefully add complementary foods or start adding under the advice of a health care doctor.

- During hot summer days, when the baby is sick, or within 1-2 days after receiving vaccinations, it is best not to add complementary foods.

Content and Sequence of Adding Complementary Foods

4-5 months: Rice powder, egg yolk, vegetable puree, fruit puree.

6-8 months: Egg yolk, thick porridge, soft noodles, fish puree, liver puree, meat puree, tofu, chopped vegetables, biscuits, and steamed buns.

9-12 months: Eggs, soft rice, dumplings, wontons, bean products, minced meat, chopped vegetables.

Expert Advice: Baby's Refusal to Eat is a Normal Phenomenon

Not every baby can smoothly accept complementary foods at the recommended time. At the beginning of introduction, some babies may show refusal, crying, or reluctantly accepting the food. This is determined by individual differences in babies and is very normal. Parents should have patience and follow the method of addition: from less to more, from thin to thick, from one kind to multiple kinds, from fine to coarse, repeating several to dozens of times, which will surely make the baby accept complementary foods. The time to start adding complementary foods should be chosen in the morning. At this time, the baby's mood and mental state are good, making it easier to accept foods other than milk.

The complementary foods given to the baby should be fresh, tender, hygienic, and tasty. Do not focus solely on nutrition while ignoring the taste, as this can not only affect the baby's taste development but may also cause the baby to develop an aversion to complementary foods, affecting nutrient intake.

Although complementary foods can be added, the baby's digestive organs are still quite delicate, so haste is not advisable. They should be gradually added according to the baby’s digestive function. Arbitrary addition can lead to indigestion or obesity in babies.
