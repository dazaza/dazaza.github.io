Cook can't replace Jobs, but Apple is still Apple

by anonymous on 2013-07-31 10:36:24

During the three years since Jobs left Apple, whenever there was a financial report release, product launch, or stock price fluctuation, many people would invariably raise the argument that "Apple has lost its innovative edge without Jobs." Recently, with the release of Apple's latest earnings report, this claim has become even more rampant.

In reality, although Tim Cook, as an operator, finds it difficult to infuse his personal style into Apple’s product lines and lacks the heroic flair in company management that Jobs had as a dreamer, this does not affect Apple’s long-standing operational mechanism. Apple has never stopped innovating.

Let’s first recall some of the major achievements Steve Jobs made after returning to Apple in 1997 — upon his return, Jobs prioritized the development of the "next-generation" operating system, Mac OS X, and publicly unveiled the preview version of Mac OS X in 1999; In 2000, he formed the Marklar team, which secretly completed the migration of Mac OS X from PowerPC to Intel X86 over five years; In 2001, he launched Mac OS X Server; In 2006, he introduced Mac OS X 10.5 with numerous new features and the Mac Pro line; From 2008 to 2011, he released various versions of the iPhone, iPad, MacBook, and iCloud…

Following that, Apple’s main innovations in products included the taller-screened iPhone 5, The New iPad with Retina display, the 7.9-inch iPad mini, iOS 7 operating system, and the new generation of MacBook running on OS X 10.9.

By comparing these achievements, you will find that Jobs’ truly unsurpassable accomplishments can be simply summarized as two things — he led the development of the Mac OS X and iOS operating systems and built around them ecosystems with complete software and hardware industry chains.

The issue lies in the fact that such transformations are not the norm for product releases by a software-hardware company. These two revolutions were accompanied by significant changes in hardware computing capabilities and the aging of existing operating systems. The next such transformation is likely to come from the evolution of computer processors towards ARM, but currently, ARM's performance cannot meet the demands, and personal computers have a wide audience with abundant program varieties and numbers. If another instruction set is adopted, it will require a lengthy modification and debugging cycle, along with rewrites and recompilations of applications. Therefore, external factors have, to some extent, deprived Apple, post-Jobs, of the opportunity to prove itself in the short term.

On the product and corporate management level, I believe that under Cook's leadership today, Apple is no less impressive than during the Jobs era — he launched the 7-inch tablet that Jobs once criticized as having no market and proved that it could actually be highly popular; He dismissed Scott Forstall who didn't get along well with executives and entrusted Jony Ive with the crucial task of designing the new iOS 7 (facts have gradually proven that Ive's work has been highly effective); He utilized new technology to extend the battery life of the MacBook Air to 12 hours; He completely redesigned the Mac Pro... Under circumstances where the operating system is already very mature and hardware is limited, where do you see this company lacking innovation?

Not only that, you will find that although Cook is low-key and often uses avoidance to answer questions, he has chosen not to become a shadow of Jobs. Besides extending the screen of the iPhone 5 and launching the 7-inch tablet, more and more signs indicate that a cheaper version of the iPhone is about to hit the market (Cook has already mentioned this in the Q3 earnings call).

I have never thought that expanding the product line would be a sign of Apple's decline — as the Android camp matures and the high-end smartphone market saturates, if Apple can capture a large number of users with high-quality low-cost phones, it will greatly expand the scale of the iOS ecosystem.

Although this may likely cause a decrease in the overall gross margin of iPhones in the short term, under the overall trend of declining hardware profits, this approach not only gradually strengthens the profitability of the iOS ecosystem and App Store but also, if iOS devices can significantly increase their share in households, makes it easier for Apple to expand into broader hardware markets. By connecting watches, fitness bands, electric vehicles, and other devices, iOS could become the control center of home devices and compete with the open-source Android system in the new hardware market.

Of course, besides innovation capability, excellent quality, and control over the supply chain, Apple hasn’t made much progress on its issues. Google and Amazon have both invaded the hardware market, especially Amazon, which has built a more App Store-like application distribution system under the Android market share, while Apple’s internet progress remains slow.

The recent incident involving the Apple Developer Center website illustrates this point — after being hacked on July 18th, Apple took three days to issue a statement informing developers of the situation and eight days to restore partial services. For a company of Apple's size serving a large developer community, this pace is simply incomprehensible.

Therefore, regardless of whether Apple has lost Jobs' influence, it is still essentially the same Apple. It still firmly controls the hardware supply chain and possesses outstanding quality and innovation capabilities. At the same time, it is still just a software-hardware company, unable to escape the impact of the current mainstream hardware market environment.

However, I believe that it still has the ability to show us some dazzling products in the next hardware revolution and remind us of Jobs’ legacy.
