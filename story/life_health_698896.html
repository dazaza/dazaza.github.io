Throat cancer originates from the malignant tumor of the throat mucosal epithelial tissue, with the most common type being laryngeal squamous cell carcinoma. It predominantly affects middle-aged and elderly men. The occurrence of this disease is associated with factors such as smoking, excessive drinking, long-term inhalation of harmful substances, and papillomavirus infection. The treatment for throat cancer has traditionally been surgery-based, supplemented by radiotherapy and chemotherapy. However, with the maturity of biotechnology and the widespread application of "tumor biological therapy," there is now a safer and more effective weapon for treating throat cancer.

Traditional treatments for throat cancer include:

1. Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM):

TCM believes that throat cancer arises due to lung heat stagnation, phlegm-heat obstruction, spleen-stomach heat excess, fire-toxin accumulation, liver qi stagnation, and blood stasis. Based on the principle of "differentiation of symptoms and signs" in TCM, treatment is divided into three types:

- Clearing lung heat and resolving phlegm-heat obstruction

- Purging fire and detoxifying while activating blood circulation and dispersing nodules

- Activating blood circulation and removing blood stasis, promoting qi flow and dispersing nodules

2. Surgical Treatment:

Surgery is the preferred method for treating throat cancer, and its effectiveness depends on case selection and adherence to indications. Specific surgical types and methods include:

- Laryngotomy + CO2 laser tumor resection for carcinoma in situ or T1 unilateral laryngeal cancer

- Vertical partial laryngectomy for T1, selective T2, T3

- Horizontal partial laryngectomy for tumors above the glottic plane, T2-T3

- Three-quarter or seven-eighths laryngectomy for selective T2, T3

- Total laryngectomy for T3, T4, with possible voice reconstruction if conditions permit

- Functional or radical neck dissection based on patient condition

Despite the goal of thoroughly curing the tumor and preserving laryngeal function, surgery can still result in trauma and complications.

3. Radiation Therapy:

Radiation therapy is mainly used for early-stage vocal cord cancer or cases unsuitable for surgery due to overall health conditions. Its advantage lies in preserving the larynx's phonation and breathing functions while achieving therapeutic goals. However, not all throat cancer patients are suitable for radiation therapy, and it may cause complications such as radiation-induced laryngeal chondritis, cartilage necrosis, and laryngeal mucosal edema.

4. Chemotherapy:

Since throat cancer primarily involves squamous cell carcinoma, which is not very sensitive to chemotherapy, it is not the first choice for treatment but serves as an auxiliary method.
