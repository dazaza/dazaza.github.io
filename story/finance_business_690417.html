Introduction to Age Spots:

The formation of age spots is related to skin aging and sun exposure, often appearing on the backs of hands and forearms of middle-aged and elderly people. As age increases, these spots also increase in number. They are brown or black spots ranging from the size of a mung bean to an almond. Slightly raised above the skin surface, they are smooth, painless, itchy-free, and do not affect health, but should avoid sun exposure.

During the metabolic process in the human body, a substance called "free radicals" is produced, which is lipofuscin pigment. This pigment accumulates on the surface of the human body, forming age spots.

In the youth period, the human body has natural antioxidants and antioxidant enzymes. These antioxidant substances turn free radicals into inert compounds, preventing the generation of peroxidized lipids, hence no cell damage occurs. However, as age increases, the body's antioxidant function gradually declines. In old age, free radicals in the body begin to cause destruction. It is generally believed that age spots are precursors of tissue aging, indicating that cells have entered the aging stage. Lipofuscin pigments not only accumulate on the skin but also invade the internal organs. If deposited on the walls of blood vessels, it causes fibrous lesions in the blood vessels, leading to arteriosclerosis, hypertension, myocardial infarction; when accumulated in brain cells, it affects brain function, thus accelerating the aging process of the brain, and can also cause memory and intellectual disorders, depression, and even senile dementia in the elderly. The accumulation of this substance within cells will hinder normal cellular metabolism, causing overall body aging, and eventually leading to death.
