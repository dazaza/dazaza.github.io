Synopsis:

The film tells a very simple story of a friendship between two pen pals. Mary Dinkle is a chubby little girl living in Melbourne who is somewhat depressed and lonely; Max Horowitz is a 44-year-old obese Jewish man living in chaotic New York City with Asperger's syndrome (a form of autism).

Their correspondence spans two continents and lasts for 20 years. This pen pal friendship continues through letter after letter. The film takes the audience on a journey about friendship, self-discovery, and introspection, revealing the mental worlds of these two individuals and narrating the essence of humanity.

Behind-the-scenes Production:

Opening Film at Sundance

2009 marks the 25th anniversary of the Sundance Film Festival, which has become a hub for high-quality low-budget independent films. Naturally, they took great care in selecting their opening film. At the Sundance Film Festival that opened on January 15, 2009, Adam Elliot's claymation 'Mary and Max' was chosen as the opening film. It’s a story about a 20-year-long pen pal friendship, and it’s also semi-autobiographical for the director. The main characters are two people from vastly different ages and educational backgrounds: an 8-year-old girl and a 44-year-old Jewish man. The story is based on the director's own life, where he himself had a pen pal relationship lasting over 20 years.

Geoffrey Gilmore, director of the Sundance Film Festival, said: "This is a movie about two lives. Both protagonists have some physical disabilities, and Elliot used clay to paint two portrait works for us. The film is filled with unexpected touching moments, humor, and situations that make us think. 'Mary and Max' is not only the opening film of this year's Sundance Film Festival but also the first animated feature to open the festival. I believe that after the audience sees the film, they will give Elliot a warm applause because he created a thought-provoking and profound work. Even more remarkable is that this film is a clay animation without relying too much on computer animation technology, which is what excites me the most."

Capturing Hearts

'Mary and Max' is the second film by this renowned Australian animator to be showcased at Sundance. In 2004, his short film 'Harvie Krumpet' received unanimous praise from the audience at Sundance and won the Oscar for Best Animated Short Film that year. It also earned him awards from the Australian Film Institute alongside his earlier autobiographical trilogy 'Brother', 'Cousin', and 'Uncle'. These films were screened at various film festivals around the world, gaining Elliot a loyal fan base. “If we start counting from when we began making this movie, it has taken us five years to premiere at Sundance. It’s not just nine months of pregnancy, but rather five years of hard work, so I should have confidence in it. However, showing it at the opening of Sundance still makes me a bit nervous,” Elliot said, “Premiering my film at such a high-level, high-quality film festival has been my dream for many years, and the feeling of achieving that dream is truly wonderful.”

Regarding the possible fate of the film at Sundance, Geoffrey Gilmore said, “A film like this about an 'abnormal' friendship will definitely not be ignored at Sundance.” Mark Gooder, CEO of ICON, the film's production company, expressed no concern about the film's future prospects. He said, “There’s no better place than Sundance to screen Elliot’s film. His incomparable talent and desire for universal love and understanding will surely touch all audiences.”

Fun Facts:

- This is the second stop-motion animated film produced in Australia. The first was '9.99 Dollars'.

- From the initial concept to the completion of filming, it took Elliot five years. The actual clay animation shooting time was 57 weeks. The entire production team consisted of only 50 staff members, and the film is 92 minutes long. On average, these people could produce 2.5 minutes of animated footage per week. Calculated by groups of six workers, they could create four seconds of animated footage per day.

- The entire animated film is made up of approximately 132,480 individual frames, shot using six Canon digital cameras.

- There were ten animation teams and seven shooting teams working simultaneously.

- Adam Elliot was not only the writer and director of the film but also the designer of all the characters in it. He stated that the unique black-and-white portraits taken by New York photographer Diane Arbus inspired his creative inspiration. Additionally, one character in the film was designed based on Arbus' image.

- All text in the film — the text in store windows on street corners, signboard text, packaging text, beer bottle text, and the letters exchanged between Mary and Max — were all personally written by Adam Elliot.

- The art crew invented a working method called "Square i". They spent large amounts of time simulating shooting angles and studying perspective effects. Only after confirming everything was correct would the clay animators and photographers begin actual work.

- During the setup process, a total of 212 clay figures were used, made from clay, composite materials, wooden sticks, and metal. Each joint on every clay figure was movable. The production team specially made several copies of the two main characters.

- The film was shot across 133 scenes. Two completely different colors were used to represent New York and Australia, gray and brown respectively. For clay animation, shooting such scenes was undoubtedly a massive project — from deserted islands to chocolate shops, everything had to be meticulously crafted. The biggest challenge for the production team was "creating" the New York skyline. This was also the scene that took the longest to shoot. A team of 20 art workers spent two full months completing it.

- The film produced a total of 475 mini props, from wine glasses to typewriters, all painstakingly molded out of clay by the staff. In particular, the vintage typewriter featured in the film took nine weeks to design and build.

- To enable the characters' faces to move and express various emotions, they needed movable mouths and eyebrows — in each frame, the staff would replace the character's mouth with a different one. The entire film used 1,026 different mouths, each a model wrapped in modeling clay containing steel wires. To make Max speak, the crew prepared 30 different mouths for him.

- The film used 886 plastic hands containing wire skeletons.

- The production team made 394 pupils, each about the size of a small ladybug. All were handcrafted by technicians, and each pupil was painted with a shiny white dot after being made.

- The production team included two costume designers and 147 tailors who handled all costume designs and productions in the film. Mary's wedding dress was modeled after the late Princess Anna's wedding dress. Ivy's onesie was inspired by the mother of photographer Annie Leibovitz's onesie.

- A total of 38 light bulbs appeared in the film. These light bulbs were individually molded out of clay by the staff, and wires were attached behind them.

- In the film, 808 Early Grey tea bags were used, which was a huge project. Almost everyone in the production team — from the director to the art department, from the producer to the costume designer — participated in making the tea bags.

- To create a vast number of characters, scenes, and props, powerful mold support was required, so the production team manufactured and used 632 clay molds. The range of these molds was astonishing, covering everything from the characters' eyeballs to the cracks on the floor.

- A total of 73 kilograms of plastic were used to make the characters' mouths, hands, and original skeletons. Every batch of plastic molds needed to be painted the same color and mixed with the same material ratio to ensure identical texture, pattern, density, and melting point.

- During filming, 12 liters of lubricant were used as fluid. Everything from water in cups to tears and rivers flowing through the jungle were made from these lubricants.

- During the filming period, the entire production team consumed 260 kilograms of tomatoes, 280 kilograms of coffee beans, drank 2,600 liters of milk, and consumed 7,800 muffins — of which 5,236 were eaten by the director.

- This film served as the opening film for the 24th Sundance Film Festival.

Goofs:

For a clay animation, goofs can only be fingerprints left by the staff on the clay.

Awards:

The film won the Best Animated Feature Award at the 2009 Annecy International Animation Film Festival, the Crystal Bear Award at the Berlin International Film Festival, and the top prize at the Ottawa International Animation Festival.
