The day before, we left the Tangbula Grassland and returned along the same route, passing through Xinyuan County, and arrived at the Nalati Town at around 10 p.m. We found a hotel to stay in. I mentioned before that what impressed me most on the way to Nalati was the vast fields of wheat stretching endlessly. I felt like the wheat fields continued for miles, with no obstacles nearby except distant mountains. It was truly spectacular. If it hadn't been for the time constraints and the impending darkness, I would have definitely asked the driver to stop so we could admire the view. Since we couldn't indulge ourselves, let's keep this fleeting glimpse forever in our memories. Nalati Town is not large, but due to the famous Nalati Grassland, the town mainly consists of hotels and restaurants. Perhaps because the season we visited wasn't peak, it was relatively easy to find a hotel at 11 p.m., and both the price and environment were much better than the previous night in Gongliu County. However, the row of hotels we stayed in had a power outage that night, while the other side of the road still had electricity. We thought the power would be restored by midnight, but unfortunately, there was no power all night, and no hot water for showers. Fortunately, the dry and cool weather on the grassland made it tolerable even if we didn't shower for several days. If we were in the mainland, especially Guangzhou, we would have been unable to endure it long ago.

On the morning of the second day, after having breakfast at a restaurant across from the hotel, we set off. I've always said that the cost of living in Xinjiang isn't low, feeling similar to Shanghai. This can be seen from the breakfast we had that day as well; eggs were either 1.5 yuan or 2 yuan each, which was quite expensive. The plan for the day was to travel from Nalati Town, through the Nalati Grassland, over the Gongnaisi Pass, and then continue on to Bayinbrooke. The distance was far, and the road conditions after the Gongnaisi Pass were poor. As we passed through the Nalati Grassland, we briefly stopped at the entrance, considering the overlap with the grasslands we had visited earlier and the commercialization of Nalati, we did not buy tickets to enter. However, the early morning Nalati was still very beautiful. If it weren't for budget constraints, we would have liked to visit the so-called "Sky Grassland." After leaving Nalati, we continued our journey. The car climbed continuously, and by the time we reached the top of the Gongnaisi Pass, we could already see large patches of snow. The scenery along this route was extremely beautiful, and the Gongnaisi Pass lived up to its reputation. I believe the Gongnaisi forest farm is just as enchanting as the legends describe. Miu classmate had never seen snow before, so she was incredibly excited when she saw the snow on the pass. L and Quan classmate were both speechless at her reaction. Well, we went from summer to winter in an instant. On the top of the pass, it felt like we were close to the sky, with mist swirling around, giving a sense of entering a fairyland. After descending from the pass, the scenery slowly began to differ from what we had seen before. If the previous scenery was lush and undulating, the subsequent route took on the flavor of a grand western landscape. The endless straight roads stretched out like celestial paths, with boundless wilderness on both sides, resembling a desert yet not quite a desert, with distant mountain ranges continuing to rise and fall. Such vastness, such blue skies and white clouds, such desolation, the greatest joy was to sit in the car and gallop freely.

With great reluctance, we left the winding roads and drove for a long time in the dark, finally returning to Bayin Town. After a simple dinner of Uyghur food, we went back to wash up and sleep, which was already late at night. In Xinjiang, it's like this: sunset at 10 p.m., dinner at 11 p.m., and sleep at midnight. I wonder, in the quiet and distant depths of Bayin, are the swans already sound asleep? Would they dance gracefully at some moment in the night?

After finishing the highway, turning onto the small road leading to Bayin became quite challenging due to poor road conditions and bumpy rides. But fortunately, the roadside scenery consoled us. Occasionally seen were crystal-clear springs, sunlight casting shadows on the mountains, adorable cattle and sheep along with their herding dogs, and even purple flowers discovered while singing mountain songs, all of which sufficiently alleviated our travel fatigue. Before entering Bayin, all the grasslands we saw were alpine grasslands of the tussock type. Bayin, however, was the true plains grassland everyone imagined - the kind that makes you think of "vast skies, boundless wilderness, wind blowing the grass low to reveal cattle and sheep." Compared to Kala Jun and Tangbula, it was open and expansive.

However, there were no swans at Swan Lake; they were hiding far away from us. Even with the camera zoomed in, we couldn't see them clearly and had to use binoculars. Actually, when we were driving along the edge of Sayram Lake, we did see swans - several of them, much clearer and closer than those at Bayin. However, at that time, the master said they weren't swans, and Quan also said they weren't. After returning, I reviewed several other people's travelogues and confirmed that they were indeed swans. Frankly speaking, the swans at Sayram Lake seemed more beautiful, probably because Sayram Lake itself was more beautiful. Although we didn't see the swans clearly, we still spotted many other birds. Being a wetland, there were plenty of birds, and Swan Lake was still very beautiful. After lingering around Swan Lake for a while, we noticed lightning and heavy rain in the distance, so we retreated towards the nine bends. Unfortunately, as we approached the nine bends, the clouds grew thicker, the sky darker, and eventually, it started raining. Once it rained, it didn't stop, and we sat in the car waiting in the parking lot below the nine bends for one or two hours. The rain was utterly disheartening. At one point, we thought that after the rain stopped, the sun would suddenly shine brightly on the earth, but alas, things didn't go as planned. By eight o'clock, when the rain stopped and we walked up the mountain, after a long climb to the viewing platform of the nine bends, the sky still hadn't cleared up, and the sun didn't show its face. Alas, it was sunny when we went to Swan Lake. It was freezing cold on the mountaintop. After waiting for a while, I reluctantly followed L down the mountain, constantly looking back to see if the sky might clear up. That feeling, that melancholy. I blamed Quan for all the rain I encountered during my trip. He always encounters rain when he travels - how could I bring him along? Sigh... actually, Quan was even more melancholy. Because everyone calls him Rain God. Alright, the legendary nine bends where nine suns can be seen, I still haven't met you. However, I know, one day I will meet you. Because on the land of the northwest, there are even more magnificent nine bends. We will have another chance.

Compared to all the grasslands and lakes mentioned in the previous few articles, Bayinbrooke is undoubtedly the most famous. Even if you've never been to Xinjiang and know little about it, I believe you've heard of Bayinbrooke from somewhere. Bayinbrooke Grassland, China's second-largest grassland, means "rich spring" in Mongolian.

We arrived in Bayinbrooke Town around 2 p.m. Miu and I went to look for rooms and negotiate prices. With our silver-tongued persuasion, we managed to reduce the price from 180 yuan to 120 yuan, successfully securing a good hotel. The master said they usually couldn't get it cheaper than 150 yuan. Bayin Town is far from the outside world, so the consumption here is also not cheap. After having a decent Sichuan meal, we rested in the hotel for a while and then set off for the Bayinbrooke Grassland. Note that Bayin Town is not adjacent to the grassland, but rather dozens of kilometers away, requiring quite some time to drive. Actually, coming to Bayin was mainly to see the swans at Swan Lake and the sunset at the nine bends. The ticket price for Bayin has increased to 40 yuan, with no student tickets, only recognizing military officer certificates and senior citizen certificates. Well, I again demonstrated my bargaining skills, trying to fool around with four expired student certificates at the window but failed. I directly showed the student certificates to their leader standing not far from the window, and the leader gave me a free ticket. Four people bought three tickets. Master Xu looked at me and said, "Qiqi is indeed Qiqi." I was sweating. Then I saved some money again on horseback riding fees. Well, I'm saving everyone small amounts of money, ha ha. Cars cannot enter Swan Lake, so after arriving at a certain place, you cannot enter further. You can only choose to ride a horse or walk into the area. Our group was lazy, plus we hadn't ridden a horse on this trip, so we chose to ride forward. Facts told us that our choice was correct, because walking would take a long time, and there were many marshes on the road, making it painful to walk. Riding a horse on such a vast and open grassland was a super enjoyable experience.
