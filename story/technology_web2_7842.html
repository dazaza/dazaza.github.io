Auto-play Extinguishing Committee

by rionkdr on 2006-10-22 22:32:48

Preamble

Unite, netizens of the world!

The Fight Undesired-autoplay Committee (FUAC) is a group dedicated to combating annoying music or video files that automatically load and play without the browser's permission. We do not oppose audio/video files themselves, nor any design that arranges multimedia objects on web pages; rather, we oppose the behavior of autoplay that deprives browsers of their freedom of choice. Regardless of whether the purpose is advertising, sharing, or whether it is a commercial website, creating a high-quality browsing environment without autoplay is the common goal of committee members.

Origin of the Committee Name

When unexpected high-volume multimedia files appear, they often leave browsers flustered, involuntarily uttering ancient greetings like "FxCK." Therefore, the abbreviation of this committee's name, "FUAC," also takes its easy-to-remember, populist pronunciation from this greeting, pronounced in Chinese as "Fa Ke." Thus, the committee is commonly known as the "Fa Ke Committee." However, for highly sensitive political reasons, this common name should not be used in official documents such as public notices or media press releases.

Definition of "Autoplay"

Any multimedia plug-in embedded on personal websites, blogs, or other web pages that is set to automatically load and play upon page opening constitutes an autoplay action.

Anti-social Nature of "Autoplay"

In the busy modern society, many people inevitably browse the internet while working, searching for necessary information, or taking short breaks. In poorly soundproofed office cubicles, automatically played music from websites is often the best indicator for employers to identify specific workers. Although there are no formal statistics showing how many employees have been fired due to sudden bursts of music, it is already a widely recognized social consensus that autoplay audio/video files pose a significant threat to the job security of desk workers.

Even outside the workplace, if you already have music playing on your own computer, room speakers, or portable audio devices, suddenly playing music when opening a webpage can cause serious secondary mixing pollution, greatly harming the physical and mental health of browsers. If the music played is filled with otaku implications, it could severely damage the family relationships and social status of the browser.
