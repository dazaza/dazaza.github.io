Believe it or not! Be careful of chronic poisoning from eating these fruits regularly in winter.

by tuoqing1188 on 2011-08-03 13:38:53

In winter, people can eat summer fruits such as watermelon and strawberry, but they are more expensive. However, nutritionists suggest that in winter it is better to eat seasonal fruits which are the cheapest. Because out-of-season fruits not only have low nutritional value, but also cost a lot, so they are not suitable for regular consumption.

The value of out-of-season fruits compared to seasonal fruits lies in the freshness rather than nutrition. Those colorful and bright out-of-season fruits are often grown in greenhouses, and their exposure to sunlight, wind, and soil quality are different from seasonal fruits. Therefore, the most nutritious fruits are usually the cheapest ones, and the cheapest fruits are often seasonal fruits.

Fruits should be bought according to the amount you plan to consume because each fruit has its optimal storage temperature and shelf life. The longer the fruit is stored, the worse its nutrition and flavor will become. Most fruits are suitable for direct refrigeration without washing, otherwise they may easily spoil and rot, and should be consumed within a week if possible. Bananas, pineapples, mangoes, papayas, and lemons, etc., should be stored in cool indoor corners and should not be refrigerated for a long time.

In addition, some unscrupulous vendors use chemical reagents to ripen out-of-season fruits for sales, which can harm human health. Below are several dangerous out-of-season fruits:

1. Grapes

Some unscrupulous vendors and fruit farmers use ripening agents - ethephon. Users dilute ethephon with water in a certain proportion, then soak the unripe green grapes in the diluted solution, and after a day or two, the green grapes turn into purple grapes. Although this drug has low toxicity, long-term consumption can be harmful to human health.

2. Bananas

To make the banana skin look tender and yellow, some unscrupulous vendors use sulfur dioxide to ripen them, but the flesh still feels hard and not sweet at all. Sulfur dioxide and its derivatives not only harm the respiratory system but can also cause brain, liver, spleen, and kidney lesions, and even affect the reproductive system.

3. Watermelons

Overuse of ripening agents, growth promoters, and highly toxic pesticides can make watermelons toxic. These watermelons have uneven stripes on the rind, the flesh inside is particularly bright red when cut open, but the seeds are white, and after eating them, there is an odd taste in the mouth.

4. Strawberries

Strawberries that are hollow in the middle, irregular in shape, and unusually large are generally caused by excessive hormone use. Strawberries treated with ripening agents or other hormones have a shorter growing period, fresher color, but a lighter taste. This kind of strawberries must be eaten with caution; if consumed in large quantities, it can affect normal human growth and development, such as causing early puberty in children.

Winter Vegetable Buying Suggestions:

1. Some vegetables, after being ripened artificially, may leave traces of immaturity on the skin or elsewhere. Pay more attention when buying, and toxins can be removed through methods such as sun exposure, peeling, blanching, and stir-frying.

2. If tempted by the variety of off-season vegetables in winter, it's best to choose onions, carrots, eggplants, etc., which contain fewer pesticide residues. Try to avoid vegetables with strange shapes and colors.

3. Try to buy fresh ingredients from the market and cook them yourself, and reduce the purchase of processed foods. Processed foods usually go through repeated processing, making it easy for nutrients to be destroyed, and it's hard to ensure freshness. They may even contain harmful additives.

Suggestions for What Vegetables to Eat in Winter:

Green leafy vegetables decrease relatively in winter, so what vegetables are good to eat in winter? In winter, you can appropriately eat some tubers like sweet potatoes and potatoes. They are rich in vitamin C and B vitamins, especially red sweet potatoes which also contain more beta-carotene.

What vegetables are good to eat in winter? Among the common vegetables available in winter, apart from Chinese cabbage, you should also choose round cabbages, radishes, white radishes, carrots, soybean sprouts, mung bean sprouts, rape, etc. These vegetables are all rich in vitamins. As long as you frequently change varieties and reasonably combine them, you can supplement the body's vitamin needs.

Different types of vegetables are rich in different nutrients. It's important to eat vegetables every day, and it's best to do so several times a week, choosing from five categories of vegetables (dark green, orange-yellow, legumes, starchy vegetables, and others). Regularly changing varieties and trying to diversify your intake allows you to fully absorb different nutrients from various foods.

You should consume about 500 grams of vegetables daily to provide a significant amount of vitamin C, beta-carotene, and a moderate amount of calcium, iron, and other inorganic salts. Of these, colored vegetables and leafy greens should account for around 50%. What vegetables are good to eat in winter? Eat more dark green vegetables like cauliflower, cabbage, and other dark green leafy vegetables. Orange-red vegetables include carrots, sweet potatoes, pumpkins, etc. For example, in a 2000-calorie diet (about 1000 calories for a one-year-old child), the dietary guidelines recommend vegetable intake: 3 cups of dark green vegetables per week (about 240 grams per cup), 2 cups of orange vegetables per week, 3 cups of legumes per week, 3 cups of starchy vegetables per week, and 6.5 cups of other vegetables per week.

Recommendations for Vegetables to Eat During Flu Season:

Brightly colored vegetables - tomatoes, carrots, spinach, etc., are rich in antioxidants. These antioxidants help keep immune cells free from adverse environmental factors and enhance the ability of white blood cells to kill foreign microbes. At the same time, since these foods also contain a large amount of vitamin C, they help fight the flu and allow patients to recover quickly. Brightly colored fruits like gooseberries and kumquats also have similar effects.

Vegetables rich in vitamin E - Studies have shown that adequate vitamin E in children significantly enhances T-cell infection resistance capabilities. Therefore, foods rich in vitamin E, such as dark green vegetables, almonds, malt, etc., are also essential during flu season.

Garlic and onions - In European and American folklore, drinking onion juice is a popular method for treating coughs. Onions enhance the immune activity of T-cells, thereby promoting overall immune response. Foods containing similar immune-active substances as garlic and onions act as cleaning and detoxifying agents in the body, playing a role in killing and clearing toxins and bacteria.
