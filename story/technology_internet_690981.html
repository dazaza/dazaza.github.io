[Qingdao Parent-Child] How do foreign countries handle the transition from kindergarten to primary school?

by qssqwe215 on 2012-03-05 13:36:32

The transition between early childhood education and primary school education is a universally important issue worldwide, and has become one of the key topics in current global educational research. Developed countries have conducted extensive studies with the aim of providing children with one or two years of preparatory education before entering primary school. This enhances the physical and mental development of preschoolers, helps them acquire some knowledge and skills, reduces the gap in transitioning from kindergarten to primary school, and eases their burden upon entering primary school.

★ USA: Emphasis on Mathematics and Natural Science Education

The United States places great emphasis on early childhood education and focuses on the transition between kindergarten and primary school education. American kindergartens are generally attached to primary schools, accommodating children aged 4-6 as a "pre-primary step." For five-year-old children about to enter first grade, there is a mandatory one-year preparatory education program that mainly promotes all-round development to prepare them for primary school. In terms of educational content, emphasis is placed on meeting contemporary requirements by teaching mathematics and natural sciences to young children, which has a profound impact on their intellectual development and lays a solid foundation for their entry into primary school.

★ UK, Switzerland: Merging Early Childhood Education with Lower Primary Grades

Switzerland has 26 cantons, each with its own education system. Geneva implements a model where the last two years of preschool and the first two grades of primary school are set within the same institution. Geneva's "Maison Des Detits" preschool center consists of the last two years of preschool and the first two grades of primary school, located in one building as an independent educational institution. Geneva's "Ecole DuLirron" school is divided into two parts: one part includes the last two years of preschool and the first two grades of primary school, while the other part consists of grades three through six. Although both parts fall under unified administrative management, they are managed separately with the second grade of primary school serving as the dividing line.

In the UK, primary education is divided into nursery schools for children aged 2-5, infant schools for children aged 5-7, and junior schools for children aged 7-11. Compulsory education begins at age 5, with all children entering infant schools after completing two years of preparatory education before moving on to junior schools. Despite being part of the primary stage, the curriculum content, requirements, and activity organization formats in infant schools still gradually transition from the preschool stage to primary education. Additionally, the UK also emphasizes the connection between daycare centers and infant schools. A common practice in London currently is merging nursery schools with infant schools, avoiding differences in environmental and social interaction ranges between the two institutions and the problems arising from them.

Design continuity is evident in school and classroom environments. Both the UK and Switzerland place significant importance on arranging and decorating the material environment, aiming to facilitate children's development through interaction with the material environment. Activity corners such as science corners and art corners appear in preschool activity rooms, reducing the unfamiliarity children may feel when entering primary school classrooms. Of course, the materials in these activity corners change in terms of content and nature once they enter primary school, gradually shifting towards concrete symbols and words. Some of the content in activity corners transforms into wall posters.

Considering the need for smooth transitions between preschool and primary school, the main trend in educational institution settings in Switzerland and the UK is to place the last two years of preschool and the first two grades of primary school in the same environment, combining or merging early childhood education with lower primary education into one educational phase. From environmental arrangements, curriculum design, to teacher training, everything revolves around creating an integrated, continuous, developmental, and coordinated learning environment.

★ Japan: Incorporating Early Childhood to Primary Transition into Lifelong Education System

Japan considers the transition between kindergarten and primary school within the context of lifelong education. The Ministry of Education explicitly states that the transition does not involve cultivating specific academic content directly connected to primary school but rather focuses on nurturing qualities that serve as a foundation for life and learning once children enter primary school.

In primary education, it is crucial to further extend, develop, and deepen the achievements of kindergarten education. In kindergarten education, the new curriculum significantly changes the content of kindergarten courses, modifying the original six domains (physical education, language, nature, society, art, music) to five domains (physical education, language, interpersonal relationships, environment, expression). In primary education, the Ministry of Education introduces a new subject—Life Studies—in the first and second grades of primary school. Life Studies incorporates elements from the previous science (with content like nature, geography, basic physics and chemistry) and social studies (focusing on social common sense and skills), but it is more than just a simple combination—it is a new comprehensive subject with a scientific education focus. Reforms in curriculum goals, content, and educational methods create favorable conditions for the transition between kindergarten and primary school, ensuring consistency in objectives post-reform, making kindergarten education a strong foundation for children's continued learning and development in primary school.

★ France: Strengthening the Transition through Three Major Laws

To ensure that early childhood education and primary school education exhibit both reasonable hierarchical structure and good transitional properties, France has successively issued three major laws emphasizing the strengthening of the transition between kindergarten and primary school. Specific measures include:

1. Increasing Male Teachers

Research by psychologists and educational experts indicates that having only female teachers in kindergartens is detrimental to the psychological and healthy development of children. Therefore, male teachers are encouraged to work in kindergartens.

2. Scientific Division of Learning Stages in Maternelle Schools and Primary Schools

Preschool and primary school children are divided into three stages: the first stage is the "pre-learning period," including children aged 2, 3, and 4 in maternelle schools; the second stage is the "basic learning period," including 5-year-olds in maternelle schools and the first two grades of primary school; the third stage is the "consolidation period," including the last three grades of primary school. Dividing children into three stages based on age aims to prevent repetitive learning. Teachers from the preschool stage and primary school meet weekly for an hour to discuss the actual levels each stage should achieve and determine the conditions necessary for each student to reach those levels.

3. Merging Supervision and Guidance Work Between Maternelle Schools and Primary Schools

4. Training Teachers from Maternelle Schools and Primary Schools Similarly is Another Effective Measure for Transition in France.
