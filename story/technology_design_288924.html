Han Han Bai Ye Debate 2006 Part 1

by yadono05qd on 2009-12-02 12:04:05

The so-called "debate" initially stemmed from Bai Ye's article "The Hidden Status and Future of the Post-80s Generation." Literary critic Bai Ye posted an article on his personal blog stating that the works by "post-80s writers" could not yet be considered literature, but rather amateur attempts. One reason he gave was that these writers rarely appeared in literary journals; the literary world did not truly understand them. He noted that "they have entered the market, but have not yet entered the literary world."

Post-80s writer Han Han, upon hearing this, gleefully responded with a blog post titled "The Literary World is Nothing but a Joke, No One Should Pretend to Be Something They're Not," arguing that whether something is literary or not should not be determined by the literary world. He criticized the literary world for fostering cliques and stifling literature.

This ignited reactions within the literary circle. Xi Zhan wrote an article supporting Bai Ye; Li Jingze, editor of *People's Literature*, penned a critique of post-80s writers; Lu Tianming discussed the disagreement between Han Han and Bai Ye on his blog; filmmaker Lu Chuan also wrote in support of Lu Tianming, and his close friend Gao Xiaosong chimed in...

Regarding the status and future of the post-80s generation:

Bai Ye

Since the term "post-80s" emerged some years ago, it has consistently been a hot topic in the literary world. The sudden appearance of this phenomenon was unexpected, and its continued fervor even more surprising. However, no one has thoroughly studied how this phenomenon arose, what it foretells, and what it brings. After encountering this phenomenon, I felt we cannot ignore or neglect it. It is actually a literary reflection and cultural projection of our societal civilization reaching a certain stage. Its emergence and development are interconnected with various aspects of our social and cultural lives. Below, through four small topics, I will discuss my understanding of the "post-80s" generation and offer preliminary thoughts on their current state and future.

One: The Quiet Rise of the "Post-80s"

Here, the term "post-80s" refers to young writers born between 1980 and 1989. Sometimes this overlaps with other concepts like "youth writing" or "new concept writing." Although the term "post-80s" isn't entirely accurate now and the writers themselves aren't entirely satisfied with it, there is currently no better term to replace it. This is because "post-80s" represents more of a cultural phenomenon than a literary trend or school. For now, we can only use this age and era-based concept as a summary.

Han Han’s response:

I fundamentally oppose categorizing authors by birth year. Each excellent author is a uniquely distinct individual, not someone who fits neatly into categories. Classifying people based on birth years is akin to selling piglets—grouping everyone born in the same year together. Should all those born between 1966 and 1976 be called the 'Cultural Revolution generation'? And if the Cultural Revolution failed, does that mean they are the 'Cultural Revolution failure generation'?

But that's not the main issue. The key problem is that he insists only the writings of the people he knows (those he's met, had meals with) count as literature. He pretends to guide young authors in a condescending manner.

Literature and filmmaking are not things anyone can just do without any barriers. Some so-called literary critics are very clumsy, ignoring bestsellers altogether and assuming anything popular isn’t pure literature. They seem to think readers are all fools except for themselves, who alone are enlightened. These critics write verbose essays filled with convoluted language. But if suddenly a crowd decides to boycott such pretentiousness, then supposedly pure literature might become popular again, and these critics would likely change their views immediately.

Selling well or poorly doesn't necessarily correlate with being literary or not. Take Bai Ye, for example—his writing is verbose, repetitive, lacking both wit and elegance. If I hadn't forced myself to comment, I wouldn't have bothered finishing such subpar writing. So, imagine him trying to write a novel—it would likely take ten thousand words just to describe a tree. If his novels don't sell well, he'd blame the times for having issues with reading habits. Such behavior is utterly absurd.

Many best-selling authors write pure literature. Regardless of how they write, someone always pays for it. Thus, they don't need to consider pleasing readers or catering to the market—they can write whatever they want. Conversely, many authors whose books don't sell well but claim to write pure literature must constantly think about adding eye-catching elements, like including a scene involving a bed on page 80 (preferably at someone else's home), a homosexual encounter on page 100 (preferably a three-way), some violence on page 200 (preferably resulting in death), and incest (preferably involving a daughter) on page 400. (These authors usually write lengthy works exceeding 500 pages.)

As for me, my writing could be described as rare pure literature. I write what I think, without participating in any promotional activities. I don't hold self-congratulatory symposiums or book release events. My books sell well because they are good. If they don't sell well one day, it's fine too. Many best-selling authors write pure literature because, regardless of how they write, someone always pays for it. Thus, they don't need to consider pleasing readers or catering to the market—they can write whatever they want. Conversely, many authors whose books don't sell well but claim to write pure literature must constantly think about adding eye-catching elements, like including a scene involving a bed on page 80 (preferably at someone else's home), a homosexual encounter on page 100 (preferably a three-way), some violence on page 200 (preferably resulting in death), and incest (preferably involving a daughter) on page 400.

In conclusion, literature is simply serious writing done freely. People can only do so much; the rest is up to fate. Literature is the one thing that cannot be overly obsessed with or practiced excessively. Younger generations especially should write sincerely without pretense.
