Features, not the product.

by pine on 2006-12-27 10:06:13

For a long time, Google has been constantly releasing all kinds of novel and interesting products. These include not only amazing products like Gmail and Google Maps, but also many isolated and immature ones. In a sense, this makes Google an engineer's paradise, where individual heroism and small-team collaboration are encouraged, programmers work for interest, and many people are keen on turning their 20% free-time work into official company projects. However, Google is ultimately a large company; it’s not really a venture capital firm that can scatter its resources across dozens or even hundreds of small products. That's why Sergey Brin said: "Features, not products (特性，而非产品)."

This reminds me of Microsoft. If there is one company that best illustrates the importance of 'features, not products,' I think it would be Microsoft. Microsoft started with operating systems, and over more than 30 years, it continuously added features to its operating system rather than developing a multitude of unrelated products. Thus, internet access became a feature of the operating system, as did the browser and media playback. The monopoly controversies Microsoft faced mainly stemmed from here: 'features, not products.' Microsoft has always dreamed of making search a feature of the operating system rather than a product with independent value.

I remember Lao Hua saying last year that Google's development model would eventually run into problems. A large technology company cannot indefinitely allow its engineers to develop products based solely on personal interest. The tedious work of maintaining and updating existing products needs to be done by someone. But a significant part of Google's appeal to the brightest minds in the world, beyond stock options, free food, and personalized offices, may very well lie in the release of creative freedom. Would Google still have the same appeal if it were no longer a safe haven for startups? That’s a question worth pondering.

Essentially, no one wants to be an anonymous cog in the machine, but any commercial machine requires a large number of such anonymous cogs to ensure smooth operation. Just like how an army functions as a whole, in a war, the personality and even life of any individual soldier are insignificant. It's hard to imagine...
