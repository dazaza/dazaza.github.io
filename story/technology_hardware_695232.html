Part 1: Pre-Shooting Preparation

What comes to mind? Equipment preparation? Of course, that's correct, but it shouldn't be your top priority. The choice of equipment will be considered by your photographer and sound engineer. During location scouting, you would have roughly discussed your shooting methods with them, and they will come up with an equipment proposal. If you want to manage it yourself, that's fine too, but it's better to first ask them to propose a plan. As a director, please learn to delegate authority (let them propose a plan and then you supplement it).

So, the most important thing in pre-shooting preparation is actually the pre-estimation of shot breakdown.

This estimation can be done mentally or written down as a demo. What is estimation? It means going through the entire shooting process for the next day, including all details, which must not be skipped. There are two things to determine here: 1) The order of shooting shots 2) The coverage length of the shots. (It's best to take out the script and paper!)

Regarding the shooting order, yes, it does depend on the situation at the scene after you arrive. You might change the shooting order at any time, but doing some preliminary organization beforehand will be very beneficial, making you more confident. There are many factors that determine the shooting order, sometimes even your mood is important... Let's do a simple analysis.

Take a regular dialogue scene as an example:

It's better to shoot the wide shot first, and the wide shot should ideally be done in one continuous take (from beginning to end). Why? Because when actors aren't familiar with their lines, the acting isn't smooth, and even the blocking isn't clear (you should be clear, but they may not be proficient), so the wide shot is for "setting" the whole scene.

Since it's a wide shot, the actors don't have to act too hard. This gives them time to get into character, after all, not all actors can adjust their state perfectly on the first take.

Wide shot lighting takes the most time (in principle, low-budget productions may not necessarily follow this). And the wide shot lighting sets the tone for the scene lighting of the entire sequence. When you move to medium or close-up shots, many base lights and environmental background lights don't need to be adjusted much, and if adjustments are needed, there's a reference.

In terms of audio recording, the wide shot is mostly unclear (can't lower the boom pole), so you don't have to nitpick the dialogue too much.

After completing the wide shot, the continuity person can take a full shot as a reference, allowing props and settings to be compared and confirmed (in case you need to reshoot later).

Next, it’s definitely medium close-ups. If your medium shot also involves multiple people, it’s likely not in the same direction as the wide shot, which doesn’t matter. Since the light adjustment isn’t significant, you can shoot directly. At this point, you should start paying attention to your coverage. If in a particular scene, you plan to cut to a medium shot at line A and another at line B, you just need to shoot from the sentence before line A to the sentence after line B, and you can randomly change it. The medium shot could be the main camera position (for situational scenes) or auxiliary (for emotional scenes), you need to be clear about it.

Generally speaking, close-ups and extreme close-ups in dialogue scenes are usually shot from over-the-shoulder angles (either inside or outside the circle). I won’t go into specific shooting techniques and standards here (this could be a separate article). One thing to note is that if you're shooting single-camera, make sure to finish one side before moving to the other. Otherwise, constantly flipping the lights will anger the lighting crew. If using dual cameras, it’s better if both sides have consistent framing. At this point, you need to have a very good grasp of the coverage, especially for close-ups. There’s no need to shoot the entire scene; if only a few lines require close-ups, just focus on those lines (with maybe a word or two before and after). Note: Close-up scenes and extreme close-ups are relatively important, so there might be many takes, thus don’t cover too much. Sometimes actors may overact, so just let them relax a bit. Never think that relaxing means starting from the beginning, as it wastes time. (You can give them a few lines at most; if you suddenly start from the beginning, actors might forget or chase lines, ruining the scene.)

Another factor influencing the lens shooting order is the difficulty of completing the lens. If a particular scene has a very complex blocking, try to complete it as early as possible, leaving easier shots for later. If your scene is too long, then the wide shot may not always be fully covered. If the lens involves a crane or track, and this lens is important and cannot be deleted, it must be shot first. Setting up and mastering the scheduling of these lenses takes a lot of time. If you find that time is insufficient, you can reduce the number of takes to shoot other things, or delete unimportant lenses. However, if you shoot others and then find that time is insufficient, you probably won’t have time to play this lens anymore.

So, in summary:

Principle One: Important lenses are shot first, less important ones or added on the spot are shot later. Arranging the lens order well (of course, being flexible is also important) can reduce your shooting risks. Precisely calculating the coverage (precise means purposeful coverage rather than covering everything or just necessary parts) can improve production quality (performance quality and reduce post-editing pressure).

Principle Two: Try to save camera positions. If two positions can be combined, try to combine them (changing lenses is faster than changing positions, zoom lenses are even better), and if the position setup can be integrated with tracks, that’s ideal. (A single track position can serve as a moving position and at least one fixed position, which requires precise position planning and relates to your blocking. Not many domestic productions use this method, if you achieve it, congratulations, you've reached Hollywood level. American TV series use it quite often. But this shooting style is not easy to implement, requiring high coordination between actors and lighting.)

Other special scenes like fight scenes, gunfight scenes, multi-person blocking, corridor scenes, bed scenes, etc., following the above two principles, involve many complex variations. Specific details won’t be elaborated here. (This could be a book already.)

I suggest drawing a camera position plan while fulfilling this estimated plan, which will be very helpful for on-site command. If each scene has been considered and kept in mind, then the success of tomorrow's shooting is almost guaranteed.

Additional notes: Non-scene lenses need to be marked and filed with the continuity person or coordinator for separate planning:

For example, in a certain scene, an actor looks toward a distant lighthouse from the window. Then it’s possible that there’s no such view outside your scene window, and you need to shoot the lighthouse elsewhere. That should be planned in the empty lens plan, or even in another scene (if you have lighthouse-related scenes), this is an off-screen lens.

2 Regarding Equipment:

Actually, this should be completed in the final check (see the preparation chapter), but since it is closely related to shooting, let's talk about it anyway (just padding words haha). The main discussion is not about equipment configuration for the latest movies in 2012, but about preparation, management, and some precautions for usage.

Principle One: Borrow if you can, rent if you can't borrow, and don't use if you can't rent.

Don't buy equipment unless you're a second-generation rich kid; otherwise, don't purchase any expensive equipment for a low-budget film. Especially when the shooting days are limited. Do the math, compare buying versus renting costs, and then decide whether to buy. Avoid impulsive consumption. Before that... integrate resources, try calling to see if you can borrow for free!

Most small budgets now use the 5D2, so it's best to ask the cinematographer to borrow it. If the cinematographer is shooting, let him be your cinematographer. If he's a photographer, it's even easier to borrow, and they often have great lenses... Hard to borrow for free? There's nothing wrong with it. Not just equipment, many props, costumes, locations, etc., prioritize borrowing for free. (What? You spent this much on your movie! Impossible! Such comments are very flattering, achieving much with little money.)

Key Points:

1. Sincerely explain to the other party that we are shooting a non-commercial independent film. The budget is extremely low (which is true), and pretend to be a student if necessary! (We have homework... but no money... pity is key.)

2. Hint that all staff members work for zero pay (must hint, then if you have a budget, give them something. No budget? Dig something up.)

3. Tell them you'll thank them in the film credits and give them a copy after completion (you really need to burn a DVD and send it to them, don't forget.)

You'll find that most people are willing to lend, and even help you. For example, if you ask to borrow a lens, he might say his friend has a better one, would you like him to borrow it for you? If you ask someone to lend you a location, he might offer to close it for a day. If you ask for costumes, he might give you a few extra sets.

Yes, movies are contagious. This is one of the wonders of movies. In most cases, they’ll ask if they can be extras, try to arrange if possible (unless you live in an area where filming is frequent, it gets complicated, like in Shanghai... various people who charge money, damn it).

Principle Two: Don't mess around with useless stuff.

Use whatever you need, and if you have something, use it as much as possible. But don't go out of your way to get fancy equipment just because you want to use it. For example, if you want to play with tracks, you rent them and arrange tracking shots, which is incorrect. Tracks and cranes look cool but are double-edged swords; setting them up takes a lot of time. A good shot is a suitable shot, not a flashy one. Does your film really need them? Are there no other options? Ask yourself multiple times.

Many students overlook sound recording... Sound recording is crucial. I'd rather miss a shot than not bring a shotgun mic and recorder. Ensure your recording plan. Actually, the sound in your film is far more important than you can imagine... Based on my experience, client evaluations of your film have nothing to do with the size of your screen, but are directly proportional to the quality of the speakers used during playback! (Oops, leaked a secret), so value the sound in your film: dialogue, sound effects, music. These three elements will determine the soul of your film.

Principle Three: The equipment stays as long as you stay alive... even if you die, the equipment must remain intact.

The borrowed equipment is more important than your own. Remember this! So all equipment needs containers. Even if you don't have airline boxes or safe boxes, bags are essential. Recorders, cameras, and lenses must have dedicated bags. If your camera doesn't fit in a bag, the cinematographer must hold it at all times. (On sets, cameras are usually carried by assistants, even in cars, without trust in boxes.) Set a strict rule within the team: equipment cannot be left lying around carelessly. Lenses should only be on the camera or in the box. Recorders, microphones, and cables must be well managed, and never dropped. If the cinematographer eats, put the camera in the bag, or place large machines on the ground (within sight), never on elevated surfaces!

If possible, do maintenance every day after shooting. All equipment handlers should carry rain gear for their equipment (in bags). If shooting special scenes, plans must be made in advance. (Okay, I admit I once let an actor bump into the lens, which is wrong.)

3 Safety

This is no joke. If you make the cinematographer climb a tree, consider the consequences. If decided during location scouting, you must have a safety plan (stand under the tree? Who exactly do you want to rescue?). If involving action scenes, running scenes, etc., carefully, completely, and excessively consider safety measures! You have no budget for medical expenses, nor have you insured the crew. So if dangerous actions occur during filming, train the actors, take safety measures, and insure them. If it's a task for the crew, I suggest option 1: Let the producer do it. Option 2: That producer is you.

Why do it yourself? Because in the entire crew, you're the only one who can still complete the shoot if injured (a dead producer doesn't affect shooting). If hospitalized, your assistant director continues shooting. If just a fracture, get a cane and keep directing.

Not joking, you really need to consider these things.

Part 2: Shooting Matters

After thorough preparation, just follow the plan. Some points to note:

1. Stay highly tense and maintain speed

During filming, the director controls the team's atmosphere. If you're tense, they will be too. If you're relaxed, they will be as well. If you swear, the atmosphere becomes oppressive. If you're always casual, it naturally becomes more cheerful.

So, the director must maintain a serious, professional attitude. Naturally, everyone else will follow suit. I advocate maintaining constant tension and high speed. Due to low budget, the pressure to complete tasks is immense, so everyone must be pushed to their limits. Due to low budget, it's essential to be fast and not waste any time. If possible, make everyone run (safely). In Hong Kong and Hollywood, large crews often run, and there's a reason for that.

Appropriate motivation can imbalance the team, preventing complacency and maximizing efficiency. The faster your working pace, the more you accomplish. The team will notice. The more you complete, the more motivated everyone becomes, resulting in faster and more effective work. This is a virtuous cycle.

Especially for long-term shoots, losing morale is hard to recover, so maintaining high speed and tension is crucial. Never slack off.

2. Rest and Meals

Mentioned earlier were high speed and tension, so isn't this dehumanizing? Therefore, rest and meals must be prioritized. Many producers and directors hope for long working hours, but this is not only unscientific but also doesn't necessarily mean faster progress. Those who have worked with Hollywood crews notice their rest periods are regular and longer than ours, yet they work incredibly fast.

Balance is key.

But directors don't know this. Yes, in creative excitement, directors often work endlessly, skipping meals and sleep (at least those who want to make good films do). Overcome this mindset. After all, when to eat and rest is up to the director, ensuring the team's rest and meals is crucial. Sharp directors pay attention to the team's rhythm, knowing when to loosen and when to tighten. You can be jovial during breaks and serious during shooting, or vice versa. Each director has their own shooting personality, as long as you can unleash everyone's potential.

3 Technical Issues (Only Mentioning Commonly Overlooked Ones)

About the continuity person: You can do without an assistant director, but you must have a continuity person, otherwise, editing will be chaotic. True, a three-minute short film may not pose big problems, but chaos is not a good habit.

Besides recording shots, the continuity person handles many tasks, some already mentioned (see the preparation chapter). On set, besides taking photos, reminding the director of shots, and noting transitions, they record shots. Ideally, records should be detailed (shutter speed, aperture value, camera position, movement, timecode), but for low-budget productions, at least record scene numbers, shot numbers, and take numbers. Then clearly mark 'don't use', 'keep', or 'pass' (my habit is dividing them into F-takes, B-takes, and T-takes). Directors can add detailed notes for the continuity person: for instance, a specific part of a take can be used, or a particular word spoken well. Even if the expression in a T-take is perfect but the lines aren't smooth or the audio is bad, you can note to use the audio from a previous take. Of course, you need to clarify this with the continuity person.

If shooting with a 5D2, the continuity person needs to clap the slate and record the file name on the recorder. Our method is clapping the slate for every take, but only reporting the passed take's file name to the continuity person by the cinematographer and sound engineer. The sound engineer separately re-recorded dialogue, sound effects, and ambient sounds, all reported to the continuity person with file names. (If the sound engineer is professional, they should also maintain their own recording log.)

Regarding sound recording: Don't focus solely on the image while neglecting sound recording. Theoretically, you have monitoring capabilities. If the sound engineer is monitoring, concentrate on the actor's lines, noticing missed lines or awkward delivery, while technical issues are handled by the sound engineer. Don't ignore the sound engineer's feedback if they say the sound is bad. If noise levels are too high, rent wireless microphones. Don't rely on dubbing in post-production, just as you shouldn't rely on fixing mistakes with visual effects.

Regarding the process:

First, the order of arrival at the location should be... Okay, small teams arrive together. Large teams have unloading issues. Upon arriving at the location, the producer should designate areas for actor makeup and temporary equipment storage. At this point, rush to find camera positions and start as soon as possible. If actors need makeup, calculate your start time accordingly. (Men need about 20 minutes, women at least 40 minutes; if you have two female actors, leave early.) If actors are already made up, direct everyone to shoot the first scene immediately!

Each director has their own shooting habits. My suggestion is: after each take, immediately inform everyone about the blocking for the next shot (train the team to follow you closely whenever you mention the next shot; you might be looking for a position, but it's fine, they need to listen to your blocking instructions simultaneously, otherwise, you'd have to repeat them individually). After explaining the blocking, the cinematographer can set up the camera, lighting can be arranged, the sound engineer finds their position, and actors touch up makeup and rehearse lines. At this point, you need to carefully consider how to shoot and what to tell the actors. Once everyone is ready, walk through the blocking with the camera. Ideally, don't shoot unless the shot is extremely simple. Theoretically, the first take is often wasted, so walking through it saves time clapping the slate. Then, you'll discover issues with the actor's lines or performance, microphone entering the frame, microphone shadows, camera lens flare, poor prop placement, etc. Resolve these issues at this moment, and then start shooting!

4 Team Management

This is related to the production department, but in small teams, you're the leader, so managing personnel is an indispensable task. You need to coordinate relationships and promote work, minimizing conflicts as much as possible. Your team members represent your production, so some principles should be determined in advance:

1 Maintain polite and humble attitudes towards outsiders

Many people feel superior when shooting, which is the main reason ordinary people have negative views of production teams. You can't treat others rudely just because you're filming. If isolation or blocking pedestrians is necessary, adopt a cooperative attitude rather than hostile expulsion. Pedestrians wandering isn't their fault, it's yours (you probably forgot to block that area). Prepare signs indicating filming is in progress, where spectators can stand, and request silence due to audio recording. Most people aren't unreasonable; they're just curious. If you scold them, they might cause trouble.

2 Leave a good impression on collaborators

Anything borrowed must be properly cared for and recorded. Before shooting at someone else's location, take photos to restore the setting afterward. If it's wooden flooring, attach foot pads to heavy equipment (budget? Do you have thick double-sided tape?). Don't use items from the location without asking (even if you want to use their water dispenser, ask first; it's respect). If items are damaged, compensate according to price, even if the owner declines. If impossible, offer equivalent items as gifts. Keep the shooting location clean and prepare garbage bags. In public places, avoid loud noises.

Leaving a good impression on location providers is crucial. You might need to return for reshoots or future projects. Avoid one-time deals; it's industry karma (often difficult collaborations stem from poor impressions left by previous crews).
