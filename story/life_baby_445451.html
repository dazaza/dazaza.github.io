Methods to help children remove sand after playing with sand_Tianya Blog_The knowledgeable are here_Tianya Community

Playing with sand can enhance a child's hands-on ability, improve parent-child relationships, and maximize the creativity and imagination of children. However, when playing in the sandbox, what should you do if sand gets into the child's mouth, nose, or ears?

1. Sand gets into the mouth

Remind your child not to touch their mouth or put their hands into their mouth while playing with sand. But, unless the child has experienced the grittiness of sand, they won't heed your warning. Especially for children around 1 year old, who are at an age where they tend to put everything into their mouths, they will certainly want to taste the sand!

1) Try to let them spit out the sand in their mouth as much as possible.

2) Wet a cotton handkerchief and use it to wipe the sand out of their mouth. Once they realize that sand is "not tasty," they won't put sand in their mouth again.

2. Sand gets into the eyes

After sand gets into the eyes, forcefully rubbing the eyes is a dangerous action that can harm the eyes. When sand enters the eyes, not only is it difficult to open the eyes, but it also causes pain. At this time, if you rub with your hands, the cornea can easily be scratched by sharp-edged sand particles or dust, leading to blurred vision, and severe cases may cause corneal damage.

1) Rinse the eyes with plenty of warm water or saline solution.

2) Use gauze soaked in saline solution or mineral water to clean from the inner corner of the eye near the nose toward the outer corner. After cleaning one eye, change the gauze to clean the other eye.

3) If the child still feels discomfort in their eyes, try to make them cry, as tears can flush out residual sand particles.

4) If you cannot remove the foreign object yourself or find the object on the cornea, go to the hospital immediately to have the doctor remove it, avoiding further trauma or infection due to improper methods.

3. Sand gets into the nostrils

1) First, use a handkerchief to wipe away sand from the shallower parts of the nostrils. If the child already knows how to blow their nose, let them blow hard to expel the sand.

2) Roll up cotton or a handkerchief into a thin strip, soak it thoroughly in saline solution, gently insert it into the nostril, and rotate the cotton strip to bring out the sand. Be sure not to insert it too deeply. Change the cotton strip to clean the other nostril.

4. Sand gets into the buttocks

Even when playing with sand in a cool place, children can work up a sweat. This way, fine sand can easily stick to the creases of the baby’s bottom, causing itching and irritation, sometimes even leading to skin inflammation.

1) Do not let the child play with sand bare-bottomed or wearing open-crotch pants.

2) Very fine sand might get into the diaper, especially when the diaper or swimsuit is wet. After playing with sand and bathing, change the child into a clean diaper or shorts.

3) After bathing, apply moisturizing cream to their bottom. The moisturizing cream can prevent dermatitis and relieve redness.

5. Sand gets into the ears

Do not let the child use their fingers to dig in their ears, as this will push the sand deeper into the ear. Wrap a cotton swab around your little finger, moisten it with saline solution or warm water, and have the child tilt their head sideways while you gently wipe away the sand from their ears. To keep them still, give them a toy to play with.

1) Clean the earlobe and behind the ear with a cotton ball.

2) Do not use cotton swabs to clean the ears. Also, do not insert the cotton swab into the ear canal to avoid it falling in and blocking the ear canal.

Children seem to be naturally fond of playing with sand. Even timid children who are frightened by the waves of the sea on the beach still love sand. Perhaps this is a gift from nature to children, and this natural material is no less entertaining than expensive toys. Perhaps this is the closest contact with nature, allowing children to become so engrossed and reluctant to leave.
