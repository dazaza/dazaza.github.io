After a Hundred Days with Google Glass: Not Easy to Use, Best for Taking Pictures

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:14:41

"The test of a horse's strength is in a long journey, and the test of a person's heart is in time." In fact, this is similar to the electronic devices you buy; first impressions do not determine the value reflected during long-term use. Google Glass sparked extensive discussions last year and this year, but that was just the excitement brought on by the initial impression.

If we want to understand Google Glass more comprehensively, it’s necessary to have an article detailing the user experience after prolonged use. Joshua Merrill from ReadWrite has been wearing Google Glass for 100 days, and perhaps he can tell us some facts about this crossover device.

He told us that the feeling given by the hardware of Google Glass is actually similar to what was previously reported by the media: lightweight, unobtrusive design, great display effect even under bright daylight, small processor and camera components. However, battery life is the bottleneck; under heavy usage, it needs recharging every one and a half hours.

In real life, Google Glass attracts a lot of attention no matter where you go or who you encounter—whether young or old, rich or poor, tech enthusiasts or technophobes, even those who have never heard of Google Glass all want to try it on. Merrill's Google Glass has photos of 300 different people stored in it.

Merrill found that for “first-time users,” Google Glass is not as easy to use as smartphones or tablets. Due to the lack of clear visual guidance, it takes some practice to get used to operating Google Glass. As a result, these people will exclaim "Wow" when they first see Google Glass, but after two minutes, they start to feel confused. Merrill believes that this is because Google Glass lacks operational prompts, making it difficult for people to form a unified behavioral cognition.

And as previously reported, in terms of social etiquette, people are not yet ready to accept Google Glass. In hotels, on dates, and during conversations, he had to take off his Google Glass.

During the 100 days of use, Merrill found several ways in which Google Glass could make life more convenient, one of which is "taking photos." He installed the Winky app developed by Mike DiGiovanni, which allows users to take pictures by blinking, greatly increasing the number of photos he took. As a result, he created a pictorial record of his life. Additionally, Google Glass made the act of searching simple; just hold down the touchpad on the glasses, voice input your question, and you’ll get the answer.

In the July update of Google Glass, a new web browser was added with the function of reading web pages aloud. Afterwards, Merrill began using this browser to read news and IMDb movie reviews aloud, finding that it improved efficiency significantly. However, this also increased the power consumption of Google Glass. Moreover, "translation" was another impressive feature; he used Google Glass to translate foreign phrases smoothly, as the foreign language text turned into English and was displayed on the screen. Nevertheless, this technology still needs further improvement.

Google Glass is good, but not good enough. Its functions overlap with most of the functions of a phone, but it can only accomplish half of what a phone can do. Merrill feels that the problem with Google Glass lies in its failure to fully utilize its unique elements, and what it truly needs is an "augmented reality" platform, allowing the glasses to become a complete bridge connecting the virtual and the real world.
