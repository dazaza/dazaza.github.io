After iOS 7, is it spring for native apps?

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 14:36:54

　　iOS 7 has just been released, and various new features have become the focus of media and public attention. Whether it's the controversial "flat" design, or the control center that closely resembles Android, or the brand-new peripheral sharing app Airdrop, they all spark lively discussions. However, among the hundreds of new features added, one particular feature caught my interest. Although it rarely appeared in the media and was scarcely mentioned during the live broadcast, it was only briefly touched upon in Apple's iOS 7 promotional materials – "Apps Near Me".

　　As Apple's promotion suggests, you can find a list of apps that may be useful to you within the App Store. This function further refines the use scenarios for apps. One can imagine how location-based and relevant information-recommended apps will help tourists and business travelers quickly familiarize themselves with local information. At the same time, independent developers outside major cities can leverage this feature to launch more localized applications compared to general software (such as WeChat, Dazhong Dianping) and achieve success.

　　For small companies or independent developers, they often face the challenge of their applications being overshadowed by larger competitors. After all, well-known software like WeChat and Yelp tend to erode the market share of similar local applications worldwide, even discouraging some developers. However, while large and comprehensive applications seem powerful in terms of functionality and coverage, they actually encounter various issues when it comes to localization. It's just that local developers have retreated, leading to the current monopolistic reality.

　　This optimization of the App Store recommendation mechanism by Apple can fully unleash the "long-tail demand" in the vast world of applications. In many places, some software can meet specific needs (for example, at the Louvre, related introductory apps and art exhibition apps). The current entire mobile application market clearly cannot achieve such an effect.

　　For some app developers, as long as they identify the demand and deeply explore user needs, the applications they create will "make the list" in recommendations based on geographical location and related information. This way, developers who are squeezed by large companies and limited by marketing budgets can focus their efforts on creating more refined applications to benefit users.

　　For example, Yelp is internationally renowned, benefiting from the strong U.S. market and the activities of American tourists around the world, making Yelp a global food and location review application. One could say that in the U.S. and some countries in Europe, Yelp can serve as your guide for dining and travel. However, in China, people are not very interested in Yelp; local applications like Dianping and Fanfan obviously better suit Chinese habits. Of course, here it is mainly due to language differences and the timing of promotions. But beyond first- and second-tier cities, many food review applications do not cover third- and fourth-tier cities and towns well. This presents an opportunity for local developers.

　　It is certain that the new features in the App Store of iOS 7 will eventually appear on other platforms. This method of recommending applications will also benefit developers on the Android and Windows Phone platforms. To date, manufacturers' approaches to enhancing user experience have tended to be similar, with system logics like control centers and notification centers permeating between manufacturers. Ultimately, the beneficiaries are ordinary consumers and developers whose development difficulties have been reduced. This is why I believe this "Popular Apps Around Me" will significantly impact the industry.

　　How can small companies and independent developers compete with market giants, gain a share of the local market, or even defeat foreign applications to dominate the market? Should we always shy away from markets already ventured into by giants?

　　The traditional coffee industry might offer us some insights.

　　In a response on the Q&A site Quora titled "How to compete with Starbucks in the coffee industry," the author uses Australia as an example to introduce how over 6500 independent coffee shops dominate more than 95% of the coffee market. In 2000, Starbucks ambitiously opened 84 chain stores, but eight years later, it had to close 61 of them. Similarly, Gloria Jeans, another Australian local coffee chain, faced similar challenges. How did numerous small coffee shops defeat almost invincible Starbucks?

The author, with experience in founding, owning, and managing 15 coffee shops, shares the following insights:

1. Do not follow Starbucks’ gimmicks; focus solely on pure espresso, which is the foundation of the business. Whether drinking it alone or using it to make lattes, cappuccinos, mochas, or others, pure espresso is essential. Starbucks, however, advocates a coffee revolution worldwide by adding caramel and milk foam, which actually results in terrible taste.

2. Learn from Italy; in the coffee homeland of Italy, Starbucks has no foothold. Focusing on coffee and coffee culture is crucial, whereas Starbucks merely represents fast-food culture.

3. Quality is above everything; this is the biggest rule to attract customers.

4. Make your coffee shop warm and comfortable, so customers feel "like home."

5. Let your customers love the coffee shop as if it were their own property. This way, they will feel unique in the coffee shop, point out deficiencies, bring new guests, and proudly say things like "this is my turf."

6. Employees at Starbucks find it hard to connect with customers, and their corporate culture does not encourage such interactions. Independent coffee shops, however, can engage with customers, thereby increasing customer loyalty.

7. Avoid opening coffee shops in tourist attractions, because these are exactly the locations favored by international fast-food giants like Starbucks. Not only is it difficult to match their rent, but passing tourists won't become repeat customers.

8. Don't emulate Starbucks' high-income, high-rent policy. This point echoes all the previous views: certain corporate behaviors of Starbucks are unsuitable for small coffee shops to follow, whether frequently changing coffee varieties or opening stores in high-traffic, expensive rental areas.

　　I’m sure sensitive readers will notice that there are many ideas here that can be applied to local applications. Consider Tencent, NetEase, and other large companies as the "Starbucks" of the application world. How can we create localized, distinctive applications that overlap with their domains?

　　Do we avoid everything Tencent does? If they follow our lead, should we stop doing it too?

　　Please! Have some backbone!

　　iOS 7 may not be a groundbreaking system, but just the "Popular Apps Around Me" feature alone can influence the entire application development field. Many talented programmers don’t necessarily have to leave their hometowns and crowd into big cities or stay in the shadow of large companies. Instead, they can discover new opportunities under the concept of "popular demands around me" and confront competition from large companies with characteristic, humanized local applications.

　　By transforming the perspective of "How to compete with Starbucks in the coffee industry" for local applications, it roughly translates to:

　　1. Do not chase after trendy functions of popular software; focus solely on your core value—how to enhance user experience and improve users' quality of life. The "plane shooting" game in WeChat performed well, but you don’t necessarily need to copy it or envy it. You have your own core advantages waiting to be discovered.

　　2. Despite the dominance of strong players in the application world, fresh and interesting software continues to emerge. They don't carry titles like "Chinese version of Instagram"; instead, they carefully capture user needs and implement satisfying functions. Even when learning from excellent foreign software, don't rush to "copy first," but rather understand how they approach it.

　　3. Similarly, quality trumps everything. No matter how excellent your idea is, frequent crashes will drive away your users.

　　4. Localization, localization, localization—this is the heart of this article. With the nearby popular recommendation function in mobile app stores, this is a new opportunity. Developers don't necessarily need to promote products on social media; the key for localized applications is how to engage people nearby and users in surrounding areas. Go out and tell them how useful and fun your application is.

　　5. Avoid developing hot applications, as these are mature markets. Unless you have unparalleled ideas and execution capabilities, it's best to steer clear of such fields. For instance, communication software is difficult to localize.

　　6. Don't imitate the "bad habits" of large companies in the early stages of development. "Small, fast, zero" is your advantage. Real-time changes in strategy and improving user satisfaction are top priorities; everything else doesn't matter much. I've seen many startup teams "without the fate of a Fortune 500 company but suffering from its diseases."

　　After tonight, perhaps app development will open up a new path—a road lush with greenery and gentle breezes. Under the new recommendation mechanism, I believe many developers can break free from the influence of large companies and trends, focus on localization, and carve out their own space.

　　Go for it!
