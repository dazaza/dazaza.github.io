Effective use of public relations strategy skills

by lsj880820 on 2012-02-21 12:28:06

Seven Techniques of PR Strategies 1) Focus on the newsworthiness attributes of your news. The news hook is an important piece of information that triggers actual interest among editors and journalists, rather than contrived or self-serving content that you think the media should be interested in. You will have greater success in securing news coverage if your announcement aligns with a timely news hook, highlighting information of genuine interest rather than being based on puffery. 2) Keep your PR toolkit up-to-date. As basic tools for journalist-friendly interactions, these include: well-written press releases, media/press kits (both online and in print), live reports, Q&A documents, background materials and historical files, bios of key employees, milestone recaps and relevant timelines, photography (high-resolution required for print reproduction), and technical documents such as white papers and case studies. The more resources you can provide to an interested journalist, the more likely it is that they will produce thorough, accurate, and engaging coverage. 3) Leverage wire services. Wire services—effectively chosen, written, and timed—can amplify the volume of media attention for your announcements. Professional distribution services—such as PR Newswire, Business Wire, WebPR, PR.com, and PR Leap—can enhance the exposure of your press releases to both media and potential customers, with your product or service releases still posted online for extended reach. 4) Become proficient in email campaigns. Email, overall, is the preferred communication method for busy journalists over unsolicited phone calls, text messages, or podcasts. Where do you get email addresses? Check the contact sections of media websites or subscribe to media contact data sources such as Bacon's MediaSource, Burrelles Luce MediaContacts, or Bullseye PR’s MediaBase. 5) Utilize information services. Information services are special PR tools that allow deadline-driven journalists to access in-depth business and personal information along with interview opportunities. Examples of such services available by subscription include PR Newswire's ProfNet and PRSourceCode. Below is an example of a recent ProfNet query: "I am writing a story about national business broadly titled 'Sales 2.0.' The article explores some of the new tools like LinkedIn, Jigsaw, and other Web 2.0 high-tech things that are changing how companies' sales prospects look, connect, sell, close sales, and then keep clients satisfied. I'm specifically interested in speaking with small and medium-sized enterprises (1,000 employees or fewer)." 6) Share your expertise through articles. Bylined articles, like the one you're reading now, occur when you write pieces for print media in your area of expertise (often at the invitation of an editor). Opportunities range from 250-word opinion pieces to 2,000-word features. Typically, a short recap at the end of the article outlines the author's credentials, mentions their company or organization name and affiliation, and provides readers with website contact information. 7) Awards attract attention. Receiving an industry or community award is a legitimate news hook that can help capture the attention of editors and journalists, ultimately leading to valuable media exposure. Highlighting the award gives recipients a reason to expand on a specific topic, providing additional information and relevant photography. Quite frequently, receiving one or more industry awards enhances the "awareness-raising" effect, safeguarding profiles or securing full features about the individual or the related company or organization.
