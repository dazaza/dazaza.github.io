World Bank president holds meeting in Beijing amid protests

by loved7k9 on 2012-02-29 16:12:46

Yesterday, World Bank President Robert Zoellick held a press conference in Beijing, which was unexpectedly disrupted by an on-site protest from independent scholar Du Jianguo. In response, Zoellick stated that the World Bank welcomes controversy and discussion.

Regarding the suggestion in the report that "China needs to undergo fundamental strategic changes," Zoellick said he estimates that China's reforms will not be sudden or explosive but will begin with pilot programs.

■ Scene Report

Zoellick Responds to External Controversy

Yesterday morning, at the Phase II of Guangzhou International Trade Center, the World Bank's office in China, President Zoellick held a press conference for domestic and international media. Zoellick, who will step down in June, answered various hot questions concerning the controversial research report released two days ago by the World Bank and the Development Research Center of the State Council titled *China 2030* (shortened as [World Bank Report]).

The World Bank Report states that China faces various risks domestically and internationally; past linear growth is unsustainable. China has reached a turning point and needs another fundamental strategic change. The report provides six prescriptions for China’s development over the next 20 years, including transitioning to a market economy.

Regarding the highly debated issue of breaking state-owned enterprise monopolies, Zoellick mentioned yesterday that Chinese SOEs have benefited significantly in many ways, including cheap financing, advantageous market positions, and substantial retained earnings. However, these benefits have not been passed on to ordinary citizens. "To lower China's savings rate and benefit more Chinese people, SOEs should return more profits to society. Structural adjustments are needed to establish a sound social security system." Zoellick added, "China’s own development experience and global development experiences show that further increasing competition can improve efficiency, productivity levels, and bring higher wage incomes."

Zoellick predicts that vested interests within the current system will resist reforms. "However, Chinese leaders focus not on certain interest groups but on all the Chinese people." Regarding China's reforms, Zoellick said he expects them not to be sudden or explosive but to start with pilots, gradually expanding, much like the reform and opening-up policy decades ago.

About his life after leaving office, Zoellick said he currently has no specific plans and hopes to fully dedicate himself to the World Bank's work before stepping down.

Independent Scholar Makes an Appearance to Protest

During yesterday's press conference, just as the host was introducing the participants, a well-dressed man wearing glasses suddenly entered the venue. He introduced himself as "independent Chinese scholar Du Jianguo" and then stood in front of the podium saying, "The World Bank's report is poison to China and will bring no benefits to China's economy or its people." Subsequently, while expressing his views, he distributed materials printed with his opinions to the attendees.

For the unexpected protester, the journalists present had mixed reactions—some were surprised, others puzzled, and some amused. Photographers, however, were delighted and quickly took photos. In contrast, Zoellick remained calm, seemingly unfazed by the disruption. Soon, a robust foreigner at the scene "carried" Du Jianguo out of the venue, and the press conference continued.

Du Jianguo did not leave but waited outside the World Bank's office in China. After the press conference ended, he continued distributing materials to the journalists and gave interviews to the media.

■ Dialogue with the Parties Involved

Du Jianguo

"The World Bank Report Made Me Increasingly Angry"

Q: Can you briefly introduce yourself?

A: I am an independent scholar, not employed by any fixed organization. Previously, I worked as a magazine editor but since 2011, I have specialized in commentary. I am interested in economic issues and have written articles or commentary for many magazines, such as Fudan University's *Chinese Social Sciences Quarterly*, *Global Times*, *China Reform News*, and *State-Owned Enterprises* magazine.

Q: What was the initial intention behind your protest?

A: A few days ago, I noticed the World Bank's material (referring to the World Bank Report). Yesterday (on the 27th), I found related reports online, and the more I read, the angrier I became. This led me to decide to protest on-site and write the protest letter. I knew about Zoellick's schedule a few days ago because it was announced by the World Bank. Thus, I took several subway rides early this morning and arrived at the venue before 9 o'clock.

Q: How do you view being "carried out" of the venue by the World Bank staff?

A: It was normal; that’s their job. The World Bank staff were very polite.

(Note: Du Jianguo - Independent Scholar, Economic Commentator)

Liu Shijin

Some Content of the Report Has Been Misinterpreted

Q: The World Bank Report faced strong protests. As one of the direct organizers and co-authors of the report, what would you like to say?

A: We plan to hold a press conference where we will address questions regarding our report.

Q: When will it happen?

A: We are too busy now, but it will be in a few days. Some media outlets or individuals have misinterpreted the content of the report. If you read the original text, it will be clear. The World Bank has already published the original English version of the report.

Q: Will the report make revisions to the parts that have sparked significant controversy?

A: No, the basic content has been finalized. The main task now is editing.

(Note: Liu Shijin - Deputy Director of the Development Research Center of the State Council, Member of the World Bank Report Task Force, One of the Authors)

Zoellick

Welcomes Underground Controversy and Discussion

Q: What do you think is the importance of this report?

A: The importance of this report depends on how China ultimately uses it. An essential point everyone needs to remember is that the final decisions are made by China itself. Eighteen months ago, when I first proposed conducting this joint research project, I received support from Chinese leaders. Last September, I came to Guangzhou and discussed the preliminary draft of the report with everyone. The reason it took so long to announce publicly was because the Chinese side conducted extensive internal consultations and discussions. I hope these internal consultations and discussions contribute to improving the report.

Q: You've seen someone express differing opinions through action...

A: Frankly, this is not new to me. As a former U.S. Trade Representative, I often dealt with complaints and protests. So, I will ask the translator to translate the materials given to us by this gentleman and understand the situation. We are accustomed to underground controversies and discussions. We hope to understand more situations through such methods and improve accordingly. I just hope that when people raise requests, they can do so in a way that allows us to express and discuss together.

(Note: Robert Zoellick - President of the World Bank)

■ Expert Commentary

Du Jianguo Does Not Understand the World Bank Report and Has Not Conducted Deep Research

◎ Yuan Gangming, Researcher at Tsinghua University's Center for China and Global Economy: I completely disagree with Du Jianguo's statements and views. Du Jianguo does not understand this report and has not conducted deep research. Such behavior has fallen behind the changes in domestic reforms and the changing global economic situation. Taking the issue of state-owned enterprises as an example, the proportion of state-owned enterprises in our country once showed a declining trend, but in recent years, it has been rising continuously, which is not a good sign and reflects the slowness of reforms. The proportion of state-owned enterprises cannot continue to rise indefinitely and must be effectively controlled.

◎ Li Chang'an, Associate Professor at the School of Public Administration at the University of International Business and Economics, Postdoctoral Fellow at the Institute of Economics of the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences: Du Jianguo's actions were somewhat extreme. Actually, the voices calling for privatization of state-owned economies have always existed and should be viewed rationally. Currently, the proportion of state-owned economies continues to rise, and I believe structural adjustments are necessary, appropriately reducing the scope of state-owned enterprises. State-owned enterprises need to know their limits, advancing where appropriate and retreating where necessary, rather than simply preserving value or increasing it. Existing state-owned enterprise policies indeed fail to consider the impact on private enterprises.

Protest Letter by Du Jianguo

(Excerpt)

(The second prescription given by the World Bank Report to China) is the privatization of state-owned enterprises. China's state-owned enterprises are currently running well, mainly due to their own efforts and competitive advantages, not government preferences or monopoly positions. At the same time, the treatment of employees in state-owned enterprises is slightly better than in private enterprises. Therefore, there is no reason to privatize state-owned enterprises. Privatizing state-owned enterprises would, on the one hand, disrupt the rapid and powerful momentum of China's overall economy, and on the other hand, make a few people rich overnight, appropriating wealth originally belonging to all the people. It would also lead to mass layoffs among workers... The reform direction of China's state-owned enterprises is certainly not privatization but strengthening supervision, maximizing profits for the benefit of all citizens, while increasing the income of grassroots employees and reducing income disparities within enterprises.

The third prescription in the report is lowering barriers to entry for private enterprises, while requiring state-owned enterprises to pay more taxes and profits, thereby increasing the burden on public enterprises in terms of social revenue. This is deliberately stirring up conflicts between state-owned and private enterprises. There are no so-called "barriers" in most sectors of China's economy, and private enterprises do not oppose state-owned enterprises. In some areas where state-owned enterprises dominate, it is solely due to their excellent performance, not because private enterprises are forbidden from entering. For example, in the aviation and high-speed rail sectors, private enterprises perform poorly primarily due to a lack of technical accumulation. Private enterprises have long enjoyed de facto tax breaks, which can be clearly seen by comparing the tax payments of state-owned and private enterprises. Favoring private enterprises and reducing taxes will, on the one hand, destroy the principle of equal competition in the market and harm the spirit of enterprise innovation, and on the other hand, lead to polarization, similar to the situation in the United States. The World Bank is making China repeat America's mistakes.

World Bank and Zoellick, you are selling the poisons that have already failed in the West and other developing countries to China. You are sincerely destroying China's economy and attempting to let a few Chinese people and Wall Street plunder the Chinese people. Poisoners and scammers, go back to America!
