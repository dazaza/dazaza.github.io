Steel Classification - Building Materials Knowledge - Industry News - Building Materials Network Reinforcing bars are steel materials in the form of straight bars or coils used for reinforcing in reinforced concrete structures. Their shapes are divided into two types: smooth round bars and deformed bars. The delivery states are straight bars and coil bars. Smooth round bars are essentially small round bars and coils made from ordinary low-carbon steel. Deformed bars are ribbed bars, usually with two longitudinal ribs and transverse ribs distributed uniformly along their length. The cross-sectional shape of the transverse ribs can be spiral, chevron, or crescent-shaped. They are indicated by the nominal diameter in millimeters. The nominal diameter of a deformed bar is equivalent to that of a smooth round bar with an equal cross-sectional area. The nominal diameters of reinforcing bars range from 8-50 mm, with recommended diameters of 8, 12, 16, 20, 25, 32, and 40 mm. In reinforced concrete, reinforcing bars primarily bear tensile stress. Due to the role of the ribs, deformed bars have greater bonding strength with concrete, thus better resisting external forces. Reinforcing bars are widely used in various building structures, especially large, heavy, light, thin-walled, and high-rise building structures.

I-beams, also known as steel beams, are long strips of steel with an I-shaped cross-section. Their specifications are expressed as waist height (h) * flange width (b) * waist thickness (d) in millimeters, such as "I160*88*6", which indicates an I-beam with a waist height of 160 mm, flange width of 88 mm, and waist thickness of 6 mm. I-beam specifications can also be represented by model numbers, where the model number represents the waist height in centimeters, like I16#. For I-beams with the same waist height but different flange widths and waist thicknesses, suffixes a, b, c are added to distinguish them, such as 32a#, 32b#, 32c#, etc. I-beams are categorized into standard I-beams and light I-beams, with hot-rolled standard I-beam sizes ranging from 10-63#. Sizes available upon agreement between supplier and buyer range from 12-55#. I-beams are extensively used in various building structures, bridges, vehicles, supports, machinery, etc.

Diamond pattern plates, also known as mesh pattern plates, are steel plates with a diamond or raised pattern on their surface. The anti-slip properties of these plates make them suitable for use as floors, factory ladders, workbench treads, ship decks, car bottoms, etc. The specifications of diamond pattern plates are indicated by the basic thickness (excluding the raised part), with ten specifications ranging from 2.5-8 mm. These plates are rolled from grade 1-3 class B ordinary carbon structural steel, with chemical compositions complying with GB700 "Ordinary Carbon Structural Steel Technical Conditions".

Round bars refer to solid long steel materials with a circular cross-section. Their specifications are indicated by the diameter in millimeters, such as "50" indicating a round bar with a diameter of 50 mm. Round bars come in three types: hot-rolled, forged, and cold-drawn. Hot-rolled round bars range in size from 5.5-250 mm. Among these, small round bars with diameters of 5.5-25 mm are mostly supplied in bundles as straight bars and are commonly used as reinforcement bars, bolts, and various mechanical parts; round bars larger than 25 mm are mainly used for manufacturing mechanical parts or as seamless pipe blanks.

Steel plates are flat steel products with a large width-to-thickness ratio and surface area. Plates thinner than 4 mm are classified as thin plates. In practice, plates with thicknesses of 20-60 mm are called thick plates, and those thicker than 60 mm are called extra-thick plates, collectively referred to as medium-thick plates. Narrower and longer plates are called steel strips and listed as a separate category. Steel plates have significant coverage and containment capabilities and can be used as roof panels, covering materials, and for making containers, oil tanks, packaging boxes, train cars, car bodies, industrial furnace shells, etc. They can be cut and combined according to usage requirements to form various structural components and mechanical parts, further expanded into welded structural steels, bent and pressed into shapes for boilers, containers, car bodies, household utensils, tools, and serve as raw materials for welded pipes and cold-formed steels. Due to these characteristics, more than 50% of total steel production consists of steel plates.

To meet the needs of large-scale infrastructure construction, there has been a sharp increase in demand for steel plates in industrial products and high-end durable consumer goods. To adapt to the new situation, China's steel plate production has significantly developed in recent years, with advanced steel plate production backbone enterprises like Wuhan Iron and Steel Corporation being established. Steel plates are supplied either in sheets or rolls. Sheet steel plate specifications are expressed in terms of thickness * width * length in millimeters. Familiarity with the specifications of plates and strips allows for efficient utilization of material width and length, improving material utilization rates, reducing unnecessary edge scraps, and lowering labor time and product costs. When purchasing plates and strips, it is advisable to select specifications that are whole multiples of product blanks. If the product is standardized, ordering plates and strips close to the required length as fixed-length or multiple-length materials and adopting a nested cutting method can significantly improve the utilization rate of plates and strips. Reusing edge scraps between enterprises and industries has also been proven to be an effective method for improving material utilization and saving materials.

Channel steel is a long strip of steel with a U-shaped cross-section. Its specifications are indicated as follows: for example, 120*53*5, representing channel steel with a waist height of 120 mm, flange width of 53 mm, and waist thickness of 5 mm, or also referred to as 12# channel steel. For channel steel with the same waist height but different flange widths and waist thicknesses, suffixes a, b, c are added to differentiate them, such as 25a#, 25b#, 25c#, etc. Channel steel is divided into standard channel steel and light channel steel, with hot-rolled standard channel steel sizes ranging from 5-40#. Sizes available upon agreement between supplier and buyer range from 6.5-30#. Channel steel is mainly used in building structures, vehicle manufacturing, and other industrial structures, often in conjunction with I-beams.

Angle steel, commonly known as angle iron, is a long strip of steel with two sides perpendicular to each other forming an L-shape. It is divided into equal leg angles and unequal leg angles. Equal leg angles have two sides of equal width. Their specifications are expressed as side width x side width x side thickness in millimeters. For example, "∠30x30x3" indicates an equal leg angle steel with a side width of 30 mm and a side thickness of 3 mm. Specifications can also be represented by model numbers, where the model number is the side width in centimeters, such as ∠3# . Model numbers do not indicate the dimensions of different side thicknesses within the same model, so contracts and other documents should fully specify the side width and side thickness to avoid using only the model number alone. Hot-rolled equal leg angle steel specifications range from 2#-20#. Angle steel can be assembled into various load-bearing components according to structural needs and can also be used as connecting pieces between components. They are widely used in various building structures and engineering structures, such as roof beams, bridges, power transmission towers, lifting and transportation machinery, ships, industrial furnaces, reaction towers, container racks, and warehouse shelves.
