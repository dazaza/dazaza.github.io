Qiao Jiangnan's "recycled oil scandal" reveals the risks of expansion in chain catering_8082

by dkycjk24 on 2011-10-28 17:52:26

For Qiao Nanjiang, which announced its intention to go public many years ago, the past month has been a "black September." After the revelation on September 2nd that dead fish were being swapped for live ones at its Qingdao branch, and following accusations of gutter oil usage at its Nanjing branch on September 17th, recent conflicts over the buyback of franchise stores have continued to escalate. The underlying issues in quality supervision and management at Qiao Nanjiang have gradually come under scrutiny.

After experiencing this "black September," Qiao Nanjiang's path to going public will undoubtedly be even longer. Similarly, Xiao Nanguo, which had already publicly disclosed its prospectus and seemed poised to list with ease, unexpectedly canceled its Hong Kong IPO last week. The journey for catering companies to go public still appears fraught with difficulties. Industry experts believe that the catering industry has consistently struggled to break free from the curse of "difficulty in going public," and the lack of standardized management may be one of the key problems.

Repurchasing franchise stores to pave the way for listing

In September, Qiao Nanjiang staged what could be described as an outstanding drama in the catering industry. Following the revelation on September 2nd of food safety issues such as swapping dead fish for live ones at its Qingdao branch, on September 17th, its Nanjing 1912 branch became embroiled in a "recycled oil scandal." This franchise was accused of using leftover oil after frying food to cook meals for employees. As Qiao Nanjiang's first national franchise, the Nanjing store's controversy sent ripples through the industry. The incident eventually escalated into a war of words between Qiao Nanjiang and its franchisee. Qiao Nanjiang claimed that it had terminated the franchising relationship, while the franchisee countered by alleging unequal treatment between franchise and company-owned stores.

Founder Zhang Lan initiated franchising three years ago, but possibly out of consideration to strengthen brand control, Qiao Nanjiang began gradually reclaiming franchises last year. Earlier this year and in July, Qiao Nanjiang successively terminated franchising agreements with franchisees in Qingdao and Guiyang. It is understood that currently, Qiao Nanjiang operates more than 50 branches nationwide and has five franchises in Baotou, Taiyuan, Qinhuangdao, and other places. However, these franchises are not included in Qiao Nanjiang's official map of branches.

"First expand the brand's visibility and market share through chain franchising, then seek to go public. Before going public, catering companies need to attract capital investors who invest funds in exchange for a certain percentage of equity, with the amount of equity tied to the company's assets. Since franchises are opened by franchisees, their revenue and profits are not included in the company's financial statements, so capital parties usually require the company to separate franchises from overall assets. This explains why many catering companies repurchase franchises before going public," said Li Wei Hua, a well-known franchising expert and doctoral candidate at China University of Political Science and Law's Business School, during an interview with our publication. He believed there was a possibility that Qiao Nanjiang's repurchase of franchises was aimed at paving the way for its listing. In March this year, UBS Securities, Qiao Nanjiang's underwriter, submitted a listing application to the issuance department of the CSRC, but no written feedback has been received so far.

Repurchasing franchises can enhance a company's internal control capabilities, reduce audit nodes, and make it easier to go public. Xiao Fuyang, which is already listed in Hong Kong, went through a similar process. Xiao Fuyang achieved rapid expansion through franchising, with the number of franchises peaking at over 700. To address management issues caused by rapid expansion, Xiao Fuyang began closing, rectifying, and acquiring its "scattered" franchises in phases. By the time of its listing, Xiao Fuyang had only 350 stores left, with 103 company-owned stores and 247 franchises.

The bumpy road to listing for catering companies

Qiao Nanjiang's setback in listing is just one reflection of the difficulties faced by catering chains seeking to go public. There have been reports that the CSRC raised the listing standards for catering chains, specifically increasing the annual profit requirement for proposed listings from 30 million RMB to 50 million RMB. Although this information has not been officially confirmed, it is undeniable that the threshold for catering companies to go public is getting higher.

Shunfeng Group, a Cantonese cuisine enterprise, and the old-established "Goubuli" both applied for A-share listings in 2010, but to no avail. Jingya Group, backed by a strong private equity team, remains excluded from the A-share market.
