Why Tencent and Chery are fighting for the QQ trademark: neither can afford to lose

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 13:56:14

Does Tencent produce cars? The answer is no, but as early as 2005, the company applied to register the "QQ" trademark on Class 12 goods such as cars, which was approved in 2008. However, this move upset Chery Automobile Co., Ltd., a car manufacturer. In 2009, Chery filed a request with the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board (TRAB) to cancel Tencent's automobile-related trademarks on the grounds of "identical or similar trademarks for similar goods," and received support. After its trademark was revoked, Tencent sued the TRAB. Yesterday, the Beijing No. 1 Intermediate People's Court heard the case, with plaintiff Tencent, defendant TRAB, and third party Chery appearing in court.

It is well known that in China, companies associated with "QQ" are mainly Tencent Technology and Chery Automobile, each operating in different fields. Tencent holds a significant position in the internet sector, while Chery's QQ car has sold over a million units. From the chronological order of using "QQ," Tencent launched its instant messaging software QQ in 1999, and Chery released its microcar, Chery QQ, in 2003. The question of who should own the rights to "QQ" has entangled the two companies for nearly 10 years.

On May 19, 2005, Tencent applied to register the "QQ" trademark on Class 12 goods such as cars, gaining approval on March 7, 2008. On November 26, 2009, Chery argued that the trademark violated trademark law by copying and imitating a famous trademark, being identical or similar to trademarks for similar goods, and infringing on others' prior rights. They then petitioned the TRAB to revoke the disputed trademark. After reviewing, the TRAB ruled that the registration of the disputed trademark constituted an act prohibited under Article 31 of the Trademark Law, preemptively registering a trademark already used by another party with some influence, and thus revoked Tencent's registered trademark.

Tencent argued that the TRAB's ruling contained obvious errors and omissions, stating that the disputed trademark did not violate legal provisions and should be maintained. Their representative introduced that on August 31, 2001, Tencent had applied to register the "QQ" trademark on Class 38 services such as "information transmission, computer terminal communications." Through extensive promotion, QQ communication service's registered user base grew from 5 million in 2000 to over 200 million in 2003. Therefore, before Chery Automobile used the "QQ" trademark, Tencent's "QQ" trademark had already become a well-known trademark. By 2009, Tencent had received certification as a well-known trademark from relevant national departments, and any other company using the "QQ" trademark would constitute infringement.

Chery Automobile, however, believed that Tencent's registration of the "QQ" trademark in the automotive field was malicious. As a third party in the case, Chery's representative stated that the trademark registered by Tencent in 2001 was not the "QQ" letters but a graphic. In contrast, Chery Automobile registered the "QQ" letter trademark in the automotive field in 2003. Thus, the two enterprises were using their respective trademarks in different fields, with Chery's "QQ" trademark in the automotive field preceding Tencent's. Furthermore, Chery QQ had sold over a million units, with advertising costs exceeding 200 million yuan, and deserved protection.

In addition, Tencent argued that its registration of the "QQ" trademark across multiple domains was protective registration for "QQ." However, Chery Automobile claimed that from the initial planning of the car name to later promotional campaigns, they relied entirely on the already well-known "QQ" trademark and penguin mascot to achieve promotional effects, constituting an infringement that could not generate legitimate prior rights.

The court will announce its verdict at a later date. For both Tencent and Chery, having operated the "QQ" trademark for nearly a decade, this lawsuit is one neither can afford to lose.

Is there a third way in the QQ dispute?

Tencent QQ is the first instant messaging software launched by Tencent in 1999, while Chery QQ is a microcar introduced by Chery in July 2003. Both "QQ"s enjoy high recognition within their respective fields.

A search revealed that as early as 2003 when the "QQ" car was launched, Tencent had raised objections regarding trademark infringement against Chery. Tencent stated that they were busy preparing for their IPO at the time and thus did not file a public lawsuit. Later, Tencent sent a lawyer's letter to Chery, but Chery never provided a substantive response to Tencent.

At the end of 2005, news emerged again about the legal battle between the two parties over the QQ trademark, but no further developments were reported. Some media even labeled it as hype.

This time, the trademark dispute has once again been brought to court, with Tencent as the plaintiff, the TRAB as the defendant, and Chery as the third party. Trademarks are the signboards for businesses to stand in the market. For both Tencent and Chery, who have nurtured their brands for around a decade, neither can afford to lose. Some believe that whoever is found to infringe upon the other may face substantial compensation. Is there really only a win-lose situation in this trademark dispute, with no third way? Tang Guangliang, an intellectual property expert from the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, told reporters that Tencent and Chery could collaborate to achieve a win-win outcome.

Tang Guangliang said that the role of a trademark is to attract consumer attention, acting as a link between merchants and consumers and representing the image of merchants in the eyes of consumers. A trademark itself is a means rather than an end, and how to use this means depends on each merchant’s consideration. In recent years, China’s trademark protection has matured increasingly, and whether something constitutes infringement does not solely depend on the similarity of two marks; instead, the key lies in whether consumers get confused. If there is no confusion, then the use by two or more merchants can enhance the influence of the trademark, benefiting all users of the trademark.

"For instance, if consumers buy Chery's QQ car because they think it is produced by Tencent, or if consumers use QQ chat because they like Chery QQ cars, then there is confusion," Tang Guangliang said. If such situations do not occur, there is no need for both sides to fight over it. Because concerning trademarks, it is not necessarily owned by whoever claims it, and even if it is owned, it is not necessary to eliminate every potential competitor. If the trademark is not maliciously tarnished or leads to losing market share, it might even have a positive effect, allowing both sides to coexist peacefully and share benefits.

Besides, Tang Guangliang said that from a rational market perspective, Tencent and Chery have no conflict. Chat tools mainly target young people, and QQ cars are also widely loved by young people. Both sides can fully collaborate, operate together, share the market, and achieve mutual benefit without necessarily disputing over the ownership of the trademark when there is no damage involved.
