How Faith Can Ruin a Programmer

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:20:59

　　English Title: How Religion Destroys Programmers

　　I've discovered something new about myself — God has given me the amazing ability to always make the right technical choices.

　　It might sound exaggerated, but it's truly incredible.

　　Looking back on my career in development, I believe every programming language I've used was the best choice for the scenarios at that time.

　　Similarly, the frameworks and even operating systems I chose were also the best.

　　Yes, I have this astonishing ability to pick the best from the vast sea of technology. Sometimes, I don't even need to try them out personally, yet I fiercely defend my choices.

　　You may already realize while reading this article that you possess this mysterious ability too.

　　Most developers have a technical faith

　　This is true.

　　Don’t be embarrassed; you’re not alone. I, almost everyone, stands with you.

　　Some of us have awakened from this self-induced brainwashing. Others are still blissfully unaware of the predicament we're in. But most of us at least hold one or more technical beliefs we staunchly defend.

　　It’s natural because most programmers enter the field of software development driven by their passion for it. Anything you love can easily lead to some very subjective views.

　　Take sports fans as an example. Though I’m not a sports fan myself, I know that fans of different sports teams fervently believe their team is the best, despite all evidence pointing otherwise.

　　Defending our choices and ideas is a human instinct. We readily adopt new ideas, but we zealously defend the ones we already have without needing much evidence to support them. The problem lies in our tendency to tie our perception of things to our identity, even our value as humans.

　　It requires deep introspection, but if you take a look at yourself, you'll likely find that you can list the best operating systems, programming languages, and frameworks, etc.

　　Ignorance is not bliss

　　The issue with this self-imposed belief is that it blinds us to the truth.

　　Before I actually used a Mac, I spent countless hours arguing why it was so terrible. Ironically, I'm writing this article on a Mac right now, using Windows Live Writer through remote desktop.

　　My point is, most of us passionately argue that the technology we choose is the best, without even trying other options.

　　Imagine that—it seems absurd. Yet, even today, I catch myself doing the same thing.

　　When I examine myself and ask why, I discover that most of the motivation comes from a combination of pride in what I’ve learned and mastered, and fear of what I don’t know.

　　I’ve found that if I spend some time learning a technology and understanding why others like it so much, I won’t so easily dismiss unfamiliar technologies as "garbage" or "junk." As others have said, one man's trash is another man's treasure.

　　The problem with blind faith

　　Even if I didn’t tell you, you already know that in this world, blind (and often destructive) fanaticism is a devastating force. You just need to visit your favorite national news site or read a bit of history to understand.

　　While not many programmers would go to war over issues like PHP vs C#—although I was once criticized for throwing chairs during a similar, provocative discussion that I now strongly oppose—many developers still enjoy loudly debating their opinions and even condescendingly dismissing others' work. I’m sure you’ve seen plenty of these scenes—if you spend much time online. When I boldly published some negative articles about JavaScript, I tasted the bitter consequences myself.

　　So, we might think that our ignorance doesn’t harm anyone, and they deserve it because they're clearly wrong. But the truth is, our ignorance causes quite a bit of damage.

　　Looking back, I feel deeply ashamed for belittling Perl programmers, completely disregarding their hard work, and ignorantly promoting my sacred statically-typed C-like language as the only savior that could cleanse them.

　　But most importantly, I realized that I was hurting myself the most.

　　Stop slapping yourself, dummy!

　　The biggest growth in my career came when, while looking for a C# developer job, I got a great opportunity to be the architect for a Java project.

　　I agonized over this decision. Because I thought Java was garbage, evil. Java lacked properties like C#, requiring manual getters and setters, so obviously anyone who wrote Java code was a fool.

　　I barely accepted the job, but I figured the pay was good enough to grit my teeth and bear it, waiting like a prisoner of war for Microsoft to save me someday. I thought, at least I’d escape that disgusting Java coding style.

　　However, after a few years of developing and writing good unit tests in Java, I realized that Java wasn’t as bad as I thought, and some of its strengths along with certain Java frameworks were worth praising.

　　More importantly, I began to realize how my past code obsessions had closed many doors for me. I started thinking that perhaps none of my past technical choices were necessarily the best. I began to consider that many of the most popular technologies weren’t as different from each other as I had imagined.

　　I began to understand that compared to blindly worshiping the technology I happened to choose, having a broad understanding of a range of technologies and programming languages made me more valuable.

　　Now, I find that I understand individual technologies much faster because I’m no longer "reinventing the wheel." Instead, I can use my mind to compare and contrast the differences between various programming languages and technologies, giving me a deeper understanding of everything.

　　To sum up, holding a closed attitude towards technical choices will ultimately harm yourself and limit your personal growth as a developer.

　　(Here are two excellent books that can help you break down these barriers: "Seven Languages in Seven Weeks" and "Seven Databases in Seven Weeks")

　　There is no "best"

　　Before concluding this article, I hope you’ll believe me when I say there is no such thing as the "best" technology or programming language.

　　I’m not insulting your intelligence by saying each programming language has its own strengths and handles different situations well. The truth is much deeper than that.

　　After creating over 40 extensive courses on technology and programming languages, I’ve uncovered some truths.

　　The truth is, there are many good ways to accomplish the same task using different tools and techniques.

　　The truth is, all programming languages and technologies have significant flaws and weaknesses.

　　The truth is, the more different technologies you learn, the more you’ll realize that the core concepts of most technologies are very similar. I mean the fundamental concepts of software development, regardless of the specific technology or programming language syntax.

　　Like me, you’ll discover that if you’re willing to embrace other technologies, admit your ignorance, and learn from them, you’ll find many friendly and helpful people wherever you go who are eager to teach you what they know.
