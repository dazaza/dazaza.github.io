Woman selling expensive eggs claims they are "virgin eggs"

by zhangdonjg4 on 2011-12-02 10:33:52

Egg without yolk There really is such a thing "This is a 'virgin egg', of course it's expensive" "Are these free-range eggs? How much do you sell them for?" Yesterday morning, Ms. Han passed by the intersection of Foniu Road and Huaihe Road when she saw an old lady selling eggs by the road, so she went up to ask about the price and planned to buy some. "Which basket do you want? This basket is 7 yuan per catty, this one is 7 yuan 50 fen per catty." The old lady replied. "Aren't these all free-range eggs? Why is there a 50 fen difference per catty?" Ms. Han was a bit confused. "The 7-yuan ones are ordinary free-range eggs, the more expensive ones are 'virgin eggs'." Seeing Ms. Han's surprise, the old lady explained that "virgin eggs" are eggs laid by young hens, with higher nutritional value than ordinary free-range eggs, naturally they are more expensive. Virgin egg? Is there really such a saying? Professor Huang, the director of the Animal Husbandry Department at Zhengzhou College of Animal Husbandry Engineering said that what the old lady called virgin eggs should be first-laid eggs. In general, the majority of first-laid eggs have a little blood on the shell. "It's hard to say about the nutritional value of first-laid eggs." Nutrition expert Yang Ning from China Agricultural University said that the proportion of the yolk in first-laid eggs relative to the whole egg is relatively higher, reaching around 32%, while the proportion of yolks in later-laid eggs is 30%. However, there is no direct relationship between the relatively high proportion of yolk and high nutritional value. Evening Post reporter Zhang Jingyi Duck egg without yolk, too scared to eat Yesterday, Mr. Zhang called to say, I found no yolk in a duck egg during breakfast, and I was too scared to eat it. "The first three had egg yolks, but this one shocked me." At 8:30 AM yesterday at Mr. Zhang's home, the already cut-open duck egg only had egg white and no yolk. "This isn't a yolk-less egg, there must be a yolk." At 4:50 PM yesterday, Director Huang Yankun of the Animal Husbandry Engineering Department at Zhengzhou College of Animal Husbandry Engineering took a look at the "yolk-less egg" and confidently said. "Duck eggs are usually pickled for over a month, the yolk being lighter and containing more oil, floats upwards." He broke open a piece of the duck egg and picked out the yolk inside. Yolk-less eggs do exist "Yolk-less eggs do indeed exist, they are about the size of quail eggs, chickens are more likely to produce them than ducks. The reason yolk-less eggs occur is due to the shedding of the epithelial tissue in the oviducts of chickens or ducks." Huang Yankun said that if yolk-less eggs appear frequently, it can be considered as a sign of poor health in the chicken or duck flock. For a flock of 3000 chickens, under healthy conditions, one or two yolk-less eggs might appear in a week, if the health condition is poor, generally two or three would appear in a day. "As long as yolk-less eggs are cooked thoroughly, they can be eaten." Evening Post reporter Zhang Yudong
