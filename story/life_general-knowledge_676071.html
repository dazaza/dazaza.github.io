What to Do When a Baby Has a Fever

Many parents, like this mother, become anxious when they see their child has a fever and rush to reduce the fever. In fact, having a cold or fever is not necessarily bad for children; there's no need to immediately reduce the fever.

Distinguish Between Low-grade Fever, Moderate Fever, and High Fever

When a child has a fever, a body temperature of 37.5°C to 38°C is considered a low-grade fever, 38°C to 39°C is a moderate fever, and above 39°C is a high fever. A fever exceeding 41°C is known as hyperpyrexia. A fever is the body's reaction to viral or bacterial invasion, and this response helps eliminate invading viruses and bacteria, which in turn benefits the normal growth and development of children. If the temperature does not exceed 38°C, it generally isn't necessary to urgently reduce the fever, especially if the diagnosis hasn't been clearly established yet. Blindly rushing to reduce the fever may mask the condition and interfere with the diagnostic process. When the temperature reaches above 39°C, it should be treated under the guidance of a doctor. About 4% to 12% of children experience seizures during high fevers. Repeated seizures can cause brain damage, act as precursors to certain types of epilepsy, and lead to other dangers. Therefore, children with a history of high fever seizures or a family history should receive immediate treatment to reduce the fever once it becomes high.

Physical Cooling Below 38.5°C

When treating a child’s fever, physical cooling methods are best used below 38.5°C, such as increasing water intake or wiping the body with warm water mixed with alcohol.

All antipyretics, such as acetaminophen and compound aspirin, contain caffeine and phenacetin components, which have significant side effects. The nervous system inhibition mechanism in infants and young children is not yet fully developed, and using these medications during a high fever can easily trigger seizures. Additionally, excessive sweating can lead to collapse, and accumulation of free bilirubin in the blood may cause jaundice. These drugs can also harm the digestive system and liver and kidney functions, and some may cause Reye's syndrome, leading to reduced white blood cells and platelets.

If antipyretics must be used, they should be chosen under the guidance of a doctor. Common antipyretics for children include ibuprofen, Lefulin, Bufening, and pediatric antipyretic suppositories. Traditional Chinese medicines such as Bupleurum, Saiga horn, and oral liquids for clearing heat and detoxifying can also be effective in reducing fevers in children.

Avoid Intravenous Infusion if Possible

Some parents believe that intravenous infusion works faster than taking medicine orally to reduce fever in children, so they ask doctors at hospitals to administer IV fluids and even request antibiotics. However, most colds are caused by viral infections, and antibiotics are ineffective against viruses. Our principle is to take medicine if possible instead of injections, and injections if possible instead of infusions, because infusions sometimes produce side effects. Intravenous fluids can be considered when antiviral drugs fail to reduce the fever and secondary bacterial infections occur, such as significantly increased white blood cell counts in blood tests, bronchitis, or pneumonia. Regardless of what medication is used, attention must be paid to ensuring the dosage is not too high and the duration of use is not too long. During the medication period, children should drink more water to aid in drug absorption and excretion, reducing the toxic effects of the drugs on the child's body. Antipyretics should not be taken simultaneously with alkaline drugs such as baking soda or theophylline, as this can reduce the effectiveness of the antipyretics.

According to traditional Chinese medicine, a cold is caused by exposure to wind evil and is divided into different syndromes such as wind-cold, wind-heat, and internal heat with external cold. It emphasizes the importance of differential diagnosis and treatment based on symptoms, which is particularly important for children. Some families have a "medicine box" at home, and when their child catches a cold, they give them stored cold medicine without visiting the hospital or buy medicine from the pharmacy themselves. This practice is very dangerous because many adult cold medicines can be harmful to children. Therefore, special caution should be exercised when giving medicine to children with colds.
