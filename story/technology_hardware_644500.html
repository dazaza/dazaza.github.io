Shen Junwei: In Hong Kong's business world, speaking fluent Cantonese makes talent more favored._1900

by ldsqayck on 2011-12-26 19:30:21

In dozens of past investment immigration seminars, many questions were often asked about life and workplace issues in Hong Kong. Among these, clients are most concerned about the future career development of their children in Hong Kong. As parents, they worry most about their children's future—whether they can find a good job, have a stable income, and achieve long-term development... The main concern is that if the children change environments halfway, they will need to readapt to the language and culture, which may affect their future career prospects.

Fortunately, China has experienced rapid development over the past decade, increasing various forms of trade and commerce between Hong Kong and the mainland. This has led to a high demand in Hong Kong for people who can communicate well in Mandarin. In some aspects, these individuals may even be more sought after than those with local academic qualifications.

The author grew up in Hong Kong and later went to the UK for study and work without receiving formal Mandarin education. In primary and secondary school, there was only a 45-minute Mandarin class each week. In the UK, when choosing foreign language courses, the school said that because the author was from Hong Kong, they were not allowed to choose Mandarin as a foreign language course, so French was chosen instead. Although the author performed well in French, it has been of no use until now. This is also the conflicting situation faced by the majority of those born before the 90s—they cannot speak or understand Mandarin well, nor can they spell it using pinyin. One must ask, how can one seize opportunities to participate in projects collaborating with the mainland under such circumstances?

In previous articles, the author mentioned that many projects within the country first collaborate with Hong Kong groups as the mainstream. For example, in Shanghai, Hong Kong's direct investment (Foreign Direct Investment - FDI) in 2009/2010 amounted to nearly $4 billion, accounting for 37.5% of Shanghai's total FDI, ranking first. There were 1122 contract projects, accounting for 36.3% of Shanghai's total projects, amounting to $13.3 billion, which accounted for 75% of Shanghai's total... According to the "2009-2010 Shanghai-Hong Kong Economic Development Report," 36.3% of foreign capital cooperation projects are targeted at Hong Kong enterprises, including real estate, hotels, infrastructure, finance, healthcare, and countless others. Most of the project personnel responsible on the Hong Kong side require excellent Mandarin skills, and those with mainland backgrounds are preferred.

Thanks to the Individual Visit Scheme, there is also a great demand for Mandarin-speaking talent in Hong Kong, whether in finance, retail, hospitality, foreign investment, tourism, healthcare, real estate, accounting, etc. Taking the financial industry where the author works as an example, the author would rather hire mainland graduates with residency rights in Hong Kong for HKD $10,000 than hire local graduates for the same amount of money. The reason is very simple: nearly 90% of the author's clients come from different provinces in mainland China, speaking different regional dialects of Mandarin. If one can hire someone who speaks fluent Mandarin, it means a significantly higher closing success rate, and it is also very likely to bring along the Warm Market Clients from their region, achieving two goals at once.

In traditional Chinese social culture, consumers/clients generally prefer to do business with people from the same place or hometown. If everyone can speak the same dialect, it greatly increases the closing ratio. At the same time, this is a clear competitive advantage over other competitors. Unfortunately, it is not easy to find people in Hong Kong who can fluently converse in Mandarin. Currently, mainland talents working in Hong Kong can be categorized into five types: investment immigrants, work visas, quality migrants plan, investment visas, and family reunions.

If one can meet any of the above five entry regulations, they can freely work and start businesses in Hong Kong. After seven years, they can obtain permanent resident identity cards and Hong Kong Special Administrative Region passports, enjoying visa-free travel to 145 countries worldwide. The author believes the most valuable opportunity is to learn some Western management culture in Hong Kong and then bring it back to contribute to the motherland. Isn't this more meaningful?
