Exclusive interview with the president of Pixar University: Science and art are always together

by aswee25 on 2012-03-01 17:04:31

Elyse Klaidman, who is the Dean of Pixar University and head of the Pixar Archives at Pixar Animation Studios, is also the curator of this exhibition. Over the past six years, she has traveled around the world with the "Pixar 20-Year Retrospective." When asked about the future of this touring exhibition, she said that while it cannot go on forever, she feels incredibly honored to see people worldwide being so delighted by the Pixar exhibition.

Oriental Morning Post: The "Pixar 20-Year Retrospective" was first held at MoMA (Museum of Modern Art). How did it all start?

Klaidman: I have been at Pixar for nearly 15 years. At the time, Pixar was still a young company, and we were moving into our current large building. I asked John Lasseter (currently the Chief Creative Officer at Pixar) if we could have a small space to display some works. He said it was a good idea. He gave me a huge space, which was very beautiful.

I began organizing some exhibitions showcasing the concepts and artworks from our films. A few years later, MoMA came to Pixar and asked if we could do an exhibition. And that's how it all started.

Oriental Morning Post: There are many hand-drawn pieces and sculptures in the exhibition. Do you think traditional art is more important than computer technology in making animated films?

Klaidman: I believe they are equally important. One point John is proud of with this exhibition is showing the world that we don't just use computers; artists can use any method they feel best visually expresses their ideas.

Some artists always design using computers, while others use oil paintings, pencil sketches, markers, clay... various forms of mixed media. Some artists like pencils because they're quick and fluid, not necessarily perfect, and they allow for mistakes, followed by constant modifications, which is very important in the creative process.

Oriental Morning Post: Will you include some interactive or high-tech exhibits?

Klaidman: There is multimedia, such as TV screens, and two large installations. We want to show people the magic of animation, the combination of technology and art. At Pixar, Lasseter always says, art challenges technology, and technology illuminates art. Therefore, at Pixar, technology and art are always together.

Oriental Morning Post: How many people work at Pixar? How many people are involved in making one movie?

Klaidman: Pixar currently has a total of 1,200 employees. Around 250 to 300 people participate in each film. In the early stages, there might only be 10 people, then more people join as the project progresses. It takes about four to five years to make a movie. At its peak, there might be 250 to 300 people involved, then the number gradually decreases.

Oriental Morning Post: How many paintings and sculptures are left after making one movie? How do you select the exhibits?

Klaidman: Each movie is different, but there are roughly 100,000 pieces. In the archive room, there are over a million works. We try to select pieces that represent each artist and also tell the story of how we made the movies. For example, in this exhibition, we specifically introduced how the character "Boo" from *Monsters, Inc.* was created. These drawings help people understand the process of designing a character.

The design process isn't always the same. For instance, with "Sulley" and "Mike Wazowski" from *Monsters, Inc.*, we drew many designs for Sulley, but the first drawing of Mike looks very similar to the final result. This time, there are about 400-500 pieces.

Oriental Morning Post: *Cars 2* is about to premiere in Shanghai. Are there any related exhibits? What do you think of this movie?

Klaidman: There are some paintings this time, and a display showing the design process for *Cars 2*. I like this movie, and I have two sons who both really love *Cars 2*.

Oriental Morning Post: Chinese people also want to make good animated films. What do you think are the key points in making a great animated movie?

Klaidman: Give them enough time to create. You cannot rush a creative product. You need to protect it, nurture it. It's a process that will involve many mistakes. In the exhibition, you can see many things that didn't make it into the final films. Many good ideas from the artists ultimately didn't fit the story, and the director had to choose the best ones. Making a movie requires close collaboration, mutual respect, teamwork, and support for the creative parts, telling their stories.
