Attached: Gold Secrets of Year-End Summaries

As the year-end approaches, it's once again time to write summaries en masse. To help everyone, I've gathered some year-end summary secrets from experts to hopefully provide inspiration to my colleagues.

Key Point One: Length Matters

After a hard year of work, how good your performance was largely depends on the weight of this "summary." If there's a word limit, it's best to write up to the maximum allowed. Without a word limit, it might get tricky. You should find out how long summaries from other peer units are so you have a benchmark and don't miss the mark. Otherwise, if you blindly write for half a day and end up with 15 pages, only to find out someone else wrote 20 pages, you immediately lose the edge in terms of volume, which would be counterproductive.

To achieve a longer length, besides putting in hard work, there’s another shortcut—bigger font and wider line spacing. Set the font size above "three-point" and increase the line spacing as much as possible without going overboard, otherwise, it may seem like "padding."

Key Point Two: Clichés Are Essential

The beginning must be something like, "Time flies, 2004 will soon be over. Looking back on the past year, I can’t help but feel countless emotions..." The ending should be along the lines of, "A new year means a new starting point, new opportunities, and new challenges," or "Determined to continue to strive and reach even greater heights," or "Certainly striving to open a new chapter in work."

In the middle sections where achievements are detailed, phrases such as "received very good results," "received praise and welcome from leaders and employees," "reached a new height," "taken another step forward," etc., should frequently appear.

A "summary" not only needs to summarize strengths but also weaknesses; otherwise, it might give the impression of being too plain. However, flaws should only be lightly touched upon. A suitable ratio of achievements to shortcomings is around 10:1. Otherwise, criticizing yourself too harshly might leave you with nothing worthwhile, jeopardizing your job.

Key Point Three: Data Must Be Intuitive

This is the digital age, so data is always beneficial. Examples include "business growth rate," "customer complaint reduction rate," "how many visitors were received," "how much expenditure was saved," "how many overtime shifts worked," "average daily phone calls received," "average days spent traveling annually," "total pages of materials written," etc.

But remember, "numbers can be dull," so it's best to present data in intuitive, visual charts such as line graphs, pie charts, and bar charts. There are three advantages to doing this: First, achievements look profound, and seeing towering columns and soaring curves, one feels quite satisfied. Second, contrast is strong. When making charts, use some historical data, average data, or industry data favorable to you as a backdrop—roses need green leaves to support them! Third, on paper, a chart is equivalent to about a thousand words, so you won't have to worry about having too few words.

Key Point Four: Use Sequence Numbers Well

The greatest advantage of sequence numbers is that they allow you to split one sentence into several and treat a few words or half a sentence as one, leaving a lot of blank space on the page, thus extending the length while making the summary appear well-structured. It's important to note that the sequences must be strictly hierarchical, following the subordinate relationships, to avoid giving the leadership the impression of unclear thinking.
