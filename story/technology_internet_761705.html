US Internet Tax Bill Draws Criticism: App Developers Also Have to Pay Taxes

by geekzhang on 2013-05-07 11:20:03

According to the American technology information website CNET, on the evening of Monday local time (May 7 in Beijing Time), taxes will be levied on mobile application developers, cloud computing services, music and movies and other digital product downloads. In addition, according to this bill, e-commerce companies will have to collect consumption tax from online shopping consumers on behalf of the government. This has aroused widespread dissatisfaction among application developers and Internet companies.Pressure on start-upsThis bill is called the "Marketplace Fairness Act". If it also gets passed in the House of Representatives, users who download digital products such as e-books, music, movies, software, etc., in the future must pay taxes to the state government. In addition, it forces retailers to collect consumption tax on behalf of the government for the physical goods they sell.Senator Mike Enzi and his Democratic allies are unwilling to talk about the details of the implementation of the "Marketplace Fairness Act". It will also impose taxes on companies that make downloadable software and network services.Steve DelBianco, executive director of the high-tech enterprise alliance NetChoice said: If I develop an application and sell it at a price of $3, then I must pay 18 cents to 25 cents of consumption tax. The members of NetChoice include eBay, Facebook, Yahoo, and the group-buying website LivingSocial, etc.If the "Marketplace Fairness Act" is officially implemented, technology companies in five states including California, New York, Maryland, Massachusetts, and Illinois will be hit hardest. This is because these states currently consider digital goods as free items. In other words, start-ups, individuals, or other companies located in these states do not need to worry about paying consumption tax.However, if the "Marketplace Fairness Act" is officially passed, any company selling a certain scale of digital products in the 24 U.S. states that have decided to levy consumption tax on online shopping must collect consumption tax on behalf of the government.According to the current law of California, Silicon Valley start-ups do not need to collect consumption tax on behalf of the government. However, once the "Marketplace Fairness Act" is passed as a federal law, Silicon Valley start-up companies will face new economic burdens, which has also angered some taxpayer rights advocacy organizations. A group named Americans for Tax Reform wrote a letter to Enzi saying: Who can be exempted from consumption tax on digital goods such as applications and music: the manufacturer, the app store, the sales platform, or the creator of the digital goods?A spokesperson for Enzi did not comment on this.May exacerbate taxpayer doubtsPete Sepp, executive vice president of the National Taxpayers Union, said that the decision of the drafters of the "Marketplace Fairness Act" to impose consumption tax on online shopping would only exacerbate taxpayers' doubts that this bill has nothing to do with simplifying procedures but more about allowing the government to grab extra cash revenue.In the past few years, the National Retail Federation and the Retail Industry Leaders Association have been lobbying legislators in order to pass such bills. The members of these two federations include large physical retailers such as Walmart, Best Buy, Target, Home Depot, OfficeMax, Macy's, and Container Store. A spokesperson for President Obama last month said that Obama also supports this bill.However, most conservative groups do not support the bill, and eBay CEO John Donahoe has sent emails to millions of sellers, calling on them to take action together to resist the legislation of the "Marketplace Fairness Act". He wrote in the email: This bill treats you and Amazon, a large online retailer with billions of dollars in turnover, equally. Those who demand change are unwilling to admit a fact, that is, the burden on small sellers will far exceed that on national retailers.Jason Brewer, Vice President of Public Affairs at the Retail Industry Leaders Association, when talking about digital downloads, said: In the future, each state will decide on its own which digital goods to tax and which not to tax. The "Marketplace Fairness Act" will put this decision-making power in the hands of governors and state legislators, who are only accountable to voters.
