Microblogging becomes a new channel for farmers to sell vegetables

by edivista on 2011-12-09 08:36:13

Click to enter Sina Weibo vegetable selling

A few days ago, I received a call from an uncle back in my hometown of Shandong asking me to help him apply for a verified (real-name certification) Weibo account. He said that he planted a lot of cabbage and radishes this year and wanted to use Weibo to sell vegetables.

My uncle said that he watched the news and saw many farmers selling vegetables on Weibo. Potatoes, cabbages, green onions, sweet potatoes, all can be sold online with good results.

Li Jiwen, a farmer from Inner Mongolia, is an example of successfully selling vegetables on Weibo. On October 13, Li Jiwen posted a message on Sina Weibo saying that there were 700 tons of unsold potatoes at 45 cents per pound. After this Weibo post was published, it was quickly forwarded nearly 100,000 times, receiving over 20,000 comments and attracting much attention from netizens and media alike. Just half a month later, Li Jiwen posted another Weibo update saying, "All my potatoes have been sold." In this era where everyone loves Weibo, using Weibo to sell vegetables seems to be becoming a new sales channel for farmers. Many people, seeing such cheap and fresh vegetables, want to participate in group buying. And government departments also use Weibo as an information release platform to help farmers sell vegetables.

A few days ago, due to weather factors, there was a serious backlog of Chinese cabbage in the suburbs of Shanghai. The official Weibo account of the Shanghai Municipal Government News Office issued multiple posts to promote the sale of Chinese cabbage, calling on everyone to buy it. It's unclear whether the government's Weibo promotion worked or if everyone just wanted to eat Chinese cabbage, but within two hours, all the Chinese cabbage in major Shanghai supermarkets was sold out.

While marveling at how farmers are keeping up with the times by moving their vegetable-selling towards e-commerce, and being happy for Li Jiwen and the successful sale of vegetables by suburban Shanghai farmers, the phenomena behind the act of selling vegetables via Weibo cannot be ignored and must be addressed: On one hand, there are vegetables in the fields worth only a few cents that no one wants, causing harm to farmers; on the other hand, vegetable prices in supermarkets and farmers' markets continue to rise, becoming one of the main drivers of inflation.

In fact, although Weibo has become a new channel for selling vegetables, there are still only a few lucky farmers like Li Jiwen. Many farmers remain worried about the unsold agricultural products. At present, selling vegetables via Weibo is not a regular channel; farmers see it more as an emergency measure to recover economic losses.

How to help farmers build a platform for exchanging supply and demand information on agricultural products, communicate market information in a timely manner, and how to improve the connection between agriculture and supermarkets so that farmers have stable sales channels and citizens can buy cheaper vegetables—these are issues we need to think about.

Also, the sharp fluctuations in agricultural product prices deserve attention. Last year, both cabbages and potatoes had good markets, but this year, prices have fallen by more than half, leaving many farmers hurt.

Not just cabbages and potatoes. Last year, due to speculative capital, ginger, garlic, mung beans, and peppers all saw skyrocketing prices, but this year they have all faced significant price drops. Last year's soaring ginger prices plummeted this year, with wholesale prices in production areas falling from a high of 9.6 yuan/kg to 0.7 yuan/kg, reaching the lowest level in nearly ten years, resulting in heavy losses for many ginger farmers.

My uncle's Weibo account is still under verification, but his first Weibo post is already written: "Pay more attention to grains and vegetables, let us start tomorrow as happy farmers!"
