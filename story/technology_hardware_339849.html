UK media selects top 10 biggest fool events in history

by xia2oo007 on 2010-04-04 14:44:33

The UK's Independent newspaper, published on April 1st, listed the top ten greatest April Fools' pranks in British history. Let’s take a look at some of the brilliant or laughable ideas that foreigners have come up with for this special day!

1. Spaghetti Harvest

On April Fool's Day in 1957, BBC's news program "Panorama" aired an absurd report about a spaghetti harvest in Ticino, Switzerland. Common knowledge tells us that spaghetti is actually made from flour processed by machines, but the BBC claimed that farmers in Ticino were planting large areas of trees that grew spaghetti naturally.

The news not only showed images of spaghetti hanging from tree branches, but the host also vividly described the local celebration of the harvest, saying seriously: “Although the planting scale of spaghetti trees in Switzerland is not as extensive as in Italy, it has become an important industry domestically.”

Since the fake news was quite convincing, hundreds of calls came into the BBC asking for more details, and many deceived viewers even asked how to plant these spaghetti trees. The BBC's humorous response to these enthusiastic viewers was: “Put the spaghetti in a can of tomato sauce, then fill your heart with hope.”

2. Left-Handed Burgers

Global fast-food giant "Burger King" issued a press release on April Fool's Day in 1998, announcing the creation of a "Left-Handed Whopper" specially designed for the approximately 11 million left-handed customers who visit their restaurants each year in the UK. It was reported that the ingredients of this burger were no different from usual, except that the food was rotated 180 degrees in placement.

After the ad was published, thousands of left-handed customers called "Burger King" restaurants to order this burger. A spokesperson from the UK charity "Left-Handers Club" also believed it, expressing appreciation for "Burger King's" initiative through the media, urging all left-handed burger lovers to visit "Burger King" and "experience the uniqueness of this product."

3. All Because of Bras

On April Fool's Day in 1982, the UK's Daily Mail reported that a series of incidents had occurred where alarm systems and broadcast signals were interfered with, and the culprit turned out to be bras containing copper wires, widely used by women at the time. This report suggested that friction between the body and nylon clothing could generate static electricity on the copper wires, thus disrupting airborne signals. Scientists later stated that this explanation was complete nonsense.

4. "Light as Air"

British television personality Patrick Moore liked to tell stories about astrology on screen. On the morning of April Fool's Day in 1976, Moore shocked audiences during a BBC program by revealing that at 9:47 AM, Jupiter, Pluto, and Earth would align in a straight line, significantly weakening gravitational pull. He also urged viewers to try jumping to feel the reduction in gravity.

Though it was just an April Fool's joke, thousands of viewers still called in claiming they clearly felt lighter. One woman even said she and her friends leapt from their chairs and "then floated lightly around the room."

5. Flying Penguins

On April 1, 2008, the BBC aired a nature documentary titled "Miracle of Evolution." Host Terry Jones introduced footage captured by the production team on King George Island in Antarctica, showing penguins taking flight in miraculous scenes. "This newly discovered species of penguin differs from others; in the cold winter, they don't need to huddle together for warmth but instead fly in flocks! It's astonishing!" he said.

Jones further explained seriously that these penguins could fly thousands of kilometers and "gracefully land" upon reaching the warm Amazon rainforest in South America. Many viewers learned that this documentary was actually a carefully prepared April Fool's gift from the BBC when they called in expressing their astonishment.

6. Nessie? Or Walrus?

The legend of the Loch Ness Monster provided excellent material for April Fool's pranks. In 1972, a group of zoologists from Flamingo Land Zoo in Yorkshire, UK, visited Loch Ness hoping to find the mythical creature. They soon excitedly discovered a floating animal carcass, which after being salvaged, appeared to be about five meters long and weighed approximately 1.5 tons, possibly the Loch Ness Monster, because it "had a bear-like head, dark brown scales, and clamp-like fins."

News of the discovery of the Loch Ness Monster quickly attracted international media attention, but after local police investigated, they found it was a carefully orchestrated prank by Flamingo Land official John Shields. A week earlier, Shields had removed the whiskers and teeth of a dead walrus, "disguised" it, and threw it into Loch Ness so his colleagues would mistakenly believe they had found the legendary monster.

7. The Nonexistent Indian Ocean Island

In the April 1, 1977 edition of the Guardian, a seven-page supplement detailed an island in the Indian Ocean that had never been discovered before. Named San Serriffe, the island looked like a semicolon, composed of Upper Caisse and Lower Caisse. Many readers were captivated by this paradise-like island and called inquiring about its exact location.

In fact, this island was entirely fabricated by the editors of the Guardian, and all the names were related to newspaper printing business. San Serriffe refers to "sans-serif font," while Upper Caisse and Lower Caisse represent "uppercase letter tray" and "lowercase letter tray," all of which are printing terms.

8. The Magic of Nylon Stockings

In 1962, color TVs were still very rare household appliances for most Swedes. On April Fool's Day, Swedish renowned technical expert Kjell Stensson told TV viewers that simply hanging a nylon stocking in front of the screen would allow their black-and-white TVs to display colored images. His explanation was that the material of the nylon stockings could reconstruct the structure of the light particles emitted from the screen, thereby changing the colors displayed. As a result, thousands of Swedes believed it and hung nylon stockings in front of their TVs. However, no matter how they hung them, the TV picture remained black-and-white.

9. Pineapple Bananas

In 2009 on April Fool's Day, the famous British chain supermarket Waitrose announced the introduction of a very novel "hybrid" fruit — pineapple bananas. Their advertisement read: "Pineapple bananas are available exclusively today at Waitrose. If you're told that all the pineapple bananas have been sold out, please don't worry, today our strawberries are all half-price."

10. Blondes Will Disappear

Major international organizations rarely play jokes with the public on April Fool's Day, but when they do, it's often "all or nothing." On April 1, 2002, the World Health Organization (WHO) released a report purportedly based on extensive research, declaring that due to the increasing prevalence of hair dyeing and human genetic issues, there would be no babies born with beautiful blonde hair after the year 2202.

The announcement sparked widespread reactions in public opinion, but the report was eventually exposed as a joke. WHO admitted they had never conducted any research on blonde infants.
