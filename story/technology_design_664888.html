In recent days, many buildings within the Expo Park have begun to adjust their night scene lighting. Under the brilliant illumination, the red China Pavilion appears majestic and exceptionally charming; all four facades of the Theme Pavilion use LED lighting technology, replacing traditional landscape lights, presenting dazzling and captivating light shows; the colorful lights of the Sun Valley in the Expo Axis, composed of six giant conical steel structures, are full of design sense, changing into seven colors, and showcasing the Expo logo, its English abbreviation, and various shapes of Hai Bao patterns for the first time, offering a delightful visual experience; the Performance Center is nothing short of stylish, resembling a "night-light flying saucer" landed by the Huangpu River.

The Shanghai Expo 2010 China Pavilion

Next year, the most splendid night view in Shanghai will not be the dazzling skyscrapers in Lujiazui but rather the Expo Park on both sides of the Huangpu River, undoubtedly becoming the main feature of Shanghai's night scenery. What kind of dreamlike world of light and shadow will the Expo present? How will lighting technology enhance the artistic expression of the park at night? And what principles will guide the night lighting?

Enchanting Nighttime Artistry

The Petroleum Pavilion is one of the 18 self-built enterprise pavilions. Do you know when the Petroleum Pavilion is the most beautiful? Designer Ms. Wu Shenshen suggests: it looks better at night than during the day, so it’s best to visit after dinner. During the day, it is a building shaped like pale blue "oil and gas pipelines," but at night, it transforms into a crystal-clear, multi-colored dreamlike "Oil Cube." This transformation is made possible by the nearly 4,000 square meters of the building's exterior walls, which function as a giant LED screen. As soon as the evening lights come on, this LED screen displays multiple colors and patterns, synchronized with the beats of a large musical fountain. One can say that the Petroleum Pavilion's greatest characteristic lies in its ever-changing appearance.

Imagine if there were no help from light sources—no matter how ornate the venues might be, they would lose much of their charm. The reporter found that to achieve an extraordinary and stunning effect, designers have indeed put a lot of thought into the lighting. SAIC-GM Pavilion designer Rong Wujie stated that simplicity and grandeur are the core principles of its lighting design. To ensure the smooth and complete spiral appearance of the car pavilion, he strives to avoid any visible fixtures, commonly referred to as "seeing the light but not the lamp," enhancing the sense of mystery and viewer comfort. Additionally, various details of the car pavilion are carefully designed with accent lighting, including hidden lights in the ramps, roof, handrails, etc., guiding visitors through their tour.

Take the Korea Corporate Joint Pavilion as another example. Its appearance resembles ripples encircling the entire building, symbolizing the integration of enterprises with people, cities with nature. Most fascinatingly, at night, through lighting illumination, it presents five different colors—red, yellow, blue, white, and green—which reportedly reflect the Eastern traditional concept of the Five Elements. Another noteworthy pavilion is the United Arab Emirates (UAE) Pavilion—"Flowing Sand Dunes." According to the designer, the "sand dunes" offer a unique sight at night. When leaving the venue, remember to look back—you'll be amazed to find the UAE Pavilion resembling a slowly rising jellyfish in the deep sea. This magical visual effect is created by cleverly designed lighting, making the "sand dunes" appear to float in the night sky over the Expo Park.
