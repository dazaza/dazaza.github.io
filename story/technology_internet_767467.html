Hands-on experience with the HTC One max

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:39:55

HTC officially launched the One max today at the Grand Hotel in Beijing, China. This 5.9" large-screen smartphone, along with the previous One and One mini, marks the completion of HTC's proud One family lineup.

A quick review of the HTC One max specs:

System: Android 4.3 (Jelly Bean) + Sense UI 5.5

Screen: 5.9 inches, resolution 1920×1080, 373 ppi

Performance: Qualcomm Snapdragon 600 1.7 GHz quad-core SoC; 2GB RAM; 16GB internal ROM (expandable up to 64GB via microSD card)

Camera: 4 million UltraPixel backlit camera, f/2.0, focal length 28mm, 1080p 60 frames/second HDR video recording

Hardware and Design

Firstly, let’s talk about the initial hands-on feel. Due to the screen size increasing to 5.9 inches, the dimensions of the One max reach 164 x 82 x 10.29 mm, providing a noticeably different feel compared to the One (4.7 inches). Meanwhile, the battery capacity has increased to 3300 mAh.

In terms of craftsmanship, it remains as excellent as the One series, including the quality of the screen display, stereo speakers, and the handling of gaps around the frame, all of which are at a relatively high standard.

In terms of appearance, the One max largely inherits the design of the One, such as the dual-speaker design above and below the screen, the aluminum unibody frame (though the back cover is now removable), and the symmetrically designed antennas above and below the camera, all of which are symbolic elements of the One family.

However, unlike the One, due to the larger screen, the One max is significantly larger in size. Additionally, the metallic edge that encircles both sides of the One has been removed on the One max, replaced by a full polycarbonate bezel, resulting in a slightly reduced sense of premium quality. According to HTC officials at the event, this change improves dust resistance and durability, which will be verified through future use.

Besides changes in the frame design, the power button, previously located on the top of the One, has been moved to the right side on the One max. This ensures that even with the increased screen size and device dimensions, the button remains easily accessible. The infrared interface from the One remains in the same position on the top of the device.

The changes on the back are more noticeable. In addition to three new metal contact points on the lower right side for the official power case charging, HTC has abandoned the closed structure design used on the One (based on the international version) in favor of a design similar to Meizu MX series — where the back cover can be opened but the battery cannot be replaced. Opening the back cover is mainly for replacing the SIM card or microSD card.

Fingerprint Recognition

Of course, the newly added fingerprint sensor below the camera is the hallmark difference between the One max and its sibling models. Building on Apple's iPhone 5s fingerprint recognition for unlocking, HTC has further enabled users to quickly access pre-set applications (up to three) directly from the lock screen using their fingerprint.

However, despite the official emphasis on the convenience of fingerprint recognition during the launch event, after actually trying it out, HTC's implementation of fingerprint technology on the One max can only be described as a half-hearted innovation.

This is mainly because the One max uses an older generation of fingerprint technology. Unlike Apple's Touch ID, the One max requires users to swipe their finger downwards over the sensor, which not only takes more time but also inevitably causes the finger to touch the camera lens due to its proximity.

Moreover, with the fingerprint scanner located on the back of the bulky device, single-handed unlocking is almost impossible. However, these issues are not the biggest problem with the One max's fingerprint recognition. The biggest issue lies in the rather poor recognition success rate, requiring two or three repeated attempts on average to successfully unlock. Considering these factors, the usability of the One max's fingerprint recognition is currently very limited.

Software and Interface

On the software side, as a high-end flagship model, the One max's system fluidity is top-notch. However, HTC's choice of the more conservative Snapdragon 600 over the higher-end Snapdragon 800 might disappoint some who were eagerly anticipating this phone. On the HTC One max, we can still see the iconic BlinkFeed feature of the series, though this time the BlinkFeed UI is based on Android 4.3, upgraded to version 5.5. Key enhancements include support for "Read it later" news articles, and a new drawer that slides out from the left side of the interface to display all news sources, making it easier for users to organize their content.

Pricing

HTC One max's biggest highlights undoubtedly include its 5.9-inch screen and trendy fingerprint scanner. The former fills the gap in HTC's lineup for a true large-screen smartphone, which could positively enhance brand influence. However, the underwhelming performance of the latter fails to add significant value to this new flagship and may even become an unnecessary feature.

According to official information, the HTC One max will go on sale in China on the 23rd of this month, with the regular version priced at RMB 5088. At this price point, I believe its relatively conservative hardware configuration, combined with HTC's current popularity in the domestic market, lacks sufficient competitiveness.
