[What If] Episode 69: The Country of Death - Facebook

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:05:50

**Question:**

When (if ever) will the number of dead people's accounts on Facebook exceed the number of living people's accounts?

— Emily Dunham

**Answer:**

It will either be in the 2060s or in the 2130s.

Currently, there aren’t that many dead people on Facebook. This is mainly because Facebook—and its users—are relatively young. Although the average age of Facebook users has been increasing over the past few years, it’s still safe to say that most Facebook users are still younger individuals. [1]

**Past:**

Based on Facebook's growth rate and the age demographics of its users over the years, it’s estimated that around 10 to 20 million registered users have already passed away.

At present, these deceased users are fairly evenly distributed across different age groups. While younger people have a lower mortality rate compared to those in their sixties or seventies, they make up the majority of Facebook users, meaning the number of young people who have died is roughly equal to the number of older people who have passed away.

**Future:**

Approximately 290,000 Facebook users in the U.S. will (or already have) died in 2013, and globally, several million people will pass away in 2013. [3] In just seven years, this mortality rate will double, and in another seven years, it will double again.

Even if Facebook stopped accepting new registrations tomorrow, the number of deaths would continue to rise for decades, as people who were university students between 2000 and 2020 will start aging.

The key factor determining when the number of dead accounts will exceed living accounts lies in how quickly Facebook can add new users—typically younger individuals—compared to how fast older users die.

**Facebook in 2100:**

Hmm, let’s talk about Facebook's future.

We can't definitively predict how long Facebook will last because we lack similar precedents. Most websites experience a rapid rise in popularity followed by a slow fade into obscurity, so Facebook's eventual fate will likely follow this pattern.

Given this, Facebook will probably begin losing market share toward the end of the next decade and will never fully recover. The turning point—when the number of dead accounts exceeds living accounts—will likely occur around 2065.

However, it’s also possible that Facebook could evolve into something more fundamental, like the TCP protocol, becoming a cornerstone for other infrastructures, making it indispensable for users. If Facebook manages to survive through multiple generations, its turning point might not come until the late 22nd century.

But this seems unlikely. Nothing lasts forever, especially in the rapidly evolving world of computer technology. The digital landscape is littered with the remnants of websites and technologies that were once thought to be permanent just ten years ago.

So Facebook's fate will likely fall somewhere between these two scenarios. [5] We'll just have to wait and see.

**The Fate of Accounts:**

Facebook has the financial resources to store all user pages and data indefinitely. Living users generate more data than dead users, and only active accounts are frequently accessed. Even if the majority of accounts become deceased (or inactive), it won’t significantly impact the overall cost of data storage.

What’s more important is what users want. What do we do with these pages? Unless we request that Facebook delete them, they’ll likely keep all the data backed up permanently. Even if Facebook doesn’t do this, there will always be data collection companies willing to preserve this information.

Currently, close relatives of deceased individuals can turn their Facebook pages into memorial pages. However, this raises many questions, such as passwords, private messages, and access rights. Should these pages remain accessible? Which information should be hidden? Do relatives have the right to access the deceased person’s emails? Should memorial pages allow comments? How should trolling or harassment be handled? Can other users interact with the deceased person’s account? On which friend lists should the deceased appear?

We’re still figuring out how to handle these issues, and there will undoubtedly be challenges along the way. Death has always been a significant, difficult, and emotionally charged topic, handled differently across various societies.

But the basic events that make up a life remain constant. We all eat, learn, grow, love, fight, and eventually die. Regardless of geography, culture, or technological advancements, our behaviors revolve around these fundamental activities. Like everyone who has lived before us, we're playing the same game on our own personal fields. Despite occasional setbacks or frustrations, we continue to update the social norms for falling in love, arguing, learning, and growing up online. Eventually, we will figure out how to properly honor and remember those who have passed.
