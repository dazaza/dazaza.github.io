A entrepreneur's autobiography: How I sell fish online

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:39:34

Actually, when I first started selling fish online, many friends around me thought I was crazy.

When everyone's eyes were on real estate, technology, mobile phones, and cars, I chose to sell fish. It was indeed a tough decision, but it felt solid.

How can O2O be played in a creative way? Should we abandon WeChat? Who is more awesome between Lei Jun and J.Wong? Can BlackBerry make a comeback? Can Cook take over from the master Jobs? I don't care about these because they are far away from us. Dreams may be big, but reality is small. People should live a little more mundanely.

Youcai failed, and it died unwillingly. But I still have no doubt about the huge potential of fresh produce in e-commerce.

In 2012, I began to try B2C online fish selling. Youcai went down, but I survived. Of course, this might be related to the fact that I am just a small-scale start-up company with low operating costs and easy execution. However, some experiences I've gained through trial and error, I believe, are still quite valuable and worth sharing with all of you. Perhaps they could give some inspiration to those friends who are starting an online business selling fish, vegetables, or fruits.

1. Product positioning

I positioned my product as: never do low-cost daily products; only focus on mid-range products. In the early stage, I didn’t do vegetables and meat; I only did frozen seafood.

Let me explain. I think your biggest competitor in e-commerce for fresh produce is the local agricultural market. Compared to them, I am at a disadvantage. First, the traditional group of people buying vegetables mainly consists of middle-aged and elderly people, most of whom would never have the habit of buying fish online. Second, you can say that selling fish online saves tens of thousands of yuan in booth fees compared to a stall. However, the harsh reality is that compared to vendors who restock almost every day at dawn with almost zero inventory, your warehousing cost, labor cost, and delivery cost throughout the year will be much higher than theirs.

Youcai mainly failed here. Observing its product structure, there were almost no mid-to-high-end products. Most of them were common agricultural by-products found in the farm markets, which directly faced the impact of local agricultural markets. You want to change the purchasing habits of traditional aunties, just like wanting cows to stop eating grass and switch to meat - it's unrealistic.

Compared to supermarkets, I found my advantages quite obvious: shorter restocking cycles, fresher products (referring to the general cycle), prices nearly 40% cheaper (don't think supermarkets are cheap), and a younger customer base (I observed that most supermarket shoppers are aged 25-40), which fits my requirements well.

Seafood has many classifications, mainly divided into live seafood, chilled seafood, and frozen seafood (I won't explain each one here; if you're interested, you can look it up yourself). Live seafood requires the highest storage standards, and the price difference between a live fish and a dead fish can be around 50%, so it's not suitable for small-cost operators like me. Frozen seafood processed initially is the easiest to preserve and has the least loss. Generally speaking, as long as the temperature is reached, the shelf life is about one and a half to two years (Ding Jingtao said that Youcai's fresh produce loss rate was 5%; I simply don't believe it).

Therefore, I positioned my competitors as supermarkets. I selected some raw material products used in self-service restaurants, Western restaurants, mid-to-high-end Chinese restaurants, and Japanese cuisine. The profit margin is high, not easily seen on the market, and easy to preserve. This way, I avoided the impact of the agricultural market while enhancing the product's own value.

2. Price positioning

Unlike luxury items such as mobile phones, cars, and houses, this market is extremely sensitive to price.

For example, a pound of cabbage or a piece of salmon, if your price exceeds the market price by more than 5 yuan, customers won't be interested in you. You can only attract them by offering prices lower than the market. However, the profit from a pound of cabbage versus a piece of salmon... well, I don't need to elaborate, you get it.

About pricing, Ding Jingtao from Youcai once said: "Even for tomatoes, prices may vary by 2 to 5 times. The quality of vegetables on the market varies greatly, and their nutritional safety is inconsistent. Youcai tries to choose the best quality goods. For instance, cucumbers can cost 1.2 yuan or 1.5 yuan per unit, we'll choose the 1.5 yuan ones."

"So the vegetable prices on Youcai are slightly higher than those in the market. Ding Jingtao emphasized that if the quality is the same, Youcai is definitely cheaper."

To these statements, I would say: Mr. Ding must have never sold vegetables himself.

Ninety percent of the aunties will absolutely not buy cabbage that costs more than the market price, no matter how much you exaggerate about the cabbage, even if your cabbage is green, organic, super powerful, and amazing. Aunties are practical; whoever offers a lower price with acceptable quality gets their business.

Since the product is positioned as agricultural by-products, even the slightest price fluctuation can significantly affect sales. If Ding's approach is followed, then only agricultural by-products with barely passing quality but much cheaper than the market should be done, aiming to increase volume. This allows for low-profit margins to capture a large market share. However, for a start-up company, without market experience and market recognition, starting out with the aim of capturing the market is very dangerous.

Without sufficient gross profit margin, it’s hard to carry out market promotions. Unfortunately, Mr. Ding’s strategy aimed to create premium agricultural by-products but expected aunties to accept higher-than-market prices without being able to check the goods on the spot. This undoubtedly pushed him into a dead end.

So, I set a very realistic price for my products: they must be below the market price but offer high profits. I don’t care about market share; I only care whether the gross profit is high enough.

You earn 1 fen from 1000 people versus earning 1 yuan from 100 people; I know how to calculate this account.

3. Channel Cost

A personal suggestion: in any industry, you must have your own unique channel. This channel doesn't mean the market can be monopolized by you, but rather that the product channels are relatively few, making your product somewhat unique and scarce.

When I was doing wholesale, I was thinking about this issue, and the solution was simple. First, try to avoid competitive first-tier cities; after all, how can you compete barefoot against someone driving a car? Second, choose less popular origins. My product origins are concentrated in three places: South America, Africa, and Northeast Asia. The weakness of big players (including other large aquatic companies) lies in their inability to pivot quickly due to their size. I just need to observe the market and select the right products to make decisions independently. By the time they get approvals level by level, my product channel foundation will already be firmly established.

Youcai positioned its channels domestically, which had too low a threshold. The principle of wholesale is similar to group buying, mainly relying on volume. So today you can negotiate with farmers, and tomorrow they can do the same. Today you can create a Youcai, and tomorrow they can replicate it as Youcaihua.

4. Marketing Model

This is something we must discuss in detail.

Youcai positioned itself as "delivering vegetables like delivering milk," attempting to build a closed ecosystem similar to iOS where customers can enjoy doorstep delivery service without leaving home. This involves two issues: 1. How to attract potential customers to your platform? 2. Why should customers stay? (This is what Mr. Ding referred to as improving customer stickiness.)

I noticed Youcai earlier because of a Sina report. Since the product differences were not significant, I was curious and clicked to check out Youcai. When I opened its UI, I was very disappointed. An outdated interface with no sense of design, apart from the introduction of product types and prices, there was no other relevant information. The variety was not rich enough yet included products like disinfectant and toothpaste, which had nothing to do with "vegetables"...

I really couldn't figure out how Youcai could retain customers. Opening its webpage felt like walking into a roadside grocery store full of miscellaneous items, even making me forget that I originally came to look at "vegetables."

Relying solely on the uninnovative milk-style sales model of order-delivery-after-sales to attract customers is undoubtedly Youcai's biggest failure.

So, before I started, I asked people to prepare my products into various dishes. Just for one type of crab, I made soups, stews, stir-fries, etc. Then I hired professional photographers to artistically process the dishes to make them look as appetizing as possible.

In this aspect, I borrowed from IKEA's concept: tempting customers with finished products but only selling semi-finished ones. This way, customers fully experience the joy of accomplishment, far stronger than giving them a finished product.

Later, when many customers exchanged ideas on QQ, I simply talked to them about how to use my ingredients to cook instead of discussing how delicious the fish or crabs were. Taobao actually sells pictures, and fresh produce is no different. Eventually, I stopped shouting about selling products and only chatted with food lovers about how to eat the best.

Actually, targeting a young customer base, many people like to eat, but they don't know how. They really want to eat, and this is when you need to teach them how to make it taste good. (In this regard, sometimes I even think Dianping is more suitable for fresh produce.)

Later, more and more customers gave positive feedback. I observed that 80% of them were foodies, and 20% came due to referrals from friends. For this, I specifically created several QQ groups, freely exchanging foodie experiences under the premise of not violating national laws, comparing seafood from other places. Don't fear competition; the more competition, the more it highlights the uniqueness and price advantage of your product.

In terms of model, Youcai actually wanted to do the electronic + commerce model, but I personally think this is also a failure. There are two paths to choose from: one is to become a platform-focused model like Taobao, establishing relationships with large offline wholesalers, and the platform itself does not engage in commerce. The second is to only do commerce, quickly building various regional distribution platforms, with many options including SNS, regional BBS, microblog marketing, WeChat marketing, etc., focusing on channel quality and price.

I lean towards the latter because it allows direct contact with customers.

My current model is mainly offline wholesale, primarily supplying hotels, self-service restaurants, and sushi shops, etc. Online, I mainly do retail, with prices slightly higher than offline. But I don't build my own platform; I only attach myself to a few popular local BBS communities, attracting customers through their traffic, and if customers respond positively to the product, it will quickly spread virally.

Sometimes, customers even upload photos of their own cooked dishes to "show off." Usually, I would give them some small extra rewards, and over time, almost no one in the QQ group would leave. Exchanges increased more and more, and by the second half of last year, I almost didn't need to promote myself online anymore, as customers helped with reviews.

Another benefit of this approach is solving the trust crisis. Doing online fresh produce in China, besides the difficulty of standardization (which I will discuss later), another point is that the public lacks trust in the hygiene of online fresh produce. To put it bluntly, after drinking Sanlu and eating Sudan Red eggs, the hardworking masses no longer have strong trust in merchants. However, if word-of-mouth spreads, this issue can be resolved.

In terms of profitability, I adopt a small-batch retail model. My minimum standard is five pounds per pack, and I do not retail one or two pounds at a time. Of course, the price is very attractive, and the quantity goes up accordingly.

5. Warehousing and Logistics

I dare say, aside from procurement costs, warehousing and logistics are absolutely another major expense. This includes manpower + transportation + warehousing costs + losses.

Youcai adopted the idea of using milk boxes, delivering door-to-door at fixed points every day. I think there is a significant problem with this approach: Suppose, just before I finish work, I order a pound of cabbage, two pounds of pork belly, and half a pound of ginger and garlic on Youcai. But when I get home, I find that what I saw online is very different from what I see in reality, and I decide not to buy it. And Youcai's customer service is great, agreeing to unconditional returns after communication.

My question is: What do I eat for dinner tonight?

If I have such an experience once, I probably won't buy agricultural by-products on Youcai again just to save a few yuan, because I'll go hungry...

And Youcai not only loses single orders and customers but also faces losses (some products degrade in quality overnight or after being out of storage for some time), along with the back-and-forth loss of manpower and transportation costs.

This is the biggest issue e-commerce faces when dealing with fresh produce: how to solve product standardization.

To be honest, regarding this issue, I currently don't have a good solution. I've heard complaints like "It's not quite the same as the online picture," and all I can do is compensate with service, offering refunds if unsatisfied.

Because my company is small, I can only save costs. Based on the number of regional orders, I deliver at fixed times and inspect the goods face-to-face. This way, I can both explain to customers on the spot and eliminate misunderstandings (some are indeed due to customers' lack of understanding of the product) and avoid the repeated losses of secondary deliveries.
