◎Introduction:

Devout believers in God - the brilliant scientist of the European Organization for Nuclear Research, Leonardo Vetra (portrayed by Efraim), devoted his life to proving the existence of God through scientific means. He and his adoptive daughter, the enigmatic and charming scientist Vittoria Vetra (played by Ayelet Zorer), conducted highly confidential experiments in the lab, successfully creating an extremely powerful form of energy - "antimatter."

Before this major discovery was announced, Leonardo was brutally murdered in the lab, with one eyeball gouged out by the killer, leaving a mysterious mark on his chest - "Illuminati"! Even more shocking was the disappearance of the hidden antimatter. If it meets matter, it will release tremendous energy capable of destroying a city. The goal of the Illuminati is to create a world government and a new world order where they rule supreme, turning the world into a state of slavery and fascism!

Robert Langdon (Tom Hanks), a symbology expert from Harvard University, was invited by the head of the European Organization for Nuclear Research to investigate this mysterious symbol. On the day he arrived, the missing antimatter was discovered somewhere under Vatican City. The antimatter outside the lab would explode automatically after twenty-four hours. That day happened to be the election day for the Pope. Catholic leaders from all over the world gathered in Vatican City, and the hidden antimatter was like a time bomb. If not found in time, the entire Vatican City would be obliterated instantly.

With the mission to save the core of the world's religions, Robert Langdon embarked on his adventure. In the process of uncovering the truth, Robert Langdon gradually approached the core of the event, and the truth shocked him greatly because it involved numerous symbols appearing in religion. These seemingly mystical religious symbols actually contained countless historical codes...

Behind-the-Scenes Production:

A Film Full of Religious Controversy

In 2006, "The Da Vinci Code," with its strong cast and best-selling original work, created immense publicity and excitement. This movie touched on many taboos in religion, causing people to have similar questions about the prequel nature of this sequel three years later. Before the film was released, the Catholic League led by William Donohue formally challenged director Ron Howard of "Angels & Demons," accusing Howard of tarnishing the sacred mission bestowed by religion. Regarding this point, Ron Howard said, "I must clarify that neither I nor 'Angels & Demons' stands against the Catholic Church. I believe that Catholics and similar churches should watch 'Angels & Demons' purely as an appreciation of the film."

For those who claim Dan Brown's original novel is full of lies, Ron Howard said, "I don't care about things outside of making movies. Whether it's true or false doesn't matter; my task is to make a good-looking movie. I want the audience, especially readers of the original novel, to have a fair judgment, not just about the story but also about the hard work of the actors and the appropriate adaptations we made to the movie. I anticipated that the Vatican would dislike this movie, but it wouldn't affect my filming. I don't expect the church people to like me, but I hope they can watch 'Angels & Demons' to hear another voice."

Regarding this controversial work filled with religious disputes, Ron Howard frankly talked about the various difficulties encountered before and after shooting "Angels & Demons": "When shooting 'The Da Vinci Code,' I encountered unprecedented resistance in my career. I knew how terrible it was to shake the deeply rooted beliefs in many people's minds, but I thought, if Dan Brown could write the original novel, what pressure am I facing? For me, the biggest challenge is actually building the scenes needed for the movie because those churches wouldn't provide us with venues for shooting. To pursue the rigor of the movie, we had to completely rebuild identical scenes ourselves, which required the cooperation of many departments. We spent a long time on this, and I was very afraid of any mistakes occurring, such as some laughable details. These would make the audience think we're not professional enough. So every part of the film I checked for a long time to ensure there were no problems. As for the real-life obstacles we faced, they have improved somewhat compared to three years ago."

Reunion of the Ace Team

The production team of "Angels & Demons" is the same as "The Da Vinci Code," with director Ron Howard, lead actor Tom Hanks, and others all participating. Naturally, the focus is on Tom Hanks. Returning to play the cryptography professor Robert Langdon, Tom Hanks said, "After finishing 'The Da Vinci Code,' I didn't think I would continue playing this character because the plot of that movie was complete and didn't seem to need a sequel. But the producers believed Dan Brown's novels all had adaptation potential, and 'The Da Vinci Code' was indeed a commercially successful work. Audiences also hoped to see stories from Robert Langdon's past, so the shooting plan for 'Angels & Demons' entered discussions. I like these intelligent characters; they have many stories."
