Will the filing of pre-installed mobile phone software become a "chicken rib"?

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 13:28:23

South People KTV

To curb the proliferation of pre-installed software on mobile phones, China's Ministry of Industry and Information Technology (MIIT) announced that starting November 1st, the "Notice on Strengthening the Administration of Mobile Smart Terminal Access" (hereinafter referred to as the "Notice") will be officially implemented.

According to the requirements of the "Notice," when manufacturers apply for access permits for mobile smart terminals, they must provide basic configuration information of the operating system version and pre-installed application software in their application materials. Furthermore, it is clearly stated that without prior notification to consumers and obtaining their consent, no software capable of arbitrarily collecting or modifying consumer personal information shall be pre-installed on the phone. This implies that MIIT will enhance its "gatekeeping" work before mobile phones go on sale—previously, only the phones themselves were inspected, and a qualified inspection was required to issue an access permit; now, supervision over the operating system and pre-installed software has been added.

However, as the "Notice" approaches implementation, industry evaluations have been mixed. Many people believe that while this regulation imposes constraints on pre-installed software at the time of phone production, there are no accompanying punitive measures, such as an exit system for pre-installed software that complements the entry system.

From this perspective, the "Notice" indeed appears somewhat redundant. I also believe that its guiding significance outweighs its actual effects. Nonetheless, it at least shows that MIIT's regulation of smartphones is becoming more comprehensive, and this process is keeping pace with the times.

In fact, as early as mid-last year, the "Notice" entered the "opinion solicitation" stage. At the time, pre-installed software was already an industry issue, but due to the much lower普及rate of smartphones compared to today, it had not yet reached the level of being a nuisance. Initially, the "Notice" was merely "proposed to strengthen the administration of access management for mobile communication terminal products that can connect to public mobile communication networks, have operating systems, and allow consumers to independently install application software." However, as the problem of pre-installed software worsened, the content of "pre-installed software registration" ultimately appeared in the soon-to-be-implemented "Notice," marking a breakthrough from nothing to something.

As for the practical effect of the current "Notice," it first needs to be discussed in terms of the categories and channels of pre-installed software on mobile phones.

Pre-installed software refers to software present in the phone when the consumer purchases it and turns it on for the first time. These can be divided into two categories: one category consists of software that consumers cannot directly uninstall, most of which are pre-installed at the time of phone production, with control in the hands of phone brand manufacturers and telecommunications operators. To uninstall these, consumers would need to gain root access through special methods, which would void the original factory warranty. The second category includes software that consumers can uninstall, mostly generated during the distribution process—for example, a mobile phone distributor might pre-install several software programs on each phone before distribution and charge the software providers based on the number of installations. In situations where profits in the distribution channel are squeezed and retail shop rent costs continue to rise, this phenomenon of "earning extra income" has long been an open secret within the industry.

According to the provisions of the "Notice," pre-installed software at the phone manufacturer and operator stages can be regulated, occurring before the phone goes on the shelf for sale. However, pre-installation at the channel level is beyond the scope of the "Notice." Thus, theoretically, the "Notice" itself has limited regulatory reach over the pre-installed software industry.

Therefore, if we say "a good beginning is half the battle," then there is still another half of the journey to protect consumer rights. Whether this journey can be successfully completed depends on the determination to rectify the situation and the severity of punishments. This will be a very complex process—from MIIT to operators and then to distributors, supervision needs to be carried out layer by layer and effectively implemented. The difficulty far exceeds what is described in the current "Notice."

Fortunately, the natural development of the market is assisting the state in improving this work. On one hand, public opinion has turned "pre-installed software" into a negative term. On the other hand, with the vigorous development of the channel pre-installation industry, some adverse competitive outcomes have emerged. In earlier years, the number of software pre-installed in the distribution phase was relatively small, and they were mostly quality-oriented. When consumers received the phone and saw these software programs, they might have gradually gotten used to them. Nowadays, from the perspective of product experience, even if the software itself is excellent, the sheer quantity of pre-installed software easily causes consumer resentment, potentially leading to resentment towards related phone brands—a result that these brands certainly do not wish to bear.
