Fishermen with corpses asking for prices? Peking University Hospital wronged? Do not believe one side of the story.

by alexsgxu on 2009-11-12 01:10:40

Fishermen carrying corpses and asking for ransom? Peking University Hospital wrongfully accused? Do not listen to one side of the story and jump to conclusions.

By Xu Shuguang, Chairman of GreenTree Inn and 519.com Wine Direct Sales Network

Recently, two news stories have been making waves in public discourse. One is about college students who heroically saved two middle school students but three college students drowned in the process. The nearby fishermen not only failed to rescue them but also demanded ransom with the bodies, claiming "we only retrieve corpses, not living people." Another story involves a medical professor at Peking University Hospital being "treated" by a student without medical qualifications, ultimately leading to his death. Instantly, societal opinions were filled with condemnation for the "immoral" fishermen and the "illegal" hospital, with many wishing to drown these unjust individuals in their own saliva.

This morning, I read an in-depth investigation on the first incident by Southern Weekly's front page, which revealed that the fishermen indeed had other circumstances. The accusation of failing to save lives was baseless. In fact, from the moment they learned someone had fallen into the water, the two fishermen had already steered their boat to the scene. However, due to their old age and frailty, they did not jump into the water to save anyone; instead, they threw life rings. The person demanding ransom for the corpses turned out to be Chen Bo, the boss of a "professional corpse retrieval company," while the two fishermen were merely hired by Chen Bo to retrieve the bodies. The journalist wrote: "When facing reporters, the two illiterate fishermen struggled greatly to explain the difficulties they faced that day... It's not hard to imagine how difficult it was for Chen Xuande and Chen Hengyun (the involved fishermen) to explain clearly amidst the chaos and despair, especially when confronted with the agitated girls."

Compared to the two illiterate fishermen, Peking University Hospital is very different. They at least know how to use the media to voice their side. In their statement, they criticized the journalists' reports as being contrary to the facts, insisting it wasn't "being treated to death," but rather "death due to ineffective resuscitation."

For both of these news stories, regardless of the ultimate truth or who is responsible for these accidents, we can at least identify one issue: we all tend to jump to conclusions too easily. In the overwhelming commentary surrounding the fishermen's case, none of us asked what the fishermen themselves had to say; we only heard what others claimed they said. In the case of Peking University Hospital, criticisms were rampant and defamatory words were everywhere even before the hospital issued any official statement.

Do not listen to one side of the story and jump to conclusions—this is an essential quality for excellent professional managers. As team leaders, one must comprehensively and multi-dimensionally understand the situation and grasp information before making any judgment to possibly make correct decisions. Listening to one side, being emotional, and acting arbitrarily can very likely lead to decision-making errors, causing disharmony within the team, affecting cohesion, and thus embarking on a very wrong or even failed path. I hope all store managers and administrators at GreenTree Inn can read between the lines of these two pieces of news, become rational, objective, and neutral judges and decision-makers, thereby growing into excellent professional managers!
