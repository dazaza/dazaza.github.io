After all, it's a disaster movie —— Brief Comments on the Blu-ray of *The Poseidon Adventure*

by diglog on 2010-10-12 16:17:08

I'm not a fan of "The Poseidon Adventure," but I enjoy disaster movies, especially the outrageous scenes and stunning sound effects. Wolfgang Petersen's "Poseidon" may fall far short of being a classic maritime disaster film and is surely sneered at by many professionals, but it is still a disaster movie that mercifully keeps its runtime within 100 minutes — any longer and there might have been riots in the theaters. To be honest, I'm not particularly fond of the director's Ocean Trilogy: "Das Boot," "The Perfect Storm," and "Poseidon." Although I've watched the first one twice, I didn't care much for them overall. On the contrary, I see this film as a step forward, finding it better than "The Perfect Storm." That movie, aside from George Clooney's aging face and Mark Wahlberg's "unhappy" demeanor, left little to remember and was tediously long. Looking back, this sound-heavy blockbuster was worth the price of the ticket. I watched it for the first time on IMAX, where the specially designed powerful surround sound was so intense that it was almost frightening — brutal, even reaching "pneumothorax-level" impact. Combined with the high-definition giant screen, I didn't find the movie unwatchable. However, my subsequent experience watching the 35mm version made me lose interest quickly. The dubbing edition had rough translations and poor mixing, making me drowsy halfway through. It seems that for this film, the visual and audio effects are the key selling points.

Warner released the Blu-ray version of this movie in mainland China. Compared to the previous DVD with embedded subtitles and subpar picture quality, this low-grade Blu-ray can be considered an improvement. A static menu with no pop-up options during playback, it essentially just copies the DVD format. The picture is clear, but the persistent graininess and occasional digital enhancement marks are somewhat annoying. In dark scenes and underwater sequences, the graininess becomes even more pronounced, though this may be related to the cinematography. It's disappointing that they couldn’t control it better. Nevertheless, the deep blacks, appropriate contrast, and minimal loss of detail are positive aspects.

The English Dolby 5.1 TrueHD audio basically delivers a stunning effect. Overall, it meets expectations without any major flaws, with good sound separation. The moment when the crane arm at the ship's bow is swept away by the waves and the steel cable passes through the surround channels left a deep impression on me. During the elevator drop scene, the sound effectively enhances the atmosphere, whether it's the sound effects, music, or characters' screams, all of which are distinct and clear. When water floods the cabin, the shattering glass and seawater impact are well-supported by the bass. Of course, the IMAX experience is hard to replicate, and the heart-wrenching impact sound is irreplaceable. Perhaps professional equipment would make a difference, but I don’t have high hopes for this disc.

The bonus features offer nothing new. My only impression of this Blu-ray is that it combines the content from the DVD extras with the HD main feature onto one disc. Similarly, I would only recommend borrowing this Blu-ray version to watch; spending 150 yuan isn't worth it.
