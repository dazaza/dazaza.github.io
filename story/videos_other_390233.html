An online store should do business by standing on the shoulders of giants.

by anonymous on 2011-03-25 14:46:07

Do you want to start a business? Do you want to make money? - Then open an online store!

According to a statistical report: 80% of white-collar workers have the idea of opening an online store, and 50% of them have already opened one. However, this report did not reveal a harsh reality: among the white-collar workers who opened online stores, 95% of them gave up halfway.

The network economy is booming, and e-commerce is at the forefront. More and more people are joining the ranks of online merchants, but have they all succeeded? What difficulties have they encountered?

Behind the giant sellers like "Lemon Green Tea," many entrepreneurs have followed suit. However, the media focuses on the brilliance of the successful ones, ignoring the pain and helplessness of those who failed.

I. Mr. Li's Online Store Story

Mr. Li was a programmer at an IT company, or rather, a former programmer, because he quit his job at the end of 2008 to start a "clothing online store" with his girlfriend.

Mr. Li's entrepreneurial impulse came from an encounter in the elevator. He often met delivery personnel squeezing into the elevator with large amounts of small packages. His intuition told him that there was a "big seller" in the same building. When he asked the delivery personnel how much the seller paid for shipping each month, he was told that it was a clothing seller who paid them 20,000 yuan per month for shipping fees.

"Shipping fees alone cost 20,000 yuan, and clothing is a high-profit product. How much profit do they make each month?" Mr. Li thought happily, got excited, quit his job, and became a "bona fide" online merchant.

However, Mr. Li, who didn't understand business, soon hit a wall. He went to the clothing market and wholesale dozens of styles of clothes in different colors and sizes, spending over ten thousand yuan of his savings in one go. Then came taking photos, uploading pictures, installing customer service software, and waiting for customers to come. To his frustration, very few people came to inquire about his small shop.

"Is it because I don't have enough 'diamonds'?" Mr. Li attributed his failure to "not having enough credit," so he took a risk and paid someone to "brush diamonds." Brushing diamonds is a prohibited behavior for inflating false credit, and if caught, the online store would have to be shut down.

Of course, more slip through the net, and Mr. Li thought as long as he didn't brush too much and wasn't caught, it would be fine. Mr. Li eventually successfully brushed to "diamond" status, making his online store look bustling with business and receiving countless good reviews. However, after a few days, he realized that "diamonds" had little value. While inquiries increased somewhat, clothes still couldn't be sold.

As the season began to cool down, fewer people were asking about summer clothes. A large amount of inventory goods made Mr. Li anxious, so he started seeking advice from some "big sellers" on how to run an online store. After being guided by big sellers, Mr. Li finally understood that registering an online store only takes a few minutes, but running a successful one is "deep water."

According to big sellers' advice: for an online store to have business, just like a physical store, it needs returning customers constantly buying. Without customer accumulation, merely relying on brushing diamonds, even if given a "crown," there will be no business. To gain sufficient old customer accumulation, one must "at least lose money for half a year." Generally speaking, if one can patiently stick it out for half a year, they can earn around several thousand yuan.

There are many people like Mr. Li who don't understand traditional business but impulsively quit their jobs to open online stores. Often these people struggle helplessly and hopelessly on the brink of survival; they need guidance and attention more than ever.

II. The Success Story of a Small Clothing Factory Owner

Indeed, many online stores on large shopping websites have made money. However, these "successful online stores" often have their reasons for success, and if analyzed one by one, it becomes clear that their success is inseparable from traditional commerce.

For example: some stores opened early, and many started making money from online stores in 2006 because they persisted until 2009, accumulating a large number of customers. This is akin to "getting the first bite" in traditional commerce. Of course, not everyone can "get the first bite," and there are also many who started making money from online stores in 2009. There is a story about a clothing factory owner that is truly remarkable.

This boss has a deep understanding of consumer psychology and owns his own clothing factory. Therefore, he first observed which Taobao sellers' clothes were "best-sellers," then had the factory produce them, and began selling them below cost to flood the market, causing smaller sellers to complain bitterly.

At the same time, he provided numerous discounts, such as free shipping, gifts, etc., and spent tens of thousands on advertising for these "money-losing deals," resulting in an initial investment loss of hundreds of thousands. With investment comes returns, and the returns were astonishing. The credibility of this big seller reached "crown" level, accumulating a large number of loyal customers.

The small boss then hired female models to shoot model photos of the clothing in overseas tourist spots like Paris and London. In this way, his clothing rose to a higher level. Young girls are naturally vain; seeing Paris and London labeled clothes, plus the fact that these clothes weren't expensive—just costing a few dozen yuan—they began placing orders like crazy.

At this point, the small boss was no longer losing money. The profit margin on his clothes, which was originally 20% at wholesale prices, skyrocketed to 200% at retail prices. Within a few months, he recouped his initial losses and gained a "super-popular large online store."

In fact, the skill level of this small clothing factory owner in operating his business far surpasses that of well-known big sellers like "Lemon Green Tea" featured in the media. Business experts who make money are often low-key people, so they are unwilling to accept public interviews from the media.

If one carefully analyzes the "big sellers" on Taobao and Paipai, a pattern emerges—these people are often experts in traditional business. Those who succeed in online sales are usually experienced and insightful in their respective industries.

The network world cannot avoid the issue of "income disparity." Some big sellers rent an entire floor for their warehouses, with inventories worth millions and employing dozens of people, while small sellers struggle to survive, bumping into obstacles everywhere. These phenomena constitute the various states of e-commerce.

III. Small Sellers Struggling to Survive: Where to Go?

In fact, small sellers face numerous problems, and online stores have not solved the various issues of traditional businesses.

For example, "inventory" is a huge problem. Take clothing as an example: women are always picky, and to satisfy their preferences, you'd probably need at least 100 styles. Each style has at least two colors and three different sizes. Even if you stock only one piece of each style, that's already 1,000 pieces in inventory.

The inventory problem in the traditional clothing sector also exists in online sales. I once visited the studio of a "big seller." Calling it a studio, it was actually an abandoned factory that had been renovated into a business park. This big seller occupied an entire floor. He didn't have many employees—only 30 people working in two small offices. Apart from his photography studio, most of the floor was used as a warehouse.

Inside the warehouse were nearly 300 styles of clothes. The big seller smiled and said, "This is worth several Mercedes-Benz cars," but his smile carried a hint of helplessness. As the weather gradually cooled, a large amount of summer clothing was being heavily discounted online. Buying ads, offering free shipping, giving away gifts—all methods were used to quickly liquidate the inventory at a loss.

Big sellers are like this, let alone small sellers. Small sellers like Mr. Li mentioned earlier lack both strength and financial resources. Once seasonal changes occur, they can only hold onto their goods. Even if they want to sell at a loss, they may not be able to sell them.

If challenged by "traditional commercial elites," it's still considered normal competition. What is even more devastating to small sellers like Mr. Li is the rampant presence of "picture parties."

"Picture parties" are also operated by some experts. Although these experts are not masters of traditional business, they excel at manipulating pictures. For example, the same piece of clothing photographed by them looks extremely desirable. The same 70-yuan piece of clothing, after being handled by the "picture party," looks like it can be sold for 170 yuan. And strangely enough, no one buys it when you sell it for 70 yuan, but they buy it when sold for 170 yuan.

Of course, becoming a "picture party" isn't easy. Mr. Li once wanted to become one and hired a photography company to take pictures. The enormous cost startled him: 300 yuan for a model and outdoor shooting fees, 80 yuan for shooting costs per style. Mr. Li backed off before even starting. It makes sense; if anyone could easily become a "picture party," then "picture parties" wouldn't survive either.

Many people think opening an online store is easy and jump into the industry without hesitation, even quitting their jobs to open one. Is opening an online store really that easy? Perhaps registration is indeed simple; within ten minutes, "the online store is set up."

But does simply registering mean you're running an online store? I don't think so.

Registering is merely the "first step of a long journey" in the process of running an online store. Ahead lie numerous difficulties, mountains, rivers, and stubborn enemies. Initial investment, advertising promotion, inventory issues, seasonal risks, picture problems, customer accumulation—each is a bottleneck and challenge. If any one of these problems isn't resolved, the online store won't succeed.

Unfortunately, many people imagine a too-beautiful future, seeing others pay 20,000 yuan in shipping fees and daydreaming, completely forgetting the old saying: "You only see the thief eating meat, not the thief getting beaten."

What should small shop owners stuck in a tight spot and facing survival crises do?

IV. The Front Desk Girl Secretly Opens an Online Store, Earning Four Thousand Yuan Monthly

Miss Fang runs her online store successfully and earns more than four thousand yuan monthly. Moreover, she hasn't quit her job and continues to receive her salary. Miss Fang repeatedly reminded me not to disclose her name because she still works at her company, earning 2,500 yuan monthly, and doesn't want to resign due to her online store.

Miss Fang works as a front desk receptionist for a company. Having just graduated, she lacks work experience but has a pretty face, so she became a receptionist. Being a front desk receptionist is essentially the face of the company; as long as she looks attractive, she's mostly decorative. She doesn't have much work to do, and her salary is the lowest, only around 2,500 yuan.

Miss Fang's day begins with handling trivial tasks. Sending contracts via courier; calling for water delivery when the water in the dispenser runs low; such tasks take up very little time.

If we consider her real work, it's playing games on 4399 Mini Game Station (www.4399.com), tank games, tractor games. Another part of her work is shopping on online stores. Since university, Miss Fang has been a fervent online shopper, spending hundreds of yuan monthly on cosmetics and clothes, all purchased online.

Of course, Miss Fang is a woman, and women get married; they don't need grand ambitions; hard work is men's responsibility. So she didn't have Mr. Li's kind of "grand vision" like opening an online store, expanding sales, then starting a factory, and eventually going public. Opening an online store was entirely accidental.

Miss Fang is a fervent online shopper and a veteran buyer. One day, while buying clothes, she noticed many sellers were selling the same clothes, even with identical pictures. Upon investigation, she learned that these clothing online stores themselves don't keep inventory but use a service called "online store drop shipping." This means the seller only displays the clothes' pictures on their online store. When a customer places an order, they place an order on the drop shipping agency's website, and the clothes are shipped directly from the agency.

"If I pretend to be an online store owner and wholesale one piece of clothing, wouldn't I save more money?" With a bargain-hunting mindset, Miss Fang went to a well-known online store drop shipping website, "Best-Seller Network" (www.baokuan.net).

Upon entering the site, Miss Fang was shocked. She found that many of the clothes on large shopping websites were "drop-shipped" from here, and the price difference was significant. But were the clothes here of good quality? Miss Fang had doubts, but they were quickly dispelled. On Best-Seller Network, there was a section called "Wow, Guangzhou VIP Information Technology Co., Ltd.! Free!" Every newly registered user could get a piece of clothing valued at 0 yuan for free with free shipping for 26 yuan. Since it was such a small amount, why not grab a piece to check the quality?

Without hesitation, Miss Fang placed an order. A few days later, she received a very nice piece of clothing. From then on, she became a regular visitor to Best-Seller Network, frequently "pretending to be an online store owner" to buy clothes. Of course, the shipping fees returned to normal prices, and the clothes were no longer free.

Miss Fang, who saved money extravagantly, suddenly thought one day, "All the money I worked so hard to earn goes towards cosmetics and clothes. Why don't I open an online store to earn some pocket money?" Thus, she transformed from "pretending to be an online store owner" buying clothes for herself into a genuine "online store owner."

Since she was doing "online store drop shipping," she didn't have to worry about sourcing, taking pictures, or even after-sales service because Best-Seller Network offered "unconditional exchange services." Miss Fang's business started intermittently. Apart from calling couriers, ordering purified water, and arranging meals for colleagues, she basically played games and ran her online store.

Like all new online store owners, Miss Fang's store initially had no business. Of course, she didn't invest much effort. All she needed to do was upload the "online store data package" from Best-Seller Network to her own online store.

Miss Fang had a good attitude. In the first month, she earned 500 yuan and was happy: "This month's pocket money is covered."

In the second month, she earned 900 yuan and felt, "The rent is covered."

In the third month, she earned 2,000 yuan and was even happier: "If my husband sells the car, the car loan doesn't have to come out of my salary."

In the fourth month, she earned 4,000 yuan and thought, "Wow! If this continues, I can use this to pay off the mortgage, and a house will be free."

Because of her positive attitude and Best-Seller Network's continuous updates of quality sources, Miss Fang's business flowed continuously. She only needed to sell the goods, and shipping was completed by clicking a mouse online. After-sales service was unnecessary, unconditional exchanges, nothing else to say. Now, the only difference is that she no longer plays "tanks" at work but spends her whole day monitoring the customer service software and chatting with customers.

Miss Fang is still a front desk receptionist, and her colleagues know she opens an online store in her spare time. But they don't know that her monthly income from the online store has surpassed her receptionist salary. Miss Fang doesn't think that working and running an online store conflict; she wants to earn both online store money and her salary.

IV. Small Online Store Owners Should "Stand on the Shoulders of Giants to Do Business"

Business elders often say: people have two legs, money has four. People chasing money can't catch up, but money chasing people can't escape.

If we analyze Miss Fang and Mr. Li, both opening clothing online stores, one struggled greatly, while the other ran smoothly. Beyond the issue of attitude, we find that men often have the problem of "too much ambition," which is often the root cause of failure.

Chinese men who start businesses often have a bit of a "poor scholar, acid scholar" temperament. Despite limited capabilities and finances, they insist on appearing overconfident, ambitious, and unbeatable, which is the most failing and foolish approach.

Once ambition grows to the extent of "eating up the entire industrial chain," it becomes extremely ignorant. For instance, some online store owners strongly oppose "online store drop shipping," feeling that profits are taken away, wanting to reserve their own inventory to "eat up the entire profit," which is extremely foolish.

Keeping your own inventory can certainly "eat up the entire profit," but do you have enough money to stock hundreds of styles? Even if you invest over a million, do you have more money to update the inventory, considering clothing styles change frequently? What about seasonal changes? How do you liquidate the inventory, and how do you control the risks? Moreover, even if you have tens of millions to stock inventory, you're only eating up a "very small portion of the profit." The profit margin of online wholesale itself won't exceed 10%, while the profit margin of online retail is generally between 50% to 100%. Spending such a high cost for such a small profit is irrational.

The biggest feature of industrial society is social division of labor and industrial chains. Industrial chains, although a "profit link," increase efficiency and reduce costs, ultimately lowering costs.

For example:

Farmers grow wheat and sell it to flour merchants, earning "wheat profit."

Flour merchants process wheat into flour, earning "flour profit."

Bakers turn flour into bread, earning "bread profit."

Adding up these "three layers of profit" is still cheaper than growing wheat yourself and baking bread. This is the value created by the social "industrial chain" division of labor: making goods more affordable.

Take Baowan (www.baoan.net) as an example. They use centralized procurement to lower clothing costs. Even with added profit margins, it's still cheaper than traditional procurement channels. Their centralized procurement volume is large, allowing for stable quality in bulk production. With large procurement volumes, manufacturers have no choice but to follow their lead, providing "unconditional exchange" services. Even with the added profit margin, it's still cheaper than ordinary channels. This is the value created by the social division of labor in the industrial chain.

DuPont, a powerful Fortune 500 company in the chemical raw materials industry, sells its raw materials to customers who make products. The profit margin increases when raw materials are turned into products. Why doesn't DuPont, such a large company, "consume the entire industrial chain profit" by producing the products themselves? Clearly, society has a division of labor and industrial chain. Small online store owners who want to succeed should learn to harness the industrial chain and learn to do business "standing on the shoulders of giants."

Newton once said, "I see further because I stand on the shoulders of giants." Conversely, if you only stand on a small stool, how can you see far?
