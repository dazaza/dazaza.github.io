Bill leaned back against the gate that divided the bee-farm grounds from the high road and indulged in pleasant musings. He was alone. Elizabeth was walking up the driveway on her way to the house to deliver the news to Nutty. James, the cat, who had come down from the roof of the outhouse, was sharpening his claws on a nearby tree. After the whirlwind of excitement he'd experienced over the past few hours, the tranquility of this moment appealed strongly to Bill. It suited the mood of quiet happiness that enveloped him. Quietly happy—that was how he felt now that it was all over. The intense emotional heat had subsided into a gentle glow of contentment conducive to thought.

He thought tenderly of Elizabeth. She had turned to wave before going into the house, and he was still smiling foolishly. What a wonderful girl! How lucky he was! Funny how they had come together! Fate, huh? Talk about destiny! He bent down to tickle James, who, having finished sharpening his claws, was now enjoying a friction massage against his leg, and began to reflect on the inscrutable ways of fate. Funny thing, fate! Most extraordinary! Suppose he had never gone down to Marvis Bay that time. He had wavered between half a dozen places; it was pure chance that he had chosen Marvis Bay. If he hadn’t, he would never have met old Nutcombe. Probably old Nutcombe had wavered between half a dozen places too. If they hadn’t both happened to choose Marvis Bay, they would never have met. And if they hadn’t been the only visitors there, they might never have gotten to know each other. And if old Nutcombe hadn’t happened to slice his approach shots, he would never have put him under an obligation. Queer old fellow, old Nutcombe, leaving a guy he hardly knew from Adam a cool million quid just because he cured him of slicing.

It was at this point in his reflections that it suddenly occurred to Bill that he hadn't yet given a thought to what was immeasurably the most important of any of the things that ought to be occupying his mind just now. What was he to do about this Lord Dawlish business? Life at Brookport had so accustomed him to being plain Bill Chalmers that it had absolutely slipped his mind that he was really Lord Dawlish, the one man in the world whom Elizabeth looked on as an enemy. What on earth was he to do about that? Tell her? But if he told her, wouldn’t she leave him on the spot?

This was awful. The dreamy sense of well-being left him. He straightened himself to face this problem, ignoring the hint of James, who was weaving circles about his legs expectant of more tickling. A man cannot spend his time tickling cats when he has to concentrate on a dilemma of this kind. Suppose he didn’t tell her? How would that work out? Was a marriage legal if the guy getting married went through it under a false name? He seemed to remember seeing a melodrama in his boyhood whose plot hinged on that very point. Yes, it began to come back to him. An unpleasant bargee with a black moustache had said, "This woman is not your wife!" and caused a great deal of unpleasantness; but there, in its usual slipshod way, memory failed. Had subsequent events proved the bargee right or wrong? It was a question for a lawyer to decide. Jerry Nichols would know.

Well, thank goodness, there was plenty of time to send Jerry Nichols a cable, asking for his professional opinion, and to get the straight tip long before the wedding day arrived.
