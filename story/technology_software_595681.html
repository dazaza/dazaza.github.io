Who Stole My Sleep?

Summer nights are a high incidence period for insomnia, and many people have their own set of adjustment methods. However, these methods may hide underlying factors that lead to insomnia. On summer nights, one should not sleep in certain ways.

Sleeping with exposed chest and abdomen

In the hot summer weather, many people like to sleep shirtless. Although this brings a cool sensation, it can easily cause an imbalance of heat inside the body, leading to blood vessel contraction and reduced blood supply, which may trigger gastrointestinal nerve dysfunction. In severe cases, it can lead to chest tightness and shortness of breath, affecting sleep. It is best to wear a sleeping garment or cover the chest and abdomen with a small blanket or towel during summer nights. This way, one can sweat effectively while preventing catching a cold and habitual diarrhea.

Sleeping with fans or air conditioners on

Before sleeping, fans and air conditioners should be used scientifically. Temperatures that are too low or direct airflow can cause discomfort, leading to colds and serious harm to the brain. Negative ions in the air can inhibit the central nervous system and relieve brain fatigue. However, air conditioners absorb too many negative ions, increasing positive ions indoors. The imbalance of positive and negative ions can easily cause the nervous system to become disordered, thereby affecting sleep quality.

Sleeping with lights on

Medical research has found that sleeping with lights on inhibits the secretion of melatonin in the human body, reducing immunity. Melatonin secretion can inhibit the excitation of the sympathetic nervous system, lower blood pressure, slow heart rate, allow the heart to rest, enhance immunity, eliminate fatigue, and even kill cancer cells. When sleeping with lights on, the secretion of melatonin is inhibited, not only affecting sleep quality but also indirectly affecting human immunity.

Insomnia accomplices:

If dinner choices are wrong, they may keep you tossing and turning throughout the long summer night. Which foods "steal" sleep?

Gas-producing foods

Some foods produce more gas during digestion, hindering normal sleep, such as beans, potatoes, sweet potatoes, taro, and corn.

Spicy and salty foods

Spicy foods like chili, garlic, and raw onions can cause some people to experience stomach burning or indigestion, interfering with sleep.

Too greasy

An overly greasy dinner or eating too much high-fat food can increase the workload on the intestines, stomach, liver, gallbladder, and pancreas, stimulating the nervous center and keeping it active, leading to insomnia.

Drinking alcohol, coffee

Caffeine in coffee stimulates the nervous system, accelerating breathing and heartbeat, raising blood pressure, making one energetic and difficult to fall asleep. Additionally, many people use alcohol to fall asleep quickly, but the cost of drinking before bed might result in waking up several times during the night or feeling unusually tired the next day.

Resisting Unavoidable Factors

Besides reasons we can exclude ourselves, many uncontrollable factors make summer nights restless.

High temperature

When nighttime temperatures exceed 25°C, the central nervous system becomes more excited, making it hard to fall asleep. Also, the longer daylight hours mean earlier sunlight exposure, directly increasing brain alertness. Thus, even if one appears to be sleeping, the body gradually transitions to wakefulness. This is why, despite napping in the morning after late-night sleep in summer, one still feels sleep-deprived.

Mosquitoes

There are various ways to repel mosquitoes, and everyone naturally develops their own techniques over decades of battling them. However, during mosquito eradication, many find that using mosquito repellents and pads eliminates mosquitoes but still prevents sleep due to strong scents stimulating the cerebral cortex. Therefore, when choosing mosquito repellents, it's best to opt for scent-free products, even if packaging claims pure natural plant fragrances, caution is advised.

Noise

In summer, with longer days and shorter nights, many people's nightlife becomes richer, while others unfortunately begin noisy summer nights. Using earplugs against noise might help, but prolonged use can be painful. Try playing soothing music like lullabies, which might induce deep sleep similar to childhood memories.

Sleep Quotient:

First proposed by American scholars, it refers to the ratio between one's sleep quality and intellectual and health conditions. Surveys in big cities like Beijing and Shanghai show that 40% of people suffer from severe sleep deprivation, 80% endure fatigue caused by poor sleep, and 50% deal with irritability due to bad sleep. Yet, only 6% feel the need to take action. "Oversleeping" has become a social issue, and promoting sleep quotient awareness is crucial.

Since ancient times, people have regarded sleep as a very important method for maintaining health. Confucius said: “Do not talk while eating, do not speak while sleeping.” The earliest medical classic in China, the "Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon," also states: “In the three months of summer, go to bed late and rise early.” The importance of sleep is evident, but finding one's perfect sleep varies from person to person. Try selecting suitable methods based on actual situations!

Counting sheep cannot induce sleep, instead keeping the brain continuously excited, making it harder to fall asleep.

Soporific Foods

Summer diets should be light, avoiding greasiness, consuming easily digestible foods, replenishing water, salt, and vitamins. Try eating before 7 PM (or at least three hours before bedtime), eating until seven-tenths full. If hungry before bed, drink yogurt or eat fruit. However, what foods are suitable for consumption before bed requires careful consideration.

Finding Your Perfect Sleep

Appropriate exercise can aid sleep (appropriate exercise means feeling pleasant rather than fatigued). Engage in 30 minutes of aerobic exercise six hours before bed, but never exercise right before sleeping.

Small Exercises to Help Sleep

Massage the Yongquan point: When lying in bed and unable to fall asleep, massage the Yongquan point, doing back-and-forth friction until the soles of your feet feel warm, then switch to the other foot. Alternating this way focuses your brain's attention on your feet, and over time, as you grow tired, you'll feel sleepy and want to sleep. Persisting regularly can also provide health benefits.

Combing hair relaxes nerves: Combing hair before bed promotes smooth blood flow, enhancing brain cell nutrition supply, delaying brain aging. Combing hair before bed improves sleep and raises sleep quality. After a day of tense work, combing hair relaxes nerves, eliminates fatigue, and allows the brain to rest well.

Hot Foot Baths Before Bed

Soaking feet in hot water before bed promotes foot blood vessel expansion and accelerates circulation. With numerous foot acupoints, hot water stimulation provides excellent health benefits, especially for those suffering from insomnia and varicose veins. Suggest soaking feet for 15-30 minutes before bed, with water temperature ideally between 30°C-40°C. If possible, take a hot bath 30 minutes before bed each night.

Find the Right Sleep Induction Method for You

Summer nights' difficulty in sleeping mainly results from temperature and other external factors. Although weather is unchangeable, there are many remedies for insomnia. Which one suits you best?

Vitamin B1, B6: Occasional insomnia (not habitual) can be treated with Vitamin B1, Vitamin B6, over-the-counter sedative sleep aids, etc. GABA has the function of adjusting vegetative nerve functions, stabilizing emotions, reducing anxiety and tension, improving sleep.

Mozart's Music vs Fan Noise: Compared to other classical music, Mozart's music is most effective in treating insomnia. It normalizes blood pressure and pulse, reducing nervous tension. However, if you're not a fan of his music, you can listen to other soothing tunes. Sounds like waves hitting the shore or seagulls calling are even better as they relax you further. If none of these work, turn on a fan—monotonous buzzing may put you to sleep.

Walking the Dog: Interacting with four-legged friends significantly reduces nervous tension, a fact confirmed by scientists. Moreover, whether you like it or not, you must walk your dog in the evening. A half-hour walk before bed soothes the nervous system well. Avoid negative emotions or anxious thoughts while walking. All these will give you a peaceful sleep.

Boring Method: Watching boring books or TV programs before bed quickly fills the brain (similar to memorizing a lot of foreign language grammar in one night). Sometimes, we notice that when we find something uninteresting or boring, our blood pressure drops, we feel lethargic, and we really want to sleep. Conversely, when we concentrate, we don't feel tired. So, the boring method can be a countermeasure for insomnia.

Apple: Legend has it that Dumas suffered from insomnia due to overwork. He insisted on eating an apple every night before sleeping and forced himself to go to bed and wake up on time. Miraculously, his insomnia improved. Later, scientists discovered that the aromatic components in apples contain 92% alcohol and 6% carbonyl compounds. Their strong fragrance has a powerful calming effect on the nerves, inducing sleep, which is one of the key reasons Dumas cured his insomnia by eating apples.

Foods That Help Sleep

Milk: The amount of tryptophan in milk is sufficient to induce sleep. The warmth and fullness from drinking milk also enhances its soporific effect.

Walnuts: Beneficial for sleep.

Longan: Nourishes blood and calms the spirit, treating insomnia, forgetfulness, and neurasthenia.

Lotus seeds: Adding a pinch of salt to lotus seed hearts, boiling them in water, and drinking before bed helps.

Vinegar: Taking a tablespoon of vinegar mixed with warm water and drinking slowly while closing eyes and staying calm helps induce sleep quickly.

Grapes contain melatonin. Wine contains antioxidants and alcohol, with higher levels of melatonin, aiding sleep even more.
