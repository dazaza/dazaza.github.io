China News Service, Liuzhou, February 5th (Lei Aolin, Yang Qiang) - The sounds of firecrackers and gongs were continuous. In front of Xilai Temple by the Liujiang River, the stage was festooned with lanterns and decorations, while performers in beautiful costumes sang and danced. On February 5th, the inaugural Xilai Temple New Spring Festival Cultural Fair in Liuzhou City, Guangxi Zhuang Autonomous Region, attracted a large crowd. A 200-meter-long street of local delicacies allowed tourists to indulge their taste buds, while the dazzling array of handicrafts drew many citizens to stop and make purchases.

As the Lantern Festival approached in the Year of the Dragon, this festive temple fair seemed to announce that the more than half-month-long cadmium pollution incident on the Long River in Guangxi had gradually faded from the lives of Liuzhou residents. By the riverbank opposite Xilai Temple, numerous tourists smiled for photos as souvenirs.

A citizen enjoying luosifen (a local dish) grinned as he told reporters: "Today is the second day of the temple fair, with plenty of delicious food and fun activities. It's lively." The once highly publicized water contamination event has gradually become blurred in people’s minds.

Upstream of the Pearl River, the Liujiang River flows gently. The nearshore water is clear to the bottom, and many citizens held large umbrellas either fishing or appreciating the scenery. Huang Lijie, who is about to turn 70 and has been fishing by the Liujiang River for three or four decades, "stationed" at the riverside since 9 a.m. He told reporters that regardless of weather conditions during the Spring Festival, he came to fish every day by the Liujiang River, which served as his exercise routine.

Huang Lijie was quite calm when discussing the short-term panic buying and stockpiling of water that occurred in Liuzhou between January 24th and 26th. He said: "I didn't hoard water; I trust that the water provided by the government should be of quality assurance."

Tang, who was also holding a fishing rod, caught a golden carp from the Liujiang River. "This is for appreciation," Tang smiled as he told reporters, "The Liuzhou government was transparent. I learned about the situation on TV on January 18th, and the water pollution did not significantly impact my Spring Festival."

On February 4th and 5th, reporters visited several large supermarkets in Liuzhou. The rare "water rush" had long subsided, and the supermarkets were orderly. Restaurants and hotels in Liuzhou continued to operate normally, with staff assuring customers, "There is no problem with the water."

On the afternoon of February 4th, the emergency command center of Liuzhou for dealing with the sudden environmental incident on the Long River announced that the cadmium concentration in the Liujiang River showed an overall downward trend over the past two days, indicating that the emergency response work had entered a stable phase.

Gan Jinglin, deputy commander of the emergency command center for handling the sudden environmental incident on the Long River in Liuzhou, stated that so far, no cases of cadmium poisoning have been reported in Liuzhou, and the water quality of the Liujiang River is safe, with no such incidents expected.

According to monitoring data as of 16:00 on February 5th, the cadmium concentration at the Ximen Cliff upstream of the Liujiang River was 0.0120 mg/L, exceeding the standard by 1.4 times; at the point 3 kilometers downstream of the confluence of the Long River and Rong River, the cadmium concentration was 0.0040 mg/L; at the Lotang section, it was 0.0030 mg/L; at the Xinwu section, it was 0.0030 mg/L; at the water intake of the Hexi Water Plant, it was 0.0020 mg/L. All these values comply with China's GB3838-2002 Surface Water Environmental Quality Standards (cadmium ≤ 0.005 mg/L).
