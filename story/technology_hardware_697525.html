[Repost] Frustration education is an important part of parent-child education.

by koucai4472 on 2012-03-08 16:06:03

There is currently a rather popular view in the West that happiness is both an external state and an internal quality. The state of happiness comes and goes easily; for example, giving a child a new toy will make them cheer, but this emotion will soon fade. However, the quality of happiness is very stable; it is a disposition towards feeling good and generating optimism. Parents and teachers play an important role in cultivating this quality of happiness in children. What aspects do you think parents can start with to cultivate this quality?

Firstly, parents should focus on creating a relaxed family atmosphere. Under the premise of grasping the big picture or major principles, they should give their children as many choices as possible, instead of forcing conformity based on their own preferences. Even though sometimes a child's choice may bring them pain, such as a toddler insisting on eating spicy food, they can learn something from the pain. Every parent should clearly recognize that they cannot protect their children for their entire lives. Children need to go out into society, experience hardships, and establish extensive interpersonal relationships, which are built on the relationship between children and parents, as well as the interactions between parents and others. Enthusiastic, sincere, and tolerant parents have a great influence on their children. Good interpersonal relationships are the foundation for overcoming setbacks.

Secondly, in some affluent countries, people have deeply realized that material conditions do not necessarily correlate with a child's sense of happiness. Under appropriate material living guarantees, we need to teach children how to create a cheerful mood internally beyond just material things. A popular saying nowadays is "Happy people lead a balanced life." We emphasize that in "setback education," we should cultivate the ability of children to gain happiness from multiple aspects. Relying solely on one pursuit for happiness often leads to eventual pain. One group of children may be gloomy all day because their father does not let them play video games, while another group quickly finds joy in another game. Broad interests and hobbies, as well as the flexible adjustment of goals, are especially important for children with "special skills."

Thirdly, another key component of "setback education" is cultivating a child's resilience after facing setbacks. Optimistic children are not without pain, but they can quickly free themselves from it and become reinvigorated. Parents should seriously cultivate in their children the confidence and skills to "see the light in the darkness."

In summary, setback education is about enabling children to not only find happiness through gifts from others or the outside world, but also to stimulate an instinct within themselves to seek happiness. In this way, they can remain calm and always optimistic in the face of any setback. I want to particularly remind parents that your attitude towards life greatly influences your child's understanding of setbacks.
