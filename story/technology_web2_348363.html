Chapter Six, Section One: Essential Nutrients for the Human Body

Nutrition is the comprehensive process of obtaining and utilizing food, an important factor in ensuring normal human growth and development. Factors influencing human growth and development are multifaceted; genetic factors determine the possibility of growth and development; numerous external environmental factors can affect the speed of growth and development. Among these external factors, nutritional factors are extremely important for human growth and development. Reasonable nutrition is an important guarantee for enhancing health, improving work efficiency, preventing and treating diseases, and prolonging life. Malnutrition not only causes various physiological functions to decline but also reduces the body's ability to adapt to and defend against changes in the external environment, and may even become a causative factor for certain diseases.

Exercise and nutrition are both important factors in maintaining and promoting human health. Nutrients are the material basis for constructing the body's tissues, while exercise can enhance the functional capabilities of the body. The scientific combination of nutrition and exercise can more effectively promote physical growth and development and improve health levels. If one focuses solely on nutrition and lacks physical exercise, it will cause muscle relaxation, obesity, and weakened activity abilities. On the other hand, if one only values simple physical exercise without necessary nutritional guarantees, the consumed nutrients within the body cannot be compensated, affecting physical development and health.

During exercise, nutrients in the body are consumed or broken down, so they must be replenished. Timely replenishment after exercise is not only necessary for the physiological recovery process but also scientifically utilizes nutrients to promote the effects of physical exercise, thereby improving health levels. The level of energy metabolism during exercise and the need for nutrients depend on various factors such as different types of exercises, intensity, density, duration, age, weight, exercise level, and environment. The main nutrients include six categories: organic proteins, fats, carbohydrates, vitamins, inorganic salts, and water. Below, each category is introduced separately.

(1) Proteins

1. Main Functions of Proteins in the Body

Proteins are the most important substances in life activities. Their primary physiological functions in the human body include constructing body tissues, promoting growth and development, forming enzymes and hormones, regulating acid-base balance and overall physiological functions, enhancing the body's disease resistance and immune capabilities, and providing energy. Once the body lacks proteins, it first affects the growth and development of the body, causing muscle atrophy, even anemia, and symptoms such as decreased disease resistance, hormonal imbalance, fatigue, unhealed wounds, etc.

2. Sources and Daily Requirements of Proteins

The daily diet's meat, eggs, milk, etc., are the main sources of animal proteins, while beans are the main source of plant proteins. Rice, noodles, and other cereal foods contain lower amounts of protein, about 10%, but due to their large proportion in people’s diets in China, they also become an important source of plant proteins. Generally, animal and plant proteins should each account for 50% in the diet.

The Chinese Nutrition Society suggests that the adult protein intake in our country should be 1.0~1.5g per kilogram of body weight per day. Adolescents should consume more, up to around 3.0g. People participating in physical exercise should moderately increase their intake based on their original needs.

(2) Fats

1. Main Functions of Fats in the Body

Fats in the body constitute cell membranes and some important tissues, participate in metabolism, supply energy, protect internal organs, maintain body temperature, and promote the absorption of fat-soluble vitamins.

2. Sources and Requirements of Fats

Animal fats come from various animal oils, butter, egg yolks, etc., while plant fats mainly originate from various vegetable oils. Additionally, nuts like walnuts, peanuts, and sunflower seeds can provide abundant fat components for the body. Given China's current living standards, ordinary diets generally meet the daily fat requirements. Carbohydrates in food can easily convert into fats within the body for utilization or storage.

(3) Carbohydrates

1. Main Functions of Carbohydrates in the Body

The primary role of carbohydrates in the body is to supply energy, with 60% of the energy required by the human body supplied by carbohydrates. They also form tissue components and participate in the metabolism of other substances, have special nutritional effects on the central nervous system, regulate lipid metabolism, detoxify, and protect liver function.

A lack of carbohydrates in the body leads to a decrease in blood sugar levels, primarily affecting the brain function of the central nervous system, reducing its excitability, causing delayed reactions, weakness in the limbs, reduced coordination of movements, and even fainting, making continued exercise impossible.

2. Sources and Daily Requirements of Carbohydrates

Carbohydrate sources are relatively extensive, with rice, noodles, grains in food being about 80% carbohydrates, thus the daily dietary supply is relatively sufficient. You can also directly consume appropriate amounts of candy and sugary drinks to increase glycogen reserves in the liver and muscles. Normal daily diets generally meet carbohydrate needs without emphasizing excessive supplementation.

(4) Vitamins

Vitamins are a class of nutrients indispensable for maintaining human life and regulating normal functions. They are stored in small amounts in the body and must be regularly obtained from food. There are many types of vitamins, classified into two major categories according to their nature: fat-soluble and water-soluble. The former includes vitamins A, D, E, K, while the latter includes vitamin B, C, etc. Various vitamins do not form tissue materials in the body nor provide energy; instead, they each have specific functions, generally regulating substance-energy metabolism and ensuring physiological functions.

1. Vitamin A

Its main function is to maintain normal vision and ensure the integrity of eye structures and upper eyelid tissues. A deficiency can lead to a decline in visual acuity and adaptation, even night blindness. The best sources of vitamin A are found in the livers and fish roe of various animals, dairy products, egg yolks, and yellow-green vegetables like carrots and spinach.

2. Vitamin D

Vitamin D is crucial for calcium-phosphorus metabolism and bone growth in the body, promoting calcium absorption, bone calcification, and normal tooth development. A deficiency affects calcium absorption, leading to severe bone salt dissolution and decalcification. Major sources of vitamin D include cod liver oil, egg yolks, and dairy products. Skin's 7-dehydrocholesterol converts into vitamin D under sunlight ultraviolet radiation, so deficiencies are generally uncommon.

3. Vitamin E

Vitamin E enhances the body's tolerance to oxygen deficiency, reduces tissue-cell oxygen consumption, dilates blood vessels, improves circulation, enhances cardiac function, increases muscle strength and aerobic endurance. When combined with vitamin C, it can alleviate and prevent arteriosclerosis. Major sources of vitamin E include animal foods, wheat germ, corn oil, and green leafy vegetables.

4. Vitamin B

Its main function plays a significant role in carbohydrate metabolism, promoting glycogen generation in the liver and muscles, protecting nervous system functions. Adequate vitamin B effectively alleviates bodily fatigue. Vitamin B is widely present in cereals and can also be taken as tablets.

5. Vitamin C

Vitamin C strengthens oxidative-reduction processes within the body, enhances ATP enzyme activity, providing more energy for maintaining exercise, increasing endurance, slowing fatigue, promoting physical recovery, facilitating wound healing, promoting hematopoietic functions, participating in detoxification processes, and enhancing the body's disease resistance. Vitamin C is widely present in vegetables and fruits.

(5) Minerals (Inorganic Salts)

There are many types of minerals in the body, totaling approximately 5% of body weight, and are the main substances constituting body tissues and regulating physiological functions. The more abundant ones include calcium, magnesium, potassium, sodium, sulfur, phosphorus, etc., while others like iron, iodine, fluorine, zinc are present in small amounts, known as trace elements. During the body's metabolic processes, a certain amount of minerals is expelled through various pathways every day, so they must be replenished from food. Minerals are widely distributed in food, and normal diets generally satisfy the body's needs. Among them, calcium and iron are the most prone to deficiency.

1. Calcium and Phosphorus

Calcium's primary role in the body is to form bones and teeth, maintain normal neuromuscular excitability, and participate in the coagulation process. Adults require 0.6g of calcium daily, while children, pregnant women, and the elderly need higher amounts. Heavy sweating can increase calcium excretion, requiring 1.0~1.5g daily. Foods rich in calcium include shrimp skin, kelp, soy products, sesame seeds, hawthorn, green leafy vegetables, etc. Since calcium and phosphorus are closely related within the body, they must reach a certain concentration level in the blood to jointly perform their physiological functions. Therefore, when supplementing calcium, attention should also be paid to ingesting phosphorus from protein-rich foods.

2. Iron

Iron's main role is to form hemoglobin; iron deficiency can affect hemoglobin production, causing iron-deficiency anemia, reducing blood oxygen-carrying capacity, and lowering overall body functions. Adult males require about 12mg of iron daily, while adolescents and females require about 15mg daily. Heavy sweating can increase iron loss, necessitating additional supplementation. Foods richest in iron include animal livers and blood, while other foods like egg yolks, meats, soy products, brown sugar, sea buckthorn berries, etc., also contain abundant iron.

(6) Water

Water's primary role in the body is to form the main component of the organism, participate in all substance metabolisms in the body, complete the transport of substances in the body, regulate body temperature, and ensure normal glandular secretions.

The water content in the body must remain constant; the body does not store excess water nor allow dehydration. Failure to timely replenish water will affect normal physiological functions. After heavy sweating, water should be supplemented along with an appropriate amount of salt to compensate for lost electrolytes.

Section Two: Implementation of Daily Balanced Nutrition

Understanding the functions, sources, and requirements of essential nutrients for the human body serves as a guideline for proper dietary nutrition. Based on this knowledge, we can arrange our daily diet to achieve balanced nutrition and enhance physical health immunity.

I. Main Sources of Nutrients

(1) Carbohydrates. Primarily derived from plant-based foods, such as grains, sweet potatoes, potatoes, vegetables, fruits, white sugar, fruit sugar, honey.

(2) Proteins. Divided into animal and plant proteins, respectively sourced from milk, eggs, seafood, livestock meat, poultry meat, grains, and legumes.

(3) Fats. Mainly sourced from animal and plant oils (such as soybean oil, rapeseed oil, corn oil, sesame oil, etc.).

(4) Inorganic Salts. Calcium, phosphorus, and iron are mainly sourced from milk and dairy products, seafood, and animal offal; copper, zinc, and magnesium are mainly sourced from green leafy vegetables, fungi algae, and hard-shelled nuts.

(5) Vitamins. Vitamins A, D, and E are mainly sourced from milk and dairy products, eggs, whole grains, and animal offal; vitamins B and C are mainly sourced from grains, vegetables, fruits, and animal offal.

II. Nutritional Components and Roles of Various Foods

(1) Grain-based Foods

Grains are the staple food of the Chinese people. 60%~70% of the calories and 70% of the carbohydrates in the diet, as well as about 50% of the proteins, B vitamins, and inorganic salts, are provided by grains. Millet and corn contain carotene, while the germ and bran of grains contain vitamin E.

Grains contain low levels of essential amino acids such as lysine, phenylalanine, and methionine, so they are not ideal protein sources. To improve the physiological value of grain proteins, they must be eaten together with complementary foods to achieve mutual supplementation.

(2) Legumes and Bean Products

Legumes are a specialty of China and include soybeans, broad beans, peas, red beans, green beans, black beans, and many other varieties, among which soybeans have the highest nutritional value. Legumes are rich in proteins, containing approximately 20%~40%, with an amino acid composition similar to animal proteins, making them high-quality complete proteins. They are also rich in plant oils, about 15%~20%, which are unsaturated fatty acids, easy to digest and absorb. In addition, they contain calcium, phosphorus, iron, and B vitamins. Tofu, soy milk, bean sprouts, and other bean products have high nutritional value and are easier to digest and absorb than dry beans.

(3) Livestock Meat and Offal

Livestock refers to pork, beef, mutton, horse, donkey, dog meat, etc., with most people primarily consuming pork.

The protein content in livestock meat is approximately 10%~20%, with high content and utilization rates of essential amino acids. The fat content in meat varies depending on the part, ranging from 10% to 30%, mainly consisting of triglycerides and small amounts of cholesterol and lecithin. The carbohydrate content in meat is low, about 1%~5%. Meat also contains abundant B vitamins.

Livestock animal offal generally contains less fat, with liver and kidney being ideal sources of iron and rich in fat-soluble vitamins A and D.

(4) Aquatic Products

Fish, shrimp, crabs, and other aquatic products are highly nutritious quality foods, easy to digest and absorb, making them the best supplements for children and the elderly.

Fish contain about 15%~20% protein, with essential amino acid compositions similar to those of livestock, but with lower fat content, thus having higher physiological value. Fish meat fibers are short, loose, and easy to digest, with protein digestion rates reaching 87%~98%. Fish fat content is around 1%~3%, mostly unsaturated fatty acids, often liquid, and easily absorbed, with fat digestion rates reaching about 98%.

Aquatic products are rich in inorganic salts, especially iodine, calcium, and fat-soluble vitamins. Besides, eels, river crabs, sea crabs, and other products are rich in riboflavin.

(5) Milk and Dairy Products

Milk products are rich in nutrition. Cow's milk is similar to human milk, differing in that cow's milk has a higher protein content and human milk has a higher lactose content. Cow's milk protein content is approximately 3%~3.5%, containing all essential amino acids for humans, with relative contents similar to eggs and higher utilization rates. Cow's milk contains about 3.5% fat, with small particles in a highly dispersed state, easy to digest and absorb, and containing essential fatty acids and lecithin. The carbohydrate content in cow's milk is approximately 5%, existing in the form of lactose, which can regulate stomach acid and promote gastrointestinal motility. Cow's milk is rich in inorganic salts, especially calcium, phosphorus, and potassium, with high absorption rates. The alkaline element content in milk is higher than the acidic element content, helping maintain the body's acid-base balance, but the iron content is low, so infants must begin supplementing high-quality iron-containing foods from four months of age. Milk also contains multiple vitamins such as vitamin A, vitamin D, ascorbic acid (vitamin C), thiamine (vitamin B1), riboflavin, niacin, etc.

(6) Vegetables and Fruits

Vegetables and fruits are indispensable important foods in people's diets, accounting for about 40% of the daily food intake, rich in fiber, pectin, and organic acids, stimulating digestive fluid secretion, promoting gastrointestinal motility, and serving as important sources of certain vitamins.

Green leafy vegetables, such as rape, amaranth, mustard greens, spinach, and leeks, are rich in carotene, ascorbic acid, and riboflavin, as well as calcium, phosphorus, iron, and other inorganic salts. Root-stem vegetables, such as potatoes, yams, taro, lotus roots, etc., contain abundant starch, providing large amounts of carbohydrates, and also contain relatively rich proteins and carotenes. Gourd-fruit vegetables, such as peppers, cucumbers, tomatoes, eggplants, etc., are rich in carotene and ascorbic acid. Fresh legumes have higher proteins, carbohydrates, calcium, phosphorus, iron, and thiamine than other vegetables.

Fresh fruits are good sources of ascorbic acid, with higher contents in fresh jujubes, hawthorns, oranges, lemons, pomelos, etc., and even higher contents in wild fruits like kiwis. Moreover, they are also excellent sources of calcium, phosphorus, iron, copper, manganese, and other inorganic salts. Additionally, because fruits contain fruit acids, fiber, and enzymes, they can stimulate appetite, aid digestion, and facilitate excretion.

(7) Fungi and Algae

Fungi and algae foods include mushrooms, shiitake mushrooms, yeast, silver ear fungus, wood ear fungus, kelp, purple seaweed, hair vegetable, seaweed, etc. These foods are delicious and nutritious, containing abundant heat, proteins, and carbohydrates, as well as calcium, iron, iodine, and other inorganic salts and rich B vitamins. They also have certain natural health care effects, such as kelp treating endemic goiter, and silver ear fungus having some efficacy on respiratory diseases.

(8) Hard Nuts

Hard nuts refer to seed plants covered by a hard shell, such as sesame, peanuts, melon seeds, walnuts, pine nuts, hazelnuts, etc. These foods concentrate various nutrients, comparable to animal foods' proteins, making them high-quality foods. These foods contain abundant proteins, fat calories, calcium, and other nutrients, and even surpass high-quality animal foods. For example, the various nutrients contained in 1kg of walnut kernels are equivalent to those provided by 5kg of eggs or 9.5kg of milk.

Particularly worth mentioning is that the fat components in hard nut foods consist of unsaturated fatty acids, forming brain tissue substances and providing energy for brain activities, making them natural brain-enhancing foods.

Section Three: Criteria for Balanced Diets

Various nutrients required by the human body must be continuously supplied and replenished through eating food every day. So what exactly should one eat? This involves a relationship of food proportions, i.e., establishing a balanced relationship between the body's physiological needs and dietary nutrient supply, forming a balanced diet.

Specifically speaking, a balanced diet refers to establishing a balanced relationship between dietary nutrient supply and the body's physiological needs in four aspects: amino acid balance, calorie-nutrient composition balance, acid-base balance, and balance between various nutrient intakes. Only then will it facilitate the absorption and utilization of nutrients. If the relationship is out of balance, meaning the diet does not suit the body's physiological needs, it will adversely affect health, potentially leading to certain nutritional diseases or chronic illnesses.

The nutritional value of food proteins largely depends on the quantity and ratio of the eight essential amino acids contained in the food. Only when the quantity and ratio approach the body's needs can they synthesize the body's tissue proteins; otherwise, it will affect the utilization of proteins in the food.
