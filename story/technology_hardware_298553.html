What is the root of Dubai's crisis

by 51hicsb on 2009-12-08 01:06:38

The debt crisis that occurred in Dubai has sent shockwaves around the world. Everyone knows that in recent years, Dubai has been undergoing large-scale construction projects, with rapid development of the real estate sector, which has taken on a trend to replace oil as the first pillar industry. Although this has indeed brought about significant changes in Dubai's landscape, transforming it from a desert town into a highly modernized metropolis, before its dream of becoming another world financial center could be realized, the crisis suddenly struck. The debt crisis currently faced by Dubai World is probably just the tip of the iceberg for Dubai’s problems. In fact, the real estate sector there has fallen into a very serious depression, with unfinished buildings visible everywhere. Consequently, some have drawn the conclusion that Dubai's problem lies in the real estate sector. And to learn from Dubai's lesson means regulating the real estate sector and curbing its blind expansion.

Such a conclusion is not entirely wrong. However, if we conduct more in-depth and rigorous research, we can likely discover more meaningful conclusions. Located in the Middle East, Dubai's primary resource is the oil beneath its ground. In recent years, with the rise in international oil prices, its oil revenue has significantly increased, boosting government strength. With money available, it was naturally first used for infrastructure. And when the local residents, accustomed to a nomadic lifestyle, began to settle down and were arranged to live a modern life, there inevitably arose a huge demand for real estate. In the eyes of the Dubai government, the oil underground will eventually run out, and to maintain the country's sustainable development, it is necessary to utilize the current period of abundant financial resources to develop other industries. The idea of establishing a world financial center was conceived under this background, followed by larger-scale urban infrastructure construction and tourism facility development. Projects like the Burj Khalifa and the Palm Islands were constructed under such circumstances. The government's thinking in Dubai, at least directionally, should be correct. For an oil-producing country to plan long-term and seize the opportunity to develop alternative industries is necessary.

But the problem lies in that when Dubai developed its city and engaged in real estate, its focus was not only on local residents but also heavily on overseas visitors. Some of Dubai's super-luxury mansions target customers like Premier League footballers, and their extravagantly luxurious tourist facilities are aimed at European and American ultra-rich individuals. However, such a consumer group is ultimately quite small, and considering Dubai's cultural traditions and social, historical, and geographical environment, it is not particularly suitable for non-Arabic individuals to reside there long-term. Building a world financial center here seems overly forward-looking. Therefore, once the global financial crisis erupted, the domestic consumption in Dubai could not fill the void left by the substantial reduction in foreign consumption, leading to a crisis even though Dubai was not at the center of the crisis.

Clearly, the root cause of the Dubai crisis appears on the surface to be due to excessive expansion of the real estate sector, but essentially it stems from strategic development errors, deviating from reality, and attempting to do things beyond its own capabilities.

Reflecting on China in light of Dubai, China's real estate market is also currently booming, and many market observers hold dissenting opinions about it, with online debates remaining intense. After the Dubai incident, public opinion strongly demanding regulation of China's real estate sector using the Dubai case as a warning has surged. Undoubtedly, China's real estate sector does indeed have too many issues, especially the rapid rise in housing prices, which has been criticized by all sectors of society. But despite this, I believe we should recognize that China's real estate situation fundamentally differs from Dubai's. China has a large population and is currently in a phase of accelerated urbanization and industrialization, making the high-speed development of the real estate sector a realistic necessity. The increase in housing prices, while driven by many unreasonable factors, also objectively reflects an imbalance in supply and demand, with constantly increasing rigid demands being a powerful driver of real estate development. The view that China should now restrict the development of the real estate sector because Dubai's crisis was caused by excessive real estate development clearly ignores reality, misdiagnoses the problem, and confuses the different issues present in different countries and at different stages of development.

In fact, the reference value of the Dubai crisis for China and the world lies less in the notion that real estate or even tourism cannot develop abnormally, and more in the need to formulate a development strategy suited to national conditions and to make domestic demand the foundation of economic development. In this sense, the Dubai incident may just be an isolated case, and viewing the problems in its real estate sector as universally applicable phenomena in other countries, or seeing the decline in its debt-paying ability as the second wave of the global financial crisis, lacks evidence.

Incidentally, after the Dubai crisis occurred, the Shanghai and Shenzhen stock markets were temporarily affected, especially with a noticeable decline in real estate stocks, which is probably related to people's interpretations of the Dubai crisis. However, in recent days, the situation has changed somewhat. Not only has the stock market rebounded strongly, but the performance of real estate stocks has also stood out. This perhaps indicates that the market's understanding of the Dubai crisis is gradually becoming more objective. And if investors' expectations and assessments of the domestic real estate industry are based on facts, then I believe investment opportunities can certainly be discovered from it.
