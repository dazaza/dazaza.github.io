1. Why do clothes of different sizes have the same price? The size is different, so naturally the cost of raw materials is different too. Why is this not reflected in the price? Explanation: a. The cost of raw materials accounts for only a small part compared to design, processing, and distribution fees, so the cost difference caused by different sizes is not significant. b. Different prices due to lack of standard packaging bags are not conducive to sales and management during storage. c. It could be seen as discrimination against customers with larger body types.

2. When carrying a backpack, we all know it's more comfortable to carry both sides, but why do we often just carry one side? Explanation: Alternate between carrying on each shoulder, allowing rest on the other side.

3. Economic phenomena in daily life - Two-bedroom one-living room one-warehouse: In the agricultural economy era, landlords always had surplus grain, so one characteristic of landlords was that they had large warehouses. Inside seemed to be everything, of course, mostly grain. Every time farmers rebelled and opened the warehouse to distribute grain, it was a classic scene from children's books when I was young. How big the warehouse was, and how much grain it contained, to some extent, even plastic buckles, were symbols of wealth.

Fast forward to the early 21st century, urbanizing China sees a large transfer of rural population to cities, business registration. Logically speaking, the new urban population might retain the habit of having a warehouse at home, real estate developers should launch products like two-bedroom one-living room one-warehouse targeting consumers. But if you flip through various real estate ads in morning, afternoon, and evening newspapers, there are one-bedroom one-living room, two-bedroom one-living room, two-bedroom two-living room,... but never any product introduction for two-bedroom one-living room one-warehouse.

This is because an important feature of urban economies is having large markets and large shelves. As long as you have money, basic necessities are the easiest to satisfy, some supermarkets even operate 24 hours a day, anytime you can go to "open the warehouse to buy grain."

Personal warehouses have already been transformed into public shelves. As long as you're willing, as long as you have "general goods," abbreviated as currency, you can go to the shelves and exchange for what you need.

So, homes no longer need warehouses. Advertisements for two-bedroom one-living room one-warehouse will never appear in modern society where commodities are abundant and the economy is developed, such as well-type furnaces.

Only under very special circumstances do people purchase items to stockpile at home, forming small warehouses. At this time, it's either an economic issue or something happening in society. For example, during the SARS outbreak in 2003, due to psychological fear, many people turned their homes into small warehouses. Additionally, several purchasing frenzies after the reform and opening up are also typical cases.

Thinking divergently about the fictional case of two-bedroom one-living room one-warehouse, rear wheel mudguards, we don't build warehouses to store grain anymore because we have currency. Shouldn't we then build another warehouse to store money?

Indeed, there are such cases. In the 1980s, there were reports like this: someone didn't trust banks and buried their money in the vegetable field, resulting in it getting ruined...

If such stories happen today, they would still be news. Because the goods we produce, sending them to the market for transactions, putting them on shelves for circulation, and then exchanging back what we need, has become the basic rule of modern economic society. Similarly, the currency we obtain through transactions, namely money, also has public warehouses on the market, these warehouses are called financial institutions, among which the ones we deal with most frequently are banks. We deposit our money in the bank and withdraw it when needed. There's no need to carry all the money you'll spend in your lifetime, just as there's no need to buy and store all the grain you'll eat in a year.

The big warehouse of the market allows goods to circulate fully, optimizing the allocation of items across society and improving efficiency. Because personal warehouses have become public large warehouses, the person managing the large warehouse will certainly try to maximize the utilization rate of the warehouse to achieve his maximum benefit.

Similarly, the special warehouse of the financial industry will also try to maximize the benefits of the currency we give them and reach the highest utilization rate. This way, the optimization and efficiency of monetary allocation across society can be achieved.

In modern families, the only place with a warehouse effect is women's wardrobes. Among the clothes inside, 80% are basically never worn again, leading to low resource utilization. Whoever can come up with a way to make them shelf-like will surely get rich.

4. Economic phenomena in daily life: Bad money drives out good money

I receive a lot of coins as change, and they are usually easy to lose when placed in my pocket. So, I put them in a basket at home, accumulating quite a few over time. Whenever delivery personnel come, I use them as change.

There are two types of 10-cent coins, and I habitually give out the ones that look worse in quality, which were minted in the 90s, while keeping the prettier ones without rough edges.

One day, I suddenly realized that almost all the coins in the basket were the pretty ones. Remembering the term from economics, "bad money drives out good money," I realized this phenomenon occurred naturally.

"Bad money drives out good money" originally referred to ancient times when, due to differences in craftsmanship, high-quality silver coins were hoarded by everyone, while low-quality currencies circulated in the market.

"Bad money drives out good money" has many extended meanings, and we often find such phenomena in social life. I intentionally leave a suspenseful moment, hoping you discover it yourself.
