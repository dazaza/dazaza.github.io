The medial side of the posterior arm flap (from Dai Zai's "Flap Superstition 483") and the lateral side of the forearm as a flap donor site were first introduced by Chinese scholar Yang Yinfan. It is not invented by foreign scholars but is one of two widely recognized flap donor sites abroad (the other being the anterolateral thigh flap). Currently, Chinese scholars have conducted in-depth research on the radial artery donor site, creating many methods to reduce the damage to the radial side of the forearm donor site, such as: 1. Radial artery retrograde island flap without skin; 2. Radial artery stem perforator flap; 3. Radial artery snuffbox perforator flap, etc. The appearance of distal pedicle fasciocutaneous flaps and fascial flaps supplied by radial artery perforators has simultaneously overcome these two minor flaws. Currently, in the clinical application of hand surgery, distal pedicle perforator flaps have almost completely replaced radial artery retrograde island flaps. The radial artery flap is a series of flaps located on the radial side of the forearm, with the radial artery and vein as the vascular pedicle. In 1981, Yang Guofan first reported the clinical application of free transplantation of the forearm flap with the radial artery as the vascular pedicle. Subsequently, Wang Wei, Lu Kaifang, and others successively reported the clinical application of forearm retrograde island flaps for repairing hand wounds. Foreign scholars refer to the forearm radial side flap and its retrograde island flap with the radial artery and vein as the vascular pedicle as the "People's Republic of China Flap." In 1985, Zheng Yu'an reported the experience of using a reverse composite island flap with a piece of radius bone for thumb reconstruction in one stage, expanding the application scope of the radial artery flap. The radial artery flap is an axial flap nourished by the main artery and its branches. Its anatomical basis is that the main artery gives off many branches, forming a rich vascular network and anastomotic branches that supply the entire forearm skin. The main advantages of this flap are fine vessel caliber, superficial location, few anatomical variations, simple surgical operation, good flap texture and color, abundant subcutaneous fat, thick and uniform for easy shaping. Its biggest drawback is that harvesting the flap sacrifices a major artery, causing significant loss, and leaves a noticeable scar on the forearm affecting aesthetics. The author believes that when choosing to use this flap, strict control of indications is necessary, ensuring good shape and function repair of the donor area, and advocating arterial transplantation before flap cutting to repair the remaining benefits of the radial vein. [Applied Anatomy] After branching from the brachial artery at the elbow fossa, the radial artery runs downward along the medial side of the brachioradialis muscle, deep to it. Its lateral edge is covered by the pronator teres muscle in the upper one-third and the flexor carpi radialis muscle in the lower two-thirds. From top to bottom, the anterior muscles are supinator, flexor digitorum superficialis, flexor pollicis longus, and abductor pollicis brevis. Depending on its positional relationship with the brachioradialis muscle, the radial artery can be divided into two parts: the upper two-thirds are covered by the brachioradialis muscle, averaging 11.7 cm in length, called the covered part; the lower one-third is superficial, directly under the skin, only covered by deep and superficial fascia, averaging 10 cm in length, called the exposed part. The outer diameter of the radial artery at its origin averages 2.7 mm, and at the junction of the covered and exposed parts in the middle of the forearm, the outer diameter is 2.3 mm. Both ends of the new radial artery flap can be used as recipient areas for anastomosis. Besides the major branches of the radial collateral artery and the superficial palmar branch at the proximal and distal ends of the radial artery trunk, the blood supply of the flap is mainly formed by many cutaneous and muscular branches given off from both sides during the course of the forearm. Among them, the cutaneous branches of the covered part number 0-10; the cutaneous branches of the exposed part number 4-18, averaging 9.0. The outer diameters of these cutaneous branches range from 0.1-1.1 mm, most being 0.2-0.5 mm. They form a rich vascular network in the subcutaneous tissue of the forearm and have extensive anastomoses with the cutaneous branches of the ulnar artery, interosseous artery, and the lower end of the brachial artery, allowing the excision range of the flap to far exceed the range supplied by the radial artery cutaneous branches, with the maximum flap area reaching 35 cm x 15 mm. During retrograde island flap transplantation, the flap relies on the retrograde blood supply from the palmar arch. The radial artery has two constant accompanying veins, and the return veins of the flap can choose the cephalic vein or the accompanying veins of the radial artery. The cephalic vein is the main returning superficial vein of the flap, originating from the radial side of the back of the hand, running upward along the radial side of the forearm, accompanying the radial cutaneous nerve, and having branches entering the median cubital vein at the elbow fossa. In the middle of the forearm, the average diameter of the cephalic vein is 2.8 mm. The flap transplantation mostly uses the cephalic vein as the return vein. The radial artery has two accompanying veins, and there are multiple bridging anastomotic branches between the two veins. The average outer diameter of the accompanying veins of the radial artery is 1.3 mm. When the flap is freely transplanted, the vein generally chosen for anastomosis is the cephalic vein, and choosing to anastomose with the accompanying veins of the radial artery also allows the flap to survive. During retrograde island flap transplantation, venous return depends on the "maze flow" of the bridging anastomotic branches of the accompanying veins of the radial artery. The lateral cutaneous nerve of the forearm is one terminal of the musculocutaneous nerve, emerging through the deep fascia laterally to the biceps brachii muscle at the elbow fossa, located beneath the cephalic vein. Its average transverse diameter at the upper end is 3.0 mm and can be used as the sensory nerve for the flap. [Indications] The forearm flap has constant vessels, long pedicle, fine caliber, and is easy to anastomose, making it one of the most commonly used flaps in clinical free transplantation. Due to the poor skin color and relatively hard texture, it is suitable for repairing soft tissue defects in the face and neck and tracheal reconstruction. 1. Organ reconstruction, such as total nasal reconstruction, penis reconstruction, tongue reconstruction, orbital cavity reconstruction, etc. 2. Repair of hard tissue defects in the maxillofacial region, including soft tissue defect repair after tumor resection in the maxillofacial region, traumatic scars left behind, and floor of mouth soft tissue defect repair, etc. 3. Repair of large-scale skin and soft tissue defects caused by hand trauma. When there is no excellent donor site available, this flap can be used for the repair of severe contractures or defect deformities in the hand, as well as correction of webbed fingers. 4. Thumb reconstruction, using a retrograde island flap plus free iliac bone transplantation for thumb reconstruction. 5. Can be used as a serial flap, carrying other flaps for transplantation. [Surgical Methods] 1. Free Flap Transplantation 1. Flap Design Draw an axis line between the midpoint of the elbow fossa and the radial artery pulse point at the wrist. Since the branches of the radial artery in the exposed part are significantly more than in the covered part, the lower end of the radial artery should be used as the transverse axis of the flap when performing free transplantation of the forearm flap. The size of the flap to be harvested depends on the size of the recipient area wound, which can include the entire forearm skin and extend to above the elbow. 2. Flap Harvesting The surgery is performed under a tourniquet. Make appropriate incisions on the radial and ulnar sides of the flap according to the design line. Perform sharp dissection towards the midline between the deep fascia and the muscle membrane. Dissect the ulnar side up to the tendon of the flexor carpi radialis and the radial side up to the tendon of the brachioradialis, taking care not to damage the larger branches arising from the radial artery. The flap must be raised from beneath the radial artery and vein. Carefully ligate the muscular branches arising from the radial artery. Cut and ligate the lateral antebrachial vein, cephalic vein, radial artery, and accompanying veins at the distal end of the flap. At this point, a forearm flap with the radial artery and vein and cephalic vein pedicle has been created. After releasing the tourniquet, observe the blood circulation of the flap. Confirm that the blood supply to the flap is good before severing the vascular pedicle and securely ligating the donor site vessels. 2. Antegrade Island Flap 1. Flap Design When transferring a retrograde island flap to repair hand wounds, the rotation axis of the flap should be located at the radial artery pulse point. Design a racket-shaped flap based on the size of the wound. Before cutting the proximal end of the radial artery and vein, the blood supply must be blocked using a vascular clamp to observe the blood supply situation of the hand and forearm retrograde flaps. If there are no abnormalities, the proximal end of the radial artery and vein bundle can then be cut and properly ligated. The flap can then be transferred through a subcutaneous tunnel to the recipient area for repair. If the volume of the flap is too large and passing through the tunnel is difficult, the skin can be directly incised and appropriately dissected to increase the tension of the pedicle. The donor site can be repaired with intermediate-thickness skin grafts. 2. Flap Harvesting The surgery is performed under a tourniquet. First, make an incision at the base of the flap to expose the radial artery and vein. Identify the direction of the radial artery, and cut the designed skin according to the plan down to the deep fascia. Perform blunt dissection from both sides toward the center of the flap. When approaching the gap between the brachioradialis and flexor carpi radialis muscles, dissect on the muscle membrane to avoid damaging the tiny branches arising from the radial artery. Ligate the radial artery branches to the superficial tissues. Once the entire flap is fully mobilized, use a vascular clamp to occlude the distal end of the radial artery, release the tourniquet, and observe the blood supply to the hand and the flap. If there are any abnormalities, ligate and cut the vascular bundle at the proximal edge of the flap to form an island flap with the distal radial artery and vein as the pedicle. This can be rotated in reverse to repair hand wounds. 3. Radial Forearm Osteocutaneous Flap for Thumb Reconstruction Besides supplying the skin through cutaneous branches, the radial artery also supplies the deep muscles and radius through muscular and periosteal branches. A forearm osteocutaneous flap with a piece of radius can be harvested with the distal radial artery and vein as the pedicle for thumb reconstruction. 1. Flap Design Design a trapezoidal flap with a width of 5 cm at the upper base and 6 cm at the lower base, centered around the radial artery about 3 cm below the wrist crease. The length of the flap should be greater than the required length of the reconstructed thumb. 2. Surgical Steps First, make an incision at the base of the flap to expose the radial artery and vein. According to the design, cut the skin and raise the flap. Retract the brachioradialis muscle radially to expose the radius and determine the range of the radius segment to be harvested. During the procedure, take care to protect the nutrient vessels entering and exiting the radius from the radial artery. Do not strip the periosteum when harvesting the radius segment to ensure the integrity of the bone flap - periosteum - skin flap complex. Ligature and cut the radial artery and vein at the proximal end of the flap to create a forearm osteocutaneous flap with the distal radial artery and vein as the vascular pedicle. Transfer the flap in reverse to reconstruct the thumb early. Anastomose the medial cutaneous nerve of the flap with the digital nerve of the thumb remnant to restore sensation in the reconstructed thumb. Repair the donor site wound with a free skin graft.
