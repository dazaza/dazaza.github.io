Hu Peng, the secretary of the Land and Resources Bureau, is known as the "Mahjong King" in his circle but has never played a hand at the mahjong table. At the behest of Cheng Wen, the head of the bank's credit department, and a "table companion," Hu Peng accompanies Yang Yingying, the wife of Cheng Wen and the cousin of the deputy secretary of the Discipline Inspection Commission, to play mahjong for entertainment, thus beginning a dramatic change in Hu Peng's career and family life.

After the corruption and underground money-laundering case involving Cheng Wen was exposed, it caused a shockwave in the officialdom. Subsequently, a fire changed the fate of everyone involved. Even Hu Peng, who skillfully used mahjong strategies to plan his career advancement, couldn't escape...

"Guo Xi" Part One: East

"Chinese people love playing mahjong and other such games. Even economists study mahjong. Li Yining once said, 'Westerners do business like playing bridge, needing to find good partners and then fully attacking opponents; Japanese people do business like playing Go, where they can sacrifice some corners but must dominate the overall situation. Chinese people do business like playing mahjong, focusing on others' hands and closely watching the next player, making sure no one takes advantage of them.'"

Hu Peng expanded on Meng Chuanqing's words, saying that Chinese interpersonal relationships are also like playing mahjong. Too many situations involve observing and monitoring others, and this applies even to married couples. Meng Chuanqing chuckled and asked if Hu Peng was strictly controlled by his wife. Hu Peng replied that it wasn't so much about him, but rather he felt sorry for some people who are constantly watched and monitored by their wives. These people feel uneasy themselves and end up tormenting others, leading to officials acting unkindly.

"Guo Xi" Part Two: South

Director Mou asked Hu Peng what the fundamental difference between mahjong and poker was. Hu Peng listlessly replied that he didn't know. Director Mou insisted on continuing the topic:

"Mahjong tiles have no hierarchy, whereas poker cards do. Every mahjong tile holds the same identity, with no distinctions of superiority or inferiority, no master-servant relationship, no distinction of good or bad... The question is what situation the tile appears in. Mahjong tiles reveal their unique value within the context of the game. Their worth is fleeting and ever-changing; they may be extremely valuable one moment and return to being an ordinary discarded tile the next. Poker cards are different. The 'King' always dominates, its existence determining inequality and implying suppression and obedience. If you possess it, you may hold all the winning cards..."
