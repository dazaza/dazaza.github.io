If you want to become a successful salesperson, if you want to become an outstanding leader, you need to become an excellent communicator. You must be good at communicating with your customers and with your superiors. In fact, customers are willing to buy your products or become your partners because they like you. Being good at communication allows you to quickly build rapport and friendships with customers.

If you lack communication skills, you will not be able to achieve great success. A person's ability to communicate determines the quality of their career. Someone who cannot communicate effectively will not succeed.

Whether or not you communicate well is one of the key factors in determining whether you do things well or poorly. Lack of communication or poor communication can lead to many inconsistencies, conflicts, and difficulties. Good communication can effectively resolve 99% of problems in business.

To become an outstanding communicator, the following six communication strategies can help you:

1. Make the customer feel important

People generally want to feel important and have a sense of high status. Customers want you to respect them, so make them feel like an important person.

Customers want to feel better than others. When you communicate one-on-one with a customer, let them feel important by talking more about them and less about yourself. Customers are more concerned with their own success than with how successful you are.

During communication, if you value the other party, the emotional distance between you and them will immediately shorten significantly.

Mao Zedong was very accomplished in his communication with others.

In the 1970s, Tang Youzhi became Mao Zedong's health care doctor for the first time. During their first meeting, Tang was very nervous. Unexpectedly, Mao looked at Tang and repeatedly thought about his name: "Youzhi, Youzhi..." and asked, "Is your name from The Analects of Confucius 'Guan Ke Shi You Zhi, Bu Ke Shi Zhi Zhi'?" He then said, "Don't follow Confucius's 'You Zhi', but rather be passionate like Chen, where flowers bloom and fall as they may." Mao's witty remarks made Tang laugh heartily, and they began a harmonious conversation.

There was once a customer who bought a suit at a department store in Taipei. After wearing it, he felt disappointed because the suit faded and dyed the collar of his red shirt black. So, he returned to the store to find the salesperson who sold him the suit to inform her of the situation. Unfortunately, before he could fully explain the situation, the salesperson interrupted and said, "I've sold dozens of these suits, and this is the first time someone has been dissatisfied." Her implication was that the customer was intentionally causing trouble dishonestly. During the argument, another salesperson came over and said, "All black clothes fade a bit at the start, there's no way around it. Clothes bought at this price won't be very good, it's the dye's fault." Hearing this, the customer became even more dissatisfied and somewhat angry. He thought, "The first salesperson doubts my honesty, and the second salesperson implies I bought inferior goods." Feeling upset, he was about to question them when the store's department manager came over. The department manager resolved the issue within ten minutes and turned the customer's anger into satisfaction. How did the department manager do it? In three steps:

First, listen to the customer recount the entire incident without saying a word.

Second, after the customer finishes speaking, allow the salesperson to state their opinion, then argue with them from the customer's perspective, not only saying that the customer's shirt collar shouldn't have been dyed black, but also that the store should not make the customer dissatisfied.

Third, acknowledge the problem with the clothing and express willingness to resolve it satisfactorily. Finally, he said, "How would you like us to handle this suit? We will certainly comply."

The department manager proposed letting the customer try the suit for another week. If still unsatisfied, they could bring it back for a refund or exchange.

After hearing the department manager's words and attitude towards handling the matter, the customer's mood calmed down. He agreed to try the suit for a week, and after a week, the suit indeed did not fade.

2. Let the customer talk more and listen more yourself

The most important step in sales is finding out the customer's needs or the problem that needs solving. If you don't understand the customer's needs or the urgent problems to solve, your sales efforts will be unable to proceed. To make the horse drink water, you must first make the horse thirsty; to make the horse thirsty, you must first feed the horse salt.

Understanding the customer's needs involves not only research and observation but also asking the customer questions, allowing them to express their needs or problems verbally. Encourage the customer to speak more. The more they say, the more information they provide you, the more you understand about the customer, the easier it is for you to succeed in sales.

As a direct seller, you might sometimes notice that when the customer leaves your home, they might tell their wife, "I like this person. He is the most understanding conversationalist I've ever met." Why? Because most of the evening might have been spent listening to them, while you might not have said much. You are an exceptionally skilled listener, and it is your listening that wins the customer's favor.

3. Talk more about "you" and less about "me"

In the process of pursuing your business, especially during customer communication, as a salesperson, you should always play the supporting role and act as an assistant, not the main character or the president. Your customer is the main character and the president. You should willingly be a green leaf to highlight the customer's red flower, preferring to stay behind the scenes and let your customer enjoy all the applause and praise.

During communication, talk more about "you" and less about "me," meaning talk more about matters related to the customer and less about yourself; empathize more with the customer's inner feelings and less with your own.

If you talk more about "me" and less about "you," you might win a debate but lose the entire sale.

During the sales process, I have found that many salespeople commit the following "offenses":

(1) Overusing sentences starting with "I" and excessively emphasizing the importance of the word "I." For example, "I think the price is not high at all," "I think the product is very suitable for you." Such statements clearly indicate that your opinions are more important, disallowing others to dissent.

(2) Habitually boasting about oneself. For example, "My product is top-notch. My product has never had any quality issues."

(3) Drawing conclusions based on personal experience. "This absolutely won't work," spoken with the air of an authority figure, using personal experience to judge matters while completely ignoring others' experiences.

(4) Questioning the customer in a threatening tone. "Are you kidding! With such a low price, you actually still think it's expensive." This tone is intimidating, turning what should be a friendly inquiry into an accusatory one.

(5) Making requests in a commanding tone. "You'd better buy this set of products," "You'd better sign yesterday." These phrases indicate the speaker wants to control the other party.

(6) Giving coercive suggestions. "You should do this," "You must do that." These coercive phrases put a lot of pressure on the customer, forcing them into a situation with no choice. These words reduce the listener's opportunity to make their own judgment and decision.

(7) Complaining and blaming others. For example, "Your consumption concept has a problem," "If you could have arrived 10 minutes earlier, I could have done more things," "Your attitude is completely wrong." Such sweeping definitive statements severely hurt the customer.

When talking to customers, unless the customer asks you to talk about yourself, don't always center on "yourself" and constantly mention "me." Instead, care more about the customer and their affairs, and talk more about things related to or interesting to the customer. When discussing issues, also listen more to the customer's opinions, and don't keep saying "I think" how. Even if you disagree with the customer's opinion, try to use a consultative tone and phrases like "I estimate it might be like this," "Do you think my idea is correct?"

For the above seven "offenses," the correct way to phrase them should be:

(1) "What do you think?" "How do you see it?" "What kind of situation do you hope for?" Start with "you" instead of "I."

(2) "Our product has been used by many people, and they all feel good about it. How do you feel?" Fully respect the customer's feelings and don't impose your feelings on them.

(3) "This approach might not work, I believe you can see that too." Ask for the other party's opinion in a consulting tone.

(4) "Why do you think our product is expensive? Saying this, I believe you must have your reasons."

(5) "If you buy this set of products today, we can offer you a more favorable price. If you sign today, we will give you a gift."

(6) "If you have no objections, I suggest you..."

(7) "Sorry, if you temporarily don't accept our product, it must be because I haven't explained it clearly enough." "If I had scheduled you 10 minutes earlier, we would have had more time." "You showing this attitude must be because there's something wrong with my communication method." Attribute the problem or mistake to yourself, and never blame the customer.

4. Listen to what the customer is saying

In studying the art of communication, I discovered that a crucial cornerstone of effective communication is showing genuine interest in others. Lacking this cornerstone, communication cannot proceed smoothly, nor can harmonious interpersonal relationships be built or sales goals achieved. Listening attentively to others speaks louder than anything else in expressing your interest in them.

Most of us tend to make a common mistake, which is talking too much. In reality, in the Amway business, your success in sales doesn't depend on how well you talk but rather on how well you listen. Listening is more important than talking. Talking shows you are interested in yourself, focusing on yourself rather than the other person; listening shows you are interested in the other person, focusing on them rather than yourself. Human nature dictates that the more you value them, the more they will value you. Many salespeople, while communicating with customers, don't carefully listen to what the other person is saying but wait silently for their turn to speak, thinking about what they should say next.

A person appears to be communicating with others on the surface, but their mind is preoccupied with other issues. Therefore, although they are listening, they don't hear what the other person is saying.

The following example illustrates this point well.

This story is about President Franklin D. Roosevelt. As the president of a country, Roosevelt also complained that no one truly listened to him carefully. One day, at a reception, Roosevelt decided to conduct an experiment. As he greeted each visitor, he muttered, "This morning, I killed my grandmother." All the guests responded, "Wonderful, please continue." "We're proud of you." "God bless you, student." It wasn't until Roosevelt reached the end of the line that the Paraguayan ambassador truly heard it. The ambassador, feeling uneasy, leaned in and whispered, "I suppose she deserved it."

Now let's look at the four steps of listening:

(1) Stop. Whether you visit the customer or the customer visits you, regardless of whether there is a sales action, when you communicate with the customer, you should stop whatever you're doing and give them proper attention. Even brief attention makes the customer feel that you are listening carefully and that you value them highly.

Someone once said, "Listening to others is like tuning into a radio station. To get good reception, you can only tune into one station at a time." I think this analogy is quite vivid. Imagine trying to listen to two stations simultaneously; the result would be just noise, nothing clear. Reading books or materials while listening to someone talk is like trying to tune into two stations at once. Eventually, you'll feel frustrated. Before listening, decide where to focus your attention. If you choose to listen to the other person, move aside anything that might distract you.

(2) Look. Face the other person, look into their eyes, and maintain eye contact. Appropriate facial expressions encourage the other person to share their true feelings and thoughts with you.

(3) Listen. Pay attention to the other person's words and tone, understand what they explicitly express and what they might imply. Focus on keywords and ideas that help you better understand them.

(4) Speak. After stopping, looking, and listening, it's time to respond. An active response should involve making appropriate comments or asking a question to guide the other person to find solutions themselves.

Active listening isn't just passively staying silent; it's actively engaging. While conversing with the customer, continuously evaluate their intent and use verbal or non-verbal cues to show that you're actively participating in the dialogue. God gave us one mouth and two ears for a reason. People who talk endlessly without listening aren't liked.

There are various ways to listen. Some categorize listening into four levels:

(1) Listening with one ear. Listening with one ear gives two negative messages to the speaker:

① Indicates you're not fully focused, appearing distracted and unfocused, making the speaker feel you're not paying attention.

② Suggests you're only half-listening, implying you don't value what the speaker is saying, hurting their feelings.

(2) Listening with a blank expression. Staring blankly or passively staring at the speaker without any non-verbal encouragement resembles watching TV, creating a dull and oppressive atmosphere that makes the speaker uncomfortable and unable to continue.

(3) Appreciative listening. Nodding and making approving "hmm" sounds encourages the speaker to continue, indicating you're following their train of thought. However, this mechanical listening lacks thoughtful consideration and digestion of the speaker's content, failing to effectively feedback your understanding.

(4) Active listening. Active listening is like dancing a social dance, both parties fully engaged and in perfect harmony. Active listening represents the highest level of listening, providing more information, deeper understanding, and better communication results. Effective exchanges arise from active listening.

Active listening stimulates inspiration in both the speaker and listener, encouraging both to participate actively in the exchange. First, it requires the listener to use active mental activities to understand the speaker's content and feedback that understanding. Second, active listening feedback helps the speaker clarify their thoughts, making the exchange more precise.

Active listening conveys that the listener has clearly understood the speaker's content. Active listening requires the listener's body language to align with the speaker's body language.

Pay attention to the content you feedback, either your understanding of their content or their feelings. Regardless of which, express it appropriately.

Here are five strategies for effective listening:

(1) Identify the key points. Repeat the key points expressed by the customer. For example, "I want to buy a house with three sunny sides." The key point here is the house with three sunny sides, so you should repeat it in your own words and note it down.

(2) Identify strengths. Praise the strengths reflected in the customer's words. For example, "I want to buy a house with a larger study." At this point, praise the customer by saying, "It's obvious that you are an educated person who loves books."

(3) Identify weaknesses. When you identify weaknesses reflected in the customer's words, don't use these weaknesses to criticize but rather understand and assist the customer in solving problems.

(4) Identify discrepancies. If you spot inconsistencies in the customer's words, you can use these discrepancies as a breakthrough to persuade them.

(5) Identify emotions. A person speaks differently under different emotional states. In communication, you should be alert to guiding the customer's emotions. In fact, customer emotions can be guided, and persuasion itself is a process of guiding emotions.

5. Provide appropriate self-responses

Communication doesn't have right or wrong distinctions, only "effective" or "ineffective" ones. During communication, clarity and precision in expression are important, but more importantly, whether the information you convey is received by the customer and elicits a response. If the customer doesn't hear or respond to your message, the communication is meaningless. The effect of communication is controlled by you but determined by the customer.

A single sentence or idea can be expressed in many ways. If the customer fully receives the information you convey and responds accordingly, it indicates that the communication is effective. Changing bad expression methods can change the listening effect.

Conversely, when the customer speaks, what's important isn't what they say or how much they say, but whether you are truly listening and responding appropriately to their speech.

Hearing what the customer says through physiological organs is one thing, but receiving and digesting their meaning is another. Not only should you listen, but you should internalize what you hear, digest it, and respond appropriately to the customer.

Effective communication occurs when you can respond appropriately to the information obtained.

What constitutes an appropriate response?

(1) Nodding. Consistent and timely nodding during listening indicates that you are listening attentively.

(2) Smiling. Maintain a smile throughout the listening process. Sincere smiles enhance your likability. Sometimes a smile is more persuasive than words. Of course, your smile should adapt to the customer's emotions and the content of their speech. When the customer talks about sad topics, you shouldn't smile.

(3) Body language. Leaning forward toward the customer, mimicking their gestures, etc., are excellent responses. Sometimes body language is more important than words for communication effectiveness.

(4) Asking questions. You can ask the customer questions related to their conversation, indicating that you are actively listening.

(5) Explaining. Explain the content of what the customer said in your own words to test your understanding of their message.

(6) Reflecting feelings. When the customer expresses strong emotions or feelings, reflect those same feelings and emotions.

(7) Feedback meaning. Summarize the content and facts of what the customer said briefly and restate them in your own words.

(8) Bold imagination. From the customer's perspective, imagine boldly.

6. Compliment the customer more

The most beautiful language in the world is compliments. Genuine compliments can help you build good relationships, ensuring smooth sailing in your career. In a certain sense, compliments are an effective emotional investment. Naturally, giving compliments brings returns. Compliments to your leaders can make them happy and pay more attention to you; compliments to your colleagues can foster better relationships and strengthen unity, making competition more enjoyable. Compliments to customers can win more sales or cooperation opportunities, thus generating more profits.

A wise clothing dealer would say, "Madam, you have excellent taste. This is the latest trendy design here. Wearing it, Madam will surely look even more beautiful." Such a few words would make the lady smile and immediately open her wallet. American businesswoman Paula Young once said, "Complimenting your customers is more important than complimenting your products. Making your customers happy means you're already halfway to success."

There is a compliment club abroad. This club has no administrators, no office, and no formal meetings. The main activity of its members is to voluntarily give sincere compliments to others three times a day. They don't wait for others to do something extraordinary or encounter a perfect person to compliment. Instead, they strive to find praiseworthy aspects in others' daily lives, even the tiniest personal advantages and characteristics. In reality, everyone has their strengths and characteristics.

This method of intentionally seeking beauty in others' ordinary lives and giving compliments has produced miraculous effects on the club members. It shifts attention from oneself to others, reducing selfishness and arrogance among the members. They become more perfect and humane, and their intelligence is fully utilized.

Gifting roses to others leaves fragrance in your hands. Compliments not only bring happiness and a sense of achievement to others but also help oneself. Dr. Cran, the founder of the Compliment Club, said that this simple method has miraculously treated people troubled, fearful, and suppressed.

Compliments are the lubricant of your life and work. As a superior, if you need to criticize your subordinate, direct criticism may make them feel bad and possibly harbor resentment against you. But if you compliment first before criticizing, the outcome will be different.

If you are a subordinate and want to advise your superior, direct advice may provoke their anger. But if you compliment first before advising, the outcome will be different.
