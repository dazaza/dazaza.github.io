**Hong Kong Company FAQs**

**Q: What conditions are required to set up a limited company in Hong Kong?**

**A:** At least one shareholder aged 18 or above. Mainland residents or overseas individuals with a passport or ID card are eligible. A registered office address and a statutory secretary for the limited company (usually provided by us) are also required.

**Q: How do I proceed with the formalities to establish a Hong Kong company?**

**A:** You just need to fill out a power of attorney form. The form includes three aspects:

1. Confirm the Chinese and English names of the company.

2. Determine the registered capital amount (minimum HKD 10,000, typically divided into 10,000 shares). It doesn’t need to be fully paid but can be increased as needed.

3. Provide the shareholder’s details and a copy of their ID. Not all shareholders need to become directors, but there must be at least one director. After receiving your information on these three aspects, we will prepare the complete legal documents for your signature the next day. The process usually takes about 10 working days after signing the documents.

**Q: How long does it take to set up a new company? How long if I buy an existing shell company? Can I rename an existing company after purchasing it? How long does renaming take?**

**A:**

- Setting up a new Hong Kong company takes 10 working days after signing all legal documents.

- Purchasing an existing shell company takes 1-2 working days; expedited service can be completed on the same day.

- Establishing a branch company takes 3 working days.

- After purchasing a company, you can change its name, which requires 15 working days.

**Q: What is the difference between a shareholder and a director?**

**A:** Shareholders are investors in a stock company, while directors are elected by shareholders and manage the company. The chairman of the board is responsible for the board of directors. In many cases, shareholders are also directors.

**Q: What are the differences in naming a Hong Kong company compared to mainland China?**

**A:** Naming a Hong Kong company is relatively free. Companies that are difficult to register on the mainland can often be registered in Hong Kong as long as the name is not duplicated. Regardless of the size of the registered capital, the Hong Kong government allows company names to include words such as "international," "group," "holding," "industry," "investment," "college," "association," "center," "foundation," or "institute." You can also add your favorite place name (e.g., France, USA, Italy, Japan, Hong Kong, Shanghai) or use your own name and hometown in the company name to reflect personalization.

**Q: Are there any restrictions on the business scope of a Hong Kong limited company?**

**A:** Hong Kong is one of the freest trading ports in the world, and there are no significant restrictions on the business scope. A company can engage in any type of business, such as finance and investment, healthcare, shipping, import/export trade, real estate, construction, interior decoration, information networks, clothing textiles, tourism, cultural publishing, etc. Many small and medium-sized enterprise owners choose not to specify a business scope to avoid limiting their operations.

**Q: Where is the registered capital reflected in a Hong Kong company? Is it necessary to pay the full amount upfront?**

**A:** The minimum registered capital for a Hong Kong limited company is HKD 10,000, and it does not need to be fully paid upfront (no capital verification required). The registered capital is only reflected in the company's articles of association. If you want to prove your registered capital, providing the articles of association is sufficient. Alternatively, our accountant can issue a certified true copy.

**Q: What services are included in setting up a Hong Kong limited company?**

**A:** Services include:

1. Government certificate fees (Company Registration Certificate, Business Registration Certificate).

2. Printed materials (18 copies of the articles of association, 1 share certificate book, 1 shareholder register, 1 set of application documents).

3. Seal fees (1 chop, 1 steel seal, 1 alteration seal).

4. One year of registered office address fee.

5. One year of statutory secretary service fee.

6. Miscellaneous fees (document fees, courier fees, travel expenses).

7. Professional accounting service fees.

**Q: How should we make payment after deciding to establish an offshore company?**

**A:** Payment methods: You can pay in cash, by bank transfer, telegraphic transfer, check, or draft to our designated accounts in Hong Kong or mainland China. Cash payments can be made at locations such as Hong Kong, LA, Kuala Lumpur, Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Chengdu, Yiwu, etc. Overseas clients can remit funds to our Hong Kong account, while mainland clients can remit to our Shenzhen account. Account details are available upon request.

Payment terms: After completing the power of attorney, you must prepay 70% of the total cost, with the remaining 30% due upon completion of the company registration. The full stamp duty (1/1000 of the registered capital) must also be prepaid. Our agency follows the same payment method as our clients.

**Q: After establishing a Hong Kong company, what annual fees are required?**

**A:** Annual fees consist of two parts: annual review and annual report filing, as well as accounting and auditing.

1. The annual review and company registration report fees must be paid annually. Prices fluctuate based on government adjustments, generally ranging from HKD 3,000 to 5,000. These fees must be paid even if the company has no business activities.

2. Accounting and auditing services depend on whether the company has conducted business. If the company operates and generates revenue, it must undergo accounting and auditing. If there is no business activity, auditing is not required, but tax reporting remains mandatory.

**Q: Who is responsible for the annual accounting and auditing of a Hong Kong company? If handled by you, what documents are required, and what are the specific charges and timelines?**

**A:** Accounting can be done by the company itself or entrusted to a Hong Kong accounting firm. Auditing must be entrusted to a certified public accountant or auditor. If handled by us, you must provide all business records, including ledgers, invoices, receipts, payment vouchers, bank statements, outstanding receivables/payables. Generally, it takes about one month to organize the accounts and 1-2 months to complete the audit.

**Q: What is the difference between statutory secretary services and business secretary services?**

**A:** By law, a limited company must have a statutory secretary, who can be either a local individual or a corporate entity in Hong Kong. We can arrange a statutory secretary for you. This role is a formal requirement by the Hong Kong government to facilitate communication and handle official correspondence.

Business secretary services are tailored to meet client needs, offering practical business support such as answering calls, relaying messages, handling mail, credit letters, logistics, etc. The advantage of business secretary services is that they help build your corporate image, making it seem like you have your own employees and office in Hong Kong, ensuring smooth business operations.

**Q: If our Hong Kong company has no office or staff, how should we manage its operations?**

**A:** Our business secretary services are specifically designed for this purpose. For more details, please refer to the business secretary service guidelines.

**Q: How does Hong Kong tax companies?**

**A:** Hong Kong is a low-tax, free port with few tax items. The only annual tax businesses must pay is profits tax, calculated at 17.5% of net profit. For example, if a company has an annual turnover of HKD 10 million, gross profit of HKD 2 million, and net profit of HKD 1 million, the profits tax payable would be HKD 175,000. If the company does not generate a profit, no tax is due.

**Q: Are products entering or leaving Hong Kong taxed?**

**A:** Except for tobacco and alcohol, goods entering or leaving Hong Kong are generally not taxed. However, all imports and exports must be declared, with customs fees charged at 0.5% (for values up to HKD 470,000) or 0.3% (for values exceeding HKD 470,000).

**Q: Are there any taxes to pay when establishing a company?**

**A:** Yes. When establishing a Hong Kong company, you must pay the stamp duty on registered capital. The stamp duty is calculated at 1/1000 of the registered capital. For example, if the registered capital is HKD 1 million, the stamp duty would be HKD 1,000, paid once during registration and not again thereafter.

**Q: Which department produces invoices and receipts for Hong Kong companies?**

**A:** Invoices and receipts are not uniformly printed by the tax authority but are issued by the company's board of directors based on the nature of the business. Invoices and other documents become valid after being stamped with the company seal and signed by the responsible person.

**Q: Can all shareholders of my Hong Kong company be mainland residents if my purpose is to set up a foreign-invested joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise in mainland China?**

**A:** Generally, a Hong Kong company can be entirely owned by mainland shareholders. However, for setting up a foreign-invested joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise, the Ministry of Commerce usually requires at least one overseas shareholder to sign the relevant documents. Therefore, having an overseas shareholder may be beneficial.

**Q: Can my newly established Hong Kong company directly apply for a foreign-invested joint venture or wholly-owned enterprise in mainland China?**

**A:** Typically, foreign investors must have been in operation for more than one year before investing in China. Specific requirements depend on local policies.

**Q: Can I open a company account with a foreign bank in mainland China, such as a branch of a Hong Kong bank in Shenzhen?**

**A:** Yes. After incorporation, the company directors can open an offshore account with a foreign bank in mainland China using their mainland ID cards. However, the directors must personally visit the bank to open the account.

**Q: How are routine matters such as tax reporting for Hong Kong companies handled? Can you assist with this? What are the annual fees?**

**A:** In Hong Kong, taxes are reported once a year. Fees depend on the actual situation of your company. If you choose our business secretary service, tax reporting is included without additional charges. However, accounting and auditing services require separate fees, which are determined by the turnover and volume of transactions. If the company has no business activity, no fees are incurred for these services.

**Q: How is the tax rate for Hong Kong companies calculated, and what is the specific tax reporting timeline?**

**A:** The tax rate is 17.5% of actual profits. Hong Kong companies typically use March 31 as the fiscal year-end, allowing you to file taxes up to one year later.

**Q: What do the terms "CORP" (business nature) and "BODY CORPORATE" (legal status) mean on the business registration certificate?**

**A:** "CORP" indicates that the company is not limited to a specific business type and can engage in any business allowed under the Companies Ordinance. You can also specify a business scope such as electronics, chemicals, or trade. "BODY CORPORATE" refers to the corporate legal status.

**Q: How can I rent an office if needed?**

**A:** Based on your requirements, we offer different options such as company incubation, desk rental, single office rental, or entire floor rental. Just let us know your needs, and we will find suitable office space for you.

**Q: Can I check the background of a Hong Kong limited company online?**

**A:** Currently, this is not possible. Anyone with questions about a company can visit the Companies Registry in Hong Kong's Admiralty district for inquiries.

**Q: After establishing a publishing house, how can I legally publish books?**

**A:** After establishing a publishing house, you are qualified to apply for publishing rights. However, two tasks remain: applying for an International Standard Book Number (ISBN) or International Standard Serial Number (ISSN), and registering as a publisher before publishing. Completing these steps allows you to legally publish books. If you wish to distribute in mainland China, you can apply through your Hong Kong publisher's credentials.

**Q: What are ISBN and ISSN, and what are their differences?**

**A:** ISBN stands for International Standard Book Number, while ISSN stands for International Standard Serial Number. Having these numbers indicates that your publications meet international standards and are registered with the respective international institutions. They allow you to query publication background information via these numbers. The fundamental difference lies in the target audience: ISBNs are for book publishers, while ISSNs are for magazine and journal publishers.

**Q: If my business performs poorly, how much must I pay annually to suspend the company?**

**A:** A Hong Kong company can apply for temporary suspension at any time. Besides paying the annual report fee to the Companies Registry and the annual review fee to the Inland Revenue Department, no other fees are required. If market conditions improve, the company can resume operations immediately, saving many costs.

**Q: Can my Hong Kong company be dissolved?**

**A:** Yes. If your company is no longer in use and not formally dissolved, the Hong Kong government will assume it continues to exist and engages in illegal operations. Penalties for illegal operations can accumulate, and severe cases may result in imprisonment for the board of directors. Since dissolving a company also incurs fees, we recommend selling the company as the best option.
