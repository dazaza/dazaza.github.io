Many people have experienced such a feeling: although you miss someone very much, you try your best to pull yourself out of this sentiment. That person could be a good friend far away, a lover living in another place, or a relative back in the hometown.

There is a kind of pain called "dare not to miss." Standing at a familiar street corner, that person once bent down here to tie your shoelaces; sitting by a familiar little road, watching other couples holding hands and walking past, doing the same actions that were once done with you; passing by a restaurant's floor-to-ceiling window, the pair who once dined together there are now gone, and the person who gently wiped the dirt from the corner of your mouth while eating is no longer there. You don't want to go outside because the outside world has too many memories; wherever you go, you will remember the "past," which are precisely the things you dare not to reminisce about.

There is a kind of pain called "dare not to miss." Although the first thing you do when you open your eyes every day and the last thing you do before closing your eyes at night are all about missing the "past"; although when you see others holding hands on the road or couples embracing and parting downstairs, you look down at your empty hands and the empty space next to you, envying them; although when you encounter sadness, difficulties, or problems you can't solve alone, you feel helpless and think of him/her. It seems that the constant yearning and the accompanying pain are the main themes of life; whenever you look up or look down, you are thinking of the person who is not by your side. I don't know where this pain comes from, but indeed, because of the fear of pain, you dare not to reminisce.

There is a kind of pain called "dare not to miss." Gradually, you get used to not joining in the fun; the busier it gets, the lonelier you feel, and you don't want to experience it again. Quickly, you get used to seeing others' intertwined fingers and then indifferently passing by without needing to feel envious anymore. That person is far away from you, that person has already left you behind for a new happiness. When you are lonely, destitute, helpless, hesitant, and facing everything alone, you hate, sob, but mostly you miss, remembering her gentle words, deceiving yourself into believing she is still there, still by your side, and then forcing yourself to become strong again, gradually becoming powerful. Only when you forget how to cry do you realize that this yearning pain has truly changed you.

Listening to this article, the excessive use of parallelism made me feel overly sentimental and nauseous. The story goes like this: there was once a little girl who fell in love with a man. A few years later, they parted ways. The story ends there. The process belongs only to the two of them; what we see is just the result. In the near future, these two intersecting lines will drift further and further apart. In the Japanese anime "The Place Promised in Our Early Days," due to the increasing cosmic distance between the male and female protagonists, the text messages take more than 8 years to reach each other. I want to ask TA: from now on, how long will it take for my yearning to reach your heart?
