Synopsis:

In a certain seaside town, there is a restaurant called "Little Shanghai". The dishes here are simple and delicious, attracting a steady stream of customers.

Takako visits this restaurant to invite the owner, Mr. Wang, to open a branch in a shopping mall, but he declines. Undeterred, Takako frequently visits as a customer. Gradually, she becomes captivated by the dishes served there. Dishes like scrambled eggs with tomatoes and crab meat dumplings help her forget the pressures of work and regain a long-lost smile.

Suddenly, Mr. Wang falls ill, forcing the restaurant to close. Upon hearing this, Takako decides to quit her job and apprentice under Mr. Wang. To reopen the restaurant, she begins an intensive cooking training...

This is a touching story centered around food. Mr. Wang, who makes people feel happiness through his cooking, and Takako, who finds happiness in cooking, their carefully prepared dishes emit the fragrance of happiness.

Yoshiko Yamashita works in the sales department of a large shopping center. One day, she comes to a Chinese restaurant - "Little Shanghai" near the sea in Kanazawa, inviting the owner to open a store in the shopping center. Mr. Wang outright rejects Yoshiko's proposal. Although unsuccessful, Yoshiko does not want to waste the trip and orders a set meal for lunch.

The unassuming Chinese dishes before her capture Yoshiko's heart instantly when she tastes a crab-filled dumpling. From then on, she becomes a regular customer. Gradually, Yoshiko gets to know Mr. Wang better. Born in Shaoxing, China, Mr. Wang was a renowned chef there. Now, after the passing of his wife and daughter, he lives alone abroad, creating simple yet deeply moving dishes that bring happiness.

One day, Mr. Wang suddenly falls ill. Yoshiko visits him at the hospital and learns that due to complications, Mr. Wang can no longer skillfully wield his culinary skills. Seeing Mr. Wang standing in the kitchen, unable to lift the wok and troubled, Yoshiko recalls her deceased father, who was a master Western chef...

Yoshiko makes a surprising decision. She quits her job to apprentice under Mr. Wang, learning culinary techniques to keep "Little Shanghai" running. Moved by Yoshiko's sincerity, Mr. Wang accepts her as a disciple. For Yoshiko, who raised her young daughter alone, the life of a chef's apprenticeship is not easy or enjoyable. A local young farmer named Akira enthusiastically encourages her, and Yoshiko gradually improves.

A local gratitude food competition is held, and Yoshiko represents the small restaurant. Unexpected misfortunes lead to a crushing defeat. Deeply affected, Yoshiko decides to abandon her career as a chef.

Mr. Wang's old friend, Lily, comes to visit him. Her son is about to get married, and they plan to hold two family banquets at the small restaurant. Mr. Wang believes Yoshiko can take over for him. To restore Yoshiko's confidence, Mr. Wang takes her to his hometown of Shaoxing, introducing Yoshiko as his daughter. After returning home, Yoshiko reignites her passion, undergoing rigorous training to become a fully capable chef. The day of Lily's family banquet approaches...

Behind the Scenes

Mitsuharu Misawa's Delicious Happiness

In the Japanese film industry, Mitsuharu Misawa is not considered a heavyweight director. He graduated from Osaka University of Arts' Art Department and began experimenting with filmmaking during his studies. Winning the Osaka Film Festival New Director Award with "SLAPHAPPY," Misawa entered the commercial film world. He doesn't focus on creating buzz but occasionally delivers unique masterpieces. Misawa's representative works often draw inspiration from everyday life, leaving a subtle yet lasting impression, making them unforgettable.

In 2005, Misawa's self-written and directed "Country Photo Studio" won the Golden Goblet Award at the Shanghai International Film Festival. The new film "Flavor of Happiness" is another original script and directing effort by Misawa. Similar to his award-winning work three years ago, the new film also centers on the theme of "family bonds." However, this time Misawa expands and transforms it; the protagonists do not share blood relations, yet the quasi-family relationships they weave have a more profound emotional impact.

Besides people, the main characters in the film include a series of delectable Chinese dishes. Red-and-white tomato scrambled eggs, fragrant fried rice, and full-bodied steamed dumplings... The dishes presented in the film are not lavish feasts or extravagant delicacies but rather everyday meals for people in southern China. The film uses these simple flavors to convey the forgotten truth of "plainness is true."

This is a visually, aromatically, and tastefully excellent film. While stimulating the appetite, it also offers a taste of warmth, guiding people to seek their own happiness.

Ryuuya Fujita trains hard in culinary arts

Miho Nakayama falls in love with Chinese cuisine

The leading actors in the film are veteran actor Ryuuya Fujita and the elegant beauty Miho Nakayama. Having portrayed hundreds of roles, Ryuuya Fujita is a long-time collaborator of director Misawa. He starred in "Country Photo Studio" and won the Best Actor Award at the Shanghai International Film Festival for his outstanding performance. After the great success of "Life of Matsuko," Miho Nakayama ascended to the top ranks of actresses. She read the script for this film and was deeply moved by the characters' simple emotions, immediately deciding to accept the role. In this film, Miho plays a kind and strong woman, exuding a gentle charm.

Both Ryuuya Fujita and Miho Nakayama have numerous kitchen scenes in the film. To perform their roles competently, they began intensive cooking training months before filming commenced. They learned not only how to stir-fry and cook but also practiced basic skills such as chopping vegetables, flipping woks, and shaping dumplings. Ryuuya Fujita attended cooking classes during the day and felt that the intensity was insufficient, so he practiced at home in the evenings. As a result, his family ate Chinese food for four and a half months straight. However, his supportive wife did not complain, consistently praising his cooking and joking that he could switch careers to become a chef. Similarly enthusiastic, Miho Nakayama invited friends to make and eat Chinese dishes at home, and both she and her friends developed a deep affection for Chinese cuisine.

Fun Facts:

The film hired seasoned Chinese chef Sojiro Motogi as a consultant. Renowned as Japan's "King of Chinese Dim Sum," Motogi has been in the business for 45 years and has worked in many famous restaurants.

There are over fifty Chinese delicacies featured in the film, including traditional dishes and creative ones using local ingredients from Ishikawa Prefecture.

Ryuuya Fujita studied Mandarin and Shaoxing dialects with a teacher to reflect the character's foreign identity in tone.

Filmed during the height of summer, the kitchen set had no air conditioning, reaching temperatures of forty degrees Celsius, but the production crew had no complaints.

It is said that director Mitsuharu Misawa is a skilled chef, particularly adept at Chinese cuisine.
