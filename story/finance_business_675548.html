Br Med J, 1998; 317:713-719 (then 242) indicates that the relationship between diabetic metabolic disorders has not yet been completely clarified. Some known atherosclerosis risk factors, such as obesity, hypertension, and lipid and lipoprotein metabolic abnormalities, occur at a higher incidence rate in type 2 diabetes than in the corresponding non-diabetic population, suggesting that various factors may be involved in the pathophysiology of diabetic vascular disease. In the case of chronic hyperglycemia, impaired insulin action directly impacts all aspects of lipoprotein metabolism, causing changes in lipoprotein quantity and composition, leading to lipid metabolic disorders and increasing the production of atherosclerosis [2]. Hyperinsulinemia and insulin resistance may represent the central link in diabetic macroangiopathy. The stimulating effect of insulin through its own growth and stimulation of other growth factors, such as insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1), can directly stimulate smooth muscle hyperplasia of the arterial wall intima and proliferation, leading to atherosclerosis [1]. Increased insulin concentration is one of the abnormalities in the pathogenesis of diabetic serum lipids [2,3]. Type 2 diabetes mellitus, due to hyperglycemia, relative insulin deficiency, and decreased insulin resistance, reduces free fatty acid activity. Combined with decreased lipoprotein activity, decreased lecithin cholesterol-acyl enzyme activity, and elevated hepatic lipase activity, this leads to lipid metabolic disorder. Insulin has been confirmed to increase low-density lipoprotein (LDL) receptor activity and lipid synthesis in arterial smooth muscle cells and fibroblasts. Macrovascular disease risk is positively correlated with LDL and very low-density lipoprotein (VLDL) levels and negatively correlated with the level of high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL-CH) in the serum. Additionally, abnormal hormone levels such as insulin, sex hormones, growth hormone, catecholamines, hyperglycemia, endothelial dysfunction, platelet dysfunction, directly or indirectly participate in the occurrence and development of atherosclerosis... Hyperinsulinemia promotes lipid synthesis and stimulates the proliferation of smooth muscle cells in the arterial intima; even mild hyperinsulinemia may accelerate the development of atherosclerosis by reducing lipid clearance and decreasing lysosome fat enzyme activity in the vascular wall. Studies from the UKPDS have shown that even with strict blood glucose control in type 2 diabetic patients, it cannot reduce the occurrence of major artery lesions [5]. Yang Zhifang et al.'s study also showed that increased free fatty acid concentration in patients with type 2 diabetes is an important cause of macrovascular disease morbidity [1]. Arterial atherosclerosis mainly affects the aorta, coronary arteries, cerebral arteries, renal arteries, and limb peripheral arteries, causing coronary heart disease, ischemic or hemorrhagic cerebrovascular disease, renal atherosclerosis, and arteriosclerosis, etc.. Our data also shows that patients with type 2 diabetes have a higher incidence of heart, cerebrovascular, and lower extremity arterial diseases compared to non-diabetics.
