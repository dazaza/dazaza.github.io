How to Define a Website Theme

by sword500 on 2008-08-04 19:04:43

Designing a website begins with one fundamental question: identifying the theme of your site.

By "theme," we mean the subject matter of your website. The internet is filled with an endless variety of topics, ranging from the bizarre to the fascinating. As long as you can imagine it, you can create it. Below are ten categories of themes for the top 100 most well-known websites in America as rated by *PC Magazine* in 1999. These provide some reference points for us:

1. Online job searching

2. Online chatting / Instant messaging / ICQ

3. Online communities / Discussions / Mailing lists

4. Computer technology

5. Webpage / Website development

6. Entertainment websites

7. Travel

8. Reference / Information

9. Family / Education

10. Lifestyle / Fashion

Each of these main categories can be further subdivided. For example, entertainment can be divided into sports, movies, and music. Music itself can be categorized by format (e.g., MP3, VQF, Ra) or by genre (e.g., classical, modern, rock). These are just the most common themes; there are also many specialized, alternative, and unique subjects to choose from, such as Traditional Chinese Medicine, tropical fish, or weather forecasts. Additionally, combining related topics can create new themes. For instance, "travel forums" combine travel and discussions, while "classic goal replays" combine soccer and video clips. With so many possible combinations, there are potentially tens of thousands of themes, so you no longer need to worry about choosing a repetitive or overly common theme, right? :)

When selecting a theme, my suggestions are:

1. **Focus on a small and refined theme.** Narrow down your focus and ensure the content is high-quality. If you try to create a site that covers everything and includes all the things you find interesting, it will often backfire, leaving visitors with the impression that your site lacks a clear theme or distinctive features. While it may cover a wide range of topics, each one will feel superficial because you simply won't have the energy to maintain everything. The internet thrives on novelty and speed, and the most popular personal homepages are updated daily, sometimes even every few hours. Recent surveys also show that "thematic sites" are more favored than "all-encompassing sites." It's like the difference between specialty shops and department stores—if I need something specific, I'll go to the specialty shop. For example, a friend of mine named KK wanted to create a literary-themed site. However, literature encompasses many areas, including novels, poetry, essays, science fiction, martial arts, and detective stories. After carefully considering his strengths and what he wanted to offer, we decided to focus on online literature, removing some unrelated works and concentrating on expanding and organizing online literature pieces. The site quickly gained popularity after its launch.

2. **Choose a topic you are skilled at or passionate about.** For instance, if you're good at programming, you could create a site for programming enthusiasts. If you're a football fan, you could report on the latest match results and player updates. When creating a site based on your interests, you won’t feel bored or overwhelmed. Passion is the driving force behind website creation, and without enthusiasm, it's difficult to produce exceptional work.

3. **Avoid overused or overly ambitious topics.** "Overused" refers to themes that are everywhere, like software downloads or free information. "Overly ambitious" means that in this particular area, there are already very successful and highly recognized sites. Trying to surpass them would be extremely challenging unless you're determined and capable of competing and surpassing them. Remember, on the internet, only the best is remembered. People tend to remember the top site much more vividly than the second or third ones.

Alright, if you've come up with a brilliant idea, let's move on to naming your website. You might think naming has nothing to do with website design and that I'm wasting time here. In fact, the name of your website is a crucial part of its design. For example, "Computer Learning Room" versus "Computer Home"—the latter is clearly more concise. "MIDI Paradise" versus "MIDI Funland"—the former is clearer. "Children's World" versus "China Kindergarten"—the latter feels grander. Just like in real life, whether a website name sounds dignified, memorable, and catchy significantly impacts the site's image and promotion. Here are some general tips:

1. **The name should be appropriate.** This "appropriateness" is my own term, meaning it must be legal, reasonable, and emotionally acceptable. Avoid names that are politically sensitive, pornographic, superstitious, or harmful to social safety.

2. **The name should be easy to remember.** Based on the characteristics of Chinese website visitors, unless specifically needed, it’s better to use a Chinese name rather than an English or mixed-language name. For example, "Beyond Studio" versus "超越工作室" (Chao Yue Gong Zu Shi)—the latter feels more familiar and easier to remember. Additionally, the number of characters in the name should ideally be kept within six (preferably four), such as "XX Pavilion" or "XX Design Room." Four-character names can even use idioms, like "一网打进" (Yi Wang Da Jin). Fewer characters also have the advantage of fitting well into standard link logos, which are usually 88x31 pixels in size. A six-character width is approximately 78 pixels, making it suitable for other sites' link layouts.

3. **The name should be distinctive.** A straightforward name is acceptable, but if it can convey deeper meaning and leave a strong visual impact and imaginative space for visitors, it's even better. Here are a few examples of excellent names I admire: "音乐前卫" (Music Vanguard), "网页陶吧" (Web Pottery), and "天籁绝音" (Heavenly Melody). These not only reflect the site's theme but also highlight its unique features.

In summary, defining the theme and naming your website are the first steps in designing a site and are also very important parts of the process. If finding a satisfying name requires spending a whole day flipping through a dictionary, it’s worth it.
