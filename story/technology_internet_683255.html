The Fanke Dilemma: The Paradox of Rapid Expansion and High Cost Management Difficulties

by tongdma4 on 2012-02-29 13:28:24

Author: Xie Peng, Southern Weekly reporter, reporting from Beijing and Nantong. After four years of rapid growth, the online clothing brand Vancl finds itself in a difficult situation. On one hand, it must continue to expand rapidly, but on the other, this expansion brings high costs and management challenges. Now, how Vancl maintains its existing advantages has become a focus of public attention. Vancl has built 28 warehouses nationwide; the picture shows Vancl's Wuhan warehouse center. (CFP/Photo) Large-scale layoffs, individual executives leaving, unsuccessful IPO attempts, rumors of heavy losses... For the four-year-old Vancl company, there was hardly any good news in the second half of 2011. The company founder and 43-year-old CEO, Chen Nian, until recently, actively cooperated with various media interviews, trying to dilute the doubts and worries surrounding him. Chen Nian, originally from Shanxi, was once an editor for the book review section of the China Book Business News in Beijing. In 2000, during the collapse of the internet bubble, he co-founded the online bookstore Joyo with Lei Jun and became the executive vice president of the website. After exiting Joyo and failing to establish the virtual item trading website Woyou, he founded the clothing e-commerce site Vancl in October 2007. Over four years, Vancl created a miracle of rapid growth - according to data provided by Vancl executives, post-tax revenue was only 1.1 billion yuan in 2008, but by 2011, it had reached over 3 billion yuan. What clearly distinguishes Vancl from other e-commerce sites is that Chen Nian infused "people's fashion" into Vancl, transforming it into a well-known internet clothing brand. "The historical opportunity came, you were just there, and you created history." On February 4, 2012, Chen Nian said to the Southern Weekend reporter while sitting in his spacious office at Vancl headquarters. Located in the Lecheng Building next to Shuangjing Bridge on East Third Ring Road in Beijing, Vancl headquarters occupies four floors of space. Chen Nian's personal office occupies one-seventh of a floor, with bookshelves covering one and a half walls filled with books. This explanation by Chen Nian is similar to the view of Meituan.com CEO Wang Xing - "Traditional industry entrepreneurship is like climbing a mountain, internet entrepreneurship is like surfing. You just need to catch a wave, maintain it, and its height will determine your height." Wang Xing was the founder of Xiaonei, Fanfou, and Hainei, but failed to "maintain it" after multiple entrepreneurial attempts, eventually leading to failure. Whether Chen Nian can "maintain" Vancl's momentum of rapid growth is now a mystery. Although Chen Nian repeatedly refutes those questioning voices to the outside world like Xiang Linshao, he has to admit that Vancl indeed needs to reflect at this point. The Rise of PPG The rise of Vancl, in addition to benefiting from the rise of a wave of e-commerce, also owes gratitude to a pioneer - the PPG company. In 2005, PPG rose abruptly with its "lightweight company" model, quickly becoming a dazzling online clothing company, known as "Dell of the clothing industry." It only made men's clothing, almost all of its business outsourced, without factories, equipment, or stores, only having marketing departments, design departments, call centers, and warehouses. It used pervasive online advertising and traditional advertising to grab consumers' attention. Although its growth speed was astonishing, it also planted huge hidden dangers for the future. After 2007, the company collapsed due to unpaid supplier bills. That year, Vancl was born. When Chen Nian founded Vancl, it was initially a complete imitation of PPG, which sold men's shirts by phone. Its advertising strategy was also identical to PPG's, mainly investing in print media before February 2008. Between 2008 and 2009, Vancl's advertising investment accounted for sixty percent of its sales. But fortunately, Vancl started experimenting with online marketing in March 2008, and orders quickly increased. At that time, the effectiveness of internet advertisements was not favored by companies, and prices were very low. Portals would even give some free ads. Vancl tasted the benefits of online marketing, quickly abandoned the direct telephone sales model, and transformed into an internet brand. This allowed Vancl to avoid the fate of heavily investing in traditional media like PPG and being unable to sustain itself. Like Vancl, other online clothing brands such as Mecox Lane and Mobasha began using internet advertisements to attract popularity. Additionally, Vancl started its first round of financing in October 2007, and by the end of 2011, it had completed six rounds of financing, raising a total of 400 million US dollars (approximately RMB 2.4 billion). An unnamed e-commerce vice president told Southern Weekend that this could also show Chen Nian's astuteness. Vancl's numerous rounds of financing might be a clever pricing technique. Multiple rounds of financing can increase premium prices. Chen Nian's extraordinary talent is also reflected in his brand-building strategy. In May 2010, Vancl bought some billboards, preparing to place advertisements featuring Han Han and Wang Luodan endorsing Vancl. However, Chen Nian noticed a large blank space on the billboard advertisement, so he placed the subtitles from the television commercial script, edited them, and put them up. Then, fans of Han Han and Wang Luodan took photos of it, and together with many netizens, it fermented into the "Fanke style." The rapidly popularized "Fanke style" redefined Vancl. Regarding Vancl's brand positioning, Chen Nian says he now emphasizes only three points: infinite fashion, high cost-performance ratio, and the best service. The rest is left to the users. By then, Vancl had walked a unique path of building its own internet clothing brand. On the other hand, JD.com, representing the first tier of e-commerce, follows the platform-type e-commerce model, expanding from 3C products to all goods related to food, clothing, housing, and transportation. JD.com's profit-making model is to purchase goods from suppliers, sell them online at a markup to consumers, and make a profit from the difference. This is also the path taken by Dangdang and Letao. However, the brand that Chen Nian painstakingly built struggled to generate a premium effect. Chen Nian believes that brand premium may be a detour taken by traditional Chinese brands. The reason is that international clothing giants like GAP, ZARA, H&M, and Uniqlo all follow a mass-market route. Domestic brands that start going high-end after making some money have all failed. Regarding this, another e-commerce person told Southern Weekend that doing branding cannot escape the rule of increasing average transaction value. The highest goal of retail is to increase the premium rate. For example, the selling price of Belle's shoes is generally five times the cost. In his view, Chen Nian breaking this spell somewhat goes against industry norms. On December 3, 2011, the second season of the 2011 Dark Horse Competition and the Fourth Entrepreneur Annual Meeting were held in Beijing. The picture shows Vancl founder Chen Nian. (CFP/Photo) The Cost of Speed Although Vancl follows the path of building its own brand, it inevitably faces the same destiny as other e-commerce platforms - it must keep running fast. This means that to become the ultimate winner, it must continuously invest in various costs to run faster than others and reach a scale that others cannot match. Chen Nian told Southern Weekend that he is currently focused on the growth of new users and the repurchase rate. In his view, these two data points are the only prerequisites for whether the company is healthy. He believes that one of the main reasons for PPG's initial failure was not achieving enough user volume and growth speed. To expand its scale, many e-commerce platforms imitate Amazon's model - expanding from a single product to an all-inclusive department store and providing a third-party platform, allowing other merchants to compete alongside them on their platform. Simultaneously, they increase investment in logistics, distribution, warehouses, and backend information platforms to better improve user experience and coverage. Vancl is no different. In May 2010, Vancl began a rapid expansion of categories, starting to offer men's and women's clothing, shoes, home textiles, and even cosmetics, rice cookers, and mops. Category expansion naturally leads to increased sales scale, but it also increases inventory costs for unsold products. T-shirts and canvas shoes are Vancl's best-selling items, but rice cookers and mops receive little attention. In the same month, Vancl launched the V+ channel, establishing a platform to attract third-party collaborators and selling general merchandise. This move directly targeted JD.com and Dangdang. However, the V+ expansion did not meet expectations, reaching only around 5 billion yuan in scale within a year, accounting for about ten percent of Vancl's total sales. The reason is that consumers have become accustomed to the low prices of Vancl's own brands, and upon entering the V+ channel, they find it hard to accept the higher average transaction value of V+ products, resulting in low conversion rates of traffic. Moreover, suppliers have concerns about cooperating with Vancl. Online shoe mall Letao discussed cooperation with Vancl's V+, but Letao couldn't agree because the items Letao sells are consigned by suppliers in Letao's warehouse, where Letao earns sales commissions. Goods in Letao's warehouse can turn over every ten days to half a month, but if placed in Vancl's warehouse, Vancl's processing speed is slower, prioritizing delivery for Vancl's own brands. It takes a month to turnover once, so suppliers naturally don't agree. On the other hand, Vancl's self-built Fengda Logistics Company does not yet have sufficient redundancy to earn money from third parties. Fengda Logistics General Manager Li Hongyi revealed to Southern Weekend that Fengda could be open in the future. However, the external orders Fengda currently accepts are still from connections, such as Xiaomi Mobile Phones. Amazon's early investments in logistics and cloud computing centers have now become important sources of profit. But for Vancl, reaching this point is still a long way off. Vancl's troubles do not stop here. Due to fierce competition in e-commerce, internet advertising prices have soared, driving up Vancl's costs. According to an insider in the e-commerce industry, internet advertising prices in 2011 increased by more than 50% compared to the same period in 2010. However, Chen Nian told Southern Weekend that the claim of a 50% increase in advertising prices only applies to a few companies like 360. The proportion of Vancl's advertising expenditure to sales has been declining over the years, decreasing from 60% in 2009 to 20% in 2011. He said that in 2012, this proportion would further decrease to 10%. Despite this, Vancl cannot avoid the pain of rising overall costs. Southern Weekend learned from Vancl's supplier Century Liaoyuan in Nantong, Jiangsu, that Vancl's gross margin is at least 20%. For instance, a garment sold for 99 yuan by Vancl has a procurement cost of around 76 yuan from Century Liaoyuan. An insider in the e-commerce industry analyzed to Southern Weekend that Vancl's gross margin is quite high, but Vancl offers unconditional returns and exchanges within 30 days, so the logistics damage rate for returns and exchanges is higher than other e-commerce companies. Furthermore, Fengda's commitment to high-standard services results in Vancl's total logistics costs amounting to approximately 20% of total revenue. Administrative costs are 8-10%, and marketing costs are 20-30%. Adding these up already accounts for around 50% of the costs. "Therefore, Vancl is definitely losing money. Moreover, the larger Vancl's scale becomes, the greater the loss will be. Of course, as the scale expands, logistics and marketing costs will decrease. It depends on how long Vancl can hold out," he said. The Mess After the "Great Leap Forward" After Vancl began its category expansion in 2010, the number of employees grew from over 100 at the start of the venture to around 10,000. At the time, the data Chen Nian saw showed that in January 2011, Vancl's growth was six times that of the same period in 2010. This made him confident that Vancl could achieve 10 billion yuan in sales for the entire year of 2011. Chen Nian began delegating authority. Many employees in their twenties were now managing sales worth hundreds of millions of yuan, equivalent to Vancl's total sales in 2008. However, by June 2011, Chen Nian often found too many employees waiting for elevators downstairs, some even gathering to chat idly. Some employees would sneak away during working hours. He realized the severity of the issue and conducted an experience survey of the company, concluding that senior management was restless and junior staff were arrogant and corrupt. By July, Vancl began laying off employees, dismissing many probationary workers. In the same month, Vancl also started analyzing the excessive categories, eliminating those that performed poorly. Meanwhile, Chen Nian began restructuring the organizational architecture. Previously, Vancl only had two product divisions, which were then split into eight departments—five business units and three production centers. The establishment of these five business units mainly followed a categorization approach. In the past, Vancl's products, promotions, marketing, and branding were completely separate. After forming the five business units, each unit integrated all functions from product development to marketing and promotion. In the past, as the scale gradually expanded, Vancl increasingly resembled a traditional clothing company, with the production department taking the lead, launching unsuccessful categories such as mops and nail clippers. Now, Vancl's production centers have been reduced to three—one for clothing, one for footwear and hats, and one for miscellaneous items. Each business unit has full authority from product planning to final marketing and is responsible for inventory. In the past, the product team only handled products, and the marketing team only handled marketing. The product team would bring in a lot of items, but the marketing team wouldn't approve them, leading to mutual dissatisfaction. In the future, Vancl plans to further divide into more business units, requiring more managers. Southern Weekend asked Chen Nian if he would consider hiring a professional manager to serve as CEO, but Chen Nian shook his head and said, "As the company grows, we need to respect history. More importantly, the issue is the learning ability and error-correction ability of the team." However, this team is not stable. In the second half of 2011, some executives at Vancl chose to leave—Vice President Wu Sheng joined JD.com, and PR Manager Li Jianxiong went to Xueersi. For Chen Nian, retaining talent presents a new challenge. Before the layoffs and the departure of executives, Chen Nian never allocated stock options to the management team. One-third of Vancl's current management consists of former Joyo employees, who can be retained through emotional ties, but Vancl also has many young people from the post-80s generation serving in management positions. Without offering real financial incentives, it is difficult for Vancl to retain these young talents. In February 2012, after reflection, Chen Nian is conducting a new strategic planning for Vancl. He said that Vancl's adjustments—what to withdraw and what to release—will all be determined by a data system. In the future, it will be the most core driving force for the company's development after products and users, and it will be sustainable. In fact, this has long been one of Amazon's secrets to success—Amazon tracks everything, and most decisions are based on data.
