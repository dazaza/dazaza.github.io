The EU's WEEE and RoHS Directives for Environmental Protection of Electronic and Electrical Products (2007-01-08 22:15:55)

RoHS Directive

On July 1, 2006, the EU established that six harmful substances - lead, cadmium, mercury, hexavalent chromium, PBBs, and PBDEs - should be banned in electronic and electrical products. Many international factories require suppliers to meet these regulations by the end of this year. 2005 was a transition period during which manufacturers had to show continuous improvement; otherwise, they would lose business opportunities.

Meaning of RoHS

RoHS (Restriction of Hazardous Substances) is an EU directive on environmental protection. Starting from July 1, 2006, certain substances were prohibited in electrical and electronic equipment products.

WEEE Directive

The main regulation involves assigning extended producer responsibility to manufacturers/sellers of home appliances and electronic devices for recycling waste after product use. Member states are required to achieve at least 70% WEEE recovery and recycle over 50% of WEEE materials and components by the end of 2006.

Meaning of WEEE

WEEE stands for "Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment" and refers to a directive concerning the recycling, classification, and disposal of unsuitable products.

Geographical Scope of WEEE and RoHS

1. 25 EU member states and 3 European Economic Area (EEA) countries, totaling 28 countries.

2. The 25 EU member states include Austria, Belgium, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Ireland, Italy, Luxembourg, Netherlands, Portugal, Spain, Sweden, United Kingdom, Slovenia, Cyprus, Malta, Poland, Czech Republic, Estonia, Hungary, Latvia, Lithuania, Slovakia.

3. The 3 EEA countries are Iceland, Norway, Liechtenstein.

Impact of RoHS and WEEE Directives

Manufacturing and selling designated categories of electronic and electrical equipment

Selling equipment produced by other suppliers under their own brand

Specializing in importing/exporting between EU member states

From August 2005, such manufacturers will bear financial responsibility for WEEE and invest in regeneration, restoration, and recycling. This directive applies to WEEE and also includes household bulbs and lighting facilities, except for medical devices and monitoring equipment.

Purpose of RoHS

To protect human health and meet environmental requirements by prohibiting certain hazardous substances in new equipment.

Supplementary Provisions of the WEEE Directive

Six substances banned by the RoHS Directive:

Cadmium

Mercury

Hexavalent Chromium

PBB (Polybrominated Biphenyls)

PBDE (Polybrominated Diphenyl Ethers)

Lead

Exclusions from the directive:

Mercury-containing lamps

Lead content in glass used in cathode-ray tubes, electronic components, and fluorescent tubes

Lead content in steel, aluminum, and copper alloys

Lead in solder used in servers, disk array systems (exemption granted until 2010)

Lead in high-temperature solder

Lead in solder used in network infrastructure equipment

Lead in electronic ceramic products

Cadmium plating

Hexavalent chromium in carbon steel cooling systems of absorption refrigerators

Weapons, munitions, and war material

This exemption clause is reviewed every four years. By February 13, 2005, the EU Commission must review the measures specified in this directive to consider new scientific evidence if necessary.

Countries Affected

RoHS as a "Single Market" directive applies to all EU member states and must be implemented uniformly to prevent different interpretations among member states. The WEEE directive sets minimum standards for waste, which member states can exceed if desired.

Benefits of RoHS

Raw material extraction and final disposal can affect environmental pollution and harm human health. Removing these substances from products will reduce health threats, especially for children, the elderly, and pregnant women.

First Part: Introduction to the EU's WEEE and RoHS Directives for Environmental Protection of Electronic and Electrical Products

One, WEEE Directive

1. WEEE: 2002/96/EC Directive on Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment

2. Core Content of the WEEE Directive

As of August 13, 2005, producers of electronic and electrical equipment on the EU market are legally obligated to pay for the costs of recycling discarded products. Each EU member state has the duty to establish its own electronic and electrical product recycling plan and related supporting recycling facilities to facilitate convenient and free disposal of discarded equipment by end-users.

3. Product Scope Covered by the WEEE Directive

Large household appliances, small household appliances, IT and communication equipment, consumer electronics, lighting equipment, electrical/electronic tools (excluding large fixed industrial tools), toys, leisure and sports equipment, medical equipment, testing and control instruments, automatic vending machines.

4. Goals of the WEEE Directive

By August 13, 2005, producers must establish or use recycling systems, achieving a recycling rate target of 50%-80% within one year (producers, including importers and distributors, are responsible for recycling and processing discarded electronic and electrical products entering the EU market).

5. Producer Responsibilities

Design environmentally friendly products, comply with ROHS directive requirements, and register with EU member states.

6. Product Identification Requirements

Producer name, production date, and relevant symbols (including the "WEEE" recycling symbol).

7. Impact

Establishing a recycling system as required by the WEEE directive targets EU internal "producers" (including importers and distributors), but ultimately, the costs will be passed on to exporters outside the EU, increasing direct and indirect export costs for China’s electronic and electrical products. Chinese companies will have to pay high electronic waste recycling fees when exporting.

Two, RoHS Directive

1. RoHS: 2002/95/EC Directive on the Restriction of the Use of Certain Hazardous Substances in Electrical and Electronic Equipment

2. Core Content of the RoHS Directive

Starting from July 1, 2006, new electronic and electrical equipment placed on the EU market must not contain the following six harmful substances, with maximum allowable concentrations in homogeneous materials as follows:

Lead (Pb): 1000mg/Kg

Mercury (Hg): 1000mg/Kg

Cadmium (Cd): 100mg/Kg

Hexavalent Chromium (Cr6+): 1000mg/Kg

Polybrominated Biphenyls (PBB): 1000mg/Kg

Polybrominated Diphenyl Ethers (PBDE): 1000mg/Kg

Important Deadlines Related to Both Directives

February 13, 2003: Publication of both directives

August 13, 2004: EU member states must convert both directives into national laws and regulations (only some countries completed this on time)

August 13, 2005: Producers should establish a free-of-charge recycling and processing mechanism for end-users; products placed on the market from this date onward are considered "new products" and must bear the corresponding "WEEE" mark.

July 1, 2006: Electronic and electrical products entering the EU market must not contain the six harmful substances (RoHS)

December 31, 2006: EU member states ensure the achievement of WEEE recycling rate targets (as per Article 7(2) of the WEEE directive); each EU household must recycle at least 4 kilograms of old electronic and electrical products annually.

Geographical Scope Covered by Both Directives

1. Covers 25 EU member states and 3 EEA countries, totaling 28 countries.

2. The 25 EU member states include: Austria, Belgium, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Ireland, Italy, Luxembourg, Netherlands, Portugal, Spain, Sweden, United Kingdom, Slovenia, Cyprus, Malta, Poland, Czech Republic, Estonia, Hungary, Latvia, Lithuania, Slovakia.

3. The 3 EEA countries are: Iceland, Norway, Liechtenstein.

Other Regulations Related to Both Directives

1. 94/62/EC: Directive on Packaging and Packaging Waste

2. 91/157/EEC: Directive on Batteries and Accumulators

3. REACH System: EU Future Chemical Policy for Registration, Evaluation, Authorization, and Restriction of Chemicals

4. 2000/53/EC: Directive on End-of-Life Vehicles

RoHS Exemption List

First batch of exemptions includes 9 product-related substances:

Mercury in small fluorescent lamps, with no more than 5 milligrams of mercury per lamp;

Mercury in general straight fluorescent lamps (with corresponding restrictions on mercury salt content);

Mercury in special-purpose straight fluorescent lamps;

Mercury in other lamps;

Lead in cathode-ray tubes, electronic components, and fluorescent tubes;

Lead in alloys, with no more than 0.35% weight percentage in steel-based, no more than 0.4% in aluminum-based, and no more than 4% in copper-based;

Lead in high-melting-point solder (such as tin-lead alloy solder containing more than 85% lead); lead in server, storage, and access array system solder (exemption valid until 2010); lead in solder for network switches, signal transmission, and communication network management equipment; lead in electronic ceramic components (such as piezoelectric components);

Cadmium plating, except where prohibited by relevant directives (91/338/EEC and 76/769/EEC);

Second batch of exemptions may include 22 products, pending release.

Controlled Harmful Substances and Limits

Specific types and limits are detailed in the core content of the RoHS directive mentioned above.

Usage of Controlled Harmful Substances and Possible Components/Materials Containing These Substances in Electrical/Electronic Products

This section helps understand the distribution of controlled harmful substances in electrical/electronic products, guiding future inspection, sampling, monitoring, and early warning work.

1. Lead (Pb)

Main uses of lead and its compounds: Plastic stabilizers, rubber curing agents and co-agents; Welding, coating materials, electrical connections; Battery raw materials; Pigment, paint, ink, dye raw materials; Electroplating solutions; Lubricants, hardeners, drying agents for paints; Ceramic components; Optical glass.

Possible materials containing lead: Packaging materials; Printed circuit boards; Batteries and battery packs; Component electrodes, guide terminals; Paints, pigments, inks, dyes; Various alloys; Electronic ceramic components; Various glass materials, including resistors, adhesives, glass powder, sealants, etc.

2. Cadmium (Cd)

Main uses of cadmium and its compounds: Plastic stabilizers; Chemical synthesis materials; Batteries, photographs; Surface treatment, connection materials; Paints, pigments, inks, colorants; Low melting point welding, fuses; Stabilizers and gloss enhancers for electroplating solutions.

Possible materials containing cadmium: Packaging materials; Plastic components; Batteries and battery packs; Component electrodes, guide terminals; Paints, pigments, inks, dyes; Various alloy components; Electronic ceramic components; Various glass materials, including resistors, adhesives, glass powder, sealants, etc.

3. Mercury (Hg)

Main uses of mercury and its compounds: Preservatives, catalysts, fungicides, bactericides; Metal etching; Batteries; Pigments; Electrodes, mercury lamps.

Possible materials containing mercury: Packaging materials; Printed circuit boards; Batteries and battery packs; Paints, pigments, inks, dyes; Fluorescent lamps.

4. Hexavalent Chromium (Cr6+)

Main uses of hexavalent chromium compounds: Catalysts, preservatives; Coloring agents for ceramics; Batteries; Electroplating solutions, rust inhibitors; Paints, pigments, inks; Tanning.

Possible materials containing hexavalent chromium: Packaging materials, housings; Printed circuit boards; Batteries and battery packs; Components with electroplated rustproof treatments; Paints, pigments, inks, dyes; Leather components.

5. Flame Retardants

Possible materials containing brominated flame retardants:

Plastic product components: Various polymer materials such as PE, ABS, HIPS, LDPE, polyester, electrical plastic housings, wires and cables, switches, etc.; Printed circuit boards.

Typical Test Components

1. Electronic components: Resistors, capacitors, integrated circuits, PCB boards, diodes.

2. Plastic parts: Housings, fan blades, connectors, tubes, plugs, knobs, thermostats, switch housings.

3. Metal parts: Wires, housings, motor shells, springs, enameled wires, solders, screws, contacts.

Penalties for Violating the Directive

If products are found non-compliant during EU market inspections, they will be returned or destroyed at cost. In fact, if OEM manufacturers cannot provide evidence of compliance, EU importers will not purchase these products.

Approaches to Compliance and Precautions

1. Although the RoHS directive assigns responsibility to OEM manufacturers without directly requiring component and material manufacturers, confirming the composition of raw materials and components is a critical issue for OEM manufacturers. Therefore, OEM manufacturers will require detection reports or certificates from raw material and component manufacturers.

2. Unlike other EU directives, verifying compliance of whole products with the RoHS directive through a few tests is nearly impossible. The most reasonable approach is to obtain material test data from raw material and component suppliers and then convert it into product compliance declarations. However, OEM manufacturers still need to assess the reliability of the supplier's test data, which can be done by investigating the reputation of the laboratory issuing the test data or conducting random sampling tests.

3. Testing for all six harmful substances is often unnecessary and very costly. It is essential to consult professional institutions to determine which components need testing for which harmful substances. For example, static coatings on integrated circuit blocks may only require lead testing since tin/lead is the most commonly used material in such coatings. Relatively, cadmium and hexavalent chromium are less likely to be found here, and mercury, polybrominated diphenyl ethers, and polybrominated biphenyls are even less possible.

4. Modifications to your quality system documents may be necessary, adding relevant content.

5. There is always a limit to product consistency. If the harmful substance content of the tested sample approaches the limit, it cannot guarantee that all your products will meet the requirements.

Fourth Part: Related Testing Techniques

RoHS Testing Steps

1. Sample disassembly

2. Screening tests

3. Confirmatory testing

4. Compliance evaluation

5. Report certificate

RoHS-Related Testing Techniques

1. Main screening testing techniques

① X-ray fluorescence spectrometry

Applicable scope: Testing lead, mercury, cadmium, total chromium, polybrominated biphenyls, polybrominated diphenyl ethers in plastic components, metal components, electronic components.

Technical features: Rapid qualitative and semi-quantitative analysis of lead, mercury, cadmium, chromium, bromine element content in one operation. No sample preparation is required for homogeneous products, allowing direct non-destructive testing.

② Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy

Applicable scope: Qualitative screening of high-content PBB and PBDE in polymers, electronic components, and other materials except metallic materials.

Technical features: Determination of PBB and PBDE brominated flame retardants in polymer and electronic component samples based on infrared absorption spectra.

2. Confirmatory testing techniques

① Gas chromatography/mass spectrometry analysis (GC-MS method)

Applicable scope: Quantitative analysis of PBB and PBDE flame retardants in plastics and electronic components.

Instrument: Benchtop GC-MS instrument.

Technical points: Extracting PBB and PBDE components from samples using Soxhlet extraction, microwave extraction, rapid solvent extraction ASE, etc., purifying with solid-phase extraction, and determining PBB and PBDE content using gas chromatography/mass spectrometry analysis.

② Gas chromatography/electron capture detector analysis (GC-ECD method)

Applicable scope: Quantitative analysis of PBB and PBDE flame retardants in plastics and electronic components.

Instrument: Gas chromatograph with ECD detector.

Technical features: Used for determining the content of organic residues containing halogen elements, good selectivity, high detection sensitivity.

③ High-performance liquid chromatography analysis (HPLC method)

Applicable scope: Quantitative analysis of PBB and PBDE flame retardants in plastic components and electronic components.

Main instrument: Liquid chromatograph.

Technical features: In PBB and PBDE testing, HPLC is suitable for testing high-boiling, non-volatile flame retardants like decabromobiphenyl and decabromodiphenyl ether, compensating for the weaknesses of the GC-MS method.

④ Spectrophotometric analysis

Applicable scope: Testing hexavalent chromium content.

Main instrument: UV spectrophotometer.

Technical features: This method is the classic method for hexavalent chromium testing and can refer to multiple domestic and international standards, such as EPA3060A.

⑤ Inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectrometry (ICP-AES method)

Applicable scope: Testing lead, mercury, cadmium, total chromium content in plastic components, metal components, electronic components.

Instrument: Inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectrometer.

Technical points: Selecting appropriate dissolution methods such as microwave digestion, wet digestion, dry digestion to dissolve samples, simultaneously determining lead, mercury, cadmium, total chromium content in one operation.
