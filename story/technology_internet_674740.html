Energy-saving vehicles and new energy vehicles in China are equally positioned.

by jintaimqr on 2012-02-24 15:28:05

The second generation of dual-mode electric vehicle F3DM will be launched at the Beijing Auto Show, BYD [http://www.jt077.cn/] revealed on February 17.

BYD [rehashes old news], the oil-electric hybrid dual-mode electric vehicle is once again highlighted as a key new energy vehicle model. Previously, President Wang Chuanfu's strategic focus was on pure electric vehicles.

Several domestic automobile [http://www.jt077.cn/] companies are also beginning to lean more towards hybrid vehicles. SAIC, FAW, BAIC, Chery, Geely and other automakers are planning to launch hybrid vehicles in 2012; joint venture automakers are even planning to introduce several hybrid vehicles this year, accelerating their efforts to capture China’s new energy vehicle market.

Automobile companies sensitive to policy clearly sense a change in the direction of national policies: recent policies by the State Council have begun to emphasize market choice, placing energy-saving vehicles on an equal footing with new energy vehicles, and plug-in hybrid vehicles are given equal importance to pure electric vehicles.

Meanwhile, insiders told reporters that the long-awaited "Energy Saving and New Energy Development Plan" will also be released soon.

Has the route changed?

A leading domestic new energy vehicle company, BYD, had once estimated that the transition period for hybrid vehicles would only take about three years, after which, by 2012, the domestic new energy vehicle market would be dominated by pure electric vehicles.

After launching the dual-mode hybrid F3DM in 2008, BYD continued its promotion for approximately one year before entering the commercialization phase of the pure electric vehicle e6.

This expectation was largely recognized by most domestic car manufacturers at the time. Zotye, Chery, SAIC, and others initially launched pure electric vehicles, hoping to capture the market in the short term. Prior to 2011, this line of thinking was prevalent.

However, the market did not provide the expected results. The electric vehicle market has remained cold over the past few years without any improvement. In the European and American markets, sales data shows that the market has chosen hybrid vehicles over pure electric vehicles.

Representative automakers like BYD began to shift direction. BYD temporarily shelved the pure electric vehicle e6 and started to re-focus on the previously neglected dual-mode electric vehicle F3DM.

Since 2009, BYD has consistently promoted the pure electric vehicle e6 at various domestic auto shows. However, at this year's Beijing Auto Show, the main attraction is not the e6 but rather the F3DM, which was the star four years ago.

[e6 has no plans for a facelift this year, nor any predetermined sales or promotional goals. However, the second generation dual-mode electric vehicle F3DM will be launched at the Beijing Auto Show and will be the focus of new energy vehicles in the near future.] said BYD.

BYD's second generation dual-mode electric vehicle is the recently mentioned Qin series dual-mode electric vehicle by Wang Chuanfu multiple times. Wang Chuanfu believes that 2012 and 2013 are critical years for the development of China's new energy vehicles. His choice of dual-mode electric vehicles implies that he has acknowledged to some extent that hybrid vehicles will dominate the market for a longer period.

The attitude of large automotive groups is also quietly changing. SAIC once launched the Roewe 350 pure electric vehicle and the E1 pure electric concept car, but hesitated in choosing a path forward, keeping the Roewe 550 hybrid in its product lineup.

Now, SAIC is tilting—SAIC, whose hybrid technology lags behind European and American automakers, chooses to cooperate with Volkswagen. Another partner of Volkswagen in China, FAW, also made this choice and decided to release a Chinese version of a hybrid vehicle in 2015.

The direction of national industrial policies has also changed. Due to the belief that hybrid technology lags behind the U.S., Japan, and Europe, relevant national departments once advocated "overtaking on a curve," skipping the transitional "hybrid technology" and directly developing pure electric vehicles.

However, this policy-driven development mode has not been recognized by the market. Except for government procurement, the private purchasing market for pure electric vehicles has not been initiated. Chen Quanshi, Director of the Electric Vehicle Division of the China Society of Automotive Engineers, once told this reporter that it cannot yet be definitively stated which technology will work and which will not, so all types of new energy vehicles powered by different energy sources should receive equal support from the government.

At the end of last year, the State Council issued the "Industrial Transformation and Upgrading Plan (2011-2015)" (hereinafter referred to as "the Plan"), which altered the original path. The "Plan" mentions in the chapter on energy-saving and new energy vehicles that both energy-saving and new energy vehicles should be developed simultaneously, and accelerate the development of pure electric vehicles, plug-in hybrid vehicles, and other new energy vehicles.

Veteran automotive insider Jia Xinguang believes that the "Plan" no longer focuses solely on the pure electric route, avoiding the previous single breakthrough method of pure electric vehicles, clearly defining the immediate work priorities as pure electric vehicles and plug-in hybrid vehicles, while also conducting research and development on ordinary hybrid vehicles. The "Plan" does not rush to position any particular route as mainstream, allowing the market to choose.

Japanese push to seize

In Germany, France, and Japan, the sales of oil-electric hybrid vehicles far exceed those of pure electric vehicles, with Toyota Prius selling as much as 252,528 units last year.

Japanese automakers, who hold an advantage in oil-electric hybrid technology, maintain a consistent understanding of China’s new energy vehicle market and are preparing to make substantial moves to capture the market in 2012.

Toyota Akio Toyoda clearly stated that the focus in the Chinese market is promoting oil-electric hybrid vehicles. Toyota has successively launched the Prius, hybrid Camry, and Lexus CT200h in China. Soon, FAW Toyota will launch the domestically produced third-generation Prius.

Honda is also introducing hybrid models on a large scale. Besides the previously introduced hybrid Civic, this year Guangqi Honda will introduce two models equipped with hybrid technology: the FIT Hybrid and CR-Z. Dongfeng Honda also plans to import the Insight hybrid vehicle.

The favorable expectations for the future hybrid vehicle market have sparked competition. The second generation F3DM, represented by BYD among domestic brands, has become more mature in technology. According to BYD, the new generation F3DM can accelerate from 0 to 100 kilometers per hour in just 6 seconds, with fuel consumption reduced to 2.6 liters per 100 kilometers.

Companies that experienced the pure electric vehicle craze have formed a relatively unified understanding, represented by Wang Chuanfu: pure electric vehicles are currently only suitable for public buses and taxis in the short term; for the private new energy vehicle market, hybrid vehicles should be the mainstay.

But there are also hesitant companies that misjudged the situation. Last year, Zotye Automobile encountered setbacks in the pure electric vehicle sector. On February 20, senior executives inside the company were interviewed by this reporter but did not explicitly indicate the specific direction of new energy vehicle development.
