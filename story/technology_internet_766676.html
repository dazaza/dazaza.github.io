Humorous Version: If Su Shi Used WeChat on the Mid-Autumn Festival

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 21:21:48

"When did the bright moon first appear? I raise my cup and ask the blue sky. I don't know what year it is tonight in the celestial palace?

I wish to ride the wind back home, but I fear the cold at the heights of the jade buildings. Dancing with my clear shadow, how can it be compared to being on earth!

It turns around vermilion pavilions, dips beneath embroidered windows, and shines upon those who cannot sleep. There should be no resentment, so why does it always become full when we part?

People experience joy, sorrow, separation, and reunion; the moon waxes, wanes, becomes full or disappears. Such things have been difficult to perfect since ancient times. May we both live long lives, sharing the beauty of the moon even if miles apart."

In 1076 AD (the ninth year of Emperor Shenzong's reign during the Song Dynasty), Su Shi was serving as the governor of Mizhou (now Zhucheng City in Shandong Province). On the night of the Mid-Autumn Festival that year, he drank until the next morning and wrote this poem while missing his younger brother Su Zhe. But what if he had used WeChat back then? Would this timeless Mid-Autumn poem have still been born?

Here, we might as well simulate a WeChat dialogue between the two brothers. (Su Shi’s WeChat ID: Zizhan; Su Zhe’s WeChat ID: Ziyou)

Ziyou: The moon is so round today, did you see it, Brother?

Zizhan: Yes. We share the same moment under different skies. Have you eaten mooncakes?

Ziyou: I'm eating one now, with five-nut filling, it's very delicious. What are you doing?

Zizhan: A bit depressed, drinking some wine to drown my sorrows.

Ziyou: Today is a holiday, don't think about work.

Zizhan: How can I not think about it? Moved here today, there tomorrow. This place is so remote...

Ziyou: Brother, answer my video call, let me see if you've lost weight.

(In the video...)

Zizhan: Did you see the white hair at my temples?

Ziyou: I can vaguely see it. Don't overthink things, take care of your health.

Zizhan: Yes. I do exercise. Seeing you in such good spirits makes me feel much better.

Ziyou: Brother, you've had too much to drink, your face is red.

Zizhan: I'm not drunk, I can still drink more.

Ziyou: Take care of yourself, go to bed early.

If "though far apart, yet close as neighbors" was merely an idealized psychological comfort for the ancients, then today we are experiencing the convenience brought by instant messaging.

Nowadays, we no longer have the anticipation of "who will send me letters from the clouds," nor understand the preciousness of "a letter from home worth ten thousand pieces of gold," and even lack the deep sentiment of "ten years of rain at night in the rivers and lakes." Efficient information transmission allows people to avoid the pain of longing, smoothing out feelings of departure. However, the mental space left for individuals has become even narrower.

In this sense, the instantaneous arrival of information is the killer of creation.

If Su Shi had used WeChat, perhaps he wouldn't have been so tormented. Serving away from home, his thoughts for his family would have been easier to resolve. Exchanging with friends could help alleviate his pent-up emotions. Conversely, Su Shi could only find release through writing, using creation to fight against the long passage of time and great distances.

It's easy to imagine that if Su Shi had used WeChat back then, "When did the bright moon first appear?" might never have come into existence.
