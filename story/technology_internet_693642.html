Smoking affects fetal development

Smoking during pregnancy is very detrimental to the development and health of the fetus. Tobacco contains a large amount of toxic substances, besides the well-known nicotine, there are also hydrogen cyanide, ammonia, carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, pyridine, aromatic compounds, and tar. These toxic substances can be absorbed into the mother's blood with smoke, reducing the oxygen content in the mother's blood and consequently decreasing the oxygen content in the placenta. Due to hypoxia, the fetus may experience delayed growth and development. Therefore, low birth weight babies (weighing less than 2500 grams) born to smoking mothers are twice as likely compared to non-smoking women. These infants are not only physically weak but also have a higher risk of serious diseases within the first year of life and a high mortality rate. Recent studies have also shown that smoking pregnant women are more likely to experience miscarriage, premature birth, and stillbirths compared to non-smoking pregnant women. A survey conducted on 14,893 pregnant women in the UK showed that smoking women find it harder to conceive and are more likely to miscarry. The survey also found that smoking during pregnancy damages the fertility of their daughters when they grow up.

High-fiber foods can lead to female infertility

American scientists have been tracking 250 women of childbearing age for nearly two years and finally concluded: the intake of high-fiber foods such as whole wheat bread and macaroni disrupts hormonal balance in women, and the more they consume, the lower the chances of becoming pregnant.

Researchers found that the intake of high-fiber foods not only lowers hormone levels but also stops ovulation. Previous studies have shown that factors such as high-intensity exercise, anxiety, and stress can all lead to cessation of ovulation, and this study is the first to link healthy eating habits to this phenomenon.

Excessive carotene intake can affect lutein synthesis in the ovaries, reduce secretion, and in some cases, even cause amenorrhea, anovulation, or irregular menstruation, increasing the likelihood of female infertility.

This condition was first discovered in patients with anorexia nervosa; even if they don't eat and have no menstruation, blood tests still show high levels of carotene. Later, it was found in some non-anorexic female patients that consuming large amounts of carrots can cause elevated carotene levels in the blood, resulting in female infertility, amenorrhea, anovulation, and other abnormal conditions. Researchers explained that this might be due to carotene interfering with steroid synthesis.

Additionally, coffee, alcohol, and other substances can also lead to female infertility, and women should avoid excessive intake. At the same time, women who wish to conceive can appropriately consume more foods that help with conception.
