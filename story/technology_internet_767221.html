How Big Should a Smartphone Screen Be?

by anonymous on 2013-10-10 19:18:20

### 1. The Era of Large-Screen Smartphones Arrives?

Before the first-generation iPhone redefined the concept of a smartphone, few people paid much attention to the size of a phone's screen. Compared to feature phones, the 3.5-inch multi-touch screen on the iPhone was already considered large. Relative to the first three generations of iPhones, Android devices with 4.0 or 4.3-inch screens could be called "large." And compared to many other Android phones with screens ranging from 4.0 to 5.0 inches, Samsung’s 5.3-inch Note clearly made anything below 5.0 inches seem less impressive.

According to a report by the well-known market research company IHS iSuppli: in 2012, the shipment volume of smartphones with screens larger than 5 inches was 25.6 million units. In 2013, the shipment volume of phablets (phones with screen sizes between 5 and 7 inches) reached 60.4 million units. This year, new flagship models like Samsung's 5.5-inch Note II, Sony's 5.7-inch Xplay, and Huawei's 6.1-inch Ascend Mate have emerged as giants. Even Xiaomi, known for its cost-effective offerings, launched its Redmi model with a 4.7-inch screen.

As consumers wonder about the limits of smartphone screen sizes, Sony’s Xperia Z Ultra and XL39h provide an answer —— 6.44 inches! No wonder Luo Yonghao, who originally planned to make a 3.5-inch "Smartisan" phone a year and a half ago, now has to carefully consider how big his upcoming hardware should be.

### 2. Reasons for the Popularity of Large-Screen Smartphones, Usage Complaints, and Interaction Issues

Let’s tentatively define smartphones with screens equal to or greater than 5.0 inches as "large-screen" phones. There are many reasons why these large-screen phones have become popular, especially when manufacturers can provide high-end hardware configurations. These include:

1. A larger visible range and clearer visual experience.

2. More detailed screen quality and vibrant colors.

3. Buying one large-screen phone can replace both a tablet and a phone.

4. Larger batteries offer longer battery life.

5. Accessory support such as bundled styluses.

6. Keeping up with trends, status symbols, and showing off.

These features appeal to gamers, movie and music enthusiasts, selfie-takers, and heavy e-book readers.

At the same time, large-screen smartphones bring many complaints. They are often described as “clumsy,” “designed for giants,” or “won’t fit in pockets.” A common complaint is this scenario: you're typing in a beautiful café when a petite, stylish woman across from you pulls out a "brick" bigger than her face from her tiny bag and whispers into it: "Hey, can you hear me?"

Clearly, large-screen phones severely challenge users' habits of single-handed grip and operation. According to data from a study published by UXmatters earlier this year on user habits of holding and operating phones: 49% of users hold their phones with one hand, 36% use a "cradle" grip (holding the phone with both hands but only using one hand to touch the screen or keyboard), and only 15% use both hands.

So, at what point does the size of a phone's screen become too large for comfortable single-handed operation?

Researchers at Springer Link conducted an experiment on the impact of smartphone size on operational performance. The study covered smartphones with screens ranging from 2.55 inches to 5.3 inches and tested single-handed efficiency in four directions: diagonal, horizontal, vertical, and center-cross. The report pointed out:

According to the analysis, 4-inch touchscreen phones result in the fewest operational errors, while 3-inch touchscreen phones require the shortest operational time. To achieve the best visual effect, a 4-inch touchscreen may be the best choice for single-handed operation.

It is evident that even though 5.0-inch smartphones sell well in the market, they scientifically exceed what is necessary. New hardware technologies, such as flexible-screen phones set to launch this month, or system improvements like Meizu MX3's Flyme 3.0, might improve the experience. However, I firmly believe that for smartphones, the question isn't whether we can keep making screens bigger, but whether we should—or more accurately, whether it’s smart to do so.

### Interaction Problems with Single-Handed Operation on Large-Screen Smartphones

To further elaborate, the unintelligent aspects of overly large screens lie in the following interaction pain points for ordinary users:

- The length of the phone exceeds the length of the longest finger (usually the index or middle finger), making it difficult to reach the top of the screen or buttons, especially the power button used for unlocking.

- The width of the phone exceeds the length of the thumb, making it hard to reach areas on the far left (for right-handed users) or far right (for left-handed users) of the screen.

- Due to the pursuit of aesthetic design, most large-screen smartphones adopt narrow bezels, leading to frequent accidental touches.

Are phone manufacturers sacrificing usability for sales, or will they show more human-centered consideration when deciding on screen sizes? That remains a question.

### 3. Ultimate Discussion: The Reasonable Range for Smartphone Screen Sizes

In conclusion, smartphones over 5.0 inches are generally unsuitable for single-handed operation. Based on average hand lengths, phones with screens larger than 4.0 inches are already difficult for women to operate with one hand. Consider that even Kobe Bryant, who stands at two meters tall, uses a 5.5-inch Lenovo K900.

Returning to the necessity of single-handed operation, the surface-level factors are mainly safety-related, such as ensuring the phone doesn't easily fall when held in one hand, or being able to text while holding onto a subway handrail. Psychologically, modern people view smartphones as extensions of themselves, and they must serve and obey human needs. No one wants to be controlled by their phone or restricted by its size from single-handed operation. From another perspective, the evolution from text to images to voice also reflects the gradual liberation of hands during phone usage.

Existence does not necessarily equate to reasonableness. Reminding ourselves of the original purpose of smartphones as handheld devices, particularly considering the convenience of single-handed operation, combined with personal experiences, I propose a reasonable screen size reference for smartphones: under 5.0 inches, ideally between 4.0 and 4.7 inches. The former can be referenced from the iPhone 5s, and the latter from the Redmi. As for next year’s Smartisan phone, I hope its screen falls between 4.0 and 4.3 inches—compact and meticulously designed. If the display needs to accommodate Smartisan icons, it might be around 4.3 to 4.5 inches. Apple, which has already started testing iPhones with screens ranging from 4.8 to 6 inches, gives us another expectation: will there be an iPhone with a larger screen? Will its appearance be due to natural upgrades or driven by market competition?

We shall wait and see.
