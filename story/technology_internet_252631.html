Will the Nobel Prize Believe in Yang Zhenning's Prediction?

Xu Shuguang, Chairman and President of GreenTree Hospitality Group

Yang Zhenning stated at a celebration event in the Chinese University of Hong Kong that "there will be Nobel laureates from mainland China within 20 years."

Yang Zhenning claimed that it is certain that mainland China will produce Nobel Prize winners within 20 years. In 1957, Yang Zhenning and Lee Tsung-Dao won the Nobel Prize for discovering the "non-conservation of parity." On July 7th, Yang Zhenning celebrated with over a hundred students and teachers at the Chinese University of Hong Kong to honor former CUHK president Charles Kao, who was residing in the United States at the time. According to Hong Kong’s Ta Kung Pao, Yang Zhenning said that Charles Kao winning the Nobel Prize increased his confidence in the prospect of mainland China producing Nobel laureates. "I have repeatedly mentioned that I believe within 20 years, including Hong Kong and Macau, mainland China will certainly see Nobel Prize-level scientific achievements emerge." (October 8th, 2009, China News Service)

The annual list of Nobel Prize winners was announced as scheduled, with Chinese-American scientist Charles Kao receiving the 2009 Nobel Prize in Physics. This good news was reported by almost every mainstream website in China. We are proud and feel honored for him. However, it also makes every Chinese person reflect: Yang Zhenning, Lee Tsung-Dao, Samuel C.C. Ting, Yuan Tseh Lee, Steven Chu, Daniel Tsui... Their blood runs with Chinese ancestry, yet not one of them is a Nobel laureate born on mainland China, which has almost become a national "heartache." Yang Zhenning's "grandiose and confident" prediction seems to have injected a "stimulant" into the hearts of the people. Although he has been saying this for many years, some still find it very inspiring. Can his prediction become reality? I cannot speculate. But for China, a major country accounting for one-fifth of the world's population, to fail to cultivate even one Nobel Prize-winning scientist is indeed a thought-provoking issue.

The American Physical Society journal once pointed out that in the past decade or so, the number of papers from China has significantly increased, but their quality has decreased. Standing Committee member of the National Committee of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference, Zhang Tao, provided an astonishing statistic: "Among the tens of thousands of research 'results' counted by the state each year, more than 90% have no practical value, forming a large amount of 'scientific bubbles,' which not only causes severe waste but also fosters poor academic practices." Academic corruption scandals, such as plagiarism, have occurred to varying degrees in China's higher education institutions, including prestigious ones. There are indeed problems in China's academic community, not just due to institutional reasons but more importantly influenced by the current socio-economic culture. If a tree grows sick, it's not just the tree's problem; we must examine whether the soil where the tree roots itself has issues. Many Chinese have won the Nobel Prize, indicating that there's nothing wrong with the Chinese gene pool. Yet these excellent seeds haven't grown into towering trees on Chinese soil but have sprouted abroad, forcing us to scrutinize our own soil and growth environment.

In the 1980s, there was a popular saying: "A person making atomic bombs earns less than someone selling tea eggs." Over the thirty years of reform and opening up, the world has witnessed the miracle of China's rapid economic takeoff. Scientists involved in atomic bomb projects have seen significant improvements in both social and economic status. However, rapid development has bred a mindset among the populace of seeking quick success and instant benefits, shortcuts, and impatience, which has spread to the academic world. The critical thinking, questioning spirit, and dedication to academia, constant breakthroughs, and pure devotion to learning that universities should possess have become rare in many institutions. Only fame and profit remain as unchanging pursuits. Academia, under the guise of various interest groups, no longer represents academia itself, thereby losing its independence and becoming increasingly incapable of bearing bountiful fruits.

Of course, more and more people are recognizing these issues and striving to improve them. Modern science in New China started much later than in developed countries. As China's economy continues to advance, the demand for technology is also increasing. We should continue to learn from advanced civilizations abroad and steadily progress by standing on the shoulders of others. By persisting in the spirit of exploring the origins of the universe, nature, and humanity and striving for the highest welfare of humankind, with the intelligence and wisdom of the Chinese people, we can surely, as Yang Zhenning predicted, see Nobel laureates emerge from mainland China in the near future!
