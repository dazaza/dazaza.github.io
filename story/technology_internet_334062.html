Deciphering the 5 Major Causes of Kidney Failure

by zhaolinling on 2010-03-19 11:07:36

Various chronic kidney diseases can lead to progressive reduction in renal function, eventually developing into chronic renal failure. These include primary glomerulonephritis, chronic pyelonephritis, tubulointerstitial nephritis, hereditary nephritis, and polycystic kidney disease;

Secondary conditions arising from systemic diseases include lupus nephritis, diabetic nephropathy, hypertensive renal arteriosclerosis, nodular polyarteritis nephropathy, multiple myeloma nephropathy, and kidney diseases caused by various drugs and heavy metals;

Urinary tract obstruction-related nephropathies such as kidney stones, benign prostatic hyperplasia, neurogenic bladder, and urethral stricture also exist. In our country, chronic glomerulonephritis ranks first, followed by chronic pyelonephritis.

However, the proportion of secondary kidney lesions is on the rise.

In Western countries, recent statistics in hemodialysis treatment for slowly progressing renal insufficiency indicate that diabetic nephropathy ranks first; hypertensive renal arteriosclerosis nephropathy ranks second; and chronic glomerulonephritis has dropped to third place.

According to statistics, the causes of renal failure in our country and other Asian countries are ranked as follows:

1. Primary Glomerulonephritis

Most cases of primary glomerulonephritis are immune-mediated inflammatory diseases, with only a few cases of chronic nephritis resulting from acute nephritis. The exact cause of the majority of chronic nephritis cases remains unclear, and they present as chronic from the onset. Clinical manifestations are diverse, including proteinuria, hematuria, hypertension, edema, etc., accompanied by varying degrees of renal dysfunction. The condition fluctuates in severity, lingering without cure, progressively developing into chronic renal failure. 2. Diabetic Nephropathy

Diabetic nephropathy is one of the three major complications of diabetes, accounting for approximately 30%~50% of Type 1 diabetes cases, and after 20 years of Type 2 diabetes onset, it accumulates to 25%~50%. Type 2 diabetes is more common in middle-aged and elderly individuals, and when combined with hypertension, it easily leads to kidney damage, i.e., diabetic nephropathy. According to data, the incidence of diabetic nephropathy in elderly diabetic patients reaches 30%~40%, particularly higher in patients with a history of 6~10 years or more. If blood glucose can be controlled within normal range, blood pressure kept below 120/80 mmHg, and dietary therapy focuses not only on restricting total calories but also on controlling protein intake, primarily adopting a low-protein diet, this can delay renal failure.

3. Hypertensive Nephropathy

Persistently elevated blood pressure over the long term can lead to progressive renal sclerosis and accelerate the occurrence of renal artery atherosclerosis, causing renal function damage. To slow down the progression of chronic renal failure, people need to first enhance their understanding of the harm caused by hypertension. Secondly, they should familiarize themselves with the early symptoms of renal failure, such as fatigue, weakness, soreness in the waist and legs, loss of appetite or nausea, vomiting, sallow complexion, or pallor of the lips and eyelids. Thirdly, closely monitor the renal function of hypertensive patients, preferably every two months. Fourthly, reasonably select antihypertensive drugs.

4. Tubulointerstitial Nephritis

The kidneys are important organs for drug metabolism and excretion, with renal blood flow accounting for about 25% of cardiac output. When toxic substances are present in the blood, the kidneys are easily damaged. Common drugs causing kidney damage include gentamicin, ampicillin, ciprofloxacin, non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, etc. Although drug-induced damage is common, it is often overlooked by both doctors and patients. Therefore, during the diagnosis and treatment of various diseases, attention should be paid to correcting the patient's insufficient blood volume and hypoalbuminemia. Diabetic patients, elderly, and children should closely monitor routine urinalysis, urinary enzymes, and renal function during medication, which is an important measure to prevent drug-induced kidney damage.

5. Polycystic Kidney Disease

Polycystic kidney disease is a congenital condition, mostly bilateral, and can coexist with congenital polycystic liver disease. The patient's renal parenchyma consists of countless cysts of varying sizes. As time progresses, the cysts gradually enlarge, compressing the renal tissue and causing it to atrophy. Eventually, the entire renal parenchyma is replaced by cysts, leading to the patient's death due to renal failure.

There are many diseases that cause renal failure, such as urinary tract stone obstruction, repeated urinary tract infections in middle-aged women, elderly men with enlarged prostates, etc. It is essential to actively prevent and treat the primary diseases to prevent their development into renal failure.

Although dialysis technology for treating chronic renal failure in our country has reached the level of developed countries, with some patients surviving more than 20 years after dialysis (the longest survival time for dialysis patients in Japan is 23 years), renal failure still significantly affects people's quality of life, increasing the economic burden and mental burden on patients. Only by better understanding and recognizing it can we stay away from it, living healthily and happily every day.
