Google's 15 Years: 10 Milestone Moments

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:49:20

Today is the 15th anniversary of tech giant Google. Over these fifteen years, Google has completely transformed our way of searching. Let's take a look at the top ten milestone moments of this company.

1. The Establishment of Google

Every legendary journey has its starting point. In 1998, Larry Page and Sergey Brin founded the company in a garage in Menlo Park, California. A few days after the company was established, they registered the domain name Google.com. The name "Google" is derived from the word "googol," which means "10 to the power of 100, an enormous number." This expresses the founders' belief in organizing the vast amount of information on the internet constructively and providing users with search results.

2. Entering the Mainstream, Thanks to Yahoo

Until around 2000, Google was not a mainstream company in the search field. At that time, Yahoo was the largest search engine on the market, having started its search business in 1994. In choosing their partners, Yahoo abandoned Inktomi and selected Google to provide organic search results, a decision that changed the landscape of the search industry for the next fifteen years.

Later, the two companies got into various disputes, including over technology patents. In 2003, Yahoo acquired the search technology company Overture, but soon after, Overture sued Google for infringing on its "pay-per-click" patent. In 2004, Google settled this matter by paying Yahoo 2.4 million Class A shares (approximately $260 million to $290 million). However, this incident also led to the establishment of Google AdWords.

3. Self-service Pay-per-click Search

In the early days, search engines like Lycos, Ask Jeeves, and Excite all used banner ads as their revenue model, which worked well in the 1990s but gradually became outdated.

In 2012, Google collaborated with 350 customers to launch the pay-per-click system AdWords. This system allowed brands to manage their own advertising affairs, bypassing intermediaries such as ad agencies, saving time, effort, and cost.

Google also added various personalized customization services to AdWords, making ads directly face the search results, making them more attractive. Additionally, Google introduced the concept of "bidding," offering better positions to advertisers who bid higher prices.

After this concept appeared, Yahoo and Microsoft quickly followed suit. By 2012, AdWords had generated a total revenue of $42.5 billion for Google.

4. Microsoft + Yahoo > Google?

Google's monopoly in the search field prompted Microsoft and Yahoo to form a search alliance in 2010. They began using Microsoft's search algorithm and paid search platform. As part of the cooperation, Yahoo became the "exclusive ad agency" for the alliance, finding advertisers globally for both companies.

This cooperation agreement was so important that it required approval from regulatory agencies in the United States and the European Union to proceed. It was approved at the beginning of 2010.

But what were the results? Although Yahoo decided to extend their partnership for another year in 2012, Marissa Mayer, who later took over as CEO of Yahoo, stated that the agreement had not contributed to expanding market share or increasing revenue for either party.

Earlier this year, Yahoo signed a "global, non-exclusive content-related advertising" agreement with Google, officially terminating its partnership with Microsoft.

5. Why Is My Website Ranking Low?

The ranking strategy of this search giant has always been controversial. In March 2006, KinderStart.com, a U.S.-based parental advisory website, sued Google, accusing it of manipulating "page rankings" to lower its search result rankings. The site claimed that Google's actions violated the Second Amendment of the U.S. Constitution and the Sherman Antitrust Act, discriminating against users and constituting a monopoly.

In the end, Judge Jeremy Fogel of the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California dismissed KinderStart's charges, stating that Google's actions were not predatory competitive business decisions and were legal.

Criticism has never ceased, with some companies complaining that Google does not consider consumers, only recommending its own products in search results. Eric Schmidt, Google's Executive Chairman, even defended these issues at a U.S. Senate Antitrust Committee hearing.

6. The Rise of the Knowledge Graph

Now is the era of platforms and big data, where excellent companies all have their own data tools. For example, Facebook has Social Graph, Eventbrite has Event Graph, LinkedIn has Economic Graph, and Google has the Knowledge Graph.

For instance, if a user types "Van Gogh," the Knowledge Graph will display information about Van Gogh's life and works, sourced from knowledge websites like Wikipedia, Freebase, and CIA World Factbook.

Currently, the Knowledge Graph contains 570 million data points and 18 billion relationships between pieces of information. Data from the Knowledge Graph is also integrated into products like Google Now.

7. Is This the Picture You're Looking For?

Originally, people wanting to find a picture online would need to input keywords related to the image in a picture search. What if you received a celebrity photo and wanted to know who it was?

To address this, Google launched an "image-to-image" search service in 2011. By uploading an image directly into the image search, users could obtain all information about the image and find similar related pictures.

8. Helping People Make Money on Their Websites

Clearly, Google, with its massive traffic, can easily earn money from advertisers. However, in 2003, the company launched a service called AdSense, diversifying how third-party websites could make money.

Previously, these websites primarily relied on banner ads for revenue. If click-through rates were low, income was difficult to guarantee. Once a website installed AdSense, Google took full responsibility for managing and maintaining the site's advertisements. Advertisers could place ads on specific sites based on their needs, such as promoting Canon cameras or photography courses on a photography forum. Moreover, Google equipped AdSense with various functions.

In 2012, AdSense generated $2.88 billion in revenue for Google.

9. Is Google a Monopoly?

Debates about Google being involved in monopolistic practices have never ceased. The U.S. government almost sued Google over this matter, and just this year, the U.S. Trade Commission (FTC) concluded a two-year investigation into Google.

Competitors like Microsoft and Yelp have requested that the government sue Google. However, due to a settlement agreement between Google and the FTC, Google avoided the fate Microsoft faced in the 1990s. According to the agreement, Google voluntarily adjusted its search and online advertising businesses.

The European Union began an antitrust investigation into Google in November 2010. Joaquin Almunia expressed four concerns regarding Google's abuse of its dominant market position in a letter to Schmidt. Earlier this month, Google proposed new concessions, but Schmidt indicated that the issue remains unresolved.

According to comScore data, Google holds a 67% share of the U.S. search market, up 0.3% quarter-over-quarter. Bing, owned by Microsoft, ranks second with a 17.9% share.

10. Bringing Search to the Road

Whether on the web or mobile devices, Google has consistently strived to provide users with a better search experience. Google was the first company to offer a search app for mobile devices, currently supporting iOS and Android systems. Users can also use Google Now through Android devices or the Chrome browser. Amit Singhal, Google's Senior Vice President, said that initially, people could only search for answers on desktop computers via the internet, but lacked a faster way to find information like movie showtimes or navigation maps.

The rise of mobile internet will change this status quo. According to eMarketer data, in August this year, Google accounted for approximately one-fifth of the total mobile search advertising revenue in the U.S. Mobile internet is forming a win-win situation for users, advertisers, and Google.

Google Search has moved far beyond the simple text box, evolving into multiple platforms. It integrates with the Knowledge Graph and is embedded in multiple products such as Gmail, YouTube, and Google+.
