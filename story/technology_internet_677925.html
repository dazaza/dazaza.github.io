Due to investors' profit-taking, the April contract of New York gold futures fluctuated downward last Friday, with a decline of $9.9 or 0.60%. Although this attempt of gold to reach $1800 per ounce failed, considering the strong support at the $1700 line and that the medium- to long-term bullish factors have not disappeared, this pullback can be considered as a buying opportunity.

First-quarter US economic growth will slow down

The annualized quarter-on-quarter GDP of the United States in the fourth quarter reached 2.8%, which was the highest level since the second quarter of 2010. Consumption expenditure and private inventory investment had a significant impact on GDP. However, despite the increase in real estate, software and equipment investment, which would lead to a certain degree of rise in fixed asset investment's contribution to GDP, due to the unexpected retail sales in the third quarter of last year, business enterprise inventories dropped sharply, and the restocking behavior in the fourth quarter would gradually end. In the last few months of last year, consumption grew faster than income, leading to a continued decline in the savings rate. This kind of consumption growth supported by savings consumption is likely to be unsustainable. The U.S. Conference Board Consumer Confidence Index in January fell short of expectations, and under the background of slow income growth and high oil prices, it is difficult for consumption to perform strongly. In addition, the U.S. trade deficit in December was $48.8 billion, slightly higher than expected, reaching the highest level in nearly half a year, and exports and government purchases may continue to have a slight negative impact on GDP. Although recent U.S. employment data has been impressive, it will take a long time to reduce the unemployment rate below 7%. The performance of the U.S. economy in the first quarter may not be as good as imagined, which constitutes a bullish factor for the gold price.

Eurozone liquidity improves significantly

The Eurogroup previously asked the Greek parliament to approve the reduction of an additional €325 million in expenditures. To obtain the total €130 billion bailout loan from international creditors, the Greek parliament also approved the painful fiscal austerity plan, thus creating conditions for the country to obtain new loans and avoid default, paving the way for the second round of bailout funds. The Eurogroup will hold another finance ministers' meeting to re-examine Greece's austerity plan. It is expected that Greece obtaining the second round of assistance is just a matter of time, and the risk of "[disorderly default]" has greatly decreased.

Since the European Central Bank implemented the first three-year Long-Term Refinancing Operation (LTRO) in December last year, liquidity in the European banking sector has continued to improve. The market expects that the amount involved in the second three-year LTRO project will be between €500 billion and €1 trillion. In addition, the macroeconomic prospects of Europe are weak, and countries such as France, Italy, and Spain have entered a mild recession due to the impact of fiscal austerity. The manufacturing PMI index of the Eurozone in January was 48.8, up from 46.9 in December last year, but has remained in the contraction zone for six consecutive months. Fiscal austerity and bank deleveraging will drag down Eurozone economic growth. It is expected that the Eurozone economy will experience a recession this year, which will in turn increase the difficulty for countries in the region to reduce debt through implementing austerity plans, making it hard to break the vicious cycle.

Unchanged medium- to long-term uptrend in gold

The previous outbreak of the European debt crisis caused liquidity tightening, and near the end of the year, capital exited the gold market, causing gold's safe-haven function to gradually disappear and more closely follow the trend of risky assets. Currently, CFTC non-commercial long positions have increased for four consecutive weeks, and there has been a significant increase in gold ETF holdings, indicating a noticeable flow of funds back into the gold market. Moreover, the Federal Reserve maintains a dovish stance on loose monetary policy, and the European Central Bank will implement the second Long-Term Refinancing Operation, so the global liquidity situation will continue to improve. In addition, the worsening of the Iranian oil embargo issue will push up oil prices, posing a new round of cost-push inflation risks to the global economy. With the recovery of gold's safe-haven demand and the enhancement of its value preservation demand, the medium- to long-term upward trend of the gold price remains unchanged.
