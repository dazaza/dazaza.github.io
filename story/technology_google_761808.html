Stop worrying about privacy: Google Glass is the worst spying tool in history

by geekzhang on 2013-05-10 20:42:08

May 10, Beijing Time - According to foreign media reports, since the concept of Google Glass was introduced, controversies regarding potential privacy issues related to this device have never ceased.In response, Will Oremus, a contributing writer for the well-known American tech magazine Slate, recently published an article based on his personal trial experience. He pointed out that considering the design features and operational steps of Google Glass, it is almost impossible to use this device for voyeurism, and people's concerns in this area are completely unnecessary. At the same time, Oremus also believes we need to pay attention to certain radical behaviors by hackers targeting this device.Below is the main content of the article:Imagine having a device that you can carry with you all day, quietly storing audio, video, and photos on its built-in memory card. And after owning this device, you can enter subways, bars, or cafes unnoticed by others and take pictures at any time.This is indeed a privacy nightmare, right?In fact, such a device already exists, but it’s not called Google Glass; it's named "BaseballCapWithHiddenSpyCameraandRecorder." This device is currently available online at U-Spy Store for $199.95. Through this device, users can shoot videos using its hidden spy camera and then view them on their smartphones.At this point, you might be thinking that Google has also released a similar device known as Google Glass. However, the price of this experimental stage device is nearly ten times more expensive than the former, and only a few early applicants can purchase it. Shortly after its release, it was labeled with titles like "the ultimate creepy stalker toy," "the end of privacy," and "an evil, evil device."For those who have hardly ever used Google Glass, the idea of wearing a camera next to your eyes is indeed frightening. But after I communicated with some Google employees and privacy experts and personally tried out this device, I came to a very definite conclusion: Google Glass is a terrible tool for voyeurism.The Worst Voyeur Tool EverFirstly, from the moment you put on Google Glass, you instantly become the most suspicious person in the room: some people will avoid your gaze, some will look puzzled, and others may even give you unfriendly smiles. It is almost certain that in the early days of Google Glass, no one will ignore the existence of this device. Therefore, if someone hopes to use this device to obtain others' private information, they are likely to leave disappointed.Secondly, when users take photos using Google Glass, it is almost impossible not to attract further attention. Currently, the method to activate the photo-taking function of Google Glass is shouting "Ok, Glass," so even if others did not notice you entering the room with these peculiar glasses before, they will certainly notice you now and start making comments. So, shouting commands like "take a picture" or "record a video" at this moment is obviously not a good choice.Of course, you can also perform these operations using other more discreet methods, such as activating some functions by tilting your head upwards. However, I had to try three times before successfully completing this operation. Additionally, you can also take photos or record videos by touching the touch button on the edge of the lens. But note that the default recording time for Google Glass video recording is only 10 seconds, and if you want to continue recording, you need to repeat the above actions.If none of the above cumbersome steps caught anyone's attention, then congratulations! You can finally secretly shoot anyone you're interested in. All you need to do is keep staring at them while wearing these strange Google Glasses. Oh, there is one more thing to remind you: when you are shooting a video, the screen of Google Glass will be lit up, and because its lens is transparent, others will clearly notice this. In fact, if they look closely enough, they may even see what you are browsing. Therefore, I suggest you maintain a certain distance from the subject during secret shooting, but not too far because Google Glass does not have a zoom function.To be honest, before actually getting my hands on this device, I also believed that Google Glass would be a device capable of continuous shooting throughout the day. However, its shooting endurance is only 30 minutes, and its storage capacity is only 12GB. Therefore, from a shooting perspective, the functionality of Google Glass is not even as practical as my 64GB iPhone 5 in my pocket.Thad Starner, a pioneer in wearable devices and professor at Georgia Institute of Technology, has been actively involved in the Google Glass project since its inception. In his view, the surveillance capabilities of Google Glass are even less effective than today's smartphones. Starner revealed that privacy issues were one of the key focuses of the design team from the beginning of Google Glass's design, and we intentionally added some social cue features, such as the screen lighting up while recording videos.From my personal trial experience, Starner and the Google design team's design has been fully successful, and I almost cannot take photos or record videos without attracting others' attention. Once, while talking to a Google employee, I tried to open the recording function as inconspicuously as possible, but the other party immediately noticed my suddenly lit-up screen and smiled at me saying, "I know what you're doing."Existing ThreatsHowever, it is not entirely impossible to turn Google Glass into a full-fledged voyeuristic device. In the past two weeks, hackers around the world have been trying to crack this device. Legendary iOS hacker and jailbreak developer Jay Freeman previously announced on Twitter that he had successfully gained root access to Google Glass, achieving a jailbreak of the device. Another geek also successfully rooted (rooting or jailbreaking removes any restrictions set by Google) this device and successfully modified the setting that forces the screen to light up during video recording.Moreover, some app developers have created a blink-to-take-a-photo application specifically for Google Glass. The Atlantic Wire even referred to it as "a peeping Tom's dream app."In fact, the existence of these hackers is precisely the reason why Google chose to launch a large-scale Explorer program instead of directly putting Google Glass on the shelves of Best Buy. Because Google hopes to understand the vulnerabilities of this product through the release of the Explorer version of Google Glass and block them in time before the official release. Unfortunately, despite Google's great efforts, the outside world seems unwilling to accept this new concept device, and some merchants and state governments have explicitly banned the use of this device in specific occasions or situations.We can be sure that when we find someone using Google Glass, we will pay special attention to any clues that may suggest its use, such as sudden eyebrow movements (just like how we are very cautious about someone pointing a camera phone at us nowadays). Meanwhile, we also need to realize that the greatest threat to personal privacy is more likely to come from devices like button cameras, but absolutely not from Google Glass that stays on our faces all day.
