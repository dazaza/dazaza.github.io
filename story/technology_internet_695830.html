Cervical cysts are one of the pathological manifestations of chronic cervicitis. During the healing process of inflammation, newly formed squamous epithelium covers the cervical gland orifice or extends into the gland, blocking the gland orifice. The surrounding connective tissue of the gland proliferates or forms scars, compressing the gland, making it narrower or even blocked. The drainage of glandular secretions is obstructed, leading to retention and the formation of cysts. The size of these cysts varies, generally small and scattered. They can appear individually or multiple at once, occurring in any part of the cervix. In gynecological examinations, only cysts located at the external os of the cervix can be found using a speculum, while B-ultrasound can display cysts in all parts of the cervix, with a detection rate as high as 100%. Therefore, B-ultrasound examination improves clinical diagnostic accuracy and also provides convenience for clinical treatment.

Are uterine cysts serious--uterine cysts affect conception

The degree to which uterine fibroids affect fertility depends on the location, size, and number of fibroids. Broadly speaking, there are three situations:

First, fibroids develop towards the surface of the uterus, protruding from the surface, known as subserosal uterine fibroids, often connected to the surface of the uterus by a stalk. These fibroids have less impact on conception and are easier to surgically remove, but when they grow to a certain size, they can affect the smooth delivery of labor.

Second, fibroids develop towards the uterine cavity, protruding inside the uterine cavity, sometimes even outside the cervical os, discoverable in the vagina. These fibroids are called submucosal fibroids and cause many symptoms, commonly increased menstruation and foul-smelling vaginal discharge. When submucosal fibroids grow near the internal os of the fallopian tube or in the upper half of the uterine cavity, they often affect sperm passage, preventing implantation and development of fertilized eggs. These fibroids can be diagnosed through vaginal examination, diagnostic curettage, hysterosalpingography, or B-ultrasound. All confirmed submucosal fibroids should be surgically removed. Generally, abdominal surgery is not required, and the tumor can be removed through the vagina via the cervical canal.

Third, fibroids are confined within the uterine muscle wall, known as intramural fibroids, accounting for about 60-70% of fibroids. They not only affect conception but also often cause miscarriages or premature births. Usually, these three types of fibroids grow mixed together as multiple fibroids or coexist, rarely being solitary.
