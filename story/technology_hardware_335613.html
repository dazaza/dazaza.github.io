Reasons for the non-drying of stone water stains, prevention and control

by bjksstone on 2010-03-29 13:47:49

The traditional wet installation process for stone is simple, convenient to operate, and low in cost. It is still widely used in stone installation projects currently. However, it also brings about many post-installation issues related to stone damage, the most obvious being the phenomenon of stones not drying over a long period or leaving water stains after drying. Especially with light-colored stones, various shapes and sizes of water stains, some wet, some dry, some dark, some light, stubbornly dominate the surface of buildings, making them extremely unsightly. This phenomenon is known as "non-drying water spots (stains)." This issue continues to trouble many property owners, construction companies, stone suppliers, and stone production plants, among other relevant units and departments.

In this article, we will analyze the cases of water spots (stains) we have encountered in our work, along with their treatment methods, for your reference.

### I. Reasons for Non-Drying Water Spots (Stains)

1. **Different Stone Varieties**: The occurrence of non-drying water spots (stains) is more common in white-light colored stones, especially granite stones. Taking gray-white granite as an example; its characteristics include being a medium-acidic igneous rock, having a relatively high degree of crystallization, abundant micro-cracks between crystals, a relatively high water absorption rate, and a higher content of various metallic minerals. Based on these features, during wet installation, the amount of alkaline substances from cement carried into the stone by moisture is relatively larger, easily reacting chemically with metallic minerals in the stone. This generates alumina and salt-based so-called water crystal bodies that exist between the cleavage planes and crystal cracks of the rock. In normal circumstances, these are hard to evaporate and dry, causing the stone to remain in a water-soaked state for a long time.

2. **Improper Use or Selection of Protective Agents**: There are many types of protective agents available on the market. Different stones, different usage areas, and different installation methods require the selection of different protective agents, a point often overlooked. More importantly, the reasonable choice of construction methods and dosage is crucial. We frequently discover that in the same building area, even when using the same stone (sometimes even the same large slab), the same cement, and sand under similar construction conditions, different non-drying water spot (stain) phenomena appear, i.e., some stones are dry while others are wet. This situation is basically due to the use of stone protective agents without strictly following the usage requirements, resulting in significant differences in the dosage and uniformity of application of the protective agent. Stones that meet the required mixing ratio and are evenly brushed dry quickly. Conversely, they are prone to exhibit non-drying water spot (stain) phenomena.

3. **Choice and Control of Cement, Sand, and Water Usage During Stone Installation**: The selection of cement and sand and the control of water usage during stone installation have a significant impact on water spots and stone yellowing. We once encountered the use of white cement, white sand, and the installation of light-colored stones, which resulted in large-scale occurrences of water spots and stone yellowing. After multiple treatments, the original condition was basically restored. During construction, the alkali content, manganese content, iron content of cement, soil content (impurity content) of sand, and the amount of water should all be strictly controlled. If not, the stones will inevitably suffer from varying degrees of soaking pollution.

### II. Treatment Methods for Non-Drying Water Spots (Stains)

First, it's necessary to determine the cause of the non-drying water spots (stains). If there is a lot of water beneath the stone (such as: after installation, using a cutter with water to cut the ground; the ground being saturated with water before installation; or a large amount of water seeping through the joints after installation), the cement layer that should be dry becomes water-saturated. For such floors, a wind-drying method should be adopted, or natural drying over a longer period should be allowed to let the water in the cement layer evaporate before treating the stone itself. If chemical cleaning agents are blindly used at this time, it will often worsen the soaking phenomenon. The reason is simple: cleaning requires water, but water cannot wash away water. Evaporation is the only reasonable solution. Some people use heating methods. If the hot air blowing method is used, it can work, but it shouldn't be applied extensively. Using fire or lamps to roast the stones is ineffective, as these methods can quickly raise the surface temperature of the stone but cannot sustainably increase the temperature of the cement layer. The effect on water evaporation won't be noticeable, and the negative effects are significant. Rapid heating can cause the stone to expand, leading to delamination from the cement layer, creating hollow drums. Sometimes, it may also cause the stone to warp, producing shear mouths, with uncontrollable consequences. Therefore, we do not recommend this method.

Based on our experience in treatment, for related pollution of light-colored granite stones, after confirming (through investigation and detailed analysis) that the cement layer is not water-saturated, an appropriate granite cleaner can be selected, mixed at a 1:1 ratio with CHJ marble deep cleaner, and then diluted with water at a 1:1 ratio. A heavy-duty scrubber machine can be used for comprehensive cleaning, allowing the chemical solution to enter the stone to decompose water crystals and metal salt crystals in the crystal fissures and cleavage planes. The stone should be cleaned with clear water within half an hour to two hours. Two points need attention: first, too much chemical solution should not seep into the seams of the stone (if the amount is small, it can be ignored) to avoid damaging the cement layer; second, after washing with clear water, it must be dried promptly, avoiding water seeping into the seams of the stone, thereby increasing the water content of the cement layer. The principle behind this method is straightforward: while destroying water crystals and metal salts in the crystal fissures and cleavage planes of the stone, micro-explosions occur, opening up the stone's ventilation channels, accelerating water evaporation. The stone dries because the water evaporates, not because it is washed dry. This method generally shows further surface effects within a few hours. Within 3-7 days, there will be noticeable effects. If there are effects but not completely eliminated, two aspects need further analysis: one, is the water content in the cement layer too high? Two, is the amount of chemical solution insufficient? A second treatment may be necessary.

### III. Suggestions for Preventing Stone Water Spot (Stain) Pollution

1. **Carefully Select Protective Agents**: We recommend using CHJ silicone water-based protective agents and adding CHJ stone bottom and side protective agents. If possible, use these two protective agents to treat the bottom and sides of the stone, leaving the front untreated until the stone is completely dry and clean, then treat the front with CHJ8765 composite anti-contaminant. At the same time, adhere to the principle: if you're going to protect the stone, carefully follow the operation and usage methods. Otherwise, it’s better not to do it at all.

2. **Select Good Cement and Sand**: Preferably choose well-known products from large cement manufacturers (small cement factories often produce products with uncontrolled impurities); try to choose Portland cement. If sulfur-aluminumate cement (white cement) must be used, choose premium white cement, as only this grade has controlled manganese and iron content. Sand: preferentially choose water-washed sand. Some directly mined sands have unimaginably high impurity contents, and the pigments and impurities they release when soaked in water will certainly be brought into the stone and pollute it.

3. **Strictly Control Water Usage**: Wet installation of stone is actually semi-dry installation. In some installations, during the final slurry application, no control is exercised, turning what should be semi-dry cement mortar into wet mortar. Even worse, some installations involve direct watering at the end, which is unacceptable. Excessive water poses a potential risk of contaminating the stone. It is recommended to adopt the stone bottom slurry application method, i.e., using a toothed scraper to apply slurry, which can maintain a fixed amount of slurry application (beneficial for flatness), reduce water content, reduce the reasons for stone contamination, and improve decoration quality.
