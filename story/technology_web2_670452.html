A child's language development largely depends on the family environment, which includes the cultural refinement of parents (or other family members), the family's book collection, and the parents' interest in their children's education. We usually can judge a person's family background and his own social status through his language. The level of language itself reflects one's cultural refinement.

Cultured parents often skillfully use various opportunities to educate their children whenever possible. Once they find their children using wrong or incorrect words, they will correct them in time. And when the children use words appropriately and accurately, they give encouragement immediately, motivating the children's learning enthusiasm. They are not only teaching the children a certain language, but also developing their intelligence during language training.

Some parents, although concerned about their children's early education, spend a lot of money buying many toys, even electronic pianos and pianos, but do not pay attention to their children's language development. When the child talks to him, he often seems impatient, just simply dismissing the child with a few words. Facts have proven that children who lack substantial contact with adults not only suffer damage to their verbal abilities, but it also directly affects the development of their thinking ability.

Of course, in the process of early education, we must prevent the tendency of over-anxiety and haste for success.

Even prodigies need to follow the laws of physical and mental development of young children. Over-anxiety, can only lead to counterproductive results.

The method used by Witt's father to educate the "prodigy" little Witt was to stretch out his finger in front of little Witt and at the same time utter a clear sound: "finger, finger." This includes listening and watching, i.e., auditory and visual training. Perhaps some people might be surprised, do these innate abilities of listening and watching need training? The answer is obviously yes.

We repeatedly present a certain object to the infant while clearly uttering the name of this object according to Pavlov's principle of conditioned reflex formation. Over time, the infant associates this sound with the object in front of him, naturally realizing that everything in the world has its specific name. For an infant, this is no different from the arrival of a new era. He will eagerly recognize new things and learn new nouns, transforming passive education into active learning for the baby.

Teaching children to talk is indeed a very arduous task. But as long as we understand the importance of language training in early education, we can persevere. The reason why humans are superior to other animals and have achieved today's success is because humans use a language that other animals do not possess, thereby promoting the development of human intelligence. Based on this principle, it is more necessary to teach children the unique tool of human society - language.

From a certain point of view, "listening" and "watching" create a favorable environment for the child's future speech. More importantly and more difficultly is the child's speech.

Generally, in the initial stage of learning to speak, due to inaccurate pronunciation, children tend to make mistakes, such as confusing velar sounds and apical sounds, easily pronouncing "brother" as "di di".

It is not surprising or frightening for children to make pronunciation errors. What is important is to correct the errors in time once they are found; otherwise, once they become fixed, it will be more troublesome to correct them.

Another point worth emphasizing is that adults should not intentionally reinforce the child's baby talk. Baby talk is a necessary stage in the development of children's language, which is beyond reproach. But some parents, for fun, intentionally reinforce the child's baby talk, which is not commendable. Because baby talk itself does not conform to language norms, may not be understood, and has great limitations. If you constantly reinforce the child's baby talk, it will inevitably affect the child's communication skills and intellectual development. In fact, talking with children requires a bit of artistry, both being acceptable to children and lively and interesting, conforming to language norms.

Below are several specific methods for oral language training:

① Situational conversation method

In family education, situational conversations do not require specially setting up a situation; it is sufficient to adapt to local conditions.

Initially, when teaching children to speak, it is advisable to present objects that are simple in shape, bright in color, simple in pronunciation, and frequently encountered by children for them to observe, while simultaneously uttering the standard name of the object. At this time, you can also hold the child and look at each item in the room, tell him the names of foods on the dining table, or even take him outside to see flowers, grass, the sky, stars, and the sun, etc. Such situations can be seen everywhere in daily life, and as long as you are mindful, they are not hard to discover. As the child grows slightly older, situational conversations no longer need to be a solo act by you. While feeding the child, you can also train the child's speaking ability. You can ask the child the names of various foods and let the child say what food he wants himself, instead of doing everything for him.

When the child initially learns to speak, you can play games similar to "playing house" with the child (it is better if there are several children participating). At this time, you can let the child play roles such as doctor, salesperson, teacher, or even police officer. The lines can be prepared in advance, or better yet, let the child improvise. The task of the parent at this time is to inspire, urge, and correct errors. This vivid and concrete game will greatly interest the child, and is very beneficial for cultivating the child's oral expression ability, enriching vocabulary, stimulating imagination, and fostering memory ability.

② Children's language games

a. Tongue twisters. Tongue twisters were called eating speech poems in ancient times. They are usually composed of phonetically similar and easily confused characters, words, and sentences. They need to be spoken accurately, fluently, without mispronunciations, off-key, and with a certain speed requirement. Since the content is generally simple and easy to understand, they are very popular among children. Parents can selectively teach tongue twisters based on actual circumstances. Initially, parents should clearly read the tongue twister once, and some key words can be separately followed by the child for several readings. Then the child can follow along until he can correctly and fluently recite it. In practice, reading tongue twisters fluently and accurately, with proper rhythm, is indeed not easy. If some parents themselves do not have good language skills, then the child can learn from the radio, like the children's programs on the radio often have this segment. This is not only beneficial for cultivating the child's oral language ability, but also helps to exercise the child's agility and clarity of thought, and of course benefits memory training.

b. Word-finding game. The word-finding game is a text game where related words are found based on a central character. For example, finding the character "electricity," you can find many words related to "electricity," such as "light bulb," "electric fan," "television," "refrigerator," etc. This is extremely beneficial for enriching the child's vocabulary and cultivating divergent thinking ability. At the same time, parents can use this game to check the child's thinking ability and the amount of vocabulary they have mastered.

This game is crucial to choosing a good center. This character must be relatively familiar to the child and have significant combinability. Do not choose those relatively obscure, unfamiliar, and hard-to-understand characters.

c. Character connection game. The character connection game requires all words to be connected sequentially to form a string of words. For example, after "light bulb," you can connect "bubble," "bubble gum," "candy," "fruit," etc.

Note that when parents play this game with the child, try to think of words whose last character is easy to connect, helping the child smoothly continue the game.

This character connection game not only develops the child's intelligence but also cultivates the child's oral language ability. The game itself requires mastering a certain amount of vocabulary, and parents can consciously teach the child some new words and characters during the game, helping the child consolidate existing vocabulary.

③ Storytelling

For young children, nothing is more important than stories, because the child is a newcomer to this world, and this world is an unknown world to him. Many outstanding literary figures, such as Tolstoy, Goethe, Lu Xun, Guo Moruo, have had an inseparable relationship with stories since childhood. Practice has also shown that stories play a very important role in the child's perception, attention, memory, thinking, imagination, and psychological activities, as well as in the formation of the child's personality traits. China has traditionally used storytelling to educate children on moral qualities. Some people liken stories to the gateway for children to understand the world, an effective agent to enhance memory, and wings for imagination. Similarly, stories are also a "stimulant" for the development of children's language. This is because stories themselves have intricate plots and vivid, interesting, and rich languages, which children are very willing to understand and accept.

Fairy tales, especially Andersen's and Grimm's fairy tales, have quite exquisite and beautiful languages. Not only do they appeal to children, but they also provide a sense of beauty for adults.

When children tell stories, they are often very engaged, with countless "whys," so storytelling is also an important means to induce children to ask questions and stimulate their desire for knowledge.

Moreover, stories themselves teach children how to distinguish between good and evil, beauty and ugliness, which is beneficial for forming their good personality traits. Sometimes combining storytelling with educating children can yield unexpected results.

In the process of storytelling, children are often listeners. We might as well let the children actively participate. After a story is told, let the child retell it to the parents, or the parents start the story and let the child continue. Even if it is illogical, it doesn't matter much, because the child's thoughts are least constrained, often coming up with imaginative ideas, which is a very precious spirit and should be affirmed and encouraged by adults. Of course, parents should also guide the child to make the story as logical as possible, paying particular attention to whether the child's line of thought is clear and whether the expression is accurate and fluent. Imaginative thinking does not equal disordered thinking; the former is a creative mental activity, and the latter is a confusion of thought, which cannot be equated.

Picture books have their own unique advantages, which can be repeatedly viewed and read, being cost-effective and popular among children.

Facing a static picture, the richer the explanation provided by the parents, the richer the child's imagination will be. Reflecting on it, isn't much of our own knowledge derived from pictures?

As the child's language ability improves, the child can be encouraged to describe pictures. A single picture or a set of pictures can be turned into a story, which is highly attractive to the child. Sometimes, the child can be allowed to arrange the pictures themselves. When the child changes the order of several pictures, perhaps the storyline changes as well.

Besides pictures, the best material for describing pictures is comic strips. Comic strips themselves have complete storylines, and children can understand the general storyline by looking at the pictures. Often, children flip through comic strips while muttering to themselves, making up the storyline. Gradually, they no longer satisfy with just looking at the pictures but develop an interest in the text below the pictures. Thus, describing pictures can transition into recognizing words in pictures.

Generally, 3-5 years old is considered the critical age for children to start learning written language.

When the child's oral language ability develops to a certain extent, he no longer satisfies with mere spoken language. On the street, it is common to see 4-5-year-old children pointing at signs and asking adults, "What is this?" Some children even bring a comic book and ask adults to read it to them. This can be considered the budding desire for the child to recognize characters, and parents should seize the opportunity for early education. From the data, intellectually gifted children generally recognize characters earlier, not only recognizing characters but also writing excellent articles, which is admirable.

Language can be said to be the tool for acquiring knowledge and achieving success, and written language is an indispensable tool for children to independently acquire knowledge. Therefore, this tool should be imparted to children as early as possible.

Reading to this point, some parents may have such doubts: Is recognizing and writing characters before school beneficial for the child's development?

The answer is definitely yes. First, from the perspective of the critical period, 4-5 years old is the critical period for the development of written language ability, so developing written language ability is imperative.

Secondly, from the child's psychological development, the intellectual development of preschool children has reached a considerable level, and oral language is already very rich. Learning written language has become a necessity for the child's own development.

At what age to start recognizing and writing characters depends on the specific situation and cannot be generalized. One thing to keep in mind is: follow the natural flow and avoid forced education. Following the natural flow does not mean letting go completely. Children always like scribbling, and when he fills the paper with symbols that have no meaning, it may be the beginning of his learning to write. Understanding some characteristics of Chinese character creation can effectively cultivate the child's interest in writing. Chinese characters are pictographic characters, having gone through oracle bone script, bronze inscriptions, large seal script, clerical script, to today's regular script. Although today's regular script is no longer so "pictographic," with a little processing, the "shape" of Chinese characters becomes apparent, making the dry text come alive, greatly enhancing the child's learning enthusiasm.

However, one point to note is that the learning burden for children, especially preschool children, should not be too heavy, and the writing time should not be too long. This is because the bones and muscles of children have not developed fully, making them prone to fatigue, which can affect their health. Excessive burdens can cause the child to dislike studying, leading to adverse consequences.

① Picture-based character recognition to cultivate the child's written language ability

When the child learns more and more oral language from picture-based speaking, his attention will inevitably shift to the square characters. At this point, it is timely to transition from picture-based speaking to picture-based character recognition.

The character recognition teaching mentioned here is aimed at infants and toddlers, thus falling within the scope of family education. Should there be a unified syllabus for when the child starts recognizing characters and how many characters they should recognize? This should depend on the individual's situation, and the idea of more being better is not advisable.

Character recognition teaching, if not done with some thought, might be very dull and fail to capture the child's interest, making the effect predictable. It would be best if the character recognition could be combined with stories that children enjoy hearing. Currently, most children in kindergartens recognize characters like "Mickey Mouse," "Donald Duck," "Snoopy," etc. These characters are strange to the child, but they recognize them from TV and associate them with those lovable images, firmly remembering them.

Parents can pick out some commonly used characters (those closely related to the child's daily life) and focus on teaching them to the child. When seeing these characters in daily life, they can also associate them with words in stories. For instance, when the child sees the word "duck" on a market blackboard, the parents can ask the child where else they have seen this "duck" character. The child might say "Donald Duck's duck," indicating that the child has learned to transfer the character.

Generally, some children can recognize characters but may not necessarily be able to write or understand their meanings. The latter requirement is higher and is generally gradually achieved in elementary school. If the child's intellectual development is good, these contents can also be taught, but note that explaining word meanings is best done during reading, i.e., putting the characters and words in context for the child to comprehend. This makes it easier for the child to accept and also cultivates the child's reading ability. Moreover, Chinese characters often have multiple meanings, and only by organically combining characters, words, and texts can the child's language ability be effectively cultivated.

The traditional method of picture-based character recognition uses cards, with a picture on one side and the character (or pinyin) on the other. Practice has proven this to be an effective teaching method. When parents use it, they can enhance it further to make it more vivid and interesting, making it more appealing to children.

Someone addressed the monotony of card-based character recognition by appropriately combining the cards, compiling some characters (cards) into stories, teaching character recognition while telling the stories, with good results.

For example, if you have a set of animal character cards, you might as well compile an animal fairy tale. When telling the story, show the corresponding animal card when mentioning a certain animal, teaching the child to recognize the character on the card. This way, the child views character recognition as a game activity, not feeling any burden but rather finding it very interesting. After hearing the story, the child will repeatedly read the cards. Over time, the child can recognize characters based on the pictures themselves and even create their own stories. This not only develops the child's language ability but also exercises their thinking, imagination, and memory.

② Transitioning from concentrated character recognition to cultivating reading ability

For young children, especially those under 3 years old, recognizing characters is often a process of forming a conditioned reflex involving sound and form. Therefore, in training, the listening, watching, reading, and writing aspects should be focused on. Of course, staying at this level is far from enough; on top of listening, watching, reading, and writing, understanding is also required.

There are six methods for creating Chinese characters, apart from pictographs, there are also ideograms, phonetic compounds, associative compounds, rebus, and loan characters. Among these, pictographs, ideograms, and associative compounds are more common. For example, the most typical "ideogram" characters are "up" and "down." The most typical "associative compound" character is "seedling." Isn't something growing in the field like grass a seedling?

If these knowledge can be applied to character recognition teaching, the child's understanding of Chinese characters can be much deeper.

Someone once said, recognizing characters cannot be separated from reading, and this statement is entirely correct. Initially, children can purely recognize characters, but when accumulated to a certain amount, they should be encouraged to read. Recognizing characters is not the goal; reading is the goal. In the reading process, many new words and characters can be recognized. It is best to teach the child to use a dictionary as soon as possible. Once they master this reference book, the child takes control of their own learning.

The methods for cultivating young children's language ability should be varied. Parents should conduct language training based on the child's characteristics and interests, anytime, anywhere, in diverse forms, even utilizing opportunities such as going shopping, grocery shopping, and visiting parks.

Everyone understands this principle: learning a language cannot be separated from the word "diligence." Truly "not putting down the fists, not leaving the mouth," if one can achieve listening more, watching more (recognizing characters), reading more (reading), and writing more (writing articles), the child's language ability will surely improve.
