Cai Chunzhu's Weibo appeal for attention to autism, known as the father of autism

by edivista on 2011-12-05 16:04:44

Click to enter Cai Chunzhu's Weibo

In February this year, Cai Chunzhu's son, Xi He, was diagnosed with autism. After crying, Cai Chunzhu named his Weibo account "Dad Loves Xi He" and began documenting Xi He's life on Sina Weibo in a humorous tone and slightly exaggerated writing style. In just a few months, the optimistic and joke-loving image of Xi He's dad touched many netizens, and his Weibo followers exceeded 80,000. In July this year, a book titled "Dad Loves Xi He" was published. Readers read through tears, slowly learning about the autistic community from the words. Parents of children with autism also found strength in the book. Cai Chunzhu is called the "Father of Autism." (Click to enter Cai Chunzhu's Weibo)

Whether it was a bolt from the blue or fate's randomness, Cai Chunzhu got hit. In February this year, his 2-year-old and 6-day-old son, Xi He, was diagnosed with autism.

Cai Chunzhu said that his mood at the time was very gloomy. So much so that later when he and his wife went to a restaurant for dinner and ordered a spicy dish, they picked up their chopsticks but hadn't even started eating when they looked at each other and burst into tears. The staff at the restaurant ran over to apologize and asked if the dish was too spicy?

This is how Cai Chunzhu speaks and writes, just like his curly hair—winding around until he draws you into his jokes. But this 38-year-old man from Hunan who claims that nine out of ten things he says are jokes carefully avoided the shadow of autism, using somewhat teasing words to show his love for his son. He is documenting, as well as narrating—documenting Xi He and narrating the autistic community.

Humorous posts about his autistic son gain attention

Driving on the Fourth Ring Road, my vision became blurry. When the doctor said the three words "autism," I knew my days of reckless behavior were over. My son is 2 years old and 6 days, diagnosed with autism. On the way home, the sound of the traffic on the Fourth Ring Road couldn’t drown out my cries. This was Cai Chunzhu's first Weibo post after learning that his son had autism. Afterwards, he continued to document Xi He's life in humorous language and details, accumulating more than a thousand posts.

In May this year, Cai Chunzhu wrote a letter of several thousand words addressed to Xi He at the age of 18. My son, I can imagine your reaction upon receiving this letter—you would tear open the envelope, pull out the letter, then rip it into strips, put them in your mouth, and swallow them down. I think there are three reasons for this: one, the content of the letter made you angry; two, you don't recognize characters; three, you have autism, tearing paper is one of your characteristics. I don't know which applies to you, looking forward to your reply. When he posted the "Letter to My Son" on his blog, almost overnight, the letter was viewed more than 100,000 times and forwarded and commented on tens of thousands of times.

This letter was an eruption, leading to a surge in people paying attention to Cai Chunzhu and Xi He. Each Weibo post about Xi He almost always received over a hundred comments. To date, his Weibo has more than 80,000 followers.

Netizen Cai Jingzhong George left a comment saying that Cai Chunzhu's Weibo posts helped everyone recognize and pay attention to this group. Even now, some local institutions still do not consider autism something that needs treatment and support. Many netizens also said that initially, they opened Cai Chunzhu's Weibo with a light-hearted and playful attitude, but soon found themselves in tears.

In July, Cai Chunzhu published a book titled "Dad Loves Xi He," which went through six reprints and sold more than 60,000 copies. Most of those who bought "Dad Loves Xi He" were parents. They mostly felt that this book was inspiring—a father who had suffered setbacks could still be so optimistic.

Unexpectedly becoming the "Father of Autism"

Even though he has a child with autism, life goes on as usual. Sleeping until noon, eating, then sending Xi He to nearby autism institutions for schooling. In the afternoon, Cai Chunzhu likes to stay in a nearby coffee shop, typing on his computer or playing games. All the Weibo posts and books related to Xi He were written in the coffee shop. In the evening, he picks Xi He up and plays with his son.

Over time, Cai Chunzhu discovered that every day, netizens would ask him whether their own children might also have autism. "I'm not a doctor, I don't have the qualifications to diagnose, I can only tell them what I know." Cai Chunzhu said that no less than a hundred people, after seeing his Weibo, realized that their children might have autism. A couple of days ago, a netizen told him that originally, they hated him because after reading "Dad Loves Xi He," they discovered that their daughter also had autism. Now, they thank him because the book allowed them to take their daughter for treatment early. It is precisely for this reason that he was called the "Father of Autism" by netizens.

"I won't say I've done something immeasurably good, but at least it's a good thing—it helps them discover their child's issues early. The earlier autism is intervened, the better. Auxiliary methods are quite effective." Cai Chunzhu said that after several months of early intervention, Xi He made great progress—he started speaking, although always shouting "bus." Many autistic patients never speak throughout their lives.

Hope for more people to understand autism

When talking about the public's attitude towards the autistic community, Cai Chunzhu straightened his body sitting on the sofa, his tone becoming somewhat low, accompanied by occasional sighs—he finally became serious.

He admitted that before his son had autism, he wouldn't have paid attention to autism. If one must experience it personally to empathize with others, the cost is too high. Therefore, he hopes that posting on Weibo and writing books will enable more people to understand, accept, and help autistic individuals.

In fact, before Cai Chunzhu, many parents of autistic children wrote daily about autism-related topics, but they didn't attract much attention. Forced indoctrination cannot attract people’s attention, so I changed the tone and made it more fun to write. Cai Chunzhu said that only by arousing others' interest can they learn about autism.

At least 50% of people who have seen my Weibo now know about autism. Only by knowing can we talk about acceptance and make sense of it, he said.

After the interview ended, Cai Chunzhu walked out of the coffee shop. The evening belongs to Xi He.

[Reporter's Question]

Some people say you're making money off your son, what do you think about that?

Cai Chunzhu: Everyone says raising children is for old-age security, isn't it fine if I start early? If my son doesn't have this ability in the future, I'll earn money for him first. Why not? Honestly, how much money can this make? So many parents of autistic children, who wouldn't want to write a book? They really want to speak out, full of bitterness but don't know how to express it. I have a pen, and I've written it out—that's all.

The first time my son called me "Dad" was toward the wall. Later, he called "Dad" toward the window, bananas, and light bulbs, but never once toward me.

I used to have many expectations for my son, like all parents hoping their children would become successful. I no longer expect him to become a Nobel Prize winner, archaeologist, or adventurer. If in the next year, he can consciously call me "Dad," this will be the greatest happiness of my life.

Excerpted from Dad Loves Xi He Weibo

[Words of Virtue]

I record my son's daily life on Weibo every day, not to beautify myself—I indeed use this platform to let the general public recognize and understand autism, thus understanding and being tolerant towards these children. China has the tradition of not airing dirty laundry in public, but I don't think this is dirty laundry, just misfortune. I hope more people will pay attention to this group. There are 1.5 million autistic patients in China who are longing for societal attention.

Cai Chunzhu
