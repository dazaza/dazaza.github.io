Reported by Zhuo Min in Silicon Valley - "Thirteen months ago, China surpassed Japan to become the world's second-largest economy. We have always paid great attention to China, but we were struggling with how to find specific and effective ways to connect." Recently, Leslie Yuen, the newly appointed executive director of the Huayuan Science and Technology Association, the largest and most influential Chinese science and technology association in Silicon Valley, revealed a sense of surprise: "Huayuan established the China Cooperation Department last year, and this year will increase efforts in this area to better leverage the smooth flow of value between Silicon Valley and China."

The key elements of entrepreneurship in Silicon Valley include: allowing failure, a wealth of good ideas, support from venture capitalists, a group of excellent entrepreneurial mentors, serial entrepreneurship, and a DIY spirit at heart. However, non-profit organizations of varying sizes are the most active cells responsible for spreading new technologies locally in Silicon Valley, promoting new products to the market, providing financing and investment channels, and seeking business cooperation opportunities. For example, the first spark of interaction between Jack Ma (Weibo) and Jerry Yang in 2005 originated from a beach trip organized by Huayuan. In fact, not only did it ignite the collaboration between Yahoo and Alibaba, but many ordinary members of the Huayuan Science and Technology Association also found venture capital for their startups within this organization.

Huayuan has more than ten years of history and currently has over 6,000 members, with tens of thousands having come and gone throughout its history. It was founded by the first batch of overseas Chinese tech elites, aiming to give back to society and build a platform for overseas Chinese entrepreneurs. Now, members such as Chen Hong, Zhu Min, and Deng Feng are well-known in both Chinese and American business circles. However, while benefiting others over the years, this Chinese organization is also encountering historical challenges.

"In recent years, we have had a large number of Chinese members return to China to start businesses. Huayuan also hopes to better organize them and cooperate with local governments and social forces in China," said Leslie. As a veteran builder of bridges between China and the United States, the Huayuan Science and Technology Association hopes to further enhance its influence globally, especially influencing and assisting mainland China beyond just American Chinese.

According to Leslie, the newly established China Cooperation Department of Huayuan consists of two parts: "Out of China" and "Into China." The former is responsible for receiving local governments and companies from mainland China, such as assisting Chinese enterprises recruiting talent in Silicon Valley; the latter supports Chinese returning from Silicon Valley to start businesses. In 2011, Huayuan organized two delegations of Silicon Valley entrepreneurs eager to return to China to visit places like Changsha. One delegation consisted of 20 people, and finally, 10 members of the delegation decided to stay and start businesses in China, achieving a success rate of 50%.

Huayuan has already established volunteer organizations in Beijing, Shanghai, Xiamen, and other places. In late March, a delegation organized by Huayuan will join the Stanford University president to visit Shenzhen and participate in an enterprise exchange meeting hosted by the Shenzhen government; in May, another delegation will visit Beijing to conduct cooperative exchanges with local enterprises.

The management teams of Huayuan over the years have all been outstanding Chinese entrepreneurs in Silicon Valley. Currently, Huayuan has completed the formation of the 2012 management team and organizational structure adjustments. The president is Ken Xie, the founder, president, and CEO of network security company Fortinet (NASDAQ: FTNT). The vice presidents are David Chao, co-founder and managing director of top venture capital firm DCM (Doll Capital Management), and Wei Zhou, president and CEO of Shengjie Technology. Leslie Yuen, vice president and executive director of outsourcing software company Wenshithink, is responsible for injecting passion, vitality, and professional spirit into Huayuan’s famous projects. This is also the first full-time executive director in Huayuan's history.

In addition, similar to the way American tech companies actively provide event support to local non-profit organizations in Silicon Valley to increase their own activity levels, since last year, there has been a significant increase in the number of sponsors from Chinese tech companies in Silicon Valley. "In the past, about 10-15% of our association's sponsorship came from Chinese tech companies, but after the establishment of the China Cooperation Department last year, this proportion reached 40%," Leslie estimated: "This year, it could reach 50%."
