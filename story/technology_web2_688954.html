Netizens expose the worst year-end bonus: male employee draws sanitary napkins in a lottery

by zxzzzi899 on 2012-03-03 16:35:02

At the end of each year, year-end bonuses become a hot topic among white collars. This year, due to two special reasons, this topic has attracted more attention than usual: the global economy is beginning to recover and the CPI (Consumer Price Index) is continuously rising.

Recently, posts about year-end bonuses on websites like Xinhua have been very popular. Yesterday, an investigation conducted by our newspaper's "First Glimpse" website and QQ800966988 found that white collars lack confidence in their year-end bonuses keeping up with the CPI, with nearly 70% of netizens believing there will be no increase.

■ Hosted by reporter Cheng Hongchuan

If it doesn't increase, it's effectively decreasing

"Commodity prices are rising but wages aren't. The same amount of money has less and less purchasing power. Doesn't this mean the money we receive is effectively decreasing?" said netizen "Tianma Xingkong," who works in administration at an IT company. If the year-end bonus doesn't rise in line with inflation, then it means the year-end bonus is effectively decreasing. Many netizens also expressed that it would be reasonable for the year-end bonus to rise with inflation.

Will year-end bonuses rise with inflation? Yesterday, a questionnaire survey was conducted among employees of over 80 enterprises in real estate, internet, finance, automobile, culture, and other industries. In response to the question "How do you think this year's year-end bonus will be?", only 30.5% of respondents said "possibly more than last year", while 45.7% thought "about the same as last year."

Netizen "Jietou Mishu," who works in manufacturing, predicted that this year's year-end bonus would be the same as last year's. "For example, our company's year-end bonus was 4000 yuan during the 2008 financial crisis, and it was still 4000 yuan in 2009 when the economy improved. I estimate that this year, despite inflation, there won't be much change."

Half perform well, few get raises

In the enterprise survey, nearly half clearly stated "This year's performance has been very good, exceeding expectations." However, when discussing the year-end bonus given to employees, over half of the enterprise executives said "the same as last year," and fewer than 15% chose "more than last year."

In the survey, 59.26% of respondents attributed the reason for no year-end bonus or a reduced one to "bad market conditions, poor company business."

69% of respondents believed the consumer goods industry would perform well; 10% thought the media/PR/advertising industry would have outstanding performance; only 5.3% believed the banking and financial services sector would perform well.

The art of getting a year-end bonus

Everyone hopes for a generous year-end bonus after a hard year of work. But how can one obtain a larger year-end bonus?

Professor Chen Wenquan from Chongqing Administrative College's Department of Public Management suggested: first, don't be arrogant about your contributions; express them moderately; second, don't use family matters as excuses; highlight work achievements; third, don't compare yourself with others; conduct a reasonable self-assessment; fourth, avoid forming cliques; focus on improving oneself.

Professor Chen indicated that "strength in numbers" is a consistent Chinese mindset. When striving for a year-end bonus, many employees habitually gather a group of dissatisfied colleagues to confront the boss together. However, what company bosses dread most is employees forming cliques, which they see as a public challenge to their authority. In such cases, the boss may either give in reluctantly and settle accounts later, or throw in the towel and give nothing. Neither outcome benefits the employees.

The worst year-end bonus should be "nothing"

In yesterday's survey questionnaire, there was another interesting question: What's the worst year-end bonus you remember? Netizens' responses were equally amusing.

Netizen "Yiye Tingyu": Last year, the boss gave us gifts through a lottery at the year-end party. I drew several large packs of sanitary napkins. Poor me, I don't even have a girlfriend! Give it to female colleagues? They'd probably think I'm a pervert!

Netizen "Toufa Mei Luan": Two years ago, the company gave each employee a box of apples. The boss said it symbolized "peace and safety," but within a few days, the apples all rotted.

Netizen "Bosi Rencai": The worst festival benefit I ever received was a book bought from dangdang.com, and it had to cost no more than 30 yuan!

More netizens expressed that the worst year-end bonus should be getting nothing after working hard all year. Regardless of what kind of year-end bonus it is, having something is always better than having nothing.

Year-end bonuses abroad

United States: Not every company gives out year-end bonuses, and not every employee receives one. Year-end bonuses are usually given to middle-level and above employees. Moreover, top executives also receive other dividends such as stock options, which may far exceed their annual salaries.

Belgium: Blue-collar employees do not receive year-end dividends, while white-collar employees' year-end dividends are divided into two parts: collective dividends and individual dividends. Every employee receives collective dividends, while individual dividends are determined based on personal performance.

France: There is no regulation requiring civil servants to receive year-end bonuses. The majority of civil servants' year-end bonuses are equivalent to 8% of their annual salary (slightly less than one month's salary).

United Kingdom: The distribution of year-end bonuses varies greatly among companies. For example, MBA graduates from London Business School in 2004 who worked in consulting, finance, and industry received average year-end bonuses of approximately £11,000, £26,000, and £9,000 respectively in 2004. Additionally, there are significant differences based on region and position.
