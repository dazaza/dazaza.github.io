The Economist: Chinese returnees face some difficulties in finding jobs

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 16:19:14

The Economist reported that the importance of "sea turtles" in China has declined for many reasons. Sometimes companies hire returnee talents, but find that after studying abroad, they are not very familiar with the rapidly developing industry situation in China. They firmly believe in Western concepts and cannot manage local employees. The quality of returnee talents also varies greatly, and many real talents choose to stay abroad.

Following The Economist's article, let's take a look at the current situation of returnees in China:

Li Sanqi recalled: "I left China in 1980 with only $3 in my pocket." After the Cultural Revolution, he was among the first group of students allowed to study overseas. Like most successful people, his performance was outstanding. He advanced to a coveted position at the University of Texas and founded several tech companies. He returned to China to help establish world-class multinational enterprises, and now he is a senior executive at Huawei Technologies (information shows that he serves as the Chief Technology Officer for Huawei's carrier network business).

Li Sanqi is an outstanding representative of the "sea turtle" group. The term "sea turtle," meaning "returnee," refers to those who have studied abroad. For a long time, all sectors of Chinese society have praised the contributions of returnees in introducing advanced technology. In the past, these individuals had no trouble finding jobs in the job market and enjoyed generous compensation. Unfortunately, this golden era has passed. The "sea turtles" are no longer highly regarded, and the wage gap is gradually narrowing; some can't even find jobs. Some jokingly refer to them as "seaweed" now. Looking back at the contributions of the sea turtle group, this is a surprising change.

This year marks the 100th anniversary of China's "Overseas Alumni Association of Europe and America." Wang Huiyao, a member of the association, believes there have been five waves of returnees. The first wave occurred in the 19th century and included the builders of China's first railway and its first university president. The second and third waves happened before 1949, with many political leaders among them. In the 1950s, many people went to the Soviet Union for further studies, which constituted the fourth wave.

The fifth wave began in 1978 and is currently the largest scale return trend. Since then, about 2.6 million Chinese have gone abroad to study. In recent years, the number of Chinese students going abroad has expanded to 400,000 annually. Although most stay overseas, the 1.1 million returnees have already brought about changes in China. Wang Huiyao believes that the first three waves prompted revolutionary changes in China, the fourth wave promoted modernization, and the fifth wave promoted globalization.

The sea turtle group helped China's economy connect with the world. They founded heavyweight tech companies like Baidu, and many became senior executives in the Chinese branches of multinational corporations. They helped China connect with global commerce, politics, and popular culture.

So, why has their importance decreased? Several studies show that on average, it takes sea turtles longer to reach relatively lower positions, and the wage gap with local employees has become smaller. One reason is the weak employment market for new graduates. Another factor is that with the rise of China's domestic market and the continuous development of industries such as e-commerce, these developments make returnees who have spent years abroad unfamiliar with the current landscape. Gary Rieschel of Qiming Venture Capital said that ten years ago, investors only invested in those returning from Silicon Valley in the U.S., but now they support entrepreneurs who graduated from local universities, as they are more familiar with local consumer trends, online gaming habits, and social media platforms like Weibo.

With the prosperity and rapid growth of China's economy, local managers have started to shed their inferiority complex. A senior executive at Tencent, a Chinese social media company, said he still recruits sea turtles from foreign companies, but finds they have difficulty managing local engineers. A European investment banker pointed out that sea turtles often insist on Western concepts such as transparency, elite management, and ethics, which puts them at a disadvantage in China's super Darwinian economy. In China, what matters more is what the boss or client wants, and everyone strives to achieve their goals.

Even foreign companies operating in China have become more selective when hiring. Yannig Gourmelon of Roland Berger, a German management consulting firm, said that the profit margins of multinational companies have been significantly compressed, leading to reduced salaries for gilded sea turtles.

There is another explanation: many newly returned sea turtles lack sufficient quality. In the past, only top students were allowed to go abroad to pursue degrees, and competition for government scholarships was fierce. However, with rising incomes, many students of medium ability can afford to pay to study abroad, attending universities of questionable quality whose degrees offer little value in terms of employment. Additionally, with the economic downturn in Western countries, many return without any work experience.

There are fewer capable sea turtles, and the best Chinese students still remain abroad. Research funded by the U.S. National Science Foundation (NSF) found that five years after graduation, 92% of Chinese students who earned doctorates in the U.S. remained in the country, compared to 81% of Indian students, 41% of South Korean students, and 32% of Mexican students.

To attract these superstars back, China offers generous subsidies and allowances. But do these measures work? The results seem unpredictable. Despite these policies, returnee entrepreneurs still face numerous issues: rising labor and land costs, rampant intellectual property infringement, and serious corruption. Top scientists rarely return. In a paper by David Zweig of Hong Kong University of Science and Technology, it is written: "If China hopes to attract top talent back, it must fundamentally reform academic and scientific institutions to break the administrative power over recruitment and funding."

A troubling fact is the inner conflict of overseas Chinese towards their homeland. Li Sanqi, working at Huawei, appears to be a typical sea turtle, but his wife and children still live in the U.S. Besides increasing subsidies, China needs to strengthen the rule of law, eradicate corrupt officials, purify the air, clean water resources, and ensure food safety. Once these are achieved, the sea turtles will certainly notice.
