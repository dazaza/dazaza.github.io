Ms. Jing claimed that when she bought the house, she and Mr. Li jointly paid the down payment, took out a joint loan, and later repaid the loan with their family's combined income until now. Now, Mr. Li has come up with various reasons to want a divorce from her and has refused to put her name on the property deed, indicating an intention to own the property solely. In order to protect her rights, she requested the court to confirm her co-ownership of the property in question.

To substantiate his claims, Mr. Li brought his mother to testify in court. As a witness, Mr. Li’s mother said that she voluntarily decided to buy a house for her son, Mr. Li. She accompanied her son and daughter-in-law to look at houses and gave her son more than 170,000 yuan for the purchase. Ms. Jing did not recognize the testimony of Mr. Li’s mother. She stated that her parents also contributed funds after marriage, and the purchase price included joint deposits of the couple.

■Court Ruling

The husband claims the house was funded by his parents.

The property belongs to the husband individually.

Because she is a local resident without a Beijing hukou (household registration), only her husband's name was on the property deed. Now that her husband wants a divorce and intends to take possession of the house alone, Ms. Jing sued the court, requesting confirmation of her co-ownership of the property. Yesterday, it was reported that according to the new "Marriage Law Judicial Interpretation III," the Fengtai Court ruled that the property in question should be considered the personal property of the husband, dismissing Ms. Jing's lawsuit. This is also the first related case in the city since the Supreme People's Court announced the "Interpretation III of Several Issues Concerning the Application of the Marriage Law of the People's Republic of China."

However, the court pointed out that the taxes paid during the home purchasing process and subsequent mortgage payments were made using joint marital assets. Therefore, if the original plaintiff and defendant were to get a divorce in the future, the owner of the house should compensate the other party at the time of the divorce. Based on this, the court dismissed Ms. Jing's lawsuit.

The defendant, Mr. Li, disagreed with Ms. Jing's statements. Mr. Li said that the down payment for the property was funded by his parents, and the house was registered under his name as a gift specifically to him personally. He never repeatedly proposed divorce. Mr. Li believed that according to the new "Marriage Law Judicial Interpretation III," the property in question should be recognized as the personal property of one spouse, and he requested the dismissal of Ms. Jing's demands.

The plaintiff, Ms. Jing, recalled that she and Mr. Li registered their marriage on August 16, 2006. After marriage, they purchased an affordable housing unit through a mortgage in January 2007 for a total price of 400,000 yuan. Since she does not have a Beijing hukou while her husband, Mr. Li, does, due to the property being purchasable only by those with a Beijing hukou, when registering the property ownership transfer, only Mr. Li's name was recorded, and Ms. Jing's name was not.

The Fengtai Court believed that the property in question was purchased during the marriage between Ms. Jing and Mr. Li. However, the down payment was paid by Mr. Li’s mother, and the property was registered under Mr. Li's name. According to the relevant provisions of the new Marriage Law Judicial Interpretation III, if one party's parents fund the purchase of real estate for their children after marriage and the property is registered under the child's name, it can be regarded as a gift solely to that child. Thus, the property should be considered the personal property of one spouse. The property in question should belong to Mr. Li personally, and the court does not support Ms. Jing's request to confirm it as joint marital property.
