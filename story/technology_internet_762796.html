A entrepreneur's account: How I sell fish online

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:19:54

　　In fact, when I first started selling fish online, many friends around me thought I was crazy. 　

　　When everyone's eyes were on real estate, technology, mobile phones, and cars, I chose to sell fish. This was indeed a tough decision, but it felt solid.

　　How can O2O be done creatively? Should we abandon WeChat? Who is more impressive, Lei Jun or J.Wong? Can BlackBerry make a comeback? Can Cook take over from Jobs? I don't care about these things because they are far from us. Dreams may be big, but reality is small. People should still live in a more practical way.

　　You Cai failed, and it died unwillingly. However, I have no doubt about the enormous potential of fresh produce in e-commerce.

　　In 2012, I began experimenting with B2C by selling fish online. You Cai failed, but I survived. Of course, this might be related to the fact that I am just a small-scale start-up company with low operating costs and easier execution. However, some experiences I've gained along the way, I believe, are still valuable, and I'd like to share them with all of you. Perhaps they could provide some inspiration to those who are starting businesses online selling fish, vegetables, or fruits.

　　1. Product Positioning

　　My product positioning is: never sell daily products at low prices, only target mid-range customers. Initially, I didn't sell vegetables or meat; instead, I focused solely on frozen seafood.

　　Let me explain. I believe your biggest competitor in e-commerce for fresh produce is the local farmers' market. Compared to them, I see myself at a disadvantage. First, the traditional group of shoppers consists mainly of middle-aged and elderly people, most of whom will never have the habit of buying fish online. Second, you might argue that selling fish online saves tens of thousands of yuan in booth fees compared to physical stalls, but the harsh reality is: relative to vendors who restock almost every morning with nearly zero inventory, your annual warehousing costs, labor costs, and delivery costs are much higher than theirs.

　　You Cai mainly failed here. Observing its product structure, there were almost no mid-to-high-end products, mostly common agricultural products found in farmers' markets, directly facing the impact of local farmers' markets. Trying to change the purchasing habits of traditional aunties is as unrealistic as trying to make cows stop eating grass and switch to meat.

　　Compared to supermarkets, I found my advantages quite evident: shorter procurement cycles mean fresher products (referring to bulk goods cycles), prices nearly 40% cheaper (don't think supermarkets are cheap), and a younger customer base (I observed that most supermarket shoppers are aged between 25-40), which fits my requirements perfectly.

　　Seafood has many classifications, mainly divided into live seafood, chilled seafood, and frozen seafood (I won't go into detailed explanations here; interested parties can research themselves). Live seafood has the highest storage requirements, with price differences of around 50% between live and dead fish, so it isn't suitable for small-cost operators like me. Processed frozen seafood is the easiest to preserve, with minimal loss. Generally, as long as the temperature is maintained, the shelf life is about one and a half to two years (Ding Jingtao said that You Cai's fresh produce loss rate was 5%; I simply don't believe it).

　　Thus, I positioned my competitors as supermarkets. I selected some raw materials used in buffets, Western restaurants, mid-to-high-end Chinese restaurants, and Japanese cuisine. These products have high profit margins, aren't easily found on the market, and are easy to maintain quality. In this way, I avoided the impact of farmers' markets, and the product itself enhances its own value through its class.

　　2. Price Positioning

　　Unlike phones, cars, or houses, this market is extremely sensitive to price.

　　For example, for the same pound of cabbage or salmon, if your price is more than 5 yuan higher than the market price, customers won't be interested in you. You can only attract them by being cheaper than the market price. But the profit margin on a pound of cabbage versus a salmon... well, I don't need to say it, you understand.

　　Regarding pricing, Ding Jingtao from You Cai once said: "Even for tomatoes, prices can vary by 2 to 5 times. The quality of vegetables on the market varies greatly, and their nutritional safety aspects differ. You Cai tries to select the best quality produce when sourcing goods. For instance, cucumbers cost either 1.2 yuan or 1.5 yuan per piece, and we would choose the 1.5 yuan ones."

　　"So the vegetable prices on You Cai are slightly higher than in the market," Ding Jingtao emphasized, "If the quality is the same, You Cai is definitely cheaper."

　　To these statements, I would say: Mr. Ding must have never sold vegetables himself.

　　90% of the aunties absolutely wouldn't buy cabbage priced higher than the market rate, no matter how much you hype it up, even if your cabbage claims to be green, organic, super-invincible bullsh*t cabbage. Aunties are very pragmatic; whoever offers lower prices with acceptable quality gets their business.

　　Since the product is positioned in agricultural products, even the slightest price fluctuation can significantly affect sales. If Ding's strategy were followed, it would only work for agricultural products whose quality barely passes muster but are much cheaper than market rates, focusing on high volume sales. This allows capturing a large market share with low profit margins. However, for a start-up company without market experience or brand recognition, aiming to capture the market right from the start is very dangerous.

　　Without sufficient gross margin, it's hard to conduct market promotions. Unfortunately, Ding's mindset was to create premium agricultural products while expecting aunties to accept higher-than-market prices without being able to inspect the goods on the spot. This undoubtedly boxed him into a corner.

　　So, I set realistic prices for my products: they must be lower than market prices but offer high profits. I don't care about market share; I only care about whether the gross margin is high enough.

　　Would you rather earn 1 fen from each of 1000 people or 1 yuan from each of 100 people? I know how to calculate that.

　　3. Channel Costs

　　Here’s a small suggestion of mine: in any industry, you must have your own special channel. Such a channel doesn’t mean the market can be monopolized by you, but rather that there are relatively few channels for the product, making your product unique and scarce.

　　While doing wholesale, I pondered this issue, and solving it turned out to be simple. First, avoid fiercely competitive first-tier cities—after all, how can you compete barefoot against someone driving a car? Second, choose less popular origins. My product sources concentrate in three places: South America, Africa, and Northeast Asia. Large conglomerates (including other major aquatic companies) have a weakness—they're slow to pivot. Their bodies are huge but inflexible, whereas I just need to observe the market, choose the right product, and make decisions independently. By the time they get approval level by level, my product channel foundations will already be firmly established.

　　You Cai positioned its channels domestically, setting too low a barrier. The principle of wholesale is similar to group-buying—it's all about volume. So today you can negotiate with those farmers, and tomorrow they can do the same. Today you can create a You Cai, and tomorrow they can replicate it as You Cai Hua.

　　4. Marketing Model

　　This is something we must discuss in detail.

　　You Cai positioned itself as "delivering vegetables like delivering milk," attempting to become a closed ecosystem similar to iOS where customers could enjoy doorstep delivery services without leaving home. This involves two questions: 1. How to attract potential customers to your platform? 2. What keeps your customers loyal? (Which is what Mr. Ding refers to as increasing customer stickiness).

　　I initially noticed You Cai due to a report on Sina. Since the products were not much different, I curiously clicked to check it out. However, upon opening its UI, I was deeply disappointed. An outdated interface with no design sense whatsoever, aside from product categories and price introductions, there was no other relevant information. The variety wasn't rich enough, yet it included items like disinfectant and toothpaste, which had nothing to do with "vegetables"...

　　I really couldn't figure out how You Cai planned to retain customers. Opening its webpage felt like walking into a cluttered roadside grocery store, making me forget that I originally came to look at "vegetables".

　　Relying solely on the uninnovative milk-style order-delivery-after-sales service model to attract customers was undoubtedly You Cai's biggest blunder.

　　Therefore, before I started, I had my products transformed into various dishes. Just for one type of crab, I created soups, stews, stir-fries, etc. Then I hired professional photographers to artistically process the dishes, making them as appetizing as possible.

　　On this point, I borrowed from IKEA's philosophy—tempting customers with finished products but only selling semi-finished goods, allowing customers to fully experience the joy of achievement, which is much stronger than simply giving them a finished product.

　　Later, when many customers exchanged ideas on QQ, I would simply talk to them about how to use my ingredients to cook, rather than discussing how delicious the fish or crabs were. Taobao essentially sells pictures, and fresh produce is no different. Eventually, I stopped shouting about selling products and only chatted with foodies about how to eat the best.

　　Positioning towards a younger customer base, many people love to eat, but they just don't know how. They want to eat, and at this point, you need to teach them how to make it taste good. (Sometimes I even feel Dianping would be more suitable for fresh produce).

　　Later, more and more customers gave positive feedback. I observed that 80% of them were food lovers, and 20% came to try new things based on friends' recommendations. For this reason, I specifically established several QQ groups where foodies could freely exchange experiences under the premise of not violating national laws, comparing seafood from other places. Don't fear competition; the more competition, the more it highlights the uniqueness and price advantage of your products.

　　In terms of models, You Cai actually wanted to do an electronic + commerce model, but I personally think this was also a mistake. There are two paths to choose from: one is to create a platform-only model like Taobao, establishing relationships with offline wholesalers without engaging in commerce on the platform itself. The second is to focus solely on commerce, quickly building various regional distribution platforms, utilizing many options such as SNS, regional BBS, Weibo marketing, WeChat marketing, etc., concentrating on channel quality and price.

　　I lean towards the latter, after all, it allows direct contact with customers.

　　My current model focuses on wholesale offline, mainly supplying hotels, buffets, and sushi shops, etc. Online retail is the main focus, with prices slightly higher than offline. But I don't build my own platform; I merely affiliate with a few popular local BBS communities, leveraging their traffic to attract customers, and if the product resonates well, it spreads virally.

　　Sometimes, customers even upload photos of their own cooked dishes to "show off." Usually, I give some small extra rewards, and over time, hardly anyone in the QQ group leaves. Exchanges increase, and by the second half of last year, I almost didn't need to promote myself online anymore—customers helped evaluate my products.

　　Another benefit of this approach is resolving trust issues. Doing fresh produce online in China, besides the difficulty of standardization (which I'll discuss later), another point is that the public lacks trust in the hygiene of online fresh produce. Simply put, after drinking Sanlu milk and eating Sudan Red eggs, the general public no longer trusts merchants strongly. However, word-of-mouth can solve this problem.

　　In terms of profitability, I adopt a small-batch retail model. My minimum standard is five pounds per pack, avoiding retailing in one or two-pound increments, but offering very attractive prices, thus increasing volume.

　　5. Warehousing and Logistics

　　I dare say that apart from procurement costs, warehousing and logistics are definitely another significant expense. This includes manpower + transportation + warehousing fees + losses.

　　You Cai adopted the idea of using milk crates, delivering door-to-door daily at fixed points. I think there's a big problem with this approach: suppose, just before I finish work, I order a pound of cabbage, two pounds of pork belly, and half a pound of ginger and garlic on You Cai. When I get home, I realize that what I saw online is vastly different from what I see in reality, so I decide not to take it. And You Cai's customer service is great, agreeing to return it unconditionally after communication.

　　My question is: what am I going to eat for dinner tonight?

　　If I have such an experience once, I probably won't shop for agricultural products on You Cai again just to save a few bucks, because I'll end up starving...

　　And You Cai loses not only missed orders and customers but also faces losses (some products degrade in quality after being out overnight or for a period of time), plus additional manpower and transportation costs incurred by returns.

　　This is the biggest challenge e-commerce faces when dealing with fresh produce: how to address product standardization.

　　Frankly speaking, I currently don't have a good solution to this problem. I've heard complaints like "it doesn't look like the picture online," and all I can do is compensate with service—if they're not satisfied, I offer refunds.

　　Because my company is small, I can only cut costs, delivering according to the number of regional orders, inspecting goods face-to-face. This way, I can both explain and resolve misunderstandings on the spot (some are indeed caused by customers not understanding the product well enough) and avoid the repeated losses associated with secondary deliveries.
