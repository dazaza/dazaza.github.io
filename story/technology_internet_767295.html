Listen to how Apple employees review the company's internal culture

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:20:59

Recently, someone posted a question on the Q&A website Quora: "What is the internal culture like at Apple?" This article has compiled some of the more interesting answers.

Apple has always been tight-lipped about its products (at least during the Steve Jobs era), and people are equally curious about the true face of the company's internal environment. Many are familiar with the story of Steve Jobs, and Apple executives often talk about the company culture, making it seem like paradise. Is Apple really as shiny as it appears? Does it truly embrace the 'Think Different' ethos? Let's see what employees have to say:

Justin Maxwell, UI Designer: The first rule at Apple is never to talk about Apple.

"If I were still working at Apple, there’s no way I’d be answering this question... In short: you're just a part of a large system, you do some things that look impressive, but that’s just your job, and Apple pays you to do that. These things shouldn't and can't become topics for your blog. Don't drag everyone down because of your personal desires."

Anonymouse Employee A: The cafeteria is great.

"Apple's campus is a nice place, lush greenery, birds chirping, flowers blooming, and the cafeteria must be mentioned. It's exactly like what you'd find in school, all kinds of self-service, eat as much as you want. The food comes quickly, the cafeteria is bustling with people, chefs wearing white robes and black Apple hats ensure every employee gets recharged in time to work at 100% efficiency."

Brandon Carson, Contractor: "Your work will constantly be reviewed by your colleagues."

"The company fosters a collaborative environment. You have to present your results to the team and get their feedback. At first, I didn’t like it; I was used to working on my own projects in isolation, but this practice does improve the quality of the final product, and various checks and balances also speed up the project process. Now, I think more companies should adopt this approach."

Chad Little, Former Apple Designer: When Steve Jobs was around, his word was law.

"Apple is quite divided. It has all the red tape of a regular big company, but when Jobs gave orders, its efficiency could rival that of a startup. Without Jobs’ attention, a project might take months to make progress, but conversely, the completion speed of the project could be faster than you could imagine. So if you want to complete any cross-departmental project, just mention Jobs’ name, and you might get results the same day."

Simon Woodside, Program Manager for Core OS: To keep engineers quiet, we intimidate them if necessary.

"Here, I want to mention a project called Marklar that I worked on in 2001... During the project, we would discover a problem with the system, and my job was to find the right engineer to solve the problem without revealing any information about the project. Then I would intimidate the engineer about the consequences of leaking information, after which he would quietly complete his work. I myself wouldn’t mention this project either, even my family and friends only knew I was working on some secret project until Jobs himself unveiled it. Apple's confidentiality measures are very good, which has brought the company great success."

Anonymouse Employee B: The company is terrible, but the cafeteria is not bad.

"In general, the company is like a pressure cooker (high-pressure environment), and all communication is top-down... Obsessive management, rudeness, constant tension, and long hours best summarize the real culture of the company. Many supply chain management department employees choose to endure silently, believing it's a phase they must go through after graduating from business school, which will look good on their resumes when they transfer to larger companies later. The culture here is top-down: any voice from below will be severely prohibited. All you can do is work hard, and don’t forget that there are many people outside who covet your job (your manager definitely won’t forget this). Oh, the food at the cafeteria is still pretty good, and there are no dress requirements for employees."

Richard Francis, Former Intel Employee, who collaborated with Apple on a project:

"1. Apple's relatively conservative corporate culture makes many senior employees coming from other tech companies feel uncomfortable. 2. Apple has an obsessive enthusiasm for its brand. All Apple store designs maintain consistency — Jobs himself would frequently visit several Apple stores on the West Coast of the United States just to inspect. 3. Apple's environment isn't as highly creative as outsiders imagine; it is very organized and process-driven."

Anonymouse Employee C: "Everything at the company is decided by the marketing team."

"Everything at the company is decided by the marketing team and two newspaper critics. It’s absurd that these two laypeople have so much power within Apple. As an engineer, I am required to follow their instructions, where is the dignity? It's a great shame, and I want to sell all my company stocks."

TC Dotson (Position undisclosed): Apple is a paradox.

"Apple is an interesting company. On one hand, it promotes its 'Think Different' culture vigorously, but on the other hand, Apple is stricter than any place I've ever worked. In short, the brand image is the thing Apple cares about the most, nothing else comes close."
