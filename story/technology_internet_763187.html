Media claims that WeChat service breakdown can be solved by just replacing parts, but there are suspicious points behind this statement.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 17:01:53

The large-scale WeChat outage that occurred a few days ago has drawn significant public attention. After the failure, many in the industry speculated that it was due to the system crash caused by the upgrade to version 5.0 of WeChat. However, Tencent emphasized to this reporter that the cause of this failure was due to municipal construction cutting the optical cable, not an issue with Tencent's system servers.

Three Questions About the WeChat Outage Incident

A telecommunications professional from Guangdong found this WeChat outage strange. She believed that the time taken to pinpoint the fault was too long. It took Tencent about three hours after the fault occurred (around 11 AM) to locate the transmission hardware failure. In fact, such faults can usually be located within half an hour. From the repair time perspective, the transmission hardware failure happened at 8 AM and wasn't fully repaired until 2 PM, which is too long. Such faults are generally fixed within four hours; simply put, it's just replacing a part.

In this incident, users noticed that QQ, also under Tencent, functioned normally. Hence, there were doubts in the industry: This seems more like a system fault rather than a hardware problem. If the communication optical cable had been cut, it would have affected other companies' communication services, yet no such news came out. In response, Tencent stated to our newspaper that its other products were also affected, but WeChat was impacted the most because the network outage caused a core data center fault for WeChat.

Some professionals questioned why user usage couldn't be safeguarded or obstacles quickly removed after such unexpected situations occurred? Tencent merely stated that they will continue to strengthen and improve the disaster recovery system construction to ensure stronger emergency handling capabilities of the system.

The Parties’ Efforts to Distance Themselves Are Interesting

After the WeChat outage, both sides made statements saying it was "not related to us." Tencent said that the reason for this failure was not due to Tencent's system server issues but rather the interruption of optical cables in the Shanghai area. China Telecom responded via Weibo: "Due to the failure of Tencent's server, WeChat cannot be used currently, and it is not due to abnormality in the telecom company’s network." China Unicom sent mass text messages to its users stating: "Our network signal is normal. If your phone can access the internet normally but you encounter issues such as inability to send or receive WeChat messages or update your Moments, it might be due to a failure in Tencent's WeChat server."

Currently, the issue of WeChat charging remains highly sensitive. An operator person from Guangzhou revealed that due to WeChat heavily occupying channel resources, mobile operators' networks are already overwhelmed. A clear sign is that when users call another party's phone, the ringback tone heard is significantly delayed compared to before, which is related to WeChat heavily occupying the channels.

Internet analyst Ge Jia said that there is a sharp conflict of interest between operators and Internet companies. If consumers use free Wifi for WeChat communication, telecom operators won't make any money.

The Three Major Operators:

Only Mobile Has Not Cooperated

China Unicom, China Telecom, and China Mobile have been jointly dealing with OTT companies like WeChat. Recently, China Mobile and WeChat were at odds over signaling issues, even escalating the matter to the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology (MIIT). Last week, Guangdong branch of China Unicom collaborated with WeChat first, seen as directly targeting China Mobile. Previously, China Telecom had also reached a pre-installation cooperation agreement with WeChat for rural markets, indicating that the united front of the three major operators against WeChat has broken internally.

An analyst said that after the division in the operator camp, it will be more advantageous for WeChat because the flow value is not going to the telecom operators but to OTTs.

Traffic Package Subdivision

Potential Harm to Users

A Guangdong Unicom insider confirmed that they will launch WeChat Wo Card next month. From an industry perspective, regardless of the specific content of the package, one thing is certain: it confirms that the basic services of WeChat will be charged, albeit in a hidden and indirect manner.

Previously, China Unicom had launched targeted traffic packages for individual applications outside of the regular package, such as Sohu Video's 15 yuan/month targeted traffic package. This monthly subscription pricing model for single websites has infinitely fragmented and individually sold mobile internet applications. For users, adding up countless application packages could result in astronomical costs.
