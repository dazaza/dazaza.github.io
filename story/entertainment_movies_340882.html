Ticketing Network Model from the Perspective of Asian Games Ticket Sales

by hipposhen on 2010-04-06 21:52:05

Online shopping "too advanced"

The development of online shopping is no longer a fresh concept. However, the categories of goods available for online purchase keep emerging. From small daily necessities to large bulk goods, the methods are becoming more diversified and the products increasingly virtualized. But the stumbling block that hinders the popularization of online shopping has remained unchanged for over a decade: payment.

As we all know, the fundamental solution to the payment problem still lies in technology and credit. However, as the saying goes, "Where there's a will, there's a way," news of various online shopping scams still abound. Is online shopping really that difficult?

By this point, many readers will surely jump out to refute, with such a large online shopping market where everyone is shopping online, you're still doubting it here. Isn't this worrying unnecessarily? Indeed, some shopping websites like Taobao and JD.com have become increasingly mature in their operations and have accumulated a certain reputation. But the chronic problems of counterfeit goods and difficulties in consumer rights protection remain unresolved. These are industry issues, and the overseas online shopping environment also has its share of loopholes, which cannot be solved overnight.

Online shopping is a new concept. For those accustomed to the face-to-face transaction method of "cash on delivery," suddenly there's "no one in sight," "not even knowing what the person buying the goods looks like," and the most terrifying part is that online payment is required. "What if my password gets stolen?" Especially for some older people who don't even know how to use a computer, let alone shop online. Due to its temporal limitations and unique methods, online shopping will, whether pushed forward or backward by ten years, remain a niche consumption behavior far from the masses.

For this reason, it is unrealistic and impractical for mainstream online shopping websites to completely break away from physical stores and instantly transcend traditional shopping. In terms of the current market environment, it is "dropping the ball." Therefore, online shopping must return to a transitional phase, completing the short path that hasn't been walked yet—combining physical stores with websites.

Asian Games ticketing, "not leaving anyone behind"

Take the 2010 Guangzhou Asian Games ticketing as an example. Unlike the narrow practice of only offering telephone ticket booking during the Spring Festival travel rush, after careful screening, the Organizing Committee of the Asian Games designated Guangdong-based company City Online to provide the ticketing system. This company, known for electronic payments, had successfully launched the 311 Ticketing Network (www.311piao.com). The website innovatively collaborated with numerous chain stores across Jiangnan, including 7-11, OK convenience stores, Circle K, Yi Mei Jia convenience stores, Tian Tian Laundry, Ri Yue Tang, etc. Young people accustomed to online ticket booking can naturally "stay at home" and wait for the tickets to be delivered. However, for the majority who do not trust the internet or do not know how to book tickets online, they can simply go to any nearby cooperative store, pick up a catalog, make their selection, and have the staff handle the operation, reverting to "cash on delivery." This not only saves the hassle of buying tickets but also retains the affordability of online ticket booking, catering to the public's preferences. This marketing model may seem more cumbersome compared to fully online transaction ticketing websites like Yongle Ticketing Network (www.288.com.cn) and China Ticketing Pass (www.piaowutong.com), but it is undoubtedly more humane. Marketing, fundamentally, is still about service. The Asian Games target ordinary people, and those who truly want to watch the games are not the ones accustomed to online ticket booking—they prefer watching free live streams online. With this analysis, it's not hard to understand why the ticketing system for the Asian Games was not entrusted to those big-name ticketing websites.

The official online booking website of the Asian Games operates jointly with current retail outlets, stemming from the 311 Ticketing Network. Accurately speaking, it is a "slimmed-down version" of the 311 Ticketing Network, as it doesn't require as many business outlets or offer as many types of tickets. According to media reports, some of the Asian Games tickets have already surpassed the prices of the Beijing Olympics. As for how well the Asian Games ticketing system performs and whether it can withstand the test of a grand event, we shall wait and see.

Further Reading:

When it comes to the ticketing for the Asian Games, it's impossible not to mention the Olympic Games' ticketing model. The current Organizing Committee collaborates with two state-owned banks that provide sponsorship, setting up offline ticketing points in bank branches. Although this method has its conveniences, the pain of queuing up in banks is something familiar to anyone who has been to a bank. In contrast, the ticketing system for the Asian Games is more humane. Does this reflect the more meticulous nature of southerners in doing things?
