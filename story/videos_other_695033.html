How should start-ups choose their business names and apply for trademarks?

by fkfykpllj on 2012-03-07 13:56:46

How should start-ups choose their business names and apply for trademarks? How can entrepreneurs avoid intellectual property legal risks (Part II)

The first article in the "How Entrepreneurs Can Avoid Intellectual Property Legal Risks" series was about patents. This one focuses on trademarks, addressing three questions: how should entrepreneurs select their company's business name and product brand as a trademark, how should they apply for trademarks, and how can they avoid trademark infringement legal risks?

1. How should entrepreneurs decide on their company's business name and product brand as a trademark?

In my opinion, start-ups should choose words with distinctiveness for their business names and product names. They should avoid selecting common names used by the industry or products as trademarks. Relying on well-known brands within the industry may not be beneficial in the long run. Two years ago, I wrote an article titled "Legal Reflections on the Failure of 3G Trademark Application," which cited the case of a famous mobile internet company failing to register its business name "3G Portal" as a trademark.

Summarizing, using industry or product generic names as business names or trademarks has the following flaws and risks:

Firstly, such names are difficult to register as trademarks. Although using a generic name of an industry or product may sound more impressive and is beneficial for promoting corporate awareness or products, in reality, the probability of registering such names as trademarks is very low. The "3G Portal" trademark application took five years, involved numerous administrative reviews and lawsuits, but ultimately failed. The Changyu Company wanted to register the grape variety "Cabernet" as a trademark, which took several years, required immense effort and legal costs, but ended up sharing this brand with major competitors like the Great Wall Wine Company under COFCO. There are successful examples like "Cao Cong Sha" and "Tian Qi", but they are extremely rare, almost one in a hundred.

Secondly, without trademark rights, there is no exclusive protection. Many friends might think that even if "3G Portal" cannot be registered as a trademark, it can still be used continuously as the company's business name or brand. In reality, when others try to take advantage of the business name or brand, the company will face significant difficulties in defending its rights. In intellectual property, the exclusive right to use a registered trademark is a relatively strong right. If a registered trademark is counterfeited, the company can initially curb infringement through administrative reports, receive higher civil compensation standards, and even pursue criminal penalties. However, if the business name and brand cannot be registered as a trademark, the company can only rely on the relevant provisions of the Anti-Unfair Competition Law regarding enterprise name rights and the names, packaging, and decoration of well-known goods for civil litigation protection. This approach is much weaker in combating infringement and protecting the brand. Moreover, if the business name or brand is a generic name within the industry, it becomes even harder to protect.

Lastly, using industry or product generic names as one's business name or trademark may benefit the shaping of a brand image in the early stages of business development. However, as the company grows, the business name and product brand face the risk of dilution, where the business name becomes a generic symbol of the industry and the product brand turns into a generic name for the product. At this point, what was originally owned by the company becomes a public resource, actually weakening brand protection. For example, Apple's online app store "App Store." Originally, "App" could refer to both Apple and Application. But with the widespread concept of online app stores, Amazon recently launched its own online app store named "App Store." Although Apple filed a lawsuit against them, it was unable to prevent Amazon from using the name. Thus, Apple's carefully nurtured brand became a generic name for the industry.

2. How should start-ups apply for trademarks?

There are two important points to consider when applying for trademarks:

Firstly, there are many categories of trademarks. Companies should apply for trademarks not only in the main category but also in related categories. During my time at Zhongrong Network managing the company's trademarks, I regretfully realized that we had missed out on applying for the "Bubble Hall" trademark in the soap powder category, despite its popularity as an online game reaching 500,000 concurrent users. One day, I suddenly discovered that someone had applied for this trademark in the soap powder category, indicating that our previous application process lacked sufficient detail and thoroughness.

Secondly, trademarks that may cause ambiguity should undergo multiple-name protective registration. Wahaha did a good job in this regard, as the company not only applied for "Wahaha" but also registered variations such as "Hahawa" and "Hawahaw" for protective purposes. Given the prevalence of counterfeit practices in the Chinese market, I believe that start-ups must conduct multiple-name protective registrations.

3. How can start-ups avoid trademark legal risks?

The legal risks referred to here are not intentional counterfeiting or imitation of other people's trademarks. As mentioned in my previous article, such trademark counterfeiting actions can result in administrative penalties or imprisonment, and these infringements lack technical content, so I won't elaborate further. Here, I am referring to the need for start-ups to conduct trademark legal risk prevention before launching major new products or engaging in significant market promotion activities.

For example, a company launched an advertising slogan: "Smoke one less pack of Marlboro, buy one more box of a certain product." The holder of the Marlboro trademark sued the company, and the court ruled that the company committed infringement and unfair competition. Another example involves a new game expansion by a gaming company that was named after a castle in the famous game "Legend." However, the name of the castle had already been registered as a trademark by the operator, forcing the company to change the name and resulting in losses in earlier marketing efforts.

Relatively speaking, start-ups are more vulnerable. If there are trademark issues in advertising or new product naming, the impact on the company will be greater than for ordinary companies. Therefore, I recommend that start-ups conduct trademark searches before naming key products and engaging in related market promotions and advertising campaigns. If it seems too complex, professional intellectual property agencies can be hired. Additionally, it is best to avoid riding on the coattails of others' registered trademarks, even though this might temporarily increase visibility. In reality, companies that engage in such practices often leave negative impressions, harming their brand in the long run. On the other hand, if the trademark owner reports such behavior to the administration for industry and commerce, the infringer may not only fail to gain any benefits but also face fines.
