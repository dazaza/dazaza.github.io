Gloomy Prospects for Microsoft's Acquisition of Nokia

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 15:54:01

 

After Microsoft acquired Nokia's devices and services department for $7.17 billion, a dramatic scene unfolded. Although the acquisition amount was far less than Google's purchase of Motorola Mobility, the backlash and curses from fans were much higher this time around. Nokia's peak market value once exceeded 1100 billion euros, and its current fate is heart-wrenching.

There is a theory that suggests Nokia CEO Stephen Elop was a Microsoft spy who prevented Nokia from producing Android phones, leading to the company's downfall. It is said that Elop will receive "30 pieces of silver" and become the successor to Microsoft's CEO. This claim is not objective and does not align with logical reasoning. Nokia's current state is not necessarily due to deliberate actions by Elop and Microsoft but rather a larger possibility of strategic errors leading step-by-step into this pitfall.

Nokia is a full-industry chain company, manufacturing hardware, software, systems, and even having its own maps. A few years ago, asking such a company to adopt the Android system would be akin to China’s People's Liberation Army abandoning self-research and development to order submarines from the United States, contradicting its inherent characteristics and commercial logic. With Symbian in its possession, Nokia was bound to take a bold leap, choosing another path to break out and strive to become an important pole in the mobile phone market.

Of course, the failure of the Symbian system is another matter entirely; no one can predict the precise future. Technologically, Symbian was not outdated, and its user experience wasn't so terrible, with quite a number of followers worldwide. After Symbian's failure, Nokia's choice of the WP system logically made sense—focusing on a technically excellent yet little-recognized system was better than enhancing competitors' influence and putting itself at a disadvantage. Besides WP, Nokia had few other good options at the time.

Tragically, despite holding 80% of the WP system market share, WP smartphones remain non-mainstream, inconsequential to the overall market situation. This isn’t surprising. Excellent hardware design and manufacturing capabilities, an operating system not inferior to Android and Apple, along with strong supply chain management, still cannot offset the costs Nokia must bear during its transformation.

Nokia used to be a large company plagued by 'big company disease,' now smaller but still unwell. In fact, Nokia's main issue lies in its lack of flexibility and slow adaptation to market trends and new developments. When it releases a new feature, it celebrates as if it's something novel, when in reality, others have already implemented it long ago. This is a common ailment among traditional equipment manufacturers; BlackBerry is also almost finished, Motorola has been sold, and Nokia hasn’t collapsed yet, which only shows how deep-rooted its foundation is.

Especially, when Nokia and Microsoft were two separate companies, there were inevitably some coordination issues in their cooperation. Microsoft could purchase some services from Nokia, adding a few hundred million dollars to its annual revenue, but it wouldn’t make sense for Microsoft to blindly support Nokia with WP, even if it wanted to, it couldn't achieve it. Thus, situations like HTC launching WP7 first, Samsung launching WP8 first, and Nokia’s WP8 phones being incompatible with WP7 occurred. Fundamentally, Microsoft’s transition strategy was clumsy, Nokia wasn’t agile either, and both parties had their own interests, making a merger necessary for smoother collaboration.

The opportunity arose after Nokia launched several highly anticipated new models but still failed to reverse the trend, gradually becoming a penny stock. Meanwhile, Microsoft had determined its direction to transform into a device and service provider. Acquiring Nokia, with its impressive hardware capabilities, not only allowed Microsoft to aggressively promote its WP system through hardware but also provided a foothold for the development of Surface and Xbox. This was a very suitable deal for both parties.

In the hardware business Microsoft developed over the past few years, Xbox was the only successful product, giving Microsoft a taste of success and the determination to heavily invest in device manufacturing. The acquisition of Nokia didn’t cost much money, with a significant portion spent on purchasing patents. If it succeeds, great; if not, it doesn’t lose much. Microsoft thought practically, fitting its status as a major company, prioritizing stability.

However, pursuing stability might be the biggest instability. Given Microsoft's current awkward situation, it may not necessarily lead Nokia out of trouble. At best, it ties Nokia more closely to itself, endorsing the WP system. As a closed-source product, WP’s future business model is likely to follow Windows’ old path, charging licensing fees. However, this has almost no market in the face of Android’s completely free open-source option.

Competing against the internet’s free strategy with the old ways of a software vendor is bound to fail; it’s just a matter of how far Microsoft can go before realizing this. Nokia aligning with Microsoft is fundamentally a good thing. Samsung probably sighed in relief, and other competitors also felt a weight lifted off their shoulders.

They know that unless Microsoft undergoes a radical change, even acquiring Nokia won’t create many opportunities in the mobile device business. The best result for a large company's transformation is stabilizing its position and gradually expanding outward. Hoping for revolutionary innovation is almost impossible.

If Microsoft wants to achieve Apple-like success, it should first humble itself like Apple did. However, if it fails to become Apple and instead becomes BlackBerry, that’s something beyond anyone's control. Microsoft likely won’t take such a risk.
