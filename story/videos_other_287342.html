Southern Metropolitan Daily reported on November 29 that in the past century, the world has experienced a significant change characterized by global warming, and haze phenomenon is one of them. On November 22, at the large-scale science popularization series forum event "Xiaoguwei Science Forum" jointly organized by Southern Metropolitan Daily and Guangdong Science Center, Mr. Wu Dui, chief researcher from Guangzhou Tropical Marine Meteorological Research Institute of China Meteorological Administration, delivered a speech on the theme of haze, revealing its secrets to the audience. The following are the main contents of his speech and Q&A session:

Two Major Causes of Haze

The causes of haze are mainly related to the combustion of fossil energy. Human activities emit particulate pollutants, such as cement plants and power plants directly emitting particles, car exhaust directly emitting black carbon particles, human activities also emit gaseous pollutants such as sulfur dioxide, nitrogen oxides, and volatile organic compounds. Sulfur dioxide is oxidized into sulfate, nitrogen oxides and volatile organic compounds (or hydrocarbons) undergo photochemical reactions under ultraviolet light (mainly the reactions of alkenes, alkanes, and aromatics), and these reaction phenomena are the increase in ozone concentration, ultimately forming secondary aerosols related to peroxyacetyl nitrates, etc. These substances are all aerosol fine particles, causing visibility deterioration, thus causing so-called haze weather. Two types of smog events we are all familiar with, the London smog event and the Los Angeles photochemical pollution event, are essentially haze weather, where the latter's main pollutants nitrogen oxides and hydrocarbon pollution are also the pollution we face now.

In addition, urbanization and land use changes have also accelerated the formation of haze. Land use changes refer to the alteration of the underlying surface. After urbanization, the underlying surface becomes hard, like cement or asphalt, which has a very small heat capacity, much smaller than vegetation and water bodies, absorbing and releasing heat very quickly, thus causing a series of complex climate warming and pollution events.

Health Hazards of Haze

Haze can cause a high incidence of rickets in children because it blocks sunlight radiation. People of different races cannot directly obtain vitamin D from food; the only way to get vitamin D is through skin photosynthesis, so we must sunbathe. Haze reduces solar radiation and ultraviolet rays, reducing the synthesis of vitamin D, thus preventing calcium fixation in bones. Children need a large amount of calcium during their growth period, and calcium deficiency leads to soft bone disease and rickets.

A Long Road Ahead for Haze Control

Haze control cannot achieve results overnight. It took London more than 50 years to clean up the Thames River and improve air quality, with visibility continuously improving over 50 years. Los Angeles has also been well managed. Visibility deteriorates due to the increase in fine particles in the atmosphere, i.e., PM1 aerosol particle concentrations increasing from 20 micrograms per cubic meter to 40 micrograms per cubic meter, visibility worsens from forty-fifty kilometers to several kilometers; when aerosol particles increase to 80 micrograms per cubic meter, visibility worsens to eight-nine kilometers; further increases do not significantly change visibility within this range. Only when concentrations decrease from 40 micrograms per cubic meter to 20 micrograms per cubic meter can blue skies and white clouds truly return. This process took nearly fifty years in the U.S. and Europe, and in our socialist country, it may take twenty to thirty years. We cannot rush it; it will take at least two to three decades.

Q: Hello, Professor Wu, I would like to ask what aerosols are and how they are produced?

Wu Dui: Aerosols are a compound word, referring to a mixture of gas medium and atmospheric particles, colloids, with the English name "aerosol." They are similar to milk, where milk consists of some fat and protein floating in water, while particles floating in the air are aerosols. The original concept of aerosols includes the gas medium and all particles in the air, with the largest being hailstones, followed by raindrops and snowflakes, then soil dust, volcanic ash, sea salt particles, and the previously mentioned emissions from human activities, such as sulfates, nitrates, ammonium salts, black carbon, organic carbon (large amounts of organic compounds). These tiny particles float in the atmosphere and are generally referred to as aerosols.

There are three stages of understanding in the academic community. First, in the broad sense, aerosols should be the total combination of gas medium and floating particles. Second, removing the medium narrows it down to atmospheric particles. Third, excluding all precipitation substances, including hailstones, raindrops, ice crystals, and snowflakes, leaves only a narrow definition of aerosols, which include soil particles, dust particles, volcanic ash, sea salt particles, and some elemental carbon particles, organic carbon particles, and biologically active protein particles (such as viruses, bacteria, and animal and plant organic fragments). Together, these form the most narrow definition of aerosols.

Q: I want to ask what causes haze and how we can reduce its formation in daily life and production processes?

Wu Dui: The causes of haze formation mainly involve two points: one is special terrain or meteorological conditions causing stagnant airflow, such as the severe haze in the Pearl River Delta region caused by the formation of a stagnant airflow zone; the second is excessive atmospheric pollutant emission sources. When meteorological conditions are unfavorable for the dilution and diffusion of pollutants, and pollutant emissions exceed environmental capacity, the air becomes polluted, leading to haze.

To reduce the emission and formation of particles, thereby alleviating the haze phenomenon, it requires not only government efforts but also participation from each citizen. This is directly related to what we call a low-carbon economy, clean energy, and environmentally friendly living habits. The formation of haze and the sources of greenhouse gases and climate change are consistent, stemming from our burning of fossil fuels, which is the fundamental issue. Therefore, we must control pollution, not just sulfur, nitrogen, or specific pollutants like PM10, PM2.5, or PM1. We should primarily control carbon, as carbon emissions determine economic scale. If carbon emissions are controlled, sulfur, nitrogen, and particle emissions will also be relatively controlled.

Thus, we cannot merely treat the symptoms without addressing the root cause. Our previous environmental policies focused on sulfur dioxide control, acid rain control, early dust control, or current nitrogen oxide control, all of which are symptomatic measures. Very successful environmental protection measures in our country include the smoke and dust removal campaign in the 1980s, which reduced large particles; then came the sulfur dioxide pollution era, where large amounts of sulfur dioxide were emitted from energy production, oxidized in the atmosphere to form sulfates; now we enter the photochemical pollution era, mainly involving nitrogen oxides and hydrocarbons undergoing photochemical reactions under ultraviolet light, forming fine particles, or submicron-scale aerosols.

These issues can be addressed by individuals. For instance, the root cause is carbon emissions, so saving energy can help. Setting appropriate air conditioning temperatures and turning off lights when leaving can reduce carbon emissions, thereby reducing haze formation. For example, many fashionable young ladies might consider buying more expensive, trendy clothing and wearing them longer rather than frequently changing outfits or throwing them away after short use. Although cheap clothing seems economical, it is highly polluting and unenvironmentally friendly in the economic cycle. It's better to wear more expensive clothes for a longer time. Other ways include reducing paper usage since paper is directly related to forests, which absorb carbon dioxide. Thus, we have a concept: any expansion of power stations should be accompanied by alternatives, such as requiring a certain number of acres of forest to be planted if expanding a power plant in Nansha. These are methods for addressing climate change and haze.

Q: Are there differences or patterns in the haze phenomenon between relatively developed and industrialized regions such as the Pearl River Delta, Yangtze River Delta, or Bohai Gulf areas? Which contributes more to haze, industrial fuel emissions or vehicle exhaust?

Wu Dui: This is a very professional question and also a currently sensitive hot topic. First, there are four major haze zones in our country, among which you mentioned three: the Yellow-Huaihai Plain, the Yangtze River Delta, and the Pearl River Delta area. Another is the Yangtze River Valley, stretching from Sichuan-Chongqing to Wuhan.

The Yellow-Huaihai region, the Yangtze River Delta, and the Yangtze River Valley are connected into one continuous area, whereas the Pearl River Delta is relatively isolated. In terms of governance difficulty, the Yellow-Huaihai Plain and the Yangtze River Delta are harder to manage compared to the Pearl River Delta, where coordinating with Hong Kong, Macao, and Guangdong effectively controls haze.

In terms of composition, haze in the Yellow-Huaihai Plain and Beijing areas still has some relation to sandstorms, with at least one-third associated with sand particles. However, the haze in the Pearl River Delta is mainly formed by pollutants emitted from human activities. From this perspective, the haze in our region poses greater health risks than in the north because it is primarily organic matter-based. The haze in the Pearl River Delta region is generated by human activity emissions, requiring greater effort to manage.

Regarding your second question about the sources of haze in the Pearl River Delta, we haven't conducted relevant research ourselves. However, we've referenced results from collaborating institutions, such as the Guangzhou Institute of Geochemistry at the Chinese Academy of Sciences. In general, in major cities in the Pearl River Delta, traffic source emissions contribute the most to atmospheric particulates, especially fine particles. Contributions range from 20% to 40%. Regardless of whether the contribution rate is 20% or 40%, it ranks first among all emissions. Industrial emissions come second.

For fine particle emissions, there are several key points to note. As mentioned earlier, traffic source emissions are the primary source of fine particles. Traffic sources include several aspects: car exhaust emits black carbon particles directly, nitrogen oxides from burning diesel or gasoline depend solely on combustion temperature, and hydrocarbons, which along with nitrogen oxides are precursors to photochemical smog. Therefore, in traffic sources, car exhaust ranks first; second is gas stations, where all gas stations have both large and small breathing losses, emitting many volatile organic compounds, and when new oil is added, exhaust is discharged; third is tire friction, where braking and accelerating cause tires to rub against the ground, directly emitting black carbon, as tires consist of rubber and black carbon.

Besides traffic source emissions, household decoration industries now need the most control. Plywood manufacturing, furniture manufacturing, shoe making, stationery making, cosmetics making—all these emit hydrocarbons, especially hair styling mousse. Therefore, everyone should minimize makeup and cosmetic use. Households should also reduce renovations, opting for once every ten years instead of every five. Solvent emissions are also important.

We now face novel complex atmospheric pollution, where traffic source emissions and other solvent emissions constitute a significant portion. Last year's economic crisis led to reduced shoe factory production, closures, and layoffs, decreasing emissions. Among the 50 air quality improvement measures implemented by the Guangzhou municipal government, three specifically targeted haze and were quite effective, being the first nationwide to directly address sources of novel complex pollution. These included car exhaust management, gas station management, and management of solvent production and use enterprises, all of which were directly aimed at haze weather.

Q: Professor Wu, can haze possibly transmit viruses?

Wu Dui: We have encountered SARS, avian influenza, and swine flu. These three global pandemics indicate the possibility of transmission between humans and animals. It's unclear who comes first or last, but these viruses can cause symptoms in both humans and animals. My research is somewhat related to this, indicating that aerosols can serve as vehicles for SARS, avian influenza, and swine flu viruses. Aerosol surfaces can be uneven, with concave areas where water condenses, providing survival conditions for viruses and bacteria. Originally, these three viruses were thought to spread via close-range droplets, but due to the presence of aerosols, viruses and bacteria can use aerosols as cars or trains, allowing aerosols to carry these viruses far distances. For example, when birds fly and defecate in the sky, it turns into aerosols, spreading the infection as they fly around. (Note: This article has been edited.)
