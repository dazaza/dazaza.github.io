The Secret to Uniqlo's Success

Uniqlo's Growth Secret

For 13 days, Tao Shengye had been sitting in front of his computer. He repeatedly clicked the right mouse button and chose "refresh" from the drop-down menu, watching the numbers continuously rise: 100,000, 200,000, 1 million, until his computer crashed due to constant refreshing. Truly, he was persistent. But Tao Shengye wasn't the only one being persistent. From Beijing to Hangzhou, from Guangzhou to Shenzhen, almost all first-tier cities in China saw 1.33 million people caught up in this collective madness. What attracted them was the Lucky Line online queuing event jointly held by Uniqlo and Renren.com four months ago. According to the event rules, netizens who virtually queued up for shopping on the Uniqlo website had a chance to win an iPhone 4, iPad, travel vouchers, special edition commemorative T-shirts, 90% discount coupons, etc.

This wasn't the first time that fashion-forward men and women were fascinated by Uniqlo. When you leave your desk and head to Beijing's Xidan Joy City, Sanlitun, or Shanghai's Nanjing Road, you'll realize similar craziness happens every day. Through the crowded crowds, their shopping routes often go like this: after browsing ZARA on the first floor, they stroll around H&M on the opposite second floor, then directly go upstairs to buy Uniqlo.

Where is the secret? Behind the display windows, even a plain T-shirt has 300 different color options at Uniqlo, most of which are designed by modern art master Keith Haring. Typically, such a T-shirt costs 29 yuan. More importantly, Uniqlo clothes can be paired with anything. Many basic items have been extensively recommended by high-end fashion magazines and are even matched inside LV or Chanel coats.

Jil Sander, the "Queen of Minimalism" who once worked for PRADA, didn't hesitate to leave her "puppy" (Jil Sander affectionately referred to her beloved brand Vukmirovic as "puppy") to join Uniqlo's design team. "Her clothing is the best; a piece of Jil Sander's clothing can last you ten years, and then you can pass it on to your daughter." The fashion design world once evaluated Jil Sander this way. Now, she is Uniqlo's creative director, hoping her designs will receive the same praise again.

All of this is because of a 61-year-old "fashionable man" from a small town in Japan. This person is not tall, calling himself a "tailor." He wears black-framed glasses, dons suits made by his own company, with the entire outfit costing less than 400 yuan, and walks so fast that accompanying staff have to jog to keep up. Media that interviewed him said this person is well-versed in the latest fashion trends in Paris, London, and New York and can predict the next season's trends. He can spark a fashion craze with a simple jacket or underwear and create a Uniqlo trend worldwide; even the most ordinary T-shirt can be transformed into an art collectible that everyone looks forward to each season and can afford. In the past 20 years, he has opened clothing supermarkets across from Hermès and Chanel; he has achieved equality in dressing.
