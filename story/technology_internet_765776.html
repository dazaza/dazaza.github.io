UK's Tesco to take on Netflix and Amazon at home

by anonymous on 2013-08-20 15:59:43

According to Bloomberg, as the overseas business of Netflix, a U.S. video service provider, has yet to turn a profit, Tesco, the largest retailer in the UK, began challenging Netflix, making the situation for Netflix in the UK market even more severe.

Currently, Tesco is increasing its investment in its digital download service Blinkbox, which mainly provides movies and TV series to users. Soon, Blinkbox will also start offering e-books and music services. The service covers multiple platforms, including PCs, tablets, TVs, and Sony PlayStation 3.

Nowadays, an increasing number of retailers are gradually shifting their focus from in-store sales to digital downloads, just like Tesco. According to a report by market research company Technavio, the digital download market grows at an annual rate of 17%, and by 2018, the global total of this market will reach around $20 billion.

Walmart acquired Vudu, a video-on-demand startup, in 2010; Sainsbury, a British retailer, also owns its own online entertainment service. Compared with Netflix, retailers have the advantage that millions of consumers visit their websites every week. These consumers mostly hold membership cards of various retailers, and they can earn membership points by purchasing their online video services, which can be used to buy daily necessities.

Analyst Michael Perry of Verdict Research said: "After watching a movie, consumers can also conveniently get discounts on buying daily necessities or refueling, or accumulate membership card points. From this perspective, Blinkbox might be more attractive than Netflix, Amazon's movie download service Lovefilm, or even iTunes. Undoubtedly, Blinkbox will definitely become a major threat to these services."

Service Model

In the UK, the monthly subscription fee for Netflix and Lovefilm is £5.99 (approximately $9.36); while Blinkbox offers a service model similar to iTunes, where users can rent or purchase the movies or TV series they want to watch. Currently, Blinkbox has one million users.

In Blinkbox's service, older movies are priced as low as 99 pence (approximately $1.55); however, the viewing fees for some new releases are relatively high. For example, the rental and purchase prices for the movie "Flight" are £3.49 (approximately $5.45) and £10.99 (approximately $17.16), respectively; the purchase price for the third season of the popular HBO series "Game of Thrones" is £17.99 (approximately $28.10).

Analyst Ruth Shaw of Tech London Advocates said: "In fact, consumers are the biggest winners. Under intense competition, these movies and TV series offer a wide range of content choices and are cost-effective."

Generally speaking, the content provided by "pay-as-you-go" services is updated faster than that of "all-you-can-eat" services. New releases usually hit the shelves four months after their theatrical release. Blinkbox co-founder and CEO Michael Comish said: "When a film is released on DVD, we can immediately provide it, which is about a year earlier than Netflix and Lovefilm." Tesco acquired Blinkbox in 2011 but did not disclose the acquisition amount.

Fresh Content

In March this year, Netflix Chief Content Officer Ted Sarandos claimed exclusive online streaming rights to the movie "The Hunger Games" in the UK, which was criticized by Comish. Comish posted on Twitter that this movie had been available on Blinkbox since last September. Netflix declined to comment on this matter.

Gartner analyst Fernando Elizalde said: "Blinkbox's model allows it to 'obtain much fresher content, which is different from subscription services.' Regarding Blinkbox's model, it is not merely a subscription service." Currently, Blinkbox's model has attracted many users. One user said that they chose Blinkbox because it was the first video service provider to offer the second season of "Game of Thrones," and the service is backed by Tesco, so it will not go out of business anytime soon.

Online Platform

This user's example demonstrates that the entertainment industry is gradually moving towards online platforms. According to estimates by the British Video Association, the current size of the UK video entertainment market has reached £2 billion (approximately $3.1 billion), of which digital video download services account for 23%. In the first half of 2013, the scale of this service grew by 43% year-over-year.

According to market research company eMarketer, by 2017, the number of British users who watch at least one video via online services each month will reach 40 million, up 23% from 2012. Blinkbox COO Adrian Letts said in an interview with Bloomberg today: "We are very pleased, Blinkbox is growing at an amazing speed."

Netflix is very popular in the U.S. market, but its overseas business performance has always been less than satisfactory. In the second quarter of this year, Netflix added 610,000 new overseas users, bringing the total number of users in Latin America, Canada, the UK, and Ireland to 7.75 million.

Netflix's overseas revenue in the second quarter reached $166 million, more than doubling year-over-year, accounting for 15% of the company's total revenue. However, over the past year, Netflix's net loss still amounted to approximately $340 million. Netflix CFO David Wells once said: "As overseas markets become increasingly saturated, the company's overseas business will continue to incur losses over the next two years."

Fierce Competition

Netflix and Lovefilm face fierce competition in the UK, where there are already some powerful broadcasters, such as Sky from 21st Century Fox. Ovum analyst Adrian Drury said that Sky owns many popular contents and has its own movie broadcasting service. Drury stated that if Blinkbox can integrate its supermarket membership program with entertainment services, then the service will have some competitiveness.

Drury believes that Comish shares some similarities with Netflix CEO Reed Hastings, as both can well grasp the direction of consumption of home entertainment content. Comish, a Canadian aged 48, once said that although there were buyers who offered higher prices than Tesco, he believed that Tesco had the clearest development plan for Blinkbox's future.

Digital Services

The acquisition of Blinkbox is just one step in Tesco CEO Philip Clarke's promotion of the company's online services. After acquiring Blinkbox, Tesco also successively acquired digital book platform Mobcast, social media company BzzAgent, and digital music platform WE7.

In addition to online video services, Blinkbox also plans to launch e-book and music services later this year. Moreover, Blinkbox has already launched free internet TV service Clubcard TV, which mainly provides children-appropriate programs for Tesco's 16 million members. The Sunday Times reported yesterday that Tesco may release its own tablet computer during this Christmas period.

Tesco's stock fell 0.5% in regular trading on the London Stock Exchange on Monday, closing at £368.30 (approximately $575.38), narrowing its cumulative gain this year to around 9.3%. Netflix's stock rose 1.4% in regular trading on the New York Stock Exchange on Monday, closing at $262.50.

IHS Screen Direct director Richard Broughton said that although Tesco will not quickly transform into a digital entertainment center, the company is gradually moving in that direction. Broughton said: "In the next 5 to 10 years, the user base of Tesco's digital services will further expand. In the long term, the company's digital service development prospects are very optimistic."
