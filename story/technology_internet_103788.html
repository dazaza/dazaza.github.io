Video websites lack profit-making model and are streamlining to get through the winter - Age1983

by hexiaoyao on 2008-11-15 20:50:45

The impact of the financial crisis on video websites is becoming increasingly evident - funding for the video industry has almost dried up, and as a result, layoffs have become one of the tools for video companies to survive the winter. On November 3rd, LiuJianFang (Six Rooms) announced a 70% reduction in staff, reducing the total number of employees from 200 to 60. Tudou.com, Uusee.com, and JiaDong.com also began laying off employees in preparation for the tough times ahead. However, the biggest cost for video websites is not personnel salaries, but hardware investment. The lack of funding sources will likely cause most video websites to fail this winter.

Collective Downsizing of Video Websites

In recent days, rumors about the merger of video websites have been rampant. First, there were rumors that YouKu.com would merge with Tudou.com, followed by rumors of a possible merger between YouKu.com and Ku6.com. Although the responsible parties for these websites neither confirmed nor denied these rumors, where there's smoke, there's fire, and the spread of such rumors reveals the severe "cash shortage" reality faced by video websites. While the merger of video websites remains uncertain, it is an undeniable fact that almost all video websites are undergoing layoffs.

On November 3rd, LiuJianFang announced a 70% reduction in staff, decreasing the total number of employees from 200 to 60. Previously favored by multiple investors, Tudou.com, Uusee.com, and JiaDong.com also began laying off employees to prepare for the winter. It was reported that Tudou.com internally announced a 20% reduction in staff. However, Tudou.com CEO Wei Wang denied this, stating, "Tudou does not have any plans or measures for layoffs; some departures are normal personnel changes." Uusee.com, a network television operator, also laid off 10% of its staff. A former employee of JiaDong.com revealed to the reporter that his department was reduced from 10 people to 2, "The director was originally planning to leave but stayed after being persuaded by the company."

According to industry insiders, among the more than 200 video websites nationwide, over half have plans for layoffs. In response, YouKu.com President Yongkeng Gu stated that this wave of layoffs stems from the Web 2.0 bubble period when 200 video websites suddenly emerged, causing resource waste and fierce competition within the industry.

Profitability Remains Distant

According to statistics from iResearch Consulting, the revenue of leading domestic video websites will exceed 1 billion yuan this year, and next year it is expected to surpass 2 billion yuan. As the primary source of profit for video websites, advertising almost entirely accounts for their income, but the current advertising revenue is far from covering the high bandwidth costs incurred by video websites. Currently, advertising agencies and advertisers have low recognition of video websites and even hold negative attitudes towards their existing presentation forms. Hongpeng Wang, executive vice president of Ogilvy Century, one of China's advertising distribution platforms, said: "Revenue is far from covering operational costs. The current presentation form of video websites makes it difficult to achieve profitability."

To obtain a stable profit model, video websites need to first find ways to streamline the industrial chain and establish a stable profit model. There are currently two pressing issues in this chain: copyright issues and content homogenization. Copyright problems have always plagued video websites and are the biggest obstacle to commercial cooperation for these sites. For a long time, most of the films and TV series on video websites have been uploaded by users themselves. Despite numerous video websites adopting exemption clauses to protect themselves, they still cannot escape responsibility. Copyright issues have led advertisers to question the effectiveness of video advertisements and the legality of video sources, making them hesitant to enter on a large scale. Additionally, the content homogenization of video websites is also a major issue. Programs seen by internet users on video websites are mostly similar, lacking innovation, which is not conducive to the expansion of new markets for video websites.

To expand profit channels, mainstream video websites have undergone collective "self-denial" this year: Tudou created a high-definition version called "Black Bean," while YouKu quietly launched a client software named "iCool," which is considered a signal of its strategic shift towards client-based film on-demand services. According to Tudou CEO Wei Wang, Tudou has begun redeveloping the P2P software "Fast Potato" that it started researching two years ago, hoping to reduce bandwidth costs through technological means.

Difficult Financing Could Be the Last Straw

It is well known that the largest cost for video websites, and the most "money-burning" area, is not personnel salaries but hardware investment. Data shows that YouKu alone spends 24 million yuan monthly just on bandwidth, and domestic video websites collectively spend 120 million yuan monthly on bandwidth alone, not including other expenses like purchasing servers.

Such astonishing "money-burning" scale and speed could, before the financial crisis, perhaps attract VC rounds of "replenishment" with beautiful IPO plans. But now? "This market will no longer see more venture capital entering," said Fuhu Zeng, vice president of operations at TianXian Video. Tudou CEO Wei Wang also indicated that video websites would be unable to successfully raise funds for the next three years.

According to venture capital conventions, a project generally undergoes at most four rounds of financing. If profitability is not visible after three rounds, VCs often sell or abandon the project. YouKu and Tudou have already completed their fourth round of financing. In other words, even without the financial crisis, according to convention, VCs would find it difficult to invest further. Moreover, facing the financial crisis, VCs are already struggling to manage themselves. Not long ago, Sequoia Capital, the world's largest VC, publicly declared that the financial crisis had caused its partners to lose billions of dollars.

Most video websites are currently under enormous financial pressure. Data from Tudou shows that since January this year, 50% of VCs investing in small and medium-sized video websites have begun to "cut off supply." Internet users almost continuously provide money to game operators, but the 24-hour bandwidth expenditure of video websites can only be recovered by advertising salespeople during the 8 hours of daylight.
