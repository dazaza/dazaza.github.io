Rhine Sentiment

by zhiyi7799 on 2012-02-16 23:18:49

Rhine River Lyric, Author: Xian Ruolei

During our visit to Germany, when our hosts kindly invited us to take a boat ride on the Rhine River, we were deeply impressed by the majestic scenery along its banks.

In late autumn, the Rhine Riverbanks are picturesque with stunning landscapes. The beech trees cast green shadows like waves, and the chestnut trees stand tall in exhaustion. Everywhere is filled with the vibrant joy of harvest. Accompanied by our German friends, Mr. Maiboer, Kuno, and their wives, we set off in three Mercedes-Benz cars from Düsseldorf, traveling north along the expressway. Along the way, our German friends enthusiastically introduced us to the towns and scenic spots we passed.

After about an hour, we arrived at the small town of Koblenz on the banks of the Rhine River. Before taking a short break, we switched to a cable car to descend the mountain. The cable car traversed through the forest, where the sun filtered through the dense branches, casting golden beams of light. The air was incredibly fresh. Upon reaching the mountaintop, we looked far into the distance. The Rhine River shimmered like a brocade ribbon flowing southward. The Moselle River and Ahr River flowed from afar, converging into the Rhine River. On both sides, the fields stretched endlessly, and flowers bloomed everywhere. At the river mouth stood the grand "German Corner," symbolizing the soul of Germany. Nearby, the mountains undulated, and Gothic-style rooftops could be faintly seen.

This scene reminded me of the Tang Dynasty poet Han Yu's famous lines: "The river flows like a green silk ribbon, and the mountains resemble emerald hairpins." The treasures of our Eastern culture harmoniously blend with the magnificent scenery of this stretch of the Rhine River in Central Europe, creating a remarkable connection that feels so fitting. The artistic charm of Han Yu's poetry over a thousand years ago left me in awe.

"Professor, shall we go to the Breisach Castle?" Mr. Maiboer asked politely.

"Excellent!" I came back to reality from my deep thoughts.

Our German friends explained that Breisach Castle is located on the northern slope of the Alsatian Mountains, guarding the strategic point of the Rhine River. As early as two thousand years ago, Roman legions stationed here. In 1019, the castle was officially built, and Teutonic knights often gathered here to discuss military strategies. During World War I, Germany lost the war, and the castle was occupied by American and French troops. In World War II, General Eisenhower led the Allied forces landing in Normandy, sweeping westward, and capturing the Breisach Castle in the spring of 1945. Once again, the American flag flew over the fortress. This star-spangled banner, as a symbol of victory, is now preserved in the War Museum in Washington, D.C.

We climbed up the stone steps to the top of the castle. There was a drill ground in the middle, surrounded by walls with various mortars and cannons lined up, overlooking the Rhine Valley with the grandeur of a lone warrior holding the pass against ten thousand enemies.

Today, the rivers and mountains remain, but where are the people? No matter how strong the fortress, it could not save the Nazi Empire from destruction.

Leaving the castle, we took the cable car down the mountain, boarded the cruise ship, and began our journey from Koblenz to Cologne.

The Rhine River is the largest water artery in Europe. It originates from the Alps in southern Switzerland, flowing through Liechtenstein, Austria, France, Germany, and the Netherlands, finally emptying into the North Sea near Rotterdam. It stretches for more than 1320 kilometers. Between Bingen and Bonn in Germany, the river winds through deep canyons with beautiful scenery. Many German folk legends and fairy tales were born here. Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, great leaders of the world proletariat, were born in the Rhine River basin in Trier and Barmen, respectively. The immortal work "The Communist Manifesto" originated from Cologne in the Rhine River basin and spread throughout the world. The mountains and waters here bear the brilliant footprints of these revolutionary mentors.

As the cruise ship sailed, we leaned on the railing of the top deck, gazing at the lush green mountains and exquisite forests. Medieval spire buildings dotted the landscape. On both sides of the valley lay endless vineyards. Below, green waves rippled, and steamboats zipped by one after another. We savored the local specialty grape wine while admiring the famous landmarks on both banks.

When the ship passed Waldnerach, Mr. and Mrs. Kuno surprisingly started talking to me about Chinese Taoist philosophy. Mr. Kuno said that they both loved Chinese Taoist philosophy, finding the Taoist principle "Nothing is still without change, nothing is constant without movement" profoundly dialectical, with infinite philosophical implications in the ever-changing sun and clouds.

They also talked about the suffering inflicted by the Nazi fascists on the German nation and the entire world. How could such a great nation as the Germans have submitted to a frenzied war-monger like Hitler? I told them that even during Hitler's arrogant and reckless times, the German people did not submit. Besides the great anti-fascist movements led by the German woman Thälmann, there were also noble figures like Stauffenberg within the ruling class who attempted to assassinate Hitler multiple times. Although they failed and met tragic ends, even Hitler's dreaded general, Erwin Rommel, the "Desert Fox," was implicated and forced to commit suicide. Now, over fifty years later, the seas have turned into mulberry fields, and the past seems like smoke. Everyone sighed deeply.

Two hours later, the cruise ship reached Linz. Suddenly, music erupted in the cabin, playing Johann Strauss's "Kaiser-Walzer." Our enthusiastic hosts invited us to dance. We entered the dance hall and danced the waltz. Once we started, it was hard to stop. Many other tourists on the ship, hearing that we were Chinese, warmly invited us to dance. After several dances, we were all drenched in sweat.

The cruise ship sailed upstream against the blue waves of the Rhine River, with seagulls circling around the hull. Remagen, Bad-Godesburg, and West Germany's capital Bonn passed by one after another. Mr. Maiboer proudly spoke about this magical land, rich in natural resources and talented individuals, nurturing a splendid culture and giving birth to many outstanding figures: Beethoven, Goethe, Marx, Engels, Hegel, Einstein, etc. The mystery of nuclear fission was first unraveled here. According to him, historically, there was almost one celebrity every 50 square kilometers in this region, his pride evident in his words.

At this moment, Mrs. Maiboer interjected, saying, "We are truly in awe of the five-thousand-year-long history of the People's Republic of China. Your Four Great Inventions, imperial palaces, and the Terracotta Warriors of the Qin Mausoleum are all world wonders!" Mr. Maiboer happily recalled his trip to China in 1985, reminiscing about climbing the Great Wall and visiting beacon towers with us. We all agreed that the world needs peace and stability domestically to build our nations. We hope that relations between China and West Germany will strengthen in trade and commerce, opening broader prospects for cooperation.

Unconsciously, the sun set in the west, and the sky was filled with rosy clouds. The cruise ship passed under the harp-shaped bridge of the Marienburg Cathedral. The 150-meter-high spire of Cologne Cathedral loomed in the distance. The Lufthansa headquarters skyscraper rose from the ground, and the setting sun's rays bathed the tower in golden hues.

By the time we disembarked at the Cologne pier, the banks of the Rhine River were already shrouded in evening mist, and the city lights had begun to twinkle.
