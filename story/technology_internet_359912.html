Dry cleaning store technology: Natural soap powder is not equal to detergent powder 

  

        Currently, there are products on the market called natural soap powder. Many consumers think it is a type of detergent powder. In fact, soap powder is not equal to detergent powder. The main differences between the two are: First, the raw materials used are different. The active ingredient in natural soap powder is mainly fatty acid, and over 90% of its starting material comes from renewable plant oils. However, the active ingredient in current detergent powders is mainly alkylbenzene sulfonate, with its starting material being petroleum. Second, due to the difference in main active ingredients, the formulation structure requirements for both are also different.

        From a functional perspective, natural soap powder is superior to detergent powder and is more suitable for washing intimate clothing. First, compared to regular synthetic detergent powder, natural soap powder does not contain polyphosphates and has natural characteristics. Therefore, it has less skin irritation, is safe, and protects fabrics. It has an affinity for fabrics, making clothes soft and fluffy after washing. This solves problems such as fabric dirt accumulation, hardening, and static electricity caused by multiple washes with synthetic detergent powder. Second, due to the addition of special calcium soap dispersants in natural soap powder, its stain removal ability is stronger. Additionally, it has a strong fragrance, leaving a pleasant scent even after the fabric dries. Third, it produces ultra-low foam, which makes it easier to rinse clean. Many people believe that the richer the foam from detergent powder, the cleaner the clothes will be. However, this is not true. In developed countries and regions such as Europe, America, and Japan, ultra-low foam powder already accounts for a significant market share. Ultra-low foam natural soap powder aligns with international fashion trends.

        When using detergent or soap powder, the correct method can achieve the best cleaning effect, while incorrect methods not only affect the cleaning effect but also have adverse effects on the clothes. For example, some consumers throw their clothes into water, sprinkle some detergent powder without stirring it evenly or dissolving it, and then wash them a few hours later. This causes undissolved detergent powder to stick to the clothes. Over time, this can cause uneven coloring on the clothes due to the high concentration of trace brighteners in certain areas.
