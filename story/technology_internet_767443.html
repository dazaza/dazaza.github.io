Microsoft's New CEO: An Impossible Task

by anonymous on 2013-10-15 21:29:34

A recent article on the tech and media blog MondayNote stated that Microsoft's new CEO will face an impossible task. The company's traditional Windows and Office licensing model is in decline, and in the realm of devices and services, it will also face fierce competition from companies such as Google, Apple, HP, and Dell. Undertaking a substantive business model transformation is also a perilous endeavor, making the realization of this transition extremely arduous.

If you were Microsoft's new CEO, how would you view the chasm between two mutually exclusive business models? The traditional Windows and Office licensing business model is on a downward trend. In the field of devices and services, you must directly confront strong rivals like Google and Apple, as well as PC partners such as HP and Dell. Will you lead the company to successfully complete its business model transformation, or will you fall into the abyss of business model transformation?

Microsoft's past was very simple and easy, with extremely lucrative profits, as the Windows + Office combination penetrated almost all PCs.

This made Microsoft one of the most successful software companies in history, and its founding CEO, Bill Gates, became one of the richest people in the world. In 2000, Microsoft's market value reached $540 billion (close to $800 billion in today's dollars). From the graph below, it is clear how significantly Microsoft outpaced other technology companies in terms of market capitalization at the time.

Back then, Windows + Office licensing was Microsoft's only truly important source of revenue; other businesses — all other software products, even enterprise server sales — either depended on Microsoft’s massive PC user base or were insignificant. Hardware and entertainment businesses were not crucial, and online service revenues had not yet reached the level we have seen in recent years.

According to the company's 2000 annual report, "Windows platform" and "productivity applications" revenues reached $9.3 billion and $10 billion respectively, totaling $19.3 billion, accounting for 84% of Microsoft's total revenue of $23 billion. More importantly, these two businesses accounted for 98% of Microsoft's operating profit.

By the first quarter of 2013, Microsoft's market value had undergone significant changes.

In many respects, Google has become Microsoft 2.0, Oracle has shown robust growth, and the highest-valued tech company has become Apple.

The Mobile Revolution

What happened in between?

Mobile personal computing devices were born, and smartphones and tablets are gradually replacing traditional PCs, desktops, and laptops.

To put it more succinctly, the iPhone triggered that change.

When Steve Jobs announced the first-generation iPhone in January 2007, there were already numerous smartphones on the market, including platforms like Windows Mobile, Palm Treo, Nokia Symbian, and BlackBerry, but the iPhone was different. It was essentially a personal computer with a modern operating system. Initially, the iPhone did not support third-party applications, but soon after its release, the SDK (Software Development Kit) and App Store were introduced.

Android quickly followed suit, and the Smartphone 2.0 war began, leading to the decline of traditional leaders.

Then the iPad leveraged the global popularity of the iPhone and its ecosystem, leaving Android tablets far behind. These new mobile personal computing devices changed consumer habits, prompting them to reevaluate their loyalty to PCs.

Attitude Shift

As these products continued to flood the market, Microsoft went through its own stages of grief, from denying the existence of these mobile products to eventually accepting the market trends they had initiated.

At first, Microsoft dismissed the emerging devices. A memorable moment was when Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer mocked the iPhone in 2007. Eddie Wu, a Microsoft executive, boldly predicted in 2008 that Windows Mobile would capture 40% of the market share by 2012.

Afterwards, Microsoft claimed there was no post-PC era, asserting that smartphones and tablets were merely companion devices supplementing the evergreen PC. Frank Shaw, Microsoft's vice president of communications, declared two years ago:

"Experts love to predict the demise of things, claiming we're in a post-PC era, but we believe the PC, which is just 30 years old, isn't even middle-aged yet."

Next came the negotiation: Microsoft launched a tablet with PC functionality, actually producing two Surface devices, one with an ARM processor and the other with a traditional Intel CPU.

The current Microsoft accepts reality: we are in a post-PC era. PCs won't disappear in the short term, but the golden age of double-digit growth for 30 consecutive years is over. We are now a device and services company!

This is a fairly logical slogan: devices create demand for Microsoft services, and in turn, services drive demand for devices. This is a great cycle of synergy.

Dilemma

But lurking behind this is a potentially serious issue that could severely harm the company: Microsoft wants to continue licensing software to hardware manufacturers while simultaneously building a competing device business against those licensees. They want to have both.

Undertaking a true business model transformation is dangerous. By true transformation, I mean not just adding a line of accessory products, but moving to a new business model that could harm the old one. Revenue streams from the old model might dry up before the new model can fully replace it, causing a performance trough.

For publicly traded companies, this kind of drying up is unacceptable. Some companies did not attempt a transformation, continuing to face Wall Street criticism and gradually fading away. Others sought privatization, stepping out of the public eye to focus on operations, with Dell being a good example.

Microsoft cannot completely abandon its existing business model; it cannot stop selling software licenses to hardware manufacturers. However, it also realizes that if it wants to avoid being left out of the tablet and smartphone battle, it must take building its own devices seriously.

The main reason for Microsoft's predicament is Android. Android is so cheap (if not entirely free) that it can eliminate Microsoft's mobile licensing business. (Thanks to its patent portfolio, Microsoft may receive some revenue from Android hardware manufacturers, but that doesn’t change the overall situation). This is why Microsoft initially offered “platform support fees” to Nokia, essentially giving them Windows Phone for free.

Another example of Microsoft trying to straddle the chasm between two worlds is the Surface tablet. It attempted to create a hybrid device that is both the best PC and the best tablet, offering two different user interfaces within the Surface. The Surface lacks the usability and agility of a pure tablet and cannot provide the productivity benefits of a Windows 7 PC. How much the new version of Windows 8.1 will help remains to be seen.

So, what about the position of Microsoft's new CEO? He or she will find the company undergoing complex structural and cultural restructuring. Traditional PC business growth is slowing due to the encroachment of mobile devices. Longtime OEM partners are dissatisfied with the company's new direction. While conducting the Surface tablet experiment, Microsoft must also retain those partners. If Surface succeeds, Microsoft may abandon traditional PC manufacturers; otherwise, it will warmly embrace them. Due to the acquisition of Nokia's phone business, the biggest customer for the Windows Phone licensing business has been lost. Integrating Nokia will be very challenging. The Windows Phone system needs a boost, including the launch of a tablet version to compete with Android manufacturers and the iPad. Talent must be retained. Shareholders must be retained as well.

Now, how do you feel about this job?
