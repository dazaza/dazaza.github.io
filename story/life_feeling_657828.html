I buried you, but I'm still living in the past.

by mimi456 on 2012-01-28 22:04:35

I buried you, but I still live in the past.

After a hundred years, you quietly lie in a cold box, forgetting the future you promised.

Are you asleep?

At exactly 8:40, I received a call from mom. The vibration of the phone numbed the cold table as I stared at that familiar number. Suddenly, I remembered the request I made to mom before leaving home: if grandpa didn't make it, she must call me immediately. After answering the phone, mom's gloomy voice came from the other end, urging me to ask for leave right away.

Leaving the office, I had a thin piece of paper in my hand, showing 8:43. My heart felt heavy, hanging in my chest cavity, and my legs began trembling uncontrollably. I was scared.

Standing at the last corner before home, I saw many people downstairs with red eyes and sorrowful faces. Entering the dimly lit shop on the ground floor, my mood became even darker. The air was filled with the greasy smell of cooking oil and the pungent odor of medicine.

My eyes passed over countless falling tears until they finally stopped at where you lay. You were quietly lying on a small bed, half-covered by the soft blanket grandma prepared for you, still wearing your usual blue shirt. But I could no longer hear the sound of your breathing mixed with the friction in your throat while receiving oxygen. You were so quiet, as if sleeping.

I turned my head away, afraid to look at you any longer because I feared crying, fearing grandma would become even more upset. Suddenly, my heart tightened, and tears involuntarily filled my eyes, spreading across my entire eyeball. Leaving the room where you lay, I faced the pale sky like dad did that day, raising my head to prevent the tears from falling. My emotions were tangled like a mess of threads, impossible to cut or sort out. Memories of the past, those sorrows, those joys, left me lost.

Returning to the room where you lay, you were now surrounded by them as they dressed you in your final new clothes. Your hands hung limply by your side, your body, drained of nutrients by cancer cells, was like the bare branches left after leaves return to their roots in late autumn. The flesh that should have firmly enveloped your bones was now loosely hanging, painful and shaking under the weight, only held together by a layer of wrinkled yellow skin. That wise head, accumulated with countless knowledge over the years, was tightly embraced by uncle. The deep grooves carved by seventy-three years of time resembled rivers, shallow and deep. Your eyes remained closed, refusing to open again.

You sat there silently. Wearing eleven layers of long robes, seven layers of long pants, seventy-three waistbands, and a long hat, you looked just like an ancient scholar who passed the imperial examination. But if you could wake up, I wouldn't care if you were a scholar or even wore a blue robe.

Ice coffin, red pillow, funeral attire, incense candles, paper money - my mind went blank. While setting up the mourning hall, dad instructed me to stay by your side, fearing someone might maliciously throw things into your box. So, I took a bench and sat beside you, guarding you without letting anyone harm you. The debate about the outcomes we once touched upon ultimately came too early for you, as you said, facing intense pain and the threat of death, you remained calm. But suddenly, I felt deeply sad. The full heartache suppressed inside could no longer be hindered by external factors. I buried my head deeply, allowing tears to flow freely down my cheeks, leaving long tear marks. The eighteen years' worth of memories emerged vividly through my tears. The little scale rod you made for me, the personal income tax you "paid" me every time we played mahjong, each game of chess we played, every birthday gift you bought me on the second day of the New Year... I suddenly remembered a poem that gazed upon us, then transformed into our story. Years of emotion turned into a cold coffin, with you inside and me outside... Then, I realized how many of those past events I could no longer recall, and how my tears had already overflowed.

Isn't it cold inside the ice coffin?

At noon, I went upstairs and ate lunch. Closing the bedroom door, I stopped crying and lay down, trying to calm myself. Then, I silently repeated in my heart, you're just asleep. Closing my eyes, everything went blank, and I drifted off into a daze. When I woke up, you would still be lying in bed waiting for me to see you, right?

It felt like being in a three-dimensional space that stood out, floating aimlessly without a place to land, until the noisy sounds brought me back. After a hasty wash, I went downstairs, expecting to still hear the sound of your breathing in the empty shop, but there was none. The repetitive blessings kept playing from inside, you weren't on the bed, you were in the ice coffin.

The dark blue rectangular screen kept changing numbers, showing 20.5. I truly hoped you could climb out of the ice coffin and tell me it was cold, and you didn't like it.

I went to school and sat on a wooden stool, but my thoughts were not in the classroom. Those cold words couldn't bind my heart, which constantly thought of you.

How many times I walked around your coffin, hoping you weren't inside.

My chess master, where are you?

Sitting in the mourning hall, ignoring the voices around me, I suddenly remembered the two games of chess we played a month ago. Through thirteen years of storms, on the sandy battlefield of the Chu River, I encountered countless enemies, but I always ended up defeated under your feet. However, that day, I took advantage of your declining physical and mental state. If I had known your departure would be so hurried, I should have listened to dad and found ways to keep losing to you. Now, besides regret, what else is there?

The chessboard you made, the wooden board you personally cut, the nails you hammered in, the grids you drew, the Han boundary and Chu river you wrote - it's not here. It's in the last foreign home where you stayed. I decided to cherish it, keeping it with me as I grow old.

I want to play another game with you, but you're gone from the opposite seat.

If crying could bring you back

On the eve of your farewell, many people came. We, draped in long mourning clothes, stood in front of your altar. Your friends and colleagues read thick piles of manuscripts, revealing the person you were in their hearts. I didn't cry, instead, I admired you even more. I read your memoirs, over twenty thousand words, three times, gaining something new each time. The bridges, reservoirs, and power stations you directed construction on, everyone who read your memoirs admired you greatly.

When dad gave his thank-you speech, I couldn't hold back my tears anymore. The delayed call of fatherhood carried too much regret. Dad's trembling voice, mixed with sobs and tears, made many people cry, filled with reluctance.

Mom clutched your portrait, her face contorted with grief, spilling out the love you had for her, treating her closer than a daughter. Yes, for years, our family loved each other dearly, burying all sadness beneath endless joy. And you always appeared before us as a gentle, kind, and hardworking figure, calmly serving as our strong support and spiritual pillar.

Suddenly, you were gone, and no matter how much we cried, we couldn't bring you back.

I send you off to an unknown future

At four o'clock in the morning, they carried you out and placed you in the car. Dressed in mourning attire, fifteen cars sandwiched you in the middle, sending you away from here. I randomly got into one of the cars, leaning against the backrest of the seat, gradually falling asleep amidst the swaying turns.

When I woke up, we had arrived at the only crematorium. Many cars had already reached the entrance before us. We got out of the car to take one last look at you in the farewell room. You lay on the crematorium's stretcher, covered by the quilt dad had prepared for you a month ago. Your eyes were tightly shut, your face covered with a thin layer of water mist. In the blink of an eye, you were pushed away by the crematorium staff. Gathered in front of the TV showing the video feed, I caught one last glimpse of you, suddenly feeling a sense of fear. Could it be that you were really gone?

Sitting on a plastic chair at the entrance of the crematorium for an hour and a half, you finally came out, but now you were a pile of disordered ashes. Dad carefully wrapped you in a red cloth, holding you as he walked out of the crematorium, got into the car, and called me to ride with you in the same vehicle.
