Apple is worried? Bazinga!

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 20:24:29

“I’ve been on too many roller coasters. I don’t get overly excited when it goes up, and I don’t think about slitting my wrists when it goes down,” Tim Cook said in his usual mild tone, as if he had no worries at all.

This statement was made the day after this year’s Apple new product launch. At that time, the market, or rather the stock trading market, was disappointed with its performance, causing Apple's stock price to drop 5% quickly, and the stock price for the year had already fallen 10% compared to last year.

Falling stock prices, market reactions not meeting expectations... Maybe you're imagining a picture of Apple's senior management deeply worried? But perhaps the answer is: Bazinga! (Which means "just kidding," originally from the game Bubble Dragon and later popularized by the TV series The Big Bang Theory). Bloomberg specifically visited three senior executives of Apple shortly after the release event: Tim Cook, Chief Designer Jony Ive, and Senior Vice President of Software Engineering Craig Federighi. The most interesting part of this interview was undoubtedly the strategic insight of Apple's senior management into the “market segmentation”, their understanding of "innovation", and their exceptionally calm and composed attitude.

“We don’t do junk business.”

Even though the sentence looks so decisive in writing, during the interview, the 52-year-old Cook delivered it in his signature soft-spoken manner. In Cook's view, competition in the smartphone market will not be reduced to a price war to the bottom but will instead become "fragmented" — a portion of the market will extend towards lower-end pricing. Cook said there will always be parts of the market filled with junk (junk part).

We don’t do junk business. I won't lose sleep over this (low-price) market because it’s not our positioning. Fortunately, (both high-end and affordable markets) are large, and many people care about superior experiences with phones and tablets, so Apple can have a very nice market share.

In the sixth year since the release of the first iPhone, Apple understands that it is difficult for them to "dominate" the market alone anymore. Due to the mergers of major giants and the rise of emerging low-cost devices, Apple’s current competitors include Samsung, Google, Microsoft, as well as Xiaomi representing "low cost" and "high cost-effectiveness" and Micromax from India. After this launch, many questioned whether Apple's "high-price" strategy is sustainable or if they are avoiding the issue. However, Cook clearly stated this time that Apple has never intended to make "low-end" products, which does not align with the brand's positioning.

Doesn’t this perspective sound familiar to anyone? In 2004, Jobs once said that Apple’s market share was already larger than BMW or Porsche’s in the automotive market, so what’s wrong with BMW and Porsche? Nothing.

"New" is easy, but "right" is hard

Talking about innovation, designer Jony Ive and Senior Vice President of Software Engineering Craig Federighi both believe that successful collaboration is the key to creating good products. In their view, the success of their collaboration lies in their shared pursuit of usability and simplicity. When they just entered the interview room, they weren’t discussing how Apple listens to market feedback and opinions, but rather spent ten minutes talking about how they pushed the iOS 7 blur background effect to its extreme and what individual users really consider when using products.

When discussing "fingerprint recognition," Jony Ive took the opportunity to express his views on "innovation":

Many people at best treat innovation as some kind of "illusory" gimmick. But we don’t speculatively add 10 technologies that are merely "worth a try" to our feature list.

Just after Jony Ive finished speaking, Craig Federighi added in perfect harmony: "New" is easy, but "right" is hard.

The outside world has long questioned whether Apple has "stopped innovating," and Jony Ive and Craig Federighi’s point is that while new features are important, they also need to be deeply integrated into the original system before being presented to consumers. For this reason, they invested a great deal of effort into the deep integration of "fingerprint recognition" with the iPhone 5s.

To sum up, regarding Apple's innovation and product strategy, a segment from Forrester Research analyst Charles Golvin is quite interesting: "We are in a phase where Apple is not falling behind, but everyone is emphasizing the low-end smartphone market." Such an Apple going against the flow does not intend to compromise, just like Cook at the beginning of the article, who also does not plan to grieve over "stock prices:"

Am I happy with such a stock price? Of course not. But I need to consider whether "we are doing the right thing" rather than letting others and the market dictate my feelings.
