Former A-League player confirms assistance in investigation, wide-ranging involvement may become a new breakthrough

by yinding999 on 2009-12-16 18:11:08

Former player of the Jia-A League confirms assisting in the investigation, involving a wide range that could become a new breakthrough

According to reports, Xing Rui, a Qingdao-born player who once played for Shandong Taishan, Chengdu Wuniu, and Changchun Yatai, has been confirmed to have gone to Shenyang to assist the police in their investigation. As a midfielder who was once very famous during the Jia-A era, plus being exposed after retirement as an illegal gambling bookie, Xing Rui's "assisting in the investigation" may likely become a new breakthrough for the "anti-gambling and anti-fake match storm" entering the Chinese Super League (CSL).

Once Renowned

Xing Rui might be an unfamiliar name to fans who started following Chinese professional football after 2000. However, as one of the first players to participate in Chinese professional football leagues, Xing Rui was absolutely a well-known name in the mid-1990s: from 1987, he represented Shandong in the Jia-A League. After Chinese football entered the professional league era in 1994, Xing Rui had already become the captain of Shandong Taishan. At that time, the midfield attacking line formed by him, Tang Xiaocheng, and Su Maozhen was once highly renowned in the Jia-A League. Especially after the 1995 league season ended, with 7 goals scored, Xing Rui led Shandong Taishan to win the FA Cup championship and was also rated as one of the "Top Ten Midfield Engines" in the league, alongside Ma Mingyu, Cao Xiandong, Gao Zhongxun, and others.

However, after Luneng took over Shandong football, Xing Rui also entered the late stage of his career. He no longer served as the captain of Shandong team and gradually couldn't enter the main lineup. In 1999, Xing Rui chose to leave Luneng and join Chengdu Wuniu, which was then in the Jia-B League. Later, he transferred to Changchun Yatai and announced his retirement in 2002.

Post-Retirement Involvement in Gambling

After retirement, Xing Rui returned to his hometown of Qingdao and went into business, but it didn't go smoothly. An insider revealed that Xing Rui became a "small bookie" in Qingdao, where gambling on football matches was becoming increasingly prevalent. Although there is no definitive evidence to confirm this, given Xing Rui's identity and professional experience, he had ample opportunities to contact multiple CSL and China League One (CL1) teams in Shandong, as there were many former teammates and "youngsters" among the teams like Luneng, Zhongneng, and Hailifeng. Due to the widespread presence of Shandong players across the country, Xing Rui's influence could potentially extend to the entire CSL and CL1 leagues. It would be easy for him as a "bookie" to bribe players to manipulate matches.

Reports indicate that after the police disclosed the fake match case between Hailifeng and Chengdu Football Club, Wang Souye, who had served as the general manager of Hailifeng since 2007, was summoned by the police to Shenyang for investigation, followed by Xing Rui being summoned. Since Xing Rui embodies too many elements of interest, if what he is involved in is not just the fake match and gambling case of Hailifeng, but also involves the other two CSL teams within Shandong province—Shandong Luneng and Qingdao Zhongneng—it will definitely become a new breakthrough in the anti-gambling and anti-fake match storm.

Since the start of the "anti-gambling and anti-fake match" campaign, Qingdao has always been at the center of the storm: from the fake match between Qingdao Hailifeng and Chengdu Football Club, to Hailifeng owner Du Yunqi fleeing abroad and the arrests of Leng Bo, Liu Hongwei, and others, as well as Wang Souye and Xing Rui's "assisting in the investigation." Speculations outside have been ongoing: Qingdao's football gambling trend is unrivaled nationwide, making it a key city in this "anti-gambling and anti-fake match" storm. Moreover, Qingdao has always been a cradle of Chinese football, not only having its own CSL and CL1 teams, but Qingdao-born players are spread all over the country, potentially forming a "Qingdao system fake gambling chain."
