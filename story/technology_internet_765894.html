Microsoft Bing VP of R&D: Bing is no worse than anyone else

by anonymous on 2013-08-29 16:28:13

Although Microsoft's Bing has a much smaller market share than Google, Microsoft executives are not willing to appear inferior. Harry Shum, Vice President of Bing Research at the company, declared that Bing search is on par with anyone and even excels in some features.

According to the U.S. tech news site CNET, since its launch, Microsoft’s search engine Bing has been battling for four years and has achieved modest success. Market research firm comScore claims that during these four years, its share of the U.S. search market has doubled from 8.9% to 17.9%. Moreover, it provides support for Yahoo's search services. However, Bing has not been able to defeat Google, which remains the king of search with over 65% of the U.S. market share. A larger market share means more revenue, as well as more search requests and click data, which in turn helps improve its search results.

Nevertheless, Microsoft executives have not shown any signs of feeling inferior. "After years of effort by the Bing team, our search quality is now on par with anyone, and we even excel in some functions," said Harry Shum, Vice President of Bing Research. He specifically mentioned Bing's image search function, believing it surpasses Google. Currently, image searches account for 10% of all Bing search requests.

In a blog post, Microsoft compared the results presented by Google and Bing for certain image search requests. For example, searching for "Prince Albert of Monaco" on Bing will only show photos of the prince; whereas on Google, it shows pictures of the prince with others. Bing believes that the user's intent is to find photos of the prince, not of any other people, and they do not need duplicate images.

Stefan Weitz, head of the Bing department, explained improvements in Bing's search functions in a video, saying that Bing determines their search intent by mining user network clicks and social information, as well as using Microsoft's Satori semantic database.

"We study what people search for and what they click on, then use computers to analyze their intent and improve our search results," Shum said. However, Google's search receives far more search requests and click data daily, theoretically giving it an advantage in understanding user intent. Like Microsoft, Google also has a large semantic database called Knowledge Graph, which is used to enhance relevance and present related information.

Bing also employs computer vision technology (such as detecting skin, faces, and gender) to understand the content within images. "Using computer vision technology, we can identify duplicate photos, remove them, and display other different ones," Shum said. Additionally, Microsoft uses neural network technology (such as sunsets, mountains, or Mickey Mouse) to better categorize images.

Although Bing lags behind Google in big data and market share, it considers itself more perfect. The Bing homepage displays cool images, while Google only has an ordinary search box; Bing's search result page presents more relevant requests and topics, along with high-definition images, compared to Google.

Both Bing and Google search have advanced auto-complete functions that suggest what you might be searching for as you type, which is an effective way to speed up entering your request information.

However, unlike Google, Bing lacks voice input functionality. Google Now and Apple Siri can understand certain natural language commands, allowing them to schedule meetings or search for nearby hotels based on your location, or even chat with you. With user permission, Google Now can also scan their email, calendar, and other activities to predict needed information, such as how long it will take to drive home after work, or reminding you if your booked flight changes.

"Internally, we've discussed when to release new things. We could release something similar to theirs now, but it wouldn't be the most advanced yet. It would be too rushed to launch now," Weitz said last month. "We'll wait until we develop something revolutionary before releasing it."
