How did Facebook and Twitter get so big?

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 15:53:31

The well-known American financial website MarketWatch published an article today stating that as a new marketing model, "Growth Hacking" is one of the key factors that has helped companies like Facebook and Twitter grow significantly.

The article points out that through this integrated technical and marketing model, these companies can achieve tremendous growth with very low advertising expenditures. This has become a new developmental trajectory in Silicon Valley, with brands like Amazon already starting to explore this model.

Below is the full text of the article:

In 2012, Microsoft's advertising expenditure reached $1.6 billion; in comparison, Facebook's advertising expenditure for the same year was only $67 million. Perhaps no one knows exactly how much Facebook will spend on advertising this year, but it will definitely be less than Microsoft. Previous reports indicated that Microsoft's advertising expenditure for 2013 would reach $2.6 billion.

Of course, the businesses these two companies are involved in are entirely different, and Microsoft's revenue far exceeds that of Facebook. But here, I want to talk less about Microsoft and more about Facebook and similar companies. When comparing Microsoft and Facebook, the former adopts a traditional sales and growth model, while the latter uses a completely new model.

How does Facebook manage to grow its company with such minimal advertising expenditure? Over the past three years, the company’s total marketing and advertising expenditure has been only $100 million, yet during the same period, its user base has increased from 600 million to over 1 billion people. Moreover, these over 1 billion users have deeply entrenched themselves in the "trenches" created by Facebook, becoming deeply rooted users of this social network, rather than just superficial ones. Undoubtedly, Facebook's performance in acquiring users can almost be described as unprecedented.

Similar high-growth trajectories can also be seen in other already-listed tech startups, whether it be social gaming company Zynga, group-buying giant Groupon, or professional networking site LinkedIn, all of which have followed this trajectory to develop rapidly. In those companies that have been acquired, there also exists this high-growth trajectory with low expenditure, such as Tumblr, which has been acquired by Yahoo!, and Instagram, which has been acquired by Facebook. Furthermore, unlisted startups such as Twitter, ride-hailing app Uber, and cloud storage service provider Dropbox are also similar.

Compared to most other companies, these young companies can acquire hundreds of millions of new users at an extremely low cost, and these users represent either current or future customers.

So, how do these startups manage to achieve this? The secret lies in a little-known philosophy known as "Growth Hacking".

In Silicon Valley and its ecosystem, Growth Hacking is opening up a new path, fundamentally changing the marketing strategies and expenditure models of companies. For investors - whether they become investors by purchasing company stocks or through venture capital and angel investment channels - this model has profound implications and inspiration. Growth Hacking not only changes the way companies grow in the future, but also changes the overall economic landscape of company growth.

The Future of Marketing

Let's listen to what Andrew Chen has to say; he is an advisor to many such startups and also an influential tech commentator. "A 'Growth Hacker' is a mix between a marketer and a programmer who focuses on the traditional question of 'how can I find customers for my product', then answers this question through A/B testing, landing pages, viral factors, email deliverability, and real-time sharing platforms like Open Graph."

"In addition, they establish layered direct marketing disciplines, focusing on quantifiable standards, imagining scenarios through spreadsheets, and conducting extensive database queries," Andrew Chen added.

There is no need to focus particularly on this tech buzzword itself; what you need to know is that this special work is now one of the hottest and most sought-after positions in the tech industry. As a result, marketing and marketing expenditures have gained a far broader yet highly effective definition. In this definition, not only press releases and billboards are considered marketing tools, but almost anything – from product development to product optimization, and even managing distribution partners – can be regarded as "marketing" as long as it drives new users to use the company's services.

For investors, this is certainly good news. And for companies, the question of which "half" of marketing expenditures is wasted is no longer a concern worth knowing. This is because "Growth Hackers" do not tolerate wasteful behavior.

In the marketing industry, self-discipline and shifting direction based on results rather than engaging in "creative" sustainable spending is not a normal practice. However, for tech startups like Facebook and Twitter, this practice is increasingly being adopted.

Adopters of the New Model

Currently, which companies are adopting the "Growth Hacking" model? In fact, any company in Silicon Valley is trying to open this "door." If tens of millions of new users suddenly appear out of nowhere, it is likely due to the efforts of "Growth Hackers."

For example, over the past several years, Facebook has deliberately not formed a formal marketing team, instead investing resources into a "growth team" whose responsibility is to help the company grow, rather than "boosting awareness" or "building brand attention share."

Dropbox is another excellent example; 40% of its customers do not come from traditional advertising but from an industry-leading referral program. Currently, the company's valuation has exceeded $4 billion, and it has become a potential acquisition target for Apple and others. I believe that very effective "Growth Hacking" is one of the important factors that has enabled Dropbox to achieve such remarkable growth.

Groupon is one of the fastest-growing companies in history. The company launched a plan that saved tens of millions of dollars in advertising costs. The plan was that users could earn a $10 reward for every friend they invited to register for Groupon's email list and engage in one shopping interaction.

Let's look at Uber's example. The company recently received a $250 million investment from Google. Google should be able to imagine that this investment will not be used for expensive and relatively ineffective television advertisements, as so far, Uber has grown rapidly through a series of well-executed "stunts," such as giving away free ice cream and presenting roses to passengers. Additionally, by offering free rides, the company will gain tens of thousands of new customers in Austin next spring. From this perspective, it is not surprising that Uber has grown from a company that initially received only $200,000 in angel funding to its current scale.

Conclusion

"Growth Hacking" must be the future of marketing. This is because startups that receive only $20,000 in angel funding from Paul Graham's Y Combinator simply cannot afford five-digit monthly marketing expenses, nor can they hire PR firms. Even worse, some companies cannot even afford a full-time marketing person.

In this situation, the burden of startup marketing can only fall on engineers, founders, and customers themselves. As a result, these people use their skills to completely change the "rules of the game." They can create a data-driven, shortcut-focused, platform-centered marketing model that is more suitable for today's environment than the marketing strategies of the "Mad Men" era.

This strategy is now reshaping the developmental trajectories of many well-known brands, from Zappos, the world's largest online shoe store, to Amazon, and from Facebook to LinkedIn. All aspects of these brands are being influenced by this marketing strategy, including their balance sheets and how customers use their products.

For investors, the task now is to try to understand this new future and learn how to tap its potential. "Growth Hacking" is a mindset, and companies with this mindset will achieve incredible growth. You would be willing to invest in companies with a "Growth Hacking" model.
