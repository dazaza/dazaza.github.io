The video industry has already seen fierce competition, and web series are gaining popularity over TV dramas.

by hoouk260 on 2012-03-03 20:15:47

The video industry is getting bloody. Recently, Liu Hong of Le Shi Network lamented that "the video industry has already become bloody." His lament is not baseless. Recently, major domestic video companies have been acquiring film and television companies, making large-scale purchases of film and television copyrights at prices that are jaw-dropping. Sohu's reputation for burning money is well-known. It is said that Sohu spent 30 million yuan to purchase the "New Princess Pearl" series, and even spent 70 million yuan on promotion. In total, Sohu spent a hundred million yuan on just one TV series. The price of film and television copyrights is rising rapidly, with prices changing daily, but the production parties are becoming increasingly picky. For popular TV dramas, the price isn't even discussed during the pre-production phase; it's only sold just before release to get the highest price possible. For video websites, what's even more terrifying is that while expenses are increasing, revenue hasn't increased correspondingly. Since most video websites haven't yet gained the attention of the mainstream audience, their advertising value is greatly underestimated. Compared to TV advertisements, online video advertisements can only be considered as "cheap goods." Moreover, due to insufficient ad inventory, the high copyright fees cannot be absorbed, becoming a "financial black hole" for video websites. Buying copyrights has become the most headache-inducing issue for video executives - investing huge sums is a path to ruin, but not buying is waiting to die. The video industry has started to come together for warmth. Letv and Tudou established a joint venture, Sohu and MSN formed a horizontal alliance, all representing industry collaborations. Sina Video has Sina Weibo behind it, iQIYI has Baidu, Ku6 has Shanda, and 56.com has connected with Renren. Micro-films have become popular. People have begun to reminisce about short videos from the video-sharing era, and a kind of micro-film with similar size to short videos has started to gain popularity. The video industry is returning to short videos. Recently, Lingsi Communication's Film Center produced the micro-film "There’s a Ghost in Weibo," releasing three works: "Witness," "Private Message," and "@Someone." After premiering on Weibo, these works were subsequently launched on 56.com's premiere event. Currently, the click count for just "Witness" has exceeded 600,000. This year, over 30 micro-films have premiered on 56.com, including works from professional film institutions and studios, as well as excellent low-budget productions by individual directors. Notably, people prefer watching micro-films on 56.com, which champions grassroots spirit. Comparing single movie and cumulative play counts and comment numbers of three movies, user activity on 56.com is significantly higher than on Sohu Video and Ku6. Video sharing sites characterized by grassroots gatherings, love for original content, and sharing are regaining their unique advantages through micro-films. Returning to short videos has become a trend. Ku6 has exited the copyright war, increasing its efforts on short videos and UGC (User-Generated Content). NetEase and Sina have parted ways with Sohu, each sticking to their own short video paths. Renren's acquisition of 56.com for 80 million US dollars was precisely due to its advantage in the short video field. The winds of fortune in the video industry keep changing, and in the era of copyright burnout, short videos and micro-films have once again become darlings. Why are micro-films so popular? Actually, the reason micro-films are popular is similar to why Weibo became popular. Micro-films require small investments, so big productions like martial arts, police action, or military themes aren't suitable for micro-films. Instead, depicting the trivialities of everyday life and recording the details of people's lives is more likely to resonate with netizens. "Play Big" depicts the process of three post-80s growing up playing games, with very delicate plots comparable to mainstream films. Such themes are hard to find in mainstream films and easily gain recognition from post-80s audiences. Micro-films, like Weibo, gain attention by documenting everyday life. Micro-films, like Weibo, are short with more compact plots, avoiding the verbosity and redundancy of "big films." Movies have become a fast-food culture, where there's no need to go to the cinema; they can premiere and be viewed online whenever desired. Plots ranging from a few minutes to ten or fifteen minutes make micro-films akin to fast-food culture, just like Weibo. Micro-films also have a wide range of uses. Besides being a hobby for aspiring directors and photography enthusiasts, they can also be used for marketing and promotion. In my software circle, many companies like to promote their products using the "micro-film" format. For instance, Kingsoft created a parody time-travel movie when launching WPS2012; Storm Player promoted their "left-eye button" via a micro-film. Recently, Nokia even collaborated with Fan Bingbing to shoot a micro-film for the N9. Some worry that micro-films lack a model, but in reality, behind micro-films lies "micro-marketing" - embedded advertising. Incorporating clips introducing corporate products and features or company logos and images into micro-films, as long as it's done naturally, users won't resent it but will subconsciously remember the product and company. Its effect far surpasses those hard advertisements that viewers instinctively switch channels or close upon seeing. Micro-films allow companies to invest relatively little capital in producing content more interesting than advertisements, achieving much more effective promotional results.
