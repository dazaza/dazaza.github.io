How I quit smoking effectively

by gddxp97717 on 2012-03-07 15:02:31

Indeed, quitting smoking is difficult for most people. After all, nicotine in cigarettes is a highly addictive drug. However, with the right methods, you can successfully quit smoking.

Firstly, congratulate yourself. Simply reading this article is a big step towards quitting smoking. Many people fail to quit because they think it's too hard, and they plan to quit at some point in the future.

The difficulty of kicking this habit

Smokers may start smoking because of their friends or because they think it looks cool. But they keep smoking because they are addicted to nicotine, a chemical found in cigarettes or smokeless tobacco.

Nicotine is both a stimulant and a sedative. This means that it speeds up the inhaler's heart rate, creating a sense of excitement (similar to caffeine, another stimulant). It then causes feelings of depression and fatigue. This depression and fatigue - and the drug derived from nicotine - prompts smokers to crave more cigarettes to regain the excitement. According to experts, nicotine in tobacco can be as addictive as cocaine or heroin.

But don't lose hope; millions of Americans have already quit smoking permanently. These methods can also help you.

Put it in writing

Those who want to make changes are usually more successful when they write it down. So write down all the reasons why you want to quit smoking, such as saving money or gaining more endurance during exercise. Keep this list somewhere visible, and add new reasons as they come up.

Seek help

People who have support from friends and family are more likely to successfully quit smoking. If you don't want your parents or family to know you smoke, tell your friends, find a trustworthy counselor, or someone you trust. If you can't find anyone to support you (for example, if all your friends smoke and have no intention of quitting), you can consider joining a support group, either in person or online.

More effective methods

Set a quit date

Choose a date when you will completely stop smoking, and tell your friends (or your family, if they know you smoke) that you will quit on that day. Treat that day as the dividing line between being a smoker and becoming a new, improved non-smoker. Mark that day on your calendar.

Get rid of all your cigarettes

Quitting is impossible if cigarettes are still around to tempt you. So throw away even those emergency cigarettes hidden in your backpack, along with your ashtrays and lighters.

Wash all your clothes

Dry clean your coats and sweaters, and wash all your other clothes to remove as much of the smoke smell as possible. If you've smoked in your car, clean it out too.

Remember the triggers

You might remember situations that make you think of smoking, like after meals, at your best friend's house, while drinking coffee, or while driving. These situations make you want a cigarette - they naturally remind you to smoke. Once you know these situations, try these methods:

- Avoid these situations

For example, if you like smoking while driving, start biking to school within a few weeks, or walk, or take the bus. If you usually smoke after meals, pay special attention to doing something else after eating, like reading or calling a friend.

- Change places

If you and your friends often smoke in restaurants, order takeout or eat in the car instead. When you go out to eat, choose to sit in the non-smoking area.

- Substitute cigarettes with something else

You might find it hard to get used to not having something in your mouth. If this is a problem for you, chew on carrot sticks, sugar-free gum, or mints, use a toothpick, or even a lollipop.

Dealing with setbacks

If you experience some setbacks, don't give up

Major changes sometimes start with false starts. If you're like many people, you may have successfully quit smoking for weeks or even months, but suddenly feel an overwhelming urge to smoke and think you must give up quitting. Or you might unexpectedly find yourself wanting to quit under certain circumstances or temptations.

If you encounter these issues, it doesn't mean you've failed. It just tells you that you're only human. Here are some ways to get back on track:

- View your setback as a mistake

Remember when and why it happened, then continue your journey to quit smoking.

- Did you become a smoker again after that one cigarette?

We don't think so. Instead, smokers gradually form over time. Remember, one cigarette won't turn you back into a smoker. So, one cigarette (or even two to three cigarettes) after successfully quitting won't rekindle your addiction.

- Remind yourself why you quit and how well you've done

Or let the members supporting your quit, family, or friends do this for you.

Reward yourself

As you know, quitting smoking is not an easy task. Give yourself a well-deserved prize! Save the money you would have spent on cigarettes, and when you've been tobacco-free for a week, two weeks, or a month, buy yourself a new CD, a book, or some clothes as a reward. Celebrate each quit anniversary. You earned it.

Physical and mental effects

Estimate the physical symptoms

If you smoke regularly, your body may already be dependent on nicotine. During the process of quitting, you may experience some uncomfortable symptoms, including:

- Headache or stomach ache

- Irritability, mood swings, or depression

- Fatigue

- Dry mouth or sore throat

- Craving to eat a lot

Fortunately, these symptoms caused by nicotine dependence pass quickly - so be patient. Don't give up for a quick fix, as that will prolong your discomfort.

Keep yourself busy

Many people find it easiest to quit on Monday because work or school keeps them busy. The more distracted you are, the less you'll crave smoking. Staying active is a great way to relieve stress and energize you, even while experiencing nicotine-induced discomfort.

Take it step by step

Some people find it helpful to reduce the number of cigarettes they smoke each day. However, this method doesn't work for everyone - you may find that you need to quit completely at once. This is known as "cold turkey."

Use nicotine replacement if needed

If none of the above methods and strategies work for you, talk to your doctor about nicotine replacement therapy. Nicotine replacements, such as gum, patches, inhalers, or nasal sprays, can be very helpful. Inhalers and nasal sprays require a prescription, and it's important to consult your doctor before buying patches or gum over the counter. This way, the doctor can help you find the best solution. For example, patches have minimal side effects but lack the instant nicotine hit that gum provides.
