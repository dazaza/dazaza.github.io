The total employed population in our country is 760 million, with 280 million employed in urban areas. The number of laborers flowing out from rural areas is 180 million, among which approximately 140 million are employed in towns and cities. 80% of students at the compulsory education stage are children of farmers. Among the 156 million students currently enrolled in schools nationwide, 110 million are in rural areas. The commonly mentioned 20 million left-behind children is actually not accurate; with the increase in migrant workers in recent years, the number of left-behind children has far exceeded 20 million. I estimate that it might be close to 30 million.

Issues regarding the education of migrant workers' children are very prominent. On one hand, there are issues concerning the education of children left behind in rural areas by their migrant worker parents. On the other hand, there are issues related to the education of children who move with their parents to their place of work.

(I) Issues regarding the education of children left behind in migrant worker-sending areas:

1. These children cannot live with their parents, lacking paternal and maternal love, leading to loneliness and depression. During our research, the Guangdong Provincial Department of Education reported that every Spring Festival after at Guangzhou railway station, cries would erupt in the station and carriages, some being fathers sending mother and child back to the countryside for the child to remain behind, others being parents sending their children back to study in the countryside. The parting at the station is a true reflection of this situation.

2. Due to financial difficulties in many places, the merging of rural schools has been too aggressive, causing many children to have to travel several kilometers to attend school. These children not only face long and unsafe journeys to school but also waste a lot of study time on the road.

3. A county-based school management system has led to some economically backward areas where schools still rely on a blackboard, chalk, and the teacher's mouth for teaching, just as they did over 50 years ago. In some places, English and computer classes cannot be opened as scheduled in the third grade of primary school according to national regulations, causing these children to fall behind their peers in cities and affluent areas right from the start of their education, a gap that will be difficult to close for life. This situation exists in places like Suiping County in Henan Province, Yongding County in Fujian Province, and Yulong County in Yunnan Province.

4. In some areas, educational resources are severely insufficient, leading to extremely poor learning and living environments for children. For example, in Nanyang City, Henan Province, only 11% of primary schools and 27% of middle schools meet the national standards for per capita school building area and sports field area; additionally, there are an excessive number of large classes. In 2006, there were five schools where each class had more than 100 students; many schools are still using dry toilets, such as in Suiping County, Henan Province, where 98% of schools use dry toilets.

5. The provinces where migrant workers' children are located are all underdeveloped regions with few universities and even fewer undergraduate institutions, and the conditions of universities are poor, making it even harder to get into university than walking a tightrope. Henan Province graduates 960,000 high school students each year, but only 6,000 can study outside the province; the number of higher education institutions per million people nationwide is 14.22, while in Henan Province, it is less than 9; the ratio of undergraduate to junior college in national higher education institutions is 6:4, while in Henan Province, it is only 4.5:5.5; Henan Province has a population of 100 million, but only one "211" university and no "985" universities; as the province that sends out the most migrant workers, it is extremely difficult for the children of Henan migrant workers to enter university.

(II) Issues regarding the education of children in migrant worker-receiving areas:

1. High housing prices and difficulty in renting make many migrant workers live in suburban areas, so their children cannot attend good schools and can only attend relatively poor ones. Some schools are almost like those in rural areas, and some are even worse than the rural schools in their home regions, making it difficult to ensure teaching quality.

2. There are few public schools, and most can only attend expensive private schools. For instance, in Dongguan, Guangdong, because the number of migrant workers is about six times that of local residents, public schools only accept local students. With government support, more than 240 private schools have been established. Tuition fees for these schools range from 1,500 to 2,500 yuan per semester, which is daunting for low-income migrant workers. It is understood that although more than 400,000 migrant children study here, only 20% are truly the children of migrant workers. Children studying in these private schools do not enjoy the "two exemptions and one subsidy", creating inequality in compulsory education.

3. Conditions in private schools contrast sharply with those in public schools. Taking Chang'an Town in Dongguan as an example, the town's annual fiscal revenue is 2.5 billion yuan, with a local population of 40,000 and a migrant population of 700,000. There are 13 private primary schools with 21,200 students and four private middle schools with 3,706 students. Public school teachers' annual salaries are: 108,900 yuan for primary school teachers and 120,000 yuan for middle school teachers. Private school teachers' salaries are only 2,000 yuan per month, or 24,000 yuan per year. Therefore, the level of teachers differs greatly when migrant workers' children attend school locally compared to public schools.

4. Migrant workers' children often face extortion when attending public schools locally. Thirty migrant workers sent their children to a local public school in Foshan, initially told there would be no charge, but later each parent was asked to pay a sponsorship fee of 18,000 yuan, and the local education bureau could do nothing about it. Some schools in Beijing refuse to enroll migrant workers' children citing lack of slots; if they want to enroll, they must pay a sponsorship fee of 20,000 yuan.

5. After completing junior high school locally, migrant workers' children cannot continue to high school. Due to the requirement to take the college entrance exam in their home province, many migrant workers' children have to leave their parents and return to their hometowns to attend high school, facing issues such as adapting to life and different curricula.

Six Suggestions Regarding the Education of Migrant Workers' Children:

1. Reform the "county-based" management system of compulsory education. Some counties in migrant worker-sending areas have fiscal revenues of only tens of millions or hundreds of millions of yuan. For counties with populations of hundreds of thousands, this is not enough to cover basic needs. Under the "county-based" management of education, some schools suffer from severe funding shortages and cannot improve their conditions. Therefore, it is suggested to change the "county-based" approach to having the state run compulsory education schools; alternatively, depending on local fiscal income, implement "county-based," "province-based," or "state-based" systems.

2. Vigorously promote the standardization of compulsory education school operations. China already has the conditions to promote the standardization of compulsory education schools, including student quotas, per capita classroom area, per capita office conditions, and per capita playground size.

3. Solve the boarding problem for left-behind migrant workers' children.

4. Strictly prohibit public schools in migrant worker-receiving areas from arbitrarily charging migrant workers' children. Arbitrary school fees have been prohibited repeatedly but continue unabated, especially in the compulsory education stage. To ensure smooth enrollment of migrant workers' children in local public schools, arbitrary fees must be strictly prohibited, and corrupt practices requiring "voluntary donations" from parents must be resolutely combated.

5. The state should implement the "two exemptions and one subsidy" policy for migrant workers' children attending private schools in migrant worker-receiving areas. The state should not only subsidize students but also support and subsidize private schools specifically serving migrant workers' children.

6. Resolutely reform the system of taking college entrance exams in one's home province. We are an open country; we can allow foreigners to study in China freely, yet we restrict our own children from taking college entrance exams elsewhere. University resources belong to the state, and students from all regions should share them equally. University admissions, exams, and enrollments should be unified. Doing so will help ensure fair development for every citizen and contribute to the overall development and social harmony of the country.

Currently, the urban-rural gap is gradually widening, with disparities in some regions reaching up to six times, and in some cases even exceeding ten times. At this point, if we cannot properly address the schooling problems of the children of migrant workers who have made great contributions to local economic development, it goes against the scientific development concept. Therefore, solving the education problems of migrant workers' children is urgent.
