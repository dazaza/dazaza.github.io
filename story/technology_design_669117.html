Comprehensive Guide to Cold Noodle Recipes from Around China

As the weather gets hotter, you may lose your appetite for regular meals or simply not feel like cooking. A refreshing and easy-to-make cold noodle dish is just what you need. You've probably noticed that in Beijing, Yanchi cold noodle shops and Xin Chuan noodle restaurants are always packed this time of year.

While noodles are traditionally a northern Chinese specialty, cold noodles have been adapted in various ways across the country, incorporating local tastes, ingredients, and preparation methods. Examples include Sichuan's "red oil cold noodles", Shaanxi's "dog meat cold noodles", Shandong's "sesame paste cold noodles", Guangdong's "egg cold noodles", Shanghai's "thick noodle salad", Shanxi's "willow leaf cold noodles", Tianjin's "braised soup cold noodles", Beijing's "vegetable medley cold noodles", Lanzhou's "clear broth beef cold noodles", Hubei's "fried sauce cold noodles", and more.

Cold noodles were invented in China as early as the Tang Dynasty, and it's said Empress Wu Zetian discovered them. Legend has it that before entering the palace, Wu Zetian burned her tongue eating hot Shanxi noodles with her lover, leading them to develop a new way to eat cold noodles. Originally called "water-crossed noodles", they were referred to in ancient times as "cold scooping".

The famous poet Du Fu wrote a poem called "Locust Leaf Cold Scooping": "Green locust leaves high above, gathered and delivered to the kitchen. Fresh flour comes from the market nearby, juice and residue perfectly blended... Cooler than snow on the teeth, I urge you to throw these pearls." The "locust leaf cold noodles" mentioned by the poet refers to green noodles made by mixing fresh locust leaf juice into the dough, which naturally gives a cooling sensation when served chilled after cooking.

Below, we'll introduce several regional cold noodle varieties for you to enjoy all summer:

Beijing - Sesame Paste Cold Noodles

In summer, many households in Beijing enjoy sesame paste cold noodles because they're both refreshing and satisfying. For a long time, the image of older men holding a bowl of cold noodles in one hand and chopsticks in the other, with a cucumber on the side, became representative of life in Beijing's hutongs.

To make Beijing-style sesame paste cold noodles, essential ingredients include prepared sesame paste, Sichuan pepper oil, garlic juice, aged vinegar, shredded cucumber, and optionally sesame salt and radish shreds. One crucial element is an electric fan - after boiling and rinsing the noodles in cold water, blowing them with a fan makes them smooth and non-sticky.

When you take a bite of the sesame paste cold noodles mixed with various seasonings, you first feel the coolness, followed by the fragrant flavors of sesame, Sichuan pepper, garlic, and cucumber, stimulating your appetite.

Ingredients: Machine-cut noodles or homemade dough, sesame paste, cucumber (or radish, celery), fine salt, cold boiled water, vinegar, garlic.

Method: In a bowl, mix sesame paste with a little salted cold water (dissolve appropriate amount of fine salt in cold boiled water), stir gently clockwise with chopsticks until water is absorbed. Add more salted cold water until it reaches a mud-like consistency. Boil noodles until cooked, rinse in cold boiled water, place in a bowl, and blow with a fan while tossing. Wash and shred cucumber, radish or blanch celery as desired. Serve noodles in a bowl topped with vegetables, vinegar, garlic, and seasoned sesame paste.

Sichuan - Chicken Shred Cold Noodles

Chicken shred cold noodles are a traditional Sichuan snack popular throughout the province. These can be made with machine-made noodles boiled until firm, then cooled quickly with a drizzle of vegetable oil to prevent sticking.

To prepare, toss cold noodles with a seasoning sauce made from chili powder, Sichuan pepper oil, vinegar, monosodium glutamate, soy sauce, minced garlic, and sesame oil, then top with shredded chicken breast. Variations include mung bean sprout cold noodles - sprouts blanched until crisp-tender, topped with cold noodles and similar seasoning sauce - and three-shred cold noodles featuring chicken, ham, and pork shreds.

Ingredients: Cooked chicken shreds, mung beans, five-spice rice, scallions, thin noodles, sesame paste, soy sauce, vinegar, sugar, ginger crumbs, garlic crumbs, sesame oil, chili oil, 1/2 teaspoon Sichuan pepper powder.

Method: Cook noodles in boiling water until done, drain and mix with 1 tablespoon each of salad oil and sesame oil, cool with a fan. Blanch mung bean sprouts until half-cooked, drain and squeeze dry, place in bottom of bowl. Top with cooled noodles and chicken shreds (chicken breast cooked, cooled and shredded). In a bowl, dilute sesame paste with water, add all seasonings, pour over noodles, garnish with scallions and crushed five-spice peanuts.

Shanxi - Northern Cold Oat Noodles

In northwest China, oat noodles are a common everyday food that can be steamed, fried, boiled, or served cold. Actually, oat flour is very nutritious, being a herbaceous plant with long pointed leaves, pale green flowers, and spiky seed heads. Oats are drought and cold resistant, suitable for planting in cold-dry regions. In China, major oat production areas include Wulanchabu in Inner Mongolia, Yunnanbei in Shanxi, and Zhangjiakou in Hebei.

Abroad, oat flour is typically used to make breads and cookies. In China, oats are made into various dishes like rolled oat cakes, oat nests, oat noodles, and oat fish shapes, which can be steamed, fried, boiled, or served cold. A particularly refreshing summer option is cold oat noodles with sour-spicy soup and seasonal vegetables. They can also be served with hot mutton soup or cooked potatoes, or customized to taste with sour, spicy, sweet, or salty variations.

This "Inside and Outside Rivers and Mountains" cold oat noodle dish draws inspiration from Shanxi's unique geography - Taihang mountains to the east and Yellow River to the west. The chef uses piled oat noodles to represent Shanxi's mountains, and sea jelly hidden beneath to represent its waters, garnished with six types of vegetables and over ten seasonings, resulting in a deliciously spicy and refreshing cold oat noodle dish.

Ingredients: Oat flour, cucumber shreds, carrot shreds, radish shreds, white radish shreds, cilantro, purple cabbage shreds, sugar, salt, MSG, sesame paste, chili oil, garlic juice, water, olive oil, soy sauce, vinegar, tomato sauce.

Method: Use oat flour from high-altitude regions of northern Shanxi, scald with boiling water, knead into dough and roll into noodles. Boil until cooked, then rinse in cold water. Mix with various seasonings and condiments to taste.

Jilin - Yanji Cold Noodles

Buckwheat noodles topped with beef, apple, and kimchi, served with a scoop of icy sweet-sour broth from a large bucket - this is the impression Yanji cold noodles leave on many people, and perhaps only this style qualifies as authentic. The key to good cold noodles lies in clear broth - beef broth must have all fat removed - and being thoroughly chilled. Only then can they dispel the summer heat completely.

Ingredients: Lean beef, pear or apple, egg skin shreds, kimchi, pine nuts, thin buckwheat noodles, cilantro.

Cooking ingredients: Scallion, ginger, wine, star anise, salt, a little pepper.

Method: Parboil whole beef for 2 minutes, then wash. Cook 4 cups of water with cooking ingredients until boiling, add meat and simmer for 40 minutes. Remove meat, let cool then slice thinly; filter beef broth, cool, remove all floating oil, divide among noodle bowls; boil water, add thin noodles until cooked, drain, rinse in cold water, place in noodle bowls with clear broth. Top with kimchi shreds, pear slices, egg skin shreds, beef slices, fried pine nuts, and cilantro segments.

Guangdong - Egg Cold Noodles

Ingredients: 200g noodles, 1 egg, 100g chicken breast, 20g green bean sprouts, 20g cucumber, 3 cloves garlic.

Seasonings: 20g cooking oil, 1 small spoon sesame oil, 1 big spoon soy sauce, 2 big spoons sesame paste, 1 big spoon fragrant vinegar, 1 small spoon fine salt, 1 small spoon MSG.

Method:

1. Cook noodles until done, drain and rinse with cold boiled water, set aside.

2. Wash green bean sprouts and blanch briefly in boiling water, then soak in cold boiled water until cool, drain.

3. Beat eggs, fry into thin omelette, then shred finely; wash and shred cucumber; cook chicken breast until tender, then shred; peel and crush garlic.

4. Mix sesame paste with a little fine salt and cold boiled water into a paste, then add soy sauce, fragrant vinegar, crushed garlic, and sesame oil.

5. Neatly arrange green bean sprouts, egg shreds, cucumber shreds, and chicken shreds on top of noodles, then pour sesame paste mixture over.

Japan - Cold Rice Udon Noodles

Japanese noodles mainly include udon (similar to our cut noodles), buckwheat noodles, somen, and ramen. Additionally, each region of Japan has its representative noodle dishes - Yamanashi's "Koshu specialty houdou", Akita's "Inaniwa soba", Kagawa's "Sanuki udon", Okinawa's "Okinawa soba", etc.

Generally speaking, noodles refer to udon and buckwheat noodles. More people in the Kansai region eat udon, with light-colored broth that's transparent enough to see the bottom of the bowl, but using soy sauce (light-colored) with high salt concentration. In summer, Japanese prefer cold noodles, with separate broth, dipped or mixed according to personal preference.

Japanese cuisine is generally divided into two main styles: Kanto and Kansai. Kanto cuisine is represented by Tokyo cuisine, while Kansai cuisine includes Kyoto and Osaka styles. Kanto cuisine tends to be heavier, while Kansai cuisine is lighter and fresher. Most Japanese restaurants in Beijing serve Kanto-style cuisine, likely to cater to local tastes. "Nadamono" represents the best of Kansai-style cuisine.

Special cold Inaniwa udon uses handmade Japanese noodles, formed into rolls, accompanied by greens, chicken, shrimp, mushrooms, egg skin, tomatoes, etc. The broth is complex, requiring kelp stock mixed with chicken broth, combined with light soy sauce, mirin, and sesame. The noodle rolls are paired with ingredients, dipped in the broth, and eaten whole, offering a savory, refreshing experience.

Tea soba noodles use tea powder-infused buckwheat noodles, mixed with ginger paste, scallions, onion paste, seaweed strips, and a sweet-salty broth made from bonito stock, offering a tea aroma and subtle sake fragrance.

Ingredients: Dried buckwheat noodles, seaweed strips, finely chopped scallions, cold dashi stock, wasabi paste.

Method: Bring water to a boil in a pot, add buckwheat noodles, boil and add water twice until cooked, immediately rinse in cold water (can add ice cubes), drain and place on bamboo mat plate, sprinkle with seaweed strips. Dilute dashi stock with twice the amount of water, pour into small bowls. Serve finely chopped scallions and wasabi paste separately with cold noodles.

Home-Style Cold Noodles

Ingredients: 150g noodles, 1 small cucumber, 1 carrot, 1 egg.

Method:

1. Make egg crepes, shred with cucumber and carrot.

2. Finely shred scallions, mince ginger and garlic.

3. Cook noodles until 80% done, rinse in cold water and set aside.

4. Pour hot oil over chili powder.

5. Place noodles in a large bowl, add all ingredients, pour soy sauce and vinegar, mix well and serve.

Four Flavor Cold Noodles

Ingredients: 100g cooked ham, 150g cucumber, 75g rehydrated mushrooms, 75g green peas, fresh ginger, fragrant vinegar, soy sauce, fine salt, sesame oil, chili oil, sesame paste, broad bean paste, mustard, stock, etc.

Method:

1. Wash cucumber, dice along with ham and mushrooms. Finely mince fresh ginger, blanch mushrooms and peas.

2. Prepare four different cold noodle sauces in small bowls. Mix ginger, fragrant vinegar, soy sauce, sesame oil into ginger vinegar sauce; mix sesame paste, fine salt, and cool boiled water into sesame paste sauce; mix broad bean paste, chili oil, sesame oil, stock into spicy sauce; mix mustard, soy sauce, fragrant vinegar, sesame oil into mustard sauce.

3. Dice cucumber, ham, mushrooms, and peas separately, serve with four sauces, allowing diners to choose their preferred flavor.

Xinjiang Cold Noodles

Method: First mix light salt water, earth alkali water, then add蓬灰water, kneading until the dough is soft, smooth, and elastic. Set aside on a cutting board. Peel, seed, and shred squash, chop bok choy and celery. Pull dough into thin strands, boil until cooked, then rinse twice in cold water and toss with a little clear oil. Heat water in wok, cook squash until tender, season with salt, make egg flower, add bok choy, thicken with cornstarch slurry to make gravy. Fry celery segments in oil until cooked, separately dilute chili powder, minced garlic, and sesame paste with cool boiled water. When serving, place cold noodles on plate, pour gravy, add vinegar, garlic, chili oil, sesame paste, and celery segments. The noodles are soft yet chewy, with a tangy and slightly spicy flavor.

Scallion Oil Cold Noodles

Ingredients: 100g shrimp, 75g rehydrated bamboo shoots, 50g mushrooms, 50g scallions, 10g vegetable oil, 5g soy sauce, 3g fine salt, 2g chicken essence, 5g sesame oil.

Method:

1. Clean shrimp, soften with a little hot water and chop finely. Wash bamboo shoots and mushrooms, dice small, blanch and cool, finely chop scallion whites.

2. Heat oil, add scallion until fragrant, cool to make scallion oil.

3. Mix scallion oil, soy sauce, fine salt, chicken essence, sesame oil in a bowl to make scallion oil sauce.

4. Sprinkle dried shrimp, bamboo shoot, mushroom dices on cold noodles, pour scallion oil sauce, mix well and serve.

Wuhan Hot Dry Noodles

Hot dry noodles are one of Wuhan's traditional snacks. The noodles undergo boiling, chilling, and oiling processes, then are dressed with a sauce made from sesame paste, fragrant oil, fragrant vinegar, chili oil, etc. The result is smooth, chewy noodles with rich, flavorful sauce that stimulates the appetite. This time, Xiwen, who enjoys singing and cooking, adds minced meat, radish, and fermented broad bean paste to enhance the spicy, crispy texture of the hot dry noodles.

Wuhan Hot Dry Noodles

Preparation Ingredients (for three servings)

Ingredients:

Ground pork (200g), thin pulled noodles (250g), radish shreds (50g), scallion shreds (1 tablespoon), roasted white sesame seeds (1 tablespoon)

Sauce:

Fragrant oil (1 tablespoon), sesame paste (3 tablespoons), chicken powder (1/3 tablespoon), soy sauce (1 tablespoon), fragrant vinegar (1 tablespoon), fermented broad bean paste (1 tablespoon), chili oil (2 tablespoons)

Seasonings:

Oil (4 tablespoons), salt (1 tablespoon)

Production:

Step 1

1. Heat 2 tablespoons of oil, add ground pork and stir-fry briefly, then add radish shreds and stir evenly until the meat turns white. Add 1 bowl of water and bring to a boil.

Step 2

2. Add 1 tablespoon of fragrant oil, 3 tablespoons of sesame paste, 1/3 tablespoon of chicken powder, 1 tablespoon of soy sauce, 1 tablespoon of fragrant vinegar, 1 tablespoon of fermented broad bean paste, and 2 tablespoons of chili oil, mix evenly to make the sauce.

Step 3

3. Bring half a pot of water to a boil, add 1 tablespoon of salt, add pulled noodles and stir apart, cook over medium heat until they float, add water twice.

Step 4

4. Drain the cooked noodles, rinse several times with cold water, and drain well. After the noodles are chilled, rinse off excess starch.

Step 5

5. Heat 2 tablespoons of oil, pour in the cooked noodles and stir apart with chopsticks so each noodle is coated with oil and smooth without sticking together.

Step 6

6. Place the noodles on a plate, pour the sesame sauce, sprinkle with scallion shreds and 1 tablespoon of roasted white sesame seeds, and serve.

Chef's Tips:

1. To make the noodles smooth and chewy: add salt to boiling water; after boiling the noodles, add water twice; chill the cooked noodles to rinse off excess starch; oil the noodles to keep them smooth and non-sticky.

2. Since sesame paste is thick and hard to dissolve, for richer sesame flavor, mix sesame paste into a paste with fragrant oil before adding to the noodles.

3. As sesame paste has extremely high oil content and low water content, more water should be added when making the sauce, otherwise it will become too thick and hard to dissolve after adding sesame paste, resulting in a dry texture.

4. Sauce can be adjusted according to personal taste, those who like spicy can add more fermented broad bean paste and chili oil, those who don't like spicy can omit them.
