Apple陷入 "People's War" in China, and the discussion on a cheaper version of iPhone

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 18:23:07

Revolutionary war is a people's war; it must rely on the people, organize the people, arm the people, liberate the people, defend the people, and fight for the people’s interests in order to win the people's revolutionary war.

- Mao Zedong

If Tim Cook were to read "Selected Works of Mao Zedong," he might be more resolute in launching a low-cost version of the iPhone. In China's smartphone market, what can truly "fight for the people's interests" is an affordable iPhone that combines excellent hardware craftsmanship with a superior ecosystem.

Let us first bring the topic back to Apple's People's War.

This discussion starts with a Reuters article titled "How Samsung Beat Apple in China." The article itself lacks significant insights, merely stating that Samsung has stronger distribution channels in China and its strategy of releasing many models (the so-called "sea of devices") has been successful. In the previous quarter, Samsung sold 72.4 million smartphones, while Apple sold 31.2 million. Apple's sales performance in China declined by 14% year-over-year, leading to Samsung overtaking Apple.

The article sparked a rebuttal from Philip Elmer-DeWitt, author of Apple 2.0. He argues that both Samsung and Apple are in dangerous positions in China for the following reasons:

According to IDC data, the fastest-growing brands in the second quarter of this year were not Samsung or Apple but smaller players like Lenovo, LG, "others," ZTE, Alcatel, etc. Samsung only grew by 44%, while Apple grew by just 20%. Lenovo and LG saw their smartphone sales double, achieving explosive growth.

During Tim Cook's earnings call on July 23rd, when discussing the decline in revenue from China, he admitted that he was "not entirely clear why this happened." However, looking at the graph above, it appears that Apple is caught in a "People's War." The growth of LG and ZTE is attributed to increased shipments globally; LG benefited from its early layout in the 4G era, while ZTE succeeded through operator channels and low prices.

The fatal factor affecting Samsung and Apple in the Chinese market is Lenovo Mobile. Although Lenovo announced its intention to expand overseas last year, given the time required to build distribution channels, its overseas sales growth remains limited. Most of Lenovo's mobile business is concentrated in mainland China, which drives its growth engine.

Lenovo has yet to release its Q2 financial report, and the numbers provided by IDC are estimates. However, Lenovo Mobile's development over the past year has been evident to all. From being ranked second in domestic reports by research firm Cino Research in June last year, to setting an ambitious goal to catch up with and surpass Samsung, to launching 40-50 new phone models as part of its sea-of-devices strategy, to announcing profitability in its mobile business in late January this year...its development over the past year has been highly aggressive.

In a media interview at CES this year, Chen Xudong, General Manager of Lenovo China, mentioned that they are considering selling phones through computer channels, intending to sell phones in stores that sell computers in fourth- to sixth-tier cities. This is a significant move targeting Samsung's channel advantages—Lenovo computer specialty stores are often seen in rural areas, indicating their extensive reach. Once implemented, this plan will put immense pressure on its competitors.

Of course, Apple's "People's War" in China is not limited to Lenovo Mobile. Other brands not mentioned, such as Huawei, Coolpad, and Xiaomi, also play a significant role in diverting customers. Huawei reported revenues of 113.8 billion yuan in the first half of the year, largely driven by smartphone sales, but since it did not disclose smartphone unit sales, it did not make the list. Coolpad focuses heavily on operator channels and has now become the "third largest player in China." Xiaomi announced that it sold 7.03 million phones in the first half of the year, equivalent to its total sales for the entire previous year. In terms of growth rate, Xiaomi could have made it onto the list above. Additionally, there are other differentiated products like Vivo music phones, Meitu selfie-enhancing phones, JD.com custom phones, and more.

It can be said that as upstream chip manufacturers provide increasingly complete solutions, the "People's War" faced by Apple and Samsung in China is just beginning.

Talking about the "Low-Cost iPhone"

According to information gathered, the low-cost iPhone does indeed exist and has undergone multiple trial productions at Foxconn. Individuals who have handled the "low-cost iPhone" say it uses a plastic shell and "feels very good."

Prior to this, rumors about the low-cost iPhone had circulated endlessly, even leading to counterfeit "low-cost iPhone back covers" produced by Shenzhen-based imitation manufacturers. These rumors were detailed, even mentioning colorful back covers.

Regarding the low-cost iPhone, I have always viewed it from a commercial perspective, believing that due to concerns over its impact on Apple's profit margins—and thereby its stock price and market capitalization—Apple would proceed cautiously. Especially after seeing a 400% growth in India by promoting older iPhone models, Apple may prioritize discounting older iPhones to address demand in the lower-end market.

However, recent developments increasingly indicate that Apple's management hopes to achieve some success in market share, especially as it faces a pincer attack in the Chinese market, which may accelerate Tim Cook's decision-making process.

As humorously suggested at the beginning of this article, Apple is embroiled in a "People's War" in the Chinese market, and only "fighting for the people's interests" can lead to victory. The majority of Chinese consumers prefer high-quality, reasonably priced products. Due to their preference for face-saving appearances, they dislike old products. Launching a low-cost iPhone is a call from many potential users in emerging markets.

Nevertheless, even if Apple prepares to launch a low-cost iPhone, the challenge of maintaining profit margins remains unsolved. Whether the shift in market share can offset the impact on stock price during the conversion from profit margins to stock price remains questionable. Especially since Apple is not particularly adept at securing absolute leadership in product share.
