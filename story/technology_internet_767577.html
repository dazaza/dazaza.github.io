Nokia CEO Elop's "Trojan Horse" Path

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 10:51:27

**October 17 — According to a report by U.S. media, Jorma Ollila, former chairman of Nokia and current non-executive chairman of Royal Dutch Shell, wrote in his memoirs published on Thursday that Stephen Elop was not the first choice for CEO when he moved from Microsoft to Nokia three years ago.**

**When Elop joined Nokia, the company was already lagging behind Apple and Samsung Electronics in the emerging smartphone market. In his book, Ollila wrote that in 2010, as chairman of Nokia responsible for finding a new CEO, he interviewed five candidates in the U.S. who had suitable backgrounds. After the interviews, Ollila's top choice was "the number two person at a famous American tech company."**

**Ollila did not name this executive but stated that the candidate, who was in his 50s, withdrew from the competition due to personal reasons. This led Ollila and the Nokia board to choose the younger Elop, who impressed them as "a good salesman and decisive corporate executive."**

**A spokesperson for Nokia refused to comment on behalf of Elop. The newly published book is only available in Finnish, titled "Mahdoton Menestys" (Impossible Success). Previously, sources indicated that after Steve Ballmer's retirement, Elop would be one of the candidates considered for the next CEO position at Microsoft.**

**Last month, Elop announced the sale of Nokia's phone business to Microsoft for $7 billion. He is no longer the CEO of Nokia and will return to Microsoft as head of the devices business. Under Elop's leadership, Nokia's market share continued to decline, and financial losses increased. Besides selling assets, he also cut thousands of jobs and made large-scale changes to the organization and operations, including altering Nokia's smartphone development approach.**

**Ollila served as Nokia's CEO from 1992 to 2006 and as chairman from 1999 to 2012. He played a key role in transforming the Finnish company into one of the most valuable and respected companies globally. Over a year ago, he left the Nokia board and has kept a low profile regarding the company. Overall, he has publicly supported Elop in his comments.**

**In his new book, Ollila mentioned being impressed by Elop's experience in the software industry and his "Finnish-style straightforwardness." However, Ollila also admitted to having doubts. He questioned whether Elop, born in Canada and 49 years old this year, might be too Americanized as an executive. He noted that Nokia had developed a low-key corporate culture, such as not being particularly adept at promoting its successes.**

**Ollila also wondered if Elop had sufficient product-oriented capabilities, "because we needed someone who could deliver results like Steve Jobs." Elop became Nokia's CEO in September 2010 and officially stepped down in September 2013. Ollila focused on the rise of the company in his book, concluding with Elop's appointment without discussing the 2011 mobile system alliance agreement between Microsoft and Nokia.**

**However, in an interview with Finland's largest newspaper, Helsingin Sanomat, on Thursday, Ollila said that Nokia's partnership with Microsoft was a mistake: "We used Microsoft's system to develop products to compete with Apple and Samsung, but it wasn't successful."**
