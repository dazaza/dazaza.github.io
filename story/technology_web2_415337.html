Sisters often study under streetlights, parents make a living by collecting rags (picture)

by iface004 on 2011-04-13 13:08:44

When the sisters came home from school, they would know their father had good business that day only if they saw some meat in the bowls. But there were not many such opportunities - only seven or eight times a year. There were many girls in the family, including Wei Rang, their mother, all of whom wore long braids. This was not for fashion but because a long braid could be sold for three to four hundred yuan, though one had to grow their hair uncut for four years.

Winter was hard to get through. Their sister's hands often got so cold they trembled and had to be warmed up in her bosom before she could continue writing. Over these years, this is how they spent winter, never affecting the next day's homework submission.

Tingting's father, Cao Bao, is 44 years old, while her mother Wei Rang is 42. They are both from Zhoukou, Henan province, and make a living collecting scrap in Luohe city. Tingting, 14 years old, is the eldest among the four siblings and is now in the second year of junior high school. She said that since she could remember, her family has been moving around without a fixed place to live. The rented plot of land costs 2000 yuan per year, with no nearby water or electricity supply, and they didn't know which department to consult. The honest father usually dared not speak when he saw someone in uniform. "We most worry about rainy days because we can't do our homework by the roadside then. At home, it not only wastes kerosene, but also leaks rain everywhere. In case of heavy rain, we even have to wear rain boots," Tingting said. Studying by the roadside was convenient and didn't cost electricity, but the noise from passing vehicles and the exhaust fumes were quite annoying. However, they had gotten used to it.

Close to the roadside was another mobile "home", a farm vehicle bought by the father for 600 yuan. During the day, he drove it to collect scrap, and at night, he opened the three side panels, laid down large cardboard, and the "home" quickly "stood" up. In summer, the siblings liked this well-ventilated "home". Sleeping inside was cool, except for the strong smell of surrounding waste that attracted many mosquitoes.

Cao Bao only went to school until third grade. He said, having too many children brought heavy burdens, and the tuition alone cost more than 20,000 yuan each year. But no matter how difficult it was, he would let his children go to school and not continue wandering like them.

Cao Bao said that the children never wasted a penny and never asked their parents for money. On New Year's Eve, he would give each child lucky money: 5 yuan for the older ones and 2 yuan for the younger ones. Even this small amount of lucky money, none of them dared to spend. The children were very filial, knowing their mother was in poor health. When cooking, they secretly put an egg in her bowl and found health books from the pile of waste to tell her how to adjust her body.

Only knowledge can change fate, Tingting deeply realized. Her mother only attended one class at school and was pulled back by her grandmother. To this day, she only recognized the words 'up', 'middle', and 'down'. Collecting scraps was already at the bottom of society, but one still needed to know how to calculate accounts, right? Now her mother could only calculate addition and subtraction under 100, and got confused with larger numbers, fortunately others wouldn't cheat her.

Although Tingting's family lived in the city, they couldn't enjoy the benefits it brought, not even basic water and electricity supplies. But the children were still content here. On nights without rain, under the dim streetlights on Songjiang Road in Luohe City, Henan Province, there were always a pair of sisters crouching on stools doing their homework. They were the daughters of scavengers, without a fixed home or decent clothes, but they had several large boxes of books picked up from the waste pile. On the path of seeking education paved by the money their parents made from selling scrap, they were striving to create their own future.

All the sisters were studying hard. Whoever performed well in exams would be rewarded with a book. Although their study supplies were all picked up, many of them were still usable. The younger sister Lele's backpack even had wheels, like a boss's bag, which could be dragged on the road to school, envied by classmates.

No matter how bitter it was, they must let their children go to school and not continue wandering.

Because they usually didn't have new clothes to wear, Tingting often received cold eyes from classmates. This year during National Day, the school held an artistic performance and selected program hosts. Due to her outstanding talent, Tingting's performance was highly praised by the judges. After the results came out, some classmates in her class said sarcastically: "Without a single piece of new clothing, how can you become a host?" Hearing this, Tingting ran outside and cried. She didn't dare to tell her parents when she returned home, fearing they would be sad. The next day after class, Tingting found the teacher: "Classmates say I don't have new clothes, so I can't participate in the host selection?" The teacher said, "You, Cao Panting, walk bravely, let others talk!"

Simple "Home"

The appliances at home were relatively complete, with color TVs, washing machines, and DVDs available, just unfortunate that they never made any sound - due to lack of electricity - covered with thick dust, unable to distinguish what brand they were. The only electronic product at home that made noise was the calculator Cao Bao used to collect scrap. Tingting's three-year-old younger sister found one of its buttons that could play simple music, and she would hold it and press it randomly when she had nothing to do.

Tingting's home was a simple shed built in a 90-degree wall corner, with only a double bed and a three-legged table inside. It was hard for the whole family to squeeze in when eating together. The walls were full of calendar posters from previous years, the "beauty" photos on them blurred by rainwater, making it impossible to recognize which "star" they were. On the door panel was a real estate developer's sales advertisement claiming to be among the world's top 500 companies.

"Worried about nowhere to do homework when it rains"

Tingting's family didn't have much savings, but they did have a "mountain." Except for food, it almost included everything, all the things abandoned by city people gathered here. Tingting and her sisters relied on this "mountain" to enter school.

In winter, they slept squeezed in the simple shed at the wall corner with their parents. Wei Rang would find a few saltwater bottles filled with hot water to warm the children's feet. Sometimes at dawn, they would find that the bottles kicked out of the quilt were frozen.

Tingting didn't feel her life was bitter. She said, "Our lives are much better than those of the children in earthquake-stricken areas who lost many relatives and some are still studying in temporary housing." On May 12th earthquake, she donated 15.8 yuan, her younger sister donated 10 yuan, and her brother donated 1 yuan. They also donated many books to the disaster area. Though the money wasn't much, it was a heartfelt gesture.

On the morning of October 17th at 6 o'clock, Tingting's 80-year-old grandfather passed away. His last words were: "Let the children be good people!"

"Wealth" is a pile of picked-up books

By the prosperous roadside, one often sees Tingting and her sister studying under dim lights.

Tingting and her family's outerwear were all picked up from the waste. The parents only spent money buying uniforms for the children. Tingting said, "Uniforms must be bought, otherwise, we can't enter the school gate."

A few days before the interview, Tingting found an old mobile phone in the waste and gave it to her mother, which could still be used but caused her father a lot of trouble as he had to take it to a relative's house several miles away to charge it frequently.

Every day after school, Tingting and her sister would climb onto the pile of collected books and newspapers to look for books they liked. Tingting said, "Finding a book I like is happier than getting anything else."

On the day of the artistic performance, Tingting was still wearing that long wool sweater. A few female classmates took out their cosmetics to carefully dress her up. Standing in the center of the stage, Tingting recited a poem she created herself: "Wild grass lonely appreciates / Lake stream flows like ice / Sunset looks westward / Who will lead the clouds again / People walk, clouds float / How many sorrows in life!" After the recitation, she won applause from the audience.

Actually, this "mountain" was just a pile of counterfeits by the Songjiang Road in Luohe City, Henan Province. One couldn't see how much wealth was hidden in this "mountain", but often saw the two sisters studying late into the night under the dim streetlights. Some people blamed the parents: "In such cold weather, letting the children sit under the streetlight to do homework isn't heartbreaking?" Over the past three years, Tingting heard countless such criticisms.

This 14-year-old girl also had a place where she was richer than others - she had many books, including newspapers, magazines, and domestic and foreign classics, adding up to several large boxes. Tingting loved reading and sat among the paper piles flipping through them when she had nothing to do, keeping the ones she liked. Indeed, communicating with Tingting, one could feel her broad knowledge. In her class, Tingting was one of the top five excellent students, and many classmates had received her help. She also often brought classics from home to share with her classmates.

From the surface, Tingting's family dressed quite fashionably, all in famous brands, just looking awkwardly mismatched. Her mother's casual wear showed her belly button, Tingting's wool sweater reached her knees, her sister's long boots had the front half curling like Charlie Chaplin's shoes, but the quality was definitely first-class, not afraid of rain. All the clothes worn outside by the whole family were collected from scrap, and just counting the shoes added up to dozens of pairs.
