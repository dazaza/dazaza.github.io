iPhone 5S, Note3, Xperia Z Camera Throwdown

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 21:02:33

Starting from the iPhone 4s, Apple has maintained the camera's pixel specifications at 8 million pixels. Compared to flagship phones today that often have tens of millions of pixels, this may seem outdated, but pixels are not the decisive factor in determining image quality. Today, we will compare the photography quality of the Samsung Note 3 (13 million pixels) and Sony Xperia Z1 (20.7 million pixels) with the iPhone 5S to see how the iPhone 5s performs.

Daytime Shooting Comparison

Under the premise of ensuring imaging quality, 8 million pixels can already meet the daily shooting needs. In the following set of samples, all three phones performed well. However, due to the difference in pixels, the Samsung Note 3 and Sony Xperia Z1 have a certain advantage in sharpness and detail retention. Since the Xperia Z1 is equipped with a wide-angle G lens, it is very suitable for landscape photography.

If you click on the picture and zoom in, you will find that in the sample from the Xperia Z1, the "Beautiful Scenery Mall" characters on the blue back panel in the upper right corner appear blurry, which is less sharp compared to the Note 3. This is probably due to the shallow depth of field when taking the shot with the Z1. Please pay attention when focusing during shooting.

iPhone 5s (ISO 32, aperture f/2.2, shutter speed 1/446)

Samsung Galaxy Note 3 (ISO 50, aperture f/2.2, shutter speed 1/160)

Sony Xperia Z1 (ISO 50, aperture f/2.0, shutter speed 1/250)

In the following set of samples, the performances of the three phones are comparable. The sample from the iPhone 5s gives the most comfortable feeling. It has sufficient saturation and contrast, accurate and natural colors, and no obvious warm or cool color temperature bias; the performance of the Note 3 is also excellent, with no noticeable flaws found. The Xperia Z1, thanks to its high pixel count, retains a large amount of shadow details, but the contrast of the photo is not ideal, as can be clearly seen from the GTA V poster.

iPhone 5s (ISO 40, aperture f/2.2, shutter speed 1/3000)

Samsung Galaxy Note 3 (ISO 50, aperture f/2.2, shutter speed 1/1200)

Sony Xperia Z1 (ISO 50, aperture f/2.0, shutter speed 1/1250)

Indoor Shooting Comparison

In terms of indoor shooting, the first group consists of photos of an indoor dinosaur model, where the indoor lighting is not very sufficient. Comparing the three pictures, the iPhone 5S performed the best among the three phones, with fewer noise points and a similar level of detail retention as the Xperia Z1. The Note 3 has more noise points due to its higher ISO value.

In the restaurant samples, the iPhone 5s performed well in maintaining the purity of the image and details, but there was insufficient exposure, making the overall image darker compared to the other two phones.

The excellent performance of the iPhone 5S in environments with poor lighting is largely related to increasing each pixel size to 1.5 microns, which increases the amount of light entering (the previous generation was 1.4 microns, HTC One is 2 microns).

iPhone 5s (ISO 500, aperture f/2.2, shutter speed 1/15)

Samsung Galaxy Note 3 (ISO 1000, aperture f/2.2, shutter speed 1/17)

Sony Xperia Z1 (ISO 400, aperture f/2.0, shutter speed 1/8)

iPhone 5s (ISO 40, aperture f/2.2, shutter speed 1/30)

Samsung Galaxy Note 3 (ISO 200, aperture f/2.2, shutter speed 1/25)

Sony Xperia Z1 (ISO 125, aperture f/2.0, shutter speed 1/30)

Nighttime Shooting Comparison

In nighttime shooting, all three phones used high sensitivity ISO 400 and above. Basically, there were already few details visible in the photos. Among them, the Note 3 performed the worst, with the least exposure among the three samples, and the lowest ranking in detail even at ISO 1000 sensitivity. As for the Xperia Z1 and iPhone 5s, the white balance restoration of the iPhone 5s was relatively accurate, while the Xperia Z1 used a lower ISO 400 under a larger aperture. It is worth noting that the anti-shake function of the Xperia Z1 is quite outstanding.

iPhone 5s (ISO 800, aperture f/2.2, shutter speed 1/15)

Samsung Galaxy Note 3 (ISO 1000, aperture f/2.2, shutter speed 1/17)

Sony Xperia Z1 (ISO 400, aperture f/2.0, shutter speed 1/8)

Below is a set of tests for close-up nighttime shooting. The iPhone 5s metering is dark, resulting in underexposure even when focused on the flowers. The Note 3 only achieves approximately the same exposure at ISO 1000, and the best result in this group is the photo taken by the Xperia Z1. Not only is the exposure sufficient, but the colors are similar to what the naked eye sees, which is also a good performance.

iPhone 5s (ISO 320, aperture f/2.2, shutter speed 1/20)

Samsung Galaxy Note 3 (ISO 1000, aperture f/2.2, shutter speed 1/17)

Sony Xperia Z1 (ISO 400, aperture f/2.0, shutter speed 1/8)

From the above series of comparisons, it can be seen that the iPhone 5S, which maintains 8 million pixels, still maintains a high standard in practical use through increasing the pixel point size and enlarging the aperture, especially in daytime and nighttime shooting. The overall color expression and control of noise are very outstanding. Unfortunately, in indoor photography, although the details and purity of the image are kept well, the problem of insufficient exposure affects the overall impression of the photo.

Compared to the Note 3 and Xperia Z1, the Xperia Z1 performs better. Perhaps due to the phone positioning, the Note 3 focuses on business and has not done much work to optimize photography, only meeting the needs of normal daylight shooting, and is powerless in darker environments; the G lens of the Xperia Z1 performed averagely in this test, able to meet the shooting needs in various environments without any obvious shortcomings. With future software upgrades and optimizations, this G lens should perform even better.
