SARFT official says order to cap movie ticket prices aims to address excessively high ticket prices

by loved7p7 on 2012-03-03 10:51:11

Our News (Wang Peng) - Recently, the Film Bureau has solicited opinions from relevant units through the China Film Distribution and Exhibition Association on the "Guiding Opinions for Further Standardizing the Ticket Management of the Movie Market" (Draft for Comments). It stated that various regions will separately set guideline prices for movie tickets. Among them, member tickets and group tickets should not be lower than 70% of the cinema's listed price.

Yesterday, Li Dong, Deputy Director of the Film Fund Office of the State Administration of Radio, Film, and Television, in an exclusive interview with our newspaper, said that one of the issues the "Opinion" aims to address is the problem of excessively high ticket prices. [Many audiences have misinterpreted the "Opinion". The current issue in the film market is that the marked prices of cinema tickets are falsely high, and retail tickets are expensive. Even if member tickets, group tickets, or bulk purchases are discounted by 30% or even 50%, they are still quite high. One of the problems the "Opinion" aims to solve is the excessively high ticket prices, meaning reaching a consensus on a minimum guideline price, which will significantly reduce the listed prices of cinemas. This is a prerequisite for benefiting the audience.]

Li Dong gave an example: take a ticket priced at 80 yuan by the cinema. A member discount at 50% would be 40 yuan, while the suggested maximum guideline price (placard price) is 50 yuan. If the price is no less than 70%, it would be 35 yuan. Audiences will actually benefit from this, and at the same time, discounts caused by malicious competition can also protect the interests of film producers. In fact, this brings a win-win situation for both the audience and the filmmakers.

In terms of structured pricing, there currently exists a situation where cinemas list a price of 80 yuan, but the actual ticket selling price is less than half the listed price. However, when settling accounts with the film distributors, only the lowest limit price specified by the distributor is used, such as 35 yuan, creating a difference of 45 yuan. Cinemas refuse to settle accounts with the distributors, claiming that the 45 yuan is from popcorn and soft drink sales. This phenomenon is clearly defined in the "Opinion."

Currently, the problem of underreporting box office revenue in cinemas is serious. Cinemas currently settle accounts with film producers according to the lowest limit price of 35 yuan. Calculated this way, if we reverse-calculate based on 70% of 35 yuan, the ticket price would be 50 yuan. This method of guideline pricing essentially squeezes out the water content of falsely high cinema ticket prices.

Shortage in First-Round Showings

Li Dong pointed out that currently, there is a shortage in first-round showings of popular films in China’s film market. Nationwide, cinemas can only provide about 500,000 seats per day. With each seat accommodating six screenings per day, the country can only meet the viewing needs of 3 million people per day. When films like "The Flying Swords of Dragon Gate" and "The Flowers of War" are released on the same day, it becomes difficult to meet the audience's demand for first-round viewings. This manifests as a situation where tickets are hard to come by for several consecutive days, providing a lot of space for scalping. [For example, when "Avatar" was released, the most expensive tickets for the IMAX screens in cinemas were sold for 120 yuan, but due to its popularity, tickets outside were being scalped by ticket touts for 700 yuan each. It is estimated that more than 5 billion yuan in ticket sales from China’s film market goes into the pockets of ticket scalpers each year.]
