Only such a Meizu M8 can have a future.

by whotalks on 2009-10-25 13:03:00

The author has been closely following the progress of Meizu M8, and Meizu was once regarded by many as a benchmark for the domestic mobile phone industry in the future.

However, nothing is perfect. After observing for a long time, I have some thoughts that I can't help but express. As for who sees it and whether it is useful, it doesn't matter!

In order for Meizu M8 to achieve real success, I personally think that achieving the following points might be helpful:

First: Change the operating system of M8 immediately. It would be best to adopt Android, or Windows Mobile (WM), even Symbian would be better than your own developed system. After all, the key thing is market share, which ensures good software support. Meizu is not Apple, and does not yet have the strength to fully develop its own system and promote it.

Personally, I think M8 should adopt the Android system launched by Google. First, this is a new system with fewer models available, so it's rare and valuable. Second, Android is the trend of the future and will surely make a big impact later on. Moreover, it is completely open-source, so the development cost is low and there’s no need to worry about software support. If there were such a miraculous domestic product, combined with price advantages, sales would definitely soar.

Second: It would be best to first launch versions supporting China Mobile's TD-SCDMA or China Telecom's CDMA2000 3G networks, rather than China Unicom's WCDMA.

Why not rush to launch the WCDMA version? The reason is simple: the waters are too turbulent over there. WCDMA is currently the most popular and mature 3G standard in the world, and almost all current 3G phones use this system. Brands like Nokia, Samsung, Apple, Sony Ericsson, BlackBerry, etc., offer quite a lot of high-end models for users with various tastes. In short, whether or not you have M8 here makes no difference, it cannot attract attention at all, and lacks personality. Moreover, although WCDMA is successful worldwide, in China, due to policy support, the 3G networks of Mobile and Telecom will not be bad either. Users actually don’t care much about choosing among these three operators.

To date, I haven't seen any large-screen smartphones that support Telecom's CDMA2000. If Meizu M8 could suddenly appear now, it would definitely be a pleasant surprise. Rarity makes things precious, and it might even become a favored guest of China Telecom!

A while ago, it seemed that J.Wong had already announced on the forum the abandonment of support for CDMA2000. The reasons given were: 1) The development cost is too high, reaching tens of millions; 2) There is little hope of being purchased by Telecom.

I doubt the first reason - would it really require such a high development cost? How come ZTE, a relatively average domestic mobile phone brand, is still eager to pursue it?

Regarding the second reason, I think it's an excuse. Other brands with insufficient quality, monotonous appearance, and no special features can still be procured, so why should Meizu alone be excluded?

China Mobile has joined forces with a large number of mobile phone manufacturers to adopt the Ophone system they developed based on Android, which is no less impressive than the iPhone. If M8 could join under the banner of China Mobile, it would undoubtedly increase its own stakes. Although there are quite a few powerful devices in the Ophone lineup, their lofty prices make most people unable to afford them. So why should M8, priced at only 2000 RMB, worry about not gaining new fans?

Third: Try to become a customized phone for operators. By relying on these big trees, with the support of subsidy from call fees, how could M8 possibly lack in sales?

Finally, let me emphasize the most critical issue: Meizu must focus its efforts on ensuring the quality of the phone. Solve the various problems that keep cropping up, such as signal issues followed by battery problems - this is unacceptable! By the way, don't forget WAPI, because it is the premise of Wi-Fi.
