[Lifestyle Tips ABC] Have you shopped online? Pay attention to safety (forwarded)

by anonymous on 2011-04-01 13:32:20

"Living cost savings of nearly 30%, online shopping enthusiasts share money-saving tips." Affordable and convenient online shopping has become the hottest consumption method today. However, a large number of hacker traps have raised alarms about online shopping security. In just December 2010, over 1200 new types of Trojans specifically designed to hijack online shopping funds were discovered, along with an additional 6775 fraudulent shopping websites, posing a significant threat to the online shopping market.

How can netizens prevent theft of their funds and important online banking and shopping accounts? In response to the varied and rapidly changing hacker traps in online shopping, combined with some previous cases of deceived netizens, this article specially proposes the following five small tips for ensuring shopping safety, helping online shoppers enhance their security awareness.

First, reject unknown files

Case: Mr. Gu from Hubei bought his wife a discounted branded handbag online. The seller sent him a compressed file through a chat tool, asking him to confirm the model. When Mr. Gu opened the compressed file, he saw a system prompt saying "the file is damaged," which he didn't pay much attention to at the time. He then returned to the shopping website to perform the payment operation. Oddly, the 500 yuan was transferred into another unfamiliar account.

Security Tip: The file received by Mr. Gu was the currently popular "shopping" Trojan. This Trojan can monitor the browser of the infected computer and hijack online payment funds when the victim pays. During shopping transactions, there is no need to receive such files; if it's indeed necessary to view product images, one should request the seller to send common image format files like jpg, rather than compressed packages or executable programs like exe or scr.

Second, verify URL links

Case: Mr. Li from Sichuan purchased a windbreaker on a shopping website. After the seller modified the price, they sent a so-called "payment" link. The page looked very similar to the familiar payment process. However, after Mr. Li made the payment, he realized that the money had been transferred to an unfamiliar payment platform account. When he contacted the seller again, they had already disappeared.

Security Tip: Many netizens are accustomed to consulting sellers during shopping. Some hackers exploit this by registering or stealing a store, sending fraudulent URLs as the seller to hijack the victim's transaction funds or alter the payment amount, even directly stealing account passwords. In such situations, netizens should carefully verify the domain name of the URL link.

Third, beware of ultra-low quotes

Case: Mr. Weng from Guangdong saw an extremely low price for a laptop on a certain online digital mall, so he placed an order. However, after transferring 600 yuan as a "deposit," the website delayed shipping, demanding an additional 3000 yuan deposit. Mr. Weng then realized he had been scammed, and upon investigation, it turned out the website had impersonated another online mall's name.

Security Tip: A common feature of fraudulent shopping websites is extremely low prices, such as computers priced at less than 3000 yuan while the market price is 7000 yuan. They usually find various excuses to ask customers to pay in advance. When visiting unfamiliar websites for shopping, one should first search online to see if other netizens have fallen victim to scams and insist on a "cash on delivery" transaction method, only paying after verifying the goods.

Fourth, refuse direct transfers

Case: Mr. Zhao from Gansu found a counterfeit travel website online and booked two tickets from Lanzhou to Beijing. After receiving enthusiastic customer service from the website, Mr. Zhao trusted them and directly transferred 1420 yuan for the ticket fare to their bank account. Later, he was told that the transfer system had frozen and needed another transfer to unfreeze it, so Mr. Zhao transferred another 1420 yuan. In the end, Mr. Zhao neither received the tickets nor recovered the fare, losing a total of 2880 yuan.

Security Tip: All legitimate shopping websites will support third-party online payment platforms or online banking systems. If asked to directly transfer money to a specific bank account, it is undoubtedly a scam website, and one should immediately refuse and report it to the local public security authorities.

Fifth, identify telephone tricks

Case: Ms. Yu from Inner Mongolia received a call from a "customer service" representative claiming to be from a well-known shopping website, promoting "discounted mobile phone recharge cards with free branded cosmetics." Since Ms. Yu was also a member of that website, she agreed to purchase and paid in cash upon receiving the goods. To her surprise, the mobile phone recharge cards delivered by courier couldn't be used, and the cosmetics were fake.

Security Tip: Today, serious privacy leaks occur on the internet, and some online shopping scams combine with tools like phones and text messages, targeting netizens whose names, phone numbers, and addresses have been leaked, increasing the deception. When receiving such calls, one should first verify whether the incoming call number is trustworthy, otherwise, they might be defrauded under the guise of a well-known shopping website, just like Ms. Yu.
