Chen Mingjie: Maxthon will not get involved in the browser engine dispute.

by yahoohk3168 on 2012-02-16 03:59:06

July 24, 2007, 14:25, Sina Tech - On the morning of July 24, Maxthon founder and CEO Mingjie Chen was a guest on Sina Tech. Watch this news video here. Sina Tech reported that in the morning of July 24, the founder of Maxthon browser, Mingjie Chen, visited Sina Tech's Silver Age to discuss the commercialization and development trends of browsers. Chen believed that although IE, Firefox, and Opera have their own cores, Maxthon would not get involved in the core competition. Regarding the previous rumor that Google would invest in Maxthon, Chen did not give a clear response but expressed that Maxthon welcomes various forms of cooperation from specific business operations to capital levels.

Key viewpoints:

Although IE7 has also started tab browsing, the current version is not well done, so it does not significantly impact Maxthon. It may be more complete by IE8 or IE9, at which point Maxthon might be somewhat affected.

Firefox has become more than just software; it represents a spirit. Some users prefer Firefox even if IE is functional, ensuring that there will always be a market for other browsers despite IE's dominance.

IE and Maxthon have a relationship of both competition and complementarity.

IE has its own core infrastructure, while Maxthon focuses on applications built on top of that infrastructure, akin to the relationship between building foundations and opening restaurants.

IE, Firefox, and Opera all have their respective cores, but Maxthon chooses not to get involved in core disputes, avoiding "political struggles." Instead, they use whatever tools help them create user-pleasing features, whether it’s currently based on IE's core or potentially switching to Firefox's core in the future.

Maxthon welcomes both business collaborations and capital investments as long as they align with the company's development needs.

Despite providing many online services in Maxthon 2.0, Maxthon still ensures better privacy protection compared to IE.

Below is the full transcript of the chat:

Host: Good morning, everyone! Today we welcome Mingjie Chen, the founder of Maxthon, to our Silver Age segment. Maxthon is a very popular domestic browser software, and today's co-host is Guoqiang Yang from The First Financial Daily. Besides participating through computers, you can also follow this chat via mobile phones by visiting sina.cn.

Let's begin. Please greet our audience first.

Mingjie Chen: Hello, everyone!

Guoqiang Yang: Hello, everyone! I'm Guoqiang Yang from The First Financial Daily.

Host: Many people are familiar with Maxthon, but not many know about the company behind it. Could you introduce the browser and your company's history from its creation to now?

Chen: Simply put, Maxthon has been around for quite some time since its development began in 2003 in Singapore. However, the official establishment of the company is relatively recent. There's a long story behind it, though I've discussed the release of different versions many times before. Let me focus on the current state of the company.

We established the company in 2005 after spending four years in Singapore, where things felt monotonous. After leaving Singapore, I returned to Hong Kong because Maxthon already had a significant number of users at that time, even though I was working alone with some contributions from netizens across the U.S., Europe, Russia, and China. We considered expanding due to numerous ideas and projects, but one person couldn't handle everything. Around that time, we had worked with several individuals online for many years, and I wanted to meet them personally. That's how we came to Beijing, where a few colleagues joined me. Since I graduated from Beijing Institute of Technology, I have many friends here, making it easier to establish a company in Beijing. This way, we could dedicate ourselves fully to improving the product while addressing practical issues like living and developing in Beijing, allowing us to focus on making the product better.

Currently, we've moved offices twice. Initially located in Yizhuang, which was remote and inconvenient, but I liked it because it reminded me of Singapore. However, others found it unsatisfactory with limited dining options. Eventually, we relocated to Zhongguancun for better dining and networking opportunities with partners. Now, we're a team of over 20 people and still growing, welcoming more talented individuals to join us.

Host: Why were you interested in creating a browser software back in 2002-2003 when Netscape had declined and Firefox wasn't yet prominent? IE already dominated the market, so why did you choose to develop a browser?

Chen: It was out of interest. As a developer, I enjoyed tinkering with software. When I found something lacking, I tried improving it myself. I started by sharing small features with friends who appreciated them. Word spread online, and feedback accumulated, motivating me to continue without any grand plans. It was fun, and encouragement from users kept me going until we reached this stage.

Host: Many things happen unintentionally.

Chen: Exactly.

Host: Guoqiang, do you have any questions?

Yang: When you were in Singapore, you developed this software part-time. When did you decide to work on it full-time, and what was your consideration?

Chen: Part-time work became insufficient as the project grew. Initially, it was a nighttime hobby involving coding, forum management, and emails. Emails increased from dozens daily to hundreds, and we organized events requiring substantial time commitments. To balance chatting with users and programming, full-time dedication seemed necessary.

Host: Did you have income back then?

Chen: Yes, we had income right from the start through a donation model. We called it free software.

Yang: Was it around 20 RMB?

Chen: Yes, 10-20 RMB. If you liked the software, you could support our development with a donation. Every donor was recorded, and we ensured that these users received special treatment in our activities, expressing gratitude whenever possible. I believe in acknowledging and thanking those who contribute. Without donations, we wouldn't have persisted, as they affirmed our efforts.

Host: How many people donated weekly or monthly back then?

Chen: Initially, foreigners were more generous, finding donations natural. Our model was inspired by international practices uncommon domestically at that time. Some users didn't understand donations, questioning why we didn't simply charge. Regardless, we embraced this approach, allowing users to accept or reject it freely, maintaining our commitment.

Host: Maxthon has developed well despite Microsoft's IE dominating the market and Firefox emerging later. What do you think contributed to this?

Chen: Firefox is an open-source movement where people collaborate to improve software. Beyond being software, Firefox symbolizes a spirit. Some users prefer Firefox even if IE is functional, showcasing diverse user preferences. While Firefox excels in functionality, the world's varied demands cannot be met by a single software. Recognizing shortcomings in IE and Firefox, we addressed these issues, gaining our own user base.

Host: Which browser do you use, Guoqiang?

Yang: I primarily use Maxthon and have been a long-term user.

Chen: Thank you! (laughs)

Yang: The new IE also supports tabbed browsing similar to Maxthon. Will this impact Maxthon significantly?

Chen: Based on our statistics, the impact is minimal. IE's implementation is subpar, with pages loading slowly and difficulty closing tabs. During my visit to Microsoft headquarters, we discussed their rushed development process. I estimate improvements will occur by IE8 or IE9. Currently, Microsoft's initial efforts aren't strong enough to dominate, so we focus on enhancing user experience beyond IE, maintaining a competitive and complementary relationship. For instance, our latest version includes an anti-crash feature surpassing IE's capabilities, achieved partly through Microsoft's support.

Host: Despite being based on IE's core, how do you maintain such a good relationship with Microsoft?

Chen: In foreign markets, companies aren't necessarily enemies. Microsoft and Apple compete but also collaborate effectively. Similarly, while we compete with Microsoft in product features, we also stabilize and enhance IE's core infrastructure. Microsoft views IE as foundational to Windows and aims to build diverse functionalities, supporting us as proof of its infrastructure's capabilities.

Host: What is the current state of the browser market, including browsers based on IE's core?

Chen: Major players include IE, Firefox, and Opera, each with their own cores. We're smaller competitors focusing on building applications rather than competing over cores. The choice of core isn't crucial to us; instead, we aim to create user-friendly applications regardless of the underlying core. For example, if Firefox provides a better experience, we could switch to Firefox-based development. Currently, IE's interface makes it easier for us to develop upon, but we remain open to exploring Firefox as resources permit.

Host: Could you launch a Linux-based version of Maxthon in the future?

Chen: Even if we developed a Linux version, only about 10% of users might adopt it. Focusing on maximizing satisfaction for the majority remains our priority, though we don't rule out exploring a Netscape-based version if feasible.

Yang: What is your user distribution like? Are Chinese users more concentrated or are international users dominant?

Chen: Currently, we have more users in China, but prior to 2005, the distribution was roughly equal between domestic and international users, with significant representation from the U.S., UK, France, Russia, and Poland. After relocating our team to Beijing, our domestic presence expanded rapidly, resulting in approximately three-fifths of our users being Chinese and two-fifths international.

Host: How do you track download statistics on your website?

Chen: It's straightforward. Our software tracks real downloads accurately. Upon installing a new version, a welcome page appears, enabling us to count installations. Only successful installations and usage are included in our statistics, ensuring accuracy. Our total downloads have surpassed 100 million.

Host: With such a massive user base, what are your aspirations for Maxthon's future development?

Chen: We hope to continually improve usability. From a core perspective, we rely on Microsoft to enhance stability and speed, while Firefox facilitates easier secondary development. Capital-wise, we secured investment in 2005 from WI Harper Group. Although rumors suggested Google's interest in investing or acquiring us, we denied them as untrue. Finding suitable investors naturally aids our growth without being decisive.

Yang: Did you ever consider collaboration with Google?

Chen: Google, along with Yahoo, eBay, and Baidu, are valuable partners discussing various collaborations. Whether business-related or capital-based, we welcome anything beneficial to our development. Long-term independence or integration into a larger entity depends on what best serves our growth. Regardless, we prioritize maintaining our core principles and identity.

Host: Do you actively seek investment?

Chen: No, we've never proactively sought investment. Our focus remains on product development. Investment is a natural extension aiding our evolution. With increasing recognition, investors see potential in our software and wish to support our growth. We appreciate all contributors, viewing capital as a catalyst facilitating our goals.

Yang: Will Maxthon remain independent or merge into a larger company for better prospects?

Chen: Our decision hinges on what benefits development most. If independent growth proves optimal, we'll continue independently. Otherwise, strategic partnerships remain viable, provided we retain creative control over Maxthon.

Host: Concluding our session, thank you, Mingjie and Guoqiang, for joining us today. Maxthon is indeed a great tool used by many, and we hope for its continued success. This concludes our chat.

Chen: Thank you!

Yang: Thank you!
