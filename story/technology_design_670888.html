Like continuously announcing new tools

by cmiin973 on 2012-02-21 12:49:54

Not long ago, the Twitter Chirp Developer Conference was held in the United States. Because Twitter is one of the most popular SNS platforms on the Internet today, it has attracted much attention from the industry. Although this conference is named after developers, the most crucial information revealed at this conference is that Twitter has begun its process of commercialization. A typical sign is the announcement of the launch of the Promoted Tweets advertising platform. This means corporate users can purchase specific keywords so that relevant ads will be displayed when users search for these keywords. In addition, Twitter also plans to release related promotional information in users' newsfeeds. It is reported that, on the occasion of Twitter's fourth anniversary, this is a major attempt to transform microblogging services into profitable businesses. Currently, Twitter has signed contracts with advertisers such as Best Buy, Bravo, Red Bull, Sony, Starbucks, and Virgin America. Adding to its previous cooperation with Google and Microsoft in search (which reportedly brings Twitter $25 million in annual income), out of the five commercial profit models predicted by the industry for Twitter, Twitter has so far adopted two of them.

So why has Twitter chosen to accelerate its commercialization process at this time? Will this commercialization process achieve the results expected by Twitter itself? As Twitter, which has grown through open APIs and user numbers, currently has over 100 million registered users, 600 million daily searches (75% of traffic comes from APIs), 55 million new tweets every day, an annual growth rate of 1500%, and 100,000 registered applications (Apps). These data announced at the Twitter Developer Conference, to some extent, confirm the indispensable role of users and application developers in Twitter's development to date. Meanwhile, such rapid growth, massive user base, and applications have made investors full of expectations for Twitter's commercialization. After all, people seek fame and profit. The concept of losing money to gain fame can only last temporarily, not forever. Based on this, Twitter launched the Promoted Tweets advertising platform. Despite this conflicting with Twitter's principle of always prioritizing users first, then developing the site's value, and finally considering profitability, this principle indeed hurt the confidence of some experts in Twitter's development. Therefore, accelerating commercialization or at least taking steps towards trying it is something Twitter had to do. Perhaps the explanation given by Biz Stone, co-founder of Twitter, in his blog post titled "Hello World" on Twitter's official website regarding the announcement of the Promoted Tweets advertising platform reflects this dilemma between Twitter's ideals and commercial reality: "I believe if you were in my position now, you would make the same developmental decision as me, but you would also be criticized."

Since the path of commercialization has been chosen, future actions will inevitably serve the purpose of commercialization. For example, at this developer conference, Twitter announced the launch of its own location-based feature and claimed that this move was not out of imitation of Foursquare (a third-party application based on open API). However, industry insiders believe that Twitter's move may lead some users who are tired of dealing with third-party location applications to ultimately choose Twitter. This implies that third-party applications will be marginalized or abandoned, which is certainly not good news for developers.

In fact, conflicts between Twitter and developer applications have recently become prominent, mainly manifested in Twitter's acquisition of Tweetie, a third-party application for iPhone, which was renamed "Twitter for iPhone" and offered for free download. Simultaneously, Twitter announced that the RIM BlackBerry application would be the "official BlackBerry version of Twitter." Additionally, after failing to acquire Bit.ly, a third-party URL shortening service provider, Twitter confirmed at the developer conference that it would launch its own official URL shortening service. Previously, these were all third-party applications based on API. This at least confirms the prior concerns within the industry that with Twitter's growing strength and inevitable commercialization, Twitter either develops its own applications or acquires third-party applications for its own use. Moreover, at this developer conference, Twitter also announced the launch of its official Android application. Thus, among the three largest mainstream smartphone platforms globally, Twitter already has its own official applications (self-developed or acquired through mergers). Considering the revelation by Evan Williams, co-founder and CEO of Twitter, at the developer conference that 37% of active Twitter users access via mobile phones and hopes this ratio should reach 100%, the ultimate goal of users using Twitter only on their mobile devices makes sense. This clearly explains Twitter's motivation to risk offending developers by developing related applications itself. After all, these are indispensable for Twitter's commercialization.

Additionally, at the developer conference, Twitter emphasized the stability and availability of applications. Is this subtly warning developers? No one denies that developers have greatly enriched Twitter's applications, but the few outages that occurred previously could also be seen as negative effects of these. Before commercialization, the impact might be smaller, but after commercialization, these issues must be taken seriously and avoided as much as possible. This undoubtedly provides a good reason for Twitter's self-developed official applications and raises the threshold for developers entering Twitter's application ecosystem.

All signs indicate that during the process of commercialization, the entanglement between Twitter and developers is unavoidable. When necessary, marginalizing or sacrificing the interests of developers is reasonable.

Finally, let's look at users. The most typical example is after Twitter announced the launch of the Promoted Tweets advertising platform, according to data statistics from Twitter Sentiment, in all Twitter messages containing the #promotedtweets tag, 71% of the messages were negative. That is, most Twitter users do not like ads being inserted into search results or even personal newsfeeds. This undoubtedly puts Twitter at risk of losing some ad-averse users. More importantly, whether this form of advertising will ultimately bring real value to clients is still debatable. Although the results await further proof, the entanglement between Twitter and users has already emerged.

Through the above analysis, although this developer conference saw Twitter introduce several measures beneficial to developers, such as continuously releasing new tools, attracting developers, and removing restrictions on accessing the data stream for application developers, the trend and actions of accelerating commercialization are equally evident. In this process, the entanglement between Twitter, investors, developers, and users will deepen. How to balance these entanglements is a challenge for both Twitter's idealism and commercialization realization.
