We boarded the boat from Koh Samui to Koh Tao, found a spot on the deck, mingling among the farangs (Westerners), though in fact, we were the farangs. I lost track of the date, only reminded by the check-out time that we needed to head to our next destination. Koh Tao is located 45 kilometers north of Koh Samui and is also known as Turtle Island due to its resemblance to a turtle. This small island, with an area of only 21 square kilometers, rests on a coral reef. Most of the island is mountainous, with only 30% being flat land, yet it boasts some of Thailand's best waters. The sea here has excellent visibility, lush and beautiful corals, and abundant marine life, providing a great environment for diving, making it a well-known diving and snorkeling destination in Thailand. There are over 40 diving companies to choose from on Koh Tao, and it is said to be the cheapest place to learn diving. We were about to check into Ban’s Diving Resort. We had already arranged this on the first day in Bangkok, but even if we hadn't, it wouldn't have mattered. As soon as we got on the boat to Koh Tao, many diving schools came to promote their services. Going to a diving school doesn’t necessarily mean you have to dive; they can also provide fairly complete accommodations. In fact, Koh Tao and diving, diving and Koh Tao are inseparable. On Koh Tao, there isn't a single hotel that won't provide diving equipment. Details about diving will be introduced later. After more than 2 hours of travel, we arrived at the Koh Tao pier. We still enjoyed the reception service. Ban’s staff held up a sign to greet us, along with a British couple. We took Ban’s taxi. As mentioned earlier, Koh Tao is actually not very big. Later, we measured it, and it only takes about 10 minutes to walk from Ban’s to the pier. There is a very good BBQ restaurant near the pier, which we'll save for later. Talking about taking the taxi, it was actually a truck modified into a vehicle, open-top, with people and luggage placed at the back. It took only a few minutes to drive from the pier to Ban’s on a bumpy road, which would later become our route for boat diving. The high-voltage lines on both sides of the road hung low, buzzing. The remote and desolate feeling, without any signs of life, felt like traveling through the wilderness, truly arriving at the countryside of Thailand, where you couldn't see the sea. Later, we learned that this road was just the main traffic artery of Koh Tao, while the crowded places were along the seaside, with a continuous several-kilometer-long coastal road lined with various restaurants, convenience stores, ATMs, tattoo shops, massage parlors, and travel agencies. Upon arriving at Ban’s, we felt like we had entered a paradise, with tropical plants densely covering the area, houses wave-like distributed beside the plants, wandering farangs, and a soothing incense smell. Looking down from where we stayed, there was an unmatched sea view. And at 500B per night, it was very reasonable. With air conditioning, hot water, TV (able to receive MTV channel), it was very clean and comfortable. We stayed on Koh Tao for 5 days.

We quickly checked in, and all expenses were settled at check-out without any deposit required. This was the only time during the entire trip. It was already sunset, and we had an appointment to meet our diving instructor Karen at 6 pm. We also met two couples from Kunming. One couple was learning intermediate courses, while the other couple, like us, was learning beginner courses, Open Water courses.

Here, let me briefly talk about diving. Why do you need to learn and get certified? Simply put, if you wear goggles, carry a tank, wear flippers, and bite a breathing tube to go underwater, you need a diving license, just like driving requires a license. If you're just in shallow water, burying your head underwater to look at fish while breathing through a tube to the outside air, that's called snorkeling, purely for entertainment. The Open Water course is the beginning of scuba diving. A 3-4 day course allows you to dive up to 18 meters deep. After 2 more days of training on top of the basics, you can reach an intermediate level and dive up to 30 meters deep. Amateur enthusiasts and enthusiasts can dive at most up to 40 meters deep. If anyone is interested, you can search for related information. The price for learning an OW course on Koh Tao is between 8,000-10,000B, including basic accommodation for 4 days, and it's claimed to be the cheapest place in the world to learn diving. The diving license issued after completion is universally recognized.

Returning to the topic, after meeting Karen, because the couple taking the intermediate course was going night diving, our learning was scheduled to start tomorrow. Karen is a Singaporean Chinese, female, speaking Chinese, English, and Thai, quite impressive. On this island, let alone Chinese, even Asians are very rare. We were lucky to meet a coach who spoke Chinese, teaching us one-on-two. The four compatriots from Kunming, we spoke Sichuan dialect, they spoke Yunnan dialect, it was so enjoyable.

The prices on Koh Tao have doubled. The same items at 7-11 cost twice as much on Koh Tao as on Koh Samui, so it's strongly recommended to prepare everything needed before going to the island. The cheapest drinking water is the white plastic bottle pack, 30B for 6 bottles. The food prices are similar to Koh Samui, seafood slightly higher than Koh Samui. To protect the ecological environment, seafood harvesting is prohibited on the islands, so the seafood on the island does not have a price advantage.

The most famous SPA on Koh Tao is Jamahkir, facing the sea while enjoying SPA. On the last day of our diving, when the nitrogen in our bodies slowly dissipated, we decided to go to Jamahkir for a thorough indulgence. If there wasn't a car to pick us up, we wouldn't have been able to find this paradise. Also, if it wasn't for their four-wheel-drive pickup, we wouldn't have been able to climb those crazy steep slopes. Traveling on unmarked mountain roads, if not driving, we wouldn't have realized there was a road underfoot. The car wound its way up the mountain, feeling like another world, indeed very quiet. Looking at the sea from the top of the mountain was even more beautiful and vast. After changing clothes, we first steamed for a while, then lay on tatami mats, covered with cleansing and moisturizing lotion, wrapped like mummies, and required to lie for 30 minutes. Listening to flowing clouds and yoga-like music, I quickly fell asleep. After waking up, I cleaned myself in the outdoor shower, then started facial SPA, followed by body MASSAGE, a series of kneading, rubbing, knocking, and patting, ending with a warm back treatment. The whole set of SPA took about 3 hours, with a cost of around 5000B for two people, considered the biggest luxury of our trip.

Because of its resemblance to a turtle, it is also called Turtle Island. This small island, with an area of only 21 square kilometers, rests on a coral reef. Most of the island is mountainous, with only 30% being flat land, yet it has Thailand's best waters.

It has excellent seawater visibility, lush and beautiful corals, and rich marine life, providing an excellent diving environment, making it a well-known diving and snorkeling destination in Thailand. There are over 40 diving companies to choose from on Koh Samui and Koh Tao. Snorkeling costs about 800 baht per session, ten sessions for around 5500 baht. A four-day professional diving certification course costs about 8000 baht.

The crystal-clear seawater and colorful tropical fish make you feel like you've walked into the world of "Finding Nemo."

Koh Tao offers comprehensive accommodation and dining services, see detailed introductions in the accommodation and dining section.

Transportation: It takes 2 hours by boat from Koh Samui to Koh Tao. Both Pho Pier and Mae Nam Pier have ferry services, with round-trip prices around 850 baht, including hotel pick-up and drop-off. The main transportation on Koh Tao is pick-up taxis, costing 50 baht per person regardless of distance.
