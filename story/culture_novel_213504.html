Ma Su's loss of Jieting

by lk0fwcc on 2009-07-24 04:14:59

After Zhuge Liang pacified the southern territories, he spent another two years preparing. In the winter of 227 AD, he led his troops to garrison in Hanzhong. Since Hanzhong was close to the border between Wei and Shu, it provided a convenient location to look for opportunities to attack Wei at any time.

When he left Chengdu, Zhuge Liang submitted a memorial to the Later Lord Liu Shan, advising him not to be complacent or belittle himself; to get close to virtuous ministers and keep away from small people; and expressed his determination to take on the responsibility of restoring the Han Dynasty. This memorial is historically known as the "Chu Shi Biao".

The following year, Zhuge Liang adopted a strategy of feinting east while attacking west. He spread rumors that he would attack Mei City (today's Meixian County in Shaanxi Province) and sent the general Zhao Yun with a detachment of troops to station in Ji Valley (north of today's Baicheng in Shaanxi), pretending to attack Mei City. The Wei army received the intelligence and indeed dispatched their main force to defend Mei City. Zhuge Liang took advantage of the Wei army's lack of vigilance, personally leading the main force suddenly from the western route towards Qishan (east of today's Lixian County in Gansu Province).

The Shu army, after several years of strict training by Zhuge Liang, had a neat formation, clear orders, and high morale. Since Liu Bei's death, the Shu-Han regime had been inactive for many years, leaving the Wei state completely unprepared. This surprise attack by the Shu army on Qishan caught the Wei army off guard, causing them to retreat in defeat. Taking advantage of their victory, the Shu army advanced further. The three commanderies north of Qishan - Tianshui, Nanan, and Anding - all rebelled against Wei and sent envoys to surrender to Zhuge Liang.

At that time, Emperor Cao Pi of Wei had already died of illness. Upon hearing that Shu-Han was launching a large-scale offensive, the civil and military officials of the Wei court were all panic-stricken. The newly enthroned Emperor Cao Rui of Wei (pronounced 'rui') remained relatively calm and immediately dispatched Zhang He with fifty thousand troops to resist at Qishan, and personally went to Chang'an to oversee the battle.

Upon arriving at Qishan, Zhuge Liang decided to send a contingent to occupy Jieting (southeast of today's Zhuanglang County in Gansu Province) as a base. Who should lead this contingent? At the time, there were still several seasoned generals around him. However, he did not choose any of them but instead selected Ma Su, who served as a military advisor.

Ma Su had indeed read a lot of military books and enjoyed discussing military matters. Whenever Zhuge Liang consulted him about warfare, Ma Su could talk endlessly and had also come up with some good strategies. Therefore, Zhuge Liang trusted him greatly. However, during his lifetime, Liu Bei had observed that Ma Su was somewhat impractical. Before his death, he specifically warned Zhuge Liang, saying: "Ma Su talks more than he can deliver; he cannot be entrusted with major tasks and needs to be carefully examined." But Zhuge Liang did not take these words to heart. This time, he appointed Ma Su as the vanguard and Wang Ping as the deputy general.

Upon arriving at Jieting, Ma Su looked at the terrain and said to Wang Ping, "This area has perilous terrain, and there is a mountain next to Jieting. It's a good place to set up camp and lay an ambush."

Wang Ping reminded him, "Prime Minister specifically instructed us before leaving to hold onto the city and firmly establish our encampment. Setting up camp on the mountain is too risky."

Ma Su lacked experience in actual combat, overly confident in his extensive reading of military books, and completely ignored Wang Ping's advice, insisting on setting up camp on the mountain. Despite Wang Ping repeatedly trying to dissuade Ma Su without success, he finally pleaded with Ma Su to assign him a thousand soldiers so that he could station nearby at the foot of the mountain.
