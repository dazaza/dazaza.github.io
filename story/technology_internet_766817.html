New Surface review roundup: "Microsoft is still reciting the PC-era mantra"

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 21:54:34

**Bloomberg: High Prices Struggle to Impress Mass Consumers**

Last June, Microsoft launched the first-generation Surface, officially entering a market dominated by iPads and Android tablets. Microsoft hoped that the Surface would help users maintain continuity with Windows software as they transitioned from PCs to tablets. Unfortunately, the Surface only managed to capture a small market share. The company reported a staggering $900 million loss in its last quarter.

"Microsoft has bet the farm on touchscreens and tablets," said analyst Wes Miller. "They need to deliver something substantial now."

The release price of the Surface 2 is $50 less than the Surface RT, while the Surface Pro 2 remains at the same price as its predecessor. According to Miller, although Microsoft has equipped the Surface Pro 2 with more powerful chips and hardware to make it seem more cost-effective, the new device's price is still too high to attract most consumers.

According to IDC's report, global tablet sales totaled 44.3 million units in the second quarter of this year, with Surface accounting for only 0.7%. Additionally, an IDC report released earlier this month predicted that tablet shipments will surpass PCs for the first time in the fourth quarter, with an estimated 84.1 million tablets shipped compared to 83.1 million PCs.

---

**Reuters: Microsoft Needs Something to Revolutionize the First-Generation Surface**

Microsoft is attempting to transform itself into a hardware and service-oriented company, with Surface playing a key role. However, last year’s initial attempt with the first Surface left little impression on the market. At last night's New York event, Microsoft unveiled two new models along with a series of accessories, promising faster performance, longer battery life, and more applications.

But the initial reaction was lukewarm.

"I don't see much incentive for people to buy these devices. Yes, they are cheaper than iPads, but is that enough?" said analyst Jack Gold. "It seems like Microsoft is still reciting the mantra of the traditional PC era—upgrading chips and hardware every year with minor price reductions. I think they need something revolutionary to improve on their first product, but I didn’t see that in the new Surface."

The widely popular Office suite might be Surface's only advantage over the iPad. The Surface 2 comes preloaded with the RT version of Office 2013, including the RT version of Outlook. "The only 'killer app' here is Office," said analyst Avi Greengart. "The Surface Pro is an alternative to ultrabooks; if you also consider the touchscreen, it could be a reasonable choice."

---

**CNN: Microsoft Must Continue to Push Developers to Create RT Apps**

Last year, Microsoft did not adequately emphasize the differences between the two versions of Windows, leading many hardware manufacturers to completely abandon Windows RT. Although the app store saw some growth last year, it still lacks the full range and quality of apps available on iPads and Android devices. Innovative apps like Paper and large games such as Infinity Blade are still absent on the Surface 2.

The good news is that Windows 8.1 RT is coming soon. This new system will bring numerous upgrades, benefiting RT-based tablets like the Surface 2 the most. The improved multitasking capabilities also make the Surface 2 more user-friendly.

However, Microsoft must continue to actively encourage developers to create applications for Windows RT.

---

**Forbes: Tablets Make People “Lean Back” Instead of “Lean Forward”**

The new Surface unveiled last night represents a significant upgrade from its predecessor, with Microsoft describing the pre-installed Office-equipped Surface as a "beast." But is this "beast" really what people want to see in a tablet?

Clearly, the new Surface is more of a productivity tool. The question is whether the nature of tablets is to boost work efficiency. Most of the time, tablets serve as consumption tools rather than work devices.

Indeed, through the Surface, people can check or edit spreadsheets and documents at any time. However, tablets are usually used in a tilted position rather than sitting upright at a desk as depicted in Surface advertisements. We believe that tablets make people "lean back," whereas traditional PCs make them "lean forward." Microsoft's positioning of the Surface as a "lean forward" device clearly doesn't make sense.

Yes, the Surface running Windows 8.1 does have plenty of games and apps, and under certain circumstances, it can become a "lean back" device. But if Microsoft truly focuses on this aspect, why does it give the device professional and productivity features, such as Office, adjustable stands, and external keyboards?

A friend who works at Microsoft revealed: "The Office team wants to see Office on everything." This skews the design of the device. What Microsoft needs to do when designing the next generation of Surface is to completely remove MBA and Office members from the hardware design department and replace them with innovative teams leading the new Windows Phone and Xbox.

---

**New York Times: Price Becomes a Barrier to the Adoption of the New Surface**

The newly released second-generation Surface has only minor differences compared to the first generation, as Microsoft continues to stick with the idea of a tablet-laptop hybrid.

Apart from slight changes in color for the Surface 2, there are almost no visible changes in appearance. The starting price for the Surface 2 is $449, which is $50 lower than the first generation and cheaper than the iPad, but it still cannot compete with Android tablets from Google and Amazon.

"At this price, it will be difficult for the Surface to gain popularity because there is no clear differentiation," said Gartner analyst Carolina Milanesi. "Bundling with accessories might make them more attractive."
