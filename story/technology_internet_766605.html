Meizu MX3: A Larger Phone That's Easier to Use One-Handed

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 20:41:38

Who makes a better large-screen smartphone? Meizu attempts to answer this question with the MX3.

From the moment I got it until now, I've used the MX3 as my primary phone for 14 days, fully experiencing all aspects of the device. I tried to approach it from the perspective of an average consumer, focusing on the optimizations that the MX3 brings to large-screen interactions.

Flyme 3.0: An operating system better suited for large-screen operations

The MX3 clearly aims to improve the interaction of large-screen phones by making them operable with one hand in various scenarios.

Based on my past experiences using large-screen phones, there are several main pain points in interaction:

Problem 1: When the width of the body exceeds the length of the thumb, it becomes difficult to click buttons located at the bottom left or right of the screen;

Problem 2: When the length of the body exceeds the length of the thumb, it's hard to reach buttons at the top of the screen, no matter if they're on the left or right;

Problem 3: If the power button is located at the top of the body and the phone is long, unlocking the phone becomes troublesome;

Problem 4: If the screen bezel is narrow, it's very easy to accidentally touch the screen while holding the phone.

There are some inconveniences that you don't notice until you encounter them, but once you do, they become quite annoying—these four problems fall into that category. To address these issues, Flyme 3.0 focuses the interactive area at the bottom of the phone screen—does this effectively avoid these problems? After using it heavily for 14 days, my answer is: Yes.

In this diagram, the semi-transparent circles represent taps or clicks, and the arrows indicate swipe directions. Some of these interactions aren't new; for example, "long-press Home button to lock screen" already existed on the MX2. However, under Flyme 3.0, when paired with "Home key swipe up to unlock," the habit forms quickly.

Moreover, compared to the smaller screen of the MX2, using the Home key operation instead of the power button is especially necessary on the MX3's 5-inch large screen. After all, the length of the MX3 exceeds the length of the fingers, so either slightly adjusting your grip or using two hands is required. In situations like standing on the subway where single-handed operation is preferred, using the power button to lock or unlock the screen can be quite painful: adjusting your grip makes you worry about dropping the phone, and using both hands makes you worry about losing your grip on the railing. When using the MX3, I basically don't have to worry about these two issues.

When the screen is already lit, the "swipe up Home key" operation changes to "back." With the Smartbar already moving the back button down within apps, what Meizu has done is essentially providing users with another option. During my experience, because the SmartBar's back button is placed at the lower-left, people who primarily use their right hand may find it inconvenient, at which point directly swiping up the Home key allows you to "go back."

However, due to the visual guidance advantage of the SmartBar, in most scenarios, I still tend to press the "back" button at the lower-left. So, these two ways of "going back," I utilize both. For me, when using applications that follow iOS design guidelines but don’t respect Android design principles, this at least reduces a significant amount of frustration.

After all, due to the limitation of screen display area, most applications generally have several layers designed to show different content. This design causes us to frequently click the "back" button to return to the previous interface. This results in "going back" being one of our most commonly used operations.

Of course, the feature that best demonstrates Meizu's thought process regarding the large-screen phone experience is the "pull-down hover." This interaction balances the need for content display with operational convenience. For instance, in a news app where the entire screen is filled with a list of news items, since the length of the large-screen phone exceeds the length of the thumb, if you want to click on a news item at the top of the screen, you would either need to adjust your grip or use both hands.

The "pull-down hover" interaction lets you pull the list down, pausing it slightly in the middle of the screen for a short time, allowing you to directly click on items at the top of the list without needing to change your grip or use both hands. The "pull-down hover" is a great idea, unfortunately, currently, there aren't many apps that support this feature, such as WeChat's contact list. Because of this, occasionally when using the MX3, I still need to adjust my grip or switch to two-handed operation.

Personally, I hope more apps will support the "pull-down hover" feature, after all, clicking on items at the top of the application list is also part of daily operations. Additionally, because the MX3 adopts a design similar to the iPad mini's "anti-mis-touch" feature, even if the palm touches the edge of the screen, it doesn't affect other operations, solving a large portion of interaction problems.

Overall, thanks to the improvements in interaction with Flyme 3.0, ordinary consumers can perform single-handed operations in most cases.

As 5-inch screens become the standard for flagship devices this year, Meizu has given serious thought to improving the large-screen interaction, and both the attitude and results deserve praise—the dance performed with shackles is even more graceful.

Other features

Without the "Meizu" stamp, the appearance of the MX3 looks more consistent. When the screen is off, the front of the device appears as a clean black plane, looking deep and mysterious. The Home button below also serves as a breathing light, flashing with vitality. The stainless steel metal frame around the body enhances the overall style of the phone and enriches its color.

After 14 days of continuous use, whether it's the appearance or feel, I still maintain the same evaluation I had when I first handled it: sleek design, rich texture.

Because of its exaggerated back curve, the MX3 looks thin, giving a sense of lightness;

The back curve of the phone fits the curve of the palm, and when holding the phone with five fingers spread out, you won't feel any stiffness. You'll find that the rounded body of the MX3 gives a rich texture.

Furthermore, it's clear that the MX3 tries to compress the size and weight of the body. In situations like "phone marathons" where the phone is held up for a long time, it doesn't make you feel tired—whereas when I used the Galaxy Note in the past, I would start feeling tired in just a few minutes. Of course, there's a significant difference in weight between these two devices, with the former weighing 143 grams and the latter 178 grams.

Compared to the MX2, the MX3 looks more like an inkstone than a brick. It fits comfortably in my pocket without causing too much pressure.

The edges of the MX3 (left) and MX2 (right) show a difference in R-angle.

In terms of craftsmanship, compared to similarly priced models, the MX3 still stands out as being well-crafted. There are no burrs around the headphone jack, three microphone holes, etc., and they are neatly cut. The stainless steel metal frame tightly aligns with the front panel. Unfortunately, although Meizu has improved the difficulty of removing the back cover, there is still a small gap between the back cover and the body, visible only upon close inspection.

As for the MX3's screen, it shows significant improvement over the MX2 in terms of display precision, color control, brightness, etc. Even in sunlight, when adjusted to maximum brightness, the content on the screen remains visible rather than completely dark.

Similarly, the heat control of the MX3 has also improved significantly, addressing issues reported by MX2 users such as "large instantaneous heat generation" and "long usage leading to overheating." Now, the heat performance of the MX3 is comparable to the Nexus 4, falling within an acceptable range.

Additionally, the camera on the MX3 is commendable, performing almost as excellently as the iPhone 5, with the only drawback being a slight yellowish tint in the photos.

Although the MX3's battery capacity is only 2400 mAh, thanks to multiple energy-saving functions, over the course of these 14 days, the power performance of the MX3 can be rated as "good": Basically, turning on at 7 AM and shutting down at 9 PM, during normal use, averaging brushing through WeChat fifteen to twenty times, checking Weibo five to six times, viewing notifications ten to fifteen times, and making three to five calls per day.

Regarding the features of the Flyme 3.0 system, they've been explained earlier, so I won't go into detail here. Personally, I find the newly added "My Footprints" function in the contacts app very practical. It automatically records how many calls we made each day, to whom, how many photos were taken, when, and how many notes (system-built-in app) were created, etc. This function is equivalent to automatically keeping a diary for me, saving a lot of effort, and making it convenient for me to review later.

Some questions Meizu still needs to answer

For large-screen interaction, the efforts made by Meizu are worth praising. However, above the application lists on the large screen, there is also the "notification center." Currently, when I want to check notifications, I still need to stretch my finger to the top of the screen to pull down the notification center. Since checking notifications is also part of daily operations, if the notification-checking operation isn't optimized, the problem of "large-screen interaction" hasn't been fully resolved.

In addition, besides Meizu, various domestic phone manufacturers are also working on the experience of "large-screen interaction," and several different solutions have already emerged. At this point, does having a good experience alone convince consumers to buy? These are the questions Meizu needs to answer as it continues to develop.

Finally, summarizing after 14 days of use, and heavy, uncaring use, the MX3's performance is commendable, considering the feelings of single-handed users in most situations, and showing significant improvement in power consumption and heat dissipation compared to the past. Its camera performance lives up to the slogan of "the best 8-megapixel camera" announced at the launch. Whether you like Meizu or not, the MX3 is an Android phone that can be used as your primary device.
