Magician guesses lottery numbers continues, claiming to achieve it by hypnotizing number selection experts

by anonymous on 2012-02-24 10:50:19

A screenshot of the live TV broadcast showing the predicted winning lottery numbers. TV experts have stated that during this time, the left half of the TV screen was replaced with a still image.

According to reports from British media on the 12th, renowned British magician Darren Brown recently astonished the entire UK by accurately predicting the winning numbers of the UK lottery under the watchful eyes of millions of television viewers. On the evening of the 11th, Brown revealed the secrets behind his "stunning magic" as promised in a TV special. However, Brown's explanation was nothing more than a bluff, claiming that he had used hypnosis to select the winning numbers. This explanation was far from convincing. British TV experts explained that video conferencing software was used, and during the live broadcast, the station employed a "screen split" technique.

In the decryption process, Brown resorted to bluffing. According to reports, the live show where Brown predicted the winning lottery numbers on September 9th attracted 2.7 million (some say 3 million) viewers, surpassing the 2.4 million audience of the live draw broadcast on the same night. When the video of the program was uploaded to YouTube, it garnered over 500,000 views within just two days. Although Brown claimed he was prohibited from buying lottery tickets and thus couldn't win the £2.4 million prize, he gained immense popularity.

On the evening of the 11th, Brown revealed the secrets of his "stunning magic" as promised in a special TV program. However, he did not disclose any reasonable secrets but instead confused the audience with his bluff. Brown claimed that he formed a group of 24 people backstage, hypnotized them, and had them each pick six numbers while in a semi-dream state. Finally, he added up all the numbers chosen by everyone and divided them by 24 to get the average, which turned out to be the six winning numbers he selected for his magic performance on the 9th.

Brown’s vague explanation disappointed the audience. He stated that this calculation method was invented by Victorian polymath Francis Galton, and centuries ago, people used this method at markets to guess the weight of a bull, thereby maximizing the use of "collective wisdom."

Clearly, Brown's nearly "fantastical" statement failed to convince anyone, leaving the eagerly anticipating British audience disappointed and angry after hearing his so-called "explanation." On the evening of the 11th, Oxford University mathematics professor Roger Brown said this explanation was "pure rubbish." A spokesperson for the UK lottery company also clearly stated: "Darren Brown is a magician who can only create the illusion of being able to predict winning lottery numbers."

Experts pointed out the use of "screen split" technology. After repeated studies of the magic video by British TV experts, they provided the most reasonable explanation thus far. Experts claimed that during the live broadcast, the station used high-tech "screen split" technology —— dividing the entire screen into two parts, with the left side showing a white rack holding six ping-pong balls and the right side showing the live TV broadcast. When Brown walked from the left side to the right side and stood in front of the TV watching the live draw, the image of the white ping-pong ball rack on the left was immediately replaced by a false still image, allowing staff to quickly write the numbers on the white balls after the results were announced. After completing this trick, the still image on the left was immediately replaced by the live broadcast, enabling Brown to calmly walk to the white ping-pong ball rack and reveal the balls.

Experts pointed out that they discovered this flaw because the TV in the video clip wobbled for a few seconds, but the ping-pong ball rack on the left remained perfectly stable. Experts emphasized that during the performance, Brown had to precisely control the timing. If he moved to the left side of the screen a few seconds earlier, he might have been covered by the still image and thus "disappeared" from the screen. Wu Wen
