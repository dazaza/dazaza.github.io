IBM and Nvidia to co-design and develop supercomputers

by anonymous on 2013-11-28 11:10:12

IBM and Nvidia announced on Monday that they will collaborate on the design and development of supercomputer systems and servers.

In the list of the world's top 500 most powerful supercomputers released this week, both IBM and Nvidia have seen their positions rise. In terms of the number of supercomputer systems built, only HP surpasses IBM, though not by a significant margin. Nvidia chips are used in 38 of the supercomputers on this list. These Nvidia chips help to accelerate computing speeds while controlling power consumption.

Nvidia primarily produces GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) chips. GPUs are particularly well-suited for floating-point operations, which form the basis of computer game image rendering and were Nvidia's initial main business. Floating-point operations mathematically resemble the computations involved in simulating and visualizing complex engineering and scientific problems, and they are also applicable to creating special effects for movies.

In these areas, GPUs outperform traditional CPUs, such as Intel Xeon processors and AMD Opteron processors. The key difference with GPUs is their ability to handle a large number of smaller computational tasks in parallel while keeping power consumption within a certain level. CPUs, on the other hand, have less parallel computing capability and are better suited for single computational tasks.

Currently, computing devices based on GPUs are rapidly developing, with the majority of GPU chips coming from Nvidia, although there are some from AMD and Intel as well. The use of GPUs in supercomputers around the world is becoming increasingly common. GPUs are the backbone of Titan, currently the second most powerful supercomputer in the world, which was once ranked first globally.

In this collaboration, IBM will work with Nvidia to develop supercomputers and Power series enterprise servers. The two companies will share technology to enable easier communication between Nvidia's Tesla GPUs and IBM's Power8 processors.

Although this collaboration mainly concerns supercomputers, working with Nvidia will benefit IBM's Power server business. IBM ranks first in server sales, with revenues of about $3.3 billion in the second quarter according to IDC data. However, overall, the Unix server market is clearly declining. IDC points out that by 2017, Unix server revenue will drop by over $1 billion.

Enterprise users are replacing expensive, specialized Unix servers with cheaper Linux servers. These Linux servers typically use industry-standard Intel or AMD processors. Introducing GPU computing capabilities may not reverse this decline trend but still benefits IBM. This also means that Nvidia's GPU computing technology will be applied to the enterprise market.
