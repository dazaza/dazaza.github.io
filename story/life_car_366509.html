Maintenance, Usage, and Taboos of Spark Plugs

1. Avoid long-term neglect of cleaning carbon deposits. During use, normal carbon deposits will form on the electrodes and insulator skirts of the spark plug. If these deposits are not cleaned regularly, they will accumulate more and more, eventually causing the electrode to leak electricity or even fail to spark. Therefore, carbon deposits should be cleared periodically, without waiting for the spark plug to stop working before cleaning. Seven major taboos in using car spark plugs include the fault of no spark on the Bluebird exhaust side spark plug.

2. Avoid prolonged use. There are many types of spark plugs, but each has its own economic lifespan. If used beyond this period, it will hinder the performance and economy of the engine. Studies have shown that as the service life of the spark plug extends, the center electrode tip will change to a rounded shape, and the side electrode will change to a concave arc shape. This shape will increase the electrode gap and make discharging difficult, affecting the normal operation of the engine.

3. Avoid arbitrary cleaning. Some people do not pay attention to the cleanliness of the spark plug's exterior when spraying silver powder on the engine or performing other maintenance, causing the spark plug to leak electricity due to dirt on its surface. When cleaning the exterior, one should not use sandpaper or metal sheets for convenience and speed; instead, the spark plug should be immersed in gasoline and brushed with a brush to ensure that the ceramic body of the spark plug is not damaged.

4. Avoid burning. In reality, some people often use fire to remove carbon deposits and oil stains from the spark plug electrodes and skirts. Although this method may seem effective, it is actually very harmful. Since the temperature during burning is difficult to control, it is easy to crack the insulating skirt, causing the spark plug to leak electricity. Moreover, the fine cracks caused by burning are often difficult to detect, bringing great trouble to troubleshooting. The correct way to handle carbon deposits and oil stains on the spark plug is either to use specialized equipment for cleaning, which will yield good results, or solution cleaning, where the spark plug is soaked in alcohol or gasoline for a certain period of time. Once the carbon deposits soften, they can be brushed clean and dried.

5. Avoid ignoring heat classification. Besides having different shapes and sizes, spark plugs also come in cold, medium, and hot types. Generally, high-compression ratio, high-speed engines are suitable for cold-type spark plugs; low-compression ratio, low-speed engines are suitable for hot-type spark plugs, and those in between are suitable for medium-type spark plugs. Additionally, the selection of spark plugs for new engines or overhauled engines can differ from old engines based on actual conditions. For example, when the engine is relatively new, the selected spark plug should lean towards the hot type; for older engines with degraded performance, spark plugs tend to produce excessive carbon deposits and become oil-stained easily, so medium or cold-type spark plugs should be chosen to enhance their oil resistance.

6. Avoid misdiagnosis and wrong judgment. When replacing new spark plugs or suspecting a fault requiring inspection, the spark plug should be removed after the vehicle has been running normally for a while, then the color characteristics of its electrodes should be observed. There are several possible situations: if the center electrode is reddish-brown and the side electrode and surrounding areas are bluish-gray, it indicates the spark plug is appropriately selected; if there is burnout or melting between the electrodes and the insulator and skirt are white-hot, it suggests the spark plug is overheated; if there are black streaks on the electrodes and insulator skirt, it means the spark plug has leaked air. If the spark plug is improperly selected or leaking air, a suitable spark plug should be reselected.

7. Avoid over-tightening. When installing a spark plug, the specified torque must be followed. However, it should not be installed too loosely either, as this can cause the engine to operate abnormally.
