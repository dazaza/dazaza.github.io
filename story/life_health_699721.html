I. Adhere to the dietary principles of low sugar, low fat, and normal protein

Dietary control should be calculated reasonably. There are generally two types: rough and fine. Fine calculation applies to hospitals; rough calculation applies to families. The daily staple food (carbohydrates) supply for ordinary diabetic patients is 250-400 grams, with 30-40 grams of protein and about 50 grams of fat in side dishes. A high-protein diet is suitable for diabetic patients who have been suffering from wasting diseases for a long time, with a total daily intake of main and side dishes of no less than 100 grams of protein. II. Prohibit eating sweets with high sugar content

Sugar and sweets should be listed as items not to be eaten. Fruits depend on the condition; they should not be eaten when the condition is unstable or severe, and can be eaten in small amounts when controlled better, while observing the effects on urine sugar and blood sugar, and fruits that significantly increase urine sugar and blood sugar are best avoided. Spicy and stimulating items like tobacco and alcohol should also be discontinued.

III. Develop a dietary restriction plan based on the severity of the condition

Mild patients are often obese, and appropriately restricting diet is the main therapy. A low-calorie diet should be adopted, and the restriction of staple foods can be achieved through step-by-step reduction or gradual reduction methods. Gradual reduction can promptly alleviate the burden on pancreatic cells, which generally yields better results. If hunger is intense, vegetables with low sugar content can be selected to quell hunger. For those who eat three meals a day, the distribution of dietary calories can be arranged according to the ratio of breakfast 1/5, lunch 2/5, and dinner 2/5. Those who adopt a small frequent meal system will find it even more beneficial to alleviate the sugar load of each meal. Moderate and severe patients need to pay attention to dietary restrictions while undergoing medication.
