Mainly used for the authentication of porcelain

by fbfshtwk0423 on 2012-02-08 10:40:03

Some high-quality pieces from eras close to or from later times imitating earlier eras are often very difficult to distinguish due to the similarity in decorative patterns, painting styles, and glaze materials. For example, Yongle and Xuande blue-and-white flared-rim bowls mostly have three layers of decoration inside the bowl and four layers on the outside. At first glance, the body and glaze seem similar; all are flared-rim bowls with circular feet. However, upon closer inspection, one can find an important difference in the degree of convergence at the lower part of the bowl's abdomen—Yongle bowls have a fuller abdomen, while Xuande bowls taper slightly. The Chenghua blue-and-white flared-rim bowls imitated during the Yongzheng period also have the defect of a slightly tapered abdomen in their shape. These subtle differences arise because when replicating artifacts based on physical objects, limitations in forming and firing techniques or occasional oversights create flaws (the shapes of replicas often differ somewhat from genuine pieces. Although replicas are made according to the original true piece, before firing, they may be identical to the original, but after high-temperature firing, there will be differences in some places compared to the original. This might be due to inconsistent preparation and refinement of raw materials, resulting in longitudinal and lateral contraction and expansion at high temperatures, which is the key to identifying the shape). As for some replicas made solely based on hearsay or speculation, like so-called "Yongle hand-pressure cups" imitated in later generations, their shapes become increasingly bizarre (starting from the Jiajing and Wanli periods, they were imitated larger and larger, and later even became large bowls instead of cups). If one has seen the genuine article, it naturally prevents rash judgments and speculations.

After understanding the basic characteristics of Yuan, Ming, and Qing porcelain shapes, one should further master the method of observing shapes. Generally, one should first pay attention to the rim, belly, and base. Many similar vessels look extremely alike at first glance, but by carefully observing these three parts, different conclusions can be drawn. For example, Ming mid-period bottles, pots, and jars often have obvious joining marks on the belly, whereas Qing dynasty products, due to meticulous trimming, rarely show such marks. Such seemingly minor differences often play a significant role in dating and authenticity identification. Another example is that Yuan dynasty large plates have smaller and shallower curves, while Ming Yongle and Xuande large plates have slightly larger and deeper curves; the former has a smaller base, while the latter has a larger base. In particular, Yongle shapes, whether large or small plates and bowls, often have a concave center and a convex bottom center, and the inner wall of the foot slants outward slightly, about half to a quarter shorter than the outer wall. Other examples include many civilian kiln plates and bowls from the late Ming to early Qing dynasties that often have obvious wheel-turned marks (so-called "jumping knife" marks), which are extremely rare in official kiln porcelains. As for Kangxi large plates, some have double-circle bases, and the bottoms of civilian kiln tri-colored flat-bottomed vessels often have "hemp cloth patterns," which are more common features of the shapes at that time. Cultural relics workers often use these as partial criteria when distinguishing between old and new, genuine and fake items.

This mainly applies to the identification of porcelain. During the Qing dynasty, both the forms and varieties increased significantly and were finely crafted. Among them, Kangxi's innovative works stood out uniquely. Examples of unique forms in carved wares include pipa vases, horseshoe vases, elephant leg vases, phoenix-tail vases, Guanyin vases, Taibai vases, apple vases, apricot leaf vases, baton vases, bubbling oranges, inverted loading vases, willow leaf vases, and convex-belly flower vases, which were rare in previous dynasties. The Yongzheng period was also rich in creativity regarding vessel forms, including Shuanglu vases, Three Sheep vases, Dragon Ear vases, Deer Head vases, Net vases, Ox Head vases, Garlic Mouth Sash如意Respect vases, flared olive vases, Taibai tanks, chrysanthemum petal plates, etc. Especially, the imitation of Song dynasty famous kilns and Yongle, Chenghua porcelains not only vividly mimicked the body and glaze but also in shape could pass off as genuine. In the Qianlong period, prominent forms included neck-turning vases, heart-turning vases, belt-turning vases, flower baskets, fans, and book-style seal boxes, book-style golden bells, etc. During this period, both innovation and imitation reached a climax, and the imitations of copper, stone, lacquer, jade, bamboo, wooden wares, and animal-like items were highly realistic. After the Jiaqing and Daoguang periods, most works followed old traditions with few innovations. Shapes evolved from exquisite to coarse, gradually losing the excellent traditions of earlier times. For instance, the Yuhuchun bottle shape had little distinction across the Kangxi, Yongzheng, and Qianlong reigns, then gradually became clumsy, and by the Tongzhi, Guangxu, and Xuantong periods, it transformed into a short-necked, full-bodied squat form, far less elegant and beautiful than before.
