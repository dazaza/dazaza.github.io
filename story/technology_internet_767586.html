Xiaomi's next battle

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 11:19:13

Yesterday, the tech blog Business Insider reported that Google will launch its own smartwatch at the end of this month, codenamed "Gem." Just ten days ago, domestic site Pingwest also revealed that Xiaomi is about to launch its own smartwatch, with rumors suggesting that the price of Xiaomi's first smartwatch will be set at 299 yuan. For a time, smartwatches have once again become the focus of industry discussion. However, an insider from Xiaomi explicitly told *The Business Value* that the circulating rumors about Xiaomi making a watch are "unreliable."

Noises from all sides make it hard to discern truth from falsehood, but one undeniable point is that Xiaomi will leverage its powerful brand momentum to expand into more areas. In the past three years, Xiaomi has been extending its reach starting with smartphones priced around 2,000 yuan. From the Xiaomi Box launched at the end of last year, to the Redmi phone targeting the thousand-yuan market segment released not long ago, and now the newly launched Xiaomi TV, Xiaomi attempts to replicate its success in more fields, keeping users continuously “screaming.” The only question at hand is whether Xiaomi will really choose smartwatches as its next battlefield?

To figure this out, let's put ourselves in Lei Jun’s shoes and consider what factors need to be taken into account when entering a new market.

For today’s Xiaomi, prudence should be the top priority. Although Xiaomi, which prides itself on being "feverish," emphasizes innovation, this kind of innovation today will certainly not be as radical as Google Glass. Competition in Xiaomi's main battleground, the smartphone market, is becoming increasingly fierce, with a large number of domestic manufacturers attempting to encircle Xiaomi. Xiaomi’s current position is vastly different from what it was three years ago, with significantly increased trial-and-error costs. Xiaomi TV could have been launched in April this year, but for the sake of prudence, it was postponed until September, allowing LeTV to steal some of the spotlight. A while ago, the popular Redmi phone also had two development plans, h1 and h2, chosen for their prudence; the final Redmi turned out to be h2.

Mi must balance prudence with innovation and will inevitably choose a relatively mature product with a certain market foundation. Xiaomi's recent entry into smart TVs fits this category: a sufficiently large market that has been dominated by home appliance manufacturers but has remained lukewarm. Representing internet forces, Xiaomi indeed holds a winning advantage over traditional forces. Yesterday, Xiaomi TV officially went on sale, and the first batch of 3,000 units sold out within 1 minute and 58 seconds.

After understanding these points, let’s examine whether smartwatches are suitable for Xiaomi.

The current smartwatch market is already quite active, whether it's foreign tech giants or local startups; there are already many smartwatches on the market. But from the current situation, smartwatches mainly replicate and optimize phone functions without producing any disruptive innovative experiences. Gu Xiaobin, CEO of GooKoo Electronics, though revealing a few days ago via Weibo that the shipment volume of the Geak Watch priced at 1,999 yuan exceeded 100,000 units, faced significant doubts, accusing him of inflating the data.

This form of product, the smartwatch, currently lacks a solid user base. Launching such a product can help Xiaomi maintain its "innovative" and "feverish" brand image, but from the perspective of market feedback, it is difficult for such a product to achieve high sales. Dedicating resources to develop such a product does not align with Xiaomi's current interests.

Xiaomi has not overlooked watches altogether. An informed source revealed that at the beginning of this year, when wearable device trends were just emerging, Xiaomi did seriously consider it and even developed prototype smartwatches. However, after Lei Jun later re-emphasized the company's "focus," the watch project was shelved.

In summary, smartwatches are not a good choice for Xiaomi at present. So, excluding smartwatches, what other fields are worth Xiaomi entering?

Actually, reviewing the aforementioned conditions reveals that Xiaomi's next target will definitely not be something completely obscure to outsiders but rather a relatively traditional direction that has already been recognized by the industry. From this perspective, sorting through the traditional hardware products that internet forces have tried to invade in the past six months gives us a glimpse into Xiaomi's next likely target.

Yesterday's biggest news in China's hardware domain was JiLuYou announcing securing tens of millions of dollars in financing, valuing over a hundred million dollars, becoming the second hardware manufacturer after Xiaomi to secure tens of millions of dollars in funding. In recent months, routers have become a new arena for competition among internet giants like Baidu and Qihoo 360, who have all launched related products.

As the network center of the home, routers are extremely important traffic entry points. In the era of mobile internet, the strategic importance of routers has reached unprecedented heights. Only routers can unify wireless and PC networks in homes, forming the most critical link in multi-screen interaction. Moreover, by occupying such an important position, internet companies can bundle many other backend services, such as video and games, which aligns with Xiaomi's consistent approach of combining software and hardware.

Another point is that today's routers are "anti-human" products. Complicated operations frustrate most users, and worse still, routers have seen virtually no substantial technological progress in the past decade. Reviewing Xiaomi's product line expansion rules over the past two years shows that disrupting such a mature market representing "backward productivity" is something Xiaomi excels at and enjoys doing. If Xiaomi really enters the router market, it would be reasonable.

Besides routers, although the market prospects for smartwatches remain uncertain, fitness bands have been popular in IT and internet circles for a long time. Entering through a fitness band and eventually upgrading to a smartwatch seems like a feasible path. A notable example is Fitbit, which recently launched a new product called Force, adding an LCD screen to the Flex band launched earlier this year, capable of displaying time information. In a sense, this is already a low-end smartwatch. Perhaps Xiaomi will start with such a band product and, when the timing is right, gradually evolve it into a true smartwatch. Actually, considering the previously rumored price of 299 yuan, it would be difficult to produce a smartwatch meeting Xiaomi's quality standards, but creating a fitness band seems much more reasonable.

If viewed from a "conspiracy theory" angle, since the rumor about the smartwatch emerged, Xiaomi's official channels have never denied it, possibly intentionally confusing public perception to divert attention and hide Xiaomi's real intentions. Looking back at Xiaomi's product release cycle, Xiaomi Box was released in mid-November last year, so perhaps it won't be long before Xiaomi's next goal is truly revealed. However, taking all factors into account, between routers and fitness bands, the possibility of routers is naturally greater. Whether right or wrong, the answer will become clear within a month.
