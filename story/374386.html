Yangshuo Shangri-La

by zhou519621 on 2010-12-11 17:17:29

Going north along the Guiyang Highway, only a few minutes' drive from Yangshuo, you arrive at "Shangri-La." It stands quietly by the edge of the city like a simple village girl, unpretentious and natural, full of true charm. If it is the season when "apricot flowers wet your clothes and willow winds blow without a chill," stepping into "Shangri-La" presents before your eyes a beautiful landscape of mountains and fields. The rippling Yanzi Lake is embedded in vast green plains, like a young girl's bright eyes filled with affection. The weeping willows by the lake gently touch the water surface. A huge water-powered cylinder wheel creaks slowly, turning with the passage of time, singing ancient country songs. Looking far away, one sees distant mountains covered in emerald green, villages shrouded in mist, crisscrossing paths, scattered houses, just like the idyllic scene described by Tao Yuanming: "fragrant grasses lush and fresh, petals falling in abundance," and "good fields, beautiful pools, mulberry and bamboo groves."

Taking a small boat to tour Yanzi Lake is undoubtedly an enjoyment. As the small boat glides gracefully on the lake's emerald silk-like surface, cutting through waves, the hearts of the tourists feel as carefree and delighted as birds "long confined in a cage, now returned to nature." With vast skies and clouds nearby, broad shores and calm waters, the fresh and vast embrace of nature will cleanse all worldly worries and mundane thoughts.

Suddenly, a clear and melodious song drifts over. It turns out that the girls on the singing stage by the shore are dancing and singing. Dressed in bright ethnic costumes, they warmly welcome the arrival of distant guests with their richly flavored ethnic dances and songs.

Passing the singing stage, the small boat enters a narrow waterway. Who would have thought that behind Yanzi Lake lies such a winding, secluded path leading to hidden beauty. - Shangri-La is an open scenic area; it has no walls or barriers. The fields, mountains, bridges, roads, and cottages are seamlessly integrated, embracing the great beauty of heaven and earth.

The waterway becomes narrower and narrower. The small boat passes through the completely dark Yanzi Cave, like night navigation, and a "very narrow passageway, barely wide enough for a person" after which it seems as if "mountains and rivers repeat endlessly, making one doubt there is no way out." Unexpectedly, the view suddenly opens up, revealing a bright and flowery scene - Peach Blossom Island has arrived. The island is not large, surrounded by water on all sides, but lush with abundant vegetation, various trees in bloom, and red peach blossoms laughing brightly and passionately. What a poetic scene of "the sun rising, the river flowers redder than fire, spring coming, the river water bluer than indigo."

After Peach Blossom Island, one can experience the charm of the primitive tribe. Suddenly, alarms sound long and loud from the hill ahead, with drum beats echoing. It turns out that the indigenous people of the primitive tribe have discovered strangers from outside and rush down the hill holding spears and fishing forks, dancing joyfully in national dances. These indigenous people cover themselves with tree bark and feathers; they are robust and strong, with dark skin, clearly belonging to an extremely resilient tribe. Through them, you can imagine how wise and tenacious the ancestors of the Chinese nation were.

Crossing the waters of the primitive tribe, the small boat slowly returns along the scattered villages. This is the real countryside, with blue-tiled roofs, mud walls, bamboo fences, and vegetable patches, the sounds of chickens and dogs clearly audible. Occasionally, groups of village women can be seen washing clothes by the river, their wooden mallets knocking out ancient rhythms on the stone slabs. Farmers carrying buckets walk barefoot through the fields, playful children frolic in the empty courtyards in front of the houses, and elderly fishermen, smoking a pipe, sit leisurely on bamboo rafts, drifting with the clouds' shadows in the water. Coming from the primitive tribe to the present-day village, history leaps across hundreds of thousands of years, truly creating a sense of bewilderment and uncertainty about what era one is in.

If interested, after ending the water journey and getting ashore, tourists can also sing mountain songs in the绣ball tower, throw embroidered balls, examine folk customs in the grand folk museum, visit distinctive architectural structures like totem poles and wind-rain bridges, and admire or purchase handwoven goods made by minority women. However, as the ancient saying goes: "the benevolent love the mountains, the wise love the water."
