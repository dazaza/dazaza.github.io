User Snatches up Xiaomi 3 With First Salary: Only Ends Up With Heartache

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 19:51:15

After replacing the phone once, the water ripple issue remains prominent.

Last week, our newspaper published a detailed report titled "The Hard-to-get Xiaomi: The Mystery of Hunger Marketing and False Purchases," which sparked significant reactions across various forums, media outlets, and users. So, for those lucky enough to secure a phone, does the experience live up to the claims of the "world's fastest" as advertised by Xiaomi 3?

From the Huang Qiang investigation, for some Mi Fans, this "luck" marked the beginning of an "unfortunate" experience: using their first paycheck to buy disappointment, issues with the Xiaomi 3 persist even after replacement, “I feel like dying,” and the disillusionment of long-time users who have transitioned from "Mi Fans" to "Mi Black"… In reality, what is more heartbreaking and disappointing for these users is not just the numerous problems with Xiaomi 3, but also the cold and procedural responses from Xiaomi’s official channels.

First Paycheck Bought Heartbreak

"I earned my first paycheck in life and immediately set out to buy a new phone." Xiao Chuan said. For this 22-year-old young man from Dalian, his tone was filled with the melancholy and heartbreak of shattered idol worship: "The result I got after following Xiaomi 3 for so long..."

Half a month ago, on October 15th during Xiaomi 3's first round of flash sales, Xiao Chuan used his first paycheck to successfully grab one of the much-talked-about "Xiaomi 3s." "The rush to purchase was far more intense than crossing a narrow bridge with thousands of people; it was utterly exhausting," he recalled.

Xiao Chuan had classmates and friends participating in the flash sale, but none were as fortunate as him: "Many of them tried multiple times but came away empty-handed. Some even bought from scalpers at a higher price."

For Xiao Chuan, a self-proclaimed "grassroots youth," the 1999 yuan priced Xiaomi 3 wasn't particularly cost-effective in the eyes of most people — this 1999 yuan was not only his first paycheck but would be his sole income for a considerable period. In the current difficult employment environment for university graduates, Xiao Chuan, having graduated from an automation major, entered the real estate sales industry but was forced to resign due to the sluggish property market and has yet to find a new job.

On October 18th, Xiao Chuan received his Xiaomi 3. The moment he opened the box, "my excitement could only be described as trembling with anticipation."

This excitement didn’t last long. He soon discovered various issues with the Xiaomi 3. "Camera imaging problems, touchscreen sensitivity issues, headphone problems, speaker problems, etc., everything except the water ripple problem others encountered is there." Xiao Chuan sighed helplessly, "What bad luck."

He attempted to contact Xiaomi customer service, but the calls were difficult to get through. Contacting the local Xiaomi store also proved unsuccessful. Later, he saw someone say that updating the system might solve the problem, but after updating to the latest development version, the issues remained unresolved. Out of desperation, he posted on Xiaomi's official forum under the Xiaomi 3 BUG feedback section, hoping to receive a "reasonable explanation and expected solution from Xiaomi's official side."

In this post dated October 25th, Xiao Chuan listed five major problems with his Xiaomi 3. For instance: touchscreen sensitivity issues where typing results in missed inputs, let alone gaming; camera imaging problems where noise points are very severe in low-light environments, almost like snowflakes, and focusing becomes blurry even in normal light conditions, manual focus and flashlight-activated photos come out black, etc.; significant electrical current sounds when inserting Xiaomi headphones without playing music; serious battery drainage and overheating while charging slowly, making the phone barely usable for half a day...

"Mainly because there are too many problems, and some of these issues I only discovered after seeing other people's feedback and then testing myself. It turned out to be true," Xiao Chuan told reporters. "We understand that newly launched phones may have some optimization issues, but Xiaomi 3 has been prepared for such a long time. Shouldn't it have undergone strict testing before mass production and sales? Regardless of the issues, they are certainly not consumer problems and should not be borne by me, right?"

Replacement Issues Persist, Feeling Like Dying

"This truly broke my heart as a Mi Fan," Xiao Chuan said to Huang Qiang on October 28th. In his view, what saddened him more was not the number of quality issues with the phone, but Xiaomi's official attitude — days passed, and his multi-page post received no response from anyone at Xiaomi.

Xiao Chuan was not the only one encountering such issues; most replies under his post were from Xiaomi 3 users seeking similar responses. Similarly, in the Xiaomi 3BUG feedback section, a complaint about the "water ripple problem" went unanswered for several days.

Out of desperation, Xiao Chuan joined a rights protection group, attempting to use collective force to gain more attention. By October 29th, there were already several rights protection groups concerning Xiaomi 3 quality issues, including "Xiaomi Touchscreen Exchange" and "Xiaomi 3 Camera Problems," each with around two hundred members — at that time, according to Xiaomi's official data, only 200,000 units of Xiaomi 3 had been released in two rounds.

On Xiaomi's forum, Baidu Tieba, and Weibo, there were also numerous complaints from Xiaomi 3 users. These issues were similar to those encountered by Xiao Chuan and other users, summarizing to "serious overheating," "13-megapixel camera black screen with severe noise and unclear focus," "severe headphone current sound," "touchscreen malfunction," "intermittent call signals," "unable to connect to mobile internet," etc.

"A few days ago, I was praising how great Xiaomi 2 was, today, Xiaomi 3 gives me a non-functional touchscreen." On October 23rd, "Ruo Han Guai Guai," who had been using Xiaomi 2 for over a year and also secured Xiaomi 3 in the first batch, commented via @Our Newspaper, saying, "Testing with official debugging software did not resolve the issues, ruling out software problems, it's a hardware defect."

For these users, it's unclear whether securing a Xiaomi 3 among millions of people is a "stroke of luck" or an "unfortunate event." Xiao Yu from Xiangtan, Hunan, was one of those who quickly transformed from "lucky" to "unfortunate." His friend described him to Huang Qiang as "almost crazy," while Xiao Yu himself said to Huang Qiang in his first sentence: "I feel like dying, I'm going to report to 12315... "

For this young man, Xiaomi 3 has so far only brought him gray memories. Successfully purchasing on October 15th, receiving the delivery on the morning of the 20th, discovering the camera black screen issue in manual exposure mode on the same day, realizing it was a hardware problem on the 24th, and sending it back for replacement, receiving the new phone on the 29th — unexpectedly, this was the start of even greater heartache.

"Upon receiving the replacement phone, I found the camera black screen issue persisted. Following online guides, I updated to the development version, still with no effect, and simultaneously discovered a new issue: intermittent phone signal jumping between one bar to four bars. Contacting customer service, their reply was actually questioning where I was? Saying 'Could the signal really be bad in the city center?' At that moment, I really felt like dying," Xiao Yu said.

Xiao Yu sent the phone back to Beijing again. He didn't know how reliable the next replacement phone would be, repeatedly asking the reporter over one night how to contact Xiaomi's senior management. He began calculating that in half a month, Xiaomi 3 had been in his hands for only four days, with normal usage less than one day — conversely, the two replacements had cost him twice the screen protector fees totaling 45 yuan, plus uncountable human and time costs.

Can replacement solve the problem? This was Xiao Yu's concern, as well as Xiao Chuan's worry. On October 28th, Xiao Chuan sent the phone back to the Beijing headquarters for replacement. On the morning of October 30th, Xiaomi's Beijing after-sales headquarters signed for it, entering the service phase at 3 PM, and by 5 PM, he received notification that "the replacement phone has been shipped." "However, the customer service provided no explanation or clarification regarding any issues with the phone."

On Xiaomi's official forum, a user named "Redmi Machine Owner" posted a complaint, stating that upon receiving the replacement "Xiaomi 3" on October 27th, tragically, the "screen wave pattern issue still exists." "When requesting a replacement, I clearly stated there was a water ripple issue on the screen. Did the customer service not test it at all?"

Pessimistic After-Sales Service

A question arises: for Xiaomi 3, which boasts various "fastest" titles, are the quality issues limited to just the first or second batch?

On October 29th, Xiaomi 3's third round of flash sales took place — surprisingly, compared to the previous one-minute sellout, this time the 100,000 Xiaomi 3 units sold out in 4 minutes and 39 seconds.

Based on user feedback, the quality of the third batch of Xiaomi 3s was also far from optimistic. On October 30th, a user named "Holy Already Fallen" posted on the Xiaomi 3 Tieba with pictures, stating that "after receiving the third batch of Xiaomi 3, the issues were too severe, only replacement can fix it": the screen drift was severe, tested with the built-in touch screen testing tool, instead of smooth curves, the finger traced scattered rays.

Several batches frequently experienced quality issues, what could be the reason? As of the publication of this article, Liu Fei, Xiaomi's PR director, did not answer the reporter's call, and Zhong Yufei, Xiaomi's operations manager, besides privately contacting the reporter to obtain the contact information of problematic phone users, did not respond to why so many Xiaomi 3 users experienced the same issues.

As of now, the official response these Xiaomi 3 users received was merely a copy-paste reply from the moderator "Mr. Jiawu" of Xiaomi's official forum under some problematic posts — the latest development version scheduled for release this Friday (November 1st) will focus on solving touchscreen, focus, and camera black screen issues. The poster can upgrade accordingly.

However, the above response failed to convince anyone. "Are camera and screen issues related to process problems resolved? Now I'm also worried about current sound issues, I fear this is a hardware issue, can replacement solve it?" a user named "BBchild Reply" asked on Xiaomi's official forum.

Other users were concerned that they couldn't return the phone after 15 days. "Customer service says it's a minority issue, always telling me to restore factory settings or update the system." Fu Jiuzhou and another user claimed that the 15-day return application deadline was approaching, but the customer service was uncertain if it was a hardware issue and refused the return.

Their questions similarly went unanswered by Xiaomi's official side. A Xiaomi 3 user complained in the rights protection group: "The phone fee spent calling customer service is almost 200 yuan." Another user also said: "I put the Xiaomi 3 in the drawer, unable to use it, just left it there."

"There are both software and hardware issues, I suspect it's the compatibility problem between Xiaomi TD version and Tegra4." "Flying Wing Z" told the reporter that his Xiaomi 3 stopped working after listening to headphones for ten minutes on the first day, installed iFly input method caused touch screen drift when typing, switching to the built-in input method slightly improved but issues still existed, on the fourth day, static noise appeared in the earpiece during a phone call, and the phone experienced serious heating and functional loss during use.

It's not just the newly launched Xiaomi 3 that has quality issues; reports on the quality and after-sales service of Xiaomi 1 and Xiaomi 2 have also surfaced occasionally. According to reports by the Beijing Morning Post, due to quality issues with Xiaomi 1 and refusal of returns, Mr. Lin had to replace his phone for the fourth time. Data from "China Quality Ten Thousand Mile Journey" also showed that Xiaomi phones ranked first in mobile phone product complaints — previously, Lei Jun stated that Xiaomi's accessories were all sourced from top international manufacturers, with a repair rate of around 1.5%.

According to the reporter's incomplete statistics, merely on Xiaomi's official forum, there were already over a thousand complaints regarding quality issues and after-sales services for various Xiaomi 1 and Xiaomi 2 products, involving frequent restarts, phone paint peeling, inability to turn on, headphone and earpiece malfunctions, charging failures, phone overheating, etc., amounting to over a dozen types of issues. What chilled the hearts of Mi Fans more was that most of these issues either received procedural "system update" replies from the official side or went completely unanswered.

Mi Fans Forced to Turn Against

"This is closely related to Xiaomi's unique marketing model," an insider in the mobile phone industry told the reporter. For electronic products, no manufacturer dares to claim their products are free of issues, especially during the initial launch of new products. Due to material and production quality rates, optimization and hardware issues are common occurrences.

Even leading companies in the industry like Apple and Samsung have issues with their flagship phones. For example, the iPhone 5 was reviewed for its "seven major problems," and the iPhone 4S faced a quality scandal due to lower-than-expected battery life. Samsung's S3 and Note 2 also responded after being reported by CCTV due to font library issues.

For Xiaomi, quality and user experience become even more important — it usually attracts users with highly tempting keywords like "fastest," "cost-performance ratio," and "good user experience." Recently, in an interview with the Wall Street Journal, Lei Jun said that "valuing customer experience and product quality has won the Chinese market."

Secondly, Xiaomi sells its products using the "hunger marketing" model, "which makes users develop extremely high expectations for Xiaomi products from the moment they start grabbing them," the mobile phone industry insider told the reporter. Meanwhile, Xiaomi is essentially an "internet company," without a single physical store to date, making it difficult for users to experience actual devices. Once the highly anticipated Xiaomi phone encounters numerous issues, combined with inadequate after-sales service, feelings of disappointment and anger are predictable.

This forces even the most loyal fans to possibly switch sides. "I just ask for a reasonable explanation from the official side! Don't break the hearts of Mi Fans! I've been looking forward to M3, from M1, to M2, and then to M3, buying them in the first three batches each time, even gifting friends, but this M3 is indeed very disappointing." On Xiaomi's official forum, "Mo Mo De Mo Mo" expressed dissatisfaction with the official procedural response in replying to Xiao Chuan's post.

For "Flying Wing Z," who introduced himself on Sina Weibo as an "old Mi Fan, MIUI expert group member," he returned and refunded his Xiaomi 3 on the sixth day of unbearable frustration, purchasing an iPhone 5S instead, "betraying" the once beloved Xiaomi.

He claimed to be a first-batch Xiaomi 1, second-round Xiaomi 2, and first-batch Xiaomi 3 user, "both Xiaomi 1 and Xiaomi 2 were repaired, and Xiaomi 3 was simply disappointing." He told the reporter. Judging from his Weibo, almost all posts were made using Xiaomi 2 and Xiaomi 3 phones.

Reporter Experience:

Xiaomi After-Sales Service Leaves Much to Be Desired

According to Xiaomi's official published materials, apart from the already established 429 authorized centers, Xiaomi plans to open 18 flagship stores providing after-sales services next year, and will also open its first physical store next year.

In Chongqing, Xiaomi Home is located on the 19th floor of Southern Skyline Building No. 3, Keyuan Road, Jiulongpo District. From the introduction, like other Xiaomi Homes, this service station offers new machine experiences, phone inspection services, self-collection of phones, repairs, and other functions, and prominently displays the notice "No Photography Allowed" upon entry.

During the reporter's first visit on the afternoon of October 29th, within a short span of ten minutes, more than ten users came to repair their phones due to various issues. The staff, who were operating without breaks, looked exhausted and were unwilling to provide detailed answers to user questions.

From the reporter's observation, except for one user updating the system, none could retrieve their phones on the same day. A user with a cracked Xiaomi 2 screen told the reporter that he didn't want to replace the screen but just wanted Xiaomi to help copy out his data, but after waiting for half an hour, no one attended to him — in fact, the reporter entered the service station at different times twice, spending quite a while without any staff coming forward to inquire or answer.

"Without any explanation, they directly said the screen needs to be replaced, costing 490 yuan. Isn't it within the warranty period?" Sitting in front of the Xiaomi TV experience table, a teenage girl named Lingling (a pseudonym), who had purchased the Xiaomi 2S less than three months ago, felt somewhat helpless and angry when talking to the reporter. Her 2S accidentally fell from a desk about dozens of centimeters high onto the ground, causing the screen to crack. She gestured toward the Xiaomi TV experience table and said: "It's not even as high as this table, and it broke upon falling, isn't it a quality issue?"

Lingling didn't know that accidental screen damage wasn't covered under Xiaomi's warranty policy, and 490 yuan was the nationwide uniform price for screen replacement at Xiaomi Homes. Compared to Samsung and iPhone screens, which often cost over a thousand yuan to replace, Xiaomi's 490-yuan screen replacement price was already quite affordable. However, Lingling didn't see it that way; she firmly believed she was being deceived. In the end, Lingling still had to replace the screen, carefully placing the repair order issued by Xiaomi into her handbag: "They didn't even mention the pickup time, just said they'd call me when it's fixed."

Tech Insight: Unsolvable Fatal Flaw of Tegra 4 Overheating

—Scholar Xue Yu, Editor-in-Chief of Hardware Evaluation Weekly

The currently available Xiaomi 3 is the TD version, adopting the Tegra4 platform. To my knowledge, there aren't many phones using this platform; aside from Xiaomi 3 Mobile Edition, there's only the ZTE U988s and the upcoming Coolpad Grand View 4.

From a platform perspective, the Tegra4, released in January this year, undoubtedly possesses powerful performance, but it also has a fatal flaw: overheating. Under high temperatures, the stability of the baseband decreases, leading to some compatibility issues. After all, the power consumption and heat generation of four Cortex-A15 cores with a main frequency of 1.8GHz cannot be perfectly resolved by 28nm manufacturing technology.

According to previous foreign media reports, due to high heat, high energy consumption, and compatibility issues, NVIDIA had to delay the release of Tegra4 by a quarter.

In May this year, NVIDIA launched the SHIELD Tegra 4 Android handheld game console, and SHIELD adopted active fan cooling, supplemented by a large heatsink. Of course, since SHIELD is mainly for gaming, once it runs, it is basically full load, so the heat generation must be very headache-inducing, using a fan is understandable. Relatively speaking, for smartphones, 3D heavy gaming sessions won't be too long, but overheating is still an issue.

Xiaomi's Tegra 4 comes with NVIDIA's own 4G baseband. The stability of the baseband decreases when the temperature rises — integrating the baseband into the chip isn't something easily achieved. Previously, NVIDIA used external third-party baseband chips, but recently acquired a company specializing in basebands and integrated it themselves. The 4G baseband included in Xiaomi's Tegra 4 is the result of this integration.

Of course, to be fair, Xiaomi 3 claims to be the first Tegra4 phone to hit the market, with no reference models, developing independently, encountering compatibility and quality issues is quite normal. This issue may be resolved in the Qualcomm Snapdragon 800 version of Xiaomi 3 for China Unicom.
