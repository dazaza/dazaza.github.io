Inner Mongolia's Wind Power Woes: Abandoning the God-Sent Breeze Every Night

by ditan360 on 2010-06-22 16:12:00

Exclusive to LowCarbon.com: In early 2010, heavy snowstorms hit northern China and temperatures plummeted. Due to an urgent shortage of coal for electricity generation, several provinces in North China implemented power rationing by cutting off the power supply.

Meanwhile, hundreds of kilometers away from Beijing in Inner Mongolia's major wind farms, wind turbines were being idled — a large number of wind turbines ceased operation, allowing the northern winds to howl past without harnessing their energy.

"At the worst point, over a hundred turbines were stopped, leaving only one or two running to ensure the wind farm’s own electricity needs," said Liang Zhenfei, Director of the Department of Safety Production at Huadian Huitengxile Wind Farm, in an interview with The First Financial Daily.

Electricity generated by the Huitengxile Wind Farm is uniformly transmitted to the Mengxi Power Grid and then sold through this grid.

"Due to the bottleneck in the transmission channels of the Mengxi Power Grid, during winter nights, the wind power output of the Mengxi Power Grid can only be maintained at 500,000 kilowatts, with up to 2.9 million kilowatts of wind power being abandoned," said Zhang Fusheng, General Manager of Inner Mongolia Electric Power (Group) Co., Ltd., expressing his helplessness.

This situation has yet to improve.

On June 10th, according to information from the "2010 National Electricity Summer Peak Meeting" held by the National Development and Reform Commission, this summer, the balance of electricity in North China, East China, and South China will be tight, with the possibility of power rationing during peak hours.

"If the fundamental problem of transmitting electricity cannot be resolved, the Inner Mongolia power grid will face widespread restrictions on wind power output regardless of the season," Zhang Fusheng said.

On one side, there is no available electricity; on the other side, there is a large amount of wasted electricity. Within a relatively small geographic area, there are conflicts and clashes between electricity supply and demand, old and new energy sources, as well as different systems that people do not wish to see.

The world is obsessed with chasing the wind, while Inner Mongolia struggles with abandoning it.

On the afternoon of June 3rd, on the boundless grasslands north of the Yin Mountains in Inner Mongolia, the grass had already turned green. Herds of cattle and sheep grazed on the hillsides and by the lakes.

By their sides and overhead were towering wind turbines, far outnumbering the livestock. The massive blades swept across the sky, making one feel dizzy.

This is Asia's largest wind power plant — the Huitengxile Wind Farm.

"Huitengxile" is Mongolian, meaning "cold mountain ridge." Due to its location on the Inner Mongolia Plateau and at a wind corridor, the wind resources here are extremely abundant.

According to calculations, the annual average wind speed at a height of 10 meters is 7.2 meters per second, and at 40 meters, it is 8.8 meters per second. The wind power density is 662 watts per square meter, with an annual average air density of 1.07 kilograms per cubic meter. The effective wind duration between 10 and 40 meters height ranges from 6,255 to 7,293 hours for wind speeds between 5 and 25 meters per second. These characteristics indicate strong stability, good continuity, and high-quality wind energy, making it an ideal place for constructing a wind farm.

In 1996, construction of the wind farm began here. According to national plans, a one-million-kilowatt-level wind power base would be built here. Currently, seven large state-owned enterprises, including Huadian, Datang, Huaneng, Beifang, Jingneng, and CGNPC, have set up operations here, with a total installed capacity exceeding one million kilowatts.

"If all were fully operational, each company could generate around three billion kilowatt-hours annually," said Li Zhi, director of the Huitengxile Wind Farm under Northern United Power Corporation.

Li Zhi has been working at this wind farm for 14 years, managing 132 wind turbines of 12 different models and two substations. During the interview, Li Zhi's phone kept ringing, and each time, he instructed his subordinates on what materials to use and which components to repair to save money.

"There's no way around it. Once power rationing occurs, the pure profit is cut," he said. Last year, due to power rationing, this wind farm lost nearly 4 million yuan in profits.

"Things were better a few years ago, with less noticeable power rationing," Li Zhi told the reporter. Before 2008, the Huitengxile Wind Farm had only five wind power companies, and their scales were not very large. However, in the previous two years, the scale of wind power expanded rapidly. Now, all seven companies are expanding their scale. "The speed of wind power expansion has far exceeded the speed of grid transformation," he said.

The problem has become more prominent each year. From October each year to April of the following year, for six months, Li Zhi's heart remains tense. He said, "What I fear most is receiving a text message from the power company dispatch room in the middle of the night. The text will tell me how much electricity I am allowed to transmit the next day. In recent years, such power rationing texts have increased significantly. At the most extreme times, you're not allowed to send any electricity at all."

The wind farm managed by Li Zhi differs from others. Among the 132 wind turbines, some have started to age and deteriorate due to longer usage times. "In winter, at minus 40 degrees Celsius, the losses from stopping the machines are very significant," Li Zhi informed the reporter. "If power rationing lasts for five hours, older machines might take seven or eight hours to restart and may not even start at all."

On the workbench in the wind farm dispatch room, the reporter saw a piece of paper clearly indicating that several numbered wind turbines must not be shut down. "The ones that must not be stopped are mostly the older ones," the dispatch personnel said.

Liang Zhenfei is also having a tough time. He told the reporter that headquarters sets annual power generation targets every year, specifying that the average utilization rate of wind turbines should not be lower than 97%.

This puts Liang Zhenfei in a difficult position, as after all, how much electricity is generated isn't his decision — the weather determines how much wind comes, and the power company decides how much electricity can be transmitted. All he can do is maintain the 120 "windmills" so they generate electricity when they should.

Recently, Qi Laisheng, General Manager of Guodian Longyuan Company's Inner Mongolia Branch, also expressed to the media that their wind farm on the Wulanchabu Grassland in Inner Mongolia was facing severe problems: "We have electricity we can't transmit, wind we can't harness. Since last winter, nighttime wind turbine shutdowns have been particularly serious, resulting in direct economic losses of 80 million yuan."

Data published by the Wind Energy Professional Committee of the Chinese Renewable Energy Society shows that Inner Mongolia's technically exploitable wind energy resource capacity exceeds 150 million kilowatts, accounting for over 50% of the nation's terrestrial wind energy reserves. By the end of 2009, the total connected wind power capacity in Inner Mongolia had surpassed 5 million kilowatts, accounting for about one-third of the country's total, ranking first nationwide both in wind energy resources and connected wind power capacity.

However, another embarrassing figure exists: due to bottlenecks in wind power grid connection and output, a large number of wind turbines are idled during periods of low electricity demand. Annually, wind power companies abandon approximately one-third of their potential electricity generation.

"The Three Gorges of Wind Power" Faces Development Crisis

According to the planning of the National Energy Administration, within the coming years, multiple one-million-kilowatt-level wind power bases and seven ten-million-kilowatt-level wind power bases will be constructed in provinces like Hebei, Inner Mongolia, Jilin, Gansu, Xinjiang, and Jiangsu's coastal areas, forming several "Three Gorges of Wind Power". By 2020, China's wind power construction scale will exceed 100 million kilowatts.

However, the predicament faced by wind power developers in Huitengxile evidently foretells that the rapidly developing wind power industry is encountering a severe development crisis.

Chen Sui, Chairman and General Manager of CGNPC Wind Power Company, frankly stated previously: "Sixty percent of our wind power investment is in Inner Mongolia, but Inner Mongolia has the most severe power rationing and the hardest time transmitting electricity. The 850,000-kilowatt installed capacity of our company yields no benefits whatsoever. We don’t know whether we should ‘hit the brakes’ or retreat!"

Surveys show that currently, over 60 enterprises are investing in and constructing wind power projects in Inner Mongolia, and each company is concerned about the constraints on wind power transmission.

"Worldwide, everyone is crazily chasing the wind, but Inner Mongolia is painfully abandoning it," said Su He, Director of the Inner Mongolia Autonomous Region State-Owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission, expressing deep anxiety.

Why can't Inner Mongolia export its excess electricity? During interviews with Inner Mongolia Electric Power Company, it was learned that the Mengxi Power Grid currently only has two 500 kV "grid-to-grid" eastward transmission channels to North China, with a transmission capacity of 4.3 million kilowatts. However, the planned transmission volume is only 3.9 million kilowatts at peak times and 2.5 million kilowatts at low-demand times, leading to restricted output of a large number of power plants, especially wind power.

Last year, after research, the National Energy Administration issued a notice titled "Notification from the National Energy Administration on Strengthening Joint Dispatching Between the North China Power Grid and the Mengxi Power Grid", requiring joint dispatching operations between the North China and Mengxi grids to maximize the benefits of wind power. The document required increasing the transmission capacity of the current Mengxi Power Grid to the North China Power Grid during low-demand times to ensure reaching 2.5 million kilowatts, aiming for 3 million kilowatts.

However, according to feedback from Inner Mongolia Electric Power Company, during the two months after the implementation of the new policy, the actual average transmission capacity only increased by about 300,000 kilowatts. During late-night hours, the Mengxi Power Grid's wind power output could only be maintained at 500,000 kilowatts, with up to 2.9 million kilowatts of wind power being abandoned.

Inner Mongolia Electric Power Company stated that despite its best efforts to accommodate large capacities of wind power, the problems cannot be solved solely by itself. Statistics show that by the end of 2009, the heat-supply units in Inner Mongolia's power grid reached 12 million kilowatts, with wind power capacity exceeding 4.3 million kilowatts. During late-night hours, the minimum load of the power grid (including eastward transmission) was approximately 12.3 million kilowatts (wind power installation capacity accounted for 33.7%). To meet heating demands, the load margin left for wind power was already minimal, and the power grid could not meet the requirements for full-scale wind power grid connection.

Currently, the installed wind power capacity accounts for 27% of the maximum power supply load of the power grid, far exceeding the tolerance level of Inner Mongolia's power grid (China Electric Power Research Institute argues that Inner Mongolia's power grid can tolerate 8% to 10% wind power).

Then, can Inner Mongolia invest more in power grid construction? The answer is no. There are two reasons:

First, there is no money. It is understood that Inner Mongolia Autonomous Region has planned multiple one-million-kilowatt-level wind power bases. The distance from wind power access to the power grid ranges from dozens of kilometers to over one or two hundred kilometers, with wind power access unit investment reaching 30 times that of thermal power access. The power grid's investment capability cannot meet the needs of wind power development, causing bottlenecks in wind power access to the grid.

Secondly, it is impossible to construct its own power grid for transmission. Neighboring provinces of Inner Mongolia are all within the jurisdiction of the State Grid Corporation. "You say, if I'm sleeping soundly with my wife, would you want someone else to stick their leg into our bed?" Zhang Fusheng said.

Can Inner Mongolia consume more of its excess electricity? It is also difficult. According to the reporter's understanding, Inner Mongolia's own electricity consumption is small. Recently, the state has successively introduced policies on environmental protection, energy conservation, and suppression of high-energy-consuming industries, halting high-energy-consuming electricity projects under construction or planned in Inner Mongolia. Existing electricity projects have largely been shut down due to insufficient electricity demand.

In 2008, the Inner Mongolia Autonomous Region attempted to boost electricity consumption by supporting a batch of comprehensive resource utilization projects, such as starting aluminum electrolysis and PVC production, but still faces environmental pressure.

An Zhongquan, President of Inner Mongolia Electric Power Research Institute, said that constrained by the power market space and insufficient external transmission channels, newly developed wind power projects in the Inner Mongolia power grid can only squeeze the market share of thermal power units within the limited local power market, causing the utilization hours of thermal power units to drop below 4,000 hours in 2009, with a large number of thermal power equipment idle, leading to difficulties in operating thermal power enterprises and resulting in losses.

Wind power grid connection difficulties are just temporary phenomena

Despite the sharp contradiction between thermal power and wind power in Inner Mongolia, when the reporter interviewed local government officials, power developers, and experts in Inner Mongolia, they did not attribute the blame to the rapid growth of wind power.

Su He believed, "It is inappropriate to evaluate or negate wind power development as 'disorderly.'"

He said that the National Energy Administration approved the planning of Inner Mongolia's wind power base, and now 66 wind farms have been built, with another 40 under preparation. Currently, all wind power projects that have been commissioned or are under construction in Inner Mongolia have gone through formal approval procedures, with most being projects tendered under national franchise rights. They are products of national wind power matching ratio policies, especially under the strong promotion of the Renewable Energy Law, fully complying with national industrial policies.

"If judged by the standards of China's commitment to international society in terms of energy conservation and emissions reduction, wind power development is not too fast, but rather should be faster!" Su He said that the current issue is merely that the construction speed of power transmission channels lags behind. The external transmission channel construction of the Mengxi Power Grid cannot be postponed year after year.

Zhang Fusheng also told the reporter that based on the current reality where the proportion of wind power accepted by the Inner Mongolia power grid exceeds 20%, if conservatively calculated at 15% of the highest national power supply load, by 2015, the national power grid could accept a wind power capacity of 150 million kilowatts (generating 368 billion kilowatt-hours annually, saving approximately 280 million tons of raw coal, equivalent to the transport capacity of 78,000 coal-specialized trains).

"As long as the problems of wind power aggregation and external transmission are solved, and wind power is absorbed nationwide, by the end of the 'Twelfth Five-Year Plan', Inner Mongolia's installed wind power capacity will reach 30 million kilowatts," Zhang Fusheng said.

Regarding the issue of expanding the external transmission capacity of Inner Mongolia's power, the State Grid Corporation seems less interested. Industry insiders analyzed that the reason lies in the fact that the Inner Mongolia power grid does not belong to the jurisdiction of the State Grid Corporation. There are reports that the State Grid Corporation has contacted Inner Mongolia Electric Power Company multiple times, hoping to incorporate the latter into its unified management. However, Inner Mongolia is unwilling to do so.

Su He indicated that the special regional system of Inner Mongolia's power grid is the strongest and most specific policy support from the Party and the state for the economic and social development of border minority autonomous regions. Since the power system reform in 2002, under the strong leadership of the regional Party committee and government, not only has Inner Mongolia's electric power industry achieved pure growth of 45 million kilowatts of power generation capacity and ranked first in the country for five consecutive years in west-to-east electricity transmission, but the region's GDP growth rate has also maintained the national "eight-time champion" status at the speed of "electricity".

Su He believed that Inner Mongolia's power grid has become a "trump card" for the region's socio-economic development, truly becoming the engine driving the region into the forefront of the country and an indispensable factor in the prosperity and stability of the northern border.

As for future development prospects, Su He is not as anxious as some leaders of resource-based provinces. "From a long-term development perspective, regardless of how coal, oil, and other resources may rise in price in the future, the 'northwest wind' bestowed by nature will never rise in price. Regardless of how energy may become scarce in the future, Inner Mongolia's wind energy resources will never be exhausted," he said.

Yang Xiaosheng, Chief Engineer of Longyuan Power Group and a wind power expert, told The First Financial Daily that difficulties in wind power grid connection might be a temporary phenomenon during the initial phase of wind power industry development. As grid construction accelerates and national new energy policies are gradually implemented, the difficulties encountered by wind power enterprises will gradually be resolved.

Since Inner Mongolia's wind energy utilization hours are high and the complete cost of wind power generation is below 0.46 yuan per kilowatt-hour (the online electricity price for southern thermal power exceeds 0.52 yuan per kilowatt-hour), plus carbon trading, wind power can receive compensation of about 0.1 yuan per kilowatt-hour, and wind power enterprises remain hopeful for the future.

However, the situation cannot wait any longer. Some wind power developers said that if a mechanism for absorbing wind power in the national electricity market is not established and the problem of wind power transmission channels in remote areas is not synchronized, it is akin to abandoning a newborn child.

The reporter learned from Inner Mongolia Electric Power Company that Inner Mongolia is trying to find ways to smooth out wind power transmission channels.

According to the ratio of wind power to thermal power of 1:2, allocate wind power to the transmission channel, forming a "wind-thermal bundled" transmission mode. Zhang Fusheng said that wind power must rely on the strong support of thermal power, achieving a high proportion of wind power integration under the deep peak regulation of thermal power, which can be described as "wind advances, thermal retreats; wind retreats, thermal advances; wind strong, thermal weak; wind weak, thermal strong; wind borrows thermal momentum, thermal assists wind power."

Inner Mongolia Electric Power Company stated that after implementing these measures, the wind power transmission capacity of the Inner Mongolia power grid could increase by 4.8 million kilowatts, transmitting about 13 billion kilowatt-hours of wind power annually. "If the eastward transmission to North China can maintain over 3.9 million kilowatts day and night, we can ensure no wind abandonment during the night," Zhang Fusheng said.

Zhang Fusheng, General Manager of Inner Mongolia Electric (Group) Co., Ltd.:

"Please don't look at Inner Mongolia with strange eyes!"

Zhang Fusheng, who has served as the general manager of Inner Mongolia Electric (Group) Co., Ltd. for just over a year, is very anxious. He wants to expand Inner Mongolia's wind power. After all, this is China's most famous wind power base known as the "Wind Power Three Gorges."

He wants to send pollution-free wind power outside, to cities like Beijing or even farther industrial bases, contributing clean energy to improving their environments, but he can't find more pathways. Every day, every year, a large amount of wind power is wasted locally.

"Could you help advocate for this?" Zhang Fusheng asked the reporters who came for interviews, slightly pleading. "Let Inner Mongolia have more wind power transmission channels." He said, "If there are channels, I can guarantee that next year, Inner Mongolia's wind power online electricity generation will surpass Spain, and in three years, it will reach the highest level of Denmark's wind power generation. But now, I can only talk empty words."

The First Financial Daily: What is the current level of Inner Mongolia's electric power construction in the country?

Zhang Fusheng: People who haven't been to Inner Mongolia all think it is backward and has a low level of economic development.
