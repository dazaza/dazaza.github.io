Samsung's $1.1 billion acquisition of a startup to take on Apple and Google

by anonymous on 2013-10-10 18:57:41

Potential Samsung Acquisition Targets

　　Samsung Electronics is accelerating its pace in searching for merger and acquisition targets, taking more measures to expand its presence in Silicon Valley. The aim is to overcome the company's main weakness: software.

　　According to internal Samsung documents obtained by foreign media, Samsung plans to acquire some software startups in Silicon Valley, especially companies in gaming, mobile search, social media, and map-related services. This document was compiled by Samsung's Media Solutions Center in February this year, outlining the company's ideas for expanding its presence in each field and listing some potential acquisition and investment targets.

　　The document shows that Samsung has evaluated several startups, such as Unity Technologies, a game platform developer in San Francisco, and Green Throttle Games, a game controller and software developer in Santa Clara, California. Additionally, Atari, a pioneer in the gaming industry, has also caught Samsung's attention. This company rejected multiple offers for its game portfolio earlier this year and is auctioning off some of its assets as part of its bankruptcy protection filing.

　　Software Weakness

　　Samsung has become the world's largest smartphone manufacturer, with its phones being launched quickly at relatively affordable prices, making them very attractive to users.

　　However, Samsung currently faces a situation where software-savvy companies like Apple, Google, and Microsoft (which acquired Nokia's phone business last month) are increasingly dominating the mobile device market.

　　In this environment, Samsung is forced to make a "last stand" and address its "software weakness." According to informed sources, Samsung bid for Waze, an Israeli mobile map service developer, earlier this year but eventually Google acquired the company for approximately $1.1 billion in July. A source revealed that before initiating acquisition negotiations, Samsung had contacted Waze about a large-scale investment and establishing a partnership.

　　According to the aforementioned internal documents, Samsung is also closely monitoring Glympse, a Seattle-based startup that develops software allowing users to share their location with friends. A source said that Samsung first made contact with Glympse in early 2012 and later increased the possibility of equity investment in the company, though negotiations are still ongoing. Last month, Glympse released an application for the Samsung Galaxy Gear smartwatch.

　　Own Software

　　Last year, Samsung's R&D expenditure reached $10.8 billion, with 67,000 employees dedicated to helping the company maintain its leading position in the global TV, semiconductor, and home appliance markets.

　　Although Samsung has taken many steps to develop its own smartphone software, it has not yet achieved success. In November 2009, Samsung launched Bada, an open-source mobile operating system, hoping this system would challenge Google's Android platform. However, due to an unfriendly user interface and poor synchronization with other devices, the system did not gain consumer favor.

　　Earlier this year, Samsung terminated the Bada project and began jointly developing Tizen, a new operating system with Intel. However, Samsung has not yet released a smartphone based on this operating system. If successful, this operating system could reduce Samsung's reliance on the Android platform. Currently, most Samsung products, including smartwatches, are based on the Android system.

　　Chinese Manufacturers

　　Moreover, if Samsung can achieve a breakthrough in its own smartphone software, it could enhance the company's position and enable it to better resist competition from Chinese hardware manufacturers like Lenovo Group, Huawei, and Xiaomi.

　　According to Samsung's previously announced preliminary financial report, in the three months ending September, the company's mobile business operating profit margin dropped from 19.8% in the previous quarter to 17.7%, mainly due to price pressure from competitors and increased advertising expenditures.

　　Expanding Presence in Silicon Valley

　　Samsung refused to comment on its acquisition plans but was open about its expansion plans in Silicon Valley.

　　In recent months, Samsung has begun construction of a large research facility near Apple's Silicon Valley offices and established a software startup accelerator covering Palo Alto, California, and the Chelsea community in Manhattan. This accelerator will make early-stage investments in startups, particularly developers of software for Samsung devices.

　　Samsung has allocated $1.1 billion for investments in early-stage startups and venture capital activities in the U.S. market.

　　David Eun, head of Samsung's Open Innovation Center responsible for operating the software startup accelerator, pointed out: "What is happening in Silicon Valley is exciting for Samsung."

　　Combination of Hardware and Software

　　Google acquired Motorola Mobility in 2011, and Microsoft purchased Nokia's phone business last month, meaning Samsung will face greater competition. Competitors like Apple possess competitive capabilities in both hardware and software. Unlike Apple, Google, and Microsoft, Samsung has no history of significant achievements in the software field.

　　For Samsung, developing its software business through acquisitions is not necessarily easier than organically building this business within the company. Although Samsung holds around $50 billion in cash, historically, the company has not been smooth in executing mergers and acquisitions.

　　Silicon Valley is cautious about potential acquisition transactions by Samsung, partly because of the poor performance of its acquisition of AST Research in the mid-1990s. AST Research was once the fifth-largest computer manufacturer globally, and Samsung acquired the company for $840 million, aiming to enter the U.S. personal computer market. However, the result was a major loss, forcing Samsung to abandon the venture. To date, this remains Samsung's largest overseas acquisition in its history.

　　According to media-obtained internal documents, in addition to the aforementioned companies, Samsung also considers Everything.me, a mobile search engine developer headquartered in Tel Aviv, Israel, as a potential acquisition target, and intends to acquire Rounds, another Israeli video chat application startup, which could help Samsung compete with services like Apple's Facetime and Google Hangouts.
