Must-read for Sanya Travel

by xiaoxiabuf on 2010-07-04 21:44:15

Sanya is a great tourist destination all year round. Personally, I think the best time to visit is in March, April, September, and October because these months are the low season for tourism. Flights are relatively cheaper during this period (except around National Day), and the weather is most comfortable. You don't have to worry about extreme heat or occasional cold snaps in winter. The months of May, June, July, and August have pleasant weather and are peak times for conferences and student tours in Hainan. From November to February, it's the high season for tourism in Hainan, which can be described as "comfortable, expensive, crowded."

The idea to write this article came from wanting to leave a memory of our first family trip after my daughter was born. It's also a reminder for a brother who plans to come to Sanya soon and a way to thank the many brothers and sisters on Ctrip who have shared their travel notes. My wife planned our itinerary based on several printed-out travel notes, and previous self-guided trips were also greatly helped by everyone's tips. So, I leave some information here for future travelers.

Travel essentially boils down to food, accommodation, transportation, and entertainment. Here, I won't write the itinerary chronologically. A detailed account may be precise but lacks interest.

Background: Four days and four nights, self-guided tour arranged by a travel agency, flying with China Southern Airlines, staying at the Kale Hotel. The three of us—my wife, child, and I—experienced the earliest nationwide wind chill of 2008.

Accommodation Section:

Sanya Kale, there are too many reviews online, so I won't go into detail. The price we got was 800 yuan on weekends and 700 yuan on weekdays, arranged by a small friend’s travel agency. What I like is its lobby, although due to the reasons of China Southern Airlines (there will be a story later), I arrived at 3 am (five hours late, and I was heartbroken over the 800 yuan room fee all the way). Entering the lobby, I saw large transparent windows on both sides and a cool breeze through the hall. Everything exuded relaxation and comfort, purely and clearly telling people, hey! Come here for a vacation.

The reception at the hotel front desk and the vast majority of service personnel encountered afterward were friendly and professional, providing service at a five-star level. The room was a luxury sea-view room. Beforehand, one must understand the difference between new luxury and regular luxury (different prices). As many netizens mentioned, frankly speaking, the room doesn't quite meet the five-star standard. I often travel on business, objectively speaking, the luxury room meets a four-star standard. Since I had read many reviews beforehand, I had psychological preparation and thus no disappointment. After all, Sanya Kale is located in a prime five-star location. Even if the indoor facilities don't reach the standard, the outdoor environment certainly does. When traveling, what matters most is a pleasant mood. Of course, if the hotel pays attention, even without renovation, there are areas for improvement, such as providing free ADSL internet access, but using ADSL requires power, and my laptop also needs an external power source, but only one outlet was left on the wall, so I had to ask the housekeeping department for an extension cord. Clearly, they had encountered this problem before, so they brought me a power strip without any explanation. The mini-fridge was empty. Could it be to make space for guests to store their own food?

Service was really good, and any reasonable request was quickly met by the housekeeping department. However, the morning call was missed once, delaying my planned morning run by the sea (I'm lazy and won't wake up unless called).

Kunming was most disappointing in terms of breakfast. It was a huge letdown. Limited varieties of main dishes, four or five types of fruits, and a crowd of people. Even after the weekend, there was no improvement. Upon inquiry, the only reason given was that it wasn't fruit season. Alas, this was the biggest regret of the trip. With the temperature drop and windy weather, I only went into the sea once, leaving me wanting more.

Dining Section:

The first evening's meal was a makeshift dinner on the plane (perhaps calling it a midnight snack would be more appropriate). The second day, we headed straight to the "Chunyuan" night market mentioned in legends. The entire night market consisted of dozens of vendors, arranged in parallel rows, each row having about ten vendors. I had seen a similar layout in Kaihua County in Zhejiang Province, but here, the night market didn’t prepare its own dishes; the vendors only cooked, and the ingredients (main ingredients) had to be purchased by oneself. Fortunately, the market was right behind the cluster of night markets. We chose the stall recommended online, numbered 3-4, where the vendor and his wife seemed a bit cramped. After asking about the rules and realizing we had to buy our own ingredients, we immediately invited the vendor's wife to help us shop (a tip from netizens). She almost didn't hesitate and led me to the vegetable stand. At first, I was startled—would I be taken advantage of? After all, she sees the vegetable sellers every day and deals with them regularly. Very quickly, I realized how petty I was being. My wife's choice of the stall was better. The vendor's wife directly took me to the crab stand, pointing out a green crab and a red crab, explaining that the red crab had more meat (I felt ashamed not recognizing the two types of crabs despite studying aquaculture). The crabs were sea crabs. After confirming I wanted the red crabs, she very seriously and meticulously picked them for me. I told her I wanted four (which turned out to be extravagant afterward), and she practically picked through thirty, constantly squeezing the crabs' legs to check if they were solid (soft legs mean recently molted, less meat). Meanwhile, the crab seller kept throwing crabs into our small basin, saying they were good, but the vendor's wife didn’t budge, squeezing the crabs and tossing them back into the big pot amidst the seller's muttering. Finally, we selected four fat crabs weighing one jin seven liang, costing forty yuan exactly. Then she helped me pick two live fish for ten yuan (again, I couldn't identify the species and forgot the local names), a pile of clams, radishes, tofu, and mustard greens (a type of leafy vegetable). Knowing it was excessive, we still bought some shrimp. What moved me was a small detail when buying the shrimp. I told the vendor's wife we were two adults and one child (my daughter was ten days shy of turning three), and we might not eat much, so we should buy fewer shrimp. She selected a few ounces, and when letting the shrimp seller weigh them, she suddenly noticed something and quickly grabbed the seller's hand, saying a bunch of things I didn't understand. But I clearly saw the shrimp seller holding a few extra shrimp ready to add to my scale pan. Having shopped before, I knew sellers often added a handful at the end, and I didn’t mind. But the vendor's wife firmly stopped the seller's action again amidst the muttering, completing the transaction. Afterward, I felt bad for initially doubting her integrity. I highly recommend her, hoping more netizens will support her business in the future. Good people will be rewarded.

After buying the ingredients, naturally, we were in a better mood. Back at our spot, my wife had already ordered the main dishes and drinks. Surprisingly, the stall didn’t have any drinks. All other foods (cold dishes, alcohol, drinks, pastries, main courses) were brought to your table by street vendors for you to choose from and pay on the spot. This continuous sales pitch went on throughout the meal. There were also singing girls circulating among the diners looking for song requests, making the entire night market filled with various sounds, from the sound of washing vegetables to the noisy singing. Such an environment was quite challenging, but experiencing it as part of the local dining culture could be worth a try. Just remember, one try is enough because apparently, none of the stalls in the night market group have proper chefs. All fresh ingredients are simply boiled in plain water, a method strongly recommended by the vendor.

Staying at Kale, we must mention the Cactus Hotel. Recommended online, we went with an open mind, pleasantly surprised by the results. Kale is over twenty kilometers from the city center, usually costing sixty yuan round trip (allegedly negotiable down to fifty). Being lazy, we didn’t want to travel back and forth. Therefore, the Cactus Hotel three kilometers away became the dining haven for the Yalong Bay hotel cluster. Let me advise you: don't attempt to walk to the Cactus Hotel from Kale—it would feel like a long march for someone on vacation. Also, don’t expect to catch a five-yuan taxi. Although on the first day, we did manage to flag down a driver willing to take us for five yuan (the third car, the first two drivers didn’t stop), the availability of cars in Yalong Bay is unpredictable—they may come quickly or take a while. On the return trip, we ended up paying ten yuan, as trying to save money isn’t why we’re on vacation, right? Why stress yourself out? My wife strictly followed the numerous price references and instructions from netizens, wasting a lot of time and almost causing an argument. If my loose spending habits influenced or raised the consumption levels of subsequent visitors, I apologize in advance. The Cactus Hotel’s prices are equivalent to those of a mid-level restaurant in Shanghai, but there are a few points worth affirming and recommending: one, the surrounding five-star hotels are extremely expensive and lack good taste (even the doormen at Kale admit the poor quality of their food); there are no other restaurants nearby of comparable quality. Two, the hotel menu offers choices from multiple cuisines, faithfully reflecting the original standards of each cuisine with excellent flavor. Three, the dining environment is top-notch, easily meeting four-star standards (no discount evaluation). The restaurant is by a lakeside, named “Panhubei” (lake side), correctly verified. Four, no tea fees or service charges (rare compared to the surroundings). Five, the waitresses are热情and considerate, very kind. Thus, I returned the next day for dinner.

Tourist Attractions Section:

I'll only mention one attraction, as it's the only one I visited (strictly speaking, Kale itself is also a great attraction). By the time I wrote this, it was already two o'clock in the morning, and sleepiness was creeping in. “Xiaoyu Hot Springs,” or locally known as “Tiandi Hot Springs,” located near Phoenix Airport, more than thirty kilometers from Kale. On the morning of the third day, the driver turned off the national highway onto a country dirt road and drove for quite a distance. Unable to resist, I started wondering internally, what kind of ghost place is this? Getting out of the car, we were informed it cost 148 yuan per person, not cheap, but since we were already at the mountain gate, how could we not enter? Buying tickets inside, we changed clothes (hehe, just swapped into swim trunks) and soaked! Soaked and soaked again. A few tips: regarding the herbal pools, don’t blindly believe the functions listed on the signboards. With constant flowing water, even if the packet of herbs is potent medicine, it has no effect. If you want to soak, choose the pool closest to the first outlet. The hot spring contains helium (should be a character with a 'qi' radical and 'dong' character, couldn't find it in full pinyin). No sulfur, so no unusual smell. Helium is an inert gas that volatilizes, so the concentration should be highest at the first outlet. Of course, different pools have different water temperatures, so you should find the suitable ones. I saw an elderly person soaking in a high-temperature pool for too long, nearly fainting. Second, you can enjoy massages or foot treatments. The techniques are average, but when they work on you, you lie on a large kang (extremely large), heated by warm spring water underneath, making lying down very comfortable and easy to drift off. Third, their biggest feature is the small fish pool. Once in the pool, fish gather quickly and nibble on your skin. Initially, the sensation feels like electric shocks, a mix of itching and pain, making it almost unbearable. After relaxing, you gradually appreciate the fun, even putting your lips underwater as the fish continuously kiss you, very amusing. It's rare for such small fish to survive in water temperatures of forty to fifty degrees Celsius. Allegedly, only Turkey has similar fish species globally, and nationwide, this is the only one of its kind. Again, identifying the fish species stumped me, a person who studied aquaculture (it should belong to the Cypriniformes order, but I dare not classify further). Photography is not allowed in the hot springs. If you want to record the joyous interaction between humans and fish, you can either let them assist (thirty yuan per photo) or sneak in a camera to take quick shots (you’ll be stopped). Another reminder: when going, be sure to arrange a pick-up time with the driver (usually pay to have the driver wait), otherwise, it's in a remote area. I paid thirty yuan to get in and eighty to get out. Other customers’ drivers pulled my job in between, but she had to rush back immediately, so a round trip of sixty to seventy kilometers, honestly speaking, eighty yuan wasn't too unreasonable, and the driver appeared quite honest.

Itinerary Section:

This section technically didn't need much elaboration, but there's a story to share if you'd like to skip it. On December 3rd, upon arriving at Hongqiao Airport to exchange tickets, we were informed of a flight delay. While sighing, a group of beauties walked by—a real group of beauties! They were participating in the Miss World competition in Sanya, scheduled for December 12th. After various twists and turns, with different versions of explanations, we waited at a nearby inn or hotel (some unlucky ones were sent to Zhongshan North Road to wait at a hotel). The reason was that flights to Sanya were delayed by three hours, and passengers protested because airlines compensate after four-hour delays. After much commotion, the flight was delayed by five hours. Later, I read on Ctrip that others successfully claimed a 100-yuan compensation. Correspondingly, the Shanghai flight was also delayed by five hours. I received a hundred-yuan compensation per person, but the whole process was chaotic. I couldn’t understand why flight delays, which are common for airlines, lacked a complete and mature response mechanism or why airline employees couldn’t execute a unified set of handling solutions. Different people gave different explanations. Sometimes, I wondered why many passengers incessantly argued with ground staff or airline employees, knowing well that the departure time was beyond their control, and whether compensation was due should be clear according to regulations. Yet, often, the benefits one should rightfully receive are only obtained through persistent arguments.

Finally, we boarded the plane. That group of beauties reappeared. Ha! It turned out we were on the same flight. Heaven arranged me to sit in the front row aisle, and as my colleague said, it was like "washing my eyes." Evaluation: all the beauties had good figures, but half of them had problematic skin. By Chinese standards, their faces might not qualify as universally stunning. But as representatives of various countries, gathering them together was truly a feast for the eyes! They stayed at the Hilton west of Kale, and those planning a trip soon could still catch them. Either look at the photos on Hilton’s walls, buy tickets for the competition, or watch it on TV.

Conclusion:

Without a conclusion, it wouldn't be a proper article. So, here's the closing. Actually, visiting Sanya in summer is also nice, with plenty of fruits and not too hot weather. It's better for family trips. Anyway, my plan is to show respect for my elders—this time it was my wife, and next time it will be my mother-in-law. I just hope that by then, she won't keep nagging me about saving money.
