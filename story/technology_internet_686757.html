How to build good interpersonal relationships with strangers

by qingian007 on 2012-03-02 11:03:54

Expanding one's interpersonal relationships inevitably involves interacting with strangers. Even those you know best were once strangers upon your first meeting. Unfortunately, many people feel awkward when speaking to strangers in social situations. At the same time, people may wonder: why doesn't it feel as difficult to talk to old friends? - The answer is simple: because you are very familiar with them. As people come to understand each other better, their interactions naturally become more harmonious. With strangers, however, you know nothing about them, and especially in a group of strangers, some may even feel discomfort or fear. If you want to turn a stranger into an old friend, you must first cultivate a desire to establish connections with others, express this through eye contact, and act accordingly.

The reason seems simple, but truly putting it into practice is not easy. As a lyric says, the distance between people can be as vast as that between stars. In fact, this refers to psychological distance. Scientific theories and experiments have shown that although the world is large, if we consider each person's social network, the distance between people is actually quite small.

In the study of interpersonal relationships, the famous social psychologist Stanley Milgram proposed the [Six Degrees of Separation] theory, which states that only six steps are needed for any two people to be somehow connected, regardless of how unrelated they may seem. The [Six Degrees of Separation] theory can be illustrated by an example: A and B attended a ballroom dance together, B is C's roommate, and from there, C knows D who rides bicycles, etc., until eventually reaching another person. In other words, through personal networks, any two strangers are at most separated by six intermediaries. By connecting through six people, this is essentially the substance of the [Six Degrees of Separation] theory.

Later, researchers at Microsoft experimentally confirmed the feasibility of this theory. They selected data over a month, recording monthly text messages sent through the Microsoft network, analyzing over 3 million addresses, and ultimately found that on average, 78% of users could establish contact with a stranger by sending just 6.6 SMS messages, or through 6.6 steps. Now, the [Six Degrees of Separation] theory has been widely applied in the practices of many people making new friends, expanding their contacts, and elevating their communication skills. According to this theory and experimental results, everyone can potentially connect with any stranger, including celebrities like Madonna or the Queen of England, creating a sense of familiarity.

It can be seen that on this planet, the spatial distance in interpersonal interactions isn't great; the greatest distance is the psychological one. So, how can we reduce this psychological distance and get to know a stranger?

First, take the initiative to introduce yourself. Before engaging in deeper conversation with a stranger, you might start with a self-introduction to give the other person some context. Then, you can ask them some questions, though not about personal secrets. After they respond, you should discuss relevant topics to achieve the purpose of communication. When conversing with a stranger, be cautious and attentive to their speech. Since your knowledge of them is limited, pay attention to any clues, their tone of voice, eyes, and responses to questions, so you can determine the next step and whether to deepen the interaction.

1. Ask for their name and remember it. Upon meeting someone for the first time, when they introduce themselves by saying "What's your name?", you should remember their name immediately. Once given, call them by their name during subsequent conversations. This shortens psychological distance and yields unexpected results.

2. Do not deny the other person's actions during the first meeting. Building good interpersonal relationships also means knowing that in such situations, calmly listening to their opinions without judgment helps avoid resentment. Additionally, when meeting for the first time, some may worry about being slightly criticized or having their opinions denied. Therefore, avoiding negating the other person’s behavior at the first meeting fosters closer relations.

3. Guide the other person to talk about things they are proud of. Everyone loves talking about what they're most proud of. If no one asks, it might indicate disinterest. Thus, posing some questions in this area will make them secretly pleased, opening up freely and fostering a harmonious relationship naturally.

4. Be a loyal listener, laughing and supporting the other person. Timely expressing your emotions can help the other person let go of strangeness and tension, discovering their strengths. Laughter plays a particularly important role—laughing even when their jokes aren’t that funny can yield surprising effects. This is because laughter between two people creates an atmosphere akin to that of close friends.

5. Seek the other person's opinion. Regardless of what you do, seeking mutual respect before acting expresses respect for the other person. In initial meetings, giving the other person the right to choose shows respect. Remember, everyone hopes to be respected by others without feeling rushed or impatient. Within a relatively short time, these skills can turn a stranger into a familiar person, expanding your network significantly beyond just one node in your interpersonal web.
