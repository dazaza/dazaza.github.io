The Tragic Legends about Guyu

by weredd123dd on 2011-04-14 15:12:44

It is said that during the reign of Emperor Gaozong of the Tang Dynasty, the Yellow River burst its banks and flooded the city of Caozhou. The floodwaters surged across thousands of acres of good farmland, houses collapsed, and countless soldiers and civilians drowned. There was a young man with excellent swimming skills named Guyu (which also means "Grain Rain"), who pushed a large wooden ventilation box through the water to carry his aged mother to the safety of the city wall. He then swam back and forth, rescuing over a dozen of his fellow villagers from the floodwaters. By this time, Guyu was utterly exhausted, as he had not eaten all day. His stomach growled loudly as he squatted on the city wall, staring blankly at the churning floodwaters.

Suddenly, he saw a peony plant bobbing up and down in the water. Its crimson flowers resembled a young woman's face, and its green leaves fluttered on the water’s surface as if waving for help. Without hesitation, Guyu stood up, forgetting his hunger and fatigue. He quickly shed his clothes and threw them to his mother, then with a splash, dived into the water and began swimming toward the peony. The current was swift and the waves were high. After great effort, Guyu finally approached the peony, just as he was about to grab its leaves, a sudden wave swept it away more than ten feet. Guyu did not give up. The peony would submerge underwater and then float back up again. Guyu swam tirelessly for two hours in the muddy water. Overcome by exhaustion, he felt dizzy and disoriented. His legs felt as if they were weighed down by heavy stones, and his arms could barely move. His mother, standing on the city wall, was worried sick for her son. The villagers shouted for Guyu to return, but he paid no attention, desperately pursuing the peony amidst the waves.

After another hour, the peony became entangled in the branches of a tree that had been toppled by the floodwaters. Only then was Guyu able to catch up to it. With both hands, he carefully lifted the peony, tears of joy streaming down his face. Exhausted, Guyu struggled to swim back to the city wall. Hardly able to stand, he collapsed onto the city wall, pale-faced and panting heavily. He handed the peony to an old man standing nearby. This old man, named Zhao Laoda, was a skilled flower grower. Trembling with emotion, Zhao Laoda accepted the peony and said: “Child, rest assured! Once the floodwaters recede, I will plant this peony in my Hundred Flowers Garden, and I will take good care of it!” Hearing this, Guyu smiled sweetly despite his weariness.

Two years passed in the blink of an eye. That spring, Guyu's mother fell seriously ill, bedridden and unable to open her eyes. She was emaciated, her skin clinging to her bones, and she lacked the strength even to speak. Guyu was beside himself with worry. He sought medical help everywhere, selling everything in the house to buy medicine, yet his mother's condition did not improve. One day, after borrowing two taels of silver from Zhao Laoda to buy more medicine for his mother, a beautiful young woman suddenly entered their humble hut. She was as beautiful as a celestial maiden in a painting, dressed in red robes, with rosy cheeks and lips. A scarlet flower adorned her hair, and her large eyes fixed intently on Guyu. Smiling gently, she asked, "Are you Guyu?"

Guyu nodded hastily. The girl introduced herself as Danfeng, saying she came from the eastern village where her family had practiced medicine for generations, treating the common people. Hearing of the old lady's illness, she had come to deliver some medicine. As she spoke, she unfolded a red handkerchief and placed a bundle of herbs on the table, filling the room with a fresh fragrance. Not knowing what to do, Guyu hurriedly pulled out the silver coins to offer her payment. Danfeng declined, saying, "We do not charge the poor." She then walked to the bedside. Guyu's mother, hearing the voice, forced her eyes open and looked gratefully at Danfeng, gesturing for her to sit down. Leaning close, Danfeng said sweetly, "Grandma, I'll go and boil the medicine for you. Once you drink this, your condition will surely improve!"

Without delay, Guyu fetched water and gathered firewood while Danfeng busied herself with lighting the fire and boiling the medicine. In no time, the decoction was ready, and Danfeng helped the old lady drink it. Miraculously, after consuming the medicine, the old lady immediately felt invigorated, her body feeling lighter, her illness nearly gone. She wanted to get up and walk around. Watching Danfeng, Guyu felt overwhelming gratitude but couldn't utter a single word. Danfeng turned to Guyu with a smile, saying, "I'll come again tomorrow," and floated away like a flame.

For three consecutive days, Danfeng came to deliver medicine to the old lady. Gradually, Guyu grew less awkward around her. They talked and laughed together, becoming as close as siblings. Soon, Guyu's mother recovered better than before, her wrinkles fading, her white hair turning black, and she felt full of energy. Seeing how close Guyu and Danfeng had become, the old lady was overjoyed. She thought to herself, "If only such a daughter-in-law could be mine!" Yet, fearing Danfeng might not want to marry into their impoverished home, she decided to privately inquire about Danfeng’s feelings.

However, since the old lady had fully recovered, Danfeng never returned. Day after day, the old lady waited anxiously, and Guyu longed for her return. Every morning before dawn, the two would look eastward toward the gate. Confused and lost, Guyu squatted among the flowers, watching a red peony sway in the wind. Suddenly, he wondered, "Could Danfeng be the Peony Fairy?" Under the moonlight, the peony's bowl-sized flower seemed to grin mischievously at him. Deeply moved, Guyu bowed deeply and prayed, "Thanks to Miss Danfeng's miraculous cure, my mother has recovered. The old lady often misses you. Please, Peony Fairy, appear and return home with me to visit!"

After finishing his plea, Guyu glanced furtively at the peony and noticed a piece of red paper fall to the ground. He picked it up and read two lines written on it:

"Wait until next year's April 8th,

When I shall arrive at the Gu family to settle."

Thus, the legend of the Peony Fairy continued...
