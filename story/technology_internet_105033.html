The Revolution from B2C to B4C

by langmzz on 2008-11-20 20:16:37

There is no doubt that e-commerce has become an indispensable part of people's lives. From small items like cosmetics and accessories to large ones like tools and building materials, a simple click of the mouse can have goods delivered right to your doorstep. This not only makes life and work more convenient but also leads the continuous expansion of trade.

E-commerce models continue to improve service platforms from C2C, B2B, and B2C. However, everything is constantly moving forward, including marketing models, service models, as well as people's consumption concepts and requirements.

When it comes to the B2C model, many people first think of Amazon overseas and Joyo and Dangdang domestically. However, whether it’s Amazon abroad or Joyo and Dangdang in China, they sometimes face headaches due to large cost expenditures. For example, inventory cost issues weaken the profitability of B2C websites; almost all B2C sites are trying to control inventory costs. Furthermore, the profit model of earning from the price difference between buying and selling also determines that B2C websites cannot give users unbiased shopping advice but will instead try to recommend products that are stockpiled in warehouses. This undoubtedly affects user experience and trust in the website. Additionally, although B2C is becoming increasingly specialized, from large supermarket-style B2Cs with a wide variety of products to those now focused on specific fields, competition has not weakened due to market segmentation. A large number of B2C websites offer homogenized goods, engaging in price wars at any cost, often neglecting service.

Therefore, as the problems of inventory, logistics, and other costs gradually decrease with the development of IT technology and management levels, competition also shifts from price competition of goods to competition of service quality. The service of B2C websites is part of the customer experience and also a part of the "post-sales" phase in the broader concept of online marketing.

In response to this, a new concept of "B4C (Business-for-Consumer)" has emerged. B4C is an elevation of the B2C model, advocating for the enhancement of online services and representing a new trend in direct sales through e-commerce.

Firstly, B4C is service-oriented, aiming to meet customer needs and improve customer satisfaction as its business philosophy. Service is the primary factor considered in online consumption. This service includes product use, product purchase, online payment, technical support, after-sales service, etc. In the functional construction of the B4C network platform, this point is exactly reflected. Regardless of which module, the process optimization design is carried out from the perspective of convenience and quick service.

Moreover, B4C advocates thinking one step ahead for customers and bringing them surprises, just as IKEA's advertisement says: "I'm not selling lamps, I'm selling ambiance." Similarly, B4C tells consumers that what we sell is not goods, but services. Service creates value, and service brings repeat customers.

Not long ago, E Road Feng created a supermarket-type marketing service model for Banglibang (www.banglibang.com), taking into account all the existing shortcomings of B2C operations and upgrading to the B4C model.

As some B2C service providers' operating costs increase and consumer service demand quality rises, the B4C operation model will inevitably become the future path for major online operators.
