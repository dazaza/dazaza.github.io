Fancy: The First Company to Make Money on Google Glass

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 19:26:31

Google Glass has drawn attention to wearable devices, but whether it can be widely adopted by the general public remains unknown. On one hand, its price is high while its functions are limited; on the other hand, people are not yet ready to embrace this form of computing. Therefore, although Google plans to target the general public, in the short term it can only attract specific industry professionals or a small number of tech enthusiasts.

In this situation, developers naturally would not invest too much effort into Google Glass, because its user base is small, making it difficult to turn a profit. However, even in niche markets and avant-garde fields, there are pioneers. The New York Times and Twitter have both tried out Glass apps, and Fancy, a startup based in New York, has even made money from it.

Fancy is a social shopping website where people exchange pictures of items they like, including luxury goods. Google Glass's convenient photo-taking and sharing capabilities align well with Fancy's needs. When users see an item they like, they can take a picture using the Glass app, then Fancy will match similar or complementary products, allowing for one-click purchasing. For avid shoppers, this is incredibly convenient.

Due to the uniqueness of Google Glass, Google does not allow developers to advertise or charge for apps. However, the terms of the Glass API do not mention in-app payments. Is this an oversight by Google or a deliberate omission? Regardless, people buy various items through Fancy's Glass app. Fancy became the first company to make a profit on Google Glass.

"The spontaneous act of discovering and buying items based on what you see is very appealing," Joe Einhorn, founder and CEO of Fancy, told Verge, "We see different types of people using the app, including interior designers who want to decorate spaces, and fashion stylists looking for accessories to match their outfits."

Google Glass is expensive, but Fancy has strong ties to luxury goods, and many of its users are wealthy. Francois Henri-Pinault, chairman of the large luxury goods company PPR, is an investor in and board member of Fancy. PPR includes brands such as Gucci, Bottega Veneta, Yves Saint Laurent, and Balenciaga. The connection to fashion may be the best way to get people to accept Google Glass, a thought that Google executives have expressed before.

Fancy's approach helps improve people's impressions of Google Glass. The Glass team expressed great happiness about this. "When we think about new cool Glassware, one idea that often comes to mind is 'the world is its experience,'" Timothy Jordan of the Glass team said, "That is, developers for Glass should not create experiences that compete with the real world." Instead, developers should help users discover the value of the world around them. This aligns with Fancy's philosophy. Of course, for Fancy, this value inevitably ties to money. "The world is its shopping mall" might better describe Fancy's app.
