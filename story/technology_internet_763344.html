Wired: How Facebook Can Avoid a Slow and Painful Death

by anonymous on 2013-07-30 11:50:52

Beijing, July 30 - According to an article published on the website of Wired magazine, for Facebook, the social networking giant, achieving growth in order to build a large audience is essential, but a network that gathers too many users can also bring trouble. The article points out that only mobile can save Facebook and even the Internet from the so-called "breakpoint," thus sparing Facebook from a slow and painful decline towards "death."

Here is the full text of the article:

Studying biological systems is likely the best way to help us understand various complex networks created by humans. All successful networks experience a "breakpoint" where their growth rate slows down. After passing this "breakpoint," some networks fail and decline, while many others achieve remarkable success. For example, the human brain contracts after going through a period of overgrowth; during this process, we acquire intelligence. This is because our brains accumulate too many neurons and neural connections during childhood, which makes us intelligent as adults. Without this process, we might never have acquired intelligence.

The Internet is the largest technological revolution conducted by humans in the 20th century and will likely be the driving force behind innovation for the next few hundred years. However, today, the situation facing the Internet is that a "breakpoint" is approaching; at the same time, many technologies and companies dependent on the Internet are also facing an increasingly imminent "breakpoint." Especially, we have witnessed the incredible growth of many social networks; initially Classmates.com and Friendster, then MySpace and Facebook.

For these social networks, each one seemed like it would grow endlessly during its respective prosperous period. Whether it was Classmates.com and Friendster or MySpace and Facebook, all these companies experienced explosive growth after reaching their "breakpoints."

Like all software, social networks' ability to attract users and keep them satisfied is limited; whether a social network can survive depends on how useful it is. For social networks, achieving growth to build a large audience is essential, and all the aforementioned social network companies have successfully done this. However, during this growth phase, most social network companies have failed.

However, a network that gathers too many users can also bring problems. Compared to the number of relationships the human brain can handle, Facebook's "Dunbar Number" (the theoretical upper limit of stable social relationships an individual can maintain) has already far exceeded the former. On Facebook, the average user has 262 "friends"; a reasonable guess is that a large part of these "friends" are not of high quality. What kind of results does this bring? Perhaps you receive such notifications: April Ridmoore downloaded the song "Call Me Maybe" on the streaming music service Spotify — wait, who is Ridmoore? You may not remember her; she's the girl who went out with your younger brother when he was still in high school, and she mocked you last year.

In a certain sense, Facebook is already trying to limit the development speed of its own network, preventing it from growing without restraint. The company has introduced functions that allow users to easily disable notification services and has made significant progress in allowing users to reduce notifications as much as possible. Moreover, usually, the users you "randomly" encounter on Facebook are people you know, once knew, or indirectly know, which is very different from MySpace, whose users often received dozens of friend requests from completely strangers every week.

Basically speaking, Facebook deserves the title of "King of Social Networks." It is more intelligent and successful than any previous social network. As Facebook itself has pointed out: "Facebook consists of many networks, each based on workplaces, regions, high schools, or universities."

Each of these social networks has very tight connections within its own network because each network has many users who are strongly connected to each other; however, from the perspective of cross-networks, user relationships are much thinner — just like how the neural connections in the brain mainly connect neurons within subnetworks.

For Facebook users, just a small number of actively initiated, low-value notifications can reduce the practicality of this social network. In such a case, it makes sense that users would clean up their friend lists, unsubscribe from updates, and block all notifications. However, almost no users do this; instead, they reduce usage and delete accounts, looking for the next new network to settle in, one that needs to have a cleaner, less distracting social structure; meanwhile, like all previous social networks, Facebook's network will slowly fade away due to reaching the "breakpoint."

How mobile can save Facebook and even the Internet from the "breakpoint"

Mobile versions of social networks are often simpler, streamlined in various aspects compared to the web version, much like an app. Clearly, mobile sites must be smaller to adapt to the physically smaller size of phones. But this is just one aspect; another reason mobile sites are smaller is that reducing what is offered can increase value and usability. The network itself has reached its "breakpoint," too many good things have put it under excessive pressure.

More novel and flexible mobile networks are emerging, much like how search engines stood out in the early stages of the World Wide Web. Still using the human brain as an example, when the brain reaches its "breakpoint," it filters external information through "cell suicide;" during this process, neurons "sacrifice" themselves for the benefit of the entire network. At the same time, the brain clears unnecessary connections while deepening the most important ones. Eventually, this paradoxical contraction of the brain makes us smarter.

So, how can Facebook become smarter? Like the human brain, Facebook can clear unnecessary relationships while strengthening important ones. Interestingly, this "return to basics" approach has been accidentally tried and achieved great success. The app version of Facebook is a simplified version of its website, where Facebook discards many things, leaving only the most basic content: posting on the "wall," reading news feeds, sending and receiving messages, etc. In other words, for Facebook users, communicating with others is the most important thing, everything else can be set aside.

Of course, reducing distractions was not Facebook's original intention; this change originated from Facebook having to do so because mobile devices are too small to accommodate too much information. However, the result of Facebook doing this brought about remarkable success: in 2012, users spent an average of seven hours per month on the mobile version of Facebook, while spending only six hours on the desktop version. According to data released by market research company Nielsen, Facebook's personal computer independent user numbers decreased for the first time in 2012.

The web version of Facebook has passed its "breakpoint" and is now in decline; on mobile devices, Facebook continues to show healthy and record-breaking growth. Specifically, a large part of the growth in mobile business is because users prefer to use social network services through their always-available smartphones rather than through computers. Of course, the popularity of mobile devices is also one of the factors contributing to this situation.

However, the role of a simpler user interface in this trend should not be overlooked; it is a very important element in the major measures Facebook has taken to transition to the mobile market. In fact, the web versions of other social networks—Twitter, image-sharing social platform Pinterest, review site Yelp, professional social network LinkedIn, and even Facebook-owned photo-sharing site Instagram—have yet to show signs of contraction.

One thing we can realize from this is that when a network is at its "breakpoint," reducing unnecessary elements is equivalent to increasing usage. What we need to be mindful of is not how to avoid the "breakpoint," but how to avoid excessive expansion after the "breakpoint." Growth is not necessarily a bad thing, unless the company sees nothing beyond growth.
