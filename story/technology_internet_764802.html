Google's "Ten Golden Rules" Behind Its Success

by anonymous on 2013-08-06 18:48:11

Among the market competitors symbolizing the innovation movement, we first think of companies like Google. Compared to the internet bubble companies popular in the 1990s, Google's biggest difference lies in its organized innovation, which can be observed from the continuous emergence of various services.

Google's former CEO, Eric Schmidt, recounted his awe-inspiring experience when he first encountered Google: "When I first came to Google, I saw that the founders Sergey and Larry highly valued direct dialogue with artists, a behavior that naturally brought forth important innovations. They have built a management style and culture within Google that promotes the growth of artistic exploration and innovation. My job is to do my best to maintain this atmosphere."

Google thus vividly demonstrates to people the arrival of a new era.

In fact, the company's unique innovation management method has long been famous worldwide. In the company, researchers from various fields and engineers familiar with frontline work and users propose ideas endlessly. The company encourages them to choose to participate in multiple projects among the hundreds being carried out simultaneously and allows them to allocate 20% of their working time to freely choose projects in areas of personal interest beyond their own responsibilities.

In this innovation-encouraging environment, almost all ideas and concepts are realized as specific new software or services at an accelerated pace within six weeks, i.e., they are evaluated in the form of "internal company applications." If they receive positive reviews within the company, the next step is to let users evaluate them. As the company puts it, after evaluation by "0.1% of passionate users," the fate of the project is decided within a few weeks.

Moreover, to drive the operation of this innovation cycle, Google introduced the following "Ten Golden Rules":

1. The committee strictly recruits.

2. Fulfill all employee needs.

3. Bring employees closer together.

4. Simplify and coordinate collaboration.

5. Practice what you preach; use your own products.

6. Encourage innovation.

7. Unify opinions as much as possible.

8. Don't be evil.

9. Data drives decisions.

10. Communicate effectively.

Google's innovation management method is about creating a space (environment) for nurturing and evaluating intellectual capital (knowledge property) and elevating this practice to an extraordinary level. Although opinions on their approach vary, their methods reflect the essence of innovation management. In today's knowledge society, relying solely on the digital divide cannot create a value gap. Not only does information technology alone fail to generate value, but also, in today's world, deriving trends through analyzing past cases and information is becoming increasingly meaningless. In other words, in the current era, only companies that possess knowledge-created differences—i.e., those with superior technology and skills to adapt to environmental changes and the ability to produce and provide knowledge while understanding the market and realizing customer value—can survive and develop.

Meanwhile, the efficiency of R&D investment needs to be continuously improved. People have recognized the need to break through existing technological limitations and step out of old-style innovation cycles. Against this backdrop, challenging "innovation" has become an increasingly critical issue.

Another point worth noting is that companies practicing innovation management often offer employees a very comfortable working environment. Many Japanese companies' excessive pursuit of high efficiency has led to severe mental illnesses among employees. In stark contrast, Google ranked first in Fortune magazine's 2007 selection of the "100 Best Companies To Work For." The primary reason for this ranking was not salary but because "work is fun."

Since the summer of 2006, Google has established a new position within the company called "Chief Culture Officer" (CCO). Naturally, the idea originated from Larry Page and Sergey Brin. The first-generation CCO was Stacy Sullivan, Google's cultural standard-bearer, who discussed the company's "happiness survey" in a certain interview, which once became a hot topic of discussion:

"Over the past few years, as part of our annual global corporate survey, we have organized 'happiness surveys.' A few years ago, Larry and Sergey proposed to investigate employee happiness and find out what factors keep employees at Google. Our intention was to investigate how committed employees are to the company, the factors influencing their levels of commitment, and the differences between employees and their immediate supervisors. The results mainly focused on career development and expansion. That is to say, compared to granting stock options or increasing annual salaries, expanding their career plans has become the focus."

The keywords appearing in their conversations—artistic sensibility, exploratory spirit, honesty, a sense of mission for working for the entire world, and the pursuit of happiness—all tell us what kind of management approach an organization should have in the innovation movement.

In the fall of 2005, at the Web 2.0 Summit in San Francisco, Sergey unexpectedly appeared at the venue and clearly stated at the beginning of the interview that "the biggest factor in Google's success over the past seven years has been 'luck,'" shocking the entire venue.

Just by reviewing the journey Google has taken since its founding, one can understand that this opening statement was neither modesty nor self-deprecation but rather an honest and straightforward revelation.

"When we were still graduate students, we did whatever we thought of, and later we realized that we had created something useful and hoped to make a more significant impact. We discussed open-source code and collaborating with universities, but both faced significant difficulties. We had to pay for computer usage fees, and we were very short of money."

As he said, they did not establish lofty ideals based on foreseeing future trends when they founded Google. On the contrary, it was precisely their pure curiosity as inexperienced young people that led them to take the first step towards ideas dismissed by seasoned and astute computer scientists. Afterwards, they simply went all-in to solve the new problems that arose every day. Additionally, Sergey mentioned "luck," which can largely be seen as the result of excellent engineers competing with each other gradually losing interest, curiosity, excitement, and morale, eventually driving themselves out of the innovation movement. Therefore, this spiritual quality is more important than anything else.
