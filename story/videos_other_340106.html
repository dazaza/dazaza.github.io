iPhone: Succeed with WiFi, fail with WiFi?

by shoujitpd on 2010-04-05 00:49:39

[IT168 Review] Ever since I got my hands on the iPhone two months ago, I have fallen madly in love with this phone. Since I often go out for interviews, I can use it to send and receive emails or prepare interview outlines while on the bus. Sometimes when I get lost, the built-in map navigation is very convenient. The recording quality of the iPhone is also exceptionally good; my digital voice recorder has been sitting untouched in my bag for months. It's also very convenient to take pictures and send them back to the editor.

However, it's rumored that China Unicom's version of the iPhone hasn't been selling well. Is there any reason for that? Some say it's because of the high price, starting at 6000 yuan, which domestic consumers find hard to accept. Last week, there were media reports that China Unicom was piloting installment payments and "zero down payment" methods in several major cities to lower the threshold for purchasing an iPhone. This can confirm that point. But no matter what sales strategy China Unicom adopts, to say that Chinese consumers can't afford the iPhone seems to underestimate our purchasing power. Haven't we seen Nokia's top-tier brand Vertu phones, where each model costs tens of thousands of yuan, sell like crazy in China?

Another reason given is the lack of Wi-Fi. Because the iPhone was originally designed for the internet, let alone features like the built-in Google Maps and App Store, even its SMS function resembles network instant messaging. Nowadays, Wi-Fi access points are everywhere in Shenzhen, with Yonghe King and McDonald's offering free Wi-Fi 24/7. Without Wi-Fi, the official version of the iPhone is destined to miss out on most popular online applications, naturally reducing its appeal. Although gray-market iPhones are only slightly cheaper than the official versions, they come with Wi-Fi, making them consistently in high demand in Huaqiangbei.

If Wi-Fi is so great, then why do most official phones sold in China have it removed? I shamelessly asked a few "bigwigs" in Shenzhen's mobile phone industry, but they all avoided the topic. In fact, for a long time, our country has been promoting domestically developed wireless local area network standards - WAPI. Therefore, to avoid direct competition between Wi-Fi and WAPI and promote the development of WAPI, restricting Wi-Fi phones from entering the market became a necessary measure.

But what exactly is WAPI? According to reports by Global Times and others, it makes wireless networks more secure. However, whether such security is needed, I believe the choice should be left to the users themselves. If consumers feel Wi-Fi is unsafe, they can switch to WAPI. Moreover, if Wi-Fi really is unsafe and harms many user interests, it will naturally be eliminated by the market.

In fact, by 2003, Wi-Fi had already become very popular worldwide, practically becoming the universal standard for wireless local area networks. Many families and enterprises in China were using it to set up wireless computer networks. Equipping phones with Wi-Fi seemed like a natural progression. However, the introduction of WAPI-related standards halted everything. It wasn't until the end of last year that the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology opened the approval process for Wi-Fi phones, allowing a batch of new phones with Wi-Fi functions to hit the market. WAPI now exists in name only. However, the high-speed wireless network that we should have enjoyed on our phones was delayed by six years.

Looking back at the iPhone, what was its initial success attributed to? If it was due to high-speed networks, initially it didn't have 3G functionality, only Wi-Fi. It was Wi-Fi that allowed its users to connect to the internet at the lowest cost, downloading millions of apps and music from iTunes. The business model of the iPhone and iTunes has long been hailed as a miracle.
