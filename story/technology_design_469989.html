The deception of stock market manipulators

by tuoqing1188 on 2011-08-03 13:12:54

1. Pulling up at the end of the market, real exit and fake entry

The major trader uses several large orders to pull up the volume in the last few minutes before the market closes, deliberately setting the closing price. This phenomenon is most common on Fridays. The major trader beautifies the stock chart to attract free recommendations from stock analysts, deceiving investors into thinking that the major trader is about to pull up the stock price, encouraging bold following when the market opens on Monday. This type of operation proves that the major trader's strength is relatively weak and funds are not sufficient. When pulling up at the end of the market, investors don't even have time to place their orders. The major trader aims for exactly this. Only daring to wage guerrilla warfare, not daring to make a direct attack.

2. Deception with price limit ups and downs

The major trader exerts force to pull the stock price to the upper limit, then places several hundred thousand buy orders at the upper limit price. Because the buy orders are not large, short-term followers from all over the country rush in, you with a thousand shares, me with a thousand shares, there will be a hundred or two followers. Then the main player gradually cancels their own buy orders and secretly sells off at the upper limit. When the lower buy orders decrease, the main player again places several hundred thousand buy orders, attracting the last batch of followers to chase the rise, then cancels the order again and distributes further. Therefore, huge trading volume at the upper limit means selling off in eight or nine out of ten cases. Sometimes some stocks open at the lower limit at the beginning of the day, knocking out all the buy orders placed during the pre-market session. Many people will see this and many bargain hunters will appear. If it's not a sell-off, the stock price will immediately recover. If the stock can still take in goods calmly at the lower limit, it absolutely proves that the main player is using the lower limit to sell off.

3. Huge volume breakout at high position

And huge volume refers to a turnover rate exceeding 10%. This kind of breakout is false in eight or nine out of ten cases. Since it's at a high position, the major trader has already made substantial profits, so why would there be huge volume during the breakout? Where does this volume come from? Obviously, the huge volume is the result of short-term followers sweeping up goods and the major trader simultaneously pulling up and distributing. The major trader uses increased volume to deceive investors. Careful analysis shows that since the major trader has already reduced their holdings, this stock price naturally won't last long. Increased volume proves that the locking degree of chips is no longer high.

4. Deception with bid-ask orders at the market interface

In the securities analysis system's bid-ask interface, the major trader likes to perform here. When the bid orders are all five-digit large buy orders, and the ask orders are two-digit small sell orders, most people would think that the main player is about to pull up. That's exactly what the major trader wants to achieve. Guiding investors to sweep up goods, thereby achieving the purpose of the major trader selling off. Water that is too clear holds no fish; if everything was straightforward, what would the major trader use to make money? This is the major trader's reverse thinking. Therefore, to make money, one must be in sync with the major trader.

5. Exploiting the weaknesses of analytical software

A patient major trader only sells 2000-8000 shares each time, never exceeding 10,000 shares, so some software analysis systems wouldn't consider these small transactions as the main player selling off, but rather as retail investors' free exchange. Two operators input on two computers respectively, buying 100 shares at the first ask price and selling 9900 shares at the third or fifth bid price, then placing orders simultaneously. It appears as a transaction of 10,000 shares, and according to the ask order. The analysis system would count this as an active buy order. This requires closely monitoring the average price. If a large active buy order appears but the minute average line drops, it proves that this is a fake buy order and a real sell order.

6. Deception with abnormal movements at the market interface

Some individual stocks, originally moving steadily, suddenly have a large order that knocks down the stock price by 5%, then immediately recovers. Those who bought in think they got a bargain, those who didn't buy also think it's worth picking up this bargain, so they actively place orders at the previous low price. Then the major trader knocks down again, even lower, wiping out all the lower buy orders, thus achieving a win-win outcome. Retail investors think they picked up a bargain, while the major trader is happy to have unloaded a large number of chips. This is a variation of the major trader's method of suppressing to sell off.

7. Strong major-player stocks release huge volume after rights adjustment and push up

This situation mostly involves the major player using counter-trading to lift and distribute. The major player uses rights adjustment to significantly lower the absolute price of the stock, thereby lowering the vigilance of investors. Due to the excellent impression of strong major-player stocks, when the volume increases and the price rises after the rights adjustment at a lower price, most people think that the major player is starting another wave, creating a fill-rights trend. Attracting a large number of followers to enter, the major player pulls up while distributing, the major player doesn't raise the price much, those already in without much profit won't exit, those not yet in feel the increase isn't significant and can follow. Plus the promotion from stock analysts, the major player, with the help of retail investors, converts the stock into cash, smoothly escaping.

8. Bulldozer-style lifting

The major player places hundreds of buy orders on every bid, then places dozens of sell orders on the ask, pushing up level by level, all large active buy orders. In fact, these sell orders are all from the major player, attracting followers to join. This type of lifting indicates that the top is not far away, and the stock price may plummet at any time.
