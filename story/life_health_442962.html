Symptoms and Consequences of Cervicitis: According to gynecological experts, chronic cervicitis is the most common type of gynecological disease. It often occurs after childbirth, abortion, or surgical damage to the cervix when pathogens invade and cause infection, usually evolving from acute cervicitis. Often, incomplete treatment of acute cervicitis allows pathogens to hide within the cervical mucosa, leading to chronic inflammation.

Many women with acute cervicitis have relatively mild symptoms that are often ignored, directly progressing to chronic cervicitis. About 20%-25% of patients with excessive vaginal discharge are caused by cervicitis. If the inflammation causes thick purulent leukorrhea, it can hinder sperm passage through the cervical canal, potentially leading to infertility.

Symptoms and Consequences of Cervicitis:

Increased vaginal discharge is the most common symptom of acute cervicitis, sometimes being the only symptom. The discharge may be purulent or mixed with blood, often accompanied by lower back pain and pelvic heaviness. Sometimes external genitalia experience itching, burning sensation, and urinary symptoms such as frequent urination and urgency due to inflammatory secretions. A few patients may experience dyspareunia, fever, etc.

Some people who develop acute cervicitis may have mild symptoms, so it's often neglected and progresses to chronic cervicitis. The main symptom of chronic cervicitis is increased vaginal discharge. When the inflammation spreads along the uterosacral ligament to the pelvis, there may be lower back pain, sacral pain, and pelvic heaviness. Clinically, it mainly manifests as cervical erosion, cervical hypertrophy, cervical polyps, cervical gland cysts, and cervical mucosal inflammation.

Generally speaking, simple cervicitis does not pose a significant threat to health, but symptoms like increased vaginal discharge, lower back pain, and pelvic heaviness can affect one’s mood. From a cancer prevention perspective, there is a close relationship between cervicitis and cervical cancer.

Cancer screening data shows that 0.39% of women without cervical erosion develop cervical cancer, while 2.05% of women with cervical erosion develop cervical cancer. This indicates that actively treating chronic cervicitis and taking preventive measures are crucial for safeguarding women's health and preventing cervical cancer.

Gynecological experts repeatedly remind that although cervicitis is a common gynecological condition, many women lack understanding about it and have misconceptions.

One misconception is that married women will all have cervicitis, which neither affects work nor life, making it insignificant whether treated or not.

Another misconception is that cervicitis is a precancerous lesion, causing fear of cancer and affecting personal emotions and daily life. These are all incorrect views.

Chronic cervicitis and cervical cancer share some common symptoms, such as post-coital vaginal spotting or blood-streaked leukorrhea. In such cases, a cervical smear, colposcopy, and cervical biopsy should be conducted to rule out cancer before treating cervicitis. Women who are sexually active should ideally undergo a gynecological examination every six months. Additionally, seek medical attention early if any discomfort is noticed to avoid delaying treatment!
