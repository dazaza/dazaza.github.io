The paintings and calligraphy of the Ming loyalists are the essence of Chinese literati painting.

by fbfshtwk0423 on 2012-02-21 14:47:57

In 2009, the sale of Ba Da Shan Ren's imitation of Ni Yunlin's landscape painting for 84 million yuan broke the global auction record for Chinese calligraphy and paintings. Since then, works by "Ming loyalists" have once again entered the vision of collectors and become new market favorites due to their unique aesthetic and historical value. In order to deeply explore the market value of these works, Beijing Kangshiqi Company will host China's first special session of "Ming Loyalist Calligraphy and Painting Works". Nearly a hundred pieces of Ming and Qing calligraphy and paintings will be showcased at Kangshiqi's spring auction, with an estimated more than 20 works exceeding one million yuan.

Dong Guoqiang, chairman of Beijing Kangshiqi Company (Weibo), introduced that the "Special Session of Ming Loyalist Calligraphy and Painting Works" was set up on the basis of successfully hosting sessions such as the "Five Masters of the Late Ming Special Session" and the "Wumen Painting School Special Session" in ancient paintings and calligraphy last year, and it is one of the most important sessions in this year's Kangshiqi Spring Auction. The calligraphy and paintings of Ming loyalists are the essence of Chinese literati paintings and represent the peak of ancient Chinese art history. Dong Guoqiang said, "Through the 'Special Session of Ming Loyalist Calligraphy and Painting Works', we hope to organize the works from this period not only to showcase the charm of Chinese paintings to the world but also to highlight the charisma of 'Ming loyalist' painters." These works of "Ming loyalists" highlight the cultural spirit of traditional Chinese scholars who did not lose their sense of the world despite losing their country.

The Ming loyalist painters spanned both the Ming and Qing dynasties. After the fall of the Ming dynasty, a large number of painters became loyalists, and Ming loyalist calligraphy and paintings appeared under the great environment of loyalist culture in the early Qing dynasty.

Dong Guoqiang introduced that loyalist culture, as a special cultural phenomenon, injected new content into literature and art. Famous painters in the early Qing dynasty like the "Four Monks" - Ba Da, Kun Can, Hong Ren, Shi Tao, representatives of the "Jinling Painting School" Gong Xian, representatives of the "Changzhou Painting School" Yun Shouping, Shanxi scholar Fu Shan, Ningdu Wei Xi, Kunshan Gu Yanwu, father and son Gui, etc., almost covered the entire art history of the transition between the Ming and Qing dynasties. They either became monks or lived in seclusion, all becoming loyalists in this transformation and leaving many classic works in art history.

Dong Guoqiang said: "During the transition between the Ming and Qing dynasties, social thought waves were intense. As a special group, the loyalist intellectuals had unprecedented feelings of national hatred and cultural concerns, developing unique cultural patterns amidst turmoil and producing extensive influence in terms of scale, geographic scope, and duration. This injected new content into history, culture, and art. In terms of calligraphy and painting, there was a penchant for antiquity, advocating the 'revival of seal script', preserving remnants while maintaining traditions. There was also a tendency towards 'madness and ugliness', where the styles of Chen Hongshou, Fu Shan, Zhu Da, and others showed a 'wild and bizarre' appearance, influencing the shift in aesthetic trends. Furthermore, loyalist calligraphy and paintings were not considered orthodox, lacking clear collection and inheritance lineages. They also underwent multiple hardships such as the 'literary inquisitions' during the mid-Qing dynasty, nearly being annihilated, with scarce records available. Their emergence has profound implications for cultural and art history."

The style of these works is often dry, thin, and unrestrained. Zheng Zhenduo once said: "The entry of the Manchus did not disrupt the long-standing historical tradition. People like Xiang Shengmo, Cheng Sui, Xiao Yuncong, Wang Shimin, Yun Shouping... some served as imperial artists, others expressed their inner emotions, yet none escaped the traditions of the Song and Yuan dynasties. However, Ming loyalists like Hong Ren, Fu Shan, Gong Xian, Kun Can, Dao Ji, Zhu Da, among others, poured their sorrow and anger into their paintings, showcasing a style that was dry, thin, and unrestrained."

Dong Guoqiang lamented that with the destruction of the land and the change of dynasties, most "Ming loyalist painters" were born at the end of the Ming dynasty, personally experiencing its fall. Their main artistic careers spanned both the Ming and Qing dynasties, with their works mostly presenting a scene of a broken homeland, predominantly characterized by desolation and darkness. He said: "These loyalist painters often added aliases to express their loyalty, such as Zhu Yi's alias 'Qian'an'; Chen Hongshou's alias 'Huichi'; Gong Xian's self-proclaimed 'Yiye'; Lu Yuanhong portraying himself in ink, calling himself 'ink person' implying no country and no home; and Ba Da Shan Ren with aliases like 'Xuege' and 'Xuenai', symbolizing purity and even 'washing away shame'. The birds and beasts in his works all displayed 'white eyes' to people, whether in changes of aliases, themes of creation, or styles, all showing the regret of 'loyalist painters' after the loss of their country, and their disheartenment about the remaining years."

Moreover, many Ming literati were skilled in painting, which had become an indispensable skill for expressing their talents. However, due to special life experiences and social environments, these works were mostly done for personal solace, hastily completed without seeking resemblance in form, with themes often being landscapes and flowers suitable for this purpose. "Even in landscape paintings, few people painted panoramic large-scale landscapes as seriously as in the Five Dynasties and early Song dynasty. Depicting their own or friends' residences was a favorite theme for loyalist painters. The leisurely years spent in ruined gardens brought the creation of 'Ming loyalist painters' into a fully mature stage," Dong Guoqiang said.

Works by Ming loyalist painters have become market favorites. In 2009, Ba Da Shan Ren's imitation of Ni Yunlin's landscape painting sold for 84 million yuan, breaking the global auction record for Chinese calligraphy and paintings; in the Spring Auction of 2011, Fu Shan's "Grass Script Seven-Character Poem Hanging Scroll" sold for 6.67 million yuan; in the Autumn Auction of 2011, Chen Hongshou's "Jiangshan Wo You Tujuan" (hand scroll) sold for 18.63 million yuan... It is evident that the change of dynasty and social unrest altered the ideological aesthetics of many people, and loyalist painters presented unprecedented stylistic characteristics. With the rise of the calligraphy and painting market, these loyalist works have become market darlings.

Dong Guoqiang said: "Currently, holding special sessions for masters like Qi Baishi and Zhang Daqian is relatively easy, but the difficulty of holding a special session for Ming loyalist calligraphy and painting works is very high. Not only must there be a certain quantity, but also masterpieces, all of which belong to scarce resources, making them rare opportunities."

Dong Guoqiang introduced that in order to showcase the characteristics of Ming loyalist calligraphy and painting works, they are currently soliciting works from the market and veteran collectors. "The collection work is progressing smoothly, with frequent appearances of masterpieces. Among them, original works like 'Poetry Album of Vegetable Planting' witnessing the grand literary events of the early Qing dynasty are appearing for the first time. This target composed of fourteen works, treasured by the Wu family of Zhejiang for three hundred years, presents over fifty giants from the political and literary circles of the time engaging in mutual poetic exchanges, reflecting a segment of history under the backdrop of the transition between the 17th and 18th centuries in China. The completeness, systematic nature, and historicity of the works have never been seen in auctions, and especially the verification of the works against historical facts highlights their authenticity and rarity," Dong Guoqiang said.
