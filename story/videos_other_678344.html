When it comes to "military parades," today's friends are naturally not unfamiliar with them. For modern countries, military parades are both a way to demonstrate national strength and enhance ethnic cohesion, as well as an important means to expand national influence. Both the former Soviet Union and the United States have had world-renowned military parades. In the history of the People's Republic of China, previous grand military parades still make the hearts of the Chinese people swell with pride and nostalgia. Nearly six hundred years ago, during the Ming Dynasty, Emperor Yongle Zhu Di also once held a shocking "Grand Military Parade" ——the Yongle Emperor's Grand Parade in Beijing.

Speaking of this parade, we must first talk about the little-known international situation at the beginning of the Ming Dynasty. The Ming Dynasty rose after overthrowing the Mongol Yuan Dynasty, but at that time, three of the four khanates established by Genghis Khan were still in existence. In the second year of Hongwu (1369), Zhu Yuanzhang mobilized forty thousand troops, advancing north in three routes, successively occupying the capital of the Yuan Dynasty and the Hexi Corridor, completely turning the far-fled Beiyuan court into a nomadic tribe. That same year, the other three khanates formed a coalition army intending to march east for rescue, but upon reaching the Pamir Mountains, they received news that the Western Army led by Feng Sheng of the Ming Dynasty had annihilated all Yuan forces in the Hexi Corridor, prompting them to hastily retreat. Subsequently, many Mongolian and Hui officials who had served in the Yuan Dynasty fled to various Central Asian and West Asian countries, quickly seizing key positions. These "foreign monks" vigorously urged their respective kings to take a hostile stance toward the Ming Dynasty.

In 1370, Temur, a powerful minister of the Western Chagatai Khanate, launched a coup, overthrowing the Western Chagatai Khanate and establishing the famous "Timurid Empire." Afterward, he waged wars in Central Asia and West Asia. Initially, Timur took a submissive attitude toward the Ming Dynasty in the East, actively sending envoys to establish good relations, recognizing China as the "superior country," and paying tribute while acknowledging vassalage. However, the Ming Dynasty remained vigilant, setting up defenses in Hami, courting the Eastern Chagatai Khanate to the east of Timur, and deploying heavy troops in the northwest under the command of founding hero Song Sheng. After defeating the Ottoman Turks, Timur began preparations for a distant expedition to China, sending spies to Beijing. He detained Ming Dynasty trade delegations and envoys sent to the Timurid Empire, publicly humiliating the Chinese envoy Guo Ji, who had been held captive for eight years, during the reception of Egyptian envoys. Subsequently, emulating Genghis Khan, he convened a "Mongol Congress" in Samarkand, raising the banner of "anti-Ming restoration." However, what was called the "Mongol Congress" mostly consisted of Central Asian nomadic tribes, with representatives from the Mongol core, including the Tatars and Oirats, absent. Instead, they sent envoys to Nanjing to establish friendly relations with the Ming Dynasty and reported Timur's intention to invade the east.

In the fourth year of Yongle, Timur launched a large-scale eastern expedition, claiming an army of a million, though the actual force was twenty thousand. By then, the Ming Dynasty had already gathered intelligence through its own network of informants in the northwest, amassing heavy troops in the Hexi Corridor and Hami, ready to confront the threat. However, soon after, Timur died on his way to the east, causing the impending war to dissipate. Thereafter, the princes of the Timurid Empire fought over the throne, plunging the empire into civil war, making the so-called eastern expedition to China nothing more than a pipe dream.

On the Ming side, after Zhu Di ascended the throne, he dispatched Zheng He to sail to the Western Seas to proclaim national prestige, while simultaneously sending envoys like Chen Cheng to visit the Western Regions. Later, the successor of the Timurid Empire, Shah Rukh, abandoned his anti-Chinese policy, sending envoys to establish friendly relations with the Ming Dynasty, restoring the "sovereign state" relationship of the Hongwu era. Nevertheless, anti-Chinese forces in Central Asia remained active. Meanwhile, although Zheng He made five voyages to the Western Seas before the eighteenth year of Yongle, most expeditions only reached the Indian Ocean before returning, without entering the Red Sea or Arab regions. This was due to the rise of the Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt after Timur's death, which controlled the Red Sea area and strictly prohibited Eastern fleets from entering, thus avoiding potential conflicts. Zhu Di's diplomatic actions reaped rewards, as Central Asian and West Asian countries frequently sent envoys to China, formally recognizing the Ming Dynasty's status as the "Celestial Dynasty" succeeding the Yuan Dynasty. Yet, doubts persisted among Western countries regarding the true strength of the Ming Dynasty. Therefore, under the premise of peaceful friendship, choosing an appropriate opportunity to display the formidable power of the Ming Dynasty to all nations and solidify the "tribute relationship" between the Ming and Central Asian and West Asian countries became particularly important. And a military parade provided just such an opportunity.

In the sixteenth year of Yongle (AD 1418), the Ming Dynasty sent Chen Cheng as an envoy to Central Asia. The following January, they arrived at Herat, the capital of the Timurid Empire. Subsequently, Central Asian and West Asian countries all formed delegations requesting to return with the Chinese delegation. In the seventh month of the eighteenth year of Yongle, this delegation, comprising twenty Central Asian and West Asian countries, with over six hundred members, reached Jiayuguan. Surprisingly, Zhu Di dispatched six thousand elite cavalry to meet them there and subsequently escorted them along the route past Jiayuguan through the nine frontier military towns of the Ming Dynasty for a "tour."

During their visits to the various military towns of the Ming Dynasty, the Ming specially assigned Mongolian and Hui officers serving in the Ming army to fully receive them, introducing the Ming's policy of ethnic equality, debunking rumors spread by Central Asian "anti-China factions" that the Ming Dynasty mistreated Mongols and Hui people. What astonished these foreign envoys even more was that the number of troops in each of the nine border "military zones" of the Ming, estimated secretly, equaled the entire armed forces of a medium-sized Central Asian country. And at the easternmost point of their journey, Beijing, a long-planned military parade was already prepared.

On November 17th of the eighteenth year of Yongle, the delegation arrived in Beijing, where the city was already brimming with troops. Coincidentally, this was when Emperor Zhu Di was planning his third northern campaign against Mongolia, and Beijing was surrounded by heavy forces. On November 20th, Zhu Di received the envoys from various countries in the Beijing Palace. All envoys performed the traditional kneeling bow, except for the envoy from the Timurid Empire, who only bowed. When Chinese officials requested him to kneel, the Timurid envoy replied, "Our country has no such custom," insisting on bowing instead. Zhu Di paid no attention to this. The reason the Timurid envoy was so assertive was that the leader of this Timurid delegation was the Prime Minister of the Timurid Empire, Al-Dusha, and the deputy was Gesuyedding, a trusted general who had accompanied Timur in numerous campaigns. Even in the Timurid Empire itself, they were exempt from kneeling.

After the diplomatic protocol, Zhu Di informed the envoys of their itinerary arrangements. First, they could travel around Shandong, Henan, and Jiangsu provinces for "sightseeing." They were also allowed free access to Ming military bases with no "military restrictions." Subsequently, these delegations were led by Ming officials to tour various places in China. Particularly, the Timurid envoys were arranged to visit the Kong family residence in Shandong and pay homage at the Confucian Temple. After two months of "sightseeing visits," the various delegations received notification that Zhu Di would begin a large-scale "hunt" near Beijing and invited all delegations to observe.

In March of the nineteenth year of Yongle, the "hunt" officially began outside Huailai City to the north of Beijing. A total of one hundred thousand troops from various units participated, showcasing the elite formations of the Ming "Five Armies Camp," "Three Thousand Camp," and "Divine Machine Camp" in exercises demonstrating cavalry flanking maneuvers, infantry assaults, combined arms operations, and more. Additionally, southern "native wolf soldiers" and "white rod soldiers" from Guangxi, Yunnan, and Sichuan demonstrated infantry crossbow volleys and spear training drills. Historical records describe the "impressive military discipline," "uniform steps," "bright armor," and "all envoys being amazed." Especially noteworthy was the fire-arm drill of the Ming "Divine Machine Camp" and the rapid cavalry charge using firearms, showcasing advanced weaponry such as "Tiger-Intimidating Cannons," "Fire Dragon Muskets" (special cavalry muskets), Annan guns (swivel guns), "One Nest of Bees" (rocket artillery), and "Fire Dragon Vehicles" (flamethrowers). These "high-tech" weapons were previously unheard of by foreigners and left experts like Gesuyedding awestruck. The grand "hunt" lasted an entire month.

After the parade, Zhu Di "warmly received" the envoys at his field camp in Tumu Fort (the place where the later Ming emperor Yingzong was captured). This time, the Timurid envoys led the way in kneeling and kowtowing, disregarding their earlier claim of "no such custom in our country." The Timurid envoy further flattered Zhu Di by stating that the fine horses presented by Timur were the very mounts used by Timur's father in his lifetime for south-north campaigns, specifically gifted to Zhu Di to express the "highest respect." Zhu Di was delighted upon hearing this and reciprocated with a "long gun" (firearm) used by his son (second son Zhu Gaoshu) during the Rebellion of the Jianwen Era, instructing the envoy to convey his "sincerest gratitude" to the king of the Timurid Empire. Thus, the exchange of gifts between the rulers of the two greatest empires of the 15th century was quite interesting.

This grand military parade undoubtedly achieved excellent results. The number of envoys from various countries attending the ceremony, including the Central Asian-West Asian delegation and envoys from Southeast Asia and Indochina, totaled twenty-seven countries. The might of the Ming army left a profound impression on all nations. Although Zhu Di passed away afterward, and starting from the Xuande period, the Ming Dynasty ceased sending envoys abroad, the friendly relations between Central Asian-West Asian countries and China continued until the end of the Ming Dynasty. Just the Timurid Empire alone sent envoys to China more than sixty times throughout the Ming Dynasty. Details of this parade were meticulously recorded in various historical documents of Central Asian countries. The deputy envoy of the Timurid Empire, Gesuyedding, frankly admitted in his memoirs about the Ming's military strength: "I have to admit, it was fortunate that the Great Emperor (Timur) died on his way to the east, preserving his lifelong reputation."

Another "chain reaction" caused by this parade was that after returning home, the envoy of the Egyptian Mamluk Sultanate lifted the ban on Eastern merchant ships in the Red Sea. From then on, silk, spices, and other goods from the East could be transported more smoothly to the Red Sea region and enter Europe through this route. Zheng He's last voyage to the Western Seas finally reached the shores of the Red Sea, not only establishing friendly relations with countries in the Red Sea region but also fulfilling his lifelong wish as a Muslim—performing the pilgrimage to Mecca.
