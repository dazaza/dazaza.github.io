Eighty percent of fans support Owen going to the World Cup, but the Golden Boy's biggest enemy is not Capello.

by qinglai888 on 2009-12-13 12:48:58

Eight out of ten fans support Owen going to the World Cup; Golden Boy's biggest enemy isn't Capello - is Owen's biggest enemy Sir Alex?

Report by Xiang Tong: "Next Monday is Owen's 30th birthday? Wow, I suddenly feel like an uncle!" This was the exclamation of a British journalist. The image of the wind-chasing youth seems like yesterday, and today's Owen is about to enter his thirties, impossible not to make one sigh at how time flies. Time has changed Owen in many ways, but no matter how he grows a beard, it's hard for people to get used to calling him a "veteran." Is Owen old? Finished? Half a year ago, many people would have answered yes. However, after seeing Owen's hat-trick in this week's Champions League match, you might reconsider your judgment.

Speaking of his December 14th birthday, Owen joked, "I'd rather you say I'm more experienced than older! My twenties are down to just one week left, so I need to enjoy it well!" How to enjoy it? When Owen ended the game against Wolfsburg, celebrating his hat-trick by stuffing the ball into his shirt and walking off the field with a "big belly," that was certainly the happiest Owen. Scoring goals is the theme of Owen's career. As he approaches thirty, although he doesn't say it openly, everyone knows how much he longs to participate in the World Cup for the fourth time and leave more significant goals in South Africa.

Super Substitution, Super Efficiency

The reason to take Owen to the World Cup? Goals. Owen always reminds outsiders that during his one year at Real Madrid, his goal-scoring rate per minute was the highest in La Liga. And this season, if calculated per minute, Owen's goal-scoring rate in club matches remains very impressive. Among the eight English forwards in contention for the World Cup squad, his efficiency of scoring a goal every 112 minutes ranks only behind Defoe, surpassing national team favorites Rooney and Heskey.

Perhaps Capello does not plan to include Owen in the starting lineup, but that’s fine. Being a super substitute, he can still be very effective. Owen’s playing time in the Premier League this season has been limited, with only two goals scored, but both were achieved as a substitute. Remember the last-minute decisive strike against Manchester City; wouldn't Capello want to replicate that moment in South Africa?

Capello may still distrust Owen's health and fitness. But it should be noted that since joining Manchester United, Owen no longer resembles the glass man he once was—there have been injuries, but they've been minor ones—by the way, how many players can stay healthy and play an entire season without interruption? As for fitness, you only need to look at Owen's ability to sprint 40 yards with speed and strength during the injury time in the Wolfsburg match to score that beautiful goal, and there shouldn’t be too much doubt anymore.

In post-match interviews, Owen refrained from commenting on his national team prospects, “I don't want to talk too much about myself; I prefer to talk about the team.” But his Manchester United teammate Carrick strongly supported him, “The World Cup squad isn't up to me to decide, but you just need to recall some of the important goals Owen has scored to form your own judgment. I don't think his performance in this UEFA Champions League match proved anything we didn't already know; throughout his career, he has consistently seized opportunities and scored important goals.”

Veteran Beardsley also supports Owen, “With Owen's achievements, he will always be in my list of 23 for the World Cup. Owen's skill lies in rarely missing opportunities, even during his time at Newcastle, which was proven again in the Wolfsburg match. Actually, the third goal wasn't really an opportunity, but he created it himself. I don't think Owen's health will be an issue; he isn't a glass man, he just had some strange injuries during his time at Newcastle. I'm not saying I would definitely make Owen the starter, but taking him to South Africa is no problem.”

Former England caretaker coach Peter Taylor said, “Taking him as a super substitute would be worthwhile. But I think it's either him or Defoe; it's unlikely both will go.”

Public opinion is also tilting towards Owen. In a Guardian online poll, 80% of fans believe Owen should go to the World Cup. In a Telegraph poll involving over 30,000 participants, the number of fans picking Owen to enter England's World Cup starting lineup significantly increased this week, rising from 12% to 22%. In this poll, Rooney naturally garnered the favor of 96% of fans, and among choices for Rooney's partner, 43% still favored Defoe, making Owen the third choice. Of course, fans' preferences won't influence Capello, after all, only 13% public opinion supports Heskey as Rooney's first-choice wingman...

Bookmakers' odds are the result of meticulous analysis, and their values are naturally more rational. Ladbrokes has adjusted Owen's odds of participating in the World Cup to 2.25, while Bet365 is more conservative at 4.0. These odds have improved greatly compared to before, but clearly, their judgment remains: being excluded is more likely than inclusion. After all, currently seven forwards have better odds than Owen for inclusion.

The Three Enemies Blocking Owen's Fourth Dream

In a certain sense, Owen's biggest obstacle to participating in the World Cup isn't someone else—it's his club manager, Sir Alex Ferguson. Many media outlets, including the Daily Mail, insist that if Owen cannot play more games for Manchester United, then the World Cup dream is still out of reach. With 25 games played this season, Owen has started only 8 times; in 15 league games, he has started just three times. Such a low appearance rate provides the most convenient excuse for Capello, who has explicitly stated that he only considers club starters.

Owen's direct competitor is widely regarded as Tottenham Hotspur forward Jermain Defoe. Capello has said that he hopes to bring only four forwards to South Africa, with the ideal combination being two big and two small players. If this is true, then Heskey and Crouch are not in direct competition with Owen; Rooney's core status is unshakable, leaving Defoe as Owen's main rival.

In the past, one could argue that Defoe's ability to seize opportunities is inferior to Owen's, but for this season's Tottenham striker who has scored 12 goals in 13 league appearances, this statement is clearly outdated. In other aspects, Defoe's footwork, shooting power, and long-range shooting ability are all superior to Owen's to varying degrees, while Owen excels in international tournament experience, his awareness, and positioning far surpass Defoe's, and he is less prone to offside traps. However, given Defoe's fiery form this season, not taking him to the World Cup seems unreasonable. Former England manager Graham Taylor stated, "At present, I would still choose Defoe. He is Tottenham's mainstay, leading the Premier League scorers list, these conditions are enough."

Meanwhile, The Times presented a unique view that Owen's real competitor is actually Theo Walcott. Walcott can play both as a winger and a forward; his hat-trick against Croatia secured his place on the World Cup list. However, like Owen, he is often plagued by injuries, having appeared only four times for England after his stunning performance in Zagreb, and this season at Arsenal, he has only one Premier League start. If Walcott's physical condition ultimately fails to meet the standards required for major tournaments, and if Capello decides to add a fifth forward, Owen might have a chance.

Of course, Owen's real obstacle is still Capello, the only iron-willed coach with the final say on player selection. In veteran Cascaisino's view, Capello has already made his decision regarding player selection. Capello's England team no longer relies on star individuals as in the past; the Italian selects players based solely on his tactical needs. In Capello's eyes, Rooney and Heskey are the preferred choices. He believes Owen doesn't mesh well with Rooney, and Owen is only suitable to pair with Heskey, making Owen's chances extremely slim because such a combination is almost impossible in South Africa.

Moreover, Capello does not appreciate opportunistic strikers like Owen. One of Capello's famous quotes is, "A forward who only scores goals is not enough." Although former star Shearer once retorted, "Do you expect Owen to save penalties?" in Capello's philosophy, forwards indeed need to possess ball control, shielding, passing, and even defensive abilities. Supporters of Owen would argue that a forward who scores goals is the ultimate truth; but Capello would argue that out of Owen's 89 national team appearances, he scored in only 32 matches, making his contribution in the remaining 57 matches quite limited.

Veteran Lineker also admitted that it would be difficult for Owen to go to the World Cup, but he insisted that Capello should not easily discard him, "If Capello were to finalize the list now, Michael probably wouldn't be included. Michael needs to play more games for Manchester United; if he can achieve this and maintain a stable goal-scoring rate, then what you have before you is a player who has been tested at the highest level."
