Looking back at the economic reforms of China over the past 30 years, we see that before the 1998 Asian financial crisis, the consensus on reform was based on breaking down the planned economic system. All reforms began as "illegal" activities, later recognized by the government through retrospective approval. The original vested interest groups had either disintegrated or found their interests unprofitable, while new commercial beneficiaries grew up outside the system.

Chapter One: Macroeconomic Threshold in Saving China's Economy

Premier Wen Jiabao once frankly admitted that 2008 would be the most difficult year for China's economy. This unprecedented statement was a clear signal, indicating that the 30-year reform and opening-up had entered a new phase. After several rounds of economic changes, the ship of marketization in industrial fields reached midstream only to encounter the stormy waves of the financial crisis. To preserve the fruits of reform for emerging industrial nations, China has two tough economic battles to fight—industrial structure adjustment and monetary marketization. If these battles are not won, the reform will regress by decades.
