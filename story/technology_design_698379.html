Year-End Reflections

There is only one day left until the New Year. After settling down in Nanjing, every year on the last day before the holiday, I would buy a few red papers and write a couplet for the Spring Festival. This year, I couldn't come up with a good couplet, so I searched online for a long time. Most of them were suitable for rural gates. Eventually, I found one that caught my eye, but it was originally intended for shop entrances. Nevertheless, I thought it would work for my door as well. The couplet reads: "Greet with words and receive with propriety; those nearby are pleased, and those afar will come." Writing the couplet gave me a sense of accomplishment. After writing it in black ink on the red paper, it felt complete and spirited. Later, I realized this was a great way to conclude my calligraphy practice from the previous year, Yi You.

I also wrote another couplet for an old leader in my family: "A new year extends longevity; virtue enriches life." The old leader had a fiery temper but became carefree after retirement. He is a rare person who knows when to enter and when to retreat, someone I deeply admire. We live in the same compound, but it seems like there's never enough time. Every year, I visit him around this time, bringing a couplet and two bottles of wine. We spend hours chatting leisurely about his life experiences. Listening to him, I often gain insights and share them with him, earning his approval, which makes me feel like a child who has accomplished something significant in front of an elder. When I visited him this year, I saw the couplet I wrote for him last year framed and displayed prominently in his living room. I was deeply moved by how much he cherished it despite my modest calligraphy skills. The two bottles of wine were merely a token of our understanding, yet they became a topic of conversation for him. Last year, he insisted on giving me his beloved large inkstone as I was leaving. This inkstone was always displayed on his living room table. He used to love writing when he was young, stopped during busy times, and now picked it up again. Such kindness overwhelmed me, and I refused to accept it, but he insisted on delivering it to my doorstep. This kind of deep friendship left me humbled and unsettled for days. As the top half of my couplet says: "Receive with propriety!" Due to his insistence, I failed to reciprocate properly, which left me uneasy for many days.

After the Spring Festival, on the sixth day, I received an email from Professor Qiu Bin at Southeast University, inviting me to set off fireworks on his blog. I met Professor Qiu last year during a management change training session. Knowing him as a rare scholar, I have wanted to get closer to him. Knowing how busy he is, evident from the times he leaves comments on my blog, I didn't dare disturb him before the New Year. After receiving his email, I quickly went to his blog to set off fireworks. Initially, I started a blog myself, and he has been following its progress closely. No matter how late it was, he always made time to visit, which deeply touched me. Last night, he made time to exchange ideas with us, providing much insight. Just as my couplet suggests, "those nearby are pleased," what does this mean? True friends transcend time and space.

This year, I didn't return to Lusi to reunite with my parents. On New Year's Day, I called my mother to wish her a happy New Year. She mentioned that a classmate of mine came looking for me and took my phone number since I wasn't there. I suspected it might be him. On the second day of the New Year, he called back, saying he was Li Xiaodong and already on a short trip to Nanjing to see me—it was indeed him. We were classmates in junior high school, and we hadn't been in touch for nearly fifteen years. Back then, we were close friends. In recent years, with the rise of phones and mobile devices, I only knew that he repeated his senior year twice and failed to enter university, subsequently venturing out into the world. Upon meeting, our fifteen-year separation felt non-existent, and our reunion was incredibly intimate. Time flies, but it hasn't changed our friendship. He said he was doing business in Qinhuangdao, and I didn't inquire about the scale of his business, feeling that it would detract from the urgency of our reconnection after so many years. Instead, I sensed his achievements through his confident demeanor. We talked about childhood memories and the feelings of meeting again after so many years. After arriving, he suggested treating my family to dinner. I declined, saying that if he came to my place, these matters shouldn't concern him. If I earned more, I'd treat him to a better meal; if less, a simpler one. He didn't insist, and I realized that offering to treat me was his gesture, while not insisting further showed his sincerity. Time may erode many things, but not the bonds of old friendships. Just like a riddle in my couplet, "those afar will come"—it was him, who else could it be? (Draft)
