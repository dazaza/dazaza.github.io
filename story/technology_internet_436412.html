Preface

Valuing etiquette, being polite, reciprocity, is a traditional virtue of the Chinese nation. It not only nurtures the civilized and courteous character of the descendants of Yan and Huang, but also earns the Chinese nation the title of "Land of Courtesy." Today, we still need to explore the contemporary value of "etiquette," enhance education in civilized etiquette, and contribute to building a harmonious society and even a harmonious world.

I. The Origin and Inheritance of "Etiquette"

The origin of "etiquette" has a long history. Its source is a religious ceremony from primitive society used to worship gods and seek blessings. In "Shuowen Jiezi," it says: "Etiquette means fulfilling obligations, which are used to serve gods and bring blessings." In "Liji - Biaoji," it says: "The people of Yin respected gods, led the people to serve gods, prioritized ghosts over rituals..." This indicates that during the Shang Dynasty, our ancestors mainly used "etiquette" to worship ghosts and gods, seeking blessings. At this time, "etiquette" was not yet a moral norm that constrained human behavior.

By the Zhou Dynasty, people began to emphasize the role of "etiquette" in regulating interpersonal relationships, extending "etiquette" to systems for governing the country and corresponding moral norms. The systematization and standardization of "etiquette" started with Duke Zhou's "creation of rites and music." Learning from the lessons of the fall of the Shang Dynasty, Duke Zhou proposed the idea of "virtuous governance," advocating the use of "rituals and music" to govern the world. The starting point and destination of Duke Zhou's "creation of rites and music" were "respect for authority" and "filial piety." Respect for authority represents "loyalty," and filial piety represents "filial duty." The former aimed to maintain imperial power; the latter aimed to uphold paternal authority. This theory of political and ethical unity became the ideological foundation of "etiquette."

During the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods, with the annexation wars brought about by great power rivalries, the slave system gradually collapsed, disrupting the ritual order established by Duke Zhou's creation of rites, leading to the situation of "ritual collapse and musical disorder." With the advent of feudal times, new economic relations, political systems, and value concepts also gradually formed and developed. During this period, thinkers began to creatively develop the concept of "etiquette."

The one who advocated "etiquette" as the highest moral principle should be Guan Zhong from the early Spring and Autumn period. Guan Zhong defined "propriety, righteousness, integrity, and shame" as the "four pillars of the state," placing "propriety" at the forefront. He emphasized that "if the four pillars are not upheld, the state will perish," pointing out the importance of "propriety, righteousness, integrity, and shame" in national governance.

In the inheritance of "etiquette," Confucius made very important contributions. Confucius proposed the slogan of "self-restraint and restoring propriety, thereby achieving benevolence worldwide," vigorously promoting Zhou propriety, advocating the use of Zhou propriety to regulate people's behavior. Confucius believed that the main reason for the "ritual collapse and musical disorder" during the Spring and Autumn period was "lack of benevolence." The difference between "benevolence" and "lack of benevolence" lies in "etiquette." What conforms to "etiquette" is "benevolence," what does not conform to "etiquette" is "lack of benevolence." In his view, "benevolence" is the "essence," an internal moral emotion and ethical thought; "etiquette" is the "use," an external moral conduct and specific behavioral norm. Confucius advocated closely combining the content of "benevolence" with the form of "etiquette" to achieve the realm of "not looking, not listening, not speaking, and not moving without propriety" (from "Analects - Yan Yuan"), realizing the social ideal of "governing through virtue, aligning through propriety, having a sense of shame and self-discipline" (from "Analects - Wei Zheng"). Confucius' ideas on propriety had a creative development of Zhou propriety, making the concept of "etiquette" last for a long time in the subsequent feudal society, having a profound impact on China's historical culture.

Mencius inherited Confucius' ethical thoughts, proposing the "theory of innate goodness." Based on this theoretical foundation, he further proposed the idea of practicing "benevolent government," summarizing the basic virtues of humans as the "four virtues" of "benevolence, righteousness, propriety, and wisdom," and summarizing the interpersonal relationship principles of feudal society as the "five relationships," namely "parents and children have affection, ruler and minister have righteousness, husband and wife have distinction, elder and younger have order, friends have trust" (from "Mencius - Teng Wen Gong Shang"). Mencius regarded "propriety" as the "heart of respectfulness," as one of the "four virtues," and further regarded the "five relationships" as the core of "propriety" in feudal society. This had a profound influence on later generations.

Xunzi opposed Mencius' views on human nature, advocating the "theory of innate depravity." However, in terms of Neo-Confucian thought, they inevitably followed the same path. Xunzi's contribution to Neo-Confucian thought lies in revealing the origin and function of "etiquette." He said: "Where does propriety arise? It arises because: people are born with desires, if their desires cannot be fulfilled, then they cannot help but seek. If their seeking is without measure or boundary, then they cannot help but contend. Contention leads to chaos, and chaos leads to poverty. The ancient kings detested the chaos, so they created propriety and righteousness to divide them, to nurture people's desires, and to satisfy their needs. Making sure that desires do not exhaust things, and things do not fail to satisfy desires, the two support each other and grow, this is where propriety arises," (from "Xunzi - Ritual Theory") This passage reveals the origin of "etiquette." Regarding the function of "etiquette," Xunzi said: "A country without propriety cannot be governed properly. The reason propriety governs a country is like a balance measures lightness and heaviness, like ink lines measure straightness and crookedness, like compasses measure roundness and squareness." (from "Xunzi - Kings and Hegemons") Xunzi also incorporated "law" into "Confucianism," embedding "law" within "etiquette," unifying "etiquette" and "law," and proposed the idea of "etiquette governance," which had an important impact on the entire feudal society thereafter.

During the Western Han Dynasty, Emperor Wu adopted Dong Zhongshu's suggestion of "abolishing all schools of thought except Confucianism," taking a series of measures to "treat people respectfully according to propriety" and "encourage learning and promote propriety," using Confucian thought to manage society, establishing a complete set of social norms, converting Confucian doctrines into guiding principles and normative political life, social life regulations and "law"-based behavioral patterns, and continuously engaging in ceremonial activities. The cultural model of "etiquette" created during the Han Dynasty was constantly consolidated and perfected over the subsequent two thousand years of history.

China's thousands of years of civilized development history shows that "etiquette" is indispensable for both society and individuals. For society, "etiquette" is a direct indicator of social morality and civilization levels; for individuals, "etiquette" is an external sign of a person's moral quality and level of cultivation. Undoubtedly, in feudal times, many specific contents of "etiquette" actually reinforced the hierarchical concepts of feudal hierarchy, and many formalities have become outdated and lost meaning. However, the functions and roles of "etiquette" in maintaining social order, coordinating interpersonal relationships, and advocating civility and politeness are needed in any era. Today, we can still use the form of "etiquette" to inherit the essence of ancient ceremonial culture, remove its feudal dross, endow it with new content of modern civilization, thereby creating a new ceremonial culture suitable for our current era.

Respect for Teachers Etiquette Section

A famous ancient Chinese saying goes: "A teacher is like a parent." You must know the idiom "Respect for Teachers and Value for the Way," right? During the Song Dynasty, there was a renowned scholar named Cheng Yi. A contemporary named Yang Shi admired him greatly and wanted to become his disciple. One winter day, when Cheng Yi was sleeping, Yang Shi had to wait outside the door respectfully. By the time Cheng Yi woke up, the snow outside the door was already more than a foot deep, and Yang Shi had become a snowman. Moved by Yang Shi's sincere and genuine spirit, Cheng Yi finally accepted him as a disciple.

As elementary school students deeply taught by teachers, it is self-evident that we should love and respect our teachers. Respecting teachers should come from the heart and be reflected in actions, one of the key points being showing courtesy to teachers. Let's get to the topic.

1. Teacher Respect Etiquette

One day’s teacher, a lifetime father. —— Guan Hanqing

Learning should prioritize respecting teachers. —— Tan Sitong

(1) Greet teachers actively and say “goodbye” when parting.

(2) Yield the way to teachers when entering or leaving school and going up or down stairs.

(3) Announce “report” before entering the office, and enter only after hearing “please come in.” Use “may I ask” when asking questions.

(4) Correct teachers’ mistakes politely.

(5) Listen humbly to teachers’ guidance and accept their teachings.

(6) Tell teachers the truth, don’t deceive them.

(7) Cherish teachers’ efforts and complete tasks assigned by them on time.

(8) Obey teachers’ management, don’t talk back.

(9) When encountering teachers on campus paths, stop voluntarily, bow slightly, and greet them. Greet multiple teachers with “teachers, hello”; when meeting teachers while marching, the leader should lead all classmates to greet “teacher, hello.”

(10) When conversing with teachers, stand up and offer them seats actively.

(11) Do not disturb teachers when they are busy or talking with others; stand respectfully aside until they finish.

(12) When teachers enter the dormitory, students should stand up to greet and offer seats; when teachers leave, stand up to see them off.

2. Three-Word Song

Morning breeze blows, sunshine shines. Good students, practice politeness.

See teachers, greet them kindly. Learn knowledge, follow advice.

Talk to teachers, be humble. See teachers busy, don't disturb.

Do homework, value independence. Meet teachers, make them happy.

Encounter teachers, yield the way. Be honest, don't lie.

Talk about learning, practice politeness. Good habits, are important.

Walking Etiquette Section

Ancients said: "Stand like a pine, sit like a bell," which is a requirement for standing posture, indeed, etiquette is everywhere, and even small details can reflect a person's cultivation, walking is no exception. Have you met these requirements?

1. Walking Etiquette

Life is fleeting, but despite this, people still have time to observe etiquette. —— Love Contribution Life

Etiquette is a subtle thing, it is indispensable in people's interactions, yet it should not be overly calculated. —— Bacon

(1) Walk with your head up, chest out, eyes forward, arms naturally swinging, moderate pace, avoid pigeon-toed or waddling, or shuffling steps.

(2) Go up and down stairs, walk through corridors on the right side, enter and exit classrooms, offices, meeting rooms along designated routes, do not crowd, enter various functional rooms quietly and slowly, do not disturb others.

(3) Greet acquaintances when you meet them, exchange greetings, do not ignore them; if you need to chat, move to the roadside or a corner to talk, do not stand in the middle of the road or crowded places.

(4) Pedestrians should yield to each other, actively give way to elders, actively give way to the disabled and those in need.

(5) When asking strangers for directions, start with polite words like "excuse me, may I bother you?" or "may I ask?" Young people should address appropriately, such as "grandpa," "aunt," "uncle," etc., and then ask the way; after hearing the answer, be sure to say "thank you!" If asked by a stranger for directions, respond seriously and carefully, if unclear, say "I'm sorry, I don't know, please ask someone else."

2. Three-Word Song

Sitting, standing, walking, be proper. Stand like a pine, sit like a bell.

Body upright, good spirit. Chest up, remember well.

Going up and down stairs, stay on the right. If asking for directions, be polite.

Polite language, keep in mind. "Grandpa," "Aunt, hello."

Respect elders, care for young. Assist the disabled, show kindness.

Classmate Etiquette Section

After coming to school, the people you interact with the most every day are your classmates. We study together, play games together, and spend every happy and unforgettable day together. Time spent with classmates is joyful, but sometimes friction can occur. So, how do we prevent such situations? What should we do when interacting with classmates? If you can meet the following requirements, I believe you must have many friends.

1. Classmate Etiquette

Politeness is the golden key to human coexistence. —— Matsusukei

When friends come from afar, joy is boundless. —— Confucius

(1) Greet classmates with "Good morning" or "Hello" with a handshake or wave.

(2) Speak sincerely and humbly to classmates, use a gentle tone, listen attentively to classmates without interrupting them easily.

(3) Use polite language in interactions with classmates. When asking classmates questions, use polite expressions like "May I ask," "Sorry," "Excuse me," "Let me ask you a question," etc., and thank them afterward; if classmates cannot answer, say "It's okay, this question is quite difficult to answer, thanks for your time."

(4) Respect classmates, do not give them nicknames or call them by nicknames, do not say things that hurt or embarrass them.

(5) Help classmates in difficulty.

(6) Respect and take care of female classmates, do not bully female classmates.

(7) Ask for classmates' permission before borrowing their belongings, take special care of their items, and return them on time.

(8) Do not gossip or stir up trouble behind classmates' backs.

(9) Do not be petty, be generous and forgiving towards classmates' mistakes or offenses.

(10) Keep promises and try your best to fulfill what you promised to others.

2. Three-Word Song

Seeing classmates, say good morning. Mutual respect, remember well.

Listening to others, focus fully. Don't interrupt, or lose friendship.

Express gratitude, speak first. Among classmates, care for each other.

Understand well, help willingly. Know tolerance, unite tightly.

Respect others, others respect you. Keep promises, act decisively.

Learn respect, understand tolerance, collective honor, personal glory.
