Nevus of Ota is common among East Asian populations and is characterized by bluish-brown spots around the eyes. The male-to-female ratio is 1:3. About half of the patients are born with this condition, but some discover it during childhood, and a few patients don't show symptoms until puberty. It appears as patches made up of brown, gray, and blue spots, with unclear boundaries. The color of the spots within the lesion can be uniform or mixed with the aforementioned colors, and the depth of pigmentation varies.

Some melanocytes are also distributed in the conjunctiva, cornea, and retina. Some patients have a tendency for the lesion to slowly enlarge, while others stabilize during childhood. Most believe that the condition stabilizes after puberty, but there are individuals who continue to experience slow growth until around the age of 30. Nevus of Ota does not have a genetic predisposition and is not clearly associated with malignancy. (To learn more about the risks of nevus of Ota, click on the expert online consultation.)
