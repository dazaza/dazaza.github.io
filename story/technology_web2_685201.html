The momentum for the world economic recovery may slow down.

Analysis and Outlook of the World Economy in the First Half of 2010

In the first half of 2010, the world economy showed signs of recovery. In the first quarter, the world economy continued the rapid recovery trend seen in the fourth quarter of 2009. Most developed countries experienced significant restorative growth, while developing countries and regions saw overall rapid growth.

Current Situation:

Key Economic Indicators Decline in Major Economies

The GDP of the Eurozone, after revision, increased by 0.2% quarter-on-quarter and 0.6% year-on-year in the first quarter, still teetering on the edge between recession and growth. The U.S. GDP grew by 2.7% in the first quarter of this year after two downward revisions, achieving three consecutive quarters of growth, but the growth rate was less than half of the 5.6% in the fourth quarter of 2009. Uncertainty has increased in Japan's economy.

Future Outlook:

Declining Indicators Suggest Slower Recovery

As some key economic indicators in major economies have started to decline, it signals a potential weakening in the momentum of economic recovery. Some countries may even experience reversals.

Impact:

Potential Changes in China's Exports and Price Trends

If the recovery momentum of the major economies in the world slows down, the domestic demand growth of our main trading partners may slow down or even decrease, significantly impacting our commodity demand.

The composite leading index trend has slowed down. In the coming period, international market prices may continue to decline. Influenced by international factors, the upward price pressure in China in the second half of the year will ease to a certain extent.

At the sub-forum yesterday, the conference released the "Analysis and Outlook of the World Economy in the First Half of 2010", also known as the "World Situation Report". The report painted a comprehensive picture of the current global economic situation from four aspects: the recovery trend of the world economy in the first half of the year, the downward risks faced by the world economic recovery, the challenges facing macroeconomic policies and international economic governance structure reforms, and the impact of changes in the international economic environment on China's economy.

The world economy is struggling to recover amidst numerous potential crises and uncertainties. As an increasingly important member of the world economy, what opportunities and challenges does China face? Four guests - Chen Yuan, Executive Deputy Chairman of the China International Economic Exchange Center and Chairman of the National Development Bank, Huang Hai, member of the National Committee of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference, Gu Yuanyang, academician of the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences and former director of the Institute of World Economics and Politics, and Zhang Yongjun, researcher at the China International Economic Exchange Center - joined the host to provide insightful commentary on the report.

Report Summary:

1. The World Economy Shows Signs of Recovery in the First Half of the Year

Most developed country economies experienced significant restorative growth, and developing countries and regions generally saw rapid growth, though a few countries failed to achieve positive economic growth.

a) Strong Rebound in Economic Growth

In the first quarter, the world economy continued the rapid recovery trend seen in the fourth quarter of 2009. Most developed country economies experienced significant restorative growth; developing countries and regions generally saw rapid growth, with larger economies experiencing faster economic growth, though a few countries failed to achieve positive economic growth.

b) International Primary Product Prices Fluctuate, Prices in Major Economies Rise

Under the influence of economic stimulus measures, the downward price trends in the last quarter of 2008 and the first half of 2009 were positively impacted. In 2009, international primary product market prices generally showed a recovery trend, and in 2010, they exhibited characteristics of high volatility.

c) Employment Situation Remains Severe in Major Economies

Impacted by the sharp economic declines in the second half of 2008 and the first half of 2009, employment situations in all countries deteriorated significantly. Unemployment rates in the seven major Western industrialized nations rose noticeably compared to pre-crisis levels.

d) Rapid Recovery in International Trade and Foreign Direct Investment

1. International trade quickly recovered based on the substantial contraction in 2009. Initially, world trade volumes plummeted sharply. During the recovery process, the trade recovery speed of emerging economies was significantly faster than that of developed countries. After entering 2010, international trade amounts showed a relatively fast recovery trend. Affected by demand recovery, international trade price levels also recovered.

2. Outward direct investment recovery exceeded expectations. Since the beginning of this year, with the improvement of the global economic situation and the reduction of negative impacts from the financial crisis, the scale of global outward direct investment rebounded significantly compared to the same period last year. According to statistics from the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development, cross-border mergers and acquisitions and equity investments in the first five months of 2010 increased by 36% compared to the same period in 2009.

2. Downside Risks Facing the World Economic Recovery

While some economic indicators in major economies have declined, some leading indicators have also started to fall, signaling a possible weakening in future economic recovery momentum, and some countries might even experience reversals.

a) Various Indicators Show Declines in Major Economies

European economies face "double-dip" risks. In the first quarter, the revised GDP of the Eurozone grew by 0.2% quarter-on-quarter and 0.6% year-on-year, remaining on the brink between recession and growth. The U.S. economic recovery has slowed. This year, after two downward revisions, the U.S. GDP grew by 2.7% in the first quarter, achieving three consecutive quarters of growth, but the growth rate was less than half of the 5.6% in the fourth quarter of 2009. Uncertainty factors in Japan's economy have increased.

b) Leading Indicators in Major Economies Signal Possible Slowdown in Economic Recovery

As some economic indicators in major economies have started to decline, some leading indicators have also begun to drop, signaling a potential weakening in future economic recovery momentum, and some countries might even experience reversals. Due to the rapid export recovery in many emerging markets earlier, their exports will inevitably be affected during the subsequent period, which will somewhat negatively impact their economic growth momentum.

c) Implementation of Active Macroeconomic Policies Faces Constraints

1. Constraints on Implementing Active Fiscal Policies

Many countries face severe fiscal deficits and pressures to improve fiscal balance. EU leaders have committed to drastically reducing fiscal deficits, and the U.S. and Japan also face issues such as large fiscal deficit scales and high proportions relative to GDP, constraining active fiscal policies.

2. Constraints on Implementing Loose Monetary Policies

The balance sheets of the U.S. Federal Reserve and European Central Bank have rapidly expanded. Moreover, the base money injected previously did not flow into non-financial institutions, failing to stimulate demand oriented toward the real economy sector. Adopting measures to inject more money into the market would be difficult for central bank decision-makers to reach consensus on, nor would it likely gain approval from national or regional parliaments or governments.

3. Challenges Faced by Macroeconomic Policies and International Economic Governance Structure Reform

Under the current circumstances, must these rules of macroeconomic policies be followed? Can the boundary values of key indicators be broken? These are questions that need to be addressed.

a) How to Maintain Continuity and Stability of Macroeconomic Policies

Currently, most countries and regions' economic recovery remains highly dependent on policy support. However, the implementation of active fiscal policies in major economies faces constraints such as larger fiscal deficits, excessive debt burdens, and poor sustainability of fiscal policies. How to maintain continuity and stability in macroeconomic policies poses a test for the economic decision-making departments of major countries and presents a challenge in judging the macroeconomic situation itself.

b) How to Strengthen Coordination of Macroeconomic Policies

The difficulty in coordinating policies among countries has increased. Moreover, due to reduced cooperation spirit compared to when the crisis initially hit, coordination among policies is insufficient, failing to form effective synergy. To achieve the purpose of expanding exports and reducing imports, some countries have adopted protectionist trade practices, which if spread globally, would undoubtedly intensify international trade frictions and reduce the scale of international trade. From each country's domestic situation, the difficulty in coordinating fiscal and monetary policies has also significantly increased.

c) How to Enhance Effectiveness of Macroeconomic Policies

Continuing to implement active fiscal policies faces significant obstacles, and the previous loose monetary policies have not clearly promoted real economic growth. Therefore, how to fully leverage the effects of monetary policy becomes an issue that needs serious study in the next step.

4. Impact of Changes in the International Economic Environment on China's Economy

Affected by international factors, China's exports may experience a significant decline, while the upward price pressure may ease.

a) Impact on China's Export Trends

The most significant factor affecting China's exports is the change in external demand. If the recovery momentum of the major economies in the world slows down or even reverses, the domestic demand growth of China's main trading partners may slow down or even decrease, significantly impacting the demand for China's goods. Additionally, the recent slight appreciation trend of the RMB, coupled with the previous significant depreciation of the euro, considering the time lag from changes in external demand and exchange rates to affecting China's exports, along with the increase in the base effect due to the gradual recovery of exports in the fourth quarter of 2009, these factors may overlap, potentially causing a noticeable slowdown in China's export growth at the end of this year and the beginning of next year.

Recently, China's export growth rate has indeed been slowing down, and the import growth rate has dropped relatively fast.

b) Impact on China's Price Trends

The influence of international market prices on China's price trends is becoming increasingly evident. Given the slowing trend of the current composite leading index, international market prices may continue to decline in the coming period. Under these circumstances, influenced by international factors, the upward price pressure in China in the second half of the year will ease to a certain extent.

Overall, by the end of this year and the beginning of next year, affected by international factors, China's exports may experience a significant decline, which will have a relatively noticeable impact on China's future economic growth. Meanwhile, the upward price pressure in China will ease. These situations should be considered as important influencing factors when formulating domestic macroeconomic policies. By Wang Dan
