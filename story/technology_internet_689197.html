A comedy film not suitable for elementary school students to watch

by hoouk260 on 2012-03-03 20:09:39

A Movie Not Suitable for Elementary School Students - Reflections on the Film *Confucius* by Chen Miao

The school organized a free outdoor screening of the movie *Confucius*, with parents required to accompany their children. Since I had some unfinished work that night, I asked my wife to go with our child instead. After watching the movie, the teacher assigned homework: to write reflections on the film. As a result, the division of labor among us parents was tacitly understood: those who accompanied didn’t have to write, while those who didn’t were responsible for writing the essay.

In order to complete the teacher's assignment, I watched the movie intermittently online over several days. Ultimately, my conclusion is straightforward: this is not a film suitable for children. The reasons are as follows:

Firstly, the dialogue in the movie contains a large amount of classical Chinese phrases from works like *The Analects* and other Confucian classics. These phrases mix classical and modern language, and the use of literary allusions is rampant... For children, this might as well be like listening to heavenly scriptures, and even for parents who don't particularly enjoy reading classical Chinese texts, it’s already quite a headache. If they can't understand the words, how are students supposed to watch and comprehend the movie? From this perspective, it's very clear that the filmmakers never intended this movie for children; rather, it's aimed at adults, specifically those who are enthusiastic about traditional Chinese learning. Therefore, arranging for elementary school students to watch it is akin to "biting off more than they can chew"—the spirit is commendable, but the effect is questionable.

Secondly, the director's mindset in shooting this blockbuster is clearly contradictory, and the direction of effort is chaotic. This directly results in the film lacking a good perspective and narrative style, making it difficult to move the audience, including children. Blockbusters mean big productions, which can either be painstaking artistic films or commercial films filled with highlights. *Confucius* seems to be caught between these two extremes, ultimately leaning towards a commercial blockbuster. Consequently, hiring popular and high-cost actors such as Hong Kong star Chow Yun-fat and renowned actress Zhou Xun to boost box office sales becomes logical within this framework. Based on the commercial film mindset, the movie heavily emphasizes scenes of military confrontation and warfare, aiming to create an audio-visual spectacle typical of blockbusters. However, these heavy segments have little relevance to portraying Confucius' character and could even be considered superfluous. Confucius represents "civilization," not "military prowess." Using "military" elements to shape his image, even if historically plausible, strays far from the essence of Confucius—this approach doesn't fit the theme. "Military" should at most serve as a historical backdrop; focusing on inner logic and emotional truth would be more appropriate. If the director had been more focused and decisive, characters like Nanzi (played by Zhou Xun), portrayed as seductive, would not have been necessary. War and beauty are common elements in many vulgar commercial films, attempting to attach themselves to *Confucius* merely for hype, reflecting the director's lack of confidence. Ultimately, the multiple objectives of the film led to its deviation from the original intent—to depict a Confucius who is both respectable and moving.

Thirdly, the portrayal of Confucius lacks credible plot design. Given the commercial demands, this is somewhat understandable. Prioritizing surface-level excitement over coherence has already been mentioned. Many parts of the storyline feel awkward, such as the death of Confucius' disciple Yan Hui. Historical records indicate he died of starvation, but the movie depicts him falling through ice while rescuing bamboo slips and books. This forced plot twist reminds one of the "tall, grand, and complete" era of revolutionary heroism imagery. In short, many of the plots surrounding Confucius give viewers the impression of being superficial and implausible. This reveals the director's average overall quality and insufficient directing skills. Based on my personal experience, well-made, profound Chinese films often originate from adaptations of excellent novels. Movies scripted directly by writers without solid sources tend to be unbearable to watch. So, if even adults find it unbelievable, why bother showing it to children?

Han Han's extreme evaluation of *Confucius* resonates with me, though I don't fully agree with it. He said: “This movie *Confucius*, whether viewed from the angles of filming significance, commercial profit, artistic pursuit, cinematic exploration, educational enlightenment, warning and touching audiences, audio-visual impact, entertainment, or historical documentation, has no reason to exist—it is a movie that could have been completely erased.” Though harsh, his critique hits the nail on the head regarding the film's hybrid nature.

However, from an educational standpoint, encouraging children to engage more with traditional culture at a young age is a commendable direction. Unfortunately, many things we do have fewer errors in the general direction but become incomprehensible in execution, which is indeed worth further reflection after viewing. Whether our education system was swayed by the commercial tactics of the film company is a secondary matter.

Note: The child's school organized a free screening of the movie *Confucius*, and the teacher required both the child and the parent to submit reflections. This is the parent's submission, while the child struggled to write anything.
