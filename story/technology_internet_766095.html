Lenovo Reportedly Begins Layoffs in September: 10% to 15% of Workforce Affected

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 15:20:21

Beijing, September 3 - According to reports by the Economic Voice program "The World of Companies," some employees at Lenovo have disclosed that the company plans to initiate layoffs in September, with a reduction reaching 10%-15% of its workforce.

Employee: There are rumors to this effect, starting this month. However, I haven't heard of anyone around me being laid off yet. It's probably a decision made by the human resources department; the ratio has been decided, but who will be laid off will likely depend on each boss’s arrangements.

Lenovo employees believe the atmosphere within the company is still good. From a business perspective, no one is idle enough to be laid off—everyone is very busy. Those whose workloads might not be fully saturated may be more concerned. Moreover, Lenovo’s salaries in the industry are not particularly high, so many people maintain an optimistic attitude towards layoffs. If they get laid off, it's okay—they can take a lump sum and travel for a while, which would be a relaxation for individuals. So, some employees don't care about being laid off.

Reporter: What can everyone do now to keep their jobs? Besides making themselves appear busier, should they try to improve relations with their bosses?

Lenovo Employee: Actually, there hasn’t been much of that. But colleagues around me are taking relevant qualification exams, which could add value to their current positions and benefit long-term development. In our company, when layoffs occur, generally lower-level employees are targeted because the compensation costs are lower, requiring less payment from the company. High-level executives won’t be cut. For lower-level employees, layoffs are usually based on business needs, and whether you have a good relationship with your boss doesn’t matter much.

Many people might find it hard to understand why Lenovo, which has already reached the top of the PC market as the global leader, and where Yang Yuanqing recently gave out $3 million of his own money as bonuses, is now laying people off. This brings up a point: layoffs don’t necessarily mean the company can’t pay wages due to revenue shortfalls. It could be contraction in certain areas and expansion in others.

Currently, although Lenovo’s PC business is doing well, the global market is shifting towards mobile terminals. No matter how strong PCs are, they may lose profitability to mobile devices in the future. Compared to HP or other PC manufacturers, Lenovo may have lower profit requirements, so it might further compress costs in the future, including labor costs.

Which types of employees are likely to be laid off first during such times? Employees who complain often, have low execution ability, always make excuses, have limited resources, and are easily replaceable often become the first choice for companies to lay off.

For layoffs, many people's attitudes have changed compared to the past, when hearing "being laid off" was like a catastrophic event. Some people adjust their mindset and move forward again, while others harbor resentment.

Employee One: People who get laid off feel uncomfortable, and sudden decisions hurt the leadership, management, and regular employees alike.

Employee Two: Survival of the fittest, it doesn’t matter. As long as there is compensation, it's fine. Everyone follows the law anyway—if you fire me, there will definitely be compensation.

Employee Three: In an economic society, if you’re laid off, just find another job—it’s quite normal.

Different companies use different methods for layoffs. Some large firms simply send a letter to dismiss employees without any preparation. Other companies might consider individual employee situations, especially for those with significant backgrounds, holding discussions and agreeing on compensations beforehand.

In recent years, news of layoffs has been constant globally, with substantial numbers involved. Yesterday, Sharp announced a restructuring plan, planning to lay off 10,966 employees globally—a terrifying number.

No matter what, for those facing layoffs, recognizing the situation, preparing for a soft landing, repositioning themselves, and reviving for life and survival is essential. At the same time, companies should respect their employees and provide them with a process to prepare for layoffs.
