More than 10 roosters stand guard to protect the flock from weasel attacks (Picture)

by jyq2011668 on 2011-06-28 16:56:18

In a flock of chickens, the rooster vigilantly looks around - photo by correspondent Lü Bin.

In the British animated film "Chicken Run," under the leadership of the rooster Rocky and the hen Ginger, the flock successfully escapes from Mrs. Tweedy's chicken farm, avoiding a catastrophic disaster. Now, in a similar scene at a farm in the Yiwu mountains, under the alternating protection of more than a dozen roosters, the flock has successfully avoided attacks from mountain beasts.

Yesterday, the reporter visited this breeding base located in Shanpen Village, Chian Town, Yiwu. Numerous roosters and hens were seen freely moving in the forest, with a fence circling the foot of the mountain.

When the reporter tried to approach for inspection, a rooster immediately raised its comb and charged over.

Under the guidance of the breeder, the reporter saw four roosters standing on tree branches and high rocks, constantly scrutinizing their surroundings cautiously. Additionally, outside the flock, there were more than ten roosters patrolling back and forth. The flock was peacefully pecking food under their "protection."

Mountain beasts frequently visited the farm

The reporter learned that this farm belongs to the Yiwu Sanyitan Traditional Chinese Medicine Hall, covering more than 200 acres. Originally, it was just a villa with some fruit trees planted inside. Every day, the pharmacy would dump medicinal residues in the mountains as fertilizer, and these roosters took the residue as feed.

The pharmacy raised over 20,000 roosters and hens, which were usually scattered in the forest.

However, soon enough, troubles arose. Although the foot of the mountain was surrounded by fences, wild animals like wild boars, mountain eagles, weasels, and wildcats often visited the farm, injuring or killing many chickens. Despite all efforts by the breeders, they couldn't prevent these attacks.

Roosters began patrolling

Half a month ago, the farm staff suddenly noticed that the frequency of attacks on the flock had decreased. After careful observation, they found that the flock had enhanced its "self-protection."

"Every day, more than ten roosters patrol around, protecting the safety of the flock," said Zhu Fujin, the head of the farm. These roosters perched at high places would immediately fly down upon spotting danger, and the flock would quickly run into the chicken coop.

Under such division of labor, intruders met no good end. A few days ago, a weasel sneaked into the farm and tragedy struck before it could act, as it was immediately spotted by three roosters. Pursued by the roosters, the weasel fell into a ditch. By the time the breeders arrived at the scene, the weasel had already been pecked to death.

"During the snowfall a few days ago, the roosters' bodies were covered with icicles, yet they remained vigilant and walked around carefully," said Zhu Fujin.

Livestock expert: It's an instinctive habit

Regarding this strange phenomenon, Zhang Miaoxian, deputy director of Jinhua City's Animal Husbandry Bureau, explained that it is actually a normal instinctive behavior in animals.

Zhang Miaoxian said that animals have a self-protection habit, but it manifests differently depending on the environment. If an animal group frequently encounters attacks, then this self-protection instinct will be prominently displayed.

"The strong will automatically stand up to protect the weak. This situation occurred at the Chian farm because it was frequently attacked by mountain beasts, prompting the strong roosters to step forward to protect the flock," (Intern Reporter Dong Qi).
