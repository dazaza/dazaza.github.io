Balmer: How Microsoft will move on without Gates

by cfan64165422 on 2008-06-24 22:21:41

CNET Technology Information Network International Report on June 22: Bill Gates will end his full-time career at Microsoft by the end of this month. Recently, Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer accepted a media interview and talked about life in the post-Gates era, Microsoft's efforts in Internet search business, and his long-term plans for Microsoft.

Q: You are finally going to get rid of that "guy." Now, we want to know what you are going to do?

Ballmer: Move forward and keep climbing.

Q: What does it mean for Bill Gates to end his full-time role?

Ballmer: In a way, you lose someone like Bill Gates, but Microsoft's culture is built on many things. It's not just based on leadership; it's also based on our experiences, successes, and failures. From this perspective, it's a cumulative of success or failure direction and experience.

I think what Gates leaves behind will continue to exist, namely the values of Bill - wisdom and focus will last for a long time. I don't think his departure will affect Microsoft's culture. I saw the same thing at Walmart. Sam Walton (the founder of Walmart) has been away from the company for quite a while, yet the company's culture continues to exist. So, I think there won't be a big issue.

Q: How do you personally deal with his departure?

Ballmer: In college, we all studied Max Weber's essay on 'charismatic institutions,' which discusses how the government should transition when an extraordinary leader leaves. I've thought a little about this issue and revisited the book, dusted off the cover, and reread it.

The key is to move forward and designate future leaders. This is an interesting transition. How should I put it? Bill founded this company, but for Microsoft people, in a sense, this company is mine as well as Gates'. Therefore, I can't be called the second-generation leader. I consider myself a first-generation leader. I am not Gates, but I have some of the brilliance of a first-generation leader.

Q: Microsoft's stock price hasn't had much performance for many years. Why is that?

Ballmer: I think our stock performance aligns with most large market-cap stocks. Additionally, all companies at a certain stage of development will see high stock prices, then you enter a hard work phase where you need to strive for the company's growth. This is what the market expects from you; the market will raise the stock price before profit growth. If your performance meets market expectations, the stock price will stabilize for a period until you can give the market another pleasant surprise.
