The Expo has been open for so many days, and I have experienced the times with more or fewer people. Except for the scariest day when 620,000 people attended, which I did not experience, I still feel quite fortunate for myself. During the breaks between visiting the pavilions, I made every effort to search for all kinds of delicious food scattered throughout the park. Here, as long as you don't mind your wallet getting lighter, being a little gourmet is truly a happy thing.

On the second floor of the Turkish Pavilion's restaurant, there are traditional Turkish western-style kebabs, fine wines, and coffee available. What I like the most is the Turkish hard ice cream at the exit. Unlike regular ice cream, Turkish ice cream is really chewy. The taste isn't particularly special, but it's fun to eat. I can't describe how it gets its chewiness, but once you try it, you'll know it's not exaggerated. There's also the Turkish kebab sliced from a huge meat column. It looks pretty good; I didn't try it, but there were quite a few people queuing up.

At the Indonesian restaurant, you can taste traditional Indonesian dishes like satay, and on the top floor of the pavilion, you can enjoy Indonesia's best coffee. Unfortunately, I'm not into coffee. In the Belgian Pavilion, if you're lucky, you can taste authentic Belgian chocolate for free. Perhaps that's why the queue area in the Belgian Pavilion is always packed with people, many of whom are here just for this piece of chocolate. There are also delicious fries, which are very different from those in fast-food restaurants. They are short and plump, crispy on the outside, and served with a sweet and fragrant mayonnaise that will never disappoint. Here, chocolate desserts are almost must-orders for every table. Vanilla ice cream is served with meringue cookies, sprinkled with chocolate flakes, and finally drizzled with warm chocolate sauce, creating a hot and cold sensation that feels smooth and comforting. This place is simply prepared for food lovers, featuring Belgium's traditional breakfast waffles, which make great snacks. Biting into them fills your mouth with a sweet and salty aroma.

The Thai Pavilion's restaurant tastes great, just as authentic as in Thailand itself. It offers classic dishes like curry chicken, fried bananas rich in coconut milk flavor, a variety of seafood, and sour-spicy soup. The fruit pudding in the Norwegian Pavilion is another dessert that made me drool for a long time. Seeing those colorful and adorable little treats makes it almost unbearable to take a bite. Salmon mixed with caviar seems to be popular too, but I just can't stand the taste of raw food, so I missed out on that treat.

The French restaurant on the top floor of the French Pavilion is both exquisite and romantic. Strolling through the rooftop garden, you can take in the beautiful scenery of the Huangpu River. Tasting one of the world's three greatest delicacies, French cuisine, here is truly a luxurious enjoyment. The delicious foie gras, oysters, and snails make your fingers itch to grab a bite.

The takoyaki (octopus balls) outside the Japanese Business Pavilion is a food no one should miss. These ping-pong ball-sized spheres have a golden yellow and somewhat crispy exterior, tender enough to break with a touch. Inside are octopus tentacles and shrimp, and perhaps some other seafood I couldn't identify. Outside, they're topped with salad sauce, a bit of fragrant seaweed, and bonito flakes, making my mouth water every time I think about them.
