Ultra-low prices harbor hidden traps.

by chengshangdao on 2007-06-07 10:03:42

Mr. Huang from Wuhan is in the computer assembly business. In November last year, he saw many computer accessories being sold at low prices on a website. On the surface, the company appeared legitimate, with an address and even a security mark from the Ministry of Public Security. Unable to resist the temptation, he contacted them using the information provided.

The other party was very confident, claiming their company was located at No. ××, Zhongying Street, Shatoujiao, Shenzhen, Guangdong Province, and asked Mr. Huang if he had a special border defense certificate. Upon hearing this, Mr. Huang honestly replied that he did not have one, and the other party said they could not come over then. Seeing such a rare business opportunity slipping away, Mr. Huang asked if they could send the goods instead. The other party agreed but demanded a deposit first.

After some haggling, at 1:30 PM on November 12th last year, Mr. Huang transferred 2000 yuan as a deposit for five computers worth a total of 12,000 yuan. At 4:30 PM that afternoon, the other party called Mr. Huang saying the goods had been shipped and would arrive in two days.

However, after waiting for two days, the other party called again saying the goods had arrived in Wuhan, but Mr. Huang needed to transfer the remaining payment before he could pick up the goods. Mr. Huang was confused and questioned them, reminding them that it was agreed upon to pay only after seeing the delivery note. However, the other party claimed that these goods were smuggled and carried significant risks. If he did not make the payment, they would return the goods to the factory. This made Mr. Huang anxious, and he began to wonder if the person was a scammer. But since he had never heard of such scams before, he thought if they were scammers, they would have fled with the money without playing games. Thus, he transferred another 10,000 yuan according to their demands.

When Mr. Huang went to pick up the goods after transferring the money, there were no goods at the location mentioned by the other party. When he called, they did not answer. Eventually, when they did answer, they claimed the goods were seized by customs and Mr. Huang needed to pay additional taxes before picking up the goods. Realizing he had been completely scammed, Mr. Huang deeply regretted his actions. Later, through reporting the incident, he learned that there was no such door number at Zhongying Street, Shatoujiao, Shenzhen.

Mr. Wang from Guangxi also experienced a similar situation. According to Mr. Wang, a few days ago he saw a website selling mobile phones and computer products online. Impulsively drawn by the cheap prices, he transferred money to buy a phone. However, he has yet to receive it. When he called to inquire, the other party said it had been sent out but was detained by customs, requiring him to pay additional taxes to retrieve it, demanding another transfer.

Due to suspicion towards the other party, Mr. Wang did not transfer any more money. He later checked the address left on the website, which was No. ××, Huancheng East Road, Yantian District (Shatoujiao), Shenzhen, but through acquaintances in Shenzhen, he learned that the place did not exist.

Upon checking the websites complained about above, it was found that they were all simple websites with novel products at very low prices. The company introductions were largely the same, all claiming to be Hong Kong companies specializing in electronic products, with addresses all located at Zhongying Street, Shatoujiao, but only leaving a mobile phone number from another region. The actual investigation results showed that there was no such door number in Shatoujiao, making it evident that the provided address was fake, the company was fake, and only the mobile phone number was real.

Online shopping requires caution.

Netizen Mr. Liang said that although consumers are defrauded in various ways, generally speaking, there are two similarities: one, those defrauded are all individuals; two, the motive for shopping is always attracted by inexpensive goods. For this reason, he reminded consumers to overcome the mentality of seeking small benefits, to be cautious about online shopping, and maintain a healthy consumption attitude. If convenience is desired, purchases should be made on reputable large websites.

Zhou Liuqiang from the Shenzhen Consumers' Committee told the reporter that they often received complaints related to online shopping fraud, most of which were about digital cameras and laptops. Regarding these complaints, since it is often difficult to find the subject of the complaint, solutions are hard to come by.

He said that online shopping, as an advanced form of shopping, carries significant risks due to the lack of a standardized protection system currently. He reminded consumers that when engaging in online shopping, they must maintain a healthy consumption attitude, meaning they must remain calm and avoid harboring侥幸psychology. Secondly, during transactions, they must have rights protection awareness and risk awareness, considering whether sending money guarantees receiving goods, as well as what to do if goods are not received.
