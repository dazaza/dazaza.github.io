Let me tell you a real内幕of the automobile industry

by ap03041 on 2008-07-31 16:00:44

The Corolla is only worth 50,000 RMB; calculated at a 30% profit margin.

First, let me clarify something: I've worked in the automotive industry for several years, so I have a firm belief that I won't buy a car within the next three years for two reasons:

1. First, I don't have the money. I'm part of the working class, specifically the working class in the automotive industry. Generally speaking, the working class in China's automotive industry can't afford cars. This is the main reason. If I had a few million, I wouldn't care about the dark and excessive profits in China's auto industry because I would have the money anyway.

2. The second reason is that I work inside the automotive industry, and the exorbitant profits within make me feel the darkness. At this kind of profit margin, even if an ordinary worker earned ten thousand or eight thousand RMB per month, it would only be a drop in the bucket.

Here, I will simply compare the huge cost differences between imported and domestically produced vehicles of the same model:

1. In imported cars, a very important part of each vehicle's cost is the R&D cost. Developing an excellent model requires a large team to spend several years researching and designing it. For example, Mercedes-Benz and BMW take five to six years for a new generation of models. When one generation is released, the next is already under development, and these investments often amount to hundreds of millions of dollars or more. This may be clearer to everyone. Dividing this cost among each vehicle isn't a small number, as modern car models' lifespans and production volumes can no longer create miracles like the old Beetle. On the other hand, domestic manufacturers can almost ignore this cost. All they need to pay is the transfer fee for existing blueprints and technology, which applies to relatively advanced models. For some models that have been almost phased out, these costs are very low. If manufacturers claim that these costs are high, there is likely unfair secrecy involved. Some might say that domestic manufacturers also have R&D costs, such as a manufacturer spending 300 million RMB to improve an old model with 21 upgrades. What do you think? Anyway, my feeling is just two words... laughable. From the perspective of R&D alone, the domestic production cost of each car is tens to hundreds of dollars lower.

2. Regarding hardware investments such as production equipment and factory facilities, domestic manufacturers basically patch up the original factory and then start production. Once they earn enough money to start burping, they will spit out a little to build a new factory or something. As for the production line, even for models synchronized with international standards, there is a world of difference between domestic and foreign production lines. These differences can be seen from the craftsmanship and calibration accuracy of the cars themselves. Domestic manufacturers generally use obsolete production lines, and only import key processing and craft equipment (which also includes junk). Moreover, foreign production lines are mandatorily scrapped after a certain number of years, whereas second-tier domestic manufacturers' production lines can run as long as they function, and using them for twenty to thirty years is not unreasonable. The automobile engine production plant where I work is also a well-known joint venture, and equipment is almost the most headache-inducing issue: foreign junk becomes exciting imported lines, and many good imported devices have activation rates lower than domestic equipment due to usage, maintenance, and spare parts problems. Parts that can barely run mostly have components made in China. From the equipment cost perspective, the cost of domestically produced cars is several dollars to dozens of dollars lower per vehicle.

3. Batch costs, this point could be the reason why domestic cars have higher costs. It is undeniable that mainstream domestic manufacturers have low production capacities. This factor alone could increase the overall cost of the vehicle by 10-30%. Especially for foreign manufacturers, once large R&D and equipment investments are made and production volumes do not increase, they may end up losing money. A significant reason why low-volume cars like Ferraris have no price-to-performance ratio is their low production volume, which increases costs invisibly. Sometimes domestic manufacturers complain about this, but as mentioned earlier, as long as their production reaches a certain percentage of the production line design value, they don't have the concept of batch costs, and claiming losses is just nonsense.

4. Component costs, this point has too much water content. Having worked in this industry for several years, I can only describe it as dark. When a sedan is first put into production, it's basically assembly, with 80-90% of the parts being imported. Imported components are indeed expensive due to tariffs and freight fees, but once localized production begins, the costs plummet dramatically. Let me give a few examples I know: an imported solenoid valve can cost close to 500 RMB, with the lowest quality ones costing 200-300 RMB. After localization, they cost less than 100 RMB. And some seemingly small parts like bolts, washers, and oil seals may differ even more. Domestic parts are generally 20-60% of the price of imported parts. Here, the term "domestic parts" includes those completely produced by domestic manufacturers and those produced by joint ventures. I once calculated the cost of our company's engine (the following prices are adjusted proportionally). If I bought all the parts separately (market prices for those available on the market, company purchase prices for those unavailable), it would cost around 20,000 RMB. Then hiring senior technicians to assemble it and test it would cost another 2,000 RMB. However, the market price for this engine is 60,000 RMB. In fact, many parts I bought at market prices could be cheaper if produced in-house or purchased in bulk by the manufacturer. Therefore, once items like motors, CDs, seats, and steel used in domestic cars are localized, the costs drop drastically. The cost of the Sail is openly known to be between 35,000-40,000 RMB. A friend of mine works at Fengshen Lanbird, and I obtained a few figures: the top-of-the-line Lanbird comes off the production line for less than 100,000 RMB. The initial quote for the front bumper at the service station was 1,800 RMB, but the manufacturer's purchase price was only 200-300 RMB.

5. Finally, there is one more cost: employee wage costs. Some people think it's negligible, but look at why foreign manufacturers keep laying off employees and why German domestic cars have persistently high costs - a big part of the reason is wage and benefit costs. The hourly wage plus benefits for a regular employee in China is 1-5 USD, while in the U.S., due to developed welfare systems, the hourly wage and benefits for each employee is 20-50 USD. This is another significant cost.

6. Development costs: International large automotive groups must continuously develop and innovate, expand, etc. I haven't studied this cost, but developing from a small workshop over several decades into a group producing millions of cars annually involves significant development costs. I believe events like Formula 1 fall under this category. No team makes money from this; it's mainly for development and brand enhancement. As for domestic manufacturers, the establishment of major automobile factories relied on national policies and the efforts of the older generation of Chinese automakers. Now, they still rely on national policy support, growing in a greenhouse environment. They only focus on selling cars and counting money; development is none of their concern. It's truly lamentable.

In summary, internationally, after removing all the aforementioned costs, the profit on each family car is only a few hundred to a thousand USD, or even just dozens of USD. High-end cars may yield a few thousand USD in profit, with a profit margin of around 5-10%. There's no need to elaborate on domestically produced cars.

Nowadays, many manufacturers tout synchronization with international standards, and many people get excited about it. Have you considered whether models like certain Volkswagen models have synchronized costs along with prices? Has the quality synchronized? The comprehensive cost of domestically produced cars with the same configuration is only 60-70% (this is an average and still conservative) of the cost of imported cars. As for quality, everyone knows the situation.

Finally, let me explain with numbers in a simple way:

A domestically produced car priced at 250,000 RMB has an international market price of about 140,000 RMB for the same model (this assumption basically fits the facts). Due to tariffs, etc., it would sell for around 300,000 RMB domestically, which is higher than the domestic car (this is what domestic manufacturers often promote as a selling point, but it's all deception aimed at Chinese consumers). Let's look at the costs: the cost of the imported car is 120,000-130,000 RMB, while the cost of the domestically produced car is 80,000-100,000 RMB. Thus, a better imported car can only make 20,000 RMB profit internationally but can make over 100,000 RMB profit in China. Who's secretly happy? The surface price may seem lower, but the hidden excessive profits... sigh.

Logically, this approach by Chinese manufacturers would make it impossible to sell cars internationally. However, with China's large population, if 50% of America's 200 million people buy cars, then 8% of China's 1.2 billion people buying cars would result in the same number of sales. Add the price gouging, and actually only 1-2% of China's population buying cars would be enough for manufacturers to make money! Furthermore, China has too many official cars, too many rich people, and too many vain individuals, so manufacturers have plenty of buyers. They don't see the crisis and won't lower prices, sitting on piles of cash and refusing to follow market rules.

Let me mention the car-buying ability of Chinese people: the income of China's ordinary working class is only 5-10% of that in developed international countries, yet car prices are nearly twice the international market price. Buying a car is tough for us, not to mention the skyrocketing fuel prices.

So, I won't scold, I'll just observe until I consider it reasonable (actually, the standard of "reasonable" in everyone's hearts is already quite low, but manufacturers show no sign of conscience).
