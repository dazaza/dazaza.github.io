Why Tencent and Chery are fighting for the QQ trademark: neither can afford to lose

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:45:22

Does Tencent produce cars? The answer is no, but as early as 2005, the company applied for registration of the "QQ" trademark on goods such as cars in Class 12, which was approved in 2008. However, this did not sit well with Chery Automobile Co., Ltd., a car manufacturer. In 2009, Chery filed a request to cancel Tencent's car-related trademarks on the grounds of "identical or similar trademarks on similar goods," and received support. With its trademark revoked, Tencent sued the Trademark Review and Adjudication Board (TRAB) of the State Administration for Industry and Commerce. Yesterday, the Beijing No. 1 Intermediate People's Court heard the case, where plaintiff Tencent, defendant TRAB, and third party Chery faced each other in court.

As is well known, in China, two companies are associated with "QQ": one is Tencent Technology, and the other is Chery Automobile. They belong to different fields. Tencent has carved out a place in the internet sector, while Chery QQ cars have sold over a million units cumulatively. From the timeline of using "QQ," Tencent launched its instant messaging software QQ in 1999, whereas Chery introduced its microcar Chery QQ in 2003. The question of who should own "QQ" has been a point of contention between these two companies for nearly 10 years.

On May 19, 2005, Tencent applied to register the "QQ" trademark on goods such as cars in Class 12, gaining approval on March 7, 2008. On November 26, 2009, Chery argued that this trademark violated provisions of the Trademark Law regarding copying and imitating famous trademarks, identical or similar trademarks on similar goods, and infringing on prior rights of others, and thus petitioned the TRAB to cancel the disputed trademark. After review, the TRAB concluded that the registration of the disputed trademark constituted the preemptive registration of another party’s already used and influential trademark as stipulated in Article 31 of the Trademark Law, and therefore canceled Tencent's registered trademark.

Tencent argued that the contested decision by the TRAB contained obvious errors and omissions, claiming that the disputed trademark did not violate legal provisions and should be maintained. Their representative explained that on August 31, 2001, Tencent had applied to register the "QQ" trademark on services under Class 38, including "transmission of information, computer terminal communication." Through extensive promotion, the number of registered users of QQ communication services grew from 5 million in 2000 to over 200 million in 2003. Therefore, before Chery Automobile used the "QQ" trademark, Tencent's "QQ" trademark had already become a well-known trademark. By 2009, Tencent had obtained a certificate of qualification for a well-known trademark from relevant national departments, meaning any other company's use of the "QQ" trademark would constitute an infringement.

Chery Automobile, however, believed that Tencent's registration of the "QQ" trademark in the automotive field was malicious. As the third party in this case, Chery's representative pointed out that the trademark registered by Tencent in 2001 was not the "QQ" letters but a graphic. Meanwhile, Chery Automobile registered the "QQ" letter trademark in the automotive field in 2003. Therefore, the two enterprises were using their respective trademarks in different fields, with Chery's "QQ" trademark in the automotive field preceding Tencent's. Furthermore, Chery QQ's sales volume had reached over a million units, with advertising costs exceeding 200 million yuan, warranting protection.

Additionally, Tencent argued that its registration of the "QQ" trademark across multiple domains was protective registration for "QQ." However, Chery Automobile claimed that from the initial planning of the car name to later promotional campaigns, everything was based on the already well-known "QQ" trademark and penguin mascot to achieve publicity effects, constituting an infringement that cannot generate legitimate prior rights.

The verdict will be announced at a later date. For both Tencent and Chery, having operated the "QQ" trademark for nearly 10 years, this lawsuit is one neither can afford to lose.

Is there a third way in the QQ dispute?

Tencent QQ is the first domestic instant messaging software launched by Tencent in 1999, and Chery QQ is a microcar introduced by Chery in July 2003. Both "QQ"s enjoy high recognition in their respective fields.

Research revealed that as early as when the "QQ" car was introduced in 2003, Tencent had raised trademark infringement issues with Chery. Tencent stated it was busy preparing for its IPO at the time and thus did not publicly sue. Later, Tencent sent a lawyer's letter to Chery, but Chery never made a substantive response to Tencent.

In late 2005, news of the legal battle over the QQ trademark between the two parties surfaced again in the media, though no further developments were seen in this lawsuit, with some media even labeling it as hype.

This time, the trademark dispute was brought to court with Tencent as the plaintiff, TRAB as the defendant, and Chery as the third party. A trademark is the signboard for a company to stand in the market. For both Tencent and Chery, who have cultivated their brands for about ten years, neither can afford to lose. Some believe that whoever is deemed infringing will face substantial compensation. Is it true that in this trademark dispute, there is only victory or defeat, with no third way? Intellectual property expert Tang Guangliang from the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences told reporters that Tencent and Chery could completely join forces to achieve a win-win situation.

Tang Guangliang said that the function of a trademark is to attract consumers' attention, serving as a link between merchants and consumers, and also as the image of merchants in the hearts of consumers. A trademark itself is a means rather than an end, and how to use this means is something each merchant must consider. In recent years, China's trademark protection has become increasingly mature. Whether there is infringement does not depend solely on the similarity of two marks; the key lies in whether consumers would be confused. If they are not confused, then the use by two or more merchants could expand the influence of the trademark, benefiting all users.

"For example, with the 'QQ' trademark, if there is a situation where 'consumers buy Chery QQ cars because they think this car is produced by Tencent,' or 'consumers use the QQ chat tool because they like Chery QQ cars,' then confusion occurs," Tang Guangliang said. If such situations do not arise, both parties need not compete. Because as far as trademarks are concerned, it is not simply a matter of which merchant claims ownership. Even if it belongs to one, seeing another use it does not necessarily mean eliminating it, especially if the trademark is not maliciously defamed or leads to loss of market share. It might even have a promoting effect, allowing both sides to coexist peacefully and share benefits.

Furthermore, Tang Guangliang said that from a rational market perspective, Tencent and Chery do not conflict. Instant messaging tools mainly target young people, and QQ cars are also popular among young people. Both companies could fully collaborate, operate together, share the market, and achieve a win-win outcome, making it unnecessary to fight over trademark ownership without any harm.
