Can breast milk predict cancer risk?

by emily29z on 2012-03-05 19:47:41

Breast milk has the potential to predict breast cancer risk, according to a study presented at the annual meeting of the American Association for Cancer Research in Orlando, Florida. Kathleen Arcaro, a professor of veterinary and animal sciences at the University of Massachusetts-Amherst, announced that breast milk could be as useful as other methods like biopsies and imaging techniques in detecting early signs of breast cancer—methods that are more developed but also more invasive.

Arcaro is reporting on the preliminary results of her ongoing study involving 250 women across 41 states, all of whom are at high risk for breast cancer and have either already undergone or plan to undergo biopsies of their breast tissue. All participants are also nursing and have agreed to provide breast milk for the study. Arcaro decided to study breast milk because retrieving other sources of breast tissue with needles is not only invasive but also provides only a snapshot of cells in one area of the breast. Breast milk, she reasoned, contains millions of cells shed from various parts of the breast and would therefore serve as a better representative of cancer activity in the tissue.

Arcaro also referenced a growing body of data showing that the DNA of malignant cells takes on a distinct epigenetic profile as a result of a molecular process known as methylation: essentially, methyl groups attach to genetic material to activate or deactivate genes, and in cancer cells, multiple such groups bind to the DNA. In fact, dozens of genes are methylated this way in various types of cancer. By looking for the presence of methylation in the cells found in breast milk, Arcaro thought she could gauge how potentially cancerous breast tissue might be. And since this process occurs in the early stages of the disease, informing women about their methylation status could help them take steps to lower their cancer risk. "We've found that testing methylation in breast tissue is a good way to assess individual risk for developing breast cancer," she and her team say.

She linked methylation rates of about a dozen genes associated with breast cancer in breast milk samples from both the biopsied and non-biopsied breasts of high-risk women. (The breasts were biopsied to check for possible tumors.) Arcaro found that the methylation rates in the cells from the breast milk of the biopsied breasts were higher than in the breasts that were not sampled.

For comparison, the scientific team also looked at methylation rates in 102 healthy women from an earlier study who showed low methylation in the same 12 genes. Even after considering other factors like environmental influences that could affect methylation rates, the relationship remained strong.

Does this mean mothers could pass tumor cells to their babies while feeding them? "One hundred percent not," says Arcaro, noting that all proteins swallowed by infants are digested and broken down in the stomach and digestive tract. "The baby would simply treat any methylated cells like any protein and digest them," she says.

How can these results help women? Clearly, they have the greatest impact on those who are pregnant or breastfeeding. And that's an important group, says Arcaro, since more women are delaying childbirth and cancer risk increases with age. If the results hold up in repeated trials, she expects that breast milk screening could become a non-invasive way to test all mothers at birth for breast cancer risk before they leave the hospital. "It's completely non-invasive, potentially cost-effective, and really accurate," she says. So what's healthy for baby could be healthy for mama too.
