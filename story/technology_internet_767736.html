The Truth Behind Samsung's "Font Gate"

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 13:18:02

Recently, after CCTV focused on Apple warranty issues, Samsung screen replacements, and Xiaomi flash sales, it turned its attention to the so-called "Samsung Font Library Gate," heavily criticizing the company. Due to the limited knowledge of the reporters and so-called "experts," there were many misleading points that needed clarification—hence this article.

**1. What is a phone font library?**

The term "phone font library" originates from the mobile repair industry during the feature phone and WM smartphone era. In the early days, it referred to an independent flash memory chip used to store fonts and other limited information.

In the Android smartphone era, large-capacity flash storage is used to store various types of information, and this large-capacity flash storage is also called a "font library" in the mobile repair industry. To put it simply in computer terms, it's like a computer's hard drive plus BIOS—it's a flash memory that stores all sorts of information and data for the phone.

**2. What is the "Font Library Gate"?**

The so-called "Font Library Gate" refers to damage to this flash memory.

Since modern large-capacity storage devices are mostly eMMC structures (which integrate a controller into the package to provide standard interfaces and manage flash memory), whether it's the controller being damaged or the flash memory itself being damaged, it can cause the phone to fail to start.

The cause of the damage could be either hardware or software-related. Hardware damage is often due to human error, but currently, most large-scale font library damage is caused by software defects.

**3. Which devices experience the "Font Library Gate"?**

The "Font Library Gate" can occur on all devices, but it tends to be more severe on certain models. This is mainly due to the popularity of Android and the widespread use of smartphones. Previously, WM phones were niche products. In CCTV’s program, a former Motorola engineer correctly interpreted the "Font Library Gate," as one of the earliest large-scale font library damages occurred on Motorola's DEFY model, giving Motorola some relevant experience.

After the release of Android 4.0, certain commands for reading, writing, and erasing flash memory had a certain probability of causing the "Font Library Gate." Early Android 4.0 system devices had quite a few problems; the HTC ONE X was particularly famous in this regard. Netizens jokingly referred to it as "a slap," meaning that a single slap could cause font library damage, although this was more of a joke about Android 4.0's issues.

The Samsung Galaxy S III (model 9300) and Note II also had significant problems, but they erupted a few months later than HTC. In fact, if you search for modern large-capacity storage phones + font library damage, you'll find numerous entries, whether it's Xiaomi, Apple, Huawei, or Nokia.

**4. Issues with Various Parties' Statements on the "Font Library Gate"**

The reasons stated by the Motorola engineer are correct, but upgrading to Android 4.1 did not completely solve the problem of font library damage. There is still a certain probability of font library damage occurring, though the likelihood has decreased. Even iOS and WP7 systems can suffer from font library damage.

Samsung's claim that the overall probability is low is correct, but the potential issue still exists in all phones. A low probability of occurrence now does not mean a low probability in the future.

**5. Solutions to the "Font Library Gate"**

With current technology, font library damage caused by software can only have its probability reduced; it cannot be completely eliminated. The key lies in post-problem repairs.

If the phone damage is due to human error, charging for non-warranty repairs is normal. However, font library damage could entirely result from normal usage and system defects, and making consumers pay for such repairs is unfair.

eMMC integrates everything together, so whether it's the control chip or the flash memory itself that's damaged, it cannot be repaired. If it were a computer, you could refresh the BIOS or perform a low-level format on the hard drive, and even replace a faulty control chip. But eMMC cannot be repaired at service centers; it can only be replaced. And the cost of large-capacity eMMC is very high, placing a significant financial burden on consumers.

Therefore, Samsung's main responsibility in the "Font Library Gate" lies in its after-sales service issues—charging exorbitant repair fees for font library damage that consumers themselves are not responsible for.

Additionally, consumers should be reminded that while phones have value, data is priceless. If your phone contains important personal information, remember to back it up regularly.

To improve in the future, methods used in computer motherboards, such as dual backups, could be applied. In the early days, BIOS damage in computers was a headache until GIGABYTE introduced dual BIOS systems, providing a backup in case one failed, allowing for normal use while the damaged BIOS could be replaced.

Phones could also have dual flash memory systems: if one experiences the "Font Library Gate," the phone could boot from the other, ensuring data backup and no loss. However, this would increase costs by several hundred to a thousand yuan. Whether consumers are willing to pay for this double insurance is a question.

In conclusion, the "Font Library Gate" highlights the importance of proper handling of both software and hardware issues in smartphones, and underscores the need for better consumer protection and education regarding data backup.
