At the beginning of 2005, when blog websites were just emerging in China and most Internet users were still unfamiliar with the concept of "blogging," on the other side of the ocean in the United States, a young Chinese immigrant named Chen Shijun (Steve Chen) and his colleagues Chad Hurley and Jawed Karim co-founded the YouTube video website. Just 20 months later, YouTube's daily click rate exceeded 1 billion views. In October 2006, YouTube was acquired by Google, the internet search engine giant, for $1.65 billion, firmly capturing 40% of the global online video market share.

Today, YouTube has already deeply integrated into every aspect of American life, even influencing the political arena. At the beginning of 2007, former U.S. President Bill Clinton's wife, Hillary Clinton, announced her intention to formally compete in the 2008 presidential election with the phrase "I'm in, and I want to win" on YouTube. Subsequently, Hillary spent three evenings inviting voters to engage in online video chats with her. This move by Hillary won the support of young American voters, and the UK's *The Times* commented: "The Internet has changed Hillary's aloof image and bridged the gap between her and the voters. In the era of Election 2.0, the Internet makes politics more approachable."
