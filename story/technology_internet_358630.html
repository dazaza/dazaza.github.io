Lawyer: Google's Wi-Fi privacy data collection was no accident

by king5b2t on 2010-06-21 18:08:55

Google Street View Car

Sina Technology News, June 4th morning Beijing time, according to foreign media reports, the plaintiff's attorney said Thursday that they had found in patent application documents that Google's Street View cars collecting private data from open Wi-Fi networks was not accidental.

Google Street View cars record network ID information to enhance its data positioning capabilities but also record "payload data" of Wi-Fi users. Although Google has admitted this matter, it blames it on a "accidental" code error. There are already multiple groups filing class-action lawsuits against Google for "accidentally" collecting user Wi-Fi privacy data. Brooks Cooper, an attorney from Oregon, USA, is one of the legal representatives in one of these lawsuits. He said, "So far, we believe this was not an accident."

Cooper's evidence is a patent application filed by Google in 2008. The document describes a method to improve the accuracy of location services. With location services, advertisers can know the precise location of mobile phones and other computer devices. This patent includes data interception and analysis technology to precisely locate users. However, Google denies that this patent is related to the collection of Wi-Fi privacy data.

Cooper submitted documents to the U.S. District Court for Oregon late Wednesday indicating that the patent application known as "776" was released by the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office in January this year. Cooper claims that this patent describes "one or more methods" to help collect information for Google Street View services.

Google spokesperson Christine Chen said, "The disputed patent has nothing to do with the software code used by Street View cars to collect Wi-Fi information." She added, "Google applies for patents based on various ideas from engineers. Some have developed into official products or services, some have not." However, she did not disclose whether the so-called "776" patent has been put into use.

Industry insiders believe that regardless of whether Google intentionally collected Wi-Fi privacy data, this incident has already negatively affected Google's public image. It will also lead to numerous legal lawsuits and compensation demands. There are already multiple groups filing class-action lawsuits against Google.

If Google can prove that it only accidentally collected this data and never leaked it, it may escape charges. This data includes users' web browsing records, emails, videos, audio, and document information.

Google must hand over the Wi-Fi privacy data it collected in the U.S. to the federal court in Oregon by this Friday. This data is currently sealed.

Street View is a feature in Google Maps and Google Earth that provides users with immersive panoramic images. Google has consistently claimed that it did not intentionally collect user Wi-Fi privacy data, stating that it was due to an early experimental code error. Google says the company did not realize this issue for the past three years until German privacy regulators began questioning what kind of data their service was collecting.
