Strengths and Weaknesses of the 4 Microsoft's New CEO Contenders

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:41:42

On October 11, Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer announced in August that he would retire within the next 12 months. An insider from Microsoft claimed that Ballmer might retire "earlier than expected."

Bloomberg reported that Microsoft's board of directors hoped the new CEO would take office by the end of this year and had been in talks with four candidates, two external hires and two internal executives. Below is a brief analysis of the advantages and disadvantages of these four individuals for the role of Microsoft CEO:

External Candidate No. 1: Ford CEO Alan Mulally

Advantages: Mulally has a good personal relationship with Ballmer and offered advice on Microsoft’s recent corporate restructuring. He has some understanding of Microsoft's current state and the measures needed to get it back on track.

Prior to joining Ford, Mulally worked at Boeing. During his tenure at Ford, the company did not request government assistance from the U.S., and he successfully led Ford through a transformation.

Disadvantages: At 68 years old, Mulally’s age raises questions about whether he still has the energy to manage a company of Microsoft’s scale. Additionally, he lacks experience in high-tech companies and is considered to lack vision. Can he handle intense competition from companies like Apple and Google? Can he foresee the next wave in personal technology?

External Candidate No. 2: Pivotal Cloud Computing CEO Paul Maritz

Advantages: Maritz served as CEO of VMware and performed admirably. He has extensive experience in enterprise business and a deep understanding of cloud computing technology. Microsoft's future lies in its enterprise products, so from this perspective, he could be an ideal candidate to lead Microsoft into a new phase. Maritz previously worked at Microsoft, where he was responsible for the development of Windows 95.

Disadvantages: Maritz is nearing retirement age. It remains unclear whether he has the enthusiasm to become Microsoft's CEO. Moreover, Maritz lacks experience leading a company through a revival, which is what Microsoft needs.

Internal Executive No. 1: Nokia CEO Stephen Elop

Advantages: Strictly speaking, Elop is an external hire since Nokia is still an independent company. However, Elop can also be considered an internal executive at Microsoft. Before moving to Nokia, Elop led Microsoft's Office division for three years. He understands Microsoft well, has served as the CEO of a public company (Nokia), and has experience in the tech industry.

Disadvantages: The reason Elop might return to Microsoft is because he failed to save Nokia. During his tenure, Nokia's value significantly diminished. Elop has not demonstrated any particular insights into the smartphone market. What measures will he take to strengthen Microsoft's business?

Internal Executive No. 2: Former Skype CEO Tony Bates

Advantages: Bates has experience in both enterprise and consumer industries. Before becoming Skype's CEO, Bates worked at SAP. His time at Microsoft is neither too short nor too long; he understands Microsoft sufficiently but has not been fully assimilated into its existing corporate culture. Employees at Skype liked working with Bates. Currently, his role at Microsoft involves seeking new opportunities for the company.

Disadvantages: Bates served as Skype's CEO for less than a year and lacks experience managing a publicly traded company. Does Bates have the ability to manage a company of Microsoft's size?
