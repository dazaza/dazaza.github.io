Introduction:

If con artistry is considered a criminal art form, then the brothers Bloom and Stephen are undoubtedly master practitioners. They began their life of deception at a young age, along with their silent partner, the explosives expert Bang Bang, and have been virtually unstoppable, never failing in their schemes.

Perhaps due to their growing age and changing mindsets, Bloom has developed a desire to retire. He no longer wishes to continue this lifestyle… However, Stephen thrives in this world and naturally does not want Bloom to leave, as he cannot design scams alone. To reignite Bloom's "spirit," Stephen carefully selects their next target: Penelope Staple, a wealthy woman living alone in a grand villa. Although Penelope is beautiful, she is eccentric and reclusive, with an unusual hobby of personally crashing expensive sports cars.

Attracting such a peculiar girl obviously requires unconventional methods. Bloom and Stephen successfully infiltrate the villa and convince Penelope to join them on a transatlantic cruise aboard a ship. During this journey, Bloom becomes deeply attracted to Penelope, strengthening his resolve to quit… How will Stephen persuade his brother to stay? He plans to focus on Penelope, who plays a key role in the scheme.

Behind the Scenes:

Old-School Con Artist Film

The film is Rian Johnson's second directorial effort after his 2005 debut feature "Brick," which had a darker tone. Johnson describes "The Brothers Bloom" as belonging to the traditional genre of con artist films akin to "The Sting" and "Paper Moon." Focusing on two brothers who are professional con artists, played by Mark Ruffalo and Adrien Brody, Johnson says: "These brothers travel the world by ship, just that aspect gives 'The Brothers Bloom' an old-school style reminiscent of the 1970s, slightly neurotic, with a story entirely character-driven. When I decided to create a story about cons, it felt like giving myself a marching order, and the excitement of making an old-school character-driven movie was palpable. The film sets its tone with a cunning narrative, as the intrigue of this genre lies in: Who deceived whom? Whom can you trust? On this foundation, we sought creative ways to build emotional investment between characters, especially when you're baffled about 'who manipulated whom,' turning everything into a unique challenge. Ultimately, I believe the success of 'The Brothers Bloom' lies not only in its twists and unexpected ending but also in its captivating characters."

Since Rian Johnson is a big fan of magician Reginald Jay, whose poster adorns his office wall framed under glass, Johnson invited Jay to provide narration for a segment of "The Brothers Bloom." At the start of the film, the young Bloom and Stephen brothers are trying hard to make their first scam successful... This series of scenes is enhanced by Jay's pleasant voice, even though his narration is limited to a small part of the film, his voice provides a stunning and great opening for "The Brothers Bloom."

The final shooting locations for "The Brothers Bloom" were in Belgrade, the capital of Yugoslavia. Rian Johnson said: "We had already been there, in Serbia, and returned to reshoot some scenes, essentially wrapping up the project." During filming in Serbia, Johnson hired English-speaking children from local diplomatic academies as extras. Additionally, the crew filmed in Montenegro, Prague, and Romania. Johnson recalls: "Not only did we hire local children, but also local actors. Interestingly, the children spoke English fluently, whereas the adults were clueless about the language... But these actors are well-known stars in their home country, a fact we weren't aware of until later. For instance, the actress playing the mother in the film, during shooting breaks, many local staff would approach her for autographs, and only then did we realize we had cast famous Serbian actors."

"The Brothers Bloom" doesn't present a single con but rather a series of interlocking cons within cons. Rian Johnson explains: "Both Bloom and Stephen develop romantic feelings for Penelope Staple, played by Rachel Weisz, hinting at a love triangle, although she was initially targeted as a victim... This film doesn't follow the structure of most crime movies, building towards a climactic case. Instead, it focuses more on the various interconnected scams among the main characters."

After tasting success in their initial scam, when we see Bloom and Stephen again, they have shed their youthful naivety and matured fully into adults. By this point, Bloom is weary of the deceit-filled world of con artists and attempts to escape the control of his brother Stephen, who clearly has other plans and intends to live off cons forever. Alongside them is their capable assistant Bang Bang, portrayed by Rinko Kikuchi, who received an Oscar nomination for Best Supporting Actress for "Babel." Stephen forces Bloom to join him in designing a scam targeting a wealthy eccentric woman named Penelope. Upon entering Penelope's large mansion for the first time, they find her using her extremely expensive Lamborghini supercar to crash into a massive statue, instantly reducing the car to pieces while leaving Penelope unscathed and seemingly unconcerned about her now ruined expensive vehicle. Shortly afterward, a tow truck removes the destroyed car and delivers a brand-new Lamborghini. Penelope's violent and bizarre behavior immediately captivates Bloom, as does her beauty... The interest in this section isn't just in the tension of the story and characters; the original score by Nathan Johnson, Rian's cousin, who also composed the soundtrack for "Brick," plays a significant role.

Innovation Amid Tradition

Rian Johnson has never hidden his admiration for Rachel Weisz's acting talent, stating: "Weisz portrays a very difficult character in the film. Penelope is strikingly beautiful but verbally cutting, a proud woman who doesn't need to flatter others, with peculiar habits. I think Weisz genuinely strives to embody all the traits that make Penelope unlikable, coloring her in a realistic manner so that Penelope transcends mere erratic behavior... In my view, this is a highly sophisticated way of portraying a character."

For a long time, Rian Johnson has followed the evolution of con artist films. He insists that while "The Brothers Bloom" belongs to this genre, it must also progress through unique elements. Johnson explains: "Take the ending of this film as an example; it contains two major cons that deviate from their expected courses and reach an unforeseen destination—this aspect alone differs significantly from traditional con artist films. Moreover, if you watch the film again, I guarantee you'll have a completely different experience, perhaps feeling as if you're watching two entirely distinct movies."

After identifying their target, the seasoned con artists set a trap for Penelope, fabricating a story about a valuable book in the library of Prague Castle, which is actually worthless... In this part, Rian Johnson decides to show how the sexual tension between Bloom and Penelope is severed: the plan is to send Penelope alone into the castle to steal the "priceless" book, while Bang Bang arranges a small explosion to trigger a smoke-induced fire alarm, allowing Penelope to escape amidst the chaos with the book. However, Bang Bang's bomb is slightly overpowered, resulting in the destruction of the entire church and nearly trapping Penelope.

After completing the filming of "The Brothers Bloom," Rian Johnson plunged into the marketing campaign for the movie. He praised the recently completed trailer highly and stated that while the marketing schedule is tight, it hasn't reached a frenzied level, emphasizing that everything should proceed methodically. His veiled comment refers to the marketing strategy used for "The Dark Knight."

Rian Johnson insisted on preserving the traditional appearance of con artist films while infusing this genre with a fresh style and atmosphere, quite different from his previous dark and somber independent film "Brick." His ability to quickly shift between such vastly different genres showcases his adeptness in capturing the natural essence of comedy. Every part of "The Brothers Bloom" offers a delightful viewing experience, filled with refreshing dialogues, brilliant music, and outstanding performances, particularly by Rinko Kikuchi, who proves through this film that she can transcend national boundaries and effortlessly handle roles with stark contrasts, delivering a unique and vibrant performance.

Following "The Brothers Bloom," Rian Johnson's next project is already underway—a science fiction film called "Looper." Currently, he is writing the screenplay for this film, explaining: "The film tightly wraps the characters in the story. Despite some similarities in plot settings, 'Looper' is entirely different from the 'Terminator' series, except for involving time travel as an auxiliary condition to connect the plot... I can reveal a little bit—it’s going to be extremely violent and bloody."
