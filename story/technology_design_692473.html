Gold - Major economies inject liquidity, gold gathers upward momentum

by zhanladaim on 2012-03-06 09:52:12

March 2012 Gold and Foreign Exchange Market Outlook

By Meng Juan, Metals Analyst, Huaan Futures

I. February Market Review

In February 2012, Greece's debt restructuring negotiations proceeded with difficulty but ultimately reached a debt reduction agreement, securing the second round of bailout funds from the Eurozone and avoiding imminent default. Positively impacted by this development, the EUR/USD exchange rate showed an upward trend amidst volatility; correspondingly, the strengthening of the EUR/USD constituted the main factor for the USD Index's downward volatility in February. However, on February 14th, the Bank of Japan announced an increase of 10 trillion yen to its existing 20 trillion yen asset purchase program, all of which will be used to buy long-term government bonds, indirectly intervening in the yen foreign exchange market. This led to a significant one-sided rise in USD/JPY, providing some support to the USD Index. As of the date of writing, the EUR/USD exchange rate rose cumulatively by 388 points in February, up 2.97%, nearly 3%; the USD Index, after failing to break through the 80 mark, fell below 78.5, with a cumulative decline of 1.13% in February.

In February, under the impetus of the euro's upward volatility and the tense situation in Iran, international gold prices overall showed an upward trend amidst volatility. International London gold prices stabilized near 1720 after a pullback and continued to rise after breaking through 1760, reaching a high of 1787. As of the date of writing, London gold prices rose cumulatively by 34.02 in February, with a cumulative increase of 1.96%. Domestic Shanghai Gold Futures' main contract 1206 rose cumulatively by 7.21 in February, driven by international gold prices, with a cumulative increase of 2.03%.

II. Analysis of the Situation in Iran - Tensions Rise as All Sides Take Aggressive Stances

In 2012, the situation in Iran has undoubtedly become a new focal point of geopolitical attention. With increasing sanctions from Europe and the U.S., tensions in the Iranian situation intensified in February. On January 23rd, the European Council issued a statement saying that the EU's oil embargo against Iran would take effect on July 1, 2012, and new restrictions would be imposed on Iran's central bank. The aim was to make Iran abandon its uranium enrichment program, but under pressure from Europe and the U.S., Iran remained unyielding. On one hand, it insisted that its uranium enrichment project was for civilian and peaceful purposes; on the other hand, it accelerated its nuclear research process and announced on February 16th that it had manufactured a new type of uranium enrichment centrifuge and produced nuclear fuel plates for reactors independently, once again touching a sensitive nerve in Western countries.

On February 20th, Iran's Oil Ministry announced that Iran had stopped selling oil to British and French companies in retaliation for the EU's decision to ban the purchase of Iranian oil starting in July. On February 23rd, it was reported that the latest actions of the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) failed to make Iran abandon its nuclear program. Market concerns about escalating confrontation between Iran and Western countries pushed oil prices to a nine-month high, surpassing $110. Currently, major oil-producing countries like Saudi Arabia are increasing oil exports to compensate for supply shortfalls. The tension in the Iranian geopolitical situation and the rapid rise in crude oil prices have propelled international gold prices out of their volatility range, surpassing $1780. The author still maintains the previous view: the global theme in 2012 remains economic recovery, and launching a war would not benefit the economic recovery of Europe and the U.S., making a localized war in Iran unlikely. However, currently, Iran, Israel, and the U.S. are in a standoff. If any one party acts irrationally, it could directly trigger a localized war.

III. Euro Trend Analysis

3.1 Greece Avoids Debt Default Risk, Beware of Shifting Focus of Eurozone Crisis to Other Countries

In February, Greece's debt restructuring negotiations and default risk became the main uncertainties affecting the euro. The European side continued its habit of dragging out solutions to the debt crisis. Greece's debt restructuring negotiations were postponed for a month and finally reached an agreement on February 21st, where private creditors wrote off 53.5% of their claims, with the remaining claims exchanged for new Greek government bonds and EFSF bonds. Of this, 31.5% will be used for Greek government bonds with maturities of 11-30 years, and 15% will be used for short-term bonds issued by the EFSF. This plan will reduce Greece's debt burden by 107 billion euros, equivalent to 50% of Greece's GDP in 2011. On February 24th, Greece's Finance Ministry officially implemented this agreement. In addition, Eurozone finance ministers also reached an agreement on Greece's second bailout package, providing 130 billion euros in funding and requiring Greece to reduce its debt-to-GDP ratio to 121% by 2020.

As a result, Greece ultimately avoided the risk of default before its debt maturity in March. The debt crisis in Greece is temporarily over for the short term, and the euro benefited significantly during the evolution of this event. However, the Eurozone debt crisis is far from over. The temporary resolution of the Greek issue merely provides a brief respite for the Eurozone debt crisis. Italy, Spain, and other countries will face peak debt repayment periods in March, and there is a need to beware of the market shifting focus to other potential debt crisis countries.

On February 29th, the European Central Bank will begin its second round of Long-Term Refinancing Operations, providing unlimited three-year loans to European banks. Currently, the market is paying attention to the scale of this Long-Term Refinancing Operation. If the loan amount is similar to that in December last year, it may boost confidence in the European banking sector, supporting the euro. Conversely, if the loan amount is too large, such as close to 1 trillion euros, it would significantly increase the ECB's balance sheet and reflect serious liquidity shortages in the European banking sector, putting some pressure on the euro.

Another focus next month is the summit of EU member state leaders in Brussels on March 1-2. The market expects the summit to reach an agreement on merging the European Financial Stability Facility (EFSF) with the European Stability Mechanism (ESM), forming a more robust firewall against the spread of the Eurozone debt crisis. There are reports that Germany has already loosened its stance to take the first step towards expansion, paving the way for further capital injections by the International Monetary Fund (IMF) to expand the scale of European bailouts. If these messages can be confirmed, the path to resolving the Eurozone debt crisis will be smoother, further supporting the euro.

3.2 The Eurozone's Real Economy Remains Weak, Maintaining Interest Rates Unchanged Meets Expectations

From the data, the Eurozone's leading economic indicator PMI performed relatively steadily in February. The service sector PMI fell from 50.5 last month to 49.4; although the manufacturing PMI rebounded slightly, it remained below 50, rising from 48.8 last month to 49, falling short of the market's expected value of 49.5, and marking the seventh consecutive month below the 50 threshold dividing expansion from contraction, indicating a still weak economic situation. The CPI and PPI year-on-year growth rates in the Eurozone as a whole showed a downward trend, with the CPI year-on-year growth rate declining from 3% in November to 2.7% in December 2011, showing some decline but still being above the critical value of 2% for the 13th consecutive month, with inflationary pressures persisting, putting pressure on the release of loose monetary policy measures to inject liquidity into the Eurozone economy. Attention should be paid to the February data for this indicator.

In terms of economic sentiment indices, with market expectations that Greece would not default on its debt being realized, investment confidence and consumer confidence indices improved somewhat. Investment confidence received a strong boost, with the Sentix investment confidence index rising by 10 points to -11.1 in February. The consumer confidence index rose for the second consecutive month in February, indicating a mild sign of increased consumer spending. The consumer confidence index rose by 0.5 points from last month to -20.2, higher than the 26-month low reached in December 2011, indicating a tendency for consumer confidence to emerge from the trough. In terms of employment, the latest data show that the unemployment rate in the Eurozone remained at a historical high of 10.4% in December 2011, unchanged from the previous month, fully reflecting the current status of weak economic recovery in the Eurozone.

The mid-term fiscal report released by the European Commission on February 23rd stated that the Eurozone economy fell back into recession at the beginning of the year. It is expected that the Eurozone GDP will shrink by 0.3% in 2012 compared to 2011, whereas the forecast in November last year was for a GDP growth of 0.5%. According to the latest forecast, the entire EU economy will stagnate this year. Against the backdrop of insufficient momentum for the Eurozone economic recovery, no effective solution to the Eurozone debt crisis has been found yet, and the European Central Bank's decision to maintain interest rates unchanged in February met market expectations.

3.3 Conclusion

Greece ultimately avoided defaulting on its debt in February, but other Eurozone countries with potential debt crisis risks, such as Italy and Spain, will also face peak debt repayment periods in March, necessitating vigilance against shifts in market focus. Additionally, the negotiation process regarding the expansion of the Eurozone's internal financial stability fund and the IMF's expansion to rescue the Eurozone debt crisis will become the focal events in March, potentially constituting important influencing factors for the EUR/USD exchange rate.

IV. Dollar Trend Analysis

4.1 Further Improvements in Labor Market and Housing Market Data in February

Regarding the labor market, economic data published in February showed that the U.S. added 243,000 non-farm jobs in January 2012, with the unemployment rate further declining to 8.3%. The weekly initial jobless claims indicator continued its decreasing trend from earlier periods. The weekly initial jobless claims for the first three weeks of February declined sequentially, dropping to 351,000, indicating further improvement in the labor market.

In terms of the housing market, the NAHB Housing Market Index in February continued the steady upward trend seen in the previous few months, rising from 25 last month to 29, better than the market expectation of 26. This marks the fifth consecutive month of steady increases and the highest level since mid-2007, signaling a strong recovery in the U.S. housing market. However, the New Home Sales Index for January remains sluggish, hovering between 20-25. Attention should be paid to the February data for this indicator.

4.2 No New Developments in the Fed's Monetary Policy, Next Month Focus on IMF Expansion to Assist Europe

There were no new developments in the Federal Reserve's monetary policy in February, continuing the policy of maintaining ultra-low benchmark interest rates of 0-0.25% until the end of 2014 and continuing the "Operation Twist" of buying longer-term Treasury securities. From recent U.S. economic data, both the labor market and the housing market show signs of improvement. If this trend continues, the Fed's timeline for raising interest rates might be brought forward.

Currently, the market's focus remains on the Eurozone debt crisis solution. Recent discussions at the G20 meeting regarding the expansion of the IMF to reinforce and strengthen the Eurozone financial firewall have become a focal point. These discussions and decisions may continue until April. If the IMF expansion succeeds, it will win time and funds for resolving the Eurozone debt crisis, reducing its impact, benefiting the euro, and possibly pressuring the dollar. However, so far, the Eurozone has not yet reached a consensus on expanding the European Financial Stability Facility, and there are differences in willingness among IMF members to contribute. Therefore, it is unlikely to happen in the short term, and developments in this matter in March will primarily influence the U.S. and Euro currency markets.

4.3 Conclusion

Labor market and housing data in the U.S. continued to improve in February, providing confidence and hope for the U.S. economic recovery. However, there are currently no new developments in the Fed's monetary policy, and internal U.S. economic factors have limited impact on the dollar index. The main factor influencing the dollar index next month will still come from the progress in resolving the Eurozone debt crisis. Ongoing discussions about the IMF expansion to assist the Eurozone will be the primary factor influencing the euro and the dollar. Without optimistic resolutions, the dollar may maintain a volatile pattern. Attention should be paid to the progress of this event in March.

V. Domestic Investment Environment Analysis

5.1 The Central Bank Cuts Reserve Requirement Ratio by 0.5 Percentage Points, Releasing Liquidity to Boost Market Confidence

On February 18th, the central bank announced a cut in the reserve requirement ratio by 0.5 percentage points starting from February 24th. This was the first cut within the year and the second cut since the reserve requirement ratio reached a historical high in June 2011. This cut was partly due to the noticeable decline in domestic CPI and mainly due to the tightening of liquidity in the banking sector in the short term. After the adjustment, approximately 375 billion yuan of liquidity was released, alleviating the suppression of bank loans caused by tight liquidity in the banking sector. For gold futures investors, this reduction in the reserve requirement ratio has a positive effect, as the central bank's proactive release of liquidity helps improve the overall investment market liquidity and investment confidence.

The preliminary value of the HSBC China PMI in February 2012 was 49.7, higher than the final value of 48.8 in the previous month, continuing to rise for the third consecutive month but still below the critical value of 50. Post-spring festival production recovery improved the PMI value, but under the backdrop of weak external demand, there remains a risk of domestic economic growth slowing down, which to some extent confirms the central bank's intention to lower interest rates and release liquidity to boost the economy.

5.2 Conclusion

The domestic reduction in the statutory reserve requirement ratio benefits the domestic investment market, appropriately releasing liquidity to boost investment markets including the stock market and commodities, with gold futures investments also benefiting.

VI. Gold Price Trend Analysis

6.1 Europe and Japan Inject Liquidity into the Economy, Supporting Gold Prices

In February, the increasingly tense situation in Iran and the weakening of the dollar index became the main factors supporting gold prices. The progress of the Iranian situation next month will also be one of the main uncertain factors affecting gold prices. Moreover, given the general environment of major economies injecting liquidity into the economy, this constitutes the main factor currently supporting gold prices.

In the UK, due to declining inflation, the Bank of England expanded its QE scale by 50 billion pounds on February 9th and maintained its low-interest-rate policy of 0.5%. By now, the UK's quantitative easing scale has expanded to 325 billion pounds. The meeting records showed that two people supported expanding the QE scale to 75 billion pounds, increasing expectations for further quantitative easing in the future.

In Japan, on February 14th, the Bank of Japan announced an increase in its asset purchase scale by 10 trillion yen (approximately 128 billion USD) to buy long-term government bonds, bringing the total scale to about 65 trillion yen, while raising the inflation target from 1% to 2% to accelerate economic recovery and combat deflation.

In the Eurozone, on February 29th, the European Central Bank will start its second round of long-term refinancing operations, providing unlimited three-year loans to the European banking sector, with a scale expected to be between 400 billion to 1 trillion euros.

Additionally, as mentioned earlier, China reduced the reserve requirement ratio by 0.5 percentage points domestically, equivalent to releasing 375 billion yuan of liquidity.

Under the background of major economies injecting liquidity into the economy through different means, the potential pressure of paper currency depreciation will persist, allowing gold to demonstrate its preservation advantage, favoring the rise in gold prices.

6.3 Fund Holdings Initially Declined but Later Increased, Bullish Sentiment Warmed Up

The latest data shows that the world's largest gold ETF - SPDR slightly increased its gold holdings in February 2012 compared to the previous month. As of February 23rd, SPDR's gold holdings increased by 32.27 tons compared to the end of January 2012, reaching 1282.80 tons, with an increase of 2.58%. The number of non-commercial net long positions in COMEX gold futures first decreased then increased: as of February 21st, non-commercial net long positions increased by 9,600 contracts, with an increase of 5.6%. The bullish sentiment in the gold market warmed up.

6.4 Conclusion

Recently, several major economies, including the UK, Japan, the Eurozone, and China, have injected liquidity into their economies using different methods. The potential factor of paper currency depreciation will constitute the main supportive factor for international gold prices in the near term, allowing gold's preservation advantage to manifest, favoring the rise in gold prices. Additionally, stable increases in international gold fund holdings will provide support for gold prices in terms of confidence.
