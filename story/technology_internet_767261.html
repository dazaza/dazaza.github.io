Chinese Mobile Phones Going Global: ZTE Heads Left, Huawei Turns Right

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:10:51

Among domestic manufacturers, Huawei and ZTE nominally call each other "friendly competitors" while privately they are rivals. This year, both companies have heavily invested in B2C transformation and the internationalization of their phone brands. However, ZTE has chosen to focus left (on the US market), while Huawei has chosen to focus right (on the European market).

In June this year, Huawei launched its flagship phone P6 for the second half of the year in London, UK, targeting the global market. Richard Yu, Chairman of Huawei Devices, revealed to me that Huawei will invest 33 million Euros (approximately 270 million RMB) on brand promotion and market deployment in Europe. In October, ZTE announced in the US that it would invest about 100 million USD over the next three years to collaborate with the NBA league to accelerate its B2C brand transformation. It also signed a contract with the Houston Rockets team to speed up its market expansion in the US. This is the largest sports marketing activity carried out by domestic mobile phone enterprises in recent years.

With Google's acquisition of Motorola Mobility, Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia, and BlackBerry being forced to sell to a consortium, the international mobile phone market is undergoing a "major reshuffle." Domestic mobile phone brands are all choosing to "go overseas," with ZTE, Huawei, Coolpad, Lenovo, and TCL all aiming for the top 3 or top 5 globally. As the most representative Chinese mobile phone brands, why did ZTE and Huawei choose two different markets in opposite directions? What kind of gamesmanship lies behind this decision? And what experiences can other domestic mobile phone brands learn from them?

I have separately tracked Huawei's journey to Europe and ZTE's journey to the US for reporting purposes, conducting long-term interviews with local operators, consumers, and employees of these two companies. Below are the summarized reasons:

1. Market Strategy

Huawei's system equipment has been active in Europe for many years with noticeable results. The major European operators (big T) all consider Huawei as one of their main equipment suppliers. As a traditional system equipment manufacturer, Huawei hopes to expand cooperation in the terminal field by leveraging its good relationship with operators.

ZTE lags behind Huawei in the European system equipment market in terms of scale, and Europe's complex operator landscape adds difficulty. On the contrary, ZTE entered the US mobile phone market earlier, where 90% of users are concentrated among the top four operators, making market breakthroughs more effective.

2. Leadership Style

Huawei founder Ren Zhengfei's influence on the company is very evident, and his personal style has affected the company's government relations and market strategies. Facing obstacles in the US system market, Hu Houkun, then rotating CEO of Huawei, publicly "criticized" the US government, demanding a response. Subsequently, a senior Huawei executive stated in an interview that the US was no longer considered as the main target market.

Compared to Huawei's "hard charging," ZTE, as a listed company, adopts a more pragmatic approach. Chairman Hou Weizhu sent a dedicated response and litigation team to communicate with US regulators. Under the situation where it still couldn't break into the system market, ZTE chose to enter the US market through terminal products closer to the consumer market, achieving good results. Currently, ZTE's share of the US mobile phone market has climbed to fourth place.

3. Product Strategy

Huawei's current strategy in the European market is similar to HTC's approach in the US, relying on a few star products to promote flagship products and high-end brands. This requires large investments, yields slow returns, and necessitates long-term commitment and persistence, but the rewards upon success are significant.

ZTE's current strategy in the US market mainly focuses on starting with mid-to-low-end products to increase volume. After gaining recognition from operators in terms of product quality, supply chain, and safety certification, it gradually upgrades to higher-end products, adopting a more pragmatic approach.

4. Operator Situation

The European operator landscape is relatively stable, but the main operators are all incurring losses, providing less subsidies for phones, thus increasing the cost for users to purchase. Compared to Apple and Samsung, Huawei's phones offer equivalent specifications but at lower prices, giving its flagship models a better chance of success compared to other markets.

In contrast, US operators are currently undergoing intense changes due to mergers and acquisitions, such as T-Mobile acquiring MetroPCS and SoftBank acquiring Sprint, which significantly impact mobile phone manufacturers. Additionally, the US's top four operators provide substantial subsidies for phones. For consumers, purchasing postpaid models from Apple and Samsung involves less pressure, making it relatively harder for domestic flagship phones to succeed in the US market.

5. Marketing Approach

Huawei has previously used sports marketing in Europe, such as sponsoring the Italian Super Cup and the Atletico Madrid football team in La Liga, though the effects were generally average and not sustained. This year, the marketing of flagship products like P2, D2, and P6 has mostly returned to traditional online + offline methods.

Previously, ZTE primarily chose brand marketing methods like embedding in TV programs, but these were often small-scale efforts that failed to continuously convey a clear brand concept to consumers. After six months of preparation and planning, ZTE announced in October that it would invest hundreds of millions of RMB to form a strategic partnership with the NBA league and sign a contract with the Houston Rockets to become their exclusive mobile phone strategic partner. Numerous NBA stars will appear collectively to endorse ZTE's mobile phone brand. This brand joint marketing campaign will last for three years.

Three Years to Determine Success or Failure

As can be seen, as domestic mobile phone manufacturers, ZTE and Huawei are moving further apart on their path to internationalization, and it's too early to determine who is better or faster.

However, as the "reshuffling period" approaches, the competition is accelerating. As Richard Yu, Chairman of Huawei Devices, told me in an interview: "The next three years will be the most crucial test for Huawei Devices. In the future, there will only be the top three global mobile phone brands." He Shiyou, Executive Vice President of ZTE, declared: "Three years will decide life or death; ZTE mobile phones will either ascend to heaven or descend to hell."
