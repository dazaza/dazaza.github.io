Some homeowners ask, with high summer temperatures, home decoration research reports show that formaldehyde is released and evaporates quickly, so can the airing time be shorter? Some families with poorer conditions, are forced by the continuous high temperatures and can't wait to move into their new homes. Industry experts point out that finding a reasonable time to move into a new home is a lesson that homeowners must learn after decorating. Let's take a look at how long it takes before moving in after house renovation.

There is currently no unified standard for how long one should wait before moving in after house renovation, nor can there be a single standard. Some say half a month, some say a month is enough, others say at least three months are needed, and nearly 30% of people believe it must be over six months. In fact, the length of airing time depends on the amount of pollution sources, and cannot be uniformly determined. In fact, everyone's cautious attitude towards new homes is not without reason. Netizen high-light8211 reminds us that one of her friends, due to the urgency of marriage, moved into a newly decorated house immediately, resulting in the discovery of fetal malformation during pregnancy. Some elderly people who moved into newly decorated houses suffered from chest tightness and pharyngitis.

How long should one wait before moving in after house renovation? An expert specializing in indoor environmental treatment says, [If a newly renovated house has not undergone any treatment, airing it for a year is not too long]. Usually asking several treatment company technicians also yields similar answers. They explain that generally when detecting in newly renovated houses, especially in summer, the results show that the formaldehyde content in every household exceeds the standard (our national standard 'Hygiene Standard for Formaldehyde in Residential Air' stipulates: the maximum allowable concentration of formaldehyde in indoor air is 0.08 mg/cubic meter, exceeding this will be harmful to human health), most new houses' formaldehyde content exceeds the national limit by 2-3 times, some even exceed by more than 20 times.

How long should one wait before moving in after house renovation? The main harmful substances in newly renovated houses are formaldehyde, benzene, and volatile organic compounds. Formaldehyde mainly comes from plywood, particleboard, fiberboard, etc., while benzene mainly comes from paint. Therefore, including finished furniture, boards, and paint are the environmental protection priorities during the renovation process. Many homeowners think that the furniture they bought is the highest environmental protection grade; the floor is a state-exempt product; the paint is non-benzene environmental paint, so isn't the entire house very environmentally friendly? Actually, it's not like that. I remember an expert saying this: [No benzene, no paint]. This indicates that all paints contain benzene, non-benzene paint is just hype. Even if all materials used are environmentally friendly, there won't be no pollution, nor can one move in immediately, because these several harmful substances mixed together have a cumulative effect. The trace harmful substances in furniture, plus the trace harmful substances in latex paint, plus the trace harmful substances in floors and cabinets, multiple traces add up to a large amount of harmful substances.

So how to remove renovation pollution? Currently, methods available in the market include using green plants and bamboo charcoal to absorb toxic gases; opening north-south windows for natural ventilation; hiring professional environmental treatment companies for overall treatment, etc. These conventional practices have almost become necessary procedures before moving into a new home. However, some homeowners think that after treatment, they can move in with peace of mind. From a professional perspective, this is not optimistic, as these single treatments all have limitations. The method of plant adsorption of harmful substances is theoretically the most environmentally friendly, but plants have their own shortcomings. Plants such as aloe vera and pothos have limited absorption ranges. It's impossible to place one or two pots of plants to completely absorb all the toxic gases in the whole house, which is similar to the principle of air conditioning cooling. Trying to use a 1.5-horsepower air conditioner to cool a 60-square-meter space, is it possible? Obviously, the effect would be minimal. Plants are the same, exceeding their capacity range, they naturally cannot function effectively. So don't rely on a few plants to absorb all the toxic gases in each room in your house. To detoxify, you need to understand the effective adsorption area of each plant. No one would leave all rooms empty to place full of plants for adsorption, so using plants to absorb harmful substances is only suitable for small apartments. The limitation of bamboo charcoal is similar to plants, it will saturate after absorbing to a certain extent and needs to be taken out to dry under the sun periodically.

It is understood that regarding indoor environmental treatment, the country does not yet have relevant industry standards. There is only a mandatory standard 'Limitation of Hazardous Substances in Indoor Decorative Materials'. And the indoor environmental treatment enterprises in the market are varied, making it confusing for consumers to choose who to treat. The methods of several professional environmental treatment companies are similar: first, air sampling detection, then photocatalytic treatment with a specific solution, requiring closed doors and windows, allowing toxic substances to undergo chemical reactions with the treatment solution.
