11 High-Risk Professions in the New Era, and Programmers Don't Even Make the List!

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:37:25

The well-known American technology blog, Business Insider, verified various data sources, including government agencies and trade-related professional publications, to find the 11 most dangerous jobs in the new economic era.

1. Tower Technician

If it weren't for these towers, you wouldn't have cellular communication, broadcasting, cable television, or even electricity. Tower technicians must climb towering structures and communication masts. What causes these technicians' deaths? It could be due to their safety equipment failing or even a complete lack of protective measures. However, final detailed statistics show that since 2006, there have been 183.6 deaths per 100,000 people. This figure makes the risks faced by offshore fishermen seem mild in comparison. So, let's try to stay on the ground to avoid the danger of falling.

2. E-waste Recycler

Recycling seems like one of the safest jobs possible. But the old phones, tablets, and televisions people discard contain precious metals such as gold, silver, and palladium, which often release heavy metals—mercury, cadmium, and beryllium—that can cause neurological problems. This type of work is usually carried out by children in India and some criminals in American prisons. According to a domestic U.S. study, among recyclers of recoverable material waste, there are 22.8 deaths per 100,000 people, so collecting materials also carries significant risks.

3. Internet Moderator

Many larger tech companies inevitably encounter potentially illegal topics when responsible for delivering information. At this point, moderators may face insults and defamation, sometimes even worse. It was reported that Google employees spent a year filtering disturbing content from search engines, such as child pornography images. In one day alone, there were 15,000 images of child pornography. He said, "This made me feel like I was in a very dark place."

4. Mining Worker

Whether extracting oil or natural gas, onshore or offshore, mining is absolutely a dangerous task. According to surveys, in this industry, there are about 15.8 deaths per 100,000 people. More specifically, in 2011, there were 15.5 deaths per 100,000 people in natural gas and oil extraction. For coal mining, this number is 29.5.

5. Lineman

Whether installing or repairing, linemen face risks. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics says that in 2011, the mortality rate for this profession was 29.1 deaths per 100,000 people. As the country pushes to establish so-called "smart" grids, more work is expected in this industry, which will inevitably increase the chances of injury.

6. Nurse

According to the magazine "Health," depression rates are extremely high in the nursing field, even compared to all healthcare workers. The problem arises both physiologically and emotionally. Among nurses who take leave due to non-fatal injuries or illnesses, there are 135.7 cases per 10,000 people, and 42.3% experience depression lasting over ten days.

7. Nursing Assistants, Orderlies, Attendants

If you think nurses are frequently injured or sick, this group experiences unhealthy conditions even more frequently. In 2011, the incidence rate was 443.9 cases per 10,000 people, with approximately 20% being falls, trips, and slips, while 56% of the time it was due to "overexertion."

8. Nursing Home Workers

Nursing home workers are also prone to depression. According to Health magazine, in the past year, compared to the general population's 7% incidence rate, these workers had an incidence rate of about 11%.

9. Cashiers

Is working as a cashier dangerous? Indeed, it is. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor, 20% of cashiers need to take time off due to non-fatal injuries or illnesses. In 2011 (the numbers for 2012 are not yet available), 27.8% took 31 days or more off. Of course, these leaves are unpaid. If you've ever heard a cashier say, "This job is making me sick," they really mean it.

10. Fast Food Workers

Fast food workers face two types of issues: first, they constantly need to manage their expressions and emotions. Health magazine states that, generally speaking, food industry workers are among the most depressed nationwide. Everyone tells them what to do, and they often operate under time pressure. A health study shows that approximately 10% of food service personnel have experienced severe depression. Although fast food work has become one of the three most common jobs nationwide, their average annual salary in 2012 was only $18,720. Even cashiers earn an average annual salary of $21,000, which explains why fast food workers are so downcast.

11. Financial Advisors and Accountants

According to Health magazine, this is another industry with high rates of depression. Some may wonder, how can dealing with money lead to sadness? First, you need to understand that this money belongs to others. If you're helping wealthy clients, you can only stand at the edge of the material world and envy silently. Mental health counselor Deborah Legge told Health magazine, "The responsibility and conflicting feelings of handling other people's finances are quite tormenting."
