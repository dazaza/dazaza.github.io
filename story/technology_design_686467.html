Gold Price - QE3 Expectation Fails, Big Fall in Gold Triggers Investment Banks' Bet on Carry Trade

by yanlaban on 2012-03-02 09:47:42

Chen Zhi

On March 1, the price of the April contract of COMEX gold futures plummeted by 4.37%. Was the trigger actually Federal Reserve Chairman Bernanke?

In the eyes of Chen Wei (a pseudonym), a trader at a domestic commodity investment private equity fund, it might indeed be so.

"Initially, I thought that the European Central Bank's second round of long-term refinancing operations (LTRO) worth 529.53 billion euros would help push the price of COMEX gold futures to $1800 per ounce. But Bernanke did not mention the third round of quantitative easing policy (QE3) in his testimony to Congress, instead causing the gold price to fall below $1700 per ounce that night."

Who is orchestrating this sharp drop in gold prices? Speculators, hedge funds, and investment banks all seem to have their own calculations.

Did speculative dollar buying pressure contribute to the decline in gold prices?

"After Bernanke's speech, the price of the April contract of COMEX gold futures fell from $1780 to as low as $1688 per ounce," Chen Wei recalled. The news coming from the financial markets at the time was that speculators, based on the difficulty of implementing QE3 in the short term, were aggressively buying up the dollar exchange rate, dragging down the gold price.

Data from the U.S. Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) showed that for the week ending February 21, foreign exchange speculators had increased their net long dollar positions for two consecutive weeks, with an amount as high as $6.62 billion.

In the view of a gold trader at an American bank, speculators betting on the rise of the dollar first believed that the negotiations for Greece's second round of €130 billion aid package would be fraught with difficulties, which would increase demand for dollar safe-haven assets. Secondly, recent favorable U.S. economic and employment data led them to reduce the probability of QE3 being introduced. "Bernanke's testimony to Congress confirmed that speculators had bet on the right direction."

On the evening of March 1, under the impetus of speculative buying, the dollar index once rose by 0.73%, the largest single-day increase in the past month.

At the same time, "some hedge funds also joined the ranks of those shorting gold," the trader revealed. Hedge funds had different reasons for shorting gold: some viewed the second round of LTRO as a European version of quantitative easing monetary policy, using it to buy up the dollar and short the euro and gold futures for dual arbitrage; others, before the redemption period in March, wanted to cash out part of their gold profits first.

"In the past two months, COMEX gold futures have risen by nearly 15%, and hedge funds need to make a great escape with their large profits," he analyzed. Before the second round of LTRO, some hedge funds had set their profit-taking level for COMEX gold futures at $1800 per ounce, but when they saw the gold price hovering around $1790 per ounce unable to resume its upward trend, they turned to shorting gold to lock in gains.

"It was precisely these three forces working together that caused the April contract of COMEX gold futures to break through key support levels of $1720, $1750, and $1780 per ounce, triggering a large number of programmatic trading stop-loss sell orders for gold, ultimately causing the gold price to fall by nearly $100 per ounce in just three hours," the trader revealed.

The Investment Banks' Calculations

However, behind the sharp fall in gold futures, there seemed to be an invisible hand manipulating events.

CFTC data showed that in February, the net short position in gold held by gold swap dealers, mainly investment banks, increased from 29,364 contracts to 35,819 contracts, making them the institution that added the most gold positions during that period. Based on the maximum decline of COMEX gold futures on March 1 approaching $100 per ounce, the additional net short position in gold held by investment banks generated approximately $358 million in dividends that day.

"Unlike most institutions that expected the second round of LTRO to boost COMEX gold prices to $1800 per ounce, investment banks instead saw this as an opportunity for profit-taking," the head of the commodities department at one investment bank revealed. This was mainly based on the observation that Indian gold demand and prices stabilized in February with the rebound in the Indian rupee, making it unlikely for gold prices to continue rising significantly in the short term due to supply-demand balance.

In the view of Credit Suisse analyst Tom Kendall, the scale of the second round of LTRO funding did not exceed market expectations by much. If COMEX gold futures prices broke above $1800 per ounce and then consolidated after retreating, it would not be surprising.

"However, the decline in gold prices in February by investment banks has some connection with the narrowing interest rate differential in current gold carry trade," the investment bank official revealed. Large banks participating in gold carry trades usually borrow gold from central banks at the London Interbank Gold Forward Rate (GOFO), then sell it at market prices to obtain dollars. They invest these dollars at the London Interbank Offered Rate (LIBOR) in government bonds or short-term bills for financing, achieving interest rate differentials. The reference value of LIBOR-GOFO is often seen as a benchmark for judging the profitability of gold carry trades. Since February, the reference value of LIBOR-GOFO for one-year gold carry trades has fallen from 0.4527% to 0.38305%.

"One shouldn't underestimate the seven basis point decline. With investment banks' gold carry trade scale potentially exceeding hundreds of billions of dollars, their profits from gold carry trades could decrease by about $70 million," he emphasized. Some investment banks can only increase their short positions on COMEX gold prices to maintain the expected returns from gold carry trades. Bernanke's sudden cessation of hinting at QE3 gave investment banks the opportunity to lower gold prices while the dollar rebounded and profit from it.

I learned that some traders in the proprietary trading departments of investment banks increased their short positions on gold on March 1, but the traces of short selling were relatively hidden. "Mainly, when COMEX gold reached a certain key support level, they suddenly used 30-40 times leverage financing to short gold futures, thereby triggering a large number of programmatic trading stop-loss sell orders for gold futures," he analyzed. "Since high-frequency trading accounts for nearly 30% of the transaction volume of COMEX gold futures, this approach can achieve a 'four ounces moves a thousand pounds' effect, and it's less likely for the SEC to detect whether there is any price manipulation."

The confidence of investment banks in shorting gold futures also comes from their quiet reduction in the likelihood of the Fed introducing QE3 in the near future.

"This is mainly due to the consideration of recent good U.S. economic data and clearer signs of economic recovery," Zeng Shaoko, head of the Retail Business Department of Legg Mason Asset Management China, told me in an analysis. As long as the QE3 monetary policy remains temporarily suspended, it provides enough room for imagination for those who are bearish on gold to engage in short-selling arbitrage.
