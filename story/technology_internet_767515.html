Amazon Employees: High Stress, Difficult Promotions

by anonymous on 2013-10-20 18:32:50

Editor's note: Senior writer for Bloomberg Businessweek, Brad Stone, recently published a new book titled *The Everything Store: Jeff Bezos and the Age of Amazon*. Over the course of more than two years spent researching and interviewing numerous Amazon employees for this book, Stone gained an in-depth understanding of the immense pressure that Amazon employees face due to the company’s highly competitive internal environment and the difficulty of career advancement. In a recent article, Stone provides insight into Amazon's internal system.

The following is the main content of the article:

While Amazon employees often enthusiastically discuss their direct impact on products and customers, they also frequently express frustration and stress over the company’s fiercely competitive culture and relentless work pace.

Amazon declined to comment on the author's new book or the internal system mentioned in Bloomberg Businessweek. However, during interviews with frontline employees, the author often heard complaints about receiving too little positive feedback from superiors and even fewer opportunities for promotion — perhaps an inevitable result of Amazon's internal system.

CEO Jeff Bezos believes that his managers must raise the bar for every hire and promotion, ensuring only top-tier talent can advance within the company. This belief has been institutionalized into a corporate practice known as the "Organization and Leadership Review" (OLR), which is closely tied to employee promotions.

The OLR involves senior leaders from various Amazon departments meeting twice a year to discuss the strengths and weaknesses of their subordinates. Based on these discussions, they approve promotions for some individuals and sometimes identify underperforming employees for termination. An internal Amazon document states: "The OLR gives us the opportunity to identify future leaders and help them prepare for new challenges. ... The bottom 10% of least effective employees will be dealt with appropriately to maintain Amazon's high standards of employee performance."

In the eyes of many Amazon employees, the OLR is a meeting that can make or break their careers in the blink of an eye. At Amazon, getting promoted isn't as simple as working hard for years, asking your boss for a raise or promotion, and having them nod in agreement. For example, if your boss is a vice president, they would need to attend the OLR meeting and present materials related to your promotion for other vice presidents to review. These executives then engage in discussions, where other VPs may share their experiences working with you. These discussions can sometimes turn into heated debates because there are limited annual promotion slots. If you get promoted, another executive’s favorite might have to stay put — anyone in the room could potentially derail your promotion.

Bezos implemented this harsh promotion system to avoid internal political struggles and unnecessary escalations, as well as to uniformly permeate corporate values across the entire company. However, many Amazon employees complain that the OLR system has instead led to internal political struggles contrary to its original intent. Ambitious employees often start having lunches and coffees with their boss's colleagues months before the OLR to ensure a smooth promotion path.

Some long-time Amazon employees also complain that what determines their career fate isn't necessarily their outstanding job performance but rather whether their boss has strong persuasive skills — newly appointed managers often fail to promote anyone for several years. Additionally, the OLR meetings tend to focus more on workplace gossip about employees rather than concrete performance data. It is well-known that analyzing sales data and adjusting operations accordingly is one of Amazon's strengths, but when it comes to employee promotions, subjective feelings and internal struggles often play a decisive role.

Besides the OLR, another reason for the difficulty in advancing internally at Amazon is the relatively flat hierarchy — the vast company has only 10 "levels":

Levels 2 and 3: Workers at Amazon’s fulfillment centers who are paid by the hour. To the author’s knowledge, Amazon does not have a "Level 1".

Level 4: Typically new hires with bachelor's degrees.

Level 5: Project managers or product managers.

Level 6: Senior product managers responsible for managing a specific product or feature.

Level 7: Typically senior managers with master's degrees responsible for overseeing Level 5 and Level 6 employees. Starting at this level, employees undergo several weeks of retraining in customer service call centers or operational centers every few years.

Level 8: Directors.

Level 10: Vice Presidents.

Level 11: Senior Vice Presidents and members of Amazon's core leadership team.

Level 12: Jeff Bezos himself.

No Amazon employee the author encountered knew why there was no Level 9. Some speculated that it might be due to oversight when establishing the levels, filling gaps between the highest and lowest tiers; others believed it was intentional, avoiding excessive bureaucracy by omitting a position like "Senior Director".

The author believes Amazon deliberately omitted a level to make climbing the corporate ladder harder for employees — removing a rung from the ladder might just be a good way to do so. (Yoola)
