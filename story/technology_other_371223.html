Conditions for Earthworm Farming

To achieve high yields in earthworm farming, it is necessary to research and familiarize oneself with the living habits of the species being farmed as well as their required ecological conditions, understanding the impact these conditions have on the worms. Earthworms require suitable ecological conditions for normal growth, development, and reproduction. The specific conditions required can vary between different species of earthworms. This technique focuses specifically on introducing the living habits and ecological conditions of the new hybrid Daping No. 3 earthworm developed at our company's base.

1. Humidity

Earthworms do not possess specialized respiratory organs; they breathe through their skin, so their bodies must remain moist. If placed in a dry environment, the worm’s skin will eventually lose its ability to maintain moisture, preventing normal respiration and causing the worm to experience spasms, leading to death shortly after. Water constitutes a significant portion of an earthworm's body, accounting for over 75% of its weight. Therefore, preventing water loss is crucial for the survival of earthworms. However, overly wet soil is also detrimental to their growth and development.

Earthworms have a temporary tolerance for unfavorable humidity conditions. To survive, they sometimes move to more suitable environments or enter dormancy, reducing metabolic activity to minimize water loss.

2. Temperature

Earthworms are ectothermic animals, meaning their body temperature changes with the external environmental temperature. As such, their dependence on the environment is generally more pronounced than that of endothermic animals. Environmental temperature not only affects the earthworm's body temperature and activity but also influences their metabolism, growth, development, and reproduction. Additionally, temperature has a significant impact on other living conditions, indirectly affecting the worms. Thus, temperature is one of the most important living conditions for earthworms.

Generally, the active temperature range for earthworms is between 5-30°C. They enter dormancy below 0-5°C and die below 0°C. The most suitable temperature for optimal growth, development, and reproduction is around 20-27°C. At 28-30°C, they can sustain some growth, but growth stops above 32°C. Activity slows below 10°C, and they die above 40°C. The ideal hatching temperature for cocoons is 18-27°C. It is evident that the lethal temperature threshold for earthworms is lower than that of other invertebrates. Indoor farms are preferable, especially in southern regions where ventilation and humidity control in summer and window insulation in winter allow for year-round production (the temperature here refers to the bedding material, which differs from air temperature. For example, when the air temperature is 0°C, the bedding temperature is around 12°C; when the air temperature is 38°C, the bedding temperature is around 28°C due to the high water content in the manure).

3. Ventilation

Earthworms breathe oxygen diffused from the atmosphere into the soil. Better soil ventilation leads to more vigorous metabolism, resulting in more egg capsules produced and a shorter maturation period. Boxed farming offers the highest yield because the boxes allow ventilation on all sides. Earthworms cannot inhabit environments with high levels of carbon dioxide, methane, fluorine, or hydrogen sulfide. Excessive hydrogen sulfide or methane can cause earthworms to flee or even result in mass death. In some northern areas, fire stoves are used inside earthworm farms for warmth, but leaks of smoke containing harmful gases like sulfur dioxide, sulfur trioxide, and carbon monoxide can lead to mass deaths of worms. During feed fermentation, harmful gases such as carbon dioxide, ammonia, hydrogen sulfide, and methane are produced, which can be toxic to worms if they reach certain concentrations. Tests show that ammonia levels exceeding 20 parts per million cause increased mucus secretion and cluster death in worms, while hydrogen sulfide levels exceeding 15 parts per million can cause neurological diseases leading to death. Methane levels exceeding 15 parts per million can cause blood leakage and death. Therefore, feeds should be fully fermented before feeding. Fermented feed should ideally be turned and left for some time before feeding.

4. Food

Food is a long-term, critical ecological factor affecting earthworms. Insufficient food causes intense competition among worms, especially in high-density farming situations, leading to increased competition for food, reduced reproductive capacity, spreading of pests and diseases, increased mortality, and escape of some worms. The effect of food on worms is not only about quantity but also quality. For instance, worms fed on livestock manure produce significantly more cocoons—up to hundreds of times more—than those fed on coarse feed like wild grass. Feeding worms with decayed or fermented animal-based organic matter produces better results than plant-based organic matter. Worms fed nitrogen-rich food (like livestock manure) grow and reproduce better than those fed nitrogen-poor food (like straw).

In this technique, soilless farming essentially means feeding the worms entirely with food, replacing unnecessary soil portions and reducing much of the material management. The entire farming box becomes food, reducing competition and allowing higher farming density.

5. pH Levels

Earthworms are sensitive to both acidity and alkalinity, as their bodies are covered with chemical receptors capable of detecting acids and bases. Worms cannot survive in strongly acidic or strongly alkaline environments but have some adaptability to weakly acidic or strongly alkaline conditions. Daping No. 2 worms thrive in pH ranges of 6-8, producing the most cocoons in the range of 7-7.5.

It is important to note that strong acids like sulfuric acid, hydrochloric acid, and nitric acid, as well as strong bases like sodium hydroxide and quicklime, should not be used to adjust the pH of worm feed. Only weak bases and acids can be used as neutralizers:

Alkaline Neutralizer: Calcium carbonate, etc.; Acidic Neutralizer: Organic acids (acetic acid, citric acid, etc.)

For specific testing methods, refer to the fly larva farming technology content.

6. Density

Density refers to the number of earthworms per unit area or volume. The size of the farming density greatly affects environmental changes, impacting overall worm yield and cost. Low density means less individual competition, leading to greater proliferation per worm, but lower overall yield across the area, requiring more labor and resources. High density, however, leads to insufficient food and oxygen, excessive accumulation of metabolic products, environmental pollution, crowded living spaces, intensified competition, slower growth, reduced fertility, spread of pests and diseases, higher mortality, and escape of survivors. Therefore, mastering the optimal farming density is key to achieving the best efficiency.

The farming density of earthworms is closely related to the type of worm, life stage, farming environment (such as food, farming method, and container), and management technology level. Box farming allows for the highest density. In a one-square-meter area with a 25 cm high substrate, the following densities can be maintained: 15,000-20,000 breeding worms, 80,000-100,000 worms up to half a month old, and 30,000-65,000 worms from half a month to adulthood. Increasing the density beyond these limits will restrict normal growth, development, and reproduction, reducing yield. Therefore, timely expansion of the farming bed, adjustment of density, and removal of adult worms are effective measures to increase yield.

7. Salinity

Experiments have been conducted on the tolerance of earthworms to salinity. William ringworms were placed in solutions of 0.02%, 0.1%, 0.4%, 0.8%, and 1.6% saltwater, and 0.01%, 0.02%, 0.04%, and 0.08% copper sulfate solutions, observing the time until death. Worms in 0.8% and 1.6% saltwater died after 145 minutes and 52 minutes respectively, while worms in the other salt solutions survived for 24 hours, showing some tolerance. Worms in all four copper sulfate solutions died within 50, 76, 75, and 57 minutes respectively, indicating the strong toxic effects of copper sulfate on worms. Therefore, attention should be paid to the effects of salinity on worms during farming, especially preventing harm from pesticides and fertilizers.

8. Light

Although earthworms lack eyes, their bodies are covered with many light-sensitive cells, concentrated more at the front (preoral lobe) and back ends. Blue light stimulates worms, red light does not, and ultraviolet light is harmful. Therefore, farming sites should avoid direct sunlight, preferably indoors with a red light bulb illuminated.
