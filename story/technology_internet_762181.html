Zhengzhou workers outside the production line

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:09:17

Labor costs are a major part of business expenses. To cut down on labor costs, companies either have to lay off employees or move their operations to places where salaries are lower. Foxconn has done just that, moving from Shenzhen to Zhengzhou, which is gradually becoming a new manufacturing hub. In this area, factories operate 24 hours a day producing international goods like the iPhone. Here, young Chinese workers refuse to be mere "worker ants."

Near the factory, there's a bar where people gather in the evening to dance to loud disco music and let loose. Lian Yulong (音), a 19-year-old inspector who works on the iPhone motherboard at Foxconn, is a regular at the bar. "Dancing helps me vent my anger and stress. Whenever I'm here, I forget everything else." He dances to escape the monotony and dullness of his daily life.

The increasing availability of recreational spaces attracts workers back to the assembly lines. These workers manufacture products that others desire but endure hardships themselves. Because opportunities are rare, their rest time is precious, and they endure the present for a better future. Each worker looks forward to the weekend with excitement, even if their plans are simple. For example, Bai Si Hai (音), a 24-year-old worker, plans to play games online and then call his girlfriend who lives far away.

Now, supervisors in the factory are beginning to see the benefits of taking breaks. In recent years, "worker suicides" have been an ongoing issue haunting companies like Foxconn and Samsung. After several reports exposed "sweatshop" conditions, Foxconn improved workers' benefits and promised not to make them work overtime. However, after another suicide incident at the Zhengzhou plant, Foxconn implemented a "silent mode," prohibiting workers from discussing non-work topics during shifts. Although Foxconn claims to have canceled this rule under external scrutiny, workers say things remain the same as before.

The working environment in domestic factories cannot compare to those abroad. Overseas workers enjoy high allowances and perks such as haircuts, rock climbing walls, table football, etc., along with luxurious offices. In contrast, domestic workers wear uniform clothing and listen to routine scolding from their supervisors. Their dormitories contain eight hard metal bed frames in each room and a combined bathroom/toilet space, nothing more.

Perhaps because of this, a large market has formed outside the factory gates. As the sun sets, Mandarin pop music drifts out from hair salons, street vendors sell DVDs, sliced watermelons, and roses covered in glitter. A flatbed truck displays colorful teddy bears, drawing the attention of young girls. Some boyfriends, upon their girlfriends' instructions, quickly buy one for 10 RMB. In this marketplace, there are art-decorated vans and large arcade games. Young workers drink light beer and smoke continuously.

Occasionally, elaborately dressed singers perform on nearby stages. As for the youth, they find ample entertainment in liquor. Luo Haojie (音), who left the factory in May to quit his job making iPhone 5 components, said, "Our factory supervisor is vicious." He complained about the unbearable food in the factory cafeteria to a circle of drinking friends.

Ultimately, he will look for another job, but for now, he enjoys his youth, drinking with friends and dating girls. Luo said, "I stayed because of my brothers. Without them, I would be miserable."

Summer is the off-season for Chinese factories, allowing many workers to enjoy weekends off, sometimes even double days. The diverse entertainment options near the factory satisfy their needs. At night, mobile food stalls pop up with names resembling KFC. Acrobats perform, and someone blows fireballs. Workers say it would be better to come a few months earlier when the acrobatic troupe had real lions and tigers.

On Saturday, another iPhone 5 motherboard tester, Zhou Peng Zheng (音), wearing roller skates worth over a thousand RMB, carefully avoided plants and began spinning. These skates cost a third of his monthly salary. He said, "It feels like I'm flying." Before the crowd gathered, he hurriedly skated another lap.

Roller skating culture is growing among those striving at Foxconn. Several teams have formed, and on weekends, they glide through the city in their skates. Fang Ti Ma (音), 17, joined a hundred-person skating team called "Shadows" shortly after starting work at Foxconn. However, due to her age, she couldn't work overtime and eventually left the factory. She said, "I used to come to the skating rink twice a week; now I come every night."

By 11 PM, the performers have left, and clock hotels begin to get busy. "We work every day, so why not go on dates?"
