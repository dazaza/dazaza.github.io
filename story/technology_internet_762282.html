"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:39:46

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of executives, received another blow. On July 17th, it was reported that according to insiders at Yougou.com, COO Zhang Xiaojun will be leaving and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun's departure is more indicative of serious crises that Yougou.com is indeed facing.

Let’s first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, to which Yougou.com belongs:

Belle quietly tested the waters of e-commerce several years ago, developing steadily through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed through Taoxiu.com). Led by group veteran Xie Yunli, a not-so-luxurious e-commerce team initially sold products under Belle's own brands, then gradually launched exclusive online models, followed by new online brands. This development path became a successful case study before 2011, regarded as a more solid approach for traditional brand companies entering the e-commerce space compared to Li-Ning.

With the rise of Letao.com and HaoLeMai receiving over $100 million in financing and rapidly developing in 2010, Belle Group, which supplied these two platforms, felt uneasy. On one hand, there was concern about being controlled by distribution channels again; on the other hand, after evaluation, they realized their significant development advantages in the e-commerce field, including:

Firstly, numerous brands: In addition to Belle's several women's shoe brands, the agency-sold international sports brands—Adidas, Nike, etc.—experienced astonishing growth in online sales;

Secondly, easier category expansion: For Belle Group, known for its women's shoe brands and agency-sold renowned sports brands, how to gain absolute market advantage and status through multiple brands and categories had been the most thought-provoking issue in recent years. Expanding brands and categories offline through opening stores obviously took too long, while the ongoing e-commerce business could easily expand brands but couldn't quickly add categories;

Thirdly, financial advantage: During cooperation with e-commerce enterprises, Belle discovered that e-commerce is capital-intensive. With its financial strength, Belle could develop without relying on capital, allowing it to outlast many vertical e-commerce businesses dependent on capital, becoming the "survivor who reigns supreme";

Fourthly, talent advantage: Through years of e-commerce practice, Belle already possessed a highly professional and strongly execution-oriented e-commerce team. The cultivation of talent could meet the scale development needs of the e-commerce business or even exceed them;

Fifthly, supply chain advantage: This excited Belle internally and was also the core reason attracting high-level executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, for e-commerce, supply chain management always captivates because it seems like the part determining life and death aside from funding. The supply chain includes a series of "business" contents such as product research and development, production, warehouse management, delivery, and after-sales service. In traditional enterprises that prioritize "business" over "e-commerce," this is considered the greatest competitive advantage over e-commerce enterprises.

On the basis of these advantages, Belle's e-commerce decided to make a big move, starting with purchasing the double-character domain yougou.com,

Abandoning the slightly imitation-style "topshoes.cn" Taoxiu website for redirection, stopping supplies to all distribution websites, while still maintaining direct-operated third-party online stores like Taobao and Tmall Mall, but reducing investment and attention. They recruited JD.com VP and Vancl VP with high salaries and fanfare to serve as CMO and COO respectively, and dispatched a group elder statesman as CEO, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to create a vertically integrated B2C mall covering all categories, implementing multi-brand strategies on one hand, expanding into clothing and other categories on the other, realizing the dream of a department store empire impossible to achieve offline!

Everything sounded perfect, but every dream needs to face the harsh reality of its backbone. The actual situation is:

Firstly, higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Belle in Shenzhen headquarters cannot recruit more excellent technical talents, and technical outsourcing service companies are scarce in Shenzhen. But technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C belongs to dynamic technology, different from relatively static technologies like ERP. Continuous changes remain a hurdle for many traditional enterprises;

Secondly, increased management difficulty due to category expansion: Someone once said doing well in women's shoes and men's shoes is difficult, something Belle must have deeply understood when expanding into men's shoes. Expanding into women's clothing and accessories within a year can only imagine the difficulties the backend would encounter. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous calculations, this rapid approach worked during special periods for Fanke, understandable within Fanke and JD.com's systems. Although Belle had Fanke's COO and JD.com's CMO, whether they could be understood and executed with "exaggerated costs" remains a big question!

Thirdly, system: Many people outside e-commerce and traditional enterprises find this hard to understand. Simply put, it refers to departmental settings (organizational structure), approvals, finance and budget, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, processes and systems that constitute an enterprise's so-called "mechanism." The conflict results in e-commerce trying to persuade traditional sectors to accept the "system" of e-commerce, while traditional teams attempt to understand but can never fully grasp the "system" of e-commerce, leading to low efficiency and unstable execution teams. Ultimately, the chairman or board might say, "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have strengths and weaknesses, and the future is certainly integration, so you must integrate in these areas..." If this so-called "integration without harmony" occurs, it leads to so-called management innovation, and surprisingly, "CEO rotation system" appeared in a company like Belle!

Originally, it was believed that Yougou.com of Belle's e-commerce had taken the lead, and the top executives were waiting for spin-off listings to enjoy success, fame, and wealth, becoming countless traditional enterprises' role model for success in the e-commerce era. However, instead, they are now entangled and distressed by these hard injuries.

After all, whether it be the veterans who grew up with Belle or the poached e-commerce professionals, it is clear they did not leave due to lack of ability. When the VP from JD.com returns to JD.com, they continue to perform admirably, and when the VP from Fanke has the courage to start their own business. Are these hard injuries the three points mentioned above? Or are these not just Belle's hard injuries but also the hard injuries of many traditional enterprises attempting to enter e-commerce?
