The mobile phone is an ocean, and it's hard for HTC to "position" itself.

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:19:21

“Positioning is an expression that originates from the mind; you need to figure out how to capture the mind.”

Nowadays, we talk a lot about fan economy, monetizing influence, and so on. In fact, all of these are related to the vague impressions in consumers' minds, which is precisely the issue discussed in *Positioning*.

From the perspective of *Positioning*, HTC’s marketing strategy has room for improvement.

HTC One

An example is the HTC One. First, its name is mediocre, too similar to the previous flagship product, the HTC One X. This makes it difficult for people to associate the HTC One with the new technological advantages of the product. Moreover, it easily reminds people of the shortcomings of the HTC One X. “Good news doesn’t travel far, but bad news spreads a thousand miles,” bad news tends to be more memorable, which is human nature.

Next, let’s look at the product positioning of the HTC One—neither on the homepage nor in the promotional video can we find a single sentence that summarily describes this flagship product.

The aforementioned video emphasizes many technical advantages and features of the HTC One, such as the metal casing, high PPI screen, and sound quality, etc. But there are too many, and our brains can only remember a limited amount of information.

The result is that we don’t know what comparative advantages the HTC One actually has. In other words, the functions it promotes all seem good, but they fail to leave an impression in our minds.

Let’s take a look at the efforts other manufacturers have made in terms of positioning:

Photography: Lumia 1020

Sound Quality:步步高 (BBK)

Photography phone for women: OPPO, MeituKiss

Almost all manufacturers are working hard on differentiated positioning, hoping to find the right consumer group through precise positioning in an increasingly saturated smartphone market. However, HTC One’s promotion is not like this; it uses slogans like "completely fulfilling your imagination," which are broad and comprehensive.

In short, “In communication, more is less.” Being outstanding in everything means being mediocre in everything.

*Positioning* says, “Being first is the shortcut to entering the mind.” However, if a brand isn’t the leader in the market, it needs to adopt a "finding empty spaces" marketing strategy. “Find the empty space, then fill it up.”

HTC

“Once you own a word in the customer's mind, you need to use it, or you will lose it.”

Before officially entering the Chinese market, HTC sold its phones under the name “Dopod” and accumulated a fairly good brand reputation. Most importantly, at that time, “Dopod” was synonymous with “smartphone” in the public eye.

This positioning was very advantageous because even Nokia, which had the highest mobile phone sales at the time, hadn’t managed to occupy such a favorable “position.” However, HTC eventually decided to sacrifice the “Dopod” brand and change the product name, which was equivalent to repositioning.

As a result, HTC spent a great deal of effort and money to gradually make the public aware of the simple fact that “HTC = Dopod.”

Tragically, HTC wasn’t better than Dopod. It’s an abbreviation of English initials, not connected to any meaningful words, lacks a catchy pronunciation, and is hard to remember.

Now, the three-letter abbreviation HTC is interpreted as “Here To Change,” while the previous slogan “Quietly Brilliant” was more vivid. However, its Chinese translation remains a big problem. “Hongda Electric” gives off a mechanical feeling, not as smooth as “Dopod,” nor does it evoke positive associations.

Fortunately, after years of promotion, people now recognize the HTC brand. But if there were a better Chinese meaning, it would be even better. After all, doing so aligns with HTC's current marketing strategy of treating Asia, Europe, and America as separate markets.

But, please stop interpreting it as “sausage.” Why link a tech brand with a common food item?
