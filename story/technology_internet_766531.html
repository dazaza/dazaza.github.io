Apple: I don't care about market share

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:44:48

Apple's newly released iPhone 5C last week was a disappointment because it wasn't cheap. People had hoped that this phone would gain more market share for Apple, but in Apple's eyes, market share has never been an indicator they cared about; "practicality", "commercialization", and "customer satisfaction" are what they pursue. Below is a summary of the article:

Last week, Apple's product launch explained one thing - Apple will not be chasing market share. For that much-anticipated launch, people were looking forward to the arrival of Apple's cheaper version of the iPhone, which would significantly increase the market share of Apple phones.

However, Apple did not release a cheaper version of the iPhone. Instead, it continued with its pricing strategy from the past few years: the contract price for the iPhone 5C is $99, while the standalone price is $549 in the US and around $733 in China. In China, the high price of the iPhone 5C was indeed disappointing. At $733, it is twice the price of a mid-range Android phone. For most Chinese people, this phone is unaffordable.

Apple's market share in China is currently declining. According to Reuters, Apple's market share in China is only 5%, ranking it seventh. The smartphone market in developed countries like the US is also approaching saturation. If Apple intends to increase its share in markets like China, a price of $733 won't attract more Chinese users.

Although Apple's decision to maintain its original pricing strategy came as a surprise, Apple has always been very clear that market share is not an indicator they care about. "For Apple, success has never been about gaining the largest market share," said Apple CEO Tim Cook. "Market share has never been the cornerstone of Apple's survival."

Because Apple's late founder Steve Jobs was "famous" for "saying one thing and doing another," many people now don't believe what Cook says. They think Apple should honor its promise and release a cheaper version of the iPhone. Is Cook foolish? As internet analyst Henry Blodget pointed out, is he a short-sighted greedy person?

I believe there are two reasons why people are so focused on Apple's smartphone market share: first, people think Apple will be defeated by Android. Second, people see that Apple has $145 billion in cash, so Apple doesn't need to rush for profit.

First, let's analyze the second reason. Most tech companies today offer lower prices to consumers. For example, Amazon released a super-cheap tablet. Google opened up its operating system, and Facebook and Twitter don't charge fees.

Especially Amazon, whose business philosophy resonates with consumers. Amazon's operating profit is almost zero, and every dollar it earns is reinvested or "subsidized" to consumers through ultra-low prices. In an era of economic instability and income inequality, Amazon's strategy of redistributing its wealth seems rare.

On the other hand, Apple adopts a completely opposite business strategy. This company holds $146 billion in cash, and Apple plans to return $100 billion to shareholders, which is equivalent to Apple admitting they have no good ideas on how to use this money to improve their products.

The iPhone does not offer discounts, making Apple look like a stingy rich person. It owns hundreds of billions of dollars in cash, but it refuses to share with others. Imagine if you, who earn little, dine with a friend worth millions, and he refuses to pay the bill - wouldn't that make you feel very annoyed?

At Apple's product launch, Apple stated that the sales of its iOS devices will exceed 700 million. If Apple chose to "subsidize" consumers with $100 billion instead of returning it to shareholders, it would mean that the prices of all sold iPads, iPhones, and iPods could be reduced by $142.86. However, Apple doesn't want to do this. It wants to create a high-end product that can be sold at a high price.

For Apple's profit-centered strategy, industry insiders say that Apple will fail in the mobile field just as it did in the PC industry. But most people don't know what exactly happened in the PC industry.

In the 1980s, the reason for the failure of Apple computers was that Apple's products were too expensive and not very excellent. Apple computers didn't even have a market share. Microsoft swept the entire PC market with its operating system, developers flocked to Microsoft PC software, and Apple became a "cloud".

Now, Apple has the best hardware and the best software. At least in the US, its pricing is on par with Android phones. Although Android leads in market share, developers haven't abandoned Apple's iOS system. In fact, the opposite is true. From the perspective of app development, more developers choose iOS over Android. For example, Twitter decided to develop its own video-sharing app Vine, which initially only appeared on the iOS platform. Instagram's early success was also related to focusing on the iOS platform.

The reason developers still choose iOS is that iOS has very active users who frequently download apps and consume on their phones. Apple stated that the indicators they focus on are "practicality", "commercialization", and customer satisfaction. As long as these indicators improve, Apple believes they will surely succeed.

However, producing a cheaper iPhone wouldn't help improve these indicators. Does Apple want to become a niche market participant? Maybe.

Despite Android having a large market share, it hasn't managed to widely promote NFC technology. Google tried to incorporate NFC into phones and also attempted to include Google Wallet in phones, but these efforts didn't yield very good results.

Why? Because Apple doesn't care about NFC technology. If Apple truly supported NFC, it would definitely become a "big technology".
