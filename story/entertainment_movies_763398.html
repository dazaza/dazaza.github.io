Introduction:

Released in 1961, with two stars, directed by Blake Edwards, based on the original work by Truman Capote, starring Audrey Hepburn, and featuring George Peppard, Martin Balsam, Patricia Neal, among others. The male lead is George Peppard, with music composed by Henry Mancini. The theme song "Moon River" is soft and captivating, enchanting fans worldwide and winning the Academy Award for Best Original Song.

This film is adapted from Truman Capote's novel and marks Audrey Hepburn's fourth nomination for the Oscar as Best Actress.

In the early morning hours, when Fifth Avenue in New York is deserted, Holly Golightly, dressed in a black evening gown with a fake jewelry necklace around her neck, stands alone in front of Tiffany's jewelry store. Her cheek presses against the shop window, holding a brown paper bag in her hand, eating a croissant from the bag while drinking hot coffee, admiring everything inside the Tiffany store with an envious gaze... However, what Holly truly desires is not breakfast but to enter the upper class that Tiffany represents. At the age of fourteen, she dreams of going to Mexico to raise horses with her beloved brother Fred. To achieve this dream and for money, she chases fame and fortune, mingling with handsome billionaires and political elites, becoming a famous socialite. For fifty dollars per hour, she even goes to the prison to "socialize" with the mafia boss until one day, a gentlemanly man moves in downstairs. His real name is Paul Varjak, dreaming of becoming a great writer one day, but relies on the "gifts" from wealthy women to survive.

Both being materialists, interestingly, as their interactions deepen, the distance between them shortens a little, and their inner worlds open up more. Gradually, a mutual understanding and affection blossoms between the two... As Paul Varjak falls in love, he reforms himself, no longer working as a gigolo, and confesses his love to Holly. Holly, however, announces the news of a Brazilian billionaire pursuing her. Soon, Holly invites Paul for breakfast and cooks personally.

Never having cooked before, Holly listens to Portuguese music while piling lettuce into a salad bowl as big as a washbasin, casually drizzling olive oil and red vinegar over it, then rhythmically mixing the three ingredients together.

Paul proposes to Holly, but she dodges the question, taking a bottle of wine from the fridge and saying she will marry the Brazilian man, although in reality, the Brazilian has not yet proposed to her. Paul proposes again, and she talks about preparing "Mole"-spiced chicken salad. Just as the pressure cooker begins to hiss, Holly leans forward to turn off the heat, only to hear an explosion. Chicken and broth splatter everywhere, and Holly instinctively clutches onto Paul. With plans to move to Brazil, Holly starts learning Portuguese and also Latin American cuisine. This failed "Mole-spiced chicken salad" seems to predict her dream — building a Brazilian farm and becoming a wealthy Brazilian housewife — will vanish like a bubble. However, how does this dramatic breakfast end?

At the moment of despair for Paul Varjak, an unexpected opportunity arises, bringing good fortune to their relationship. Holly is arrested by the police on suspicion of drug trafficking. In the end, Holly, wearing the ring engraved with Tiffany's name given to her by Paul, embraces her lost cat — or perhaps her lost romantic innocence — and Paul in the pouring rain. After all! What could be sweeter and more luxurious than sharing a "love breakfast" with the person you love?

"Breakfast at Tiffany's" is a famous American movie, hailed by critics as the "best comedy of the 1960s in America." Its charm remains undiminished after forty years, still attracting a large number of new viewers each year. The film is one of Audrey Hepburn's most notable performances, her lively and natural acting adding brilliance to the film. The scene where Hepburn sits on the fire escape singing "Moon River" is one of the most unforgettable moments in cinematic history.

As one of the top three box-office actresses of the 1960s (the other two being Doris Day and Julie Andrews), Hepburn's "Breakfast at Tiffany's" was one of the highest-grossing films of 1961 and also the most attended among all her films. The film won two Oscars at the 34th Academy Awards in 1961 for Best Music (Song) and Best Music (Score for Dramatic and Comedy Picture). The film's music also won the Grammy Award for Song of the Year that year.
