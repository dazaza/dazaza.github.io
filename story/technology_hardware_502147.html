The Impact of the Economic Crisis on Domestic Enterprises

Since September 2008, the U.S. financial market has experienced several shocks: Bank of America's acquisition of Merrill Lynch, AIG Group falling into crisis, and Lehman Brothers Holdings going bankrupt. These events strongly shook the U.S. financial market and caused massive waves in the international financial markets, turning the prolonged U.S. subprime mortgage crisis into a severe, century-defining, global financial and economic crisis. Since this financial crisis is still ongoing, to what extent will it grow? The enormous threat covers the globe and further affects enterprises at all levels in China.

Precisely due to concerns about the transition from financial crisis to economic crisis, domestic enterprises have successively or jointly adopted a series of measures to strengthen their resistance to the economic crisis so that they can survive through the first direct major threat since the reform and opening-up. In addition to directly enhancing capital turnover speed, drastically reducing management costs, and exercising cautious investment, these measures also involve adjustments to some enterprises' industrial and product structures. Those small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) that couldn't adjust in time were wiped out by this crisis. SMEs in Guangdong and Zhejiang provinces took the lead in "going" one step ahead. More than 30% of SMEs went bankrupt, over 30% struggled to survive, and more than 30% were in a marginal profit or unhealthy state. Only less than 10% of enterprises could operate normally due to resource or power reasons.

Such an enterprise survival situation forced most existing enterprises to take actions such as layoffs or salary cuts. Specific manifestations include various enterprises adopting layoffs or salary cuts. Large state-owned enterprises like Wuhan Iron and Steel (WISCO) and Baosteel are considering salary reductions, with WISCO employees expected to see a 20% cut and managers above section level facing a 50% reduction. Real estate giants Vanke and China Overseas Land & Investment are cutting salaries and laying off workers; aviation leaders Eastern Airlines and China Southern Airlines are doing the same; private sector stars like Bird and Amoi are being forced to reduce labor costs... Salary cuts and layoffs have affected industries such as real estate, aviation, petrochemicals, electricity, IT, securities, finance, and printing.

It is evident that the economic crisis triggered by the financial crisis has directly impacted the demand for human capital and its value reassessment in the market. Enterprises most affected by this economic and market instability have seen salary and bonus cuts and layoffs across all levels, from senior management to frontline workers. This will inevitably cause employee anxiety symptoms, leading to large-scale job-hopping or conservative behavior at work.

On the other hand, when the economy and market recover, the most talented individuals may be the first to leave. Therefore, despite the current environment, it remains crucial for companies to retain key talents to preserve and enhance their continuous competitive advantage. Effectively retaining top talent not only helps companies navigate difficult times but will also help build a competitive edge when better times return.

Strategies to Prevent Loss of Talents During the Economic Crisis

While making appropriate adjustments to human resource management, companies must balance actual operating cost pressures with the need to retain human resources and ensure the company’s continuous competitive advantage. Companies need to adapt to the new business environment brought by the economic crisis, construct a new framework for human resource management, formulate new strategies, and adopt new methods. To avoid going astray during the economic downturn and causing a series of thorny problems, companies can consider the following strategies:

Firstly, companies should enhance their social responsibility.

Corporate social responsibility should be the highest guiding principle for corporate growth. Premier Wen Jiabao once said, "Entrepreneurs should not only understand operations and management but also possess moral integrity." Corporate social responsibility should be integrated with the company's culture, values, and concepts, implemented in actual actions, and give back to society. As a significant entity in the market economy, companies must fulfill their social responsibilities in a unified, legal, honest, orderly, competitive, and open market economy. In fact, companies are socialized, personified citizens who enjoy rights and bear obligations towards society, the nation, and even the international community. To cope with difficulties, both domestic and international companies may take actions to reduce costs and improve productivity, but large-scale layoffs are detrimental to the良性cycle of economic growth. Companies should try to avoid drastic measures like large-scale layoffs to deal with the current economic crisis. With the sudden reversal of the economic situation, the market economy relies even more on trust to function; as creators of wealth, opportunities, and higher living standards, companies should display greater confidence in their moral standing and actively establish mutual trust between themselves and the entire society.

Secondly, when rewarding or punishing management teams, consider that some factors are beyond their control.

A company's operational performance is closely related to the capabilities of its management team. However, some factors that significantly impact operational performance and stock prices are beyond the control of managers, such as investor sentiment and overall market conditions. During economic downturns, some highly capable management teams undoubtedly receive unfair punishments. Therefore, during economic recessions, adjustments should be made to the performance evaluation methods of management teams, fully taking into account the influence of the overall market situation.

Thirdly, align the company's future growth vision with personal goals to retain people through careers.

To achieve the goal of retaining people through careers, leaders need to set a clear and defined growth objective and vision for the company, along with a practical growth plan. At the same time, companies should develop career plans for employees, especially key employees, integrating their personal growth and pursuit objectives into the company's growth plan. Even if the company has a clear future goal, if employees cannot clearly understand the relationship between their future growth and the company's future objectives, as well as their role in achieving those objectives, it will fail to serve as motivation. Therefore, employees should have clear goals and feel that they are "valuable, ambitious, and rewarded" within the company, willing to stay long-term.

Fourthly, enhance communication and interaction with employees, building a harmonious, people-oriented corporate culture.

Organize timely employee meetings, employee satisfaction surveys, employee interviews, etc. Share employees' work experiences over a period of time, promptly recognize and reward high-performing employees, and assist struggling employees in solving problems encountered in their work and lives. Truly establish a harmonious, people-oriented corporate culture. Emphasize emotional retention, increasing employees' loyalty to the company. Humans are emotional beings; company leaders must subtly understand employees' true needs and employ various incentive methods to promote the realization of organizational goals.

Additionally, while no company wants to lose key talents, employee turnover is an unavoidable issue.

In fact, turnover can also be managed effectively. The most important point in retaining key employees is preventing their ultimate departure through active communication. When employees first submit resignation applications, department supervisors and HR should immediately communicate with them. In many cases, at this point, many employees' intentions to resign are not yet very clear or firm; sometimes, it may be due to the stimulation of a particular event. If timely communication can resolve their temporary impulse, it often leads to employees withdrawing their resignation decision without things becoming irreparably strained. For those employees who decide to leave after careful consideration, sincere communication should be conducted to understand their true reasons for leaving, and concrete measures should be taken to examine whether the causes of their departure exist within the company, and take immediate action to prevent other excellent employees from leaving for the same reasons. In this way, it not only makes other employees feel valued by the company, giving them a sense of warmth and home, but also sends a signal to already departed excellent employees, encouraging their return.
