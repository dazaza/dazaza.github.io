Dianping: A Bone the Giants Can't Chew, Living in the Zone of Discomfort

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:58:44

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com, has once again postponed the company's IPO.

In 2011, after securing a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, Dianping’s valuation reached $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an appropriate time to go public within the next one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it secured another $60 million in Series D funding.

Given Dianping's scale and development momentum, going public would not be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that an IPO now would be too costly. He breaks down the big internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point presents opportunities for giants. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is the seat for the fourth giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating resources for this opportunity, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where mobile phones merge "location" and "service." Following Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the image of the fourth giant.

But outsiders don’t think so. In recent months, with BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent) frequently making acquisitions and strategic moves, Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; under any of the BAT brands, it could help them achieve great success early on. On the other hand, Dianping’s size pales in comparison to BAT, and it is premature to declare it as a future giant. Moreover, whether it’s Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, all are targeting Dianping’s territory — local life services centered around dining, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two things, Dianping's IPO would be equivalent to a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs time to demonstrate that what Dianping is doing now is something BAT cannot do. Second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." For all the speculation and rumors, Zhang Tao remains deaf to them. He is clear: "The internet has indeed entered a great era of convergence, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao divides companies that might be acquired into three categories. The first category consists of companies with platform attributes but are better suited to develop within the ecosystem of a giant. For example, maps and email services can easily attract large numbers of users, but it's hard for them to find independent business models. Some, like browsers, may make money, but the profits they generate through navigation services are far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money nurturing email products because they help with branding and user stickiness, but there hasn't been a successful standalone email company," he says. The second category involves industry mergers, such as in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third category includes companies facing financial difficulties, selling themselves to survive.

Now Meituan has already been brought under Alibaba's wing. Theoretically, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated due to competitors obtaining capital and expanding rapidly. Zhang Tao, however, says, "The giants offer us limited help. We indeed need users, but execution is even more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities, and we might even be marginalized by a large company." Not only that, he also aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would be akin to "taking sides," turning Dianping into an enemy of the others.

Thus, logically speaking, acquiring Dianping becomes a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why would we take their money? We're not short of cash; what we need are resources. If a giant were willing to take a small stake and provide us with many resources, I might consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they held only a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Tiring Work

In the mobile internet era, opportunities for pure internet product companies are shrinking. New opportunities lie in those that intersect with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that lifestyle services primarily revolve around dining, entertainment, and leisure. Other niche markets, such as ride-hailing, can form decent business models, but it's unlikely for any of them to grow into giants.

For dining, entertainment, and leisure, user needs are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is at the core of lifestyle services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in terms of content, member accumulation, or brand recognition, newcomers find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form for discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship match, and in 2013, Dianping will face off against Meituan. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as there are two strong players, the industry's gross margin will be pulled down, making stable profitability impossible.

A visible future lies in reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but they must be based on Dianping having an absolute advantage in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop is difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao says, "Currently, feedback from users and merchants is good, but if we did reservations alone, it would be hard to solve the chicken-and-egg problem."

Of course, lifestyle services present some even "heavier" opportunities. For instance, food delivery is a rigid demand for users and helps merchants increase sales. In the past couple of years, food delivery companies like "To Your Doorstep Gourmet" have thrived. However, the core of food delivery is distribution — delivering within a very short time frame while dealing with issues like soup spilling. The logistics requirements are higher than even fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao says, "If executed well, food delivery still holds opportunities, but it's harder dirty and tiring work, and Dianping is already doing enough dirty and tiring work."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant data and user reviews, which Zhang Tao calls "dirty and tiring work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but the pain of the past has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that, aside from Meituan, Dianping has no real rivals in the lifestyle services sector.

However, he still needs to beware of BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users who can use the map to search for nearby dining and other lifestyle services. Tencent, considered to have secured its place in mobile internet, has launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest physical goods trading platform, and expanding its offerings to include services is a natural progression, especially since it has incorporated Meituan and AutoNavi into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there haven't been any signs of explosive growth in reality or data," Zhang Tao says. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to dine, drink, or entertain themselves.

Moreover, in the vast field of lifestyle services, giant competition will inevitably overlap, and no one can completely monopolize the domain. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that in this way, Dianping is actually safer. "In segmented fields like car rentals and business travel, the giants' massive user base could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in AutoNavi, infiltrating Dianping's territory isn't that simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized no matter how peculiar. A user's experience of a meal depends on factors such as the environment, waitstaff, and chef on that particular day. Managing merchants cannot rely solely on systems and tools like Alibaba does; a 'ground force' must be established, managed by people.

"All three giants want to take this path, and there is room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing lifestyle services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, which BAT lacks the genes and ability to manage offline teams. Second, they lack the willingness; lifestyle services require focused effort, which cannot be accomplished by dispatching a small team, and earning money is slow. "It's hard for a company to excel at two important tasks simultaneously, let alone consolidate advantages in their original domains."

Weapon Two: Small Ships Turn Easily

When Dianping raised $100 million in 2011, Zhang Tao told the world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogies fell from grace.

He doesn’t seem anxious about it either. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams, and now they want to become the American version of Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang Tao has gradually explored himself. When he felt that selling keyword ads and discount coupons was outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his attention. Initially, he understood group buying as a marketing platform, similar to the Americans, offering one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized group buying had the characteristics of a trading platform. Thus, he envisioned Dianping as a life service trading platform, and the two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used to transition Dianping from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this transformation, Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth giant's seat today. But this transformation, though seemingly simple, is equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process filled with immense pain.

First, compared to separately selling advertisements, the gross margin of a trading platform decreases, requiring stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, a trading platform demands higher cross-department communication skills.

Previously, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and the sales team were entirely separate. Zhang Tao even deliberately set up a "firewall" between departments — even for significant clients, sales couldn't alter information on the platform. This once led to some hostility between the content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication. Creating a good marketing plan requires not just planning but also editorial cooperation. He says, "Maintaining information quality is still important, but there's no need to reinforce enmity. We need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors may logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and collectively, the entire company's inertia is greater. To address this, Zhang Tao specially convened several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. Months later, an editorial manager casually told a sales manager, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I see you as a colleague."

The sales department also needed transformation. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team dual identities. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing plans. Second, for the company, these marketing consultants are procurement agents. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety must be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users procure group-buying products. Of course, this dual identity will inevitably lead to a series of complex problems. For frontline personnel, it's quite headache-inducing to balance the scales themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual identity" is very similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. By decentralizing decision-making to the front lines, the company gains faster response speeds but increases management difficulty. Likewise, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffered more after the transformation.

Each year, Zhang Tao decomposes Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, which then further break them down layer by layer. However, this decomposition is not a simple arithmetic operation. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping and responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds that unresolved merchant demands stem from sales issues but affect sales. Therefore, Li Jing introduced a new position: communication specialists who collect information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being adept at embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change. When faced with change, everyone feels uncomfortable. All employees must go through a period outside their comfort zones, including myself."

The figures reveal the "weight" of Dianping's execution team. Out of more than 3,000 employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground forces are responsible for directly communicating with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group-buying deals, and combined marketing solutions.

For this core team capable of resisting BAT's clutches, Dianping has developed a unique management evaluation system.

They are not called "salespeople"; from day one, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the phase of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in their understanding of the internet. Marketing planning consultants need to tell merchants what online users need and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing says, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing advisors wouldn't suggest a grouponsale on abalone or shark fin, possibly opting for other dishes, because online and offline user demands differ."

An outstanding marketing advisor must be a versatile player. They need to explain to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the fundamental differences between discounts and group buying and determine which discount aligns better with their positioning. If facing a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing advisor must know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise during certain seasons, and which decrease, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users love.

This places high demands on marketing advisors, who must not only be internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing says, "I must admit, it's impossible for every advisor to be a double expert, so we've set star ratings for advisors." Similar to hotels, marketing advisors range from one-star to five-star, with the best being Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees are not tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing advisors, containing knowledge points and related cases, accessible to both new and experienced employees. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, and thousands of consulting advisors nationwide will help answer them.

A large number of knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees combining practical experiences. Dianping has set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping also conducts skill assessments for employees, linking the results directly to promotions.

Compared to internet knowledge, industry knowledge is harder to acquire. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after years of struggle in the industry. A Super Star marketing advisor told Li Jing that the hairdressing industry should avoid offering group-buying packages for washing, cutting, and blow-drying because men lack motivation to buy them, and women prefer blow-drying without washing and cutting together. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing advisors are not only divided by city but also by industry, such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with a marketing advisor responsible for only one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing advisors can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, ensuring merchants do not reject the sale of products," he says.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: What challenges did a certain type of merchant encounter, what marketing needs they had, what solutions were provided using the company's products, and what were the results. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition — Entrepreneurial Winner. In the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If responsible for a hot pot restaurant, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees present their defense on-site, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, with the company providing certain rewards.

Strategically, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, the tasks they need to accomplish are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with a basic understanding of the industry, and their words sound alike. "The era where storytelling and concept creation alone could secure success is over. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao stated.
