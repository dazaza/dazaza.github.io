Japan makes violin strings with thousands of spider silk strands, soft in tone NetEase Exploration

by iqing9jin on 2012-03-09 16:14:43

A Japanese researcher has made a set of violin strings out of thousands of individual strands of spider silk. The strings, it is claimed, have a "soft and profound timbre" compared to traditional gut or steel strings - possibly due to the fact that they are twisted together into a "compression structure", leaving no gaps between the individual filaments. They will be described in an upcoming issue of Physical Review Letters. Shigeyoshi Osaki of Nara Medical University has been interested for several years in the mechanical properties of spider silk. He has specifically studied spiders' dragline silk - the thread they suspend themselves with - quantifying its strength in a 2007 paper in the journal Polymer. Dr Osaki has now perfected a method to harvest large quantities of this dragline silk from captive-bred spiders, and he is attempting to put this remarkable material to use. "Stringed instruments such as violins have been considered by many researchers as good scientific subjects for study," he writes. "However, not all details have been clarified, because violinists are interested in the body of the violin, rather than the bow or strings." Dr Osaki used 300 female Nephila maculata spiders - one of the "golden orb-weaver" species known for their complex webs - to provide the draglines. First, he twisted 3,000 to 5,000 individual filaments in the same direction to form a bundle; then each string was formed of three of these bundles twisted in the opposite direction. He then began testing their tensile strength - a crucial factor since violinists would not want their strings snapping mid-concerto. Compared to traditional but now little-used gut strings, the spider-silk strings can bear slightly less tension; however, they are stronger than aluminium-coated, nylon-core strings. Examined with an electron microscope, the strings themselves are perfectly round, and in cross-section each filament is compressed into a range of polygonal shapes packed tightly together, leaving no space between them at all. "This finding explains why the spider silk strings possess such a soft and profound timbre," Dr Osaki says. "In particular, the G-string... possesses a unique timbre, being able to create a new type of music." Several professional violinists have tried them out, saying that the spider-silk strings "have a preferable timbre". Dr Osaki concludes: "The violin strings are a novel practical use for spider silk as a kind of high value-added product, and offer a distinctive type of timbre that even expands the repertoire of tone colours for violins."
