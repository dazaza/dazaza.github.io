Famous Director Ang Lee's Biography: A Decade of Movie Dreams Author: Zhang Liangbei

by jqrxass on 2009-10-31 20:14:31

The dream-chasing journey of the first ten years of Ang Lee's film career is truly reviewed in this book. It covers his growing years, the intentions behind each film creation, the difficulties he encountered, his views on actors and how to get along with them, his experiences in participating in film festivals, the backstage stories of winning awards, his thoughts on the cultures of the East and West, and his insights into life.

"The Decade of a Movie Dream" Part 1

I stayed at home every day feeling very bored, so I would read newspapers and practice English when I had time, but I didn't make much progress. I am the type who gets energized when there are films to shoot, but loses energy when there aren't any. So Huijia once said: "He is like a dead man when he doesn't make films, I don't need a dead husband!" If I have a project to work on, I will be very happy, go downtown to find people to write scripts, conduct my own research, and feel very excited. If I look busy, she won't bother me; if she sees me sitting around doing nothing all day, she will ask: "What exactly are you doing? If you're bored, find something to do, it doesn't have to be something that makes money." And so, I wasted six years, breaking countless hearts, yet always holding onto hope. Every now and then, you would see an old classmate hit it big, while most people just squandered their youth and indulged in self-pity. If I were a Japanese husband, I would have committed seppuku long ago. After nearly six years since graduation, I had achieved nothing. At first, I could still talk about ideals, but after three or four years, as I approached forty and was still stuck like this, I no longer felt comfortable talking about ideals, so I started to become somewhat reclusive.

"The Decade of a Movie Dream" Part 2

It was hard to find an actor for the role of Jamie. He had to look like a mixed-race child, be able to speak Chinese and English, have his mother accompany him during filming, and spend a month acting. There was no other choice but to beg my eldest son, Li Han (Ah Mao), to step up. Ah Mao was only six years old at the time, with big eyes, fair skin, and yellow hair after applying styling gel, which made him passable as a mixed-race child. My son said seriously: "Let me think about it!" I told my son: "Help your dad out, otherwise if I collapse, we'll have to live on the street!" He got scared and said: "Alright, I'll help you." As a result, we avoided the hassle of hiring a child star.

"The Decade of a Movie Dream" Part 3

In my opinion, the competition at film festivals is very similar to beauty pageants. How can one judge which woman (this movie) is better than other women (other movies) based solely on good looks and a great figure (artistic style), or answering questions cleverly and appropriately (lasting impact), or appearing compassionate (social issues, humanitarianism), or cultural background (national conditions)? Beauty pageants themselves are inherently superficial things. Everyone knows that the beauty and value of a woman cannot be judged this way. But strangely, everyone still loves watching televised beauty pageants. To me, there is no hierarchy between directors and beautiful women. From the perspective of reviewing films, competitions make no sense, but they are important film activities. Winning awards also excites me, just like watching beauty pageants.
