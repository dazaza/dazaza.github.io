Rare consumption motives include value, norms, habits, identity, emotions, and others. According to Jack Trout's positioning theory, human behavior is always driven by certain motives. Some people refer to consumers' consumption motives as consumer psychological positioning. Correspondingly, consumer psychological positioning includes value psychology, norm psychology, habit psychology, identity psychology, emotional psychology, etc. Below is a brief introduction.

1. Consumers' Value Psychology

According to the research findings of Shanghai Management Consulting Company: The reason why consumers like a certain product is because they believe that this product will bring them greater value than similar products, which means it has greater potential value. Potential value depends on the potential quality of the product. What we call potential quality does not refer to the quality detected by quality supervision departments, but rather the quality perceived in the minds of consumers, which is the subjective evaluation of a brand by consumers. The reason why Coca-Cola has led Pepsi for more than a century is because it claims to be "authentic", "original", and "unique", making consumers believe that it has irreplaceable value, which is the potential value. In fact, the reason why a brand can open up sales channels is often not due to its real value, but because of its potential value. Potential value has uniqueness, independence, credibility, and importance. Potential value is the famous brand effect, just like the celebrity effect, it is a concept, and this concept has been deeply rooted in the minds of consumers.

2. Consumers' Norm Psychology

Norms play a huge role. Norms refer to the sum total of all moral behavioral rules commonly followed by people. In real life, they influence my thoughts, restrain my words and deeds, and affect all aspects of my life. The faces of norms are varied, including principles, rationality, obligations, politeness, friendship, loyalty, understanding, and other factors. In many cases, norms can trigger the motivation for consumption behavior. According to temporary surveys and research by marketing experts, the reason why consumers like a certain brand is often to prevent or eliminate an inner conflict that contradicts their norms and prices. When consumers decide whether to buy or not buy a product of a certain brand, norms are an important influencing factor. In the early 1980s, a global environmental protection craze swept across the world. As Germany's first mass brand focusing on environmental protection, "Frog" not only regarded the environmental protection perspective within the scope of norms as a supplement to its value advertising strategy, but also proudly placed it at the center of its advertising promotion. Within three short years, its product sales tripled. This victory was due to its alignment with the global environmental awareness, giving consumers a good feeling that matches and harmonizes with the norms they believe in.

3. Consumers' Habit Psychology

Habits are behaviors temporarily formed and difficult to change. Different people and different ethnic groups have various habits. For example, people in southern China mainly eat rice as their staple food; people in northern Europe prefer beer, while those in southern Europe favor red wine; some love smoking, others love dressing up, etc. Habits are often irresistible and sometimes even play a bigger role in decision-making than value psychology. Consumers generally have specific consumption habits, which are formed through long-term consumption behaviors in daily life. For instance, when consumers initially use a certain branded product and feel satisfied, they form a perception of the product's quality and efficacy, gradually developing a liking for the brand, establishing trust in the brand, and enhancing confidence in using the brand. Under normal circumstances, they won't switch to other brands and become loyal customers of that brand. For example, some consumers prefer to buy clothes and appliances at large malls, while purchasing daily necessities and food at supermarkets. Once consumption habits are formed, they usually don't change easily. Brand positioning expresses philosophical emotional appeals, which can stimulate consumers' desire to consume, cultivate consumption habits, and enhance brand loyalty. Due to the subtle influence of habits, people gradually form fixed lifestyles. These lifestyles accumulate over history to become cultural customs, and when these customs reach a certain depth, they become cultural heritage. After years of exploration and discussion, marketing experts have developed a set of business theories that fully utilize this potential cultural heritage, leveraging consumers' habit psychology to achieve sales goals. In the early 1990s, Wrigley's gum was launched in Germany. In the minds of consumers, gum had a unique secondary function of preventing cavities. At the same time, mixed mouthwash was also launched. In the minds of consumers, the main function of mixed mouthwash was dental hygiene, while gum was its secondary function. After a period of market competition, mixed mouthwash eventually failed, while Wrigley's gum took the lead with a 90% market share. The reason was actually very simple: consumer habits were at play, as most consumers were already accustomed to gum primarily being for chewing, with cavity prevention as a secondary function.

4. Consumers' Identity Psychology

People unconsciously reveal their identities, especially those who have achieved a certain reputation, power, and status. Everyone has a certain identity, and they are always paying attention to their own identity, trying to make their speech and actions align with their social activities and identity. The best way to express one's identity is through clothing, food, housing, transportation, and usage, such as wearing high-end name-brand clothing, driving Rolls-Royce cars, staying in five-star luxury hotels. When this information is conveyed to the outside world, the person's identity naturally becomes apparent. Therefore, marketing experts, based on this psychological aspect of human nature, have summarized a corresponding marketing theory called identity principle, making the brand an effective weapon for consumers to express their self-identity. For companies, developing products that surpass competitors and reveal consumers' identities becomes an important issue, as this directly affects consumers' purchasing decisions and subsequently influences product sales.

5. Consumers' Emotional Psychology

Emotions are psychological responses to external stimuli, such as liking, admiration, sadness, fear, anger, disgust, etc. Whether consumers like or dislike a certain product reflects natural expressions of consumer emotions. Experienced brand operators have long recognized these aspects, often going all out through advertisements, public relations, and other means to uncover the potential growth of brands, touch consumers' emotions, and fully utilize consumers' emotional psychology to enhance brands.
