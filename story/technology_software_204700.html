Motherboard

The motherboard is a key component in the entire computer, playing a crucial role. If the motherboard fails, it will affect the operation of the entire PC system. Below, let's take a look at some of the most common motherboard failures.

Common Failure One: No Display on Startup

When there's no display upon startup, the first thing we need to check is the BIOS. The BIOS on the motherboard stores important hardware data, and it's also one of the more fragile parts of the motherboard, easily susceptible to damage. Once damaged, it can cause the system to fail to run, and this kind of fault is generally due to the motherboard BIOS being corrupted by the CIH virus (of course, it cannot be ruled out that a motherboard fault causes the system to fail to run). Generally, after the BIOS is corrupted by a virus, all data on the hard drive will be lost, so we can determine if the BIOS has been corrupted by checking if the hard drive data is intact. If the hard drive data is completely undamaged, then there are three other reasons that could cause the phenomenon of no display on startup:

1. Due to issues with the motherboard expansion slot or expansion card, the motherboard may not respond when an expansion card like a sound card is plugged in, resulting in no display.

2. Incorrect setting of the CPU frequency in CMOS for jumperless motherboards may also cause a no-display fault. This can be resolved by clearing the CMOS. The CMOS jumper is usually near the lithium battery on the motherboard, with its default position typically being a short between 1 and 2. To resolve this, change the jumper to a short between 2 and 3 for a few seconds. For older motherboards, if the user cannot find the jumper, simply remove the battery, wait until the CMOS settings appear after turning on the computer, then shut down and reattach the battery to achieve the purpose of discharging the CMOS.

3. The motherboard failing to recognize the memory, damaged memory, or incompatible memory can also lead to a no-display fault upon startup. Some older motherboards are picky about memory types. Once an unsupported memory type is inserted, the motherboard cannot start up, sometimes without providing any fault prompts (beeps). Also, expanding memory to improve system performance by using different brands or types of memory can result in such faults. Therefore, during repairs, attention should be paid to this aspect.

For the issue of corrupted motherboard BIOS, you can plug in an ISA graphics card to see if there is a display (if there is a prompt, follow the steps as indicated.). If there is no startup screen, you can create a floppy disk for automatic BIOS updates and refresh the BIOS again. However, in some cases where the motherboard BIOS is corrupted, the floppy drive may not work properly. In such situations, you can try the hot-swap method (I have tried it before; as long as the BIOS is the same, it can successfully burn in compatible motherboards.). However, using the hot-swap method may damage some components on the motherboard, so a reliable method is to use a coder to write the BIOS update file into the BIOS chip (this can be done safely by a computer service provider).

Common Failure Two: CMOS Settings Cannot Be Saved

This type of failure is generally caused by insufficient voltage from the motherboard battery. Replacing it should solve the issue. However, if replacing the battery does not resolve the problem, there are two possible causes:

1. A circuit issue with the motherboard. In such cases, professional repair personnel should be consulted.

2. A CMOS jumper issue on the motherboard. Sometimes, incorrectly setting the CMOS jumper to clear options or external battery mode prevents CMOS data from being saved.

Common Failure Three: Deadlocks or Slow CD-ROM Read Speed After Installing Motherboard Drivers in Windows

This phenomenon occurs occasionally on some generic motherboards. After installing the motherboard driver, restarting the computer may prevent normal entry into the Windows 98 desktop, and the driver cannot be uninstalled in Windows 98. If this happens, it is recommended to find the latest driver and reinstall it. Generally, this resolves the issue. If not, the only option may be to reinstall the system.

Common Failure Four: Mouse Unavailable During Windows Installation or Startup

The software cause of this failure is generally due to incorrect CMOS settings. In the power management section of the CMOS settings, there is an item called "Modem Use IRQ," with options ranging from 3, 4, 5... to NA. The default option is usually 3. Setting it to an interrupt option other than 3 should resolve the issue.

Common Failure Five: Frequent System Lockups, Including During CMOS Setup

Lockups occurring in the CMOS are generally due to issues with the motherboard or CPU. If the following methods cannot resolve the fault, you may need to replace the motherboard or CPU.

Such failures are often caused by problems with the motherboard cache or poor heat dissipation design of the motherboard. I once encountered this issue on an 815EP motherboard. After the system locked up, touching the motherboard components around the CPU revealed they were extremely hot. After replacing with a higher-power fan, the lockup issue was resolved. For cache-related problems, entering the CMOS settings and disabling the cache can resolve the issue smoothly, though speed will definitely be affected.

Common Failure Six: COM Port, Parallel Port, or IDE Port Malfunction

Such failures are generally caused by live insertion and removal of related hardware. In such cases, you can use a multifunction card as a replacement, but before doing so, you must disable the onboard COM port and parallel port on the motherboard (some motherboards require disabling the IDE port as well).

Graphics Card

Common Failure One: No Display on Startup

This type of failure is usually caused by poor contact between the graphics card and the motherboard or issues with the motherboard slot. For integrated graphics motherboards, if the shared memory is used, ensure that the memory module is installed in the first slot. If the graphics card causes the no-display issue, the computer will typically emit one long beep followed by two short beeps upon startup (for AWARD BIOS graphics cards). If this happens, check the hard drive data integrity to determine if the BIOS has been corrupted. If the hard drive data is intact, there are three possible causes for the no-display issue:

1. Poor contact between the motherboard expansion slot or expansion card, causing the motherboard to have no response and no display after inserting an expansion card like a sound card.

2. Incorrect CPU frequency settings in CMOS for jumperless motherboards may also trigger a no-display fault. Clearing the CMOS can resolve this issue. The CMOS jumper is usually near the motherboard's lithium battery, with its default position being a short between pins 1 and 2. Changing it to a short between pins 2 and 3 for a few seconds can solve the problem. For older motherboards, if the user cannot find the jumper, removing the battery, waiting until the CMOS settings appear after turning on the computer, and then reattaching the battery achieves the purpose of discharging the CMOS.

3. The motherboard cannot recognize the memory, the memory is damaged, or the memory is incompatible, which can also lead to a no-display fault upon startup. Some older motherboards are particular about memory types. Once an unsupported memory type is inserted, the motherboard cannot start, and sometimes, certain motherboards do not provide any fault indications (beeps). Additionally, expanding memory to enhance system performance by using different brand or type memories can also cause such faults. Therefore, attention should be paid to this aspect during repairs.

For the issue of corrupted motherboard BIOS, you can plug in an ISA graphics card to check if there is a display (if there are prompts, follow the steps accordingly.). If there is no startup screen, you can create a floppy disk for automatic BIOS updates and refresh the BIOS again. However, in some cases where the motherboard BIOS is corrupted, the floppy drive may not function properly. In such situations, you can attempt the hot-swap method (I have tried it before; as long as the BIOS is the same, it can successfully burn in compatible motherboards.). However, using the hot-swap method might damage some components on the motherboard, so a reliable method is to use a coder to write the BIOS update file into the BIOS chip (this can be done safely by a computer service provider).

Common Failure Two: Mottled Screen, Illegible Text

This type of failure is generally caused by the monitor or graphics card not supporting high resolution. When encountering mottling, you can switch to safe mode and then in Windows 98, enter display settings, select "Apply" and "OK" under 16-bit color mode. Restart the computer and delete the graphics card driver in normal mode of Windows 98, then restart the computer. Alternatively, you can enter pure DOS environment, edit the SYSTEM.INI file, changing display.drv=pnpdrver to display.drv=vga.drv, save and exit, then update the driver in Windows.

Common Failure Three: Abnormal Color Display

This type of failure is generally caused by the following reasons:

1. Poor contact between the video card and monitor signal line.

2. Faulty monitor.

3. Color abnormalities occur in certain software, commonly seen in old machines. In the BIOS, there is an option to verify colors; enabling it can help.

4. Damaged video card.

5. Magnetization of the monitor, usually caused by excessive proximity to magnetic objects, leading to distorted display images.

Common Failure Four: System Freeze

This type of failure is commonly due to incompatibility between the motherboard and the graphics card or poor contact between them. Incompatibility between the graphics card and other expansion cards can also cause freezes.

Common Failure Five: Abnormal Spots or Patterns on Screen

This type of failure is generally due to issues with the video card's memory or poor contact between the video card and motherboard. Cleaning the gold fingers area of the video card or replacing the video card can resolve the issue.

Common Failure Six: Loss of Video Card Driver

After loading the video card driver, it automatically disappears after running for a period. This fault is generally due to poor quality of the video card or incompatibility between the video card and motherboard, causing high temperatures and system instability or freezes. In such cases, only replacing the video card can resolve the issue.

In addition, there is a special case where the video card driver could load previously but causes a freeze upon entering Windows after loading. You can replace it with another model video card, load its driver, and then insert the old video card to resolve the issue. If this still doesn't work, it indicates a registry fault, which can be restored by restoring the registry or reinstalling the operating system.

Sound Card

Common Fault One: No sound from the sound card.

Common causes for this type of fault include:

1. Default output set to "Mute" in the driver program. Click on the small speaker icon in the bottom right corner of the screen, adjust the volume slider, and deselect the checkbox next to "Mute" to restore normal sound output.

2. Conflict between the sound card and other expansion cards. Adjust the system resources used by PnP cards to prevent interference. Sometimes, even though no yellow exclamation mark appears in "Device Manager" indicating conflicts, the sound card still won't produce sound, which actually indicates a conflict that the system hasn't detected.

3. The sound card stops producing sound after installing DirectX. This suggests that the sound card is not compatible with DirectX, requiring an updated driver.

4. No sound from one channel. Check if the audio cable connecting the sound card to the speakers is broken.

Common Fault Two: Excessive noise from the sound card.

Common causes for this type of fault include:

1. Misaligned expansion card. Poor manufacturing precision of the case or improper installation of the sound card's outer shield plate can prevent the sound card from fitting tightly into the motherboard's expansion slot. This is a common fault visible in both ISA and PCI cards and can generally be corrected with pliers.

2. Powered speakers connected to the sound card's Speaker output. For powered speakers, they should be connected to the Line Out port on the sound card, as the signal output here bypasses the amplifier on the sound card, reducing noise. Some sound cards have only one output port, and whether it's Line Out or Speaker depends on the jumper settings on the card. Manufacturers often default to Speaker mode, so you may need to remove the sound card and adjust the jumpers.

3. Poor built-in Windows drivers. When installing sound card drivers, choose "Manufacturer-provided drivers" instead of "Default Windows drivers." If using the "Add New Hardware" method, select "Install from Disk" rather than choosing from the list box. If Windows' own drivers have already been installed, go to "Control Panel → System → Device Manager → Sound, Video, and Game Controllers," select each sub-device, and choose "Properties → Driver → Change Driver → Install from Disk." Insert the disk or CD provided with the sound card to install the manufacturer's drivers.

Common Fault Three: Sound card unable to perform "Plug and Play"

1. Try using the latest driver or alternative programs. I once had a sound card that wouldn't install drivers correctly in Windows 98, so I replaced it with Creative SB16 drivers, which worked fine. Later, when upgrading to Windows Me, it stopped working again, so I switched to the sound card drivers included with Windows 2000 (Full Version), which worked perfectly.

2. The most frustrating problem is when Windows 9X detects Plug and Play devices and insists on installing drivers for you, which turn out to be unusable. Every time you delete and reinstall them, the same problem recurs, and "Add New Hardware" doesn't fix it. Here's my secret trick: Go to the Win9xinfother directory, delete all *.inf files related to the sound card, and restart your computer. Then manually install the drivers. This method works 100% of the time and has saved countless sound cards... Of course, modifying the registry can achieve the same result.

3. Installation of non-PnP sound cards (also applicable to PnP sound cards that cannot be installed via the PnP method): Go to "Control Panel" / "Add New Hardware" / "Next." When prompted, "Do you want Windows to search for new hardware?" choose "No," then select "Sound, Video, and Game Controllers" from the list and install using the driver disk or directly selecting the sound card type.

Common Fault Four: No sound when playing CDs

1. Complete silence. If using Windows 98's "CD Player" to play CDs produces no sound, but the "CD Player" works normally, this indicates that the audio cable from the optical drive is not properly connected. Use a four-core audio cable to connect the CD-ROM's analog audio output to the sound card's CD-in port, which usually comes with the CD-ROM when purchased.

2. Only one channel produces sound. The output ports of optical drives generally have left and right signal lines, with the middle two lines as ground lines. Since the colors of the four audio signal lines are usually different, you can match them based on their colors. If the sound card has only one interface or none of the interfaces match the audio cable, you'll need to rearrange the wiring order of the audio cable. Typically, swapping just two of the wires will suffice.

Common Fault Five: Burst noise from PCI sound card

This is usually due to the Bus Master technology used by PCI graphics cards amplifying background noise during read/write operations on the PCI bus or mouse movements. Solution: Turn off the Bus Master function of the PCI graphics card, switch to an AGP graphics card, and move the PCI sound card to another slot.
