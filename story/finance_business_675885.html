Baiji pharmacists introduce that vitiligo is commonly known as white spot disease. The lesions present as pure white circular or irregular depigmented spots, with smooth surfaces, clear boundaries, and increased edge pigmentation. The whitened skin is more sensitive to sunlight than normal skin, turning red even after slight sun exposure. The course of the disease progresses slowly, gradually expanding outward, merging together or stopping at a certain point without changing for a long time. Vitiligo on the face tends to have a short onset time and small area, and if treated early, the treatment effect is better; thus, early treatment is crucial.

The majority of white spots are symmetrically distributed, but there are also cases where the damage follows the nerve segments. Besides skin damage, some mucous membranes may also be affected. The skin lesions are local pigment loss, presenting as white spots with milky white coloration. The hair within these spots may turn white or remain normal, but there are no other changes in the skin and no self-symptoms. Due to the lack of pigment protection, exposure to the sun can cause burning pain, redness, and blisters. The number of white spots is not fixed; they may be limited to certain parts of the body or distributed along a specific nerve segment (or dermatome), rarely changing or disappearing on their own. However, in most cases, they tend to increase gradually, expand, and even spread throughout the body.

The degree of pigment loss varies from person to person and from location to location, showing three tones: white, gray-white, and near-normal skin color. Clinically, according to the shape, location, and scope of the white spots, as well as the treatment response, it is generally divided into five types: localized type, scattered type, generalized type, extremity type, and segmental type. Based on the degree of pigment loss in the lesion areas, it can be classified into complete and incomplete types. This disease can be divided into three stages: progression stage, stationary stage, and improvement stage. Some patients may experience involvement of retinal, choroidal, and soft brain membrane pigment cells. The course of the disease is chronic and can last a lifetime, with few cases achieving complete spontaneous recovery. Diagnosis involves identifying patches of varying shapes and degrees of whiteness, which often merge into larger patches with darker edges and are usually symmetrical. There are no self-symptoms, and the hair above may remain normal or turn white. The progression speed is unpredictable, and it can spread throughout the body or naturally heal.
