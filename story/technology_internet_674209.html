All films are black. There is usually an English symbol at the corner of the film, which is the film number indicating whether it is C, M, Y, K or one of the spot colors. This shows what color the film outputs. If not, you can identify the color by looking at the screen angle. The stepped color bar next to it is used for dot density correction. Besides checking if the dot density is normal, it also helps identify C, M, Y, K. The color bar in the lower left corner represents C, in the upper left corner M, in the upper right corner Y, and in the lower right corner K. Therefore, the printing factory can determine C, M, Y, K based on the color bar. That is to say, in order to facilitate the inspection of the film development concentration, there is a color code on the corner of the film. As for how many colors to print, it depends on the screen lines of each film. The dots on the film tell you [the degree of a certain color here]. For example, a text version usually has only one black, so there is only one film. If you use red ink for printing, the result will be red characters. One film represents one color; for color printing, at least four films are needed, representing C, M, Y, K four colors! It could also be four spot colors. Spot color films are output as separate files into K versions. The color bars next to them cannot indicate the specific values of C, M, Y, K, but are used for comparison to check if the film meets the requirements.
