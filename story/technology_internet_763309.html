Wu Xinhong: Meitu's attempt to make phones is a case of ignorant bravado.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 18:24:28

Wu Xinhong. The 80s-born founder of Meitu, and its CEO.

Cheng Lingfeng: Before, when I talked with Cai Wensheng about why Meitu wanted to make phones, it was all high-level theory. That's superficial. Today, I want to ask you about the specific difficulties encountered while doing the hard work. That's practical. A combination of the superficial and the practical will give a complete picture of your grand phone-making endeavor.

Wu Xinhong: The first difficulty is "ignorance."

A group of young people who previously worked on apps on the internet suddenly found that making phones was a very cool thing, so we jumped right into it. We didn't think too much about the difficulties we would encounter, big problems like supply chains, hardware structures, system engineering—actually, even if we had thought about them, we wouldn't have imagined them. Making phones and making apps are completely different things. But we immediately started the project. Looking back, it was adventurous, naive, stupid.

Cheng Lingfeng: Did you not consult industry veterans before starting the project?

Wu Xinhong: No. At the time, investor Cai Wensheng agreed, and we set off. Actually, if we had done detailed arguments and looked ahead with caution, we might not have dared to do it.

Cheng Lingfeng: Young calves are fearless. What was the next step?

Wu Xinhong: We originally didn't want to make our own phones. We wanted to collaborate with mainstream phone brands to jointly launch a phone. It would still use their big brand, and we would embed Meitu's expertise in photography modules, focusing on self-portrait functions. Then both parties would jointly market it. But we hit a wall. Big manufacturers ignored us. We were just a small company they looked down upon, a group of impetuous young people.

Cheng Lingfeng: Is this similar to the 360 Special Edition phone?

Wu Xinhong: The 360 Special Edition phone is a joint brand. We didn't require a joint brand; we only asked to use Meitu's name in marketing. But the big manufacturers were very domineering. They didn't recognize this point; they didn't want to see their phones promoted under the Meitu banner. They wanted to highlight their own brands.

Cheng Lingfeng: Was it because you asked for too high a share of profits?

Wu Xinhong: No. Actually, we were very simple; we just wanted to prove ourselves. We wanted to provide technical solutions free of charge and, with their help, delve into the hardware layer to create more stunning self-portraits. Previously, as an app developer, we could only work at the system level without access to the underlying computing power. After achieving that, we would help with market promotion. But they thought self-portraits weren't a strong technology, nor did they need our promotion. Perhaps being a large company, they had overall brand planning and were inflexible.

Cheng Lingfeng: This resonates with me. When I first started doing independent media, I approached traditional media outlets, offering articles in exchange for higher royalties. But traditional media saw me as a beggar or a thief trying to steal their kidneys. Repeated rejections left me no choice but to grit my teeth and persist with independent media. Looking back, if I had really sold my articles to them, I would have become a beggar. The transition between old and new paradigms must be like this.

Wu Xinhong: There was once an animal called a dinosaur on Earth.

Cheng Lingfeng: Why didn't you partner with less dominant phone manufacturers?

Wu Xinhong: We felt it wasn't good enough. It was better to do it ourselves. So we decided to do it ourselves. Thus, making our own phones was a matter of being forced onto Liangshan (a reference to being pushed into a corner).

Cheng Lingfeng: What difficulties did you encounter on the mountain?

Wu Xinhong: We went to ODM manufacturers; we designed, and they produced. China has a mature mobile phone manufacturing industry chain. Initially, several manufacturers we approached refused, thinking we were unreliable. We kept searching, relying on luck, and finally found one that shared our philosophy. Some ODM manufacturers, having grown larger, were moving towards vertical fields, and photography was a function point everyone wanted to capture. They had this intention themselves, and we hit it off.

The ODM provider offers complete machine solutions, and we are responsible for research and development from the bottom layer of photography, software, marketing, pricing. All revenues belong to Meitu.

Cheng Lingfeng: This process must have been difficult.

Wu Xinhong: Our phone is different from any previous model. The front-facing camera on previous phones was 5 million pixels; ours was the first 8 million pixel phone, equipped with an independent chip, which was previously only used on professional cameras. Most phones didn’t even have chips on their rear cameras, which violated the conventional design of hardware.

A mobile phone is something where pulling one thread affects the whole garment. Therefore, the motherboard and drivers had to be completely redesigned, optimization and debugging had to be redone, adding many procedures, increasing R&D costs, and extending the cycle. For example, we, NTK, ODM, and chip supplier Fujitsu needed to adjust together. Fujitsu adjusted for over two months… This phone was entirely designed by ourselves, inside and out.

Cheng Lingfeng: Did you visit industry veterans during this stage?

Wu Xinhong: Still no. Because we didn’t see any company with a path similar to ours. The 360 Special Edition phone didn’t require redesigning; the main brand was the phone manufacturer, and 360 only simply customized, pre-installed software, and established channels. We also didn’t visit Xiaomi. We were learning sideways, not seeking direct advice.

Cheng Lingfeng: Can you give a specific example of the biggest difficulty?

Wu Xinhong: All the difficulties came from the fact that we were doing it for the first time. The tasks were fragmented, and individually they seemed insignificant, but you would encounter countless ones every day. You need to go through this process, pay all the tuition fees, and then similar issues won’t arise again when you do it next time.

Cheng Lingfeng: How did the team feel?

Wu Xinhong: Excited but hardworking. The workload was two or three times what it was before. Poor sleep, little sleep. But everyone liked it. It’s like playing a game, the feeling of passing levels, defeating monsters, and upgrading.

Cheng Lingfeng: So the phone was made. How did you come up with the price of 2199 yuan?

Wu Xinhong: It was calculated based on cost. First, it was our first attempt, with a small batch size, resulting in high comprehensive costs. Besides the visible components, there were also R&D, taxes, marketing, and project operation costs. The market says the cost of iPhone5 is over 1000 yuan, but you have to look at its comprehensive cost, considering how much R&D and operational costs are spread across its large volume. Our bottom line was not to lose too much money. Our first and second batches didn’t make money.

Cheng Lingfeng: You can subsidize the phone through service charges.

Wu Xinhong: Meitu has always been free, earning money through advertisements, not much. We indeed haven’t evaluated this aspect.

Cheng Lingfeng: This pricing has brought criticism.

Wu Xinhong: There has been significant controversy. Men don’t understand women’s selfie needs; men just look at the hardware and think it lacks cost-effectiveness. They assume domestic phones should be a few hundred yuan, yet yours exceeds 2000, is even more expensive than Xiaomi, calling it outrageous and unbelievable.

However, our target users quite like it. They care whether you really make me look better. The first batch of fewer than 20,000 units sold out in less than an hour. The second batch added another 5K, but due to the shortage of core components, only this much could be produced in the short term.

Cheng Lingfeng: With the phone gaining popularity, what is the next difficulty? What is the target for this year in terms of units?

Wu Xinhong: Once we passed the initial market validation phase, we could gradually increase production. Increasing production allows us to make money and offset the previous costs. We may sell 200,000 units this year. However, the first-year goal isn’t selling goods but refining the product and building a reputation. Now we’re entering the ramp-up phase.

To increase production, the supply chain will be challenging. Core components are monopolized by international brands; we're too small to get them. Only when the brand and reputation grow and get validated will manufacturers take notice. Xiaomi now faces core component shortages but is definitely in a much better position than us. We're still tiny. Continuous effort is needed; it's hard to solve all at once.

Cheng Lingfeng: How many units will you sell at the peak? Or, how many core users do you have?

Wu Xinhong: Our monthly active users exceed 50 million. Many have demand but are observing. Most people dare not be the first to try, they're watching how the reputation develops.

There's a reference. Casio's selfie wonder, priced at 5000 yuan, sold over 2 million units last year in China, and possibly even more counterfeit goods.

Cheng Lingfeng: You're not selling phones; you're selling the desire for beauty.

Wu Xinhong: You can consider the selfie wonder as "virtual cosmetics." Women want to appear more stunning and confident online when sharing with others. We serve this need.

In first- and second-tier cities, women don't hesitate to buy cosmetics worth hundreds of yuan, and spending one or two thousand is normal. This is a market about "beauty." Twenty years ago, China had almost no cosmetics, but now 70% of women use them, with various brands flourishing. Acceptance of new things takes a process. Once used, it becomes a habit, and they can't live without it.

Cheng Lingfeng: Those mainstream manufacturers that initially rejected you must regret it now.

Wu Xinhong: They’ve approached us again for cooperation. They've realized that the phone market used to compete on performance and price, but now it's about finding breakthroughs in niche areas. There are now phones focused on music playback. But we don't currently need to cooperate with them in the short term.

Cheng Lingfeng: Difficulties regarding talent?

Wu Xinhong: To make phones, we introduced over 40 professionals, with the highest being a vice president; I'm the overall supervisor. Meitu originally had 150 people, and now we're close to 200.

Cheng Lingfeng: Time-consuming? Costly?

Wu Xinhong: The project started in July last year, roughly taking 11 months. China's mobile phone ecosystem chain is relatively mature. We invested tens of millions.

Cheng Lingfeng: Your feelings?

Wu Xinhong: Something you've never done but want to do shouldn't be overthought, or you'll never do it. If you want to do it, just dive in and figure it out later. Others might think we're irrational. Our style is "do it first, talk later." During the process, we'll improve, and we won't overthink it. As for the difficulties, we're not afraid. Stay calm, solve the problems.

Cheng Lingfeng: Any pleasant surprises?

Wu Xinhong: Making phones touches on the ecology of mobile internet. We started from scratch, inside and out. Entering many stages, the team was exercised, and our vision expanded—a valuable practical experience that most companies lack such opportunities for.

We have a clearer direction for development: Android, which is a trend. The explosion of mobile devices exceeded our expectations. The demand is enormous. Originally, we thought it would explode last year, but this year it's even stronger. Manufacturers are all short of supplies, unable to meet demand. Samsung S4 sold over ten million units in just ten days.

Cheng Lingfeng: What's your core function?

Wu Xinhong: When we delved into the lower layers, we discovered that images could be further explored, reaching more extreme levels. Just doing apps doesn't allow us to master these technologies. It's a great exercise for the technical team. Having done the lower layers, doing the upper layers becomes easy.

Currently, we just want to make the product better. We don't have grand ideas or plans. We're boldly experimenting and innovating in this niche field. Users and markets naturally follow.
