In 2012, is there anyone who still makes you depressed? People with insight from Tianya Blog are all here in the Tianya Community.

by cfgsdftre77 on 2012-02-11 23:07:11

― Enterprise Leadership Culture

The movie "2012" presents absolute suspense and absurdity. Even if humanity has witnessed the fulfillment of Mayan prophecies in the past, I don't believe the end of the world will happen in the 21st century. Moreover, we have every reason and wisdom to educate people about their concern for the Earth and the environment in other ways, rather than resorting to scaremongering like the exaggerated and ridiculous depiction of catastrophic events shown in "2012." On the contrary, we should trust science; all changes in the Earth's crust and subtle changes in the universe cannot be fully explained by something as dramatic as a "great shift." Therefore, this film carries a "negative" significance, making people's already tense nerves even more (excessively) strained. After this movie was released, some people even began to adopt an indifferent attitude towards everything, shouting about the end of the world all day long, and started behaving cynically. Of course, from the standpoint of paying attention to the environment and life, or any other "noble" perspective, the educational impact of the movie "2012" on humanity far exceeds our current desire for health. Because only by living can we talk about health. However, one point cannot be overlooked: severe psychological and mental illnesses are constantly troubling people. Humanity coexists with peculiar physiological and psychological behaviors characterized by "contradictions," such as rapidly changing mental states and volatile emotional fluctuations. As a result, we see that emotional counseling, psychological clinics, psychiatric hospitals, and related industries are bustling with activity, whether people come voluntarily or involuntarily. Every day, there are continuous phone consultations and an endless stream of visitors, forming a stark contrast to the negative influence created by "2012." Undoubtedly, people yearn for a good living condition, and emotional experts and psychologists have become one of the most fashionable and profitable professions.

Depression and schizophrenia have become the number one killer for office workers! Regardless of whether this disease originates from the patients themselves or from their work environment, one thing is undeniable: it has already entered the realm of public health issues. Due to the increase in various social contradictions, greater competition pressure, and noticeable changes in population and family structures, the incidence of serious mental illness is showing a clear upward trend... Mental health has become a major public health issue and a prominent social problem. Unfortunately, university graduates face difficulties in finding employment; people's jobs are insecure, and they are always at risk of being fired. Some employees not only have to look at their leader's expressions but also must constantly understand their intentions. Otherwise, if anything goes wrong, they might be punished lightly or, in severe cases, forced to leave. "Harmony" becomes just a facade and slogan. In reality, no one wants to be forced to work, but the premise is that you must respect your own job and excel in your skills.

My friend Hui is an experienced psychologist. Based on years of clinical practice, she has developed a complete set of暗示procedures for treating mental illnesses, providing different interventions based on different patients. One day, my colleague Fang wanted to check her health status, so she asked me to accompany her to Hui's clinic for testing using a computer system. This is a very scientific system that provides accurate diagnoses after completing questionnaires. Afterwards, Fang wrote down her answers on scratch paper. The diagnosis results shocked me: "Moderate Depression!" I never expected this result because I spent time with Fang every day and never noticed anything "wrong" with her. However, Hui told me that Fang is not an isolated case. Many people appear normal, masking their emotions with a perfect psychological need. In reality, they are already in a sub-health state, which usually goes unnoticed until an event triggers the symptoms. If left untreated, it could lead to future problems. Later, Hui provided Fang with a treatment plan, and the effects were indeed noticeable.

Situations similar to Fang's cannot be ignored. A few random incidents recently sparked my interest in the "distance" between bosses and subordinates. I also surveyed and observed the thoughts and mental states of some knowledgeable people around me. Regrettably, in their view, it has become commonplace for enterprise leaders to prioritize maintaining their "face" over respecting the subordinate status of their employees. They seem to think that it is natural for bosses to be harsh on subordinates and for subordinates to endure grievances.

So, how can we reduce the survival pressure brought about by these "public resources"? Previously, I heard that some companies popularized a game called "Emotional Release," one method of which involved venting frustration on a "model" of the boss. For example, if Zhang San suffered unjustly from Boss Li, he could kick the "model" of Boss Li when given the chance. Similarly, if Wang Er was scolded without cause by Boss Zhao, he could gouge out the eyes of the "model" of Boss Zhao. If this kind of "emotional release" could be promoted, we might consider it a "cheap" prescription that company leaders provide to relieve employee stress. At least, it gives the parties involved an opportunity to vent their frustrations. However, "games" remain just games or legends; no one has seen their units set aside a room to place several "models" of leaders for employees to vent their anger. Even so, for the sake of health, we do not wish for any company boss to cause depression or mental confusion among their subordinates, nor do we need bosses to prescribe such "cheap" remedies! There is a saying: gold is not pure, and no man is perfect. I believe that no employee expects their boss to care for them like a parent. Most employees merely hope for fair treatment from their leaders. Zizhong said, "A gentleman's mistakes are like eclipses of the sun or moon: when he makes a mistake, everyone sees it; when he corrects it, everyone looks up to him." In this world, where everyone seeks their own gain, both the perfectly virtuous sage and the irredeemably wicked devil are exceptions in life. Most people (regardless of whether they are leaders or subordinates) have some selfishness, some cowardice, and make various kinds of mistakes. However, we cannot forget that human nature also contains some valuable emotions. We need equality, and we also need to create and build a "healthy" environment together with you.

I am not a boss, but I recall an ancient saying suitable for bosses: "If one's conduct is upright, orders are unnecessary; if one's conduct is not upright, even orders will not be followed." With this, I would like to remind those bosses or leaders who inadvertently exert pressure on employees and cause their depression: democracy is not empty talk. Do not impose on others what you do not desire yourself. Water can carry a boat, but it can also overturn it. Speaking of which, "2012" has arrived. At this juncture of bidding farewell to the old and welcoming the new, many organizations are immersed in year-end summaries and the selection of advanced models. Some organizations may even conduct leadership reshuffles, objectively creating some "ripples" and "complaints" among the grassroots. Just yesterday, after a grand year-end meeting, a friend said they were attending a council meeting and feeling drowsy. They jokingly asked me if there were any drugs to treat "affective psychosis." I replied that feeling sleepy during meetings is quite normal—I had just been awakened by a round of applause. Then, I discussed with my colleagues during our break on this topic—what if every day was a "world's end" imposed by your boss? Would you cherish it positively or abandon it passively? (1.9)
