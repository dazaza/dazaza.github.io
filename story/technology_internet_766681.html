Twitter is said to be planning to hire more investment banks to underwrite its IPO deal

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 21:22:38

According to a Reuters report on Thursday morning of September 20th Beijing time, three well-informed sources revealed that Twitter is in talks with more investment banks to hire additional underwriters for its IPO (initial public offering). The sources also disclosed that Twitter is currently in the process of finalizing the structure of underwriting fees.

Normally, the fee charged by underwriters accounts for about 7% of a company's overall IPO revenue, but larger transactions usually come with lower underwriting fees. For instance, during Facebook's IPO last year which raised $16 billion, the underwriters only charged a fee of 1.1%, partly due to the massive scale of the transaction and partly because underwriting such a high-profile deal enhances the reputation of the investment banks. It remains unclear what proportion of the fees Twitter plans to pay to the underwriters.

A source revealed that Twitter’s CFO, Mike Gupta, is responsible for IPO-related matters and is currently in contact with investment banks. Another source indicated that Twitter is likely to raise over $1 billion in its IPO, and it is expected to go public before Thanksgiving.

Other sources mentioned that Goldman Sachs will serve as the lead underwriter for Twitter's IPO, with Anthony Noto, co-head of the Global Communications, Media, and Technology Group at Goldman Sachs, playing a key role in this transaction. Previously, other underwriters reported to Reuters included Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan Chase, and Bank of America, among others.

Twitter declined to comment on the news. Based on analysts' estimates, Twitter's valuation is approximately $15 billion. Assuming it issues around 10% of its shares, the proportion of total underwriting fees could reach 4% to 5%, meaning that investment banks would collectively receive underwriting fees ranging from $60 million to $70 million. In comparison, Facebook's underwriters received a total of $176 million in underwriting fees. (Tang Feng)
