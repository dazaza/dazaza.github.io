First, the composition of car paint

Car paint consists of three parts: primer, intermediate coating, and topcoat.

Since car beauty care and maintenance mainly focus on the topcoat, we will introduce the components and characteristics of the car topcoat in detail.

Car topcoats are divided into two types: solid color paint (commonly known as plain color paint) and metallic glitter paint (commonly known as metallic paint), each roughly accounting for half. In recent years, the development of metallic paint series has been rapid, and colors have also developed towards high transparency, depth, and high color direction.

Most car topcoats are composed of film-forming substances (resins), pigments (including extender pigments), solvents, and additives.

1. Pigment is the substance that gives color to the topcoat. It does not evaporate and gives the paint its covering ability.

2. Film-forming substance is the material that determines the type of paint. It is generally composed of natural resins or synthetic resins. Its function is to keep the pigment bright, make it strong and durable, and enable it to adhere to the surface of the car body.

3. Solvent is the volatile component in the paint. Its main function is to fully dissolve the resin in the paint film so that the paint can be applied normally.

4. Additives generally account for no more than 5% of the paint, serving to accelerate drying and enhance gloss, improving the paint's chemical resistance.

Where does rust hide?

The most annoying part about the car appearance is rust, which is mainly caused by direct contact between metal panels and the outside world. Besides common causes like collisions, scratches, and neglect leading to rust over time, there's another situation: when driving, small stones kicked up by the tires of the car in front can cause point collisions, making the paint surface peel off in small spots, forming small rust spots. These small traces are often overlooked, so regular checks of the car body, engine hood, and around the car are necessary once any signs are found, immediate action should be taken.

In addition, places where water easily accumulates on the car body, such as the edges of the wheel arches, the bottom and corners of the doors and trunk, can easily produce rust over time. If the drainage holes at the bottom of the door are blocked or not very smooth, water seeping in during rain or washing will accumulate inside the door for a long time. After a period, rust will start from the inside out, and by the time it's noticed, it will be difficult to handle.

Therefore, keeping the drainage holes of the doors and various parts of the car body clear is an important task that cannot be ignored in daily maintenance. The keyhole of the car door, the area around the glass strips, and the movable parts of the wiper bracket are also places prone to rust, requiring constant attention.

Handling minor car body injuries:

If the rusting is not severe, you can first use fine water sandpaper dipped in water to gently grind away the rust spots, completely wiping them off, then applying a layer of primer to ensure that the rust will not expand or worsen. If it's a new scratch, you can clean it and directly apply the primer. Most vehicles come with a small can of original factory paint. Although this treatment might not look great, it poses no significant issue for small areas.

You can also buy a touch-up pen beforehand. Once the primer dries, use water sandpaper to level it, then use the touch-up pen for coloring. This method can be used for small area scratches or inconspicuous locations, allowing you to repair the damaged areas yourself.

Composition of Car Paint and Afflictions:

Automotive paint provides cars with a handsome appearance and excellent protective properties, increasing their value and extending their lifespan. Since car paint contains organic components, it naturally oxidizes and deteriorates due to exposure to harmful substances in nature.

Second, Common Types of Damage to Car Paint:

Since car paint is primarily made of resin-based organic compounds, it has characteristics such as low ignition points, easy oxidation, and volatility. Many substances in nature can severely damage it, including natural oxidation, scale buildup, car washing, bird (insect) droppings, iron powder, acid rain, tree sap, and improper care. All these factors can destroy its decorative effect, reduce its protective capacity, and even cause cracking of the paint surface, making it difficult to repair.

1. Oxidation Hazards:

- Hazard Level: Severe

- Nearly 99% of car paint fading is caused by oxidation.

- Car paint is made up of organic components, which naturally possess oxidative characteristics. Over time, the paint itself will naturally oxidize, leading to dullness, loss of luster, and what is commonly referred to as the degradation of the paint.

2. Improper Care Hazards:

- Hazard Level: Severe

- Nowadays, the quality of automotive paint is relatively reliable. Normally, oxidation and discoloration occur about six months after the car is manufactured. However, improper care can cause oxidation and fading within a short period (2-3 months).

- While caring for car paint stems from good intentions, choosing incorrect products and methods can lead to counterproductive results—damaging rather than protecting the paint.

- There are roughly three types of improper care:

- Using oxidizable products (all products containing oil components oxidize themselves), causing harm to the paint.

- Frequent polishing and grinding (including pure polishing, waxing, and sealing), which thins the paint layer over time, ultimately losing its decorative effect and protective ability.

- Dry wiping of dust on the paint surface. Many car owners habitually wipe dust with towels or blankets, dragging sand particles across the paint surface and causing scratches.

3. Scratch Hazards:

- Hazard Level: Moderate

- During driving, due to high speed, sand particles in floating dust can create fine scratches on the car paint. Similarly, during car washing, sand particles stuck on the sponge can scratch the paint when rubbed against the surface. Accumulated fine scratches can cause the paint to lose its luster and become dull. Additionally, scratches on the paint surface can retain dirt and acid rain, accelerating the oxidation process and making the paint age faster.

4. Bird (Insect) Dropping Hazards:

- Hazard Level: Severe

- Birds and insects flying around can easily drop their acidic droppings onto the car paint. Although the affected area is small, the strong acidity can melt the paint entirely, causing discoloration or, in severe cases, swelling and cracking.

5. Scale Buildup Hazards:

- Hazard Level: Moderate

- After washing the car, many white spot marks left behind are due to scale buildup.

- Typically, tap water or well water is used for washing, which contains large amounts of calcium and iron ions. If the water isn't wiped dry promptly after washing, these components will remain on the paint surface after evaporation. If not cleaned immediately, they form hard spots known as "paint surface stones." To remove "paint surface stones," abrasive agents or polishing agents must be used, inevitably causing wear on the paint.

6. Iron Powder Hazards:

- Hazard Level: Severe

- Near factories, especially along railways and roads, the air contains a large amount of metallic dust (commonly known as iron powder).

- When the car is stationary, iron powder in the air settles on the paint surface and can be washed off.

- When the car is moving, due to forward thrust, the iron powder penetrates the paint surface. Penetrating iron powder cannot be removed by regular washing methods (iron powder removers can be used).

- Since the molecular structure of iron is unstable, it oxidizes easily. It reacts chemically with the paint, causing resonance oxidation. Unlike other harmful substances that erode the paint from the outside inward, iron powder penetrates the paint, causing rapid corrosion both outward and inward. Thus, the damage caused by iron powder to car paint is very serious.

7. Tree Sap Hazards:

- Hazard Level: Severe

- Trees produce secretions during spring, summer, and autumn, known as tree sap. If a car is parked under a tree, tree sap will inevitably fall on the paint surface. It can be washed off while still wet, but if left for too long, it hardens on the paint surface and cannot be washed off. Due to its strong acidity, tree sap can severely damage the paint, causing pits and cracks, which are difficult to repair.

8. Acid Rain Hazards:

- Hazard Level: Severe

- Acid rain (or fog and snow) has strong oxidizing properties. It chemically reacts with all components of the paint molecules, destroying the paint structure and significantly reducing its decorative and protective abilities. Acid rain oxidation causes the car paint surface to become uneven, even forming tiny spots. During subsequent rain or car washes, water droplets linger on these spots, further exacerbating the damage. Paint surfaces damaged by acid rain cannot return to their original state, and even polishing cannot eliminate the damage caused by acid rain.
