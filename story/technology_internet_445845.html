Financial Management Principles

Young white-collar workers should learn the "live within your means" financial management lesson from the older generation, adhere to the "four-part" moderate financial management approach, and emphasize the "mid-term" investment principle in the gold market.

Young white-collar and golden-collar friends often complain about not having enough money. I myself have unknowingly fallen into a state of financial chaos. However, when we look at our parents' generation, their salaries were only a fraction of ours, and their living conditions were much worse than ours now. They also never heard of scientific financial management concepts, yet they always managed their limited income well—how much for food, how much for buying clothes at the end of the year, how much as a reserve—and their lives seemed very systematic.

The difference between the two generations comes down to one point: whether or not one can consistently follow the basic principle of "living within your means." We young people, upon seeing something nice, can't help but buy it; hearing that stocks are rising, we hastily "follow the trend," never considering whether we have the funds or ability to handle such operations, leading to many financial management mistakes.

"The Four-Part" Financial Management Never "Exceeds"

Because I realized this truth, over the past few years, I have clearly divided my assets into four parts: 40% as real estate, which is the foundation for urban young white-collar workers to settle down and build happy families, and must be the most important part. 30% goes into stock and fund investments because this method has a wide range of choices, relatively low risk, and relatively high returns, so I consider it my most important investment method. 20% is allocated to gold speculation, since this is an industry I am familiar with, and I also have some "near-water pavilion gets the moon first" opportunities. The remaining 10% stays in the bank for emergencies and serves as temporary investment funds, which can participate in investment activities such as collections and trusts.

After having these four parts of "wealth" in mind, I strictly follow the rules and never exceed them in investment. I wouldn’t empty my wallet just because gold was booming, nor would I blindly follow trends due to market rumors about a certain stock's rise. Because real estate and bank deposits together account for 50% of my total assets, my life is stable and happy, and my financial management skills have improved with practice.

Some friends might think that my "four-part" investment method is overly conservative and cautious, not quite in line with the progressive spirit of contemporary white-collar workers, thus missing out on many money-making opportunities. But I don't see it that way. We shouldn't only see those who became rich overnight by "luck" while ignoring the reality that many impetuous investors lost money; it’s like buying lottery tickets where the chance of winning is only one in hundreds of thousands. As young people, we tend to be too eager for quick gains, focusing on shortcuts while neglecting the efficiency achieved through gradual progress.

Following Trends Rarely Earns Big Money

Take the gold industry, which I’m familiar with, for example. Gold has been booming for two whole years, but how many professionals in the industry have really made big money from it? From what I’ve seen around me, there are still very few. When it comes to gold investment, blindly following trends with an overly aggressive attitude is completely unacceptable. Mid-term investment, prudent operation, and steady returns are the best strategies.

Many friends hear that gold makes money and assume it offers high returns, so for months they've been frequently buying and selling. They've paid a lot in fees but haven't seen much profit. After all, gold costs over 150 yuan per gram, and its price is expensive. No matter how much it rises, the increase won't surpass the growth potential of high-performance individual stocks priced at a few yuan or tens of yuan per share. Applying the short-term stock trading principle to gold trading is fundamentally wrong. Of course, if you buy a gold product and wait patiently for two years, you will definitely make money, but the return rate won't exceed 30%, making the opportunity cost too high and less suitable for our taste as young people.

Gold Investment Should Be Operated in "Waves"

My usual approach is to focus on "wave" operations. I identify the recent upward trend and set an upper and lower limit of 5 to 10 yuan per gram for myself. I buy at the low point and sell once it exceeds the upper limit, never lingering. Therefore, my gold investment has almost never lost money, and my average return rate exceeds 5%. As a young person, with these returns, I can purchase more luxury items that I desire.
