【Qing Dynasty Culture】 How did the Torghut return to their motherland?

In 1771 (the 26th year of Emperor Qianlong's reign), a Mongolian Torghut tribe living downstream of the Ejin River in Europe (today's Volga River in Russia) embarked on a journey back to China, greatly exciting Emperor Qianlong.

Due to similar living customs and cultures, before the Qing Dynasty entered the pass, they already had close ties with various Mongolian tribes. After entering the pass, their relationship became even closer, and many Mongolian leaders were conferred the title of prince by the Qing Dynasty.

The Torghut Mongols are one of the four tribes of the Oirat Mongols. They originally herded near Yal in the Tarbagatai area (today's northwest of Tacheng in Xinjiang and parts of Central Asia). In the 1630s, due to conflicts with the leader of the Zunghar Mongol tribe, Batu Erhun Taiji, the tribe's leader Ho鄂erlek led the entire tribe and some herdsmen from other tribes, carrying tents and livestock, westward to graze on the grasslands downstream of the Ejin River. However, this area was within the sphere of influence of the Russian Empire. The Tsar sent troops to harass them and forced them to swear allegiance to the Russian Empire. The Torghut Mongolian herdsmen refused to swear allegiance and frequently clashed with the Russians. The Torghut Mongolian herdsmen longed for their homeland and repeatedly sent envoys to the Qing government to present tributes, recount their hardships, and express their longing and loyalty.

In 1712, envoys from the Torghuts arrived in Beijing again. Emperor Kangxi warmly received the envoys who had traveled a long distance and commended their loyalty. Subsequently, Emperor Kangxi dispatched Tulichen to lead an envoy team through Siberia in Russia to visit the Torghuts. Two years later, they reached the lower reaches of the Volga River, met their long-separated Mongolian compatriots, and conveyed the emperor's greetings to Ayuqi Khan, the leader of the Torghuts. The envoys from the Qing Dynasty were warmly welcomed by the Torghut herdsmen. They said that the Mongols and Manchus share common roots, and their clothing and hats resemble those of the people of their homeland. They are also children of the homeland.

In 1758, the leader of the Torghuts sent envoys again, traveling for three years to reach Beijing, presenting gifts to Emperor Qianlong and expressing respect.

The Torghut people continuously resisted the aggression and enslavement of the Russian Empire. In the 1660s, they responded to the Don peasant uprising led by the famous Russian peasant leader Razin; at the end of the 17th century, they rose up again to support the Bashkir uprising.

In the 1760s, Wobasi, the great-grandson of Ayuqi Khan, inherited the khanate. He was a young and spirited man.

At that time, the Russian Empire was at war with Turkey and urgently needed manpower. Therefore, they forcibly ordered all Torghut men over sixteen years old to join the Russian army and fight on the front lines, simultaneously weakening the Torghuts. Some elderly people sighed: "The end of the Torghuts has come."

At this time, the Russians also forced Wobasi to hand over his son and three hundred noble youths as hostages. An angry Wobasi led all the herdsmen and shouted: "We will never be slaves! We are going to where the sun rises!"

On January 4, 1771, Wobasi gathered all the warriors of the tribe and announced that they would break away from the rule of Empress Catherine of the Russian Empire. The next day, a world-shocking uprising of the Torghut tribe against the Tsar broke out.

They killed Russian officials, packed up their tents and belongings, and set off in three groups towards the east where the sun rises.

When Empress Catherine learned that the Torghut Mongolian herdsmen had rebelled and were leaving Russian territory, she flew into a rage, saying: "This will bring an indelible shame to the Romanov family and the guardian eagle wearing the crown of Peter the Great!" She ordered the dispatch of large numbers of troops to surround and intercept them, attempting to keep the Torghut herdsmen enslaved under the Russian Empire forever.

Under the leadership of Wobasi Khan, the Torghut Mongolian herdsmen finally broke through the Russian army's blockade, overcoming difficulties brought by harsh climates, and after half a year of travel across thousands of miles, returned to Ili. However, they paid a heavy price. Of the seventeen ten thousand tribal members who set out, only seven ten thousand safely returned to Ili, with most losing their lives to war, disease, and hunger. The survivors were emaciated, ragged, and the children were pale and naked. Their cattle, sheep, and camels, which they relied on for survival, were almost entirely lost.

After learning that the Torghuts had reached Ili, Emperor Qianlong immediately sent officials to Ili to distribute relief items and arranged for them to graze in the Ili River basin. People from Ili and various places in Gansu, Shaanxi, Ningxia, and Mongolia raised more than two hundred thousand cattle and sheep, more than forty thousand stones of rice and wheat, more than fifty thousand fur coats, more than sixty thousand pieces of cotton cloth, and a large number of felt tents, which were delivered to Ili and handed over to the returning brothers and sisters.

In the autumn of 1775, when golden hues enveloped the northern land, Emperor Qianlong received and entertained Wobasi and other leaders multiple times at Mulan Hunting Ground and the Mountain Resort in Rehe (today's Chengde, Hebei). Emperor Qianlong conferred the title of Zhuo Li Ke Tu Khan on Wobasi and granted him the "Seal of the Administrator of the Southern Right Banner of the Old Torghut Tribe." Emperor Qianlong also personally wrote two inscriptions, "Record of the Entire Torghut Tribe Returning" and "Record of Favoring the Torghut Tribe," which were engraved on stone tablets inside the Putuo Zongcheng Temple in Chengde as a permanent memorial.
