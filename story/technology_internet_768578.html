Facebook publishes statistics on European governments' requests for user data for the first time

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 19:54:40

From the "PRISM scandal" to the government's request for user information from Internet companies, the United States has become the leading protagonist. However, in reality, European governments are just as aggressive as the U.S. when it comes to investigating and requesting online user data. On November 12, Facebook executives disclosed statistics on European national governments' requests for internet users' information.

On the 12th, the European Parliament held a hearing regarding network surveillance. While the eavesdropping and monitoring by U.S. intelligence agencies were the main topics, the relevant actions of European national governments were also addressed.

Richard Allan, Facebook’s EMEA (Europe, Middle East, Africa) Director of Public Policy, revealed during the hearing that in the first half of this year, Facebook received 8,500 requests from EU national governments for user information, involving more than ten thousand users.

The European countries that most frequently investigated Facebook users were the UK (1,975 requests in the first half), Germany (1,886 times), Italy (1,705 times), and France (1,547 times). According to European law, law enforcement departments in each country can directly request Facebook to provide certain users' profile information.

It is reported that in the United States, federal, state, and local law enforcement departments collectively made 12,000 requests for Facebook user information in the first half of the year, involving 21,000 users. During the same period last year, there were 10,000 requests, involving 19,000 users. Clearly, the frequency with which U.S. governments at all levels investigate Facebook users is on the rise.

Linda Griffin, Facebook's spokesperson in the UK, stated that the data disclosed by Allan was merely a part of the investigations conducted by European national governments on Facebook users. Because many European countries have signed Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties with the United States, law enforcement agencies in these European countries can also make requests to Facebook through the U.S. government to investigate users. Facebook finds it difficult to distinguish which requests originate from Europe and which come from U.S. law enforcement departments.

Although the telephone eavesdropping and network surveillance by U.S. intelligence agencies have drawn global attention, the actions of European countries have also caught media attention. On Monday, Allan called on governments around the world to be more flexible and transparent when investigating internet users under the guise of national security.

Allan noted that what is perplexing is that while governments request user data from Facebook, they themselves almost never provide any information.

Currently, Facebook does not charge government departments investigating user data, but Allan revealed that Facebook is still considering whether or not to implement charges.

Allan also stated that Facebook has 1.2 billion active users worldwide, and overall, the proportion of accounts under investigation is very small, affecting only a small number of users temporarily.
