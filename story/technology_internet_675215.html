Didiani Warm Reminder: Didiani [http://www.banglikonggu.com/] Sensory Development Status

Newborns appear to be always in a sleeping state, seemingly having little interaction with the outside world, let alone perceiving this all-new world. In fact, infants have sensory perception from birth and can perceive changes in the world, responding instinctively to external conditions. This is why babies cry due to hunger, cold, or a wet diaper, and smile sweetly when gently touched. As parents, you should understand your baby's sensory development and appropriately stimulate these abilities to promote their growth.

Vision:

Newborns respond to objects in front of them by looking at them right after birth. Carefully observing the eyes of newborns after a few days, you'll notice that the child’s eyes become much more sensitive than when they were first born. After waking up, a quiet child will open their eyes wide, curiously staring at you, appearing especially spirited. The time they keep their eyes open will be slightly longer, and they start to show interest in large objects. They like looking at faces, especially their mother's face, and also enjoy colorful things. Not only will they stare at you, but their gaze will follow you as you move left and right. By the end of the first month, their vision becomes even sharper.

Hearing Didiani [http://www.banglikonggu.com/]:

Within the first week, newborns' hearing is relatively poor because the middle ear drum room has not yet been fully filled with air, and there is a small amount of amniotic fluid in the outer ear canal, affecting hearing. After a few days, these factors disappear, and the newborn's auditory sensitivity improves. They like human voices, especially their mother's voice, and react positively when the mother speaks softly to them. Near the end of the first month, the child's hearing shows a relatively concentrated response, allowing them to better perceive sounds from the outside world.

Smell:

Newborns have a relatively developed sense of smell and make instinctive repulsive reactions to strongly stimulating odors, indicating that babies are born with olfactory preferences. Moreover, a sensitive sense of smell helps the baby distinguish and seek out familiar scents, which is why the baby can always find the breast position in the mother's embrace.

Taste:

Taste helps newborns identify the flavors of objects and is the most developed sensory perception at birth. Newborns are born with a relatively complete taste system, capable of distinguishing sour, sweet, bitter, and spicy flavors, and may even be more sensitive to flavors than their parents. Babies like to suck on sweet-tasting foods from birth and resist other flavored foods. This acute sense of taste is an instinctive expression of the baby's self-defense capability and represents an early awareness of self-protection. Parents can train and adjust the baby's appetite according to their taste preferences.

Touch:

Newborns already have a sense of touch from the moment they are born. When you pick them up, they like to cling tightly to your body, snuggling against you. When a baby cries, parents picking them up and gently patting them fully demonstrates the need to satisfy the newborn's tactile comfort. Newborns have tactile sensation capabilities for different temperatures, humidity levels, textures of objects, and pain. That is, they can feel hot and cold, as well as pain, preferring to touch soft-textured objects. The lips and hands are the most sensitive parts for touch, and they will immediately respond when lightly touched.

Temperature Sensation:

Newborns are already quite sensitive to temperature and can differentiate between high and low temperatures of objects. They are more sensitive to cold than heat. For example, newborns will cry uncomfortably in response to milk that is too hot or too cold, and they will also cry in discomfort when wearing cold clothes or wet diapers and underwear.

Pain Perception:

Newborns already have a sense of pain, but it is relatively dull, especially in areas such as the torso and armpits, where they are less sensitive. Therefore, even if accidentally hurt, the reaction might not be obvious. To prevent accidental injuries, parents should pay special attention to this characteristic of newborns.
