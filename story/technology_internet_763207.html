The Helplessness Behind the NOKIA 1050 Hot-selling Phenomenon

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 17:12:12

Friends in the mobile phone industry told me that Nokia has a very popular feature phone lately. It only supports 2G, has a small screen, no camera, cannot access the internet, yet it's selling incredibly well. Some optimists predict that it will become another phone with over 100 million units sold after the 1280. You have to know that feature phones can't be promoted through carrier channels now, so it can only be sold through traditional agency channels and e-commerce channels, without any subsidies. Its price of 160 is close to the subsidized low-end smartphones, but it still sells well.

Meanwhile, Nokia's high-end smartphone products, Lumia 920 and Lumia 925, are selling lukewarmly, so they hurriedly launched the Lumia 1020 to rescue the situation. But whether the gimmick of a 41-million-pixel camera can impress consumers is still unknown. At least I think the protruding part at the back of the phone is fatal, making one feel like playing with a camera all the time.

What does Nokia's success in the low-end market but failure in the high-end market imply?

Why is the 1050 selling so well?

When you get the 1050, you have a sense of time travel, as if returning to 8 or 9 years ago before the era of smartphones. It's very compact, very light, with a full plastic feel, and the charging interface is still the old fine round insert type from many years ago, making people familiar with mini USB interfaces feel nostalgic.

After playing with it briefly, here are some feelings: the boot speed is super fast, it turns on instantly; the functions are extremely simple, but it still comes with two games; the screen is very small, with a very low resolution; the keyboard feel is pretty good, giving me a sense of security compared to clicking on a screen; there's no camera. Overall, this kind of phone can only be used for communication, not entertainment.

The packaging lists a few highlights: a standby time of up to 35 days, an FM radio, and an LED flashlight function. However, quite annoyingly, the flashlight cannot be used if no SIM card is inserted. There are also two other highlights not mentioned on the packaging: affordable price and Nokia's long-standing durability quality.

Looking at it this way, this "miracle" phone doesn't have much to commend itself. It's even worse than past feature phones. From the perspective of a tech blogger, there's no technological progress in it, just rehashing products from years ago. But it's selling very well, why? Here are my guesses:

First, the feature phone market is still larger than expected.

Although we've entered the smart phone era, there are still 2 billion people in the world without a phone, and more than half of those who own a phone are still using feature phones. Even now, the shipment volume of feature phones is comparable to that of smart phones, which is quite astonishing. Quite a number of people still only use their phones as basic communication tools, like my parents.

Second, competitors have withdrawn, providing opportunities.

In the past, brands like Samsung, Sony, Moto, LG, etc., have almost all transitioned to making smart phones. Meanwhile, many counterfeit manufacturers in Huaqiangbei that were once creative have started to disdain making feature phones and instead focus on the low-end Android market. This has turned the feature phone market into a rare relative blue ocean, giving opportunities to models like the 1050.

Third, the 1050 has the Nokia brand and is cheap enough.

At a price of 160, in today's era of skyrocketing prices, it's not much. Think about buying a fixed-line phone, 160 can only buy something very ordinary. The 1050 is not only cheap but also has the Nokia brand. Compared to counterfeit phones, its quality is still solid.

Fourth, it doesn't generate data traffic, it's safe, and the standby time is long enough.

In fact, many smart phones are now quite affordable, but the purpose of buying a smart phone is still "to play" rather than "to make calls". Data usage can easily increase, and the cost of use isn't low. This phone returns to the most fundamental aspects of a phone, satisfying many people's needs. Some say it's a backup phone because of its long standby time; others say it's a student phone because it can limit addiction to the network; still others say it's an elderly phone because it won't accidentally incur data charges...

Demand + good price is the reason for the miracle comeback.

Nokia: rely on the low end or the high end?

As the former dominant player for many years, Nokia has always refused to follow trends, maintaining a reserved demeanor. This may be the reason for Nokia's repeated failures in the smart phone market.

On one hand, it couldn't humble itself to cooperate with its competitors who beat it, so it had to use Microsoft's WP platform. Of course, humility is just an excuse. To team up with Android, Nokia would have to give up too much. Moto's fate could very well be its future.

On the other hand, in smart phone product design, Nokia is still innovative. In a sea of similar smart phones, Nokia stands out in terms of shape, style, and configuration, with quite a bit of recognizability. But this leads to a result: a small group of loyal fans like it, while the mainstream consumer base doesn't approve. Because the mainstream customers have been educated that smart phones should be like that, not like this.

To be honest, after playing with the 920, I felt two words: heavy and clunky. The WP system is also quite inconvenient to use. Numbers reflect this point. In the first quarter of this year, Lumia's shipment volume was 5.6 million units, while iPhone was 47.8 million units, and Samsung was 66 million units. In China's smart phone market, Nokia's brand didn't rank in the top 10, and in carrier contract phone sales, the model rankings were all outside the top 20. So basically, it can be said to be a non-mainstream product, and people criticize it for being too expensive. Is this due to excessive pride?

In the face of lackluster performance in the smart phone market, Nokia's low-end feature phones have shown frequent highlights. In the first quarter of this year, Nokia sold 55.8 million feature phones, becoming the foundation for Nokia's survival. In fact, Nokia also treats these classic familiar veterans as a strategy. It announced that it would consider feature phones as the focus of its "next billion users" strategy.

Actually, the profit margin of feature phones is not low. After years of production capacity accumulation, Nokia has developed superior cost control capabilities. According to them, the profit margin of feature phones is about the same as smart phones, so you can imagine how much the cost of the 1050 machine is.

Therefore, it's not hard to see that Nokia is making a difficult choice between survival and the future. The low-end feature phone market is still large, enough to support Nokia's scale. But everyone can see that this is a declining market, and smart phones are the direction. However, Nokia still hasn't found a way to break through in the smart phone market.

If I had to choose, I'd still go for survival. Compared to the already lively smart phone market, there are still many poor people in the world waiting for better and cheaper products.
