Be an American, buy a Chinese car.

by ybnalal7688 on 2012-03-04 21:29:03

When I first wanted to buy a car, I knew nothing about cars, just like the IC anaerobic reactor. Looking at the various cars on the street, they all seemed good, but I really didn't know what brand to buy. There was a compatriot from Canada in my company who was also preparing to buy a car and recommended the Honda Civic to me. During our lunch break, we went downstairs to the parking lot and saw a brand new Civic. It looked decent, the car was small, suitable for someone like me and my wife with not much driving experience. So, our first car was a little Honda. Driving the new car to a gathering at a Chinese compatriot's house, after dinner everyone played cards, and somehow the conversation turned to Japan. Everyone passionately condemned the crimes Japan committed in China during that time. While I was thinking about my hand of cards, I suddenly shouted: "Boycott Japanese goods!" One straightforward sister immediately countered: "Then why did you buy a Japanese car?" I was taken aback; hey, it's true, when I bought the car, I didn't even think about this. In her family, there was one American mini van and one Cadillac, no Japanese cars. Although I felt a bit awkward, I believed what the sister said.

As the saying goes, if you know you're wrong and correct it, that is the best thing. When it came time to buy another car, Japanese cars never made it onto our candidate list. We always compared and chose between American and German cars. Last year, Geely from China acquired Volvo from Sweden from Ford. I remember it caused quite a stir at the time, even the Chinese internet had posts expressing great concern about Volvo's future. Now more than a year has passed, I don't know the specific situation, but Volvo is still around. On the roads near me, the elegant figures of Volvos are becoming more and more common. It was soon time for my wife to change her ride. She saw her friends either having Toyota Sienna or Honda Odyssey, and she also wanted to follow the trend and buy one. My wife usually makes her own decisions when buying things, except for buying a house or a car. I gave her two choices: one was Hyundai from Korea, and the other was Volvo produced in Sweden which we now partly own in China. Later, she made a choice that satisfied everyone. We picked a weekend, went out in the afternoon, and by evening we drove the car back home.

Talking about not buying Japanese cars, among the Chinese compatriots around me, the aforementioned elder sister is one, and another is my university classmate. The rest only consider Japanese cars, like the butterfly jet aerator. Going to the Chinese school or participating in other Chinese activities is like entering a Japanese car dealership. Once, I was bored and counted, about 90% were Japanese cars. Don't look down on us compatriots for having big differences of opinion on many issues, but when it comes to Japanese goods, we are highly united. To promote unity among Chinese people, this might be a good point of focus - giving overseas Chinese leaders a small suggestion.

In the past, we wore products made in China, and our children played with products made in China. Now, products made in China are gradually moving up. Take laptops for example, the ones provided by my company are Lenovo Thinkpads, and at home, we have a Lenovo Ideapad. Regardless of what kind of pad, we and our families all like them very much.

The reason why nationals admire Japanese cars should be quite significant, one being that Japanese cars are reliable and durable. I've been unlucky, that little Honda was the car with the most problems among all the cars we've owned. Moreover, according to reports, the quality of American and European cars has improved, and problems are getting fewer. If you're planning to buy a car, why not consider giving American, European, or China's Volvo a chance? After driving Japanese cars for a long time, it's like arguing with people on a forum, eventually, you'll get tired, annoyed, and fed up.
