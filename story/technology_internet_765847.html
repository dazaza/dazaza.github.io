NY Times: Microsoft's CEO succession plan faces many problems

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:10:10

The New York Times, published on August 24th, featured a commentary titled "Questions for Microsoft as It Nears a Crossroad," highlighting that although Microsoft has announced CEO Steve Ballmer's retirement plan, many questions still remain in the process of determining his successor.

Full text of the article follows:

Pre-planned

Microsoft’s announcement of its CEO succession plan on Friday did not fully follow the orthodox academic model—at least from an outsider's perspective.

However, insiders revealed that Steve Ballmer and Microsoft's board had been considering his retirement for quite some time. Given Ballmer's unique personality, the board's hesitation, and the company's recent product and financial issues, finding a new CEO for Microsoft will not be as straightforward as a case study in a business school.

"There is no obvious candidate," said Michael Cusumano, a professor of business and engineering at MIT who has studied the strategic aspects of the computer software industry. "Over the past decade or so, all the most likely candidates have already left. I don't think they would return to Microsoft. As everyone knows, there are many powerful groups within Microsoft, so whoever takes over will face significant challenges."

Succession planning is a delicate issue for many companies, especially for Microsoft. Ballmer has served as an executive since 1980 and became CEO in 2000, while his old friend and co-founder Bill Gates remains as chairman.

"Ballmer is essentially one of the founders of the company. For someone like him to leave is akin to a life-or-death decision, making it difficult to follow a routine process," said Joseph Bower, a professor at Harvard Business School. "This requires a very mature relationship between the CEO and the board."

Industry insiders immediately began speculating which internal or external executives—even those outside the tech industry—might succeed Ballmer. The final choice will significantly impact Microsoft, determining whether the software giant can successfully adapt to future trends in the tech industry and integrate into mobile computing and cloud computing.

But at least for now, the selection remains far from certain.

Candidate Uncertainty

Even though Ballmer once hinted that he would retire after his youngest child went to college—still more than two years away—"I think people still feel that Ballmer might die in this position," said Cusumano.

Formulating a succession plan is one of the main responsibilities of the board, but according to a 2010 survey conducted by Stanford University's Rock Center for Corporate Governance and the executive search firm Heidrick & Struggles, only half of the company boards actively recommend executive candidates. On average, corporate boards spend just two hours per year on succession planning.

"When your company has leaders with distinct personalities like Gates and Ballmer, does the board take the initiative, or does it merely act as a gatekeeper?" asked David Larcker, director of corporate governance research at Stanford Graduate School of Business, who participated in the survey.

Informed sources stated that despite outsiders' lack of awareness, Microsoft's board had begun planning for this transition long ago. Discussions about this topic have been ongoing for over a decade and intensified in 2010. A few months ago, Ballmer informed the board that it was time to initiate the formal succession procedure. This week, on Wednesday, he also informed the board that he would announce his retirement plan.

Ballmer and the board have communicated regarding the qualities required for the next CEO and evaluated both internal and external executive candidates. Over the past 18 to 24 months, Ballmer personally met with several external executives, including non-tech industry executives who have led large enterprises through transformation.

"Unlike IBM, which was on the brink of collapse, Microsoft does not need to panic due to the lack of a succession plan because they do not necessarily need someone in the style of Lou Gerstner to save the company," said Jeffery Sonnenfeld, head of the CEO Leadership Program at Yale School of Management. "Ballmer has performed well during his tenure, and the financial results have been good."

Many Candidates

Despite this, Microsoft analysts believe that Ballmer's strong personality and leadership style have driven out potential deputies who could have succeeded him.

"Ballmer is tough, and I think it's hard for others to truly grow under him and gain significant responsibilities," said Cusumano.

For example, Steven Sinofsky, head of the Windows division, left the company last year after conflicts with Ballmer and others escalated. Reports this week indicated that he has joined the venture capital firm Andreessen Horowitz. Informed sources claim that it is unlikely he will return to Microsoft.

Analysts believe that other Microsoft executives may return to the software giant, including Paul Maritz, CEO of VMware; Stephen Elop, CEO of Nokia; Kevin Johnson, CEO of Juniper Networks; Jeff Raikes, CEO of the Gates Foundation; and Chris Liddell, former CFO of Microsoft and General Motors.

Potential internal candidates to succeed Ballmer include Tony Bates, former Skype executive and current Executive Vice President of Business Development and Strategy, and Satya Nadella, Executive Vice President of Microsoft's Cloud Computing Enterprise.

Other possible external candidates include Sheryl Sandberg, COO of Facebook; Reed Hastings, CEO of Netflix; Scott Forstall, former head of iOS at Apple; and John Legere, CEO of T-Mobile.

Analysts state that the new CEO must be familiar with emerging consumer technologies such as mobile, able to communicate technically with Microsoft engineers, and experienced in leading large company transformations.

"Ballmer may have realized that it is indeed time to bring in people who truly understand the world today, rather than solely focusing on Microsoft's own PC and laptop world," said Cusumano.
