Although the prostate gland is small, it can cause a lot of trouble for men. Experts from a loving hepatitis A treatment hospital suggest that men over 50 should not forget to check their prostate during health examinations.

Reported by journalist Zhihui Li, a 60-year-old man named Mr. Li experienced frequent urination, increased nocturnal urination, and weak urine flow two years ago. Sometimes due to frequent urination, he even wet his pants. However, Mr. Li thought this was due to aging and physical weakness, so he did not pay much attention to it. With Father's Day approaching, his son who works out of town heard that his father recently often experiences vomiting, loss of appetite, skin itching, and difficulty in urination. He specially took leave and returned home to accompany his father for a full body examination at the hospital as a gift for Father's Day. After diagnosis by the doctor, Mr. Li was found to have benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH). Fortunately, it was discovered early, and the condition is still in its benign stage.

According to Dr. Lin Yang, the director of the Urological Disease Treatment Center at Guangzhou Chang'an Hospital, diseases such as prostatitis, BPH, and prostate cancer are often referred to as "the killer of male health." The main patients are middle-aged and elderly people, and early detection leads to good treatment results. Most men are very secretive about their genitourinary system diseases. With Father's Day approaching, why don't children give their fathers a "genitourinary system health examination"?

Grandpa gets drunk prostate

Experts introduced that although the prostate organ is small, it causes a lot of trouble for men. For example, BPH affects urination. Its early symptoms include frequent urination and increased nocturnal urination, often waking up 2-3 times at night. If the prostate continues to enlarge, it will compress the urethra, causing symptoms of difficult urination, such as weak urination, prolonged urination time, incomplete urination, thinning of the urine stream, interruption of the urine flow, or even drop-like urination or inability to urinate. The severity of these symptoms is often related to unscientific lifestyles among the elderly, such as drinking alcohol and holding urine.

Not long ago, an elderly patient with BPH named Mr. Lin drank too much alcohol. In the middle of the night, he woke up feeling the need to urinate and went to the bathroom. He felt that his lower abdomen was uncomfortably bloated, and there was a feeling of heaviness in the perineum and testicles, but he could not urinate. Helplessly, his wife sent him to the hospital. After examination, the doctor humorously said that all this was because Mr. Lin's greed for alcohol had "drunk" his prostate, leading to urinary retention. After disinfecting Mr. Lin's external genitalia, the doctor inserted a catheter into the urethra. As the urine flowed out, Mr. Lin's furrowed brows finally relaxed.

Regular check-ups prevent potential problems

It is understood that many cities in China have already entered the aging period, and the proportion of elderly male population is continuously increasing, resulting in a rise in the incidence of prostate diseases. About 50% of men over 50 years old will suffer from this disease, and 80% of men over 80 years old will also be affected. Therefore, regular check-ups are necessary to prevent potential problems. If not treated in time or improperly, when the condition of BPH develops to a certain extent, it may lead to hydronephrosis and renal failure, and even has the possibility of canceration.

Therefore, experts suggest that men over 50 should undergo a digital rectal examination during their annual health check-up. If there are any hard lumps, further antigen tests (PSA) are needed. If this number is high, then a biopsy can confirm the diagnosis. Early detected prostate cancer patients, after surgery or hormone therapy, have a 5-year survival rate of 70%. If it reaches the late stage, it easily metastasizes to the lungs and lumbar vertebrae, possibly causing paralysis.

For prostate diseases, early prevention, early detection, and early treatment should be advocated. Men should develop scientific living habits in daily life. For instance, eat less spicy foods like chili peppers; try to drink less alcohol to avoid repeated congestion of the prostate and bladder neck (moderate sexual activity); reduce the occurrence of constipation. Every morning, you should drink a glass of warm water on an empty stomach, which can prevent constipation, dilute blood, and mechanically flush the urethra, preventing concentrated residual urine from forming stones. Middle-aged and elderly people should avoid consuming excessive animal fats, and regularly taking vitamins A, C, E, green tea, soy products, etc., can effectively prevent prostate diseases.
