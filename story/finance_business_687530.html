Enoki mushrooms and Japanese shiitake mushrooms can help fight cancer, Medical Seeking Network reports.

by lokes350 on 2012-03-02 16:24:17

Researchers from the National University of Singapore's Faculty of Medicine have discovered that enoki mushrooms contain a protein that can prevent allergies such as asthma, rhinitis, and eczema. This protein can also boost immunity and even fight viral infections and cancer.

Professor Tsai Ko-Yuan from the Department of Pediatrics at the National University revealed that allergies are immune system disorders triggered by Th2 cell cytokines. Antibodies should combat foreign substances invading the body, but when the immune system is out of balance, the antibodies produced by patients do not attack the "enemy" but instead attack their own cells and tissues.

The stems of enoki mushrooms contain a large amount of protein that can stimulate the body to produce more Th1 cell cytokines, which suppress diseases caused by Th2 cell cytokines. She said: "Our bodies actually contain many mechanisms to fight disease, but when some parts malfunction, we need external factors to adjust them back. The protein in enoki mushrooms plays this role." Moreover, Professor Tsai also found that this protein can stimulate the natural anti-cancer mechanism in cervical cancer patients, allowing them to fight cancer cells with their own immune capabilities. She said: "Even if you don't have cancer, you can eat enoki mushrooms to strengthen your immune system, but overcooking should be avoided. Eating hot pot is one of the best methods."

However, Professor Tsai indicated that patients with lupus or arthritis should avoid eating enoki mushrooms regularly because these diseases are triggered by Th1 cell cytokines, and eating enoki mushrooms might worsen the condition.

Another research team from the National University of Singapore's Faculty of Medicine studied shiitake mushrooms (commonly known as "Japanese winter mushrooms") and found that they are highly effective in preventing and treating cancer. Associate Professor Mary Wong from the Department of Microbiology revealed that lentinan, a polysaccharide found in shiitake mushrooms, has many immune-boosting properties, including enhancing natural killer cells, thereby improving the immune system to fight viruses or cancer cells.

She extracted lentinan from the caps of shiitake mushrooms and fed it to mice for seven consecutive days. Then she injected the mice with cancer cells, and the mice were able to suppress 95% of the tumors. When another group of mice already suffering from cancer consumed lentinan, their tumors shrank by 85%. Thus, lentinan in shiitake mushrooms can both prevent and treat cancer. She revealed that in Japanese hospitals, lentinan has been approved for use in human medical treatments. It was reported that injecting lentinan into patients with stomach cancer and colorectal cancer increases their chances of survival. However, the current method of extracting lentinan is very expensive. She and her student, Dr. Andrew Yeh, developed an extraction method that can extract over 100 times more lentinan than traditional methods.

Associate Professor Wong said: "Our research focuses on enoki and shiitake mushrooms, but in fact, various types of mushrooms contain proteins and lentinan that enhance immunity and fight cancer, just in different quantities. Therefore, eating more mushrooms is beneficial to health. However, mushrooms are high in fiber, and eating too much may cause diarrhea."
