So instead of beating around the bush, I might as well tell you some specific and important X concepts, along with a lot of information about preparing for interviews.Additional DisclaimerThis blog is unrelated to Google. Google doesn't know I'm publishing these tips. This is just between you and me, alright? Don't tell them I gave you advice. Just ace the interview, and we'll both be gentlemen.Here, I'll only talk about general software engineer positions and corresponding interview processes.These tips are universal and not specifically tailored for Google but applicable to other software companies as well. In fact, I could have written tips for my first software development job 20 years ago. That is, these tips are timeless, at least within our careers.Obviously, these tips won't guarantee you a job. My hope is that following these tips will help you perform at your best during the interview.Oh, uh, why mention Google then?Ah, you're asking why I mentioned Google? Alright, let's start the conversation then, shall we?You: Should I work at Google just because they say so, or are there other benefits? Will I be safe and happy there? Should I apply right away?Me: Yes.You: Which question was that? Wait, what do you mean by yes? You don't even know who I am!Me: Buddy, the answer is yes. (You might be a woman, but I'll still call you buddy.)You: But... but my laziness is almost paralyzing. I'm comfortable staying at my current company, or at least I've developed some tolerance for its discomforts. I know a lot of people here, but no one at Google! I might have to learn Google's build system and technical stuff! I'll have no trust or reputation there, and I might have to start from scratch! I waited too long, and there's no room for advancement there! I'm scared...Me: Buddy, the answer is already yes, right? It's a constant. Everyone else who joined Google was in the same situation, except for a small group of famous people with beards that make Gandalf look inferior. But they're rare exceptions. Every applicant has reasons not to apply, just like you. And everyone here says: Wow, I'm so glad I came to work here! So just apply, but prepare first.You: But what if I'm misjudged? Instead of taking the risk of failure, I might as well abandon this opportunity completely!Me: That's partially correct, at least. I didn't do well in my first interview, but I begged like a stray dog until they gave me a second chance. I seized the moment when they were vulnerable and nailed it. In the second round, I prepared in advance, and my performance improved significantly.The key is that Google has a famously high false negative rate in the industry, meaning we sometimes reject qualified candidates because we prefer that over occasionally hiring unqualified ones. This problem exists across the entire industry, just with varying proportions in different companies. At Google, the false negative rate is quite high. I don't know exactly how high, but I do know many smart and qualified candidates didn't pass our interviews. It's unfortunate.But the truly important takeaway is: If you don't get an offer, you may still be capable of working here. So, don't let it crush your fragile ego.Anyone I know understands that false negatives are entirely random and unrelated to your skills or qualifications. False negatives can arise from a series of factors, including but not limited to:Ah no, don't get into the Interview Adversary Circle!Yes, unfortunately, you need to worry about this.What is this, you ask? Okay, back when I worked at Amazon, we conducted (and undoubtedly still do) extensive discussions on this topic. Our final conclusion was that every Amazon employee E has at least one Interview Adversary Circle: a group of other employees S who would reject E during interviews. Understanding the root cause is important for candidates, so I'll share some of my findings over the years.First, you cannot tell interviewers what is important and what isn't. Not in any company. Unless they explicitly ask for your opinion. There's only a very narrow window after an engineer graduates from university where they can be taught about interviews. After that, the window closes, and they believe they are already excellent interviewers. They never change their questions, styles of questioning, interview styles, or feedback styles ever again.This is a problem. But I've hit my head against this wall multiple times and stopped trying.Second issue: Every experienced interviewer has their own favorite topics and possibly specific questions they believe accurately assess a candidate's ability. Any two interviewers' question pools may vary widely, or even have no overlap at all.A common example everywhere is this: Interviewer A always tests C++ details, file systems, network protocols, and discrete mathematics. Interviewer B always tests Java details, design patterns, unit testing, Web frameworks, and software project management. For any given candidate, if Interviewer A and B are both in the interview circle, they may give very different evaluations. In fact, back in the day, A and B might have mutually rejected each other during their interviews, but they happened to encounter Interviewer C, who gave them problems related to data structures, Unix utilities, and processes and threads, which A and B were proficient in.That's almost exactly what happens when you get an offer from a tech company: pure luck. Due to this inherently flawed interview process, it's possible that someone in the interview circle may dislike you, even if you're Alan Turing. Actually, if you were Alan Turing, it would be worse because, obviously, you wouldn't know C++.The bottom line is, if you're going to interview at any software company, you must prepare for the possibility of encountering one or more people from your Interview Adversary Circle among your interviewers. If this happens, you'll try your hardest and then be told you're not suitable for the position, and you'll feel frustrated. As long as you're not extremely frustrated, it's no big deal. You should be happy that you feel frustrated because it means you're a normal human being.Afterward, you should wait 6 to 12 months and then reapply. That's basically the best solution I (or anyone I know) can come up with for the false negative problem. Let's forget the past and start over. Many people here were hired on their second or third attempt, and they're doing great now.You can do it too.By the way, I feel much better about the potential rejection risks.Nice! Let's dive into these tips then.If you read carefully earlier, you'd realize I'm Interviewer D. This means my personal preferences for questions and topics are just that—personal, neither better nor worse than others'. So I can't tell you what they are, no matter how much I'd like to. Doing so would offend Interviewers A, B, C all the way to Interviewer X who prefer other questions.On the contrary, I want to help you prepare for some common topics that I believe most interviewers at companies like Google use. Generally speaking, these companies develop a lot of their own software and conduct significant distributed computing. Other types of tech companies, in stark contrast, outsource everything to consultants and try to use as much third-party software as possible. My tips are only effective for companies similar to Google.So you might as well consider it just Google, right?First, let's discuss non-technical preparation.Warm-upNo one steps into a boxing ring without warming up. Tip: You can bring boxing gloves to your interview. No, wait, sorry, I mean you should warm up beforehand.How do you warm up? It basically involves short-term and long-term warm-ups, and you should do both.Long-term warm-up means: Spend one to two weeks studying and practicing before the interview. You need to get your mind into the general mode of solving problems on a whiteboard. If you can solve problems on a whiteboard, all other mediums (laptops, shared network files, etc.) will be a piece of cake. So focus on the whiteboard.Short-term warm-up means: Get plenty of rest the night before the interview, and then do a lot of fast-paced warm-ups (i.e., practice problems) on the morning of the interview.The two best long-term warm-up methods I know are:1) Review a book on data structures and algorithms. Why? Because it's most likely to help you improve your ability to recognize problems. When you understand the category of the interviewer's question without needing more explanation, many interviewers will be satisfied. For example, if they ask you about coloring U.S. states with different colors, recognizing it as a graph-coloring problem in graph theory will earn you points, even if you don't remember the exact algorithm.If you do remember it, you might quickly arrive at the answer. So the best preparation for interviews is to practice recognizing problem types and figuring out which algorithms and data structures are best suited for each type.My absolute favorite for this kind of preparation is Steven Skiena's "The Algorithm Design Manual." It helped me more than any other book in understanding how universally prevalent (and important) graph problems are, so I think every programmer should have a copy. The book also introduces basic data structures and sorting algorithms, making it worth the price. However, the real treasure is in the second half of the book, where there are single-page encyclopedias covering countless useful problems and methods to solve them, presented concisely. Almost every page has a simple diagram to make it easy to remember. It's an excellent way to learn how to recognize hundreds of problem types.Other interviewers I know recommend "Introduction to Algorithms." It's a true classic and invaluable, but reviewing it might take you more than two weeks. If you want to feel confident for the interview, you might consider delaying your application until you've mastered its essence.2) Find a friend to simulate interviews. Your friend should randomly ask you some interview questions, and you should write out solutions on a whiteboard. Regardless of how tired or lazy you feel, finish all the questions. Simulate as much as you can handle.Before my first Google interview, I didn't do either of these preparations, and I was shocked to find that I performed terribly on the whiteboard because it had been seven years since my last interview. It was difficult. Moreover, I had forgotten a bunch of algorithms and data structures that I once knew or at least heard of.By spending a week practicing these methods, I was well-prepared for my second Google interview. My performance improved significantly in the second round. These exercises made all the difference.For short-term preparation, all you can do is keep yourself in top form. Don't start without warming up. Solve some problems, review your materials. Drink some coffee; it helps sharpen your mind. Believe it or not, I do! Ensure you spend at least an hour practicing before your interview starts. Treat the interview like a sports game, concert, or exam—whatever gets you to your peak performance if you warm up first.Mental PreparationAlright! Now you're a programming expert with a long list of achievements. It's time to forget all that and focus solely on passing the interview.You must present yourself as humble, open-minded, and focused.If you appear arrogant, interviewers will wonder if they want to work with you. The most common sign of arrogance is questioning the validity of the interviewer's questions, which will undoubtedly annoy them, as I previously pointed out. Remember when I said you can't teach interviewers how to interview? Exactly, and if you're the candidate, this is even more true.So, don't ask things like: Good grief, are algorithms really that important? Have you ever had to do that in real life? I'll never need to do that. Asking that will get you rejected, so don't ask those kinds of questions. Treat every question as valid, even if you're frustrated because you don't know how to solve it.If you get stuck, seek help or hints. Some interviewers might deduct points for this, but occasionally it can help you break through difficult questions that might otherwise leave you silent for half an hour, allowing you to achieve excellent results.Don't mumble while thinking.Don't try to change the subject to answer a different question. Don't try to divert the interviewer's attention by telling war stories so they can't ask you questions. Don't try to intimidate your interviewer. Focus on each question they ask and do your best to answer them fully.Some interviewers may not strictly require you to write code, but they often expect you to write some code on the whiteboard at some point in your solution. They might hint at it, but they may not directly say: Now I require you to write some code on the whiteboard. If you're unsure, you should ask if they want to see code.Different interviewers have vastly different expectations for code. Personally, I don't care much about syntax (unless what you write is clearly impossible in any programming language, in which case I'll interrupt to confirm you're not a circus clown and that part of the code is indeed wrong). However, some interviewers are very picky about syntax, and some might silently reject you for missing a semicolon or curly brace without telling you. I think these interviewers are... well, it's a technical term that sounds like 'asshole,' but they think they're top-notch technical evaluators, and there's no reasoning with them.So, ask. Ask if they care about syntax, and if they do, do your best to get it right. Check your code carefully from different angles and distances. Pretend it's someone else's code, and you're tasked with finding bugs. You'll be surprised at how easily you can make mistakes when standing two feet from the whiteboard while the interviewer stares at your shoulder blades.It's okay to ask clarifying questions (and highly encouraged), occasionally confirming with the interviewer that your approach is correct.
