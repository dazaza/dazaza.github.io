Liu Yishou's 19-Year Search for His Son Continues: Child Left Address but Was Still Sent to Welfare Institute

by anonymous on 2011-05-12 17:44:05

Yesterday, the bizarre story of Li Xuwén, a man from Anhui who has settled in Suzhou and has been searching for his lost son - "19 Years of Searching, Son Adopted by Americans" - was reported by our newspaper. After being exposed to the public, society sympathized greatly with the experiences of this father and son duo, sparking a large discussion on emotion and law. Many media outlets wanted to do something for the father and son, while some netizens even initiated a Weibo relay to find Li Xiáng. In addition, some meticulous readers and netizens raised four detailed questions about Li Xuwén's 19-year search for his family. The reporter followed up on these issues.

[Dynamic Section]

CCTV plans to follow up, hoping to convey the voices of the parents to Li Xiáng

A series of coincidences led to this event that cannot clearly be called a tragedy. I can no longer tell if this child is lucky or unlucky. ... After yesterday’s article was published and reprinted by hundreds of websites, there were numerous comments from netizens. From early morning, reporters' phones kept ringing with calls from media colleagues. Central Television, Jiangsu Television, Suzhou Television, Global Times English Edition... All these colleagues hoped to contact Li Xuwén and Deng Pēng for further interviews.

Unlike the usual intensive media coverage seeking explosive news, many of these colleagues had very simple intentions. "I just want to help this father," one said. "If we can't get the address through litigation, then perhaps the most effective way is to pass on the couple's tortuous experience of looking for their family through various means, so that Li Xiáng takes the initiative to contact them."

Li Xuwén himself maintained a relatively low profile towards the media. After the reporter conveyed the hopes of his colleagues, this father expressed gratitude but also gently indicated that his original intention was just to find his son, not to create a sensational effect. Therefore, apart from agreeing to give his phone number to a few media outlets that contacted him earlier, he refused any other requests.

Some readers called for a Weibo relay, others queried international conventions

Someone needs to tell him. Preferably on Twitter, everyone should shout out loud - Li Xiáng, your parents didn't abandon you, they are calling you home to recognize your roots... Enthusiastic netizens also left messages suggesting on our Sina Weibo official account. This method gained recognition from many people. They stated that the key is to let the child know that his parents did not abandon him; otherwise, he will live in the shadow all his life or only understand the hardship when he becomes a parent himself.

Other readers suggested that Li Xuwén could review domestic and international regulations regarding transnational adoption and foster care practices. If the identity of the parents is unknown, authorized institutions must make every effort to find these parents. The search period for the child's birth family is determined by the relevant authorities of each country. Only after this can the adoption process begin. Readers wondered if this international convention would be useful.

[Doubt Section]

Lost at the train station, how did he end up at the Fuzimiao police station?

Maybe he took a train near Zhonghuamen (Nanjing South Station)

Yesterday, some meticulous readers read the article several times and found some details issues. For example, Li Xuwén's wife lost her child at a dumpling stall at the train station, so how did the child later end up at the Fuzimiao police station? These two places, one in the north and one in the south, are quite far apart!

Soon, a reader provided an answer: In the 1990s, there was a train station near Zhonghuamen, known as Nanjing South Station, where trains to Wuhu and Ma'anshan departed, which was very close to Fuzimiao! She herself studied at a university in Nanjing in 1995 and had a roommate from Wuhu who always bought tickets at Zhonghuamen station, which was then also called Nanjing South Station. Yesterday, the reporter found online that the Zhonghuamen station is the original Nanjing South Station, located at the start of the Ningwu Railway.

Why send the child to the welfare institute if he already gave an address?

Children under 14 years old were sent to the welfare institute at that time

Some netizens lamented that since the child already said his name was Li Qiáng and lived in Anhui Huí Fán Chāng, why couldn't the police check and connect with Anhui authorities?

Yesterday, the reporter contacted an old policeman at the Fuzimiao police station who has worked there since 1992. The officer checked the archives and found a record from May 16, 1992: A boy aged 6, claiming to be Li Qiáng, was brought in by the public.

The officer explained that the shift worker who handled the case has since transferred to another police station, so he could only speculate based on his own memory: The procedures for handling such cases in 1992 were different from today. At that time, the common practice was to send children over 14 years old to the repatriation station and those under 14 to the welfare institute. He explained that for the police station, the welfare institute was just a temporary place for the child since the police station couldn't take care of the child 24 hours a day. After sending the child to the welfare institute, the police station wasn't completely hands-off; during the observation period, if the parents came looking, the police station still had the responsibility to notify them.

Why didn't the police station check the information the child provided in Anhui? This old policeman also regretfully speculated that back then, there were no special standard requirements for handling such cases, and the police station was understaffed. He guessed that the officer handling the case probably thought it was another abandoned baby and thus followed the usual procedure, sending the child to the Nanjing Welfare Institute that same day. Back then, the conditions of the police force were far inferior to now, without the current interconnected police information platform, internet, and communication systems.

Does the welfare institute have records of Li Xiáng?

Records have been found, and only after 3 years with no contact was adoption considered

Yesterday, the reporter also found the record of Li Xiáng being sent to the welfare institute by the Fuzimiao police station on May 16, 1992, registered under the name Li Xiáng.

A person from the childcare department of the Nanjing Children's Welfare Institute introduced that usually, after children are sent over, they are announced for two months. If no parents or guardians come forward, then adoption is considered. Li Xiáng was adopted after living in the welfare institute for 3 years.

This person introduced that although it wasn't their duty, the welfare institute would go to great lengths to contact the biological parents of the children, both emotionally and reasonably. For example, between the previous year and the year before last, they helped a girl from Jiangxi find her parents. That child stayed in the welfare institute for a year. We all felt she wasn't an abandoned baby, so we searched through the police network and eventually found her, reuniting the family. Therefore, this person especially hopes that if there is any information, the police station should try their best to find the family, even if they need to search after sending the child to the welfare institute first.

A person from the childcare department also said: In the past, there were also cases where American adoptive families returned to Nanjing for a visit. If Li Xiáng and his family come back for a visit, we will definitely inform them of the information about his parents looking for him.

Besides litigation, is there any possibility of leniency?

Lawyer believes emotions allow, but laws don't

Some readers expressed that the adoption center was too rigid, wouldn't bending the rules and giving the address suffice? However, Luo Lijūn, the chief lawyer of Jiangsu Xidian Law Firm, introduced that since the Adoption Law clearly stipulates keeping the adopter's secrets confidential, and it involves foreign adoptions, touching on the laws of the other country, the actions of the Jiangsu International Marriage and Foreign Adoption Registration Service Center were beyond reproach.

"This matter is indeed quite bizarre. Emotionally, we all hope to quickly obtain an address to contact Li Xiáng," he said. However, legally, litigation cannot solve the problem. He pointed out that if they bent the rules and gave you the address, the adoptive family could sue this center for administrative infringement through the laws of the other country.

"The law is indeed harsh, and the lawsuit might even fail." Luo Lijūn shook his head and said, actually, if you think about it from their perspective, the adoptive family has its own reasons: originally living peacefully, why should their lives be disrupted? He personally believed that if Li Xuwén wants to sue, he could sue the Fuzimiao police station for nonfeasance because the station did not verify the information promptly at that time.

But what's the point of suing? The Fuzimiao police station doesn't have the address of the adoptive family either, Luo Lijūn said.
