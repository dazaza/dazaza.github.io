The low-priced iPhone: embodying Apple's offensive and defensive strategies

by anonymous on 2013-08-19 15:14:41

The rumored iPhone 5C

Over the past few years, Apple has been very successful in selling iPhones with ultra-high profit margins, achieving massive sales volumes. As a result, the company has reaped billions of dollars in profits. However, as Apple begins to release a large number of new products, profits have not increased, and the global smartphone market share of the iPhone continues to decline. This has led to a drop in the company's market value, indicating that the good times for Apple may be over.

This is undeniable. For Apple, the question is how to respond. The company has the most loyal customers globally, substantial assets available for expanding its product lineup, and what seems to be an inexhaustible cash reserve. Now, Apple needs to buy itself a future.

A Wall Street Perspective

I've previously written that Apple CEO Tim Cook should ignore Carl Icahn's plan to repurchase more shares. A report by Bloomberg more or less confirmed my point: stock buybacks are ineffective. Buyback programs rarely push stock prices to desired levels, nor do they easily convey to outsiders that the company is heading in the right direction.

BTIG analyst Walter Piecyk told Bloomberg reporter Adam Satariano, "What matters for Apple's stock is introducing a product that can put the company back on a path of profit growth."

You only need to look at the stock price movements after Apple announced its buyback program. On April 23, Apple announced an increase of $50 billion in its stock buyback fund, when the stock was trading at $406 per share. Despite a brief rise to $460, it hit a low of $394 on July 27, then largely rose steadily to $500, partly boosted by news of Icahn's stake and also due to anticipation of new products – primarily the two new iPhone models expected to launch on September 10.

What evidence is there to show the impact of the stock buyback program? Either you argue: 1) Apple's stock performance has been better than expected, making this just a psychological comfort since the increase exceeds the possible effect of the buyback on the stock price; 2) The program failed to prevent the significant drop in July; or 3) The impact on stock performance isn't actually noticeable.

However, we know this hasn't changed Wall Street's long-term view – Apple cannot keep making a lot of money indefinitely; Apple's competitors are more valuable. Wait, you might say: Apple is still the highest-valued company. Yes, but we're talking relative value – the price-to-earnings ratio. Consider this: even when Apple's stock reached over $700, it was only 17 times earnings per share, about the same as the current average of the S&P 500 index. Currently, Apple's P/E ratio is around 12, half of Google's and similar to Microsoft's. Analysts predict Apple's profit growth over the next five years will be twice that of Microsoft and comparable to Google, yet its stock performance mirrors the former. Think about it.

Abandoning Sacred Goals

Regardless of whether you believe Apple should operate for the benefit of shareholders or if you simply like Apple's products and hope the company thrives, the current situation is unsatisfactory. Therefore, perhaps Apple should start thinking differently ("Think Different") like one of its old slogans. Internally, Apple has always been panicked about declining product profit margins. Meanwhile, the company has long been concerned about its market share, which, according to Gartner data, dropped to 14% of the global smartphone market in the second quarter. Gartner analyst Anshul Gupta stated in a press release regarding rumors of a cheaper version of the iPhone: "Apple's move to introduce a new lower-cost model is risky. There's a much higher chance of cannibalization compared to the iPhone 4 – something that's already happening with the iPhone 4."

Apple's real danger lies in its 14% global market share. BlackBerry's market share reached 20% in 2010. Now it's below 5% and could potentially reach zero. While iPhone sales remain strong in the U.S. and in markets where carriers subsidize high-end phones, 14% is a proportion that could vanish quickly. If Apple launches a cheaper iPhone next month, known as the 5C, regardless of its actual price, you'll hear people criticize Apple for joining the "race to the bottom." Ignore these criticisms. What you want to hear is the absolute lowest price because, frankly, this is a survival battle in the mobile phone industry.

Carriers cooperating with Apple represent less than half of the total globally, partly because the prices Apple demands aren't acceptable to them. (An agreement with China Mobile, the world's largest carrier with 740 million users, seems imminent.) Apple won't introduce a model that satisfies everyone, but consumers will definitely be cost-conscious in both contract phone markets and countries where they are required to pay full price for their devices. If the iPhone 5c is priced at $450, matching the current price of the iPhone 4, this means Apple would merely be defending the high-end market and risk becoming further marginalized. On the other hand, if it's priced at $299, Apple would gain the opportunity to double its phone sales.

For developers, pricing is crucial. Even though Android surpasses iOS in terms of absolute user numbers, iOS remains the starting point for developers. Simply put, iOS's advantage at the top of the developer pyramid is neither inherent nor permanent. Without this advantage, more excellent apps will first appear on Android, reducing the appeal of the iPhone, especially if Apple only seeks to defend the high-end market. This is not a sustainable position, so now Apple is taking action.

Others have pointed out that Apple should have entered the low-end market earlier, which is correct, albeit meaningless now. However, the current agenda is how much Apple should reduce prices, and on this point, Tim Cook should consult someone smart like Evans rather than Icahn.

There seems to be some confusion about the practical significance of pricing, so let's do some math: Apple sells the iPod Touch, which lacks a rear camera and radio functionality, for $229.

The wholesale cost of these components is less than $50. Considering that the iPod Touch certainly meets the company's profit targets (it launched a few months ago, $70 cheaper than the then-lowest-priced model), a $299 iPhone should have a similar gross profit margin.

However, if thinking about profit margins this way doesn't make sense, we should consider total profit amounts. Assuming a $299 phone yields a 35% profit margin or over $100 in gross profit, if the price drops to $249, Apple would need to sell one additional unit to achieve the same profit.

But Apple doesn't just want to make money by selling phones. If consumers purchase apps, music, and movies, the company earns revenue and can even generate income from ads within certain apps. Even if lower-price phone buyers use the App Store less frequently, it's reasonable to view them as potential long-term customers, not just for iPhones, but also iPads and Macs. If they buy Android phones, Apple can't make money from them.

There's no doubt that setting the phone price at $399 would yield slightly higher profit per unit, but roughly calculating the gross profit per unit at $100 and $200 respectively doesn't tell us how much more total profit selling at $399 would generate compared to $299. At $399, the iPhone 5C would still be considered relatively expensive in most parts of the world, and this price wouldn't satisfy many carriers that haven't yet agreed to sell iPhones. This would also cause Apple to lose all the aforementioned advantages.

A relatively lower sales volume multiplied by a higher sales price results in a shinier profit margin, but the total profit amount would definitely be less.

Invest More to Sell More

Cook has expressed a desire to increase the proportion of iPhones sold directly through Apple retail stores, but the introduction of a lower-priced iPhone will make this goal harder to achieve. Of course, unless Apple opens more stores. Although some worry that the revenue per square foot in Apple stores has slightly decreased this year (no new products have been released since the iPad mini last year, which shouldn't surprise anyone), the company's retail performance remains enviable.

So, why not add more stores? Although Apple tried opening smaller stores a few years ago and eventually abandoned the idea, for a company increasingly focused on mobile devices and mainly selling iPhones and iPads, this approach makes more sense now than ever.

The existing stores are competitive weapons because they offer top-notch tech support and enthusiastic sales teams. Since dismissing John Browett 10 months ago, Cook has not appointed a new retail chief. Thus, projects like smaller, mobile-focused stores – even though everyone wants to see them – won't happen now. Given the dual role of Apple stores in sales and promotion, and the company reaching record production levels, Apple has missed an opportunity – it could have pursued a more aggressive expansion strategy, especially outside the U.S.

Cook has been criticized for lacking boldness, but he has shown he's willing to "break" with Steve Jobs by launching small tablets, which Jobs once publicly ridiculed as a bad idea.

If Apple hadn't introduced the iPad mini, its leadership position in the tablet market might have disappeared. However, one product isn't enough. Cook dismissed Browett without finding a replacement, a decision that didn't inspire much confidence externally. Apple hasn't fully utilized its retail advantage, but this isn't the only thing it hasn't fully utilized.

The introduction of a cheaper iPhone is a good move that further solidifies Cook's leadership. He has mentioned not leaving a pricing umbrella for competitors to exploit, but such space does exist for the iPhone.

We'll see how aggressively he chooses to pursue this. But even opening more stores, this market-share-grabbing measure is profitable from the outset. Therefore, it won't deplete Apple's cash reserves or truly push the limits.
