The first time I heard about people wanting to come to the U.S. to give birth so their children could gain citizenship, and the parents could then immigrate, was in college. A good friend and her husband were extremely admiring of the American lifestyle and wanted to stay in the U.S. by any means. When she seriously told me, I was surprised and asked her if having a child in the U.S. meant that the two of you could stay in the U.S.?

Last year, there were quite a few posts online about coming to the U.S. to have a second child. Due to China's family planning policies, financially capable parents arranged for their second child to be born in the U.S., not only avoiding fines but also naturally making their child a legal U.S. citizen. In the future, they could emigrate as a family, achieving two goals at once.

Last week, I coincidentally met one of the customers from a club near my husband's company. I found out that she and her husband came to New York with B1/B2 tourist visas, traveling back and forth multiple times a year. They claimed it was for tourism, but in reality, they were there to have their second child. The woman's belly was already noticeably around five or six months along. I asked her, "Are you going to give birth here?" She frankly said, "I already have a son. I am currently handling my husband's 'special talent' immigration, then I will immigrate as his wife, and then our son in Beijing. Currently, the lawyer is expediting the process for us. The only problem is that our current visa does not allow us to work in the U.S., and to immigrate, we must have an official employment contract. So, they approached my husband, hoping he would issue a letter stating willingness to hire her husband. But the woman also honestly said that after successfully immigrating, they might not necessarily continue working in this job; it was just a necessary step for immigration.

Shanghai recently announced that couples can now have a second child, but at the same time, surveys also show that raising even one child places a heavy economic burden, let alone a second? It is estimated that only wealthy families can afford it. For ordinary salaried families, even if they can afford to have another child, they cannot afford to raise them.

Many people who come to the U.S. to give birth hear that education in the U.S. is completely free, childbirth is cheaper, etc., and consider coming here to have children, which is rather impulsive. Although I have only lived in New York for less than five months, I have already worked in public, private, the richest postal code areas in the U.S., and refugee special education zones, so I understand the educational situation here better than many people.

My simple conclusion is: the rich and the poor benefit the most in the U.S., while the middle class suffers the most.

In terms of education, the rich in the U.S. have the money to send their children to top private schools, with strong faculty and complete facilities, allowing them to attend prestigious universities all the way up, rising quickly until they take over family businesses, or are hired by well-known large companies, or start their own businesses, becoming increasingly wealthy.

The poor, mainly consisting of refugee children, orphans, and families without the financial resources, energy, or physical ability to send their children to school, will have social workers responsible for sending them to special education centers (the U.S. has many social workers helping the poor, including homeless services), and teachers who choose to engage in special education often have hearts full of love, are conscientious and responsible, almost taking the children home. Therefore, the quality of education the children receive is high, and often one teacher, with a master's degree, teaches two children.

As for the middle class, they pay the most taxes but receive the least social returns. They don't have enough influence to speak at board meetings, nor do they qualify for any welfare. They work hard, buy houses on mortgage in good, safe school districts to attend free public schools, providing a good educational atmosphere for their children, which is very valuable.

Therefore, those who want to come to the U.S. to give birth and hope these children stay in the U.S. to receive education, unless they have substantial financial resources and the ability to send their children to private schools, live in good school districts to attend free public schools, or enter the U.S. as refugees, otherwise, the economic pressure of raising children in the U.S. is not much different from China.
