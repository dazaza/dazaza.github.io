The four types of despair each have their own characteristics.

by lxdhr2012 on 2011-08-19 16:01:03

Depressive Despair

This type of despair is accompanied by significant biological changes. Research indicates that in individuals with pronounced depressive moods, there are noticeable alterations in brain physiology mechanisms, such as a decrease in serotonin levels. On the cognitive level, these individuals often appear overly realistic in their daily lives. They lack imagination and anticipation for life, harbor intense dissatisfaction with themselves, and do not believe in a bright future. Once they encounter setbacks, they easily fall into despair, thus being plagued by suicidal thoughts, which are difficult to control and from which they cannot extricate themselves. An example of this is the suicide of Hong Kong's renowned singer Leslie Cheung.

Loss-induced Despair

The biological changes in this type of despair are not as evident. The primary factors at play are "social" and "authentic" cognitive levels. These individuals often bear immense life and societal pressures, which, when sustained over long periods, lead to physical and mental exhaustion, making them unwilling to continue facing these pressures. Consequently, they increasingly yearn for death. A notable example is the suicide of the famous writer Lao She.

Imaginary Despair

This type of despair is mainly the result of social and illusory cognitive effects. Individuals experiencing this type of despair often subjectively imagine scenarios, exaggerate their sense of despair, turning fiction into reality, eventually forcing themselves into a corner. Because if a person consistently perceives the world through an illusory lens, they easily fall into self-created misconceptions. In their view, it may seem as though the world cannot tolerate them, but in reality, they cannot tolerate themselves. A well-known instance is the suicide of Hong Kong's celebrated actress Wendy Huong.

Doubtful Despair

This type of despair is primarily the result of biological and illusory influences. It manifests as persistent death-related thoughts. Furthermore, those in this state of despair often believe that death equates to liberation. The progression from world-weariness to suicide in despairing individuals always involves accumulations of mental and psychological factors, as well as the combined effects of social and physiological functions. A relevant example is the suicide of China's renowned poet Gu Cheng.

Categorizing despair helps people understand the essence of the despair behind suicides. Not everyone’s reason for committing suicide is the same, nor are the approaches for guidance and treatment identical.
