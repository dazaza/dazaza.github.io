In English, there is a group of words that share similarities in spelling and also have certain connections in meaning. We call these words coherent terms. Understanding these characteristics makes it easier for us to memorize and guess the meanings of words. For example: we are all familiar with the word "positive" (enthusiastic), but if we encounter the word "negative" while reading, do we necessarily need to look it up in a dictionary? If we understand the rule that the word forms are related and the meanings are opposite, we can easily guess and remember that the meaning of this word is "negative."

When you see a word, think of it as a root, and find other related words as branches, such as synonyms, antonyms, similar words, and homophones. For instance, when we memorize the word "enormous" which means "huge, large," we can combine other English words with the same meaning at the same time, such as: big, large, huge, great, grand, immeasurable, colossal, gigantic, titanic, terrific, prodigious, gargantuan, gross, massive, mammoth, etc. At the same time, associate its antonyms, such as: little, small, tiny, minute, petit, microscopic, etc.

In English vocabulary, some letter combinations are neither roots nor affixes, but these letter combinations still have a certain meaning. There are certain commonalities in the words formed by them. Therefore, we can consciously summarize and deduce some meaningful combinations during English vocabulary memorization, which can help us master some less easy-to-remember words. It can also lay the foundation for us to predict the basic meaning of certain words. For example, the meaning combination "wr-" refers to "twist, wring, twist, distort," etc. The words starting with this meaning combination include: wrestle (wrestling), wrangle (quarrel, argument), wrap (wrap, roll, surround), wreath (wreath, garland), wreathe (cover, conceal, surround), wrench (twist violently, pull suddenly), wrest (seize, wrest), wriggle (crawl, twist, move forward), wring (squeeze, twist, wring out), etc.

Meaning Combination Memorization Method

Analyzing English word formation is very helpful for us to memorize English words. Accurately determining semantic roots and affixes can help us analyze how each new word and unfamiliar word is generated and how its meaning evolves. By knowing both the what and the why of memorizing vocabulary, we can achieve twice the result with half the effort. Root-affix memory method mnemonic: prefixes change meaning, suffixes change parts of speech. For example, the root "it" represents the meaning of "go." How to remember? At first glance, you might panic, but just think of "exit" and it becomes easier. The purpose of an exit is to let people go out, i.e., "exit = go out," and the prefix "ex" = "out," so the remaining "it" = "go"; then look at "visit," the meaning of visiting is to walk around and take a look, i.e., "visit = go and see," and "vis = see" (we know that vision is "sight" and visual is "vision"), so the remaining "it" still means "go."
