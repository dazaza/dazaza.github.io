"Boy Caesar" Mark Zuckerberg: Him and His Country

by ming1980k on 2012-02-19 13:20:22

Facebook claims to have 840 million users, which if it were a country, would be the third most populous nation after China and India. Its founder, 27-year-old Mark Zuckerberg, dreams of running an "empire without borders" as he creates and leads a whole new online society. As the company moves towards its official Initial Public Offering (IPO), both he and his long-lost former business partner have come to center stage.

Facebook is eight years old.

We have lived genuinely on this site for eight years. It records our preferences, achievements, joyful or embarrassing photos, books we've read, songs we've listened to, and people we've loved. Now, we are about to see how much our honesty is worth.

On February 1st, this social giant with 850 million users officially submitted its IPO application to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) after market hours. The company plans to raise $5 billion, making it the largest tech IPO in U.S. history. The company's valuation could likely reach as high as $100 billion.

As the founder of Facebook, Mark Zuckerberg released a public letter last week analyzing the company's IPO plan in detail and outlining the core values of the company. Zuckerberg said that Facebook was not created to become a company. Its birth was to fulfill a social mission: to make the world more open and interconnected.

"Our first goal upon waking up is not to make money, but we know that the best way to accomplish our mission is to build the strongest and most valuable company. This is why we are going public; we are doing it for our investors and employees," Zuckerberg wrote in the letter.

This company, which does not aim to make money, is about to earn substantial profits. According to a 28.4% stake and a market valuation of $100 billion for Facebook, 27-year-old Zuckerberg is worth $28.4 billion, ranking ninth on Forbes' 2011 Global Rich List, surpassing Li Ka-shing and trailing behind Bill Gates and Warren Buffett. Early investors in Facebook and Zuckerberg's co-founders have also joined the billionaire club.

He still maintains some habits from his youth, driving a cheap Honda car, practicing fencing, and being disheveled. In every press conference that has changed Facebook's fate, he has faced the world in a T-shirt, jeans, and flip-flops, and in 2011, it was no exception – though this seems to have become a deliberate symbol akin to Steve Jobs. In reality, he has become a true CEO, learning how to run an empire-like company, how to maturely answer the media, and how to maintain an approachable demeanor in public.

He bought a house last year and also a private jet, but doesn't use it often.

There are character analyses of Mark Zuckerberg's handwriting circulating on the Internet: "You are a shy, idealistic person who isn't easy to form relationships with, especially intimate ones."

The man who connects 800 million people globally, does he now feel it's easier to make friends?

The Challenge of Working with Zuck

In January 2004, Zuckerberg paid $35 to Register.com to register the domain name thefacebook.com for one year. On the afternoon of February 4th, he clicked on a link to his account on Manage.com, and thus Facebook was launched. Zuckerberg applied for login accounts numbered one through four (the first three were for testing purposes). Number five was his Harvard roommate Chris Hughes, number six was roommate Dustin Moskovitz, and number seven was Eduardo Saverin. Zuckerberg met Saverin at a Jewish gathering. Zuckerberg's friend and classmate Andrew McCollum used an image of Al Pacino found online as a base, overlaying ones and zeros to design a logo. Since Zuckerberg is red-green colorblind, the page primarily uses blue and white.

The idea for Facebook originated in Zuckerberg's dorm room at Kirkland House at Harvard University. There was a whiteboard over two meters long in the dorm room, Mark's favorite. This board was always filled with complex equations and colorful lines. He often stared at the board, calculating or moving to his computer at his desk, immersed in calculations displayed on the screen. On one side of the room were piles of empty Red Bull cans and beer bottles that he hadn't thrown away.

Each early office location of Facebook continued in the style of a college dormitory: tables were messily scattered, piled high with computers, cables, modems, cameras, interspersed with trash and everyday bottles, jars, and cups.

Zuckerberg – his friends call him Zuck – is short and thin, with thick brown curly hair covering a freckled face, somewhat resembling classic Greek sculptures. Even in winter, he wears rubber sandals and a T-shirt with unexpected patterns or words, a typical look for a nerd.

Chris Hughes, Dustin Moskovitz, Eduardo Saverin, and Andrew McCollum were all co-founders of Facebook. Saverin's name was once removed from the co-founder page, only to be added back later.

Hughes, with his blonde hair, was very friendly and served as the website's initial spokesperson. After graduating from Harvard with a degree in literature and history in 2006, he gradually stepped away from Facebook to help Barack Obama's campaign. He coordinated all of Obama's activities on Facebook, described by Obama as "that guy around me who knows the internet." Currently, he serves as a director at a Silicon Valley investment company and founded Jumo, a charity based on online community concepts. Hughes owns 1% of Facebook, valued at $1 billion.

Dustin Moskovitz was among the first to help Zuckerberg promote Facebook at university and later became a key leader of the technical team. He studied economics at Harvard for two years, learned programming for Facebook, dropped out of Harvard with Zuckerberg, and moved Facebook to Palo Alto. In 2008, Moskovitz resigned from Facebook and started his own web software company, Asana. He owns 7.6% of Facebook, worth $7.6 billion. Zuckerberg was sad when he left. "Dustin always had Facebook's interests at heart; he's always someone I want to consult," he said.

Eduardo Saverin was Zuckerberg's initial business partner. Together they invested $1000 into Facebook, and Saverin later became Facebook's earliest financial officer. However, in the summer of 2004, they disagreed on the direction of the website's development. In 2005, they fell out due to a monetary lawsuit. Saverin joined the Winklevoss twins in suing Zuckerberg for fraud, claiming that Zuckerberg stole their idea. This lawsuit became the main theme of the movie "The Social Network." After the lawsuit, he owned 5% of the shares, and according to himself, sold half on the secondary market. He currently lives in Singapore and has invested in several social networking companies.

Andrew McCollum, a programming genius and Facebook's earliest graphic designer, once met venture capitalists from Sequoia Capital wearing pajamas and a T-shirt with Zuckerberg, a classic scene in "The Social Network." He left Facebook in September 2006 to return to Harvard to continue his studies. He is still in Silicon Valley, working as an angel investor. He is low-profile and mysterious, with an unknown shareholding amount.

Finally, Facebook has reached its IPO time, but none of the original dorm mates are there with him to pop champagne in the office. As of today, the longest-serving executive at Facebook alongside Zuckerberg is Sheryl Sandberg, who only joined him in 2008.

Hughes once tried to explain the real reasons why his friend Jelle left Zuckerberg repeatedly.

"Leaving Facebook was because of boredom... working with Zuckerberg is very challenging, you can never be sure if he likes what you do, being friends with him is better than working with him," he said.

The First-Round Millionaire Club

Facebook's headquarters is located in Palo Alto, California, a city with only 60,000 people, known as the center of Silicon Valley. The garages where Google and Apple were born, their current headquarters, and Yahoo!'s headquarters are all gathered in this small town. From moving from Harvard's dormitory to a rented small house in Palo Alto, Facebook has now relocated to its third office location, still within this small town.

Facebook originally settled at 156 University Avenue outside Stanford University's campus. Now, its successor is a software company that helps the government track terrorists and criminals, co-founded by Peter Thiel, one of Facebook's earliest investors. The second office wasn't far away; just around the corner from Stanford University's main entrance is Zuckerberg's favorite bar, "Antonio's Nut Room." Next to the bar is the popular group-buying company Groupon. The building they currently occupy was originally Sun Microsystems' headquarters, not far from the garage where Google was born.

This is a place where startups are closely connected. The probability of a startup encountering a like-minded person in Palo Alto is quite high. In the summer of 2004, when Zuckerberg brought his partners from Harvard to check out their newly rented studio here, they ran into Sean Parker, who was moving nearby. At that time, Zuckerberg was preoccupied with perfecting the website's various functions. Although they had already established a company, they had no concept of how to operate it. Upon meeting Napster's founder Sean Parker, he almost couldn't wait to invite him to join Facebook. Parker took full responsibility for Facebook's restructuring in 2004 and officially became the site's first president. Not unlike the depiction in "The Social Network," fine wine, gourmet food, women, art, and music are all part of Parker's personality.
