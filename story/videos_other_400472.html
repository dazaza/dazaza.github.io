The incident began last month when a friend of my husband, who runs a Taobao store specializing in imported milk powder, baby food, and other products, invited us over for dinner. After dinner, I accidentally overheard the male host say to the female host: "Go buy milk powder at the supermarket quickly, or we'll run out of time." Without thinking, I blurted out, "Why go to the supermarket to buy milk powder? Don't you sell milk powder yourselves? Could it be that your milk powder is fake?" The atmosphere immediately became awkward, and I regretted my words. However, after a brief pause, the male host replied subtly, "It won't be fake, but the milk powder has gone through too many stages. Even I can't guarantee its true origin and source. For our own child, safety comes first, hehe." After this sentence, my husband quickly changed the topic to avoid further embarrassment, but I couldn't sit still for a minute. My son had already consumed seven boxes of milk powder bought from Taobao!!! Although it wasn't purchased from this friend's store, I started to doubt the source of the milk powder I bought.

Before this day, like many mothers who buy milk powder online, I believed I had the ability to distinguish between real and fake milk powders. I read numerous online posts teaching people how to identify genuine milk powder by looking at the printing, the can spray codes, the sealed tin foil, the milk powder color, the stickiness tested with fingers, the milk aroma, and even the membership card inside the can...

I thought I was very professional, but today I dare say that the counterfeiting ability of Chinese people always goes beyond our imagination. If we non-professionals can spot flaws with the naked eye, it only means one thing: the counterfeiters aren't professional enough yet.

During my research (my husband calls me crazy), I luckily contacted an old friend who learned that she and her husband are currently studying in Sapporo, Hokkaido, Japan. They've been living there with their daughter for more than two years. I then began bombarding her with requests to take photos, consult, and make phone calls. Here, I would like to thank her for her patience in satisfying my curiosity and helping me by running around supermarkets and drugstores and making countless phone calls. Thanks so much!

Because I'm raising my son alone, I don't have much free time, so after nearly a month of inquiries and investigations, I feel like I understand some of the secrets behind Meiji milk powder sales on Taobao. I hereby declare that everything I write represents only my personal opinion. Many of these opinions are based on inference and speculation, and I share them only to draw the attention of mothers.

1. Sales volume:

This is a sales chart I randomly captured from Taobao (since my son is currently drinking stage 2, I use stage 2 as an example). The top-selling store sells about 4000 cans per month, which accumulates to 6800 kilograms! According to information from my friend, in Japan, Meiji milk powder has always been limited. Normally, each person is restricted to buying 4 cans per purchase, and during special offers, each person is limited to 2 cans. Her daughter also eats Meiji, and when I asked her to buy milk powder for my son, she and her husband had to visit several stores to gather enough for a box.

This raises questions:

1. Why are the Japanese now being so nice to the Chinese, limiting purchases for their own citizens but being so generous to China?

2. Everyone knows that Meiji has a specific product line for the Chinese market called Meiji Zhen'ai'er. If large quantities of domestically produced Japanese milk powder dominate the Chinese market, then who will buy the milk powder produced in China???

If you ask these two questions to Taobao shopkeepers, they mostly answer: "We get our supply directly from Meiji's official distributor."

Then we come to question 2: Does Meiji really have distributors?

According to my friend's direct phone consultation with Meiji Company, the response was as follows: They only consider Japan's large supermarkets and chain drugstores as distributors, never approving any individual agents. Meiji milk powder has always been supplied in limited quantities. Even at large malls like JUSCO, you can buy up to 8 cans at a time, but they won't give you the original box, and the price is shockingly high. Large chain pharmacies respond that dealers determine the monthly allocation based on the population in the area where the store is located. Currently, the most severely targeted group is those selling large amounts of milk powder to the Chinese. With products limited even for Japanese nationals, how do Taobao sellers manage to acquire such bulk quantities? I cannot explain this, perhaps mothers buying the milk powder could listen to the shopkeepers' explanations.

Price:

Mothers can see on Taobao that most Meiji stage 2 prices are around 140 RMB. Based on my understanding, the price of Meiji milk powder varies by region in Japan. In Hokkaido, it should be relatively cheap. During promotional activities like Mother's Day, the lowest price can reach 1280 yen per can, usually around 1380 yen. Supermarkets are slightly more expensive than drugstores, ranging from 1480-1580 yen. Let's assume these Taobao shops are sourcing from Hokkaido at 1380 yen per can, using the lowest exchange rate of 7.4 RMB to 1 yen (usually around 7.6). So, 1380 * 0.074 = 102 RMB. From Japan to China, the cheapest option is sea freight, costing 3750 yen per 10KG. The Taobao shopkeepers claim that container shipping is much cheaper, fine, let's calculate the transportation cost per can at 10 RMB. 102 + 10 = 112 RMB. According to the regulations of the Chinese Customs website: import milk powder tariff is 10%, VAT is 17%, so 102 * 27% = 27.54 RMB + previous 112 = 138.54 RMB. Are all shopkeepers philanthropists? Making less than 2 RMB profit per can??

When mothers ask this, the shopkeeper replies: "We don't pay taxes, we go through Hong Kong customs clearance companies." Alright, I trust you, Hong Kong does have many specialized tax avoidance customs clearance companies, but their prices aren't low either. How do your milk powders bear such high customs clearance costs? Moreover, smuggling large quantities of milk powder is illegal! And this is the most important link in producing fake milk powder! There will be an explanation later, please mothers continue reading.

Some individual stores claim: "I'm a tour guide, every month I travel to Japan multiple times, and I bring back milk powder added to tourists' luggage for free air transport, no need to pay freight, so I sell cheaply." I checked her sales records, and she sells thousands of cans each month. Okay, please answer: 1. How do you manage to purchase so much milk powder in a short time while traveling to Japan, aren't you subject to purchase limits? 2. Do all your tourists agree to have milk powder packed in their luggage? 3. What tourism company are you working for, allowing you to conduct private business so openly without interference? Even if you go to Japan 10 times a month with 20 people per group, you can only bring back over 200 boxes of milk powder, yet her online store sells thousands of cans of Meiji stage 2 alone each month... Other Japanese brands like Morinaga also sell well. The numbers just don't add up.

4. About customs clearance companies:

If you ask Taobao sellers selling Meiji milk powder about the source of their products, approximately 90% of the shopkeepers will reply: "Purchased locally in Japan, shipped by sea to Hong Kong, then enters the mainland through Hong Kong customs clearance companies." Here, I cannot say that all Meiji milk powder sold on Taobao is fake, nor can I say that all milk powder entering through this channel is fake. But at least, this is the most critical link in producing fake milk powder. Because, according to my knowledge, most Meiji milk powder sold on Taobao is sourced from so-called distributors in Hong Kong. These suppliers do not provide receipts from purchases in Japan, no sea freight bills from Japan to Hong Kong, no tracking records for sea freight, only a Japanese label attached to the outside of the entire box of milk powder, claiming it's the shipping label from the Japanese company. At most, they provide documents for shipment from Hong Kong to the mainland. This is considered the sole evidence proving the milk powder was shipped from Japan. I cannot say it is definitely fake, but this link provides too many loopholes for counterfeiters. Therefore, I dare say many shopkeepers confidently telling buyers that their milk powder is absolutely directly shipped from Japan might themselves be victims, as the trusted Hong Kong company may be the one counterfeiting. How many Taobao shopkeepers actually have concrete evidence proving that every can of milk powder in their store is directly shipped from Japan??

During my investigation, the most reliable claim regarding counterfeit Meiji milk powder is that Japan has strict control over the expiration dates of milk powder. Most large baby stores, supermarkets, and drugstores handle milk powder expiring within three months either by destroying it or offering it at a discount. Below are excerpts from relevant company regulations in Japan:

The Food Sanitation Law requires manufacturers to ensure quality until the expiration date, leaving the decision to purchase nearing-expiration products to consumers.

Q: I bought powdered milk in bulk, and the expiration date is approaching.

A: Powdered milk generally has an expiration period of one to one and a half years from production. We continuously ship large quantities, so the powdered milk delivered to customers typically has an expiration period of about one year. If you provide the product name and inquiry content, we can respond with the current expiration date of shipments. For other foods, we continuously monitor expiration dates and handle products three months before expiration either by destruction or discount.

Due to this time difference, unscrupulous individuals may purchase at low prices, alter the expiration date on the bottom of the can, and resell it. There are also claims of collecting empty milk powder cans for refilling, but since these are merely speculations without solid evidence, they are not widely acknowledged. However, a unified claim is that these batches of milk powder are eventually shipped to Hong Kong and then wholesaled back to the mainland.

5. About receipts:

Many shopkeepers post pictures of Japanese shopping receipts in their stores, but observant mothers may notice that many stores' receipt photos remain unchanged for long periods, and some receipts are clearly fake. Furthermore, having receipts proves that the milk powder was purchased from supermarkets or drugstores, places that don't allow bulk purchases. If you're purchasing in batches, shouldn't each can have a corresponding receipt? Why not include the receipt in the milk powder box and send it together with the package to reassure mothers? For most stores' blurry, altered, watermarked receipts that are difficult to verify, mothers should remain vigilant and try to obtain clear images showing detailed data such as names, addresses, and quantities before purchasing.

Below is a confirmed case of counterfeit Meiji milk powder:

I discovered differences between the Meiji milk powder I bought from Taobao and the one purchased from Hong Kong's Choysan: the TB-bought N character is blue with a LOT mark on the can bottom, while the Choysan-bought N character is red with no LOT mark. Strangely, I bought stage 1, so I seek help from mothers whose babies consume Meiji.

After consulting Meiji Company via phone, the reply was as follows:

For Meiji stage 1 Hohoemi 930g cans manufactured before March 2008, indeed, LOT was printed on the can bottom, but after March 2008, it was removed. This means that products with expiration dates before August 2009 should have the LOT mark, and after that, they should not.

Therefore, it can be determined that the milk powder with the LOT mark and an expiration date of January 2010 mentioned by this mother is absolutely fake.
