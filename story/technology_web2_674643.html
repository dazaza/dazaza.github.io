How to Build a Product-Oriented Business Website and Make Money

I have been working on product-oriented business websites, and recently a good friend from the forum mentioned wanting to start one. So, I will share some unique insights with you, especially for those transitioning into building business websites. Some concepts must change if you want to succeed in this area.

What is our purpose when doing products? The goal is to make money by promoting our products. First, you need to have this mindset: we aim for rankings, but rankings are not the most important thing. The main goal is still to generate profits. I will now provide an idea of how to build a good product-oriented business website starting from this concept.

On-Site Strategy: "Storefront Renovation"

Let's start with the website itself. For a product-oriented business site, the website mainly serves as a "storefront." This is something many people can quickly understand. Customers come to your site through search engines or other channels and judge whether your company is legitimate or large-scale based on the appearance of the site. Although this is an unscientific way to differentiate, it does affect customer conversion rates. Therefore, it's crucial to choose a memorable domain name and a pleasant-sounding website name. After handling the "storefront" issues, the next step is to deal with the website text. When selecting keywords, remember to base them on your industry or product, choosing terms with high conversion rates that bring in customers more effectively. This depends on your specific industry and product. For example, searching for "XX price" or "XX agency" might lead to higher transaction volumes, while searches like "how about XX" or "what is XX used for" may result in more inquiries but fewer transactions. Therefore, the primary focus of our website should be on the former type of keywords. As for the latter, I'll explain in the next section on off-site strategies. Regarding website descriptions and homepage texts, my personal view is to consider what the customer needs, what makes them comfortable, and what keeps them on the site. Don’t overthink keyword structures or density; write from the user's perspective. I believe this approach creates the most natural keyword structure, and you’ll see great results if you try it.

Off-Site Strategy: "Pervasive Presence"

I don't advocate focusing solely on rankings when running a product-oriented business site. If that's your main concern, perhaps a traffic-driven site would suit you better. A business site should adopt a "rural encirclement" strategy because there are countless external platforms you can leverage, such as B2B commerce sites, free classifieds platforms, blogs, forums, and microblogs. Utilize these platforms fully to achieve pervasive presence, ensuring no shortage of potential customers. Many people might think they're already doing this. Yes, you may be active on these platforms, but what are you using them for? Mostly for backlinks aimed at boosting your main site’s ranking without considering user experience. This only taps into 30% of their potential. If you improve user experience on these platforms, they can directly bring in customers and also help rank long-tail keywords with lower transaction volumes mentioned in the on-site strategy. Here's how to do it:

B2B Commerce Sites and Classified Platforms: Many of these platforms have shops. Make sure to use them effectively, and once you open a shop (whether free or paid), take time to design and decorate it professionally. Finally, include a link to your main site. Many users checking your posted information may enter these shops. A professional-looking shop will encourage further exploration, leading to visits to your main site. This brings both relevant backlinks and customers.

Blogs: Blogs may not be as effective for backlinks as before, but they are still valuable for business sites. Blog marketing is a known method where you promote through blogs. Set up a blog on high-authority platforms, preferably related to your niche. Post authoritative articles daily about your products or strong industry-related content. This will attract customers interested in learning more about your field. Then, find ways to direct them to your main site. One trick is to include promotional images at the end of each article, leaving contact details like phone numbers or URLs. Even if blogs don't allow anchor texts, they usually allow images.

Forums: You can post soft articles on forums related to your industry. This not only builds backlinks but also brings direct customer traffic. Remember, quality over quantity. Focus on relevant forums rather than unrelated ones to avoid wasting time and effort.

Microblogs: Currently the most popular social platform, microblog marketing is no longer unfamiliar. Similar to blog marketing, I won't elaborate much here.

Building a product-oriented business site requires a different mindset, focused on generating profit. Your thinking must be broad and not limited to just the main site's ranking. Otherwise, if one day your site gets penalized by Baidu (just hypothetical), you'll realize you've lost everything.
