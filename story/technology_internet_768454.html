Ex-Apple Siri Product Manager Helps Samsung Develop IoT Platform

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:28:11

On the afternoon of November 9, Beijing time, Luc Julia, Vice President of Samsung's California Menlo Park Innovation Lab, showcased an Internet of Things (IoT) service at a conference in San Francisco.

Julia, who was previously responsible for developing Apple's Siri voice assistant technology, presented a project called "Samsung Multi-Modal Interaction Architecture" (referred to as "SAMI"). The aim of this initiative is to create a platform that collects data from all connected devices and provides it to other devices.

This platform aims to serve companies producing wearable computers, home automation devices, and automobiles by helping them offer additional services to their users.

To demonstrate this service, Julia showed one potential application of SAMI in personal healthcare. He wore a Fitbit fitness tracker and a wearable heart rate monitor, measured his weight using an internet-connected scale, and then ran a few laps around the stage to illustrate how these devices share data, moving beyond functioning as isolated applications.

He also interacted with the service as if it were Siri, asking, "SAMI, how am I doing?" The app informed him that he had met his exercise goal for the day. Eventually, the system will perform more complex analyses, such as advising users whether they need to increase their activity or take a break.

Julia stated that the biggest advantage of SAMI is its ability to collect and store data in raw form. Samsung plans to standardize the data and make it available for other applications to use.

Julia served as the Director of the Siri project at Apple for about 10 months before leaving last year. SAMI bears some resemblance to Siri, with the difference being that while Siri is exclusive to Apple, SAMI aims to be accessible to more companies.

Although Julia did not provide many details, he mentioned that Samsung aims to enhance the openness of the system as much as possible. He acknowledged some challenges Samsung faces: the company has traditionally excelled in hardware and lacks experience in network services. Moreover, many potential customers of Samsung's SAMI service are also competitors.

However, Samsung is currently collaborating with approximately 50 partners to develop and test SAMI. Additionally, Samsung's Menlo Park Innovation Lab has received a $100 million fund to invest in related technologies. Although Samsung started developing this platform six years ago, Julia emphasized that it is still in its early stages of development, and the Menlo Park lab typically focuses on projects spanning three to five years.
