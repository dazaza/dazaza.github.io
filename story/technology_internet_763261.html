The Tragedy of Facebook's Open Platform: Broken Promises and a Crisis of Trust

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 17:54:07

The Facebook Open Platform did not achieve the expected results and cannot be compared with Google's and Apple's mobile ecosystems.

Introduction: The American tech blog PandoDaily published an article today titled "Move fast, break things: the sad story of platform, Facebook's gigantic missed opportunity." It states that although Facebook launched its open platform strategy just three years after its establishment, due to various execution errors, the company has still failed to align with its intended direction. As user attention shifts from desktops to mobile devices, Facebook's platform disadvantages have become more apparent. Facebook's failure in the platform battle was entirely self-inflicted, missing the chance to stand shoulder-to-shoulder with Apple and Google.

Below is the full article:

On May 24, 2007, Facebook held its first F8 developer conference at the San Francisco Design Center in California, USA. Mark Zuckerberg had just turned 23.

Dressed in a black sweater and striped flip-flops, this young founder and CEO of Facebook made his debut on stage, delivering a keynote speech to over 800 developers, investors, and entrepreneurs, confidently claiming that Facebook, which had only been online for three years, would change the internet.

At the conference, Zuckerberg boldly declared: "All current social networks are closed platforms; Facebook will end this status quo. The evolved Facebook platform will be open to developers worldwide, allowing any developer to create complete applications targeting the top social graph within Facebook's framework."

The launch of the Facebook Open Platform was undoubtedly a radical move, as it provided developers access to 24 million registered Facebook users. More shockingly, developers could create applications that competed directly with Facebook’s own services. At the time, Zuckerberg promised developers a fair competitive environment.

It was then that Facebook transitioned from being a competitor to MySpace into one for Google. By establishing an open platform, Facebook transformed from a Web 2.0 startup into a Web 2.0 startup platform, starting to develop its ecosystem.

Conflicts of Interest Annoy Developers

Unfortunately, Facebook's path to an open platform proved unsustainable. Within just a year of its launch, the company began changing the rules and adjusting the terms of service for developers.

After 18 months, the company started breaking its promise of fair competition by cracking down on similar apps that conflicted with its own services. Privately, some developers began complaining they had been "fooled" by Zuckerberg, turning what was once seen as a magnificent engineering feat into a major disappointment.

Today, six years later, Facebook's development status is far from Zuckerberg's original vision, possibly missing out on market opportunities worth hundreds of billions of dollars. Aside from games, Facebook's developer platform has few noteworthy applications.

Zynga may be the only game service that completely originated from Facebook. Other promising applications like Slide and iLike eventually had to abandon their reliance on Facebook and were sold at lower prices to Facebook's competitors, Google and MySpace.

Due to numerous mistakes in building the developer platform, Facebook failed to match up with Apple and Google, making its development platform insignificant compared to the application ecosystems of these two companies. In comparison to Apple's iOS ecosystem, Facebook's developer platform pales in significance. However, Facebook remains optimistic, believing that many iOS applications still depend on Facebook Connect, using it as a login account, and thus considering it not a "zero-sum game," but rather an opportunity for multiple platforms to grow together.

But Wall Street analysts don't buy this argument, seeing no growth potential for Facebook. Currently, Facebook's stock price has fallen from $38 per share at IPO to $12. Despite having over a billion active monthly users, the revenue Facebook generates from applications is negligible compared to the application sales revenue from Apple's iOS ecosystem. Currently, Facebook's market value is approximately $60 billion, while according to Goldman Sachs analysts, just Apple's app market alone is valued at $300 billion.

Platform Disadvantages Amplified on Mobile

Worse still, Facebook's mistakes in platform strategy continue to harm the company as it faces the challenge of embracing the mobile market.

Data shows that the activity level of Facebook's mobile users has surpassed that of desktop users, with the company's mobile ad revenue expected to reach $2 billion in 2013. To date, Facebook's expansion in the mobile platform has only resulted in the launch of an Android app homepage (Facebook Home), which has received much criticism. Partner at Khosla Ventures, Keith Rabois, believes: "Without a mobile platform, Facebook cannot attract the interest of developers and consumers."

Meanwhile, mobile IM applications such as WeChat in China, KakaoTalk in Korea, Line in Japan, and Kik in North America are gradually establishing their territories and have all become popular apps on both iOS and Android, posing threats to Facebook's mobile development.

Even without forming a platform, Facebook remains an important internet company. After all, the service covers one-seventh of the global population, undeniable advantages. But we can no longer witness how Facebook would have developed if it had followed its initial platform strategy. For those familiar with Facebook's early situation, early followers among developers, and some of the company's investors, Facebook's failure in platform strategy has been very painful.

However, even so, Facebook has no one to blame but itself.

Brief Honeymoon Period

To summarize the current phenomenon in one sentence: Facebook never delivered on its promises.

The relationship between the company and developers is complex and ever-changing. Over the years, Facebook's randomness has undermined the confidence of developers, who no longer believe the platform offers a stable environment to build businesses upon. Whether it was adopting broad rules affecting good applications due to bad ones or ignoring users' expectations about how Facebook builds itself, the environment created by Facebook is considered subpar.

There should have been equality. When Facebook launched its platform, the FAQ document stated: "We designed the Facebook platform so third-party applications compete fairly with Facebook's own applications. Ultimately, end-users decide which applications are most useful to them, and these applications will become the most popular."

In an interview after the platform's launch, iLike co-founder and CEO Ali Partovi told VentureBeat's Eric Eldon that he found the platform highly attractive. iLike was already a popular music-sharing site with over a million users but was enticed by Facebook's immense potential and promise to leverage its rich social database. iLike joined the platform, creating artist and album pages for Facebook and sending reminders to users when their favorite bands held concerts nearby.

Partovi's brother Hadi, aged 24, had served as the product management director for Microsoft's IE browser. He saw the platform's potential and told Partovi, "In the history of computing, there have been personal computers, Windows, the Web, and now it's the Facebook platform."

Despite Nat Brown, iLike's CTO and one of the founders of Microsoft's Xbox project, having reservations about the Facebook platform, the iLike team felt reassured by Facebook executives' promise to treat all applications on the platform fairly.

"For instance," Partovi told Eldon, "they completely refused to give us any special treatment, insisting that the market needed fairness."

iLike was willing to pay or even offer Facebook equity to gain an advantage on the platform but was rejected. "In the future, they will develop their own video application, but it will compete with third-party applications rather than being pre-installed for every Facebook user," Partovi said. Facebook's early actions strengthened iLike's confidence in the platform.

Initially, everything went smoothly. Thanks to Facebook's viral information flow, iLike achieved great success without using fancy strategies. In just two weeks, iLike gained 10 million new users, growing rapidly and acquiring new users faster than Facebook's new registrations. "At the time, 80% of Facebook users had installed our application," Partovi said.

However, good times didn’t last long. Soon, Facebook started making continuous adjustments, such as changes in the user interface and even the cancellation of key APIs. By December 2008, the Facebook platform had transformed from a virgin land full of opportunities into a temporary residence where developers could no longer use the features that initially attracted them.

To effectively address spammy information flows and the abuse of the notification system by applications like RockYou and Slide, Facebook began changing its API rules. Instead of kicking out the bad applications, Facebook imposed stricter notification system restrictions on all applications, eventually canceling the API altogether, leaving developers unable to communicate with users.

Facebook also actively made many other changes, no longer allowing developers to install applications on user profile pages, eliminating promotional functions, and shutting down third-party advertising networks.

The motivation behind these changes was simple: Facebook wanted to protect its user experience, but it set the rules entirely based on its own ideas. To reduce the harm caused by a few applications, it punished the majority, resulting in the destruction of the platform's openness.

"I think it doesn't feel like a planned platform," Brown recalled, "it didn't provide a stable platform for us to start a business."

For iLike, worse things were yet to come.

Crisis of Trust

By the end of 2008, rumors surfaced that Facebook was developing its own music player and artist pages, integrating native user experiences, despite previously assuring iLike that it wouldn't do so. TechCrunch's former editor-in-chief Michael Arrington pointed out the critical issue: "This isn't just about Facebook's music strategy," Arrington said, "but whether the company truly wants to be a social platform or social system, or to own everything valuable on the Facebook platform."

From 2009 until now, Facebook's platform underwent two major changes, further cementing its reputation for inconsistency. In 2010, Facebook tried to force social game developer Zynga to adopt Facebook Credit and accept a 30% revenue share, angering this key partner. Zynga did not accept the proposal. Although they still collaborate, their relationship has never fully recovered. Struggling Zynga has shifted its focus to the relatively newer mobile platform.

Last year, Facebook changed the way news was displayed in news agencies' "social readers." Facebook traffic for newspapers like The Guardian and The Washington Post significantly decreased. This was a significant blow to The Washington Post, especially given that its owner, Don Graham, was a member of Facebook's board and one of Zuckerberg's earliest mentors. These news readers were touted as central to Zuckerberg's vision of "frictionless sharing," but were suddenly abandoned. Unable to tolerate this, The Guardian shut down its reader last December.

This unpredictable situation has continued to the present. After Facebook adjusted its information flow algorithm, the user numbers for video-sharing applications SocialCam and Viddy plummeted. According to research institute Appdata.com, earlier this year, Viddy's monthly active users dropped from 35 million at its peak last year to only 500,000. Reuters noted that LinkedIn competitor BranchOut's monthly active users drastically fell from 39 million at its peak to just 100,000 after Facebook disabled the notification function it used to attract users.

Meanwhile, Facebook took strong measures against potential competitors in the messaging app field like Path, Voxer, and MessageMe. They were all banned from using the "Find Friends on Facebook" API. Similarly, Facebook blocked Vintage Camera, a competitor to video-sharing app Instagram. Facebook also blocked Russian search engine Yandex because its social search product competed with Facebook's own Graph Search.

Facebook's rationale was that these applications exploited its platform without providing useful data in return. Facebook claimed they attempted to replace rather than extend Facebook's core functions. However, this explanation did little to ease tensions between Facebook and developers.

The cumulative effect of all these changes over the years has left developers skeptical of the platform. Ben Brown, founder of digital publishing company XOXCO and senior developer, described developing applications for the Facebook platform as a "terrible idea." He said, "If it counts as a platform, it's an unreliable and constantly changing one, making everything extremely difficult and complex."

An unnamed former Facebook employee admitted, "As a developer, you must be prepared to be shut down by Facebook at any moment."

Ben Ling, former head of the Facebook platform and partner at Khosla Ventures, expressed it more gently. "Developers make a living through hard work, investing a lot to develop successful products, expecting the platform to remain stable." Facebook's inconsistent policies destroyed or harmed many companies, including Slide, iLike, Flixster, PickPals, SocialMedia, Viddy, SocialCam, and countless others, turning venture capital investments of hundreds of millions of dollars and startup valuations of tens of billions into nothing.

Lack of Planning Before Launch

However, Facebook's erratic behavior on the platform wasn't malicious but influenced by an engineer-dominated corporate culture.

Under the leadership of programmers, the Facebook platform started from scratch, seeking solutions that required minimal human intervention to keep operations lean and allow quick action. This approach led to a lack of clear policies, stability, and security essential for a development environment. It also meant minimal human involvement in quality control processes.

Facebook relied on modifying algorithms to combat spam and excessive exposure of certain applications, differing sharply from Apple. Apple requires all applications listed in the App Store to pass manual reviews. An unnamed former Facebook executive said, "We should have realized that we needed people to make judgments." He believed that the App Store was Facebook's biggest failure: "The development of this platform was driven by the engineering team and technical product team. We understood product development but didn't know how to establish payment systems and manual review organizations."

This executive and other Facebook insiders also pointed out that the company could have better helped developers profit from the platform early on. After banning payments and third-party advertising networks, developers had limited sources of income. At the time, this issue sparked heated debates within Facebook. Zuckerberg believed that developers should first create applications with long-term value before considering revenue. Clearly, Zuckerberg didn't realize that not all developers had millions of dollars to spare, allowing them to prioritize revenue issues.

This former executive also stated that the platform's value proposition leaned too heavily towards distribution. Due to the lack of effective monetization models, developers chose to maximize sales, leading to spamming behaviors. He said, "If there had been a slight balance and some convenient monetization tools, the situation might have been better."

Social analytics tool ThinkUp founder and CEO Anil Dash speculated that Zuckerberg hadn't learned from software companies that existed before Facebook. Boldly pointing out, Apple's iOS succeeded because it had already operated Mac OS as a platform for decades and learned from Microsoft's mistakes.

Apple implemented strict quality control measures for the App Store from the beginning. Moreover, it explicitly designed ways for developers to profit on the platform. It distributed 70% of total revenue and in-app purchase revenue to developers, keeping 30% for itself, creating a win-win situation.

Thus, the iOS ecosystem supported the development of thousands of companies, including productivity tool Evernote, location-based social network Foursquare, and messaging apps like Line. It performed even better in gaming. Supercell earns about $2.5 million daily from just two games, Clash of Clans and Hay Day.

Conversely, although Facebook was once fertile ground for game development, that was all. Zynga is a clear example, though the platform also contributed to the success of Playdom, PlayHaven, Kixeye, SGN, Cmune, and King. Outside the gaming sector, it's hard to think of any companies dependent on Facebook, and even King's majority revenue comes from mobile platforms, with Facebook merely aiding in user acquisition.

Facebook's acquisition of Instagram effectively demonstrated the value of iOS while confirming the decline of its own platform. Zuckerberg thought third-party developers on the Facebook platform could develop an application like Instagram to replace the native Facebook photo application. Instead, it emerged on a platform outside Facebook. This transaction highlighted Facebook's inability to support any company outside the gaming sector.

Dash said, "There were countless successful small Facebook applications, but none were particularly outstanding." He indicated that Facebook's platform clearly encountered difficulties two years after its launch.

iLike's former CTO, Nat Brown, spoke more sharply: "I no longer consider it a platform. I think it now resembles an ad network more."

Changes Over Six Years

Currently, the most popular consumer applications don't rely on Facebook for promotion. Photo-sharing service Snapchat shares 200 million diverse photos daily, valued close to $800 million, and completely avoids Facebook's influence. Facebook subsequently developed a similar application called Poke, but since its launch in the Apple App Store, its ranking has plummeted. Video application Vine, despite being removed from Facebook's "Find Friends" feature, still develops well through its collaboration with Twitter.

Multimedia chat application MessageMe is similarly thriving, currently boasting over 5 million users. Messaging app WhatsApp has over 250 million users and can directly access user contact lists. WeChat, KakaoTalk, and Kik are currently competing with Facebook, each building their own social graphs and application platforms. Notably, WeChat dominates in China, similar to Facebook. Like messaging apps Line and KakaoTalk, WeChat is aggressively competing with Facebook to gain more new users in the rapidly growing Southeast Asian region.

These realities prompt people to question what happened to the Facebook platform created in the desktop Web era. Khosla Ventures partner Keith Rabois succinctly summarized the current decline of the Facebook platform: "I haven't heard anyone mention 'Facebook platform' in over a year."

Now, the initial vision of the Facebook platform has failed, and its positioning has become vague. Currently, Facebook executives define the platform as a social layer based on other operating systems (Android and iOS). They no longer boast about the number of applications integrated into Facebook but discuss how applications like Spotify and Nike+ integrate with Facebook.

Today, the Facebook platform consists of four parts: universal registration tool Facebook Connect, Open Graph enabling application users to share information and content, advertising and payment services, and application services from mobile development platform Parse, acquired by Facebook in April this year.

These four components differ greatly from Facebook in 2007, but the significance of the Facebook platform continues to diminish. Facebook data indicates that as of December 31 last year, over 10 million applications and websites were integrated with Facebook. Last year, game developers connected to Facebook collectively earned over $2 billion in revenue. The company claims that 250 million people play games on Facebook monthly, and nearly 500 million play Facebook-connected games on external websites.

MySpace co-founder Chris DeWolfe praised Facebook highly. He is now the CEO of social gaming network SGN, which received $17 million in venture funding and has annual revenue of $50 million. "Facebook is an important part of our success," DeWolfe said, "if you use the Facebook platform correctly, it will bring you great benefits."

DeWolfe stated that Facebook made these changes to maintain a good user experience, saying, "These changes are fair," he said, "if they didn't make these changes, users would leave."

Causes' chief revenue officer James Windon said that the Facebook platform could have adopted clearer strategies, but he indicated that Facebook remains crucial to people, "Facebook has always been the best way for us to acquire users."

Causes, developed by Zuckerberg's friends Sean Parker and Joe Green, was one of the earliest applications on the Facebook platform. Windon said it has 180 million users, most of whom come from Facebook. Causes was also a partner in Facebook's short-lived "Great Apps" project, which aimed to reward trustworthy applications with higher visibility. Another partner was iLike, which Facebook initially promised to compete fairly with but ultimately abandoned.

Rabois is satisfied with Facebook Connect, which promotes Facebook's social graph outside the Facebook website, "Facebook Connect is pretty good," Rabois said, "just that people don't have enough confidence in it."

Facebook executives express satisfaction with the platform's operations, "It has brought us billions of dollars in revenue," said Doug Purdy, Facebook's product management director, "we've become more diversified," with most revenue coming from in-game purchases.

Purdy said that many strategic changes in six years are normal, as the concept of creating a social platform was quite forward-thinking at the time. Facebook has been continuously developing new features, but the vision of the platform hasn't fundamentally changed, "our overall strategy hasn't changed," he said, "we believe that when applications become socialized, they develop better."

People shouldn't view the Facebook platform as an execution environment but as a collection of callable APIs. Purdy said, "Facebook is not a platform like iOS and Android; from 2007 onwards, Facebook was completely based on web technology."

Dan Rose, Facebook's vice president of business development and sales, who has worked for Facebook since 2006, said that the platform's initial vision was to extend Facebook's core elements—identity and sharing—to third-party applications, "in this aspect, our wishes have been fulfilled."

The keyword is "in this aspect," Rose's explanation contradicts Zuckerberg's descriptions at the first f8 developer conference and other statements, as the 2008 release of Facebook Connect fragmented the platform's user consistency.

Rose acknowledged that Facebook made mistakes, especially in explaining their products to developers. Rose said that Facebook always hoped developers would create applications that extended Facebook's core functions.

"We've made significant progress in communicating and expressing ourselves to developers," he said, "I think we communicated poorly in the early days. The company was small and young, with only a small team working on platform development services."

He also criticized some misbehaving developers: "Many applications viewed the platform as a great opportunity to promote themselves, which isn't surprising because we provided a relatively open platform that was easy to exploit. Frankly speaking, we found many developers using the platform to send spam to users and employing exhaustive means to grow rapidly."

Rose admitted that Facebook was too lenient in its early stages, "I admit that trying to balance rights caused some bumps along the way, and we initially didn't handle it well enough."
