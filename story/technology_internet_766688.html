iOS 7: Seven Improvements and Seven Sins

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 22:19:26

Forbes technology columnist Mark Rogowsky, after conducting a comprehensive test and experience of Apple's upcoming iOS7, published two articles that separately pointed out several improvements and seven shortcomings of the system. Below is a summary of the content:

iOS7 System Improvements:

1. Re-emphasizing the basic function as a "phone"

Often, when we use our iPhones for browsing the internet and playing games, it seems like we forget that it is actually a phone device. Fortunately, Apple hasn't forgotten this - the new iOS7 system adds a very considerate feature to the iPhone. When receiving an incoming call, in addition to the options to answer or reject, there is now an additional "remind" option. This choice allows users to reject the current call but will receive a reminder after an hour.

In addition, the "text message" option has become more intuitive; with just two clicks, you can refuse the call and simultaneously send pre-set messages such as "I'll call you back later" or "I'm on my way." Of course, users can also customize these message contents through the system settings.

2. Notification reminder functionality expanded

Previously, when receiving notifications from apps like Facebook or Gmail, user operations were quite limited while in lock screen mode. Starting from iOS7, simply swiping your finger allows you to directly open the app sending the notification. Apple is trying hard to close the gap with Google Android in the "notification reminder" field.

3. iTunes Radio will be Pandora's fierce rival

The iTunes Radio music broadcasting function is free for all users, and if users are willing to pay $25 per year, they can enjoy ad-free service. Apple's service won't completely destroy Pandora, but it will definitely become its fierce competitor.

Using Apple's iTunes Radio will be familiar to users accustomed to Pandora's music service. Both have numerous preset radio stations, and users can also choose their favorite singers or music styles to create their own radio stations. For example, if you like hip-hop music but don't want to listen to Jay-Z's songs, you can create a station and request to filter out all of his works. Compared to competitors Spotify and Pandora, iTunes Radio's sound quality is significantly better than the latter and comparable to the former.

4. Photo management function

iOS7 introduces the Collections photo management function, which automatically organizes and sorts photos based on time and location. Additionally, iOS7 allows users to create private photo stream sharing services via iCloud - this function effectively avoids public platforms like Facebook, allowing private photos to be exchanged and shared only among family members.

iOS7 also supports square photos, adapting to the increasingly popular Instagram photo-sharing service. Apple has even added its own filters to the camera function to facilitate user editing and sharing.

5. Control Center

Apple's addition of "Control Center" to iOS is a very popular move. By swiping up from the bottom of the screen, users can quickly adjust some system settings such as Bluetooth, Wi-Fi, and Airplane Mode. In addition, Apple has also placed the flashlight, calculator, and alarm reminder applications in the "Control Center," which is a very thoughtful change.

6. Automatic application updates

When U.S. Senator John McCain met with Apple CEO Tim Cook, although the conversation was about how to change the tax mechanism, what McCain really wanted to ask Cook was "Why can't the iPhone automatically update apps?" Now, this feature has been realized, and users no longer need to manually enter the App Store at irregular intervals to complete app updates.

7. The seemingly strange "Near Me" feature in the App Store

If you live in a big city, selecting this feature usually only shows people around you using traffic-related apps. This obviously isn't very useful. But if you're traveling and walk into an unfamiliar coffee shop or museum, this feature might recommend apps related to that location. It's a very thoughtful touch.

8. Some detailed changes, but very humanized

FaceTime now supports voice-only conversations without video. Of course, this will consume a certain amount of data.

Siri is better than before, expanding content support to include Wikipedia, Twitter, and Bing search information.

Safari eliminates the separation between the search bar and the address bar.

AirDrop supports one-to-many wireless sharing of any data. As long as you're nearby, you can share wirelessly. Currently, Mac and iOS sharing isn't available, but it's just a matter of time. AirDrop is supported on iPhone 5 and fourth-generation iPad and above.

You can see the percentage while charging.

Seven Sins of iOS7:

1. Background frames for desktop icon groups have been removed, causing contrast issues

Previously, when multiple apps were grouped together in an icon group (folder), the background would show shadows and a clear white frame. However, in the new iOS7, this display has been removed. Since the background desktop and icons may have similar color differences, if the contrast isn't obvious enough, it can be difficult to see, especially for white font displayed app names, which are often unclear.

2. Calendar app still lacks sufficient improvement

Safari and Siri have received excellent improvements, but the calendar app lags behind in iOS7. Compared to calendar apps like Sunrise, entering "Lunch Tuesday 1pm at The Creamery San Francisco" into the system calendar will only generate an automatically created appointment event for "Tuesday afternoon 1 pm," without indicating the location. Sunrise doesn't have this problem.

3. Apple has removed the limit of storing only 9 apps per icon group

This change itself isn't problematic, but each icon group in iOS7 can only display the first 9 icons, and the rest will be blurred. Therefore, it's unclear how much substantial improvement removing this restriction brings.

4. Apple's way of saving and backing up user photos via iCloud isn't user-friendly

If you're a long-term iPhone user, from 3GS to 5s, you may have many photos saved on iCloud, including many older low-resolution ones. Apple shouldn't count the space occupied by these photos toward the current iCloud usage. Simply put, if you bought a 16GB iPhone 4 and then upgraded to a 32GB iPhone 5s, you should get a total of 48GB of relative iCloud storage space.

5. Confusion with downward swipe gestures

Sliding your finger down from the top of the screen can bring up the notification menu in the iOS system. In iOS7, Apple defines that if you don't start swiping from the very top of the screen but rather from somewhere in the middle, it will directly bring up the search bar. However, in reality, errors occur far more frequently than expected. Also, Apple has added the "Control Center" feature in iOS7, which can be brought up by swiping up from the bottom of the screen and closed by swiping down. However, if you perform a downward swipe gesture within the "Control Center" frame, the control object will be to turn off the Bluetooth module or Wi-Fi module. Very confusing.

6. Lack of sharing support

Apple likes to maintain high control over the system, which is good from the perspective of platform stability and operability. However, when it comes to sharing, this isn't necessarily the case. Currently, iOS only supports three photo-sharing platforms: Facebook, Twitter, and Flickr. In comparison, Android is much freer and can share to platforms like Pinterest or Instagram. Of course, we can still complete content sharing through apps, but Apple needs to seriously consider redefining the supported platforms.

7. Keyboard input remains unchanged

Don't expect any improvements in keyboard input with iOS7; everything is the same as before. Autocorrect still requires users to click on the tiny 'x,' and third-party software remains a better choice.
