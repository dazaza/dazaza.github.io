Xiaomi Technology has successfully raised funds again, with its valuation rising once more to reach $10 billion.

Since its inception, Xiaomi Technology has generally maintained a rhythm of raising funds annually (excluding the angel round), and each time there has been a significant increase in valuation: In late 2010, Xiaomi was valued at $250 million; by the end of 2011, Xiaomi's valuation had reached $1 billion; in June 2012, Xiaomi was valued at $4 billion; and by August 2013, Xiaomi's valuation had reached $10 billion.

Since the company's valuation has already risen to $10 billion, how far is Xiaomi from a $100 billion valuation? Perhaps we can look back at the stories of internet companies whose valuations rose from $10 billion to $100 billion. Google

In 2004, when Google went public, the company's market value was $27 billion. It quickly surpassed $100 billion in 2005, but it wasn't until 2012 that the company's market value truly broke through $300 billion.

Over these 8 years, Google can be divided into the "Schmidt era" and the "Page era," the former being from 2004 to 2011, and the latter from 2011 to the present. From 2004 to 2005, due to the company's financial statements exceeding Wall Street expectations for three consecutive quarters, under optimistic market sentiment, the company's market value broke through $100 billion, which was quite rapid.

From 2004 to 2011, Google gradually diversified its development. Although it appeared that its business lines were broad and deep, advertising still accounted for the majority of the company's revenue. During this period, Google undertook several bold and socially beneficial projects, such as launching Google Translate to make communication less difficult; releasing the digital version of the Dead Sea Scrolls so everyone could see them; and initiating Google Labs to encourage internal employee innovation. Many of the side projects born during this time became the foundation of Google's current businesses, such as Gmail, Google Maps, and Google AdSense.

During this period, Google also made continuous acquisitions, including AdMob mobile advertising and YouTube, which has now become the king of online video.

In the "Schmidt era," Google continuously extended the possibilities of the internet, striving to provide various types of services for netizens, making Google an indispensable part of online life. However, one action that significantly influenced future valuations was made separately by Larry Page and Sergey Brin. In 2005, without Schmidt's knowledge, they represented Google in acquiring Android.

This proved to be a very correct acquisition decision — analysts often said that Google's revenue was overly dependent on advertising. Now, Google's revenue is still dependent on advertising, but there are more outlets for it. Of course, the popularity of Android is closely related to Andy Rubin's decision to open Android to other manufacturers. Without the help of other manufacturers, Android wouldn't have become the most popular mobile operating system.

In 2011, as Schmidt gradually stepped away from the company's daily operations and no longer served as Google's CEO, his successor was Page. After taking office, Page began consolidating Google's product lines and started making Google X Laboratory known to the public, announcing imaginative plans like autonomous driving cars and Google Glass, making the company more story-driven. This is exactly what the capital market hopes to see.

As a result, Google's market value and stock price continued to rise with the popularity of Android and the emergence of Google X, finally surpassing $300 billion in 2012. Facebook

In 2011, Goldman Sachs invested $450 million in Facebook, valuing the company at $50 billion. When Facebook went public, the company's valuation was $100 billion, with a stock issuance price of $38 per share. The current stock price is $38.55. After a year of ups and downs, Facebook's stock has returned to its issuance price.

However, before going public, in the secondary market, Facebook's valuation also experienced a process of rising from low to high: In July 2008, Facebook's internal valuation was $4 billion; in October 2009, Russian venture capital firm DST purchased Facebook shares at a valuation of $6.5 billion; in June 2010, pre-IPO research institution Next Up valued Facebook at $12.5 billion; in November 2010, Facebook's largest investor Accel Partners sold "a large amount" of Facebook shares at a valuation of $3.4 billion; in January 2011, Facebook raised $500 million from Goldman Sachs and DST at a valuation of $50 billion; in June 2011, GSV bought 225,000 Facebook shares at $22.98 per share, valuing Facebook at $70 billion.

In 2010, Google criticized Facebook for not opening up to search engines, preventing Google from searching its content. This meant Facebook became a closed garden where any interaction generated by users inside would not bring traffic to Google. In the internet economy, traffic is a lifeline. In this discussion, the "Facebook threat theory" escalated more and more, even affecting Google's stock performance. Meanwhile, the media environment increasingly favored Facebook's prospects.

Of course, after 2008, Facebook's user growth skyrocketed, which was the reason people were optimistic about it. It was also the basis for Mark Zuckerberg's claim to turn Facebook into public infrastructure. However, after going public, Facebook's stock price fell below its issuance price because people discovered that Facebook's performance in mobile was lackluster. The company bet on HTML5 technology, making a wrong bet. "Queen of the Internet" Mary Meeker pointed out in the "Internet Trends Report" that Facebook had difficulties in mobile monetization.

It wasn't until this year that Zuckerberg publicly admitted his "mistake," turning the company's development direction towards mobile applications, developing Facebook Home, continuously optimizing the Facebook app experience, and even copying SnapChat to launch the Facebook Poke app, showing changes in the company's attitude.

Consequently, Facebook's stock price has recently risen again. A year ago, those who warned others not to buy Facebook stock probably regret it deeply now. Summary

Essentially, the rise in valuation for both Google and Facebook is closely related to their active investment in "mobile." Additionally, the rise in valuation is also related to the company's "themes." Google uses wearable devices and an intelligent future to give the company more substance; Facebook, on the other hand, presents itself as "internet infrastructure," revealing its potential.

Xiaomi Technology's current high valuation is related to China being the world's largest smartphone market, the company's rapid growth, the popularity of Redmi, and the business model it advocates. Apple's stock decline is because investors don't see a bright future for hardware profits, but Xiaomi Technology proposes maintaining high-speed development through software and service charges.

Regardless, Xiaomi Technology aligns with the capital market's imagination of a typical excellent internet company, and $10 billion is a manifestation of this affirmation. This year, Lei Jun can continue discussing the "dream multiple."
