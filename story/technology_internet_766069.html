Analysis: Nokia finds the best destination, Microsoft's dream of devices and services comes true

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:43:54

When people were still guessing who would become Microsoft's CEO, Microsoft announced the acquisition of Nokia, an ending that was both surprising and expected. The marriage between Microsoft and Nokia means Nokia has found its best destination, and through this acquisition, Microsoft’s device + service strategy is further improved.

A brief commentary on this event:

Nokia Finds Its Best Destination

After Nokia abandoned MeeGo and Symbian and chose Windows Phone, it seemed to outsiders that they had boarded a ship in turbulent waters. Although the Windows Phone camp included heavyweight players like Samsung, HTC, and Nokia, their commitment to Windows Phone was relatively light. However, through the joint efforts of Microsoft and Nokia, Windows Phone has now become the world's third-largest mobile operating system, and Nokia, as a "latecomer," has become the spokesperson for the Windows Phone camp.

Despite the pressure from iOS and Android, Windows Phone is still experiencing steady growth, and Nokia has firmly established itself in the market with its Lumia series of smartphones.

Observant individuals will notice that on Microsoft's official online store, there is a dedicated section for "Nokia" recommending Nokia's Windows Phone smartphones, which seems to confirm the subtle relationship between Nokia and Microsoft.

Currently, Windows Phone doesn't have many standout features, but Nokia's PureView technology has become a highlight, especially the Lumia 1020, which has drawn significant industry attention.

Windows Phone is a respected force in the smartphone market, and Nokia is its most steadfast supporter. Now, as an integral participant, Nokia will bring Windows Phone to a new milestone.

Prior to this, rumors about Nokia launching a Windows tablet were rampant. In the future, Nokia's device team will work closely with Microsoft's device team, and I am already looking forward to the next generation of Surface and Windows Phone.

However, Nokia's slow pace has been overshadowed by the rapid updates of the Android market, leaving outsiders feeling dissatisfied. Since the beginning of 2013, Nokia's pace has noticeably accelerated, with almost monthly releases of new products. Nevertheless, Nokia's efforts in the smartphone market are no longer enough to capture people's attention. The current hot topic is wearable technology, and missing out on this market might make Nokia appear outdated. After joining the Microsoft family, perhaps more exciting developments will emerge.

Microsoft Plugs Its Weaknesses

With the acquisition of Nokia, Microsoft's device + service strategy finally comes to fruition.

Microsoft aims to be an end-to-end solution and service provider, and this move is the best interpretation and explanation of cloud computing era practices.

To provide smooth transitions between business and entertainment services for enterprise customers and consumers, this was the original intention behind Ballmer's "device + service" strategy. Although Microsoft repeatedly claims to already be a device + service company, its own hardware business has always been a weak point. While Xbox is one of the most popular gaming consoles, Microsoft's keyboards and mice are also well-received, and the industrial design of Surface is commendable, overall, Microsoft's hardware revenue is very low.

So, after acquiring Nokia, will Microsoft transform into Apple?

Yes, but not entirely.

In a sense, the most successful company of the 21st century could be said to be Apple. Its vertical integration capabilities and influence have made it the most envied company on the planet, a model known as the Apple model.

Since 2012, Microsoft has proposed transitioning to a device + service model, with all its products and initiatives centered around this strategy. In July of this year, Microsoft announced a reorganization, marking its transformation into a device + service company. The acquisition of Nokia's devices and services business improves Microsoft's device department further, and Nokia's CEO taking charge of Microsoft's device department raises speculation about becoming Ballmer's successor.

In a certain sense, Microsoft, like Apple, is becoming more vertically integrated, but Microsoft is not a complete version of Apple; it possesses many traits that Apple lacks.

Microsoft has numerous partners, serves a wider variety of users and markets, and is a company with stronger social responsibility. In contrast, Apple is highly innovative in technology but is, to a large extent, a company with strong control over all high-tech aspects.

Additionally, Microsoft is deeply rooted in the commercial market, having cultivated the enterprise-level and cloud computing markets for years. In the U.S., Microsoft even provides containerized data centers, and it operates public cloud services such as Windows Azure and Office 365. From this perspective, Microsoft is a deeper-rooted company.

It can be anticipated that after acquiring Nokia, Microsoft's hardware business will see greater development. This is different from Google's acquisition of Motorola. Microsoft acquired Nokia to help Windows Phone grow stronger, while Google acquired Motorola mainly for its patent shield and as an Android lab.
