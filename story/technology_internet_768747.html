Microsoft CEO's hottest candidate Murally may close the search engine and spin off the Xbox business

by anonymous on 2013-11-21 14:03:12

Microsoft is in the process of selecting a new CEO to replace Ballmer, who will be responsible for leading Microsoft's transformation into the mobile internet era. The CEO and President of Ford Motor Company, Alan Mulally, has emerged as the top candidate. Recently, Bank of America Merrill Lynch predicted in a research report that if Mulally takes the helm at Microsoft, he might shut down or abandon the online search and Xbox gaming console businesses—areas where Microsoft has already failed or which do not align with future trends.

The analyst at Bank of America Merrill Lynch, Kash Rangan, downgraded Microsoft’s stock rating to "underperform," but maintained a target price of $36 per share (Microsoft closed at $36.74 on Tuesday).

This analyst pointed out that with an impending change in leadership, shareholders and investors should prepare for significant shifts in Microsoft's business. Before his retirement, Ballmer announced that Microsoft would transform into a "devices and services" company, launching tablets and acquiring Nokia's phone business. However, the next leader may still overturn Ballmer's strategy.

Indeed, some media have pointed out that Ballmer was forced to retire early due to his inability to lead Microsoft into the mobile internet age. What is puzzling is that Ballmer did not leave enough strategic room for his successor but instead executed a 180-degree strategic shift just before his retirement. Some industry insiders question Ballmer's approach of restructuring just before leaving.

Analyst Rangan noted that continuing with Ballmer's current strategy, such as integrating Nokia's phone business, would create a massive burden for the new CEO and disappoint investors.

This analyst analyzed the possible changes in Microsoft's stock price and strategy depending on different popular candidates chosen as CEO. He said that if Ford's Mulally becomes the CEO, Microsoft's stock would likely rise, potentially increasing by about 10%.

It is reported that if Mulally leads Microsoft, he might make significant adjustments to Microsoft's operations, including shutting down the Bing search engine and transferring the video game console business, Xbox.

These two businesses are considered underperforming and not aligned with future trends. Years ago, Microsoft launched Bing search and formed a strategic alliance with Yahoo! hoping to end Google's monopolistic advantage in online search. However, Microsoft's efforts completely failed, with Google maintaining over two-thirds of the U.S. search market share for many years. Microsoft and Yahoo! merely played a game of shifting search shares from one hand to another. Due to dissatisfaction with the partnership, Yahoo! hoped to delay or terminate the cooperation, leading both parties to awkwardly take the matter to court (the judge ruled that the cooperation must continue).

In fact, PC internet businesses like Bing have become a bottomless pit for Microsoft. Microsoft's internet business has been losing money for a long time, unable to challenge Google's position. While Ballmer was busy chasing Google, the internet quietly entered the mobile internet era, with Android and iOS rising. Microsoft found itself once again lagging behind in a new field.

In the video game console sector, although Sony and Microsoft will launch new consoles this Christmas shopping season (Sony's PS4 initially sold well), many media outlets believe we have now entered the mobile gaming era. Players increasingly prefer using smartphones and tablets to play games, making traditional TV-connected video game consoles no longer representative of the future direction. They may only survive for a few more years. In fact, statistics show that the sales of video game software in the U.S. have plummeted by as much as 20%, with some video game developers going bankrupt.

According to analysis, Mulally could reduce Microsoft's operating costs by $6 billion to $7 billion, using part of the funds for a Microsoft stock repurchase plan, which would stimulate the stock price.

This analyst pointed out that Mulally's large-scale adjustments to Microsoft's business could face resistance from founder and chairman Bill Gates, making it difficult for Microsoft to easily exit the gaming console and online search markets.

Gartner analyst Brian Bla also pointed out that for Microsoft, Xbox is a relatively complex business. There have been rumors in the industry that Microsoft incurs losses every year in its gaming console business, though there is currently no detailed data to confirm this.

It should be noted that the Xbox gaming console is seen as Ballmer's sole achievement during his more than ten years at Microsoft. If the Xbox business is transferred, Ballmer will leave no innovation record in Microsoft's history (Office and Windows are both regarded as "legacy" from founder Bill Gates).

Besides the top contender Mulally, other candidates for Microsoft's CEO include former Nokia CEO Stephen Elop, Skype's long-time head Tony Bates, and Microsoft's Chief Operating Officer Kevin Turner.

The aforementioned analyst believes that if Microsoft fails to find an external candidate to serve as CEO and inject fresh blood into the company, opting instead for an internal executive, this would put pressure on Microsoft's stock price, with an expected decline of around 10%.

Industry predictions suggest that if Elop becomes the CEO of Microsoft, he would not make major changes but might introduce iOS and Android versions of Office.
