The Provisions of the Supreme People's Court on Several Issues Regarding the Enforcement of Property Sentences: A Defense Perspective

by bianhu314 on 2010-04-27 17:21:56

Shangquan Law Firm in Beijing specializes in the defense of criminal cases. Website: http://www.sqxb.com

The property penalties prescribed by China's Criminal Law include fines and confiscation of property. Both are additional penalties within the penal system. The fine is a method of punishment where the People's Court sentences the defendant to pay a certain amount of money to the state. Confiscation of property refers to forcibly and gratuitously transferring part or all of the personal property of the defendant to state ownership as a form of punishment, which applies in cases like smuggling defense attorneys. Property penalties, as one of the means of punishment, their effective execution is an important manifestation of the state's realization of its penal authority.

Since the Criminal Law and the Criminal Procedure Law do not clearly specify the enforcement body and procedures for property penalties, this leads to ambiguity regarding the specific department responsible for enforcement and the procedures involved. Although many criminal judgments impose property penalties on defendants, in practice, courts rarely take the initiative to enforce these penalties when defendants fail to voluntarily comply. Coupled with the difficulties in enforcing property penalties, they have not been effectively enforced, thus failing to fully realize their functions and roles. The phenomenon of "empty judgment" in property penalties has become increasingly prominent, seriously affecting the seriousness and authority of court rulings. To improve the enforcement system of property penalties, standardize enforcement work, fully utilize the functions of property penalties, and uphold the judicial authority of the People's Courts, the Supreme People's Court announced the "Several Provisions on Issues Concerning the Enforcement of Property Penalties" on February 10, 2010. These provisions came into effect on June 1, 2010. The regulations clarified aspects such as the enforcement body, enforcement time, and applicable laws:

(1) Enforcement Department: Property penalties shall be enforced by the enforcement bureau of the first-instance People's Court; if the property to be enforced is located elsewhere, the first-instance People's Court may entrust the same-level People's Court at the location of the property to execute on its behalf.

(2) Enforcement Time:

- For fines: If the defendant fails to pay in full within the period determined by the judgment or ruling, enforcement will commence after the expiration of the period.

- For confiscation of property: Execution begins immediately upon the judgment taking effect.

(3) Applicable Laws: In the absence of specific provisions in criminal law concerning enforcement, relevant provisions in civil enforcement can be applied.

Due to the ineffectiveness of property penalty enforcement, it has largely been in a state of "empty judgment". As a result, defenders often do not emphasize whether the defendant should be subject to property penalties or the severity thereof during the defense process. After the interpretation took effect on June 1, 2010, courts will increase their efforts in enforcing property penalties, making the strict enforcement of property penalties truly painful for the defendant. As defenders, we should change our approach. For cases where property penalties may be imposed according to the law, while defending the severity of the deprivation of liberty penalty for the defendant, we should also defend whether the defendant should be subject to property penalties, as well as the scope and extent of the property penalty.

Article 52 of the Criminal Law stipulates that when imposing a fine, the amount should be determined based on the circumstances of the crime. However, the Criminal Law provides relatively vague guidelines on fines, with many offenses only specifying that a fine may be imposed without detailing how the amount should be determined. To further clarify the application of fines, the Supreme People's Court issued the "Provisions on Several Issues Concerning the Application of Property Penalties" on December 13, 2000, providing detailed rules on how to impose property penalties and considering the defendant's financial status as an important basis for imposing property penalties. First, for crimes where the Criminal Law specifies that both confiscation of property and fines may be imposed concurrently, the People's Court should decide whether to impose a fine or confiscate property based on the specifics of the case and the financial status of the offender. Second, when imposing a fine on the defendant, the court should consider factors such as illegal gains, the extent of losses caused, and comprehensively evaluate the offender's ability to pay the fine, determining the amount accordingly.

Currently, investigative agencies generally do not investigate the financial status of the defendant when handling criminal cases, resulting in few evidence materials in the case file related to the defendant's personal property, or even no such evidence. When defenders handle cases where property penalties may apply, they should give full attention to evidence materials and clues regarding the defendant's personal financial status in the case files. During meetings with the defendant, they should also inquire in detail about the defendant's economic situation. If the defendant is economically disadvantaged and unable to pay the fine, the defender should collect and obtain evidence in this regard, proposing that the defendant should not be subject to property penalties. For cases where the law mandates a fine, the provision of evidence materials regarding the defendant's financial status by the defender facilitates the court's comprehensive consideration of the defendant's financial status, enabling an appropriate property penalty judgment for the defendant.
