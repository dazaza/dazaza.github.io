How to properly use a toothbrush?

A toothbrush is the primary tool people use to maintain oral hygiene. There are many varieties and brands of toothbrushes available on the market, but how do you choose the most suitable one? The following factors should be considered:

(1) Size of the brush head: According to the regulations set by the American Dental Association, the length of the toothbrush head should be 2.5 to 3 cm, the width should be 0.8 to 1 cm, with 2 to 4 rows of bristles, each row containing 5 to 12 bundles of bristles. The front end of the brush head should be blunt. These regulations can serve as a reference when choosing a toothbrush.

(2) Hardness of the bristles: Generally, they can be divided into soft, medium, and hard. A medium-hardness toothbrush is usually more suitable for daily use. When purchasing a toothbrush, press the bristles with your finger; if it feels painful, it's too hard and shouldn't be chosen. Most toothbrushes currently available have nylon bristles, which are superior in elasticity, uniformity, and hardness compared to pig hair toothbrushes, making them more beneficial for oral health.

(3) Angle between the brush head and handle: Toothbrushes available on the market come in two types - straight and angled. Straight toothbrushes are more forceful to use, while angled toothbrushes provide better cleaning for the back teeth. An angle of 17 to 20 degrees is appropriate.

(4) Top of the bristles: Each bristle tip should be blunt, without sharp angles. You can judge this by touch when purchasing.

(5) Special toothbrushes: For patients with periodontal disease or older individuals with receding gums and increased tooth gaps, interdental toothbrushes are recommended in addition to regular ones. These toothbrushes have cone-shaped or long-strip bristles that can be selected based on preference. There is also a type of toothbrush with only two rows of bristles, used for gum massage. A concave-shaped toothbrush with longer outer bristles than inner ones is specifically designed for those undergoing dental alignment correction. This toothbrush not only cleans the tooth surface but also removes food residues from orthodontic appliances. For individuals with limited hand and foot mobility (such as stroke patients), electric toothbrushes or light energy ion toothbrushes can be used.

Proper methods of using a toothbrush include:

(1) After each use, thoroughly wash the toothbrush and shake off as much water as possible. Place the toothbrush head-up in a cup or in a ventilated area with sunlight to dry and disinfect it.

(2) If conditions allow, purchase 2 or 3 toothbrushes for rotation, extending the drying time. This is especially important for people suffering from gingivitis and periodontitis. Rotation also helps maintain the elasticity of the bristles.

(3) Old toothbrushes with spread-out or curled bristles, having lost their elasticity, must be replaced promptly, as they can harm teeth and gums.

(4) For children living in group settings, conduct a thorough cleaning and disinfection of the toothbrushes once a week.

(5) Toothbrushes should not be shared to prevent the transmission of diseases.
