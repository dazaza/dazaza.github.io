Microsoft Launches Surface 2: A Road to Persistence Despite Repeated Setbacks

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 22:07:42

Just after launching, the product line suffered a painful failure. Should such a product line continue to be promoted?

Perhaps most companies would choose to abandon it, but Microsoft gave a firm and affirmative answer. This product line is Microsoft's own tablet, Surface, and the signpost on this road reads "Micro-hard".

A history of repeated defeats and continued battles

Last year, Microsoft launched its own brand tablets, Surface RT and Pro, with high hopes to bring the advantages of desktops into the tablet field and challenge the dominant positions of Apple and Google. However, the market's reaction was like icy cold water. In the first quarter of this year, the total global sales volume of Surface RT and Pro was only 900,000 units, with a market share of less than 2%, while Apple's iPad sales during the same period were 19.5 million units. The difference in sales between products with similar hardware was enormous.

What made Microsoft even more awkward was that by the second quarter of this year, the total sales revenue of the Suface product line was $853 million, but they had to record a $900 million asset impairment related to RT, plus an additional $900 million spent on marketing for Surface and Windows 8. Microsoft suffered heavy losses on Surface. Due to the poor performance of the RT tablet, HTC, Samsung, and other OEM manufacturers abandoned their RT development plans one after another. Currently, the only company still pushing forward is Microsoft itself (Nokia, a good partner, has already been acquired by Microsoft).

Facing such a defeat, Microsoft still did not give up. Today, they released the second generation of Surface in New York, continuing on the path towards "Micro-hard". If we look back at the development of the past decade, we can see that Microsoft is not afraid of failure; as long as repeated defeats and continued battles can lead to a future, they will persevere.

Besides Windows and Office, these unshakable money trees, software giant Microsoft has also faced many setbacks in developing new products. Xbox, Zune, Bing, all started poorly, with continuous losses for several years, constantly criticized by the market and industry. These products that struggled at the beginning have had different outcomes: Xbox has now become one of Microsoft's most successful products, Zune was decisively abandoned after years of persistence, and Bing, although not performing brilliantly, is a pillar business for Microsoft to compete against Google.

Perhaps, Microsoft hopes that Surface becomes the next Xbox, breaking through in the tablet market dominated by Apple and Google just as they did ten years ago against Sony and Nintendo. But even if it cannot achieve the same success as Xbox, the significance of Surface to Microsoft is no less than Bing, because mobility has become something Microsoft cannot afford to lose. The failures in smartphones and tablets are important reasons for Ballmer stepping down as CEO earlier than expected.

Ballmer set the strategy for Microsoft to transform into a "devices and services company." Software giant Microsoft's business spans both enterprise and consumer markets; for Microsoft, which has continuously succeeded in the enterprise market, the transformation means achieving breakthroughs in the mobile phone and tablet fields. Spending $7.2 billion to acquire Nokia's hardware department is an important manifestation of this strategy. Nokia Lumia phones account for nearly 90% of the Windows Phone camp's shipments. With this acquisition, Microsoft almost monopolizes the WP product line.

In the tablet field, Microsoft does not have a strategic partner like Nokia, so it can only rely on the Surface product to drive various OEM manufacturers to follow, especially the Pro product line. Microsoft is not unwilling for Surface to achieve huge sales, but using Surface as a demonstration to promote hardware partners to jointly create a Windows tablet ecosystem is the only way to occupy a share under the Apple and Google platforms. This is the most important goal of Microsoft's Surface product line. Although the sales volume of Surface Pro is not large, from the fact that mainstream manufacturers like Acer, Asus, and Dell have launched Win 8 tablets this year, the first-generation Surface Pro has partially achieved Microsoft's goals.

Entertainment and work balance

Therefore, even though the first-generation Surface performed dimly, Microsoft will firmly continue to push the Surface strategy. Differentiation is the possible breakthrough path for Microsoft's tablets, specifically speaking, creating tablets that balance entertainment and work, achieving functions that Apple and Google tablets cannot.

Apple and Google position tablets mainly as content consumption devices. Although there are deficiencies in content creation, the complete application ecosystem provides more entertainment functions. The first-generation Surface, while excelling in work efficiency, fell short in applications and portability, making its entertainment function unable to compare with Apple and Android tablets. Moreover, with pricing equal to or even higher than Apple's iPad, the poor sales of the first-generation Surface were understandable.

In the second-generation Surface products, Microsoft continues to enhance processor performance: the upgraded version of RT, Surface 2, uses Nvidia's Tegra 4 quad-core chip; while Surface Pro 2 incorporates Intel's latest Haswell Core i5 processor, significantly improving battery life (by 75%) while continuing to boost performance (by 20%). If combined with the latest Power Cover keyboard cover, Surface Pro 2 can already achieve all-day battery life like Apple's Macbook Air.

At the launch event, Microsoft Surface Vice President Panos Panay emphasized the "full performance" of Surface Pro the most: this is a tablet that balances work and entertainment, a tablet whose performance can rival laptops. From the on-site demonstrations of Surface Pro 2 running large games and various document processing tasks, as well as editing videos shot by professional cameras like RED, Surface Pro 2 deserves the title of a workstation.

Microsoft's thinking is very clear. How many electronic devices does a consumer need? Smartphones and laptops are indispensable, tablets are currently more focused on entertainment, occasionally handling emails. When people go out, they don't want to carry both a tablet and a laptop. If there is a product that can be used like a tablet for handheld tasks and transform into a truly full-performance laptop through accessories, plus a reasonable price, then such a product is almost sure to be popular.

This is the positioning of the Surface Pro product line. If Surface Pro truly perfectly balances entertainment and work, while possessing portability and full performance, then Microsoft's tablets will definitely have the strength to win the market, after all, the vast majority of consumers are Windows users. However, judging from the pricing of Surface Pro 2, the more likely competitor might actually be the ultrabooks promoted by Intel, a Microsoft ally. Thin, portable, and high performance are also important selling points of ultrabooks, and increasingly more ultrabooks are starting to come equipped with touch screens, and can even transform into tablets.

In terms of pricing, the starting price of $900 for Surface Pro 2 is roughly equivalent to ultrabooks, approaching the level of Apple's Macbook Air. Microsoft believes that future laptops will merge with tablets, and Surface Pro is such a product; meanwhile, ultrabooks are evolving in this direction under the impetus of touch screens and convertible designs. Both product lines belong to Microsoft and Intel's products, but they are concepts each company separately promotes, and now are more likely to compete internally.

Surface 2 is Microsoft's persistence in the low-end tablet market. Although after acquiring Nokia, RT tablets are left solely supported by Microsoft, at least for now, Microsoft shows no intention of giving up. Surface 2 has seen significant improvements in chips, screens, cameras, and more. From a hardware perspective, this tablet is no longer inferior to Apple's iPad and performs better in work-related tasks. The obvious shortcoming remains the number of applications. However, it is worth noting that from its official release last October until now, it took 12 months for the RT Store's app count to reach the hundred-thousand level, whereas it took iPad apps 14 months to reach this threshold initially.

High pricing remains the Achilles' heel

To support this crucial Surface product line, Microsoft has virtually utilized all available strategic resources. Office, Xbox, Skype, SkyDrive, all these have become the main selling points Microsoft uses to attract consumers to buy Windows tablets. From these numerous free services, it is evident how significant the Surface tablet is to Microsoft's "devices and services" transformation strategy.

However, Surface still has flaws, and may remain difficult to popularize in the short term. Overpricing is currently the most realistic issue. Price reductions could potentially boost sales, but lowering prices would impact the profit margins of other hardware manufacturers, affecting the overall ecosystem of Microsoft tablets. Therefore, Microsoft faces a dilemma in pricing Surface.

Given that the brand appeal and entertainment performance of Surface RT are far behind iPad, pricing Surface 2 close to iPad might only hinder its sales. Judging from the immediate increase in sales after previous price promotions, Microsoft perhaps should adopt more practical pricing for Surface 2, winning over consumers through substantial price cuts or directly bundling the ultra-slim keyboard cover. After all, the overall capabilities of RT tablets still fall far short of iPad currently.

The pricing of Surface Pro 2 (starting at RMB 6588) could also hinder its sales. From online reactions, consumers generally recognize the performance of Surface Pro products, with the only regret being the excessively high price. Perhaps consumers still largely view Surface Pro as a tablet rather than a fully functional notebook that can double as a tablet. However, this high pricing gives Microsoft's partners ample room to maneuver. In the vast space between RMB 3000 to 6000, hardware partners like Acer and Asus are fully capable and have profitable space to launch fully featured Win 8 tablets.

Promoting the Win 8 tablet ecosystem is also one of the missions of Surface Pro. IDC predicts that the sales volume of X86 platform tablets this year could reach 10 million units. This is precisely the situation Microsoft hopes to see: opening up space for Win 8 tablets through the Surface Pro product line, attracting more hardware partners to join, thereby securing Microsoft a place in the tablet field.

Perhaps none of the first few generations of Surface will sell hotly, nor will they be able to challenge iPad in terms of sales volume, but the significance of this product line to Microsoft is no less than Xbox, Bing, and Windows Phone. It is a direct embodiment of Microsoft's strategy of "using hardware to drive the ecosystem." Therefore, even if Microsoft incurs losses, they will continue to push forward. After experiencing the massive asset impairment of the first generation, this time Microsoft will approach the production capacity of the second-generation Surface more rationally, avoiding overproduction under overly optimistic expectations.

From a software giant to a company balancing both software and hardware, Microsoft is steadily moving forward on the bumpy path towards becoming a "devices and services company." Acquiring Nokia Lumia phones and promoting the Surface tablet line, Microsoft is advancing steadily despite difficulties. Fortunately, Microsoft has substantial financial reserves, allowing them to use funds and patience to exchange for a foreseeable future. In Microsoft's eyes, today's Surface might just be yesterday's Xbox.

The path to "Micro-hard," traced back, is long and arduous.
