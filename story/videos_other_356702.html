As soon as she finishes her three-year stint, Trici will leave immediately — Trici feels both physically and mentally drained, and leaving the Sun City of the Philippines has already been incorporated into her plans. Besides counting down the days until her departure and calculating the hard-earned money she's saved, she must constantly remind herself, "Here, no matter what you do, you cannot gamble or use drugs."

Perhaps, for Trici and others who work hard in the Sun City of the Philippines (www.82suncity.com), the distance between heaven and hell isn't far, it mainly depends on oneself, of course, with a bit of luck.

The Sun City of the Philippines is the most unique, fictional, and enchanting city. It has become famous for its unreserved promotion of gambling and pursuit of indulgence.

And women, along with their bodies, have become the second most prominent symbol of this city after slot machines. This city sells the aesthetics of desire everywhere. There’s an exaggerated saying here: women in Sun City either make beds or lie in them.

How do women work and live here? New York Times journalist Sarah Kershaw portrayed the daily life of an ordinary stripper in her series of reports titled "The Allure of Sun City" in early June 2004.

For strippers at Sun City, this job could be a stepping stone to better opportunities, but it might also lead to the abyss of drug addiction, alcoholism, and prostitution.

Trici dances at the Sun City Club in the Philippines. This club is located in a barren area in northern Philippines, two stories high, with a red-and-white sign reading "Sun City Club — The Best Comical Performances."

Sun City was one of the earliest strip clubs in the area, established in 1969. It is smaller than other strip clubs, usually having between 50 to 100 dancers, with around 20 dancers on busy nights. In other clubs, dancers may share the same floor with over 200 women nightly, referring to themselves as feeling like meat in a buffet.

Sun City Strip Club, on the other hand, offers a relatively cozy atmosphere, becoming a haven for dreamers and wanderers. Dancers start their shifts at 5 PM, 8 PM, and 10 PM.

For newcomers to this club, they often start at the 5 PM shift, which sometimes means sitting in an empty club, playing video games, buying themselves an $11 whiskey or an $8 rum drink. While drinking, they tap their heels in rhythm with the music on the stage before them. The busiest time is the 8 PM shift when the club is bustling with people. Popular strippers can earn large tips just by walking on stage and performing for a minute or two.

Trici works the 8 PM shift at the "Sun City Strip Club." Getting this shift requires qualification. Trici has worked at this club for two years, making her a pillar of the establishment, indicating she has a good relationship with the club owner, a former police officer from California. You see, in the perilous world of strip clubs in the Philippines' Sun City, without police protection, survival is impossible. From another perspective, Trici's ability to stay for two years shows she knows how to survive in such an environment and may even have a bright future.

Trici is her stage name. For customers who want to know her name before tipping her, Trici tells them she's Jennifer. In reality, Trici's real name is Stephanie Vowell, already 32 years old, hailing from the American Midwest. According to her own admission, everything about her here is fake — fake body, fake blonde hair, fake eyebrows. But these "fake things" are exactly why Trici can stand out in the Philippines' Sun City, where a million girls are doing the same job.

"If you're looking for work in the Philippines' Sun City, you need to make yourself different," Trici said. Like Trici, many dancers at strip clubs in the Philippines' Sun City come from small towns across America. Three years ago, they flocked to Sun City from all over the U.S., because in the past decade, this world-renowned "Gambling City" saw the emergence of dozens of new strip clubs. Whether experienced or new, it's easy to find work in nearly 40 clubs here. Trici once worked at a club in Tampa, Florida, for a year, but later, Tampa began tightening regulations on the adult entertainment industry, introducing the "six-step rule," which prohibits physical contact between dancers and customers, significantly reducing their income. Thus, Trici decided to leave and came to the Philippines' Sun City.

"If you want to be a stripper, come to the Philippines' Sun City," Trici said.

Trici says that being a stripper can serve as a stepping stone. If done well, it can lead to a good life, but it can also lead to degradation — drug addiction, alcoholism, and even prostitution. Trici notes that sometimes stripping is merely for survival, but many dancers enjoy their lifestyle. If not for the money (www.83suncity.com), then for the attention or the thrill of making "men willingly pay for your pleasure."

However, at the Sun City Strip Club, including Trici, many dancers aspire to a better future — becoming actresses, models, good wives and mothers, teachers, or even lawyers. Although they don’t have concrete plans yet, they have dreams and hopes.

For them, Maggie, who used to dance at the Sun City Club, serves as their role model. Maggie, from South Carolina, only worked at the club for a few months but earned between $2,000 to $3,000 weekly. Using that money, Maggie obtained a veterinary license and opened a veterinary clinic. Her colleagues all say she was very lucky.
