◎ Plot

A hero of the Civil War, Lieutenant Dunbar, in search of a new way of life, rides his horse Sico to the magical west. Fort Hays's Major Fambrough assigns Dunbar to the most remote outpost, Sedgwick. The grandeur of the prairie deeply attracts Dunbar, who decides to stay alone at the deserted outpost. However, due to Major Fambrough's suicide and the death of the driver at the hands of the Pawnee Indians, Dunbar becomes isolated.

Dunbar leads a peaceful yet monotonous life alone. This vast expanse of blue sky and fields seems like his paradise. Dunbar also befriends a wild wolf with two white front legs, naming it "Two White Socks."

However, this tranquil life does not last long as Dunbar makes contact with the Sioux Indians. The Sioux are abuzz over Dunbar's arrival, leading to disagreements on how to handle him.

Dunbar decides to actively seek out the Sioux. On the way, he rescues a Sioux woman named "Stands With A Fist" who attempted suicide. Originally white, she lost her family at a young age and was raised by the Sioux. After returning "Stands With A Fist" to the Sioux, Dunbar rides away.

Dunbar's visit greatly stirs the Sioux. The Indian leaders unanimously agree to return the visit to Dunbar. The next day, several Indians arrive at Dunbar's post. Thus, both sides begin slow but friendly interactions. Gradually, Dunbar becomes friends with the Sioux and earns an Indian name, "Dances With Wolves."

During buffalo hunting, Dunbar's horsemanship and marksmanship impress the Sioux. He also saves an Indian boy. From then on, Dunbar moves into the Sioux camp, beginning to become one of them. Meanwhile, sincere feelings develop between Dunbar and "Stands With A Fist."

When the savage Pawnee attack, Dunbar gives all his strength, distributing the weapons and ammunition from the outpost to the Sioux, helping them defeat their enemies. Dunbar once again becomes a hero among the Sioux. All the Sioux admire Dunbar, blessing his love and union with "Stands With A Fist." Dunbar deeply recognizes the simplicity, kindness, and friendliness of the Indians, living freely and happily among them. Except for skin color and appearance, Dunbar has become a complete Indian.

However, when Dunbar attempts to retrieve his notebook from the outpost to live permanently among the Indians, he discovers that a group of white soldiers have already stationed there. Seeing Dunbar dressed as an Indian, they open fire. His beloved horse Sico dies, and Dunbar becomes a prisoner. His defense of the Indians brings repeated abuse and beatings. They believe Dunbar is a traitor. Thus, Dunbar stops speaking English and resolutely speaks the Indian language instead. The commanding officer, having no other options, orders a team of soldiers to escort Dunbar back to Fort Hays. The Sioux discover this situation, eliminate the escort team, and rescue Dunbar.

Winter arrives, and the Sioux settle in a deep valley. To not involve his Sioux friends, Dunbar declines their retention and leaves the Sioux village with his wife, "Stands With A Fist."

Eighteen years later, the Sioux were forced to sign an agreement with the government, giving up the land they had inherited for generations.
