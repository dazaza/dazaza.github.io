What are the commonly used surgical approaches for facial bone plastic surgery?

by 865512 on 2009-01-08 09:17:33

What are the common surgical approaches for facial bone reshaping, and what are their respective advantages and disadvantages?

For zygomatic bone reshaping, the commonly used approach is an intraoral incision; while for mandibular angle osteotomy, there are two approaches, as follows:

**Intraoral Approach:** The greatest advantage of this approach is that it leaves no visible surgical scars. However, the complexity of the surgery significantly increases, along with a higher risk during the procedure. The postoperative recovery period is also relatively longer.

**Extraoral Approach:** The advantage of this approach lies in its simplicity of operation, good exposure, precise osteotomy, and faster recovery. The biggest drawback is the presence of visible surgical scars on the face. For the correction of mandibular protrusion and reduction of zygomatic bones, both procedures generally use an intraoral incision approach, which is relatively simple to perform and yields reliable results.

---

1) **Temporal Augmentation**

There are generally two methods: injection and implantation, with the latter being safer and more convenient.

**Implantation Method:** An incision is made within the hairline, and a silicone implant is inserted subperiosteally under the deep temporal fascia. Postoperatively, compression dressing is applied, and a negative pressure drainage tube is usually placed for 3 days, with sutures removed after about 7 days.

2) **Zygomatic Bone Reshaping**

Two commonly used methods are osteotomy and grinding.

**Osteotomy:** "L"-shaped zygomatic bone reduction is performed via an incision in the gingivobuccal groove between teeth 6-3│3-6. Subperiosteal dissection exposes the anterior wall of the maxilla, the zygomatic alveolar ridge, the zygomatic bone body, and the anterior portion of the zygomatic arch. According to preoperative design, an "L"-shaped osteotomy line is marked on the lateral side of the zygomatic alveolar ridge, below the infraorbital margin, and at the junction of the zygomatic arch and orbital rim. After using a fissure bur to cut through the bone plate, a chisel is used to remove the bone block between the zygomatic alveolar ridge and the zygomatic bone. A right-angle chisel is then used to break the oblique osteotomy line. The zygomatic bone is pressed inward and downward, causing a "greenstick" fracture at the root of the zygomatic arch. The width of the zygomatic arch is narrowed, and the high point of the zygomatic prominence is lowered. The bone ends are approximated and fixed with small titanium plates, and the bone edges are smoothed.

**Grinding Procedure:** This method is relatively simpler, involving grinding down the corresponding high points of the zygomatic bone based on the condition. The postoperative reaction is also milder, and this method is currently quite popular!

3) **Mandibular Angle Osteotomy**

There are currently two methods: osteotomy and grinding.

**Osteotomy:** The incision starts from the outer side of the upper edge of the mandibular ramus, along the vestibular groove slightly toward the buccal side up to the first mandibular bicuspid tooth. The mucoperiosteum is incised, and the "degloving" technique is used for subperiosteal dissection to expose the middle and lower parts of the mandibular ramus, the mandibular angle, and the posterior part of the mandibular body. According to the preoperative design, small round drills are used for marking, followed by arc-shaped osteotomy using a long-handled round drill and oscillating saw. The remaining bone connections are broken using a curved bone chisel. The attachment of the medial pterygoid muscle is separated, and the mandibular angle is completely removed. The osteotomy line is then polished and refined until smooth and even.

**Grinding Procedure:** Also known as milling, the incision design and periosteal stripping are similar to the previous method. A milling head is used to grind the outer and lower sides of the mandible, creating a wedge-shaped change. Therefore, the effect of this surgery is quite different from the former method. In the former, both appearance and touch show obvious changes, whereas in this method, the patient may still feel a prominent square jaw upon touch postoperatively, but visually, the symptoms are not present, thus achieving a similarly noticeable surgical result. This method is currently quite popular!

4) **Chin Reshaping**

For microgenia (small chin), either chin augmentation or chin bone reshaping can be performed to achieve the corrective purpose.
