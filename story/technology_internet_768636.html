Zhang Chaoyang's video strategy: three ways to counter Baidu

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:17:02

An unconventional matter placed at a sensitive time will yield a banal result.

Zhang Chaoyang said, if you interpret this matter strategically, then you are being banal. Perhaps Zhang Chaoyang's concern for the country and its people is not banal, but when Liu Chun went to Baidu, iQIYI was about to go public, and Zhang Chaoyang condemned Baidu in English, he indeed reaped a banal result.

Zhang Chaoyang is by no means a hermit in the Chinese Internet world. He is not numb to the landscape and changes of the Internet world, but he doesn't chase after every change.

Zhang Chaoyang has a more macroscopic judgment on the general trend of business and the Internet trends. Zhang Chaoyang excels at finding the logic of "gradual change" in things. This foresight cycle often lasts three to five years or even longer; unlike Zhou Hongyi, who focuses more on how to capture what he wants.

There's no denying that Zhang Chaoyang harbors ambitions for society. His idealistic sentiments have not completely regressed. This can be seen from his comments on social issues reflected in TV dramas and films in a certain program on Sohu Video.

However, Zhang Chaoyang does not shout or call out against all social contradictions and injustices. But if it touches upon what he considers as Sohu's "pain points," he will not tolerate it.

Where does Zhang Chaoyang see Sohu's "pain points"? In the issue of the "two children," Sohu has chosen to make media business the foundation of Sohu's development, while treating Sogou as a negotiable chip in Sohu's development strategy.

We can imagine that if Ma Huateng, Ding Lei, and Zhou Hongyi were in Zhang Chaoyang's position, they would certainly use Sogou as a strategic platform to develop derivative models; instead of using Sohu.com as a platform to incubate product businesses. In Zhang Chaoyang's mind, Sohu must be a media company, not a product-oriented company.

Although Zhang Chaoyang has also made some product-oriented acquisitions, such as Changyou. In Zhang Chaoyang's mind, the role of Changyou for Sohu is more of a financial acquisition that brings profit and cash flow. Ma Huateng coordinated QQ Games with QQ and WeChat, but Zhang Chaoyang would not coordinate Changyou's web games and mobile games with Sogou. This is not due to technical infeasibility, but rather an ideological and conceptual choice not to do so.

We can see that when Sogou and Baidu were competing, Zhang Chaoyang did not take the stage; when 360 fought against Sogou, Zhang Chaoyang did not take the stage. But when Sohu Video gained nothing in the merger and acquisition field, iQIYI was about to go public, and his beloved general joined the opponent's camp, Zhang Chaoyang couldn't hold back.

In the video domain, Zhang Chaoyang's last outcry was four years ago. In September 2009, Sohu teamed up with YouPengPuLe, Jidong.com, and others to form an "Anti-Piracy Alliance," targeting Youku and Tudou. Zhang Chaoyang's anti-piracy campaign directly affected Youku and Tudou's IPO timelines. Youku and Tudou had to resort to legitimate content, adding two rounds and one round of financing respectively after September 2009 to purchase legitimate dramas. Youku's two rounds were $40 million in December 2009 and $50 million in September 2010; Tudou added $50 million in July 2010.

As a result, the copyright market price reached a historical high point under these circumstances. According to financial reports, in the first nine months of 2010, Youku's loss amounted to $25 million, which was $5 million more than Youku's loss in the first nine months of 2009 (before Sohu began its anti-piracy campaign).

The extra $5 million was exactly the cost Youku paid because of Zhang Chaoyang. Additionally, there was the time cost Youku had to bear. Youku completed its IPO in December 2010. And precisely in 2010, Sohu Video launched its "American Drama Plan," thereby shortening the competitive time gap with Youku. (However, Zhang Chaoyang's anti-piracy strategy also helped Gu Yongqiang. Gu Yongqiang had stronger financing capabilities than Wang Wei. Transitioning from UGC to capital-driven copyright competition benefited Gu Yongqiang in shedding Tudou. But that's another story.)

How to counter iQIYI's IPO, Zhang Chaoyang has his own strategies. That is to optimize and upgrade the "anti-piracy" alliance strategy from 2009.

In this alliance, Zhang Chaoyang found common interest partners in Youku and Letv. Just like Zhang Chaoyang, Gu Yongqiang does not want Baidu's iQIYI to have a smooth IPO. The "iQIYI PPS" story is much more appealing than "Youku Tudou." Mobile, client-side advantages, and having a "godfather" like Baidu are all superior resources for the former; and in terms of market share, the former is no worse than the latter. After the former goes public, the attractiveness of Youku's stock will undoubtedly decrease significantly. Although Letv is listed on the A-share market, after iQIYI raises funds through its IPO, it will impact Letv's advantage in copyright procurement. And the copyright library content is the starting point of Letv's full industrial chain layout. As for Tencent, there is no plan or idea to spin off the video segment for listing, but video is an indispensable part of Tencent's full-media business layout. With the intention to increase presence, why wouldn't Tencent participate?

Having found allies, it’s time to talk about “strategy.” Zhang Chaoyang has three strategies.

Firstly, in publicity, linking Baidu with video piracy, blacklisting Baidu in the capital markets, and reinforcing the image of iQIYI as a pirating entity in the capital markets before its IPO next year. For this purpose, Zhang Chaoyang invited the Motion Picture Association of America to stand with him and specifically used English to accuse Baidu.

Secondly, by cracking down on traffic-generating products like Baidu Player, cutting off future traffic channels from Baidu Player and Baidu Video Search to iQIYI.

Once the number of pirated videos on Baidu Player decreases and the number of pirated links on Baidu Video Search reduces, the traffic for Baidu Player and Baidu Video Search will also be affected, thereby reducing the "gray traffic" delivered to iQIYI. Furthermore, once the perception of Baidu Player as a piracy tool is established, Baidu will become more cautious about transferring traffic from Baidu Player to iQIYI, so as not to affect its prospects. Moreover, since Baidu is a publicly listed company, targeting it with anti-piracy measures is more effective.

Thirdly, blocking Baidu. Preventing Baidu from indexing video content will significantly reduce Baidu's video search traffic, thereby greatly diminishing its ability to supply traffic to iQIYI.

Isn’t Baidu trying to replicate its cooperation model with Qunar and promote the "intimate search" mode to video? If other platforms don’t let you index their content, what will you have left to search? This move also aligns with Gu Yongqiang's long-standing desire to make Youku a video portal.

A few days ago, iQIYI announced that it will stop distributing copyrights in the future, indicating its preparation to strengthen its own traffic in anticipation of its IPO. The aforementioned "three moves" by Zhang Chaoyang and his allies can be summarized as: labeling iQIYI with a black tag in the capital markets, cutting off its traffic sources, and dismantling its ability to become a video portal. The latter two tactics aim to target iQIYI's traffic specifically.

But this is not enough. Striking traffic is just one aspect of countering iQIYI's IPO. By forming a copyright procurement alliance to isolate iQIYI, they can further increase iQIYI's operational costs.

It is worth noting that iQIYI has already stated it will halt copyright distribution, meaning its short-term revenue inflow capability will weaken, leading to some cash flow pressure. Forming a copyright procurement alliance could very well be Zhang Chaoyang and his allies' next move.

In fact, besides countering iQIYI, Zhang Chaoyang also has some strategies centered around himself. For instance, cooperating with 360 to leverage 360's channel advantages to help Sohu Video increase traffic. It can be seen that during the recent anti-piracy alliance gathering, a representative from 360 attended. Old Zhou has always been relentless in matters concerning Baidu. It is rumored that Zhang Chaoyang sent a message to Old Zhou regarding the formation of a video alliance. However, Old Zhou still harbors resentment over the Sogou incident and hasn't fully reconciled with Zhang Chaoyang. But businessmen prioritize interests. Collaborating with Sohu Video to aid 360's video search, Old Zhou won't refuse, and Zhang Chaoyang has the need. Video is the son, Sogou is the daughter. The daughter married into Tencent, upsetting Old Zhou, but the son can marry into Old Zhou's family, providing resources for Old Zhou to fight Baidu in the video search category.

Another interesting point is that although the anti-piracy alliance nominally criticized Kuaibo, Kuaibo CEO Wang Xin is not worried, seemingly realizing he is merely a scapegoat and won't be seriously targeted. I asked Wang Xin, who are Kuaibo's shareholders now? Wang Xin replied, Zeng Liqing, Zhou Hongyi, Softbank SAIF, still in the A-round. I asked Wang Xin, how much shares does Zhou Hongyi still own? Wang Xin said, not much. I asked Wang Xin, did Zhang Chaoyang notify you before doing this, did he discuss acquisition with you beforehand? Wang Xin said, these I cannot disclose.
