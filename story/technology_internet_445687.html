Laid-off workers PK newcomers with a monthly salary of 5K, the same in confusion_ Yan Xiaofu_ Sina Blog

by anonymous on 2011-06-14 12:36:46

Author: Yan Xiaofu

During the three-day New Year holiday, I became a complete homebody. It's really because this winter is too cold. Staying at home to watch TV and entertainment programs, that's how I welcomed the new year. If I have to say which TV series I watched the most, it would be "The Heroine". I watched it once online before, and unexpectedly, watching it again still felt so unappealing. The other program I watched a lot was "You're the One", as I had seen some episodes with quite distinctive guests online before, so I thought watching the latest episodes during the holiday would also be a good choice.

Perhaps due to the guests I encountered in the show previously, such as Fairy Sister, Male Version of Zhou Xun, or a certain lady who criticized Pan WeiBo and Hu YanBin as accomplices of Jay Chou, these guests can be said to be the most entertaining gossips from "You're the One" and also the ones who quickly captured the audience's attention. Many people accuse the production team of having the suspicion of hype, but what's wrong with a little bit of hype? Without the stir, how would it smell fragrant? As long as the hype is within the limits, the television station can do it.

But this show is not just about attracting attention through contestants like angry scolding of gender-neutral effeminate men. Many guest applicants, despite lacking in entertainment appeal, can still spark attention and topics. For example, in the previous few episodes and the recent ones, inexperienced graduates could earn a monthly salary of 5000 yuan (Liu Zhaoting in episode 2, signed with Prestige Shopping Guide); overseas returnee Lin Yuanzi, unemployed at home, starting salary of 8000 yuan; Liu Jingyan, newly entering the modeling circle, earning 5000 yuan for one show (signed with Elite Model Management). These ordinary "normal" job seekers, because of their own conditions being absolutely different from the hiring companies and salaries, still become hot topics of discussion.

In addition, the program also features many disadvantaged groups, such as 90s job-seeking girl Yaoyao, whose father suffers from kidney failure; Sister Huang Jinhong in her 40s, whose son is seriously ill; laid-off couple Zhang and her husband, homeless and wandering around. When these disadvantaged groups appear, the boss panel goes all out to help these applicants, trying their best to provide job opportunities. But even so, some viewers still suspect that the boss panel uses these disadvantaged groups to hype themselves up, saying that do they really need these people to work for their companies?

As an ordinary viewer, I don't think these companies are taking advantage of the guests. If you've watched a few episodes, you'll find that these companies have strict requirements for hiring employees. For instance, in one episode, a highly confident young man resigned from CCTV and wanted to apply for public relations planning and organization work. However, many well-known company executives not only gave him advice but also warnings, and most of them refused him. In the end, only Hangyue provided him with a position. From this guest's example, it's not that the boss panel is hyping themselves up for publicity, but rather they truly have a need.

Therefore, when looking at these new models, inexperienced university students, or disadvantaged groups, there must be their own reasons for hiring them. As for the high salaries, it's because of their excellent conditions, such as new models who really have great potential for development. If properly planned and packaged, they will surely become the backbone force in the modeling industry. The boss panel values their potential. Similarly, new university students and overseas returnees have strength and talent, and potential exists, and potential is the most valuable!

As for disadvantaged groups, I personally believe that the boss panel may have elements of sympathy. If I were on the boss panel, I would certainly try my best to help them. Most of the bosses are not second-generation rich kids but have worked hard to succeed. When they look back and face disadvantaged groups, it's very normal to have compassion in their hearts. Precisely because of this point, it's difficult to say that they use the disadvantaged to highlight their abilities. After all, solving a problem for someone in need gives them another chance for happiness and reduces social pressure. This is the social responsibility a successful enterprise should have after achieving success.

So, you can say that you don't like "You're the One", that it plays tricks with reality shows and entertains the masses as much as possible. But it cannot be denied that the program encompasses all kinds of highlights, both entertaining and profound, both funny and touching, a bit of everything. A comprehensive program, whether it's the sharp exchanges between the boss panel or mutual understanding, these are all real. As for the doubts, a little bit doesn't hurt, at least it confirms from one side that the program is still quite popular. It's also hard to say that a program can be both visually appealing and have good reviews.
