The world financial system dominated by the World Bank and the IMF, representing Europe and America, has been in operation for more than half a century. It seems to have reached the brink of adjustment. Yesterday, Vice Premier Wang Qishan met with World Bank President Robert Zoellick at Zhongnanhai. Since Zoellick took office as president of the World Bank in June 2007, he has visited China every year. However, this visit might be a bit difficult because at the G20 summit currently being held in Mexico, the "BRICS Five" (Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa) unanimously requested that the selection of the World Bank president should be open to the whole world, not just Americans, and the selection should be based on competence rather than nationality.

In the famous Bretton Woods Conference of 1945, two major international financial institutions were established: the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank. According to the agreement between the major European and American countries at the time, the president of the World Bank would be an American, while the managing director of the IMF would be a European. This rule has been followed for 67 years. In fact, after former IMF Managing Director Dominique Strauss-Kahn resigned in haste last year due to allegations of sexual assault, the BRICS Five had once proposed the requirement for an open selection process, but it ended without any result. So, can the BRICS Five succeed this time? From the current situation, the possibility is very small. There are three reasons:

Firstly, according to the rules of the World Bank, any major decision must be made with over 85% voting rights. Currently, the United States holds 15.85% of the voting rights, Japan 6.84%, Germany 4.00%, and both the UK and France hold 3.75%. The U.S. alone is enough to veto any proposal. If we also consider the voting rights held by various major European interest groups, it will be extremely difficult to shake the current mechanism for appointing the president.

Secondly, the BRICS Five themselves find it hard to come up with a candidate that everyone can agree on within a short period. According to the regulations, the nomination for the World Bank president must be submitted before March 23rd. Each of the five countries has its own national conditions and interests, so it is difficult to reach a consensus on the candidate in such a short time.

Thirdly, the nomination and appointment of the World Bank president is a matter that affects everything else. The Euro-American countries will not easily give up this power. Looking back at the 67-year development history of the World Bank and the IMF, it is clear that they basically represent the interests of the developed countries in Europe and America: the low-interest loans and various aids provided by the World Bank to developing countries over nearly 50 years often come with additional conditions; and the performance of the IMF during the Asian financial crisis, the U.S. subprime mortgage crisis, and the European debt crisis fully proves this point.

It is difficult to establish a new order, but breaking an existing order and set of rules is often even harder. The world financial system dominated by the World Bank and the IMF, representing Europe and America, has been running like this for more than half a century. But in recent years, due to the consumption of much strength by the U.S. subprime mortgage crisis and the European debt crisis among the developed countries in Europe and America, this order seems to have reached the edge of readjustment. Taking China as an example, in recent years, Lin Yifu's election as Senior Vice President of the World Bank and Zhu Min's election as Deputy Managing Director of the IMF all represent the results of China's efforts to change the old order.

However, despite the difficulties faced by the Euro-American countries, when facing any "challenge" from the BRICS Five and the emerging economies they represent, they still will not easily yield. Because they know that a concession in this negotiation may mean an even greater compromise in the next negotiation. The positions of the World Bank president and IMF managing director are merely one stage and stake in the competition between the developed economies in Europe and America and the emerging economies.
