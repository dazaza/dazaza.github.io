Let's talk about the stir-fried gourd with peppers and tomato scrambled eggs I made tonight!

For the stir-fried gourd with peppers, first wash the gourd and peppers, then cut the gourd first. Why cut the gourd first? If you cut the peppers first, the spiciness will affect the gourd, so I suggest cutting the gourd first. The order of cutting vegetables is quite important and must be taken seriously. If you want to perfect a dish and make it taste good, you must consider each step. I have a habit of peeling all the vegetables after buying them, washing them separately, then cutting them separately. After cutting, I use different plates to hold them, and only after preparation do I start cooking. I don't wash, cut, and cook simultaneously. I prefer to prepare all the vegetables before starting to cook. Enough chatter, let's talk about cutting the peppers. When cutting peppers, you should hammer them lightly with a knife before cutting. Breaking the peppers allows you to easily slice them into thin strips. Why hammer first? If you don't, it's hard to cut them into fine strips. Why cut them into strips? Cutting into strips improves the texture, making them softer in the mouth, and it complements the gourd well because gourd is a tender vegetable. If you cut the peppers into large chunks, it won't match well with the gourd, and naturally, the dish won't taste good. Haha, don't blindly imitate. Depending on the dish, peppers should be cut differently. Here, I suggest cutting them into thin strips. If you prefer slices or chunks, I won't object. Any choice is correct, just leading to different results. So I feel that cooking is like being a person, like life itself. Then cut the tomatoes. I bought two medium-sized tomatoes. Why medium-sized? Large tomatoes aren't as tasty, probably lacking juice, and small tomatoes might not be fully ripe. Medium-sized tomatoes are just right. When cutting tomatoes, usually cut them in half first, then place the elliptical side on the board. If you place the flat side on the board, if the knife isn't sharp enough, you'll waste a lot of tomato juice, so to reduce waste, follow my method—it's absolutely correct. After cutting the tomatoes, I began cutting the meat. By the way, I cut the green onions first. I love green onions, and they are essential for soup. Why cut the meat last? Because meat is different from vegetables—it sticks to the board, so cutting it last prevents transferring its fishy smell to other vegetables. If you're cooking for the first time, after cutting the meat, you need to scrape the board with a knife to clean off the residue. Regular washing or using a scrubber won't clean it thoroughly. Then, it's time to prepare for cooking.

I wait until the pan is hot, pour some oil—not too much, not too little. Too much makes it greasy, too little lacks flavor. After pouring the oil, put the pork in the pan to fry—not too long, not too short. Too long makes the meat tough and unpalatable, too short makes it undercooked and not tasty. So it should be just right. When the meat is almost cooked, add a little salt—not too much. Why add salt now? Adding salt now helps the meat absorb it. If added later, the salt won't penetrate the meat, and the finished dish will lack flavor. After adding the salt, pour in a little soy sauce. I like soy sauce, but unfortunately, I didn't have any tonight. This way, the cooked meat has both color and flavor. After the meat is cooked, scoop it out into a plate, then sprinkle some green onions on top. Why add green onions now? If added while frying, it might cause heatiness after eating. Adding it now gives the aroma of green onions without causing heatiness. Then stir-fry the peppers. The pan must be very hot. A not-so-hot pan won't bring out the true flavor of the peppers. Undercooked peppers are hard and unpleasant. The peppers must be cooked thoroughly. So in this step, the peppers should be stir-fried briefly over high heat to remove the surface flavor and spiciness. Then scoop them out. Remember to wash the pan at this point. If not washed, the dish won't taste good. Then heat the pan again over high heat, pour in some oil, and when the oil is hot, add the gourd. Stir-fry a few times, then cover with a lid and simmer for a while. Once the gourd is cooked, add the meat, stir for a bit, then add the peppers. Keep stirring and flipping while tasting and adjusting the salt. Once the salt is sufficient, add a little monosodium glutamate. It's almost ready to serve. If you're worried about heatiness, you can add a little water to the pot and cook it together with the vegetables. If you don't add water, just let the three ingredients completely blend together until cooked, then it's ready to serve.

Then make tomato egg soup, the simplest dish. However, to make it tasty, you must put your heart into it. Cooking reflects a person's character, testing their patience, attention to detail, and capability. I usually pour some oil in the pan first, and when the oil is very hot, I add the tomatoes to stir-fry. Why do this? Most people directly pour water in, wait for it to boil, then add the tomatoes and eggs. Tomato egg soup made this way doesn't taste good. First, stir-frying the tomatoes in oil brings out their juice. Only after the juice is released does the soup have a tomato flavor. If you add water first and then the tomatoes, the soup won't taste good. So making a simple tomato egg soup isn't that simple. You must start by buying suitable tomatoes for soup and try not to lose the tomato juice while cutting. When making the soup, you must first fry the tomatoes in oil to release their juice, then add water. Then beat the eggs and stir. When the water boils, pour the eggs along the edge of the pot. While stirring the eggs, you must do it evenly and pour carefully, letting the eggs flow into the pot like a stream around the edges. This way, the eggs won't clump together but will blend uniformly with the tomato juice. Then add salt immediately. After adjusting the salt, add more monosodium glutamate. Once the water boils, you can serve the soup in a large bowl, then sprinkle the green onions on top. Never add the green onions directly in the pot, as their flavor will quickly disappear. Once the soup is served in the bowl, add the green onions so they float on the surface and are slowly absorbed by the soup, releasing their flavor gradually. That's why the tomato egg soup tastes best, and why I enjoy eating food I cook, and why my stomach gets so full when I eat it.
