One year, a group of talented young people graduated from Harvard University in the United States. Their intelligence, education level, and environmental conditions were almost the same. Before leaving the school, Harvard conducted a survey on their career planning. The results were as follows:

27% of them didn't know what kind of work they wanted to do.

60% of them had some idea about what kind of work they wanted to do, but it was relatively vague;

10% of them clearly knew what kind of work they wanted to do in the near future;

3% of them clearly knew what job they most wanted to do for the rest of their lives.

25 years later

25 years later, Harvard conducted another follow-up study on these students. The results were as follows:

These 3%, over the course of 25 years, have been striving tirelessly in one direction. Almost all of them have become successful individuals in various sectors of society, including industry leaders and social elites;

These 10%, their short-term goals were constantly being achieved, becoming professionals in various fields, most living in the upper-middle class of society;

These 60%, they worked and lived steadily, but none of them achieved anything extraordinary. Almost all of them lived in the middle-lower class of society;

The remaining 27%, their lives lacked purpose, they lived unhappily, and often complained about others, society, and this "world that wouldn't give them opportunities."

Successful people are often those with long-term goals

Dr. Edward Banfield of Harvard University's research on the driving force behind American social progress found that successful people are usually those with long-term time perspectives. When making daily, weekly, or monthly activity plans, they consider long-term concepts. They plan five, ten, even twenty years ahead. Their allocation of resources or decision-making is based on where they expect to be in a few years. This research result has important implications for newly employed professionals just entering society.

Career planning involves analyzing and evaluating subjective and objective factors affecting personal career choices, determining personal struggle goals and career goals, and reasonably planning one's career life.

Career planning requires you to base your decisions on personal factors such as "career interest, personality traits, ability orientation, and professional knowledge skills," while also considering various external factors. After comprehensive consideration, position yourself where you can best leverage your strengths to maximize self-fulfillment. A person whose career goal aligns with their life goal is happy, and career planning essentially seeks the optimal career path.

Case:

At a large job fair in Shenyang, Xiao He, a graduate of a prestigious university, applied for a mechanical engineer position at a Zhejiang-based automobile company. He studied mechanical engineering and excelled in all his courses during college. In the five or six years after graduation, he worked in sales and quality management roles for pharmaceuticals, air conditioners, motorcycles, and other products, changing jobs six or seven times, but without any experience in the mechanical field. After reviewing his situation, the recruiter believed that if he had consistently worked in the mechanical field after graduation, he would have been an ideal candidate for the company. However, due to his lack of relevant experience, the company could not hire him.

Xiao He's example illustrates the harm caused by many college graduates blindly choosing jobs. Due to the lack of long-term planning, many young graduates frequently change jobs without establishing a clear career direction by their thirties. In this situation, continuing down the same path offers little future potential, and repositioning requires significant effort, leading to an awkward situation.

Long-term changes accumulate life force, which requires a long-term perspective. Taking sales as an example, the essence of a sales career generally means you must be willing to invest three to five years or more to become a true sales expert. This long-term commitment will completely change your attitude towards training, daily work, customers, yourself, the community, and other aspects of your job. Hironaka Ichiro, the legendary insurance salesperson in Japan, didn't sell a single policy for the first seven months of his career and had to sleep on park benches due to hunger.

Outstanding individuals typically have long-term plans for themselves and their lives. Having a long-term perspective on career planning is a crucial condition for achieving greatness in our future careers. Success comes at a price; you must work hard for several years before reaching your maximum earning potential and enjoying the desired lifestyle. You must make sacrifices and continuously strive for several years before achieving truly valuable goals. Regardless of the industry you want to excel in, you should invest at least five years in preparation. Regardless of the field you're in, if you want to stand out, you need to be fully committed for at least three to five years mentally. It takes a long time to develop sufficient professional skills to succeed in competitive markets. Otherwise, if we frequently change cities, industries, and specific jobs, the accumulated knowledge, skills, work experience, and networking relationships we built up may no longer be useful, ultimately leading us into a predicament similar to Xiao He's—many years after graduation, still not finding a suitable career or establishing a unique competitive advantage, resulting in a difficult situation where continuing in the current job offers little future potential, and repositioning requires significant effort.
