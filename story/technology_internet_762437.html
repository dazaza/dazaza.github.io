Dianping: A Bone the Giants Can't Chew, Living Outside the Comfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:28:17

CEO of Dianping, Zhang Tao

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping, has postponed the company's IPO once again.

In 2011, when Dianping received a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, its valuation had already reached $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an appropriate time to go public within the following one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it conducted another $60 million Series D funding round.

Given Dianping's scale and development momentum, going public wouldn't be difficult at all. The issue is that Zhang Tao feels it would be too costly to list now. He breaks down the big internet (PC + mobile) into four elements—people, information, goods, and services—and believes each connection point offers an opportunity for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is where the fourth giant could sit. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating resources for this very reason, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, which ties "location" and "service" together through smartphones. Following Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping to occupy the fourth giant's position.

But the outside world doesn't see it this way. In recent months, with frequent acquisitions and strategic layouts by BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a speculative acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition candidate; regardless of which letter in BAT it aligns with, it would help them achieve greater success early on. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it’s still premature to predict its rise to giant status. Moreover, whether it’s Tencent WeChat's official accounts platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in Gaode Maps and Meituan, they are all targeting Dianping's territory—local life services centered around dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two points, Dianping's IPO would amount to a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs some time to prove that what Dianping is doing now is something BAT cannot accomplish. Second, Dianping is about to enter the final battle of group buying, and if it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase exponentially.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Zhang Tao remains indifferent to speculation and rumors, stating clearly: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great convergence, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type possesses platform attributes but is better suited to develop within the ecosystem of a giant. For instance, maps and email products can easily attract large numbers of users, but finding independent business models is challenging. Some products, like browsers, can generate revenue, but the money earned through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money maintaining an email product because it helps with branding and user retention, but I've never heard of any standalone email company succeeding." The second type involves industry consolidation, such as mergers in the video industry to expand market share. The third type occurs when a company faces financial difficulties and sells itself to survive.

Now, Meituan has already been brought under Alibaba's wing. In theory, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated by a rival gaining capital and expanding rapidly. However, Zhang Tao argues, "Giants can offer us limited help. We do need users, but execution is even more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities and risk marginalizing us within a larger company." Furthermore, he aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would equate to "taking sides," making Dianping an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically speaking, acquiring Dianping becomes a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why would we take their money? We're not short of funds. What we need are resources. If a giant is willing to invest a small stake and provide us with many resources, I might consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only hold a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for pure internet product companies are decreasing, while new opportunities arise from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that life services primarily revolve around dining, drinking, and entertainment. Other niche markets, such as ride-hailing, can form decent business models, but it's unlikely any of them will grow into giants.

For dining, drinking, and entertainment, user demands are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand recognition, later entrants find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship race, and Dianping plans to face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as there are two strong competitors, the industry's gross margin will be suppressed, preventing stable profitability.

A visible future lies in reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but this must be built on the foundation of Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, building a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Both users and merchants have given positive feedback so far, but if we were to focus solely on reservations, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services also present some heavier opportunities. For example, food delivery fulfills a rigid demand from users and allows merchants to increase sales. In the past few years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet" have thrived. However, the core of food delivery is delivery—getting meals delivered within a short time frame while solving issues like soup spills. Its logistics requirements are even higher than those of fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao says, "Food delivery has potential if executed well, but it's harder dirty work, and what Dianping is doing now is already dirty and exhausting enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant data and user reviews, which Zhang Tao refers to as "dirty work." This primitive growth model may have once limited its speed, but the pain of the past has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, aside from Meituan, Dianping has no real rivals in the life services sector.

Nevertheless, he must remain vigilant against BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. With over 100 million users, Baidu Maps enables users to search for nearby dining and other life services. Tencent, considered to have secured its place in mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest physical goods trading platform, and expanding into services is a natural progression. Moreover, it has incorporated Meituan and Gaode Maps into its strategic map.

"Neither Baidu Maps nor Tencent WeChat shows explosive growth in reality or data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to eat, drink, or play.

Moreover, in the vast field of life services, competition among giants will inevitably overlap, making complete monopolization impossible. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao believes that in this way, Dianping is safer. "In niche areas like car rentals and business travel, if giants redirect their massive user base, they could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which invested in Gaode Maps, invading Dianping's territory isn't that simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized no matter how peculiar. A diner's experience depends on various factors like the day's environment, waitstaff, and chefs. Managing merchants can't rely solely on systems and tools like Alibaba uses; it requires establishing a 'ground force' managed by humans.

"All three giants want to take this path, and there's room for imagination, but it's not a highway—it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing life services necessitates deploying ground teams across various levels, and BAT lacks the genes and capability to manage offline teams. Second, they lack the intention. Life services require focused effort; it's not something a small team can accomplish, and profits come slowly. "A company finds it hard to excel at two critical tasks simultaneously, let alone consolidating its advantage in its original domain."

Weapon Two: Small Ships Turn Easily

When Dianping secured $100 million in financing in 2011, Zhang Tao told the media that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change quickly, and before Dianping went public, his analogous targets fell from grace.

He wasn't overly anxious about it. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams, and now they're trying to be America's Dianping."

Today's path is one Zhang Tao has gradually explored step by step. When he felt that selling keyword ads and discount coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying lit up his eyes. Initially, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform, similar to the American model of one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized group buying possessed transactional platform characteristics. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a life service transaction platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used to transition Dianping from an information platform to a transaction platform.

Without this "genetic modification," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth giant's seat today. But genetic modification sounds simple, yet it's equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process extremely painful.

First, compared to selling advertisements separately, the gross margin of a transaction platform decreases, requiring stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, a transaction platform demands higher cross-department communication capabilities.

In the past, to maintain Dianping's fairness and independence as an information platform, the content and sales teams were completely separated. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments—no matter how important a client, the information on the platform couldn't be modified for sales purposes. This once led to some mutual hostility between the content and sales teams. Now, as a transaction platform, editors and sales teams need deep communication to craft good marketing plans. Planning aside, editor cooperation is essential. He said, "Maintaining information quality is still important, but there's no need to reinforce hostility. The firewall needs to be dismantled."

Though editors may accept this logically, individuals' behaviors and values have inertia, and collectively, the entire company's inertia is even greater. To address this, Zhang Tao held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. Months later, an editorial manager casually mentioned to a sales manager, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needed genetic modification. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team dual roles. First, for merchants, salespeople should transform into marketing consultants—not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants act as procurement agents. Dianping is akin to a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety must also be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Of course, dual roles inevitably lead to a series of complex issues. Frontline personnel find this quite challenging, as they must balance the scale themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual role" is very similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. By decentralizing decision-making to frontline staff, the company gains faster response speeds but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffered more after the genetic modification.

Each year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, then executives further decompose them layer by layer. However, decomposition isn't simple arithmetic. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping, responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds unresolved merchant demands due to sales issues affecting performance. Therefore, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists who gather information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change doesn't mean liking change. No one likes change; everyone feels uncomfortable when encountering it. All employees, including me, must go through non-comfort zones."

Numbers reflect the weight of Dianping's execution team. Currently, among the over 3,000 employees, more than 2,000 work offline. The ground force communicates directly with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group-buying deals, and comprehensive marketing packages.

Dianping has a unique management evaluation system for this core team resisting BAT's influence.

They aren't called "salespeople"; their business cards have always labeled them as "marketing planning consultants" since day one. Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the phase of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in terms of internet understanding. Marketing planning consultants must inform merchants about the needs of online users and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even a high-end restaurant shouldn't be advised to do abalone or shark fin group-buying deals; perhaps other dishes instead, as online and offline users have different demands."

An excellent marketing consultant must be a versatile player. They must explain to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the fundamental differences between discounts and group-buying deals and determine which discount suits their positioning better. If facing a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant should know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise during certain seasons, and which decrease, proposing a group-buying menu that pleases both merchants and users.

This imposes high demands on marketing consultants, who must not only be experts in internet marketing but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, not every consultant can be a double expert, so we set star ratings for consultants." Like hotels, marketing consultants range from one-star to five-star, with the best being Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping established a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and related cases that both new and old employees can browse. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions within the knowledge base, where thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer.

Most knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experiences. Dianping set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, directly linking the results to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to obtain than internet knowledge. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after decades of struggle in the industry. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that the hairdressing industry should avoid offering shampoo-cut-blow dry package deals, as men lack motivation to buy, and women prefer blow-drying without washing and cutting. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never have thought of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are divided not only by city but also by industries such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, ensuring merchants don't resist when selling products," he said.

Each marketing team holds its own case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a specific type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, what solutions the company's products offer, and what effects result. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Entrepreneurial Winner." Within the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If they handle hot pot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Similar to graduation, employees present their plans on-site, and industry supervisors evaluate and reward the winners.

Strategically, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, their objectives are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with basic industry knowledge, and their statements sound alike. "The era when storytelling and concept creation alone could lead to success has passed. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao remarked.
