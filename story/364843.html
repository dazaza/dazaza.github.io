China's Great Nest: On East Asian authoritarianism, patriarchy, and other internal economic developments

by ideas on 2010-09-17 10:19:32

The continuous growth of trade surpluses, increased friction, and the pressure of currency appreciation are growing; domestically, asset market prices such as real estate are increasingly out of control, macro-control is ineffective, domestic demand is unable to grow effectively and replace external demand as the main driver of economic growth. This cluster of phenomena is gradually spreading in emerging market economies in East Asia. The International Monetary Fund report predicts that South Korea's economic growth rate this year could rise to 6.1%, with per capita GDP hopefully returning above $20,000. However, at the same time, South Korean President Lee Myung-bak expressed earlier: "The fruits of South Korea's economic growth have not yet benefited all the people, for which I feel deeply burdened."

In fact, not only South Korea, but many other emerging market economies in Asia are also facing similar issues. For instance, Singapore has experienced rapid economic growth since the beginning of this year, with continuously increasing trade surpluses and rising pressure for currency appreciation. However, real estate prices, crucial to national livelihoods, have repeatedly gone out of control, leading to recent policies reducing mortgage loan ratios. Perhaps from the current perspective, this does not yet qualify as a "group illness" for East Asia's export-oriented economy. Even during economic crises, this may help gather momentum for economic recovery. For example, South Korea recently announced a series of measures to boost the housing market to promote a swift economic recovery. However, from a long-term view, as one of the new poles of world economic growth, the collective emergence of similar economic symptoms in East Asia perhaps warrants our early attention and corresponding strategies.

Whether it’s South Korea, Singapore, Thailand, or Indonesia, all have achieved extraordinary development by following Japan's past model of an export-oriented economy for catch-up. After World War II, Japan on one hand accepted Western aid to restore its post-war economy; on the other hand, it accelerated its own technological catch-up, striving to occupy global markets through technological progress and product innovation. It was through these two points that Japan was able to quickly rise after the war. Later, South Korea, Singapore, and other Asian Tigers emulated Japan, achieving rapid economic takeoff and entering the ranks of middle-developed countries, thereby driving more countries in East Asia to join in. These nations and regions utilized their local cheap labor and resources to develop outward-oriented economies, gradually escaping poverty while hoping to further achieve the socio-economic development accomplishments of the past Asian Tigers.

However, reality is harsh. First, the external economic and political environment faced by emerging market economies in East Asia has significantly changed compared to what Japan faced in the past. Traditional developed countries in Europe and America have begun to examine the challenge posed by East Asian emerging market economies to their global economic dominance and have formulated many response measures against these emerging economies. The overall strategic thinking of developed countries towards East Asian emerging market economies can be summarized as "restricted development": sharing their socioeconomic development achievements within a certain scope, including cheap goods and capital reinvestment in Europe and America, while simultaneously using means such as currency appreciation and restrictions on technology exports to limit their development within controllable ranges. This ensures the relative developmental advantages and economic-political appeal of Europe and America. Didn't Obama propose the "export revitalization" plan to compete with East Asian countries?

Secondly, the internal technological progress and endogenous sustainable economic growth driven by consumption in East Asia's emerging market economies are far inferior to those of Japan in the past. As the saying goes, "internal causes are the fundamental driving force of things," which is the most crucial. In fact, Japan had already secured a place among developed countries in terms of technology, corporate strength, and comprehensive national power before World War II. The reason why it was able to quickly recover after the war and become the second-largest economy was fundamentally due to its industrial economic foundation. In contrast, the technological and industrial foundations of South Korea and other East Asian emerging market economies are much weaker. A problem arising from this is: once the initial phase of low-end labor-intensive industries reaches a bottleneck requiring upgrading, the momentum for sustained economic growth will be limited. Perhaps it is precisely for this reason that during periods of economic structural adjustment, East Asian emerging market economies generally choose to support the economy with real estate economics, a model that emerged in Japan only after it became a developed country.

For East Asia's authoritarianism, paternalistic internal economic development models, real estate economics can effectively stimulate domestic demand while also providing a buffer period for the real economy to further improve its technological level and adjust its economic structure. From this perspective, this policy has its rationality. However, this economic policy also carries very significant potential negative impacts. Once the rapid development of the real estate market combines with the asset bubbles brought about by the economic growth and currency appreciation of emerging market countries, even the authoritarian government management model of emerging markets will find it difficult to implement effective control. If real estate prices soar rapidly beyond the regulators' expectations, it will trigger a series of social problems, including excessive price increases with asset bubbles, high real estate prices causing anxiety among young middle-class individuals, and excessive speculation in capital goods markets leading to systemic financial risks.

Moreover, the buffer period provided by real estate economics for industrial structure upgrades may not necessarily work. In many cases, settling for short-term gains instead boosts the laziness of enterprises and individuals without stimulating their innovative potential. Once real estate economics falls into a bubble trap without achieving technological progress, the economic crisis of East Asia's emerging market economies will loom. At this point, if Europe and America intensify trade shocks on emerging market economies to shift the subprime crisis, emerging market economies might find themselves in a very disadvantageous position.
