Da Zhangmen reconciles with Alibaba Cloud: the cloud ecosystem is about to emerge

by geekzhang on 2013-05-10 18:02:20

Abstract: With the rapid development of cloud computing, more and more technical/non-technical issues have been exposed. Although the ones who suffer the most are undoubtedly cloud service users, facing widespread criticism, service providers often find themselves in a difficult position to explain. AWS is far away, GCE is outside the firewall, and now let's take a look at domestic cloud events between "Big Master" vs. Alibaba Cloud.

In the past two weeks, the Chinese cloud computing circle has been focused on one event: due to technical and service issues, on April 27th, Ye Kai, CEO of PlayCrab Technology, the developer of "Big Master," lashed out at Alibaba Cloud on Weibo. Many others joined in the complaints, with many spectators as well. Detailed diary-like records from Cnblogs were quickly unearthed, and even several Alibaba Cloud migration plans emerged. In response, Alibaba Cloud promptly replied on Weibo to Ye Kai, hoping for private communication, but after receiving no effective response, they remained silent. It wasn't until Alibaba Cloud President Wang Jian came to Beijing and met with Ye Kai on May 6th, reaching a reconciliation and furthering deep cooperation. This event may seem small, but it holds significant meaning at a critical juncture in the rapid growth of China's cloud computing. Although such incidents are common in the U.S. cloud computing market, this time, it happened domestically, right within our circle. For this reason, CSDN's Cloud Computing Channel specially interviewed Ye Kai, Cnblogs CEO Du Yong, Ucloud CEO Ji Xinhua, and conducted a face-to-face interview with Wang Jian on May 7th. Using this event as a cause, we gathered typical cloud computing users and cloud service providers to discuss the urgency and possibility of building a cloud ecosystem.

Background

Ye Kai: Criticizing Failures and Services

Following the wave of cloud computing applications in the U.S., start-ups' understanding and usage of cloud platforms have reached a new level, exposing more problems. For example, James Somers of RapGenius criticized Heroku for privately modifying routes, leading to collective litigation lawyers getting involved; AWS also mimicked products from its technology partners like Newvem (cloud optimization enterprise), Zencoder (video code converter), Rightscale (providing cross-platform cloud monitoring, alerting, and management services for AWS, Rackspace, HPCloud), Cloudyn, Cloudability, etc. In contrast, the relatively calm domestic cloud computing scene is in a rapid development phase. Although there have been sporadic pieces of information, none have been representative enough. Until Alibaba Cloud's recent "My Story with Alibaba Cloud" theme activity on Weibo brought out Ye Kai, revealing the vortex that started to surface.

Event Replay: On April 27th at 11:04 AM, @Ye Kai Kevin: We used over 20 machines on Alibaba Cloud. Within half a year, there was one instance where all machines lost power, two instances where multiple hard drives suddenly became read-only, three instances where disk IO suddenly filled up (the explanation given was that other virtual machines on the same physical machine competed for resources with us), one instance where customer service restarted without notification. There were over ten instances where operations and maintenance did not respond, and phone calls were never answered within five minutes. We are preparing to gradually migrate all machines out.

Ye Kai's anger was evident. Subsequently, during the event's escalation, several other industry users applying cloud platforms raised similar issues: for example, @Zheng Ruisheng proposed a compensation plan due to network instability but it went unresolved; @Zheng Dunhuang felt the customer service response was indeed terrible; @Zha Zha Bird experienced an RDS database crash; @Jumping Qu couldn’t access the internal SLB network, and then there was Cnblogs. All problems concentrated on technology and service aspects. This was confirmed during the journalist's interview with Ye Kai:

"They just asked us to modify our architecture to adapt to their deficiencies, but our services run fine on other clouds and IDCs. Of course, we’ve been optimizing our own architecture. But mainly, our systems run smoothly on other cloud platforms and IDC physical machines. They never seriously address their own flaws, don't talk about solving their own problems, just shift responsibility. Both product and service quality lag behind, and their attitude is bad, which is quite fatal. And the frequency of failures is too high. After acquiring Wanwang, people are in disarray. Those who came over don’t know who they are or what they’re responsible for. It feels like they don’t value the development of this business."

If Ye Kai’s mentioned issues are concentrated bursts, then Cnblogs’ diary records provide a more continuous and realistic account.

Escalation

Cnblogs: Issues and Solutions Recorded in Diaries

Cnblogs can represent a potential category of users in cloud computing applications— websites. Since their migration to the Alibaba Cloud platform, every problem encountered by their CEO Du Yong has been meticulously recorded in blog diaries. Experiences such as recording IP addresses through Request.UserHostAddress, resolving the strange issue of images.cnblogs.com slow response speed, database write timeout issues and solutions, why memcached gets congested, etc., offer valuable references for other cloud users.

However, as Cnblogs readers pointed out, since last year's preparation to migrate to Alibaba Cloud, the number of issues that have arisen is indeed substantial. In communication with Du Yong, they believe that technology is the fundamental constraint, while service deserves affirmation.

CSDN: Seeing your series of articles, I feel deeply moved. What do you think is the most painful problem?

Du Yong: The instability of cloud server disk I/O. Initially, we used cloud servers to run databases, which worked well when the disk I/O was stable; later, disk I/O issues caused several database server failures. Our search product still suffers from cloud server disk I/O problems.

CSDN: What causes these frequent problems?

Du Yong: Insufficient consideration of actual user application scenarios, such as lack of effective isolation between resources occupied by users; insufficient emphasis on system stability.

CSDN: Have you encountered poor service quality (operations not responding, unreachable phone numbers)? How do you view Alibaba Cloud's service?

Du Yong: We haven't encountered any non-responsive situations. Currently, the most commendable aspect is Alibaba Cloud's service. Through their service, we feel that they are making efforts to solve problems, not just going through the motions.

CSDN: From hosting to using a cloud platform, what is Cnblogs' most prominent feeling?

Du Yong: Having the freedom to expand computational capabilities at any time, add servers when needed, reduce them when unnecessary, this feeling is wonderful.

CSDN: If you had the opportunity to communicate face-to-face with Wang Jian of Alibaba Cloud, what would you ask? What suggestions do you have for Alibaba Cloud?

Du Yong: I would ask: What level does Alibaba Cloud's virtualization technology stand at in the industry?

Suggestions for Alibaba Cloud: Stability comes before everything else because tens of thousands of websites serve hundreds of millions of users on the cloud; improve products, the user experience on the Alibaba Cloud website is really bad.

CSDN: AWS, Heroku also frequently encounter problems, what are the differences between domestic and international markets? How do you view the entire cloud ecosystem? What suggestions do you have for constructing a domestic cloud ecosystem?

Du Yong: Whether the problem affects users domestically or internationally, there is no difference—it only shows the technology isn't mature enough. Why can we use electricity normally even if the power plant malfunctions? My understanding of the cloud ecosystem isn't deep, but I think cloud service providers are key roles. As long as cloud service providers think first thing every day about ensuring no issues like the power grid or water companies, that will be the moment when cloud computing begins to become popular.

CSDN: Some netizens suggest migrating from Alibaba Cloud? Do you have plans to migrate?

Du Yong: We will only evaluate based on our actual usage experience. We haven't migrated from Alibaba Cloud because we believe Alibaba Cloud can solve the problems we've encountered. We don't want to migrate, I think any cloud platform user's migration isn't planned but forced.

This clearly aligns with the analysis of technical and operations aspects by bystander netizens:

@Architect Jack: Public clouds require knowledge beyond distributed file systems, including network deployment planning capabilities. Two instances of multiple hard drives suddenly becoming read-only indicate that Alibaba Cloud's distributed file system design for handling disk failures needs refinement. Three instances of sudden full disk I/O indicate a lack of sufficient I/O QoS mechanism design in its file system. These all indicate that Alibaba Cloud needs to improve its reliability design/performance design and testing capabilities in the storage subsystem.

@Operations Old Zhou: No matter how miraculous cloud servers claim to be, resource contention is unavoidable. The foundation is virtualization, the key lies in whose monitoring mechanisms, dynamic migration, expansion, and automation capabilities are better, as well as whether the self-management platform is complete. There's still a long way to go, initially saving funds and manpower, but the problems won't be fewer than self-hosted hardware.

The most practical sentence reveals the sentiment of cloud platform users: migration itself is difficult.

Advice

Migration: Continue moving to the cloud or return to IDC hosting?

With the popularization of cloud computing services, such questions are increasingly common. However, whether abroad or domestically, the solution seems to be only one: migration. For instance, some suggested Ye Kai migrate to UCloud, Hangzhou Webank's Linkcloud, Pinggao, hosted data centers, qcloud, and UpYun (static file storage and CDN acceleration), while others recommended returning to traditional IDC hosting services.

But the latter voice is much smaller. Although currently, quite a few enterprises adopt a hybrid cloud approach, once they taste the benefits of quick deployment and flexible resource configuration offered by cloud computing, very few are willing to revert.

As for the former, as the most vocal migration target, the reporter interviewed Ucloud CEO Ji Xinhua. He believed:

This is an inevitable path for the cloud computing industry. Users care most about the stability of products and the professionalism of services, so cloud computing needs to focus on enhancing these aspects and requires more contributions from core R&D and operations experts. Our cloud computing industry is currently in its infancy, and we need everyone's efforts to make the industry grow. Cloud computing itself must also pay more attention to products and technical support to ensure business stability. We suggest CSDN can organize some experts to introduce how to better use cloud computing. UCloud will provide more stable products and services to support China's entrepreneurial teams.

In fact, the stability and service capability of cloud platforms have always been controversial, both in the U.S. and China.

However, similarly, some typical cloud computing users have developed and open-sourced highly helpful technologies and applications while using cloud computing services. The kind of experts Ji Xinhua referred to include those like Netflix and Instagram on AWS, not only cloud users but also crucial parts of the ecosystem, sometimes directly filling in for AWS's service shortcomings. For example, Netflix's open-source tools like JanitorMonkey (cloud management tool) and Eureka (load balancing service) have helped several companies better utilize AWS.

Resolution

Wang Jian: I will become the biggest customer manager, urging the improvement of service quality

The circle is watching Alibaba Cloud's attitude and resolution methods: quietly resolving privately and meeting relevant demands? Ignoring it and waiting for time to dilute the comments? Or something else. On May 7th, the reporter exclusively interviewed Wang Jian of Alibaba Cloud immediately after his speech at GMIC 2013. Reasonably, after hours of conversation (on May 6th), both parties reconciled, and Alibaba Cloud would provide more services to PlayCrab Technology. Surprisingly, Alibaba Cloud would support "Big Master" in developing technology and services targeted at the gaming industry (like Netflix, Instagram, etc.), with Wang Jian becoming Ye Kai's customer manager, urging the team to improve service quality.

CSDN: I believe you already know about Ye Kai's incident with PlayCrab? Is it resolved?

Wang Jian: Yesterday (May 6th), I sat down with Ye Kai and their CTO and chatted from afternoon till evening, for four or five hours. I could clearly feel that this is not an issue of whether we can provide service or not. Providing good service is our duty, and we need to build an ecosystem, introducing partners to provide services tailored to different industry users. I told Ye Kai, first, to restore his system on our platform, the cost will be on us first, and once it runs stably, he can continue to use it. Second, together we will deepen and optimize services for the gaming industry, and we are willing to invest money in them. Third, continue deep cooperation for mobile game service optimization. He was very willing. In the future, PlayCrab will become like our partner Yunluo, helping customers use Alibaba Cloud services.

CSDN: The main issues are concentrated in two areas: technology and service. Especially regarding service, why did these issues occur?

Wang Jian: Ye Kai told me that half a month ago, feedback was provided on the issues, but someone confidently said they would resolve it, and then nothing happened, and there was no follow-up from the customer manager, nor were the promised phone calls made. Later, someone came to discuss compensation issues, but it ended without resolution. He was most upset because it wasn't a matter of who was right or wrong, but rather that things weren't handled properly. When I heard this, I almost cried. Because if I put myself in his shoes, I might also get angry. After seeing the Weibo post, I called my colleague responsible for customer service and asked him one question: Do you think this customer is unreasonable? He said: Yes. At that moment, I knew something was wrong. Because he had already judged, and the issue was no longer the original one. I didn't continue asking, but on May 3rd at the airport, I called Ye Kai and said I wanted to see him in Beijing.

CSDN: Is this a PR crisis?

Wang Jian: Not really. When I called Ye Kai at the airport, I just said I would come to see him and apologize, with no other intention. So after landing, I went straight to his company to meet Ye Kai and his CTO, but what they said made me feel ashamed. Why did I feel ashamed? They said they respected me, and this incident made them a bit embarrassed. They didn't want compensation, they just wanted Alibaba Cloud to pay attention. But the issues were real—our services simply weren't good enough, and failures occurred repeatedly. This was a great opportunity for me (to understand front-line information). What I need to reflect on most is why I didn't know about these things beforehand, but only learned about them through Weibo afterward.

CSDN: This is similar to the TeamCola complaint incident years ago. Then you proposed improving the productization degree of platform components, better enabling self-service, and explicitly separating operations. To handle this, what are you planning to do?

Wang Jian: I used to think employees were doing a good job in service. But this case made me realize there are still many areas that need improvement. The biggest thing this year is to make ticket processing transparent, allowing customers to see the ticket handling process and track progress. I am currently working on this system. Only transparency can make customers feel more assured. For the media, we must also be transparent. Only transparency can truly show the full picture of events, understand progress, and solve problems.

CSDN: For typical industry users like games, will Alibaba Cloud design more optimized solutions/products/services? Does this align with the advocated general platform concept?

Wang Jian: It aligns. Although many people misunderstand this point. However, Alibaba Cloud builds a general platform, which will host many solutions, and deeper industry services should be the space for partners. We won't do industries; in some industries, enterprises within the system understand them much better. For example, Ye Kai, they develop both mobile games and web games, with this experience, they are more suitable for industry service roles. We will practice this time around. And as their demand for big data emerges, perhaps such cooperation will go deeper. This is what I've consistently emphasized—learning cloud computing from customers. This is not just a slogan, but a genuine insight, and it has become part of Alibaba Cloud's DNA.

CSDN: What is Alibaba Cloud's plan for services?

Wang Jian: When Alibaba Cloud was initially planned, the principle was to provide minimal services to balance scale and service. But when implementing services, we found a lack of applications. Alibaba Cloud has many reserves, such as big data processing, which are currently not open. We want to stabilize existing products first, build a strong team, and then develop steadily. In the next 3-5 months, you will notice many qualitative changes, such as Wanwang being fully integrated. Domain name filing in China is an inseparable part of cloud computing. Wanwang is strong in domain name filing and operations. Through integration, we will provide end-to-end, shortest-chain cloud computing service experiences. On the other hand, we need to better transform our service mindset. Ye Kai said there was no customer manager, but actually, there was. However, the customer manager was not in the customer service team. This is indeed disconnected, causing miscommunication. The first thing I will do upon returning is to tell him who his customer manager is. Before this issue is resolved, I will be his biggest customer manager. Additionally, there are currently at least 30,000 customers using various services on Alibaba Cloud, but we don't know their application directions. In reality, each customer's purpose and required services are different. We are learning and will conduct customer analysis in the future to improve service quality.

CSDN: We have always believed that China needs a larger cloud computing ecosystem. Among these, Netflix or possibly PlayCrab in the future could be one link.

Wang Jian: I think today's soil in China is actually very good, much better than I imagined. What does good soil mean?
