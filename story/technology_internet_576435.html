Annual Dragon Tiger Battle: Kindle Fire VS. B&N Nook Tablet Part II [System and Software]

by diglog on 2011-11-21 21:50:13

In yesterday's [Appearance and Hardware Section], we examined the hardware configurations and exterior materials of the Kindle Fire and B&N Nook tablets. Now, let’s compare their internal capabilities. Without a doubt, for content consumption-oriented tablets that do not rely on hardware supremacy, consumers place more emphasis on the software user experience and the experience of consuming digital multimedia content.

System, Operation, and Navigation:

Although both systems are based on Android 2.3, after deep customization, they each present interfaces and operation methods unique to their respective brands. From the color schemes alone, one can see the different backgrounds of the two companies: Amazon is an internet technology company, using a cool deep gray with orange accents. Barnes & Noble (B&N), on the other hand, aims to express a humanistic quality, adopting white paired with a light blue.

Comparing the interfaces of both, they generally use a concept similar to a "bookshelf" for displaying e-books. The Nook's home screen is closer to that of a traditional Android device, featuring multi-faced switchable desktops. In contrast, the Kindle Fire uses its own horizontal list of super-large icons to display recently opened content (e-books, web pages, music, movies, and apps).

Regarding the overall system experience, it is unfortunately less favorable for Chinese users as both companies have simplified the system settings for input language, preventing users from changing the input language or keyboard. Currently, there isn't a good solution available. In terms of typing comfort, both devices feel very similar. Kindle Fire's virtual keyboard has wider keys, resulting in smaller gaps between them. The Nook's keys are narrower, thus having larger gaps. As a 7-inch tablet, when held vertically, you can comfortably type with both hands. When placed horizontally, the device still needs to be rested on another flat surface.

As a service provider capable of offering various media contents such as music, movies, and e-books, Amazon emphasizes this advantage throughout its customized system. In the default main menu of the Fire, you can find that music, books, and movies are categorized separately and independently displayed. Under each category, you can choose to use digital content either from the cloud or locally. In this aspect, the Nook is much weaker, simply setting up a Library to display purchased e-books. To access other types of files, you have to use traditional folder browsing.

After a full day of heavy use, my conclusion is: Amazon Kindle Fire excels in overall system content design, consistency in experience, and navigation.

The Nook tablet focuses on details, with some small designs appearing quite humane. Specifically, when using the Kindle Fire, you can complete your operations with the simplest steps. The Fire system will not confuse or disorient you. Every control and icon is pointed out very clearly and understandably. Initially using the Nook, I found it harder to adapt to the Nook's system design because there are already two rows of information bars at the bottom of the screen, and pressing the physical Home button brings up a third row of bottom buttons. There is also a somewhat confusing navigation bar at the top. This undoubtedly adds operational burdens for consumers, requiring time to figure out the functions of each icon and button, which does not provide a good experience. The main advantages of the Nook in system design lie in push status notifications and back/gesture operations. The Nook's push window is located at the lower left corner of the screen, not only categorizing different content but also directly showing small icons of programs like QQ or Skype in the status bar when they are running, eliminating the need for additional check operations.
