Bill Si - How to Grasp Consumer Psychology

by stonepx918 on 2012-03-06 09:48:22

Rare consumption motives include value, norms, habits, identity, emotion, etc. According to Jack Trout's positioning theory, human behavior is always driven by certain motives. Some people refer to consumers' consumption motives as consumer psychological positioning. Correspondingly, consumer psychological positioning also includes value psychology, normative psychology, habitual psychology, identity psychology, emotional psychology, etc. Below is a brief introduction.

1. Consumer Value Psychology

According to the research of Shanghai Management Consulting Company: Consumers like a certain product because they believe this product will bring them greater value than similar products, that is, it has greater potential value. Potential value depends on the product's potential quality. What is referred to as potential quality is not the quality detected by quality supervision departments, but the quality perceived in the heart of consumers, the subjective evaluation of a brand by consumers. The reason why Coca-Cola has led Pepsi for more than a century is because it claims to be "authentic" and "original" and "unique", making consumers believe it has irreplaceable value, which is potential value. In fact, the reason why a brand can open up sales channels is often not because of its real value, but due to its potential value. Potential value has uniqueness, independence, credibility, and importance. Potential value is the famous brand effect, just like the celebrity effect, which is a concept, a concept that has been deeply rooted in the hearts of consumers.

2. Consumer Norm Psychology

Norms have a huge role. Norms refer to the sum total of all moral behavioral rules that people commonly follow. In real life, they influence my thoughts, constrain my words and deeds, and affect various aspects of my life. The faces of norms are diverse, including principles, rationality, obligations, politeness, friendship, loyalty, understanding, and many other factors. In many cases, norms can induce the motivation of consumption behavior. According to temporary surveys and studies by marketing experts, consumers like a certain brand often to prevent or eliminate an inner contradiction between their norms and price. When consumers decide whether to buy or not buy a product of a certain brand, norms are an important influencing factor. In the early 1980s, a global environmental protection craze arose. As Germany's first mass brand to pay attention to environmental protection, "Frog" did not only take the environmental protection perspective within the scope of norms as a supplement to its value advertising strategy, but also proudly placed it at the center of its advertising propaganda. In just three years, the sales of its products tripled. Success was precisely because it was in line with the global environmental awareness, allowing consumers to have a good feeling that is in line with and coordinated with the norms they believed in.

3. Consumer Habit Psychology

Habits are behaviors temporarily formed and difficult to change in a short time. Different people and different ethnic groups have different habits. For example, southerners in China take rice as their staple food; Northerners like beer, while Southerners prefer red wine; some people love smoking, some people love dressing up, etc. Habits are often irresistible and even play a bigger role in decision-making than value psychology. Consumers generally have specific consumption habits, which are formed through long-term consumption behavior in daily life. For instance, when consumers initially use a certain branded product and feel good about it, they form a perception of the quality and efficacy of that product and gradually develop a preference for this brand, establishing trust in this brand, enhancing confidence in using this brand, and usually do not switch to other brands, becoming loyal customers of this brand. For example, some consumers like to go to large malls to buy clothes and appliances, and go to supermarkets to buy daily necessities and food. Once consumption habits are formed, they generally do not change easily. Brand positioning expresses a philosophical emotional appeal, which will stimulate consumers' desire to consume, cultivate their consumption habits, and improve their brand loyalty. Due to the subtle influence of habits, people gradually form fixed lifestyles. These lifestyles settle over history and become cultural customs. When these customs settle to a certain depth, they become a cultural heritage. After years of exploration and discussion, marketing experts have already formed a set of business theories that fully utilize this potential cultural heritage, using consumers' habit psychology to achieve sales targets. In the early 1990s, Wrigley's gum hit the German market. In the minds of consumers, gum, cavity prevention is a unique additional function. At the same time, mixed mouthwash gum was also launched. In the minds of consumers, the main function of mixed mouthwash gum is teeth cleaning and protection, and gum chewing is its additional function. After a period of market competition, mixed mouthwash gum eventually failed, while Wrigley's gum took the lead with a 90% market share. The reason is actually very simple: consumer habits are at work, most consumers have become accustomed to gum first and then cavity prevention.

4. Consumer Identity Psychology

People also unconsciously reveal their identities. Especially those who have gained a certain reputation, power, and status, everyone has a certain identity. They are constantly paying attention to their own identity, showing their identity, trying to make their speech and actions and social activities conform to their identity. The most revealing of a person's identity is clothing, food, housing, transportation, and use, such as someone wearing high-end name-brand clothing, driving a Rolls-Royce car, staying in a five-star luxury hotel. When this information is transmitted to the outside world, then this person's identity will naturally be revealed. Thus, marketing experts, based on this psychological aspect of human nature, have summarized a corresponding marketing theory - identity principle, making the brand an effective weapon for consumers to express their self-identity. For enterprises, developing products that better reveal consumers' identities than competitors has become an important topic, because this directly affects consumers' purchasing decisions, thereby affecting product sales.

5. Consumer Emotional Psychology

Emotions are psychological responses to external stimuli, such as liking, admiration, sadness, fear, anger, disgust, etc. Consumers like or dislike a certain product, all of which are natural expressions of consumers' emotions. Experienced brand operators have already valued these and often spare no effort to dig out the potential of brand growth through advertisements, public relations, and other means, shaking consumers' emotions, and fully utilizing consumers' emotional psychology to enhance brands.
