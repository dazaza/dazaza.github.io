Since most items are not subject to tariffs, the prices of goods in Hong Kong are relatively low, and it has long been known as a "shopping paradise." The shopping areas in Hong Kong can roughly be divided into two locations: "Hong Kong Island" and "Kowloon." In Kowloon, the four key areas along the MTR line are "Tsim Sha Tsui," "Jordan," "Yau Ma Tei," and "Mong Kok." On Hong Kong Island, the four key areas along the MTR line are "Central," "North Point," "Admiralty," and "Causeway Bay," and there is also Sheung Wan, which reflects Hong Kong's unique culture. Here, various stores sell goods with different characteristics from all over the world, and most service industry employees have received professional training. They are diligent and friendly, customer-oriented, and the prices of goods are reasonable, much lower than in other countries.

Hong Kong has many famous shopping streets that concentrate similar types of stores in one place. For example, Nathan Road in Kowloon is mainly focused on gold jewelry, Mong Kok's Sai Yeung Choi Street specializes in audio-visual equipment, and Hollywood Road in Sheung Wan is home to many antique shops.

If you go to Hong Kong, you must visit jewelry stores. The quality of Tse Sui Luen, Chow Tai Fook, and Chow Sang Sang is the most reliable.

In addition, each area has large shopping malls, such as the Peninsula Hotel Mall, Harbour City, and New World Centre in Tsim Sha Tsui; the Landmark and Taikoo Place in Central; Times Square and Sogo Department Store in Causeway Bay; and the New Town Plaza in Sha Tin. Generally speaking, the malls around Central are mostly high-end, with high-quality goods and naturally higher prices. As for Causeway Bay and Tsim Sha Tsui, these are good places for ordinary citizens to shop. Regarding Yau Ma Tei and Mong Kok, the quality of goods is not high, but the prices of goods are more affordable.

Friendly reminders:

Δ Before going to Hong Kong, make a list first to understand what you need to buy and check the prices in mainland China as a reference for shopping in Hong Kong.

Δ Try to choose malls and restaurants that have the "Quality" mark posted by the Hong Kong Tourism Board. These merchants offer quality service guarantees, clearly marked prices, and can give consumers certain discounts. Do not shop at stores without clear price markings.

Δ After buying something, keep the receipt and credit card slip carefully, and check if the item placed in the shopping bag is what you bought to avoid being switched by the merchant.

Δ All member travel agencies under the Hong Kong Tourism Commission have committed to providing 100% refund shopping. If you feel dissatisfied after consuming during the tour-arranged shopping activities, you can handle it through the tour guide or file a complaint with the commission within 14 days of purchase, and return the completely unused and fully packaged goods to process a full refund. Remember to keep the original receipts.

Δ Most stores in Hong Kong do not accept complaints or returns after leaving the counter, so ask clearly about the return and exchange details before paying to avoid unnecessary trouble.

Δ If a dispute occurs with the merchant while shopping, do not argue in the store. Keep your shopping receipt and file a complaint with the Consumer Council. If the situation is severe, you can directly call the 24-hour emergency assistance hotline 999.

Δ Streets where consumption is concentrated, shoe stores, and restaurants are places where pickpockets are more common, so you must be vigilant and preferably not act alone.
