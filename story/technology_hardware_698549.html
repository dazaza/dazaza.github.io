Exhibition Trip to Euro Marmor 2012

The Journey to Participate in the European Tombstone Supplies Exhibition

Participating in the European tombstone supplies exhibition was naturally an exciting event for us who have always stayed within the confines of our domestic market. Moreover, this time we were going to France, a country renowned for its romance. Would we encounter a romantic journey and find our ideal partner while completing our exhibition work? Would we have the chance to see the breathtaking Paris night views that are so impressive in our minds? What would the actual appearance of Gothic architecture be like? With these excited yet nervous feelings, we began our preparatory work before the exhibition.

After starting the exhibition work, I realized that everything was far from the beautiful dreams I had imagined. From selecting exhibits and purchasing booths to finding companies to handle visas, booking hotels, and shipping exhibits, a large pile of things kept everyone busy and stressed out. Especially, the choice of exhibition intermediaries caused us a real headache.

For small enterprises like ours in the stone industry, which are still in the development stage, the cost of participating in exhibitions in Europe was squeezed out from every possible penny. The exhibition service providers varied widely; among them, half were scammers. Fortunately, we conducted strict screening work and managed not to fall victim to fraud. Here, I can share some methods to distinguish between genuine and fake exhibition service providers.

Firstly, one should be cautious about those who proactively call to offer services. Generally speaking, popular European exhibitions are almost impossible to get tickets for, as many exhibitions are reserved a year or even six months in advance. Large-scale exhibition service companies barely have enough booths for their fixed old customers, let alone need to solicit business everywhere.

Secondly, the Internet can also be of great help. First, we can check through the Internet's industrial and commercial system whether the company exists, what its registered capital is, if it has its own website, and how good the quality of the website is. This point is very important. Imagine, a company without industrial and commercial filing, without a professional website, could hardly have much financial strength and cannot claim to be professional.

Of course, only relying on the Internet is not enough. There is still much homework to do offline. For example, check the telephone and fax numbers of the exhibition organizing company to see if they match the company. When booking booths and routes, confirm repeatedly, and if necessary, communicate directly with their general manager.

After all preparations were ready, we could finally set off. The venue for this exhibition was in Lyon, France. Our flight needed to make a stopover at Frankfurt Airport. We were accompanied by two other exhibitors, making a total of five people in the entire delegation, including a Polish couple who were very nice. They introduced us to a lot of European customs along the way, and we proudly told them about our 5000 years of brilliant culture.

When the plane arrived in Lyon, it was already midnight. We went straight to the hotel to rest, but due to the time difference, we didn't sleep well, tossing and turning until we got up at around seven in the morning. It wasn’t until then that we paid attention to the hotel. The hotel had six floors, the rooms weren't very large, but they were clean and tidy, and there was even a kitchen where we could cook ourselves.

After finishing washing up, we went downstairs for breakfast. The tour guide from Jicheng Travel Network had already come early to the restaurant waiting for us. Unlike most hotels in China where breakfast is charged separately, hotels in Europe generally include breakfast. According to the guide, in France, three-star hotels mainly offer cold dishes for breakfast, four-star hotels have hot food, and higher-class hotels provide gourmet breakfasts. We stayed at a three-star hotel, and although it was just cold dishes, the variety was quite abundant, including croissants, cakes, fruits, raw meat, smoked meat, milk, jam, etc. The croissants left a deep impression on me. Freshly baked, they were crispy on the outside and tender on the inside, with a unique texture. Even someone like me who isn't particularly fond of bread couldn't resist eating several more.

After satisfying our hunger, it was time to focus on the main task, heading to the exhibition area to set up the booth. Our booth wasn't in a great location, on the right side of the entrance. This was unavoidable since the better booths had been snapped up long ago. Therefore, we warn everyone to book early when participating in European exhibitions. The booth was a standard stall with one table, two chairs, three projectors, and a header board (the header board only had an English company introduction, no Chinese).

Setting up the booth took much longer than we expected because our exhibits had to be transported here through customs. Being stone products, they were both large and cumbersome. We spent 20 euros in tips to hire a staff member from the exhibition hall to help us, and we barely managed to set up the booth. At noon, our local guide thoughtfully delivered lunch to us, which was chicken rice made by a Chinese restaurant. Only abroad did we truly feel the depth of Chinese culinary skills; our dishes were simply better.

After a day of hard work, by around six in the evening, the booth was finally set up. We finally had time to wander around the streets of France. We first found a restaurant for dinner, then went to the supermarket to buy daily necessities and small items such as perfume and trinkets as gifts for friends back home. By the time we returned to the hotel, we were already exhausted and fell into a deep sleep.

On the third day in France, the exhibition officially started. Since it was Friday, not many people came that day, but there were still some European guests inquiring at each booth. Nowadays, more and more domestic companies are engaged in foreign trade, and foreigners also know how to compare prices. Often, they would ask questions that interested them, leave their business cards or contact information, and then move on to the next booth.

There were also quite a few Chinese companies participating in this exhibition, and some domestic exhibitors who didn't understand French hired local Chinese as interpreters. After a day of interacting with these burly foreigners, we collected contact details of over twenty companies. Some customers showed strong intentions to place orders, which made us feel that all our previous efforts were worthwhile, and we could return and report satisfactorily to our boss.

With the experience gained from the first day of the exhibition, the subsequent two days went much smoother. As Saturday and Sunday approached, the number of visitors increased. We skillfully moved among these potential customers, vividly introducing our exhibits to them. Our efforts were recognized, and on Saturday alone, we obtained contact details of over fifty European companies dealing with stones. From our conversations with them, at least two to three companies showed interest in cooperating with us.

It wasn't until late afternoon on Sunday that the peak period of the exhibition gradually subsided. At this point, most of the visitors were companies coming to collect leftover exhibits. Because transporting stone exhibits back to China would be inconvenient and costly, it wasn't worth it, so we sold them at a low price to these companies.

By around six in the evening on Sunday, all exhibition-related work was basically completed. Taking advantage of the remaining time, despite being tired, we insisted on dragging the Jicheng local guide to take a nighttime tour of Lyon, France. Without the pressure of work, we relaxed a lot, indulging in the dazzling neon lights and occasionally bursting into unrestrained laughter at the local guide’s witty remarks.
