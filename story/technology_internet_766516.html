Apple's Potential Crisis: Chinese Manufacturers Have Better Supply Chain Advantages

by anonymous on 2013-09-18 21:42:14

Nobel laureate in Economics, Paul Krugman, recently published an article in the New York Times stating that Apple is facing a crisis surpassing Microsoft. "Apple is a company that needs to rely on the loyalty of mass consumers, while Microsoft's customers are mostly enterprise IT managers who are more conservative," he noted. Interestingly, Apple had previously been the best reflection of Krugman’s theoretical framework in the technology sector. It leveraged network effects to accumulate irreplaceable user experiences, and its vast user base had also brought about economies of scale. "But it has now lost the network effect," Krugman bluntly remarked.

Fortunately, this article was published before Apple's new product launch event. Perhaps Apple's two new products will further solidify Krugman's views. Once again, there was no applause; Apple's innovation merely swapped out a few colored shells.

Speculating on how the late Steve Jobs might understand today's Apple is actually quite a dull exercise. Even he would have to admit that hardware innovation in smartphones has little potential left for exploration. Screens, sound, photography, processing speed... everything that once could undergo qualitative changes through component upgrades has now surpassed human limits.

Topics such as whether we need an "8-core" phone or if camera resolution needs to reach 41 million pixels have replaced the surprises that technological upgrades themselves used to bring us. Phones are no longer purely a technical issue.

Software is much the same. Developers still prefer to release their products on iOS first, but an increasing number of developers recognize that the more open Android system represents the future. Moreover, at this stage of development, platforms are showing signs of homogenization.

From this perspective, what do you think Tim Cook, Apple's current CEO, will do? Apple's revenue in China during its third fiscal quarter dropped sharply by 43% year-over-year. Nowadays, it's not just Samsung taking its market share; Chinese manufacturers like ZTE, Huawei, Coolpad, and Lenovo (collectively known as “China Cool United”) may be eating up even more.

Cook is doing many things similar to what Microsoft's two former CEOs did in China: courting more partners (the long-stalled cooperation with China Mobile is nearing completion), expanding sales channels (Apple retail stores in China will increase several-fold), and expanding the product line (a low-cost iPhone is already in the planning stages, breaking the single-model strategy).

Apple increasingly exhibits characteristics of the industrial age, or one might say it is becoming more of a sales company rather than the value-selling "art company" it once was. Microsoft followed a similar path; when Microsoft dominated the world's PC desktops, Ballmer became a consummate businessman.

Surprisingly, Apple has listed China as one of the first countries for product launches, and for the first time ever, held a simultaneous product launch in China. Cook says Apple will be more proactive in China, and everything he does appears aggressive, but these actions are purely market-driven and have nothing to do with the products Apple takes pride in.

This is precisely Apple's underlying crisis. When the battle extends to the supply chain, where direct confrontations occur, Chinese manufacturers, once derided by Apple as "hardware monsters," have an advantage. They are more flexible and better at dealing with price-sensitive Chinese users, which is enough.

This is also why Wall Street favors Lenovo even more. Samsung's penetration into the supply chain has ensured at least a three-year advantage, but Lenovo might be even scarier. In the next wave of tablet computers, Lenovo and other PC manufacturers may have an edge over Samsung.

Thus, Cook has not been negligent in his duties; he understands Apple's situation better than anyone. His best choice is to sell products more aggressively, through various means, but this will inevitably affect the deeply rooted values within Apple.

One day in the future, Apple's internal environment might resemble Microsoft's past, with the pervasive sentiment: "Cook is just a businessman." The more they reminisce about Jobs, the more Apple risks losing its way. Cook must make Apple face reality.
