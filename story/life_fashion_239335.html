Why can't masquerade balls become popular?

by longersing on 2009-09-17 14:24:49

Since we were very young, we have seen some ghostly costumes in foreign films. Those parties and gatherings always create a special atmosphere for us. In the late 1980s to early 1990s, the American family comedy "Growing Pains" was extremely popular across China. As a film familiar to those born in the 70s and 80s, it naturally introduced us to American culture of that era. However, genuine costume parties are not exclusive to America nor did they become popular only in the 1980s. Costume parties originated from the nobility of the British Empire and gradually spread to the common people, then around the world, influencing the entire cultural progression of humanity.

Yet, we in the 21st century have yet to truly experience the excitement and pleasure of such events. This pleasure is precisely the blend of horror and entertainment brought by foreign cultures. Costume parties require no elaborate planning, few regulations, and no sustained maintenance. All that's needed is a group of free-spirited enthusiasts gathering together to unleash their passion. The appearance that you've imagined but dared not wear outside your home can now be freely displayed because at a costume party, everyone is just like you. There are no constraints here; there is only joy.

I’ve attended two costume parties of our time, and I feel that these are still far from true happiness. One was a masked ball where all masks were purchased, lacking the fun of personal creation. If given the choice, I would prefer to make my own costumes and masks. Another occasion was a complete promotional event.

The reason why such parties cannot be widely accepted in China largely lies in the fact that people’s cultural concepts cannot easily shift from "formal" to informal. Actually, life is informal. Displaying what you love and doing what you enjoy without constraints—that is life. This also reflects the contrast between Chinese and Western cultures.
