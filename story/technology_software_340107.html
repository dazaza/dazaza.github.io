iPhone: Succeed with WiFi, fail with WiFi?

by heimeirem on 2010-04-05 00:56:18

[IT168 Review] Ever since I got my hands on the iPhone two months ago, I've fallen madly in love with it. Since I often go out for interviews, I can use it to send and receive emails, prepare interview outlines on the bus, and when I can't find my way, the built-in map positioning is very convenient. The recording effect of the iPhone is also not bad at all; my digital voice recorder has been sitting untouched in my bag for months. It's also very convenient to take pictures and send them back to the editor.

However, it's said that China Unicom’s iPhone sales have not been good. There's no reason for this, right? One reason given is the high price - starting at 6000 RMB, which domestic consumers simply cannot accept. Last week, there were media reports that China Unicom was piloting installment payments and "zero down payment" methods in several major cities to lower the threshold for purchasing an iPhone. This can confirm the point. But regardless of China Unicom's sales strategy, saying that Chinese consumers can't afford the iPhone underestimates our purchasing power. Haven't you noticed that Nokia's top-tier brand Vertu phones, whose images can be downloaded, cost tens of thousands of RMB each, yet they are still selling like hotcakes in China?

Another reason mentioned is the lack of WiFi. After all, the iPhone was born for the network - forget about built-in Google Maps, app stores, and such. Even its text messaging function is made to look like a network instant chat. Nowadays, Wi-Fi access points are everywhere in Shenzhen, and places like Yonghe King and McDonald's offer free Wi-Fi 24 hours a day. Without Wi-Fi, the official version of the iPhone is destined to miss out on most popular online applications, naturally reducing its appeal. Although gray-market iPhones are only slightly cheaper than the official ones, they come with Wi-Fi and thus remain in high demand in Huqiangbei.

If Wi-Fi is so great, then why do most official phones lose this feature when they come to China? I shamelessly asked a few "big shots" in Shenzhen's mobile phone industry, but they all avoided answering. In fact, for a long time, our country has been promoting the domestically developed wireless local area network standard WAPI. Therefore, to avoid direct competition between Wi-Fi and WAPI and promote the development of WAPI, restricting Wi-Fi phones from entering the market became a necessary measure.

But what exactly is WAPI? According to reports by the Global Times and others, it makes wireless networks more secure. However, whether or not such security is needed, I believe the choice should be left to the users themselves. If consumers feel that Wi-Fi is insecure, they can switch to WAPI. Moreover, if Wi-Fi really is insecure and causes harm to many users' interests, it will naturally be eliminated by the market.

In fact, as early as 2003, Wi-Fi had already become very popular worldwide and was practically the universal standard for wireless local area networks. Many families and enterprises in China used it to build computer wireless networks, so it made perfect sense for mobile phones to have built-in Wi-Fi. However, the release of WAPI-related standards brought everything to a halt. It wasn’t until the end of last year that the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology opened up approval for Wi-Fi phones, and a batch of new phones with Wi-Fi functionality hit the market. As a result, WAPI now exists in name only. Nevertheless, we could have enjoyed high-speed wireless networks on our phones six years earlier.

Looking back at the iPhone, what was its initial success attributed to? If it was due to high-speed networks, initially it didn't even have 3G capability, just Wi-Fi. It was because Wi-Fi allowed its users to connect to the internet at the lowest possible cost, enabling them to download millions of applications and music from iTunes. The business model of the iPhone and iTunes has long been hailed as a miracle.
