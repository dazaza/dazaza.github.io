Choosing the Best Golf Ball

All golfers have one thing in common: they can't play without golf balls. Skill level and/or experience have nothing to do with this one no-brainer. However, it's not just about whether or not one needs golf balls; it’s the critical question of which golf balls to choose. The answer is not universal and typically depends on the golfer's preferences and tastes. It comes down to how much the golfer wants to spend and their expectations from the ball. These expectations can vary depending on whether you golf only on weekends or golf all the time.

Some golfers play using only one specific brand of golf balls. There are no expectations for such golfers. Cost is not an issue. They golf with that ball or don’t golf at all. Yes, this could be an extreme case, but the fact remains that many people consider this as the norm. Golfing is probably almost as essential to them as food and water, well, at least it seems, and they seem to take golfing equipment to the extreme.

Now, for a more practical approach to choosing a good golf ball, let’s start with the new golfer. Our beginner golfer should forget everything they’ve heard about types of golf balls. Disregard all the various reviews and recommendations because, in the end, the ball doesn’t apply far beyond the basics. First, playing good golf is not all about the quality of the ball but about learning and playing. First and foremost, beginner golfers lose a lot of golf balls at first. During this learning stage, cost should come before quality every time when selecting another golf ball. "Been around" balls are a great option for beginner golfers. These balls are sold in bulk and are considered recycled balls; they have already been on the golf course once. They were lost by others, found, and now offered at approximately fifty to a bag. They’re an excellent starting point for beginner golfers. Typically, these used golf balls aren’t brand-name golf balls. Does it really matter at this point? Absolutely not. The beginner golfer is more focused on mastering the basic skills needed to actually play the game of golf. They are learning not just how to hit a ball but how to hit it straight and avoid obstacles like trees and water. Since dozens or even hundreds of balls will likely be lost, it is always more logical for the beginner golfer to buy used balls in bulk at a lower cost. As a golfer improves, he or she can gradually transition to a higher-quality ball. However, it is still important to consider the cost of the ball in relation to skill level. Avoid rushing to the nearest golf shop and buying the most expensive ball on the market. Generally, golfers improve as they play more. He or she can experiment with the variety of brands for balls as their skill level improves. Soon, they should find the ball that is best suited for them while they are on the course.
