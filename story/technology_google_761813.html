Google Brain Father: Computers Will Eventually Be Able to Simulate the Human Brain

by geekzhang on 2013-05-10 22:42:06

Google Brain's Father, Andrew NgOn May 10th, news broke that Andrew Ng, the director of Stanford University’s Artificial Intelligence Lab and one of the dozen or so tech figures featured in Time Magazine’s list of the world’s most influential people in 2013, is currently leading Google Brain. Within Google, he is driving forward deep learning algorithms. In June of last year, the Google Brain project applied the results of its deep learning research to create a neural network with 1 billion connections using 1000 computers. This enabled the machine system to learn how to automatically recognize cats, making it an internationally well-known case in the field of deep learning.Recently, Wired magazine published an article providing a detailed introduction of this father of Google Brain and his research area. Below is a summary of the article's content:The Single Algorithm Hypothesis Changes the Direction of AI ResearchThere is a theory that human intelligence comes from a single algorithm. The experimental basis for this theory is that in the early stages of human brain development, the responsibilities of each part are uncertain, meaning that the part of the human brain responsible for processing sound can actually also process visual images. In essence, the human brain is a general-purpose machine that can be fine-tuned to perform specific tasks.Seven years ago, Stanford University computer science professor Andrew Ng accidentally came across this theory, which fundamentally changed his understanding of the nature of artificial intelligence and reignited his passion for AI technology, altering his career trajectory. He recalled that for the first time in his life, he felt that there was a possibility of making progress in the field of artificial intelligence research.Ng said that in the early days of AI technology research, the mainstream idea was that human intelligence formed through the collaborative work of thousands of simple agents, what MIT professor Marvin Minsky called the "society of mind." Engineers therefore believed that to achieve artificial intelligence, they would have to build thousands of independent computer modules. One module, or algorithm, simulates language, another handles speech production, etc., and so on. In short, according to this early concept, the amount of work required to achieve artificial intelligence was enormous and difficult to realize.Ng's childhood dream was to invent machines that could think like humans, but when he entered university and began studying artificial intelligence, he encountered the above-mentioned prevailing idea, which discouraged him and led him to abandon his research into artificial intelligence. Later, as a university professor, he even dissuaded his students from pursuing what he considered the impractical dream of artificial intelligence. Until one day, he came across the hypothesis that human intelligence originates from a single algorithm, realizing that his previous understanding of artificial intelligence might have been completely wrong, and he finally picked up his childhood dream again. The proponent of this single algorithm hypothesis was Jeff Hawkins, an entrepreneur in the field of artificial intelligence with a background in neuroscience.It now appears that this hypothesis has changed more than just Ng's career. His current main research area is deep learning in machine learning technology, a relatively new field in computer science. The primary goal of deep learning research is to create machines that can handle data like the human brain. Currently, deep learning research is no longer limited to academic circles; large companies such as Google and Apple have also realized the immense opportunities contained within. Together with other researchers at Google, Ng established the most ambitious artificial intelligence project ever, Google Brain.The guiding principle of the Google Brain project is to combine computer science with neuroscience, something that has never truly been achieved in the field of artificial intelligence. Ng said, "I discovered a huge generation gap between engineers (skilled in computer science) and scientists (skilled in neuroscience). Engineers want to build successful artificial intelligence systems, while scientists still fail to fully understand the complex workings of the human brain. For a long time, neuroscience couldn't provide answers for engineers trying to build intelligent machines."Moreover, according to Bruno Olshausen, the director of the Redwood Center for Theoretical Neuroscience at UC Berkeley, scientists always feel that brain research belongs to their domain and are unwilling to collaborate with researchers from other fields.The end result is that engineers start building artificial intelligence systems that do not fully mimic the human brain's operations. What they come up with can only be called pseudo-intelligent systems, products like Roomba robots that can only be used for vacuuming, unlike Rosie the robot maid from the cartoon "The Jetsons."Ng Explains Deep Learning with ComputersUnder the efforts of Ng and others, this situation has finally started to change. Dr. Thomas Insel, director of the National Institute of Mental Health, introduced that a consensus has formed in the industry: whoever figures out how the brain computes will design the next generation of computers.What Exactly is Deep Learning?Deep learning is the first step in artificial intelligence technology towards a new direction of research. Simply put, deep learning involves building neural networks that can mimic human brain behavior. These multi-layered computer networks, like the human brain, can collect information and produce corresponding behaviors based on the collected information. These computer networks can gradually perceive and understand the shapes and sounds of things, meaning they can recognize objects.For example, to give machines vision, researchers need to establish a basic layer of artificial neurons to detect basic information such as the edge shapes of objects. The second layer of neurons can piece together the object edges detected by the first layer to identify larger blocks of object shapes. Then adding another layer will further assemble the information detected by the second layer, enabling the machine to understand the overall shape of the object. A key point here is that the software can do all this itself—unlike older pseudo-AI systems that often required engineers to manually input visual or auditory information about objects, which would then be processed by machine learning algorithms.Ng explained that under deep learning algorithms, we can feed this system a lot of data, allowing it to independently learn some concepts about the world. Last year, one of Ng's deep learning algorithms recognized cats by scanning countless cat pictures on the internet. The machine did not know the word "cat," so Ng had to input this word. After some time, the machine associated this furry little animal with the word "cat" and could independently identify what constitutes a "cat."The method of teaching machines to learn mimics the way humans learn. When we were infants, we began recognizing things we encountered by observing our surroundings. However, if our parents didn’t tell us the name of something, we wouldn’t be able to know it ourselves.Of course, Ng's deep learning algorithms currently cannot match the precision and flexibility of the human brain, but he says, "That day will come."From Google, China to the Obama Administration, Who is Studying Deep Learning?Andrew Ng is not fighting alone; deep learning has become a major trend in the development of computer science. In 2011, Ng led the establishment of the Google Brain project within Google. In recent months, Google has significantly increased its investment in the field of deep learning, acquiring the artificial intelligence institute founded by Geoffrey Hinton, a professor at the University of Toronto in Canada (referred to as the "godfather of neural network research"). Baidu, China's search giant, has also established a deep learning laboratory, pledging to invest substantial resources in the field of deep learning. Ng stated that other tech giants like Microsoft and Qualcomm have also begun hiring more scientists specializing in computer algorithms based on neuroscience.Meanwhile, Japanese engineers are building artificial neural networks to control robots, and South African neuroscientist Henry Markman is collaborating with scientists from the EU and Israel to simulate the human brain on a supercomputer using data from thousands of experiments.The difficulty in research still lies in our inability to fully grasp how the human brain works, but scientists are making rapid progress in this area. Chinese scientists are researching a new brain atlas, naming it the "Brainnetdome" project. In the United States, with the announcement by the Obama administration supporting the interdisciplinary scientific research project, the BRAIN Initiative (Brain Research Through Advancing Innovative Neurotechnologies), many similar projects are emerging, marking the arrival of the era of big neuroscience.The BRAIN Initiative steering committee held its first meeting over the weekend, and more project preparations will take place this week. One of the goals of the BRAIN Initiative is to develop new technologies needed to map the complex circuits of the brain, and signs indicate that the focus of BRAIN's work is on artificial intelligence. Of the $100 million allocated by the U.S. government for the BRAIN Initiative, half comes from the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA), exceeding the amount allocated by the National Institutes of Health (NIH). The U.S. Department of Defense's research department stated that it hopes the BRAIN Initiative will lead to new information processing architectures or computational methods.If we can figure out how tens of thousands of neurons in the human brain connect with each other and how the central nervous system stores and processes information, then engineers like Andrew Ng will have a clearer vision for artificial brains. The achievements and data from human brain research will help the study of deep learning algorithms and accelerate the development of technologies such as computer vision, language analysis, and voice recognition provided by companies like Apple and Google for smartphones.So we need to learn the techniques that biological organisms use to survive, the key issue being that these secrets are hidden too deeply. "We don't yet have the tools necessary to uncover these secrets," laments Bruno Olshausen, a computational neuroscientist at UC Berkeley.The Future: Whoever Masters Artificial Intelligence Will Rule the WorldWith the rise of mobile devices, cracking the code of human neural mechanisms has become increasingly urgent. As devices get smaller, we need them to compute faster and more accurately. However, as the basic component of electronic devices—the transistor—continues to shrink in size, making them more precise and efficient becomes increasingly challenging. For example, to speed up device computation, you need to supply more power, but more power makes the device's computing system noisier, meaning its accuracy decreases.Olshausen introduced that currently, engineers can only deal with these issues by avoiding the core problems, striving to balance device size, computational speed, and energy consumption. Artificial intelligence technology provides better solutions. Biological sciences allow us to confront the root of the problem directly. Internal conversion mechanisms in biology are inherently noisy, but they have found a way to adapt and tolerate these disturbances—even utilize them. If we can figure out how biology deals with these noises internally, we can create a completely different computational model.The goal of scientists is not merely to make computing devices smaller; their goal is to enable computers to do more. Regardless of how complex the underlying algorithms may be, current computers cannot assist humans in buying groceries or selecting appropriate clothing or wallets. To handle such tasks, computers need to add advanced image intelligence recognition technologies, as well as attention and memory akin to humans. If this can be achieved, the possibilities for what computers can handle will become infinite.The world has realized that if these problems can be solved, the infinite business opportunities in the field of artificial intelligence will be unlocked. Olshausen predicts.The reason why companies like Google, IBM, Microsoft, Apple, and Baidu are competing to develop advanced machine learning technologies is due to the immense commercial potential behind them. Yann LeCun, a professor at New York University and an expert in deep learning, predicts that within two years, a large number of startups in the field of machine learning will emerge, many of which may be acquired by large companies.Although top engineers are generally not experts in human brain research, for engineers in the field of computer science today, having some knowledge of neuroscience may become a significant advantage. "We need to collaborate more closely with scientists in the field of neuroscience," says Kai Yu of Baidu. Yu is considering hiring a scientist from the field of neuroscience, "We are already working with them, but we're not doing enough."Andrew Ng's dream is becoming a reality. "I have hope, not just hope, but we may be able to achieve real artificial intelligence," he said. "We certainly haven't found the right algorithm yet—it might take decades. Achieving it won't be easy, but I see the hope."
