The food sources of centipedes are extensive, but they require fresh food. The Pingquan centipede breeding base states that centipedes do not eat rotten food. Therefore, in artificial breeding, fresh feed must be provided every 2 to 3 days. Before feeding, the remaining food from the previous time must be thoroughly removed. Centipedes have a large appetite and strong resistance to hunger. When hungry, their intake can reach 1/5 to 3/5 of their body weight. After eating enough, they can survive without food for ten to fifteen days.

At the same time, centipedes cannot endure thirst and need to drink water daily. Therefore, water containers must be placed in the breeding area, and the water must be changed regularly to keep it fresh and clean.

Agricultural Science Feed: Centipedes are typical carnivorous insects, quite ferocious. With their venomous fangs, they can capture various small animals much larger than themselves. The food sources of centipedes are extensive. Based on field observations and trial breeding results, combined with breeding experiences from different regions, the main feeds for centipedes include the following categories: one category is various insects, such as crickets, locusts, tobacco bugs, scarab beetles, rice bagworms, horned beetles, cicadas, grasshoppers, dragonflies, spiders, flies, bees, etc., including adult insects, pupae, larvae, and eggs. Another category includes worms, earthworms, snails, slugs, and other invertebrates, as well as muscles, bones, and internal organs of vertebrates like frogs, snakes, lizards, geckos, sparrows, mice, and bats. A third category consists of tender grass, branch tips, root tips, watermelons, cucumbers, and other fruits, as well as eggs, milk, bread, and other cooked products when the above food sources are insufficient. Additionally, centipedes bred in Zhejiang Province mainly consume loaches, eels, small fish, shrimp, and crabs.

(2) Egg-laying and Hatching: Every late spring to early summer is the egg-laying period for centipedes. Each female centipede usually lays 20 to 60 eggs, mostly 40 to 50 eggs, with a few laying fewer than 10 eggs. Before laying eggs, the centipede's abdomen tightly adheres to the ground, digging a shallow hole by itself. During egg-laying, the centipede's body curves into an "S" shape, laying the eggs serially in the self-dug shallow hole. Under undisturbed conditions, egg-laying takes about 2 to 3 hours. After laying, the centipede immediately turns its body sideways, using its legs to gather the eggs into a cluster and hugging them in its "arms" for incubation. The laid eggs are elliptical in shape, varying in size, with a general diameter of about 3 to 3.5 mm, light yellow, semi-transparent, and with elastic egg membranes.

Centipedes have a long incubation period, generally requiring 43 to 50 days. During the hatching process, both the external appearance and internal structure of the eggs change gradually with the development of the embryo. After about a month, the initial larval form appears within the egg, measuring approximately 1.2 cm in length, and the movement of the larvae inside the egg membrane can be observed; after 35 to 40 days, the larva measures around 1.5 cm in length, capable of climbing up and down but still unable to leave the mother; after 43 to 45 days, the larva measures about 2.5 cm in length and can leave the mother to search for food independently. Throughout the incubation period, the mother has already stored sufficient nutrients and does not need to be fed, as doing so could easily cause the eggs or larvae to be contaminated by food and eaten by the mother, affecting the hatching rate and survival rate of the larvae.

During the incubation period, the mother never leaves the egg cluster or the larvae, carefully incubating and guarding them. She frequently uses her antennae to drive away nearby small insects and often uses her feeding claws to manipulate or suckle the egg cluster or larvae. Observations suggest that this liquid secreted by the base of the mother’s feeding claws or oral glands is used to clean the egg cluster or larvae, preventing bacterial contamination or other pollutants.

If disturbed during egg-laying or incubation, centipedes will stop laying eggs and may eat all the eggs that have been laid or are being incubated. This is known as the "protective" reaction of centipedes. After eating the eggs, centipedes can usually lay and incubate eggs again. However, this significantly delays the egg-laying and incubation periods, reduces the number of eggs laid, and lowers the hatching rate, impacting the quantity and quality of centipedes. Therefore, during the egg-laying and incubation periods of centipedes in artificial breeding, the surrounding environment should remain quiet, avoiding any disturbances, which is a crucial point to note in breeding management.
