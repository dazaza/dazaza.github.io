Three Misconceptions to Avoid When Babies Drink Beverages

by da2j4g9z5o9 on 2012-02-18 18:48:23

The summer weather is scorching, and babies have a greater thirst for drinks than in other seasons. Although sweet and sour drinks are refreshing, drinking them in excess can negatively impact a baby's health, causing issues such as loss of appetite, hyperactivity, emotional instability, diarrhea, and fussiness. Therefore, it is essential to be cautious about avoiding the following three misconceptions when giving drinks to your baby in summer.

**Misconception 1: Replacing plain water with drinks**

Babies love sweet and sour drinks and don't care whether they're nutritious or not. Many babies find it hard to enjoy drinking tasteless plain water, so some mothers buy various flavored drinks and let their babies drink to their heart's content. They think that since drinks are also water, drinking water or drinks makes no difference. However, this practice is very wrong and can harm the baby's growth and development.

Frequently drinking beverages like cola, fruit tea, artificially flavored juice, and soda can irritate the stomach lining, dilute gastric juices, and affect digestion and nutrient absorption. It can also increase the burden on the liver and kidneys, weakening the baby’s immune system and making them more prone to respiratory, digestive, and other infections. Drinks containing caffeine or similar components can severely hinder the baby’s growth and development if consumed regularly. Drinking too many beverages may lead to symptoms such as loss of appetite, abdominal pain, diarrhea, vomiting, weight loss, and fatigue.

In fact, clean plain water is the most effective at quenching thirst. Once inside the body, it quickly promotes metabolism, regulates body temperature, transports nutrients, and cleanses the internal system. Especially boiled water that has naturally cooled down helps penetrate cell membranes, promoting metabolism, increasing hemoglobin levels in the blood, enhancing immunity, and improving the baby's resistance to illness. Drinking cool boiled water can also reduce lactic acid buildup in muscles, alleviating fatigue.

**Misconception 2: Using drinks to quench the baby's thirst**

Most babies prefer drinking beverages. Some babies even refuse to eat unless they are given a drink first, so mothers often keep various sweet drinks at home. However, these drinks contain high amounts of sugar but little nutrition. Not only do they fail to quench thirst, but they also easily create a feeling of fullness, affecting normal eating.

As a result, babies who frequently use drinks to quench their thirst often appear pale and underweight. In fact, nothing quenches thirst better for babies than plain water or water mixed with pure fruit juice. Drinks cannot truly satisfy thirst because the sugar in them triggers oxidation-reduction reactions in the body, consuming a certain amount of water. The result is often that the more drinks a baby consumes, the thirstier they feel, leading to excessive water intake.

**Misconception 3: Drinking beverages immediately after a meal**

Some babies ask for drinks right after finishing a meal, and if not given, they will cry incessantly. Sometimes, mothers give in out of annoyance. However, babies have weak gastrointestinal functions, and drinking large amounts of beverages, especially carbonated ones, immediately after a meal can cause bloating and, in severe cases, even lead to stomach rupture. This is because the stomach lining secretes more gastric acid after eating, and if carbonated drinks are consumed immediately, the sodium bicarbonate in the carbonated drinks reacts with the gastric acid, producing a large amount of carbon dioxide gas.

At this time, the stomach is already full of food, and both the upper and lower openings (the cardia and pylorus) are blocked, making it difficult for the carbon dioxide gas to escape. As a result, the gas accumulates in the stomach, causing bloating and discomfort. If it exceeds what the stomach can handle, it could lead to stomach rupture. Generally speaking, carbonated drinks should only be consumed when the stomach is empty or half-empty.
