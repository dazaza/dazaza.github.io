Domain Name Squatting: Infringement Incidents Increase Year by Year, Opportunities and Violations Go Hand in Hand

Source: Webmaster News 365 Space   March 1, 2012

Lin Shuhao quickly became famous in the American Basketball League, and a [Lin Storm] also arose on the internet. Many domestic enterprises and individuals rushed to register Lin Shuhao-related domain names, with 22 domains related to Lin Shuhao already being registered, including both Chinese and English versions. The [.cn domain] of [Lin Shuhao] was registered by two companies from Xiamen successively, and [www.linshuhao.com] was also openly listed for sale online.

"Once someone has gained reputation and influence through their own efforts, as a latecomer to the market, one should respect their market reputation rather than disrupt the rules or use another's reputation to seek improper gains." Associate Professor Cui Guobin from Tsinghua University Law School believes that using the inherent market value of a domain name is not inherently wrong, but malicious domain name squatting should be restricted.

Domain Name Infringement Cases Increasing Yearly

At the start of the Dragon Year, many hot cases related to infringement have unfolded, and domain name infringement has become an important part of this. Apple got involved in an iPad trademark infringement lawsuit in China, and taking this as a lesson, Facebook registered over 60 trademarks and domain names in China. Ctrip, Tongcheng, and Elong, three major travel websites, subsequently purchased double-pinyin domain names. Domain names have become a battleground for businesses.

Where there are business opportunities, people will act. Domain name squatting has clearly become a new tool for making money. In 2008, just after the international translation of Beijing Olympics mascots "Friendlies" was changed to "Fuwa," sensitive domain investors immediately registered related domain names and auctioned them off at high prices on Taobao, China’s largest shopping website.

Linghu Changhai, a doctoral candidate in automation at Tsinghua University, feels that domain names are now crucial resources, and squatters mainly see the value in advertising promotion, waiting for the most desperate bidder to pay top dollar for the domain name.

Registering a domain name is actually not complicated. You just need to register an account on a company's website that assists users in applying, input the contact information, and make payment to obtain the right to use a domain name. When I opened the domain registration and application website [Wanwang], its homepage prominently displayed the price quotes for domain names. Domain names with the suffix [.cn] are priced at 99 yuan per year, while more common [.com] and [.net] domain names are priced at 139 yuan per year.

In 2010, the World Intellectual Property Organization Arbitration and Mediation Center (WIPO Center) received 2,696 cases of domain name squatting, involving a total of 4,370 domain names, an increase of 28% compared to 2009 and 16% compared to 2008. From the data, it is clear that arbitration cases related to domain name squatting are increasing rapidly.

According to Liu Honghui, a lawyer specializing in intellectual property, domain name squatting is not limited to China or specific countries and regions but occurs worldwide, often involving celebrities, well-known brands, representative terms, etc. Therefore, international cooperation and coordination among organizations are strengthening. Currently influential international arbitration bodies like the WIPO Arbitration and Mediation Center generally have strong credibility and are recognized globally. At the same time, the Uniform Domain-Name Dispute-Resolution Policy (UDRP) formulated by this organization standardizes the criteria for recognizing such arbitration cases.

Based on statistics from the China International Economic and Trade Arbitration Commission, out of 1,383 [.cn] and Chinese domain disputes handled, 1,191 resulted in the arbitration decision of "transferring the domain name," meaning the failure rate for malicious squatting of domain names where others have legitimate rights approaches 90%.

Malicious Domain Name Squatting Can Be Illegal

Domain name squatting behavior can be divided into domain name investment and malicious squatting. Liu Honghui believes that malicious squatting refers to acts aimed at profit or other purposes, using improper means to preemptively register trademarks, domain names, or trade names that others have already used and have some influence in the field or related fields, which constitutes an infringement of intellectual property rights.

Domain name investment involves registering trademarks based on one's judgment of the market, waiting for them to appreciate, and then transferring them to earn the difference. Liu Honghui stated: "This kind of investment also carries risks, meaning they may appreciate, or they may not. Registration requires spending a certain amount of funds, consistent with general investment rules, so it is permissible."

Undergraduate student Xu Liang believes that regarding domain name squatting, one should still talk business. "Squatting on domain names indicates that others have seen the business opportunities brought by the future network era. Network resources can be utilized not only in e-commerce or network service charges but also extend beyond traditional concepts of goods or services, enriching the connotation of network resources and commercial activities."

The method of squatting on domain names can be divided into two types, Liu Honghui introduced. One is when the domain name has not been registered, discovering its potential value and pre-registering it, waiting for its appreciation, which conforms to the general rule of first-come, first-served in domain name registration; the other is waiting for the domain name to expire and then squatting on it before it gets renewed, though the success rate is small, there is still a chance for success.

Liu Honghui indicated that the first type reflects individual investment insight, and as long as it does not harm others' rights, "I think whether from a moral or legal perspective, it is blameless. If it involves others' rights, such as the right to one's name or reputation, legally speaking, relevant legal responsibilities may be pursued, and morally, it is a despised behavior." For the second type, if registration succeeds, morally speaking, "it is a petty act, exploiting loopholes and taking advantage of others' misfortunes." Legally speaking, if it infringes on others' prior rights or similarly infringes on their right to their name, reputation, trademark, etc., it is illegal. Liu Honghui reminded consumers that they can reduce the probability of being squatted on by extending renewal periods, and detailed regulations concerning malicious squatting have already been stipulated in the "China Internet Network Information Center Domain Name Dispute Resolution Measures."

Cui Guobin believes that obtaining a domain name itself is a market behavior, and using a domain name to promote products or find business opportunities is normal. However, attempting to squat on personal names, company names, trademarks, etc., would involve legal issues. First, it could violate the right to one's name, according to relevant provisions of the General Principles of Civil Law, everyone has exclusive rights to their own name, so it cannot be used for related commercial activities without permission; second, for a company, its enterprise name is also protected by law, using the enterprise name for related commercial activities could easily infringe on the enterprise name rights; third, using a domain name to promote related products could potentially infringe on trademark rights.

Individuals Should Have Awareness of Domain Name Protection

In Liu Honghui's view, relevant institutions should strengthen the review of certain domain names with special meanings, such as the names of celebrities, scenic spots, or other words or numbers with special meanings, and restrict domain name applications that infringe on others' rights to their name or reputation during the review process.

He stated: "We can refer to other intellectual property norms to improve laws and regulations related to domain name registration. This kind of intellectual property appeared later than trademark rights and copyrights, but within the scope of intellectual property, there are many similarities. Relatively speaking, legislation on trademarks, etc., is already relatively complete, and some beneficial provisions can be referenced."

Cui Guobin believes that under current laws and regulations, explicitly defining the rights to the names of celebrities, enterprise names, and trademarks, once infringement occurs, if the court can effectively rule that the infringement is established and require the infringer to provide sufficient compensation, it will make future potential infringers feel that there is no profit in it, thereby reducing the number of infringement behaviors.

"It's not that we don't have these rules, but people must take these rules seriously," Cui Guobin believes that we must seriously treat the existing rights of others and provide very adequate protection, not just for fair resolution of individual cases, but more importantly, to give the whole society a clear expectation.

Liu Honghui believes that everyone, especially well-known figures, should learn to protect their domain names. First, they should renew domain names in continuous use on time to avoid being exploited by others. Second, in cases of malicious squatting, evidence should be preserved, and legal means should be used to protect their own rights.
