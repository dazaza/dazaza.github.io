The richest tech company

by yinyin on 2008-10-17 09:04:39

Where did the debt crisis in Silicon Valley, U.S. come from? The impact of the financial crisis is inevitable, but apart from the possible reduction in product sales of mobile operators Alltel and Nextel due to the economic cold wave, the freezing of the credit market has little direct impact on the business of IT companies. This is because the leaders of the IT industry have always liked to hoard huge amounts of cash to prevent market fluctuations from impacting cash flow. During the golden period, active shareholders complained endlessly about their stinginess, but now, IT companies that hoarded cash in the past are better off, creating conditions for raising the banner of acquisition high. Microsoft was once a company that went against the wishes of its shareholders. When Bill Gates was in charge of this company, he was worried about unfavorable market changes and liked to keep an amount of cash equivalent to one year's expenditure at hand, not withdrawing it from the bank as needed. Nowadays, Microsoft no longer controls cash so strictly; they distribute part of it as dividends to shareholders, giving shareholders $32 billion in returns over the years. Fortunately, Microsoft failed to successfully acquire Yahoo! for $44 billion, otherwise, with the financial markets now in turmoil, this deal might have plunged Microsoft into a situation of lacking cash and being burdened with heavy debts. Even so, Microsoft's balance sheet is no longer the best among high-tech companies. Which companies have a firm grip on cash? Valleywag ranked the top ten richest technology companies in the world based on data from Yahoo Finance. 1. China Mobile Cash: $31 billion The oil, steel, and financial giants of China are all investing overseas. As China's leading telecommunications operator, why doesn't China Mobile follow suit? 2. Cisco Cash: $26.2 billion Cisco is extremely proud of its current cash reserves, and its investor relations director even talked about this point in a blog. However, investors have no confidence in Cisco's strange move to acquire social networks, after all, social websites have no connection with Cisco's well-performing core business of telecommunications equipment production. A reminder to Cisco's M&A team: don't try to bring social networks under your wing just because of the word "network." 3. Microsoft Cash: $21.2 billion At the beginning of this year, Microsoft planned to make a big acquisition of Yahoo! for $44 billion, offering a solution of half cash and half stock. Once this deal went through, it might have exhausted all of Microsoft's cash reserves and left it in debt. In hindsight, the failure of the deal was indeed very fortunate for Microsoft. 4. Apple Cash: $20.7 billion In the 1990s, Apple almost spent all of its cash. From the current situation, Apple will not face such danger in the short term. Although Apple has never carried out large-scale acquisitions and has always been low-key, when the right opportunity comes, Apple may change this habit. 5. Google Cash: $12.7 billion Google's stock has recently started to soften, which may mean that Google will use more cash than stocks for acquisitions. 6. Intel Cash: $12 billion Intel's chip factories require billions of dollars in investment; analysts estimate that Intel will use the cash for chip production investment rather than for acquiring small tech companies. 7. Nokia Cash: $10.8 billion Like Cisco, Nokia also wants to enter the internet field, and this company is particularly interested in blogs and "life casting" (lifecasting). Nokia may raise the banner of acquisition high and acquire Six Apart, Automattic or Tumblr. 8. Dell Cash: $9 billion If it successfully sells its PC factory, Dell will have more cash in hand. As HP continues to seize market share from it, the outside world believes that Dell might take this measure. Dell's acquisition target is software and related service companies. 9. Motorola Cash: $7.2 billion Before solving the challenges faced by its mobile phone business, Motorola will find it difficult to carry out large-scale acquisitions externally. 10. TSMC Cash: $7 billion AMD's market value is only $2.6 billion, and TSMC has been producing chips for AMD. Since the cash far exceeds AMD, why not acquire it?
