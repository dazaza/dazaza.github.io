Related reading: The US is considering launching free wireless broadband services. A US federal investigation report shows that 65% of Americans use broadband internet. The US Department of Commerce reports that the broadband penetration rate in American households has reached 63.5%. Currently, the coverage of the Internet in the US continues to expand, providing services to the public ranging from phone calls to movies, music, and banking.

Analysts point out that to achieve the mid-term goal of an average internet speed of 50 megabits per second for American households, companies like AT&T and Qwest need to invest more funds in network construction. Analyst Christopher King of Stifel Nicolaus said, "The next five years will be particularly crucial. Efforts must begin now to achieve this goal." Some analysts also believe that unless the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) provides broadcast companies with more auction revenue sharing, these companies will not be willing to easily give up their idle bands.

Sina Technology reported on the morning of March 16th that according to foreign media reports, the US Federal Communications Commission (FCC) released the 'US Broadband Internet Plan' (Connecting America: The National Broadband Plan) on Monday. This broadband upgrade plan focuses on increasing internet speed, emphasizes expanding network coverage, and plans to allocate more bands for mobile services.

The plan proposes to provide 100 million American households with broadband speeds of 100 megabits per second by 2020; this speed means that consumers can download a high-definition movie lasting one or two hours in less than 10 minutes. To achieve this goal, the plan proposes to provide 100 million American households with speeds of 50 megabits per second by 2015.

The FCC stated that it is seeking congressional approval to expand its authority to conduct wireless band auctions, encouraging broadcasters to transfer some bands to mobile providers. The agency claims that if broadcasters are unwilling to give up idle bands, it does not rule out the possibility of taking compulsory measures.

Some contents of this plan have been disclosed over the past few weeks. The FCC will hold a meeting on Tuesday, where the five commissioners will vote on the plan and officially submit it to the US Congress. Congress previously required the FCC to propose recommended measures regarding the current state of broadband internet access in the US.

The plan also emphasizes the need to allocate more bands for the foreseeable rapid growth of smart mobile devices; these devices can make calls, send emails, play movies, and TV shows.

Currently, the average internet speed in American households is about 4 megabits per second. Households with fiber-to-the-home connections have an average speed of about 5-6 megabits per second.

FCC Chairman Julius Genachowski said on Friday that many broadcasters welcome his win-win plan for band reconfiguration because they can gain more benefits from it.

The US House Committee on Energy and Trade will hold a hearing on this plan on March 25th. Chairman of the House Committee on Energy and Trade, Henry Waxman, stated, "Broadband is the greatest infrastructure challenge we face in the early part of the 21st century."

The FCC plans to reallocate more bands from broadcasters such as CBS and Sinclair, which will boost mobile providers like AT&T and Verizon Wireless. Additionally, upgrading wireless and fixed-line networks will drive equipment manufacturers like Alcatel-Lucent, wireless chip manufacturers like Qualcomm, and fiber optic equipment providers like Corning.
