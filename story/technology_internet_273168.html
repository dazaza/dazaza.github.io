8 Key Metrics for Website Operation and Planning

by fantexi2009 on 2009-11-23 14:01:08

The purpose of website planning is for operation. Taking a B2C website as an example, in addition to planning the front-end pages of the website, planning the back-end system is also indispensable (such as the product release module; some large websites may also have order processing backends, settlement processing backends, etc.). We must first specify the applicable roles for key metrics. The role of operating the backend undoubtedly includes data entry clerks, order processors, and settlement personnel.

For them, their key metrics are: improving efficiency and reducing error rates!

How to improve efficiency while reducing error rates becomes a key indicator and principle of the website operation planning that I am referring to:

**Principle 1:** If something can be done on one page, it should not be implemented on two pages!

This involves the issue of forms. Typically, our principle in designing the front-end is to make users fill out as little content as possible to obtain the most satisfactory information. However, when designing the back-end, this principle is reversed. Every extra step of saving or page jumping results in time loss and increases the likelihood of errors! Especially during product maintenance, if maintaining a piece of data requires navigating through multiple steps just to modify a single field, how can we ensure efficiency?

**Principle 2:** If possible, never forget batch operations!

For customers, batch operations undoubtedly increase the learning cost of using the system. However, in back-end system design, our data operators should have received system training, so the learning cost is not an issue. Batch operations for products significantly reduce the time cost for operators.

**Principle 3:** Form design should align more closely with actual business needs!

When designing forms, we often focus on the overall structure or logical flow, but we must not overlook user habits, operational norms, and procedural rules. Our form design should be more aligned with actual use cases. For instance, data entry personnel usually input data based on files provided by product managers. Therefore, our form design should try to stay consistent with these files, which will greatly reduce the time spent on judgment during data entry, thereby increasing work efficiency and reducing error rates to a certain extent.

**Principle 4:** If the system can do it, don’t rely on human brains!

This mainly applies to functional design. Many functions can be designed to automatically retrieve or calculate data via the system instead of relying on human cognition, which will significantly improve work efficiency.

**Principle 5:** System design must fit practical needs and avoid increasing complexity!

Many systems cannot achieve fully automated operations and still require manual offline operations. In such cases, we need to judge whether our system design hinders offline work, ultimately affecting the efficiency of actual operations.

**Principle 6:** Back-end design also needs to be aesthetically pleasing!

Many developers often think that as long as the back-end works, it doesn’t matter if it’s ugly since only internal staff will use it. However, our back-end operators spend over 60% of their time using the back-end. If they frequently use an extremely unattractive back-end, not only will their work efficiency be hard to maintain, but over time, their work mindset may change, which could negatively impact all business operations.

**Principle 7:** Place critical buttons carefully and differentiate them clearly!

This principle mainly applies to reducing error rates. Errors often occur during deletion or batch saving, where higher error rates are common. Therefore, it's necessary to identify positions and functions prone to errors, consider whether their placement is reasonable, whether reminder messages are complete, whether color differentiation is possible, and gradually optimize accordingly.

**Principle 8:** Don’t forget about information, interaction, and feedback in back-end design!

Reasonable function design and user-friendly design can greatly enhance both efficiency and reduce error rates. Small design improvements can sometimes have extraordinary effects.

The purpose of a company building a website is to make money. The front-end focuses on how to build a customer base and increase sales volume, while the back-end or operational platform emphasizes reducing costs. Both are equally important for a business!
