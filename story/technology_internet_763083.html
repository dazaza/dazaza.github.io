ENGAGE: Learning computing through games

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 14:03:15

When it comes to computer games, we often think of them as ways to pass the time, seek thrills, provide entertainment or even cause addiction; rarely do we associate them with academic progress, self-education, gaining knowledge, or improving one's quality. In fact, from a broader perspective, games can absolutely serve as an excellent educational tool. Currently, there have been some positive attempts in this area. For example, some teachers at the University of California designed a game called CodeSpells to teach the Java language, achieving good results.

Now, two professors from the University of North Carolina also want to use games to teach computer knowledge. Unlike CodeSpells, they are not aiming to teach specific programming languages but rather important concepts in computer science.

According to Wired, the game is called ENGAGE. It is set in a research facility that has been taken over by "evil scientists." Players assume the role of a good engineer tasked with restoring the network of the research station and defeating the bad scientists. During this process, players need to solve many computer science problems, such as encryption, data analysis, converting code into text images, etc. The game has two modes: one is a first-person 3D environment, and the other mode is a visual programming environment.

To test whether the game is effective, the two professors conducted an experiment at a high school. The experiment adopted a "pair programming" method, meaning that when playing the game, two people were required to cooperate, with one person using the game controller to execute commands and the other providing feedback. The reason for adopting this method was because they wanted to emphasize the importance of cooperation.

Professor Kristy Boyer said that companies frequently tell her that computer science students lack collaborative skills, so she wanted to focus on collaboration early in the experiment. Additionally, students tend to be more engaged when working together.

Since this experiment was part of an elective course in the school and targeted students already interested in computers, the results are not representative. However, Professor Boyer said that they plan to expand its reach to a wider audience in the future.

For this project, Stephen Foster, one of the developers of CodeSpells, expressed interest but does not fully agree with the game. "One concern is that it makes players take on the role of a computer scientist, which may not appeal to certain groups. Historically, women and minorities have not been as fascinated by computer science as white males," he noted.

Kristy Boyer insists that their decision is correct. She said that they worked hard to make the game appealing to everyone. Furthermore, she believes that girls will also find interest in the characters in the game, and this could change the entrenched belief that computer science is only suitable for boys.
