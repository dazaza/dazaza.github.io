Epidemic Fashion Disease: Knowledge Anxiety Syndrome

Recently, many normally educated adults aged between 25 and 40 have suddenly developed a strange disease: without any pathological changes or organic alterations, they will suddenly experience nausea, vomiting, irritability, neurasthenia, mental fatigue, etc. Women may also suffer from gynecological diseases such as amenorrhea and dysmenorrhea. The intervals and onset times of the disease are not fixed. Relevant experts believe that this is a psychosomatic disorder, unofficially named: Information Anxiety Syndrome.

Information Anxiety Syndrome, also known as Knowledge Anxiety Syndrome. The concept was first proposed by Professor Sun Peter from the Department of Medicine at the Chinese University of Hong Kong. He found that in the information explosion era, people's absorption of information grows exponentially, but human thinking patterns have yet to adjust well enough to accept such large amounts of information, thus causing a series of self-compulsions and tensions, very similar to anxiety symptoms in psychiatry. Professor Sun calls it: Information Anxiety Syndrome.

As the name suggests, Information Anxiety Syndrome refers to anxiety symptoms caused by excessive absorption of information. Excessive absorption of information is not usually an active awareness; in most cases, it is a passive behavior. In daily life, people who continuously watch TV or listen to the radio every day, and those who spend their days in libraries or online researching materials, are prone to develop anxiety. Professionally speaking, reporters, advertisers, information officers, website administrators, intelligence personnel, listeners, spies, sonar operators, etc., are high-risk groups for this syndrome.

In fact, Information Anxiety Syndrome itself is not scary and does not need to be worried about turning into a mental illness. As long as you are aware of its cause and treat it correctly, it is possible to effectively alleviate anxiety symptoms.

Treatment prescription: Prozac tablets, twice a day, one tablet each time; Vitamin C tablets, three times a day, one tablet each time; Oryzanol tablets, three times a day, one tablet each time; Ensure 9 hours of sleep every day; List daily work plans and try to minimize unexpected situations; Do not use more than two media sources for information intake each day; Exercise for 15 minutes before bed each day; Drink plenty of water, exceeding 3000 milliliters per day; Live a regular life, reduce entertainment, and strictly prohibit alcohol consumption.

Who is prone to neurosis?

We always like to describe someone's personality as either introverted or extroverted. So which type of personality is more prone to neurosis? To find the answer, we must start with what introversion and extroversion mean.

1. Introverted and Extroverted Personality

Introverted personality refers to a person having a strong tendency for self-reflection and self-criticism. Such people are meticulous and cautious, enjoy fantasizing, easily get lost in deep thought, are indecisive, often missing out on rare opportunities for success. Due to their strong self-reflective nature, they easily discover their own flaws and weaknesses, frequently lacking confidence. Some people are like self-measuring devices, constantly detecting any physical or mental weaknesses or diseases. Therefore, once overly cautious and focused only on self-preservation, they can easily become a breeding ground for neurosis.

Extroverted personalities, on the other hand, have a strong tendency towards active engagement with external objective matters, rarely engaging in self-reflection, with fewer worries and less self-inflicted distress. They act swiftly and decisively, quickly implementing ideas to avoid trouble, sometimes even rashly and recklessly. They are usually helpful, ambitious, love being in the spotlight, and enjoy talking. It is generally believed that people of this type are less likely to suffer from neurosis. However, they may develop neurosis due to career failure, overwork, or feeling unwell. Once afflicted with neurosis, their previous extroverted personality may transform into an introverted one.

That is to say, once neurosis develops, regardless of the original personality, an introverted aspect will manifest.

However, neurotic individuals are not simply introverts. On the other side of their introversion, they possess a strong desire for self-development, ambition, and a hard-to-give-up rebellious psychology. Because of these conflicting aspects, they are prone to fall into a state of mental conflict.

2. Theoretical and Conscious Tendency

Due to their introverted nature, they tend to rationally and consciously analyze their own physical and mental phenomena, searching for causes and easily becoming dissatisfied with themselves. From academic performance, patients' thoughts are scattered, mostly above average, with fewer being below average. In interpersonal relationships, neurotic individuals are more conscious of their clumsy behavior, stiff expressions, trembling lips, and flushed faces, thus becoming more reserved.

3. Strong Desire for Perfection

Another aspect of introversion is a strong desire for self-development. And because it is rational and conscious, they set high standards for their physical and mental states and work. They often view trivial flaws and weaknesses as major issues and feel distressed. They are usually serious, detailed, and honest in their work, but if taken too far, they become perfectionists, criticizing everything and striving for perfection, leading to endless distress.

4. Sensitive Perception

Neurotic individuals have more sensitive perceptions than ordinary people. They are more likely to feel stronger unease and pain. Once fixated on something, their sensitivity increases, making them feel even more pain. A small story illustrates this point: Two old friends from school who hadn't seen each other for years met on the street but didn't greet each other. The extroverted person thought it might be because they hadn't seen each other in years and were hesitant to recognize each other; the introverted person thought the other had become arrogant and indifferent, vowing never to speak to them again until the other called first, realizing they had been too sensitive.

As described above, the personality of neurotic individuals is complex. They often process received information in various artificial ways, making it easy to trigger various neurotic symptoms. For example, they may artificially perceive minor physical discomfort as a disease state, and this subjective assumption easily leads them to desperately want to eliminate the discomfort, instead falling into a strong internal conflict.

According to Morita and his disciple Kora's theory, the four main characteristics of neurosis are anxiety, stubbornness, self-reflection, and strong desire. Related traits include caution, detail-oriented, patience, thoroughness, sensitivity, seriousness, and honesty. These traits both contribute to the occurrence and intensification of neurosis symptoms and help overcome them, having both hindering and beneficial effects on social life.
