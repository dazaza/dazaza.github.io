YotaPhone's vision for transforming the mobile experience with its dual-screen smartphone.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 15:39:25

We once reported on an unusual mobile phone manufacturer: Yota, a company from Moscow, designed a dual-screen smartphone called YotaPhone. One side of the YotaPhone is an LCD screen and the other side is an E-ink screen, which is the first of its kind in the history of smartphone design. Thanks to its unique design, the YotaPhone recently won the Innovation Lion Award at the Cannes Advertising Festival.

In an era of homogenized smartphone competition, the dual-screen design brings differentiated competition. However, as a consumer product, Yota must make consumers understand the philosophy behind its design, which is not easy. At the same time, it has to bear the pressure of increased costs and unpredictable potential risks.

Yota's CEO, Vlad Martynov, shared with the foreign media Fastcocreat the ideas and concepts behind this phone. It started several years ago during a brainstorming session within the team, aimed at solving unpleasant experiences associated with using a phone. They realized that technology had changed human behavior and the way we communicate. However, the result was that people became less empathetic, lacked emotional connection, and even engaged in less social interaction, despite spending a large amount of time on social media.

After the advent of smartphones, people's daily focus is often distracted by their phones to check for notifications. Martynov obtained data showing that people look at their phones on average 150 times a day, what people commonly refer to as "phone anxiety syndrome".

"Technological progress has changed our communication methods, but it has led us down the wrong path," Martynov said. He believes that the YotaPhone can alleviate this issue.

The two screens of the YotaPhone have separate functions: the LCD screen is used for displaying videos and dynamic content, while the E-ink screen is used for reading, browsing pictures, and showcasing information. Content from the LCD screen can be swiped over to the E-ink screen with two fingers. A major function of the E-ink screen is serving as a notification center. Most of the time, you don't need to open your phone to check content; the E-ink screen will display the latest messages in real-time. Since the E-ink screen doesn't consume much power, it stays illuminated continuously, allowing you to just glance at the E-ink screen to see if there are any new messages.

The E-ink screen can display customized messages such as photo albums, emails, calendars, news, or social media, all updated in real-time. You can also use it to showcase some content, just like using a piece of paper, without worrying about the screen turning black.

After the phone hits the market, YotaPhone will open up the dual-screen API to developers, and Martynov finds this very interesting. He envisions that the content of some applications can be visually presented in images on the E-ink screen, which is more attractive than text alone, and you don't need to search within the app. It's right there on the back, just like the advertisements displayed on a Kindle.

Martynov firmly believes that the YotaPhone will change the user experience of smartphones.

The biggest change brought by the YotaPhone is that it has a dynamic yet extremely power-efficient display board, which eliminates many operations needed to check the phone, providing a simpler and more convenient way of operation. However, the risk lies in whether users would be willing to pay for these simple conveniences, and endure the thicker and more expensive phone?

This question will be answered after the product launches. The YotaPhone will be available in Russia in the fourth quarter of this year, and in other regions somewhat later. Its initially announced price was $500, but now the price will be reduced by 10%, making it $450. Would you be willing to pay for such an "innovation"?
