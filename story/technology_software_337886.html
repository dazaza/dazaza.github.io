The Path of Bank Sales in 2005, the "land grab" by foreign capital in China's banking sector reached its peak. During this period, whether it was the commercial banks in second-tier cities in central and western regions, nationwide joint-stock listed banks, or even state-owned giants critical to the livelihood and safety of the people, all had foreign investors appearing on their shareholder lists. In fact, this was just one scene in a series of reforms in China's banking industry, including prior不良 asset cleanups (including government-funded buyouts), government capital injections, and shareholding system transformations. Behind accusations of "selling out cheaply" and "signing unequal treaties," we also see that the entry of foreign capital not only solved the capital hunger of Chinese banks but also introduced modern management experience. Moreover, while enhancing the international reputation of Chinese banks, it brought more added value. It can be said that in this intimate contact between Chinese and foreign financial institutions, none of the parties—foreign investors, fundraising banks, regulators—are losers; otherwise, agreements would not have been reached. As described by Liu Mingkang, chairman of the China Banking Regulatory Commission: "Life does not need to be perfect to be beautiful. Now is a good time to buy bank shares, cooperate with regulators, and reap profits." Regulatory Approval The enthusiasm for selling domestic banks in recent years has been closely related to the active support of banking regulatory authorities. Regulatory authorities have always welcomed and encouraged foreign strategic investors to purchase stakes in domestic banks. Before the establishment of the CBRC, only three Chinese commercial banks sold equity to foreign institutions. After the establishment of the CBRC, the market access supervision strategy for foreign banks was promptly adjusted, shifting the focus of opening up from mainly introducing foreign banks to set up branches in China to promoting stronger business collaboration and equity cooperation between Chinese and foreign banks. On December 8, 2003, the CBRC issued the "Measures for the Administration of Investment by Foreign Financial Institutions in Domestic Financial Institutions." These measures raised the maximum proportion of equity in domestic financial institutions that could be purchased by a single foreign financial institution from 15% to no more than 20%. It also stipulated that if multiple foreign financial institutions collectively purchase more than 25% of the equity in a non-listed domestic financial institution, that institution will be supervised as a foreign financial institution. If the collective equity purchase exceeds 25% in a listed domestic financial institution, it will still be supervised as a domestic financial institution. This administration method paved the way for transactions such as Newbridge Capital purchasing 17.89% of Shenzhen Development Bank’s shares, Hang Seng Bank and others purchasing Xingye Bank's shares, and HSBC purchasing shares in the Bank of Communications. In February 2004, the CBRC put forward six requirements for newly established commercial banks, one of which was “the initiating shareholders should include qualified foreign strategic investors.” Under this requirement, Bohai Bank, newly approved for establishment, became the first joint-stock commercial bank in China to introduce foreign strategic investors at the initiation stage. On September 6, 2005, Standard Chartered Bank invested $1.23 billion in cash to acquire 19.99% of Bohai Bank’s shares, becoming the second largest shareholder. Facing concerns from many people about the impact on financial security due to the sale of state-owned bank shares to foreign institutions, at the beginning of March 2005, before the introduction of strategic investors by state-owned banks, the CBRC’s "Corporate Governance Reform" research group pointed out in an article titled "Improving Corporate Governance is the Core of State-Owned Commercial Bank Reform": comprehensively considering, during the introduction of strategic investors, the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China (ICBC) and the Construction Bank of China (CBC) should adhere to four principles: long-term holding principle; governance optimization principle; business cooperation principle; competition avoidance principle. This principle laid the groundwork for subsequent introductions of foreign strategic investors by CBC, ICBC, and other banks. Currently, 20% is the upper limit for a single foreign institution to hold shares in a domestic bank, but this policy ceiling may be broken. Recently, Yan Qingmin, director of the first department of the CBRC, stated that although the boundary of 51% Chinese capital control will not change, in the future, the shareholding ratio of a single foreign strategic investor may exceed 20%. This will pave the way for further foreign participation in domestic banks. "Capital" Needs The reason why regulatory policies actively encourage foreign institutions to purchase domestic bank shares and why domestic banks are willing to sell shares to foreign institutions lies in the strong demand of domestic banks to obtain capital injection from foreign institutions to replenish capital. After the establishment of the CBRC, banks were subject to the regulatory requirement of "five-level loan classification mm adequate loss provisions mm capital adequacy rate compliance". Capital adequacy rate, non-performing loan rate, and provision coverage rate became the three major indicators for the CBRC to evaluate banks. In February 2004, the CBRC issued the "Measures for the Management of Capital Adequacy Ratio of Commercial Banks", imposing hard constraints on the capital adequacy ratio of commercial banks. According to these measures, the capital adequacy ratio of commercial banks must not be less than 8%, and the core capital adequacy ratio must not be less than 4%. Ma Weihua, president of Minsheng Bank, calculated that to meet regulatory requirements, domestic banks face a funding gap of several hundred billion yuan in terms of capital replenishment. With the continued instability of the domestic A-share market, relying on domestic market financing faces many difficulties. However, foreign investment in domestic banks means they will directly inject real money into domestic banks, which for domestically undercapitalized banks is like coal in a snowstorm. So far, the funds provided and soon to be provided by foreign strategic investors have reached nearly 200 billion US dollars. In fact, Shanghai Bank, Xingye Bank, and others rely on the capital injection from foreign strategic investors to maintain capital adequacy ratios while rapidly expanding their asset scale. Is it enough to make the Chinese banking industry come back to life and compete with foreign peers in a more open market by simply replenishing capital to meet the capital adequacy ratio? The answer is no. According to the relevant leaders of the regulatory authority, since China can invest hundreds of billions of US dollars in recapitalizing state-owned banks, it proves that what the Chinese banking industry lacks most is not money or the funds invested by foreign strategic investors. What the Chinese banking industry needs most is the experience and technology in risk management, internal control, product development, etc., brought by foreign strategic investors. Facing the intense competition environment after the full liberalization of the Chinese banking industry, domestic banks can only compete with foreign banks if they make significant progress in these areas. And these experiences and technologies are scarce in the domestic banking industry, mostly held by foreign institutions, and these technologies cannot be obtained free of charge from foreign institutions. By selling part of the equity to foreign strategic investors, it is possible to obtain these urgently needed experiences and technologies by domestic banks. "In this sense, this is similar to the 'market-for-technology' approach proposed in the early stages of China's reform and opening-up in industries like home appliances and automobiles," pointed out an insider. Chairman Liu Mingkang of the CBRC is particularly clear about the changes brought by the introduction of foreign strategic investors by domestic banks. On December 1, 2003, Liu Mingkang pointed out at a press conference held by the State Council Information Office that after introducing foreign strategic investors, Shanghai Bank, Pudong Development Bank, and Nanjing Bank were undergoing positive changes: At Shanghai Bank, foreign shareholders played a role through various technical aids and dispatching directors, making significant progress in improving governance structure, introducing advanced management concepts and technologies, and strengthening internal controls; After absorbing Citibank's 4.6% strategic stake, Pudong Development Bank actively introduced Citibank's technology and management in developing credit card businesses, especially in importing experience and technology in risk management, financial management, marketing, and technical support; After IFC invested in Nanjing Bank, it made numerous suggestions on improving Nanjing Bank's governance structure, management system, loan structure, accounting system, etc. Many of these suggestions are already being implemented, playing an important role in promoting Nanjing Bank's improvement in management level. Therefore, from state-owned banks to joint-stock banks and then to city commercial banks, agreements signed with foreign strategic investors often stipulate that foreign investors will provide technical assistance in areas such as risk management, internal control, retail banking, credit cards, wealth management, etc., for the domestic banks they participate in. For state-owned banks, strategic cooperation is particularly important. On June 17, Bank of America purchased a stake in Construction Bank for $25 billion. As part of the entire transaction, Construction Bank and Bank of America also signed a strategic cooperation agreement, where Bank of America will provide strategic assistance to Construction Bank in many fields. These fields include corporate governance, risk management, information technology, financial management, human resource management, personal banking services (including credit cards), and global cash services, among others. On the morning of September 28, the strategic assistance project between Construction Bank and Bank of America was officially launched. Experts from Bank of America moved into Construction Bank to start fulfilling the strategic assistance agreement covering the main business and management areas of commercial banks. The significance of technical assistance from foreign strategic investors to domestic banks has been recognized by rating agencies. On September 28, Standard & Poor's upgraded the long-term foreign currency rating of Bank of Communications from "BB+" to "BBB-", with a stable outlook. At the same time, Standard & Poor's upgraded the bank's basic strength rating from "D+" to "C", reflecting Standard & Poor's expectation that Bank of Communications' operational performance will significantly improve in the short term. Zeng Yijing, director of Standard & Poor's Financial Services Ratings, told reporters: "The adjustment of Bank of Communications' rating mainly reflects that the closer alliance with the HSBC Group will likely enhance its performance and bring other benefits to the bank in the future. The bank's corporate governance, internal control, risk management capabilities, and management skills in retail banking will also benefit from this developing alliance." Dr. Hu Zuliù, managing director of Goldman Sachs (Asia), believes that in terms of supplementing capital, foreign capital is helpful, but not the only help; in terms of improving management, foreign capital is very important. If management authority matches the equity ratio, then the role of foreign capital will be very small, having only marginal effects without decisive ones. For foreign capital to exert more influence in management, it does not necessarily mean that the board must be completely controlled by foreign capital. As long as some key management positions are held by foreigners, foreign capital can truly play a role. Institutional Factors On September 17, Construction Bank announced that Bank of America and Temasek Holdings' wholly-owned subsidiary Asia Financial Holdings recently completed the first delivery of the shares of Construction Bank held by Huijin Company. Thus, the nature of ownership of Construction Bank has changed—from a state-owned sole proprietorship commercial bank to a state-controlled commercial bank with foreign investment. Subsequently, Bank of China and Industrial and Commercial Bank of China will also undergo such changes in ownership nature. From regulatory authorities to scholars in the domestic financial community, there is a consensus: the huge burden of non-performing loans accumulated by state-owned banks is closely related to the system of state-owned sole proprietorship banks. Dr. Zhang Chun, professor of finance at CEIBS, pointed out that decision-makers in China's banking industry have realized that without the entry of foreign strategic investors, it is difficult to change the game rules of the domestic banking industry. Only through the introduction of foreign strategic investors can true changes be brought about. Foreign investment in domestic banks will help domestic banks establish effective corporate governance, which is the biggest change brought by foreign investment recognized by regulatory authorities. After strategic investors participate in domestic banks, they often obtain one or more seats on the board of directors of that bank. These foreign-appointed directors will be responsible for the investments of foreign investors. Through their active exercise of directorial powers, they can effectively prevent administrative interventions by the government in the normal operations of banks, avoid directive loans and shareholder-related loans, and ensure that the board plays a true role through effective institutional design, making directors no longer mere "ornaments." This helps domestic banks truly establish effective incentive and restraint mechanisms, avoiding internal control issues, thus playing a positive role in helping domestic banks establish good corporate governance. This is the greatest significance of domestic banks selling equity to foreign strategic investors.
