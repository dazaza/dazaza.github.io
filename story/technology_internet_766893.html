Oracle Investors Unhappy About CEO's High Pay, a Rebellion May Happen in the Shareholders' Meeting

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 10:15:36

Oracle investors have increasingly become dissatisfied with the high compensation of the company's CEO Larry Ellison. At the annual general meeting held on October 31, this issue may become a focal point of discussion among Oracle shareholders.

Last Friday, a filing submitted by Oracle to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) revealed that due to Oracle's net profit failing to meet the company’s prior expectations and the abandonment of bonuses, although Larry Ellison's total compensation for the 2013 fiscal year decreased by 18% compared to the previous fiscal year, it still amounted to a staggering $78.4 million. Bloomberg's Billionaires Index data shows that Ellison, at the age of 69, has a personal net worth of $41.8 billion, ranking eighth on the list. Data collected by Bloomberg also indicates that Ellison is the highest-paid CEO in the U.S. this year.

Oracle's stock price increased by 12% over the past 12 months; however, after releasing financial results on June 21 showing flat revenue for the second consecutive quarter, the company's stock plummeted by 9% on that day. Although Ellison remains a major shareholder of Oracle, the executive compensation plan barely passed at the annual general meeting in 2011. In the 2012 shareholders' meeting, the company's executive compensation plan failed to gain shareholder approval. Private equity firm Blackstone Group, which is an Oracle shareholder, even voted against the re-election of five Oracle directors, including members of the Compensation Committee.

On Wednesday, investment company CtW Investment Group sent a letter to Bruce R. Chizen, chairman of Oracle's Board Compensation Committee, stating that if Oracle does not impose restrictions on executive equity incentives, the company will clearly oppose Oracle's executive compensation plan at this year's general meeting and seek to remove the Compensation Committee director, replacing them with new external directors to oversee this issue.

As of now, Oracle has not commented on this report. In the filing submitted last week, Oracle's directors expressed disappointment over the shareholders' vote opposing last year's executive compensation plan. However, the document stated, "The Oracle board decided to make significant adjustments to the executive compensation program." The document also mentioned, "Because Oracle's growth did not meet expectations, Ericsson received $1.2 million less in bonuses last year."

Oracle released its first-quarter financial report for the 2014 fiscal year last week. The report showed that Oracle's total revenue for the first quarter was $8.372 billion, up 2% from $8.181 billion in the same period last year; net profit was $2.191 billion, up 8% from $2.034 billion in the same period last year. Oracle's adjusted earnings per share exceeded Wall Street analysts' expectations. Since the beginning of the year, Oracle's stock price has risen by less than 2%, underperforming the S&P 500 index's rise of more than 18% during the same period. The sluggish performance of Oracle's stock price is partly due to investor disappointment with Oracle's financial data.
