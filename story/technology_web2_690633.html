SD legislative panel approves $4 billion state budget

by xiaojiaozygb on 2012-03-05 11:17:31

Members of the Senate Republican majority attempted to allocate $2.1 million each to schools and Medicaid providers, but House Republicans blocked the move, arguing that the funds should be reserved in case the economy weakens. "The economy is improving, but it's far from being fully recovered," said House Appropriations Chairman Dean Wink, R-Howes. After disputes on some proposed changes to the budget bill, the Joint Appropriations Committee unanimously voted to pass it. The Senate and House planned to vote on the measure later Friday, which would conclude the main part of this year’s legislative session. The $4 billion budget will spend about $1.2 million in state general funds, $1.75 billion in federal funds, and $1 billion in other funds in the year starting July 1. Jason Dilges, budget director for Gov. Dennis Daugaard, said the state cut aid to school districts by $47 million last year as it tried to close a $127 million budget gap. Bills approved this year will give the schools back about $43.7 million, counting state aid, an increase in local property taxes, and funding for teachers' training, he said. The budget includes the governor’s plan to provide school districts with a 2.3 percent ongoing increase in state aid to account for inflation. A separate spending bill suggested by Daugaard gave schools another $4 million in one-time state aid and set aside $8.4 million for training teachers to use a new curriculum. The Legislature also independently approved a special spending measure that granted schools an additional $8.7 million one-time bonus. Lobbyists for education groups said they appreciate the extra money the Legislature has provided to school districts, but they stated that schools could have used the extra $2.1 million blocked Friday by the House members of the committee. "We're in a crisis when it comes to school district budgets," said Wade Pogany of the South Dakota Associated School Boards. Dilges also said the governor’s budget plan and other measures will give an extra $20.8 million to nursing homes and other facilities that provide care to poor people in the Medicaid program. However, representatives of some of those facilities said the unsuccessful attempt to give them another $2.1 million would have helped boost salaries and reduce a high turnover rate among their employees. The budget also includes Daugaard’s plan to give state employees, who have gone three years without a raise, a 3 percent across-the-board salary increase next year. At the governor’s urging, the Legislature also passed a separate bill giving state employees a one-time bonus this year equal to 5 percent of their annual salaries, with all employees who have been with the state for at least three years getting no less than $2,300 and no more than $7,500. The dispute among Republican House and Senate leaders began Friday with a disagreement on a bill that would have spent $1 million to start a scholarship program for students at South Dakota’s four technical institutes. The House killed the bill, which was favored by the Senate, but the dispute on the scholarship measure held up consideration of the budget for several hours. The Appropriations Committee later added $500,000 to the technical schools’ budgets without specifying how the money would be spent. The panel also agreed to spend over $400,000 to establish two new drunken driving courts and expand drug court programs, both intended to keep people out of prison while rehabilitating them. Another $450,000 was added to the budget to permit the Agricultural Experiment Station at South Dakota State University to conduct research for making fuel from sunflowers, other oil seeds, and some specialty crops. Sen. Larry Tidemann, R-Brookings, said the U.S. Navy plans to use the fuel in its airplanes and ships in a few years. Using a rule that allows House and Senate members of the panel to vote separately, the House members rejected a Senate proposal to add doctoral programs in physics at two state universities. Supporters argued that South Dakota needs to produce its own physicists with doctoral degrees to work in the national underground laboratory in the former Homestake Gold Mine in the Black Hills.
