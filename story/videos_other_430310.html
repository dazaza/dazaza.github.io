Suicide Forest

At the foot of Mount Fuji, the dense and eerie Aokigahara forest attracts dozens of special tourists each year. They come here not to enjoy the forest scenery but to end their lives. The British newspaper "The Independent" sent a reporter to follow local Japanese police and participate in the annual corpse retrieval activity, reporting on the matter in detail.

Autumn is the season for collecting corpses. Aokigahara is a natural forest. Wild mushrooms cover mossy caves, and the outline of Mount Fuji is faintly visible above the trees. On Friday, October 24th, as in the past 30 years, the Japanese began their annual search for suicides. Police, volunteers, and accompanying journalists all entered the forest.

The dark forest is mysterious and unpredictable. Uniformed team members call out to each other as they enter one after another. Indeed, it's a frightening jungle, with dense trees and a ground covered with decaying branches and leaves.

Compasses malfunction in the forest due to the magnetic effects of volcanic lava. Getting lost in these jungles is truly being lost. Some skeletons have been trampled by wild animals into pieces. It must have been several years. Those who come here to die must know that committing suicide here makes it hard for others to discover their remains.

The body collection team set off from the foot of Mount Fuji towards the Forest of Death. In less than 20 minutes, they found the first body just a few meters away from a narrow path. This is an eternal dawn scene in the sea of trees. It was raining, and he was kneeling on the ground, his face and arms pressed down, in a posture of pain and pleading. His hair was short, slightly gray. His shirt was clean, and there was a long cross-shaped cut on the right side of his neck, making it impossible to tell his age. Then, the police shouted, parted the crowd, started taking pictures of the deceased with cameras, put the body in a plastic bag and sealed it, using a metal cart to send it to a nearby parking lot. Before the body could be pushed into the ambulance, another body was found deep in the dense forest.

As the number of suicides increased, the scope of the body search also expanded. On the day of the investigation, 300 volunteer firefighters and 44 police officers gathered at the parking lot, most of them over 60 years old.

Boxes were nailed to the trees, containing notes left by the police asking people to wait a moment! Your life is a gift from your parents, don't keep troubles to yourself, please seek counseling. Local police also had a special patrol car in the forest. According to police figures, up to 48 people who intended to die have been saved. We sometimes receive letters saying thank you for saving me, now I will try to live. said the head of the local police.

This large-scale annual investigation started in 1970. For the first few years, the number of bodies collected remained stable at about 20, but ten years ago, this number greatly increased. In 1994, 57 bodies were found, and by 1999, it reached a record high of more than 70 bodies. This year, four bodies were found just on Friday alone. Leading this search was Seikata Kosan, who said that most of them were middle-aged adults. In previous years, no matter when or where, couples could be seen entering the forest together for love-based suicides, but this year there hasn't been a single case like that, nor has a teenager's body been found, which makes me quite happy.

Here, taxis only run one way. If you encounter a taxi driver here, they definitely have stories to tell. They will describe how passengers arrive at the forest like zombies or ghosts and never return. They always run one way, only delivering those who intend to die to the edge of the forest, and then it's impossible to pick up anyone else to go back.

If you report on the wonders of walking, the mystery of the forest sea, and its various beauties, then we will love the forest sea, said Kosan. People who like climbing mountains insist on doing so on the other side of Mount Fuji because they fear suddenly encountering a corpse in front of them. I've seen relatives of suicide victims come here to look for fathers or sons. I've witnessed the immense pain and heavy blows people suffer when they lose loved ones. I want people to understand that suicide is an ineffective act that only creates sorrow.

Most people who successfully commit suicide there do so by hanging themselves. A small portion use sleeping pills or poisoning, and in winter, some simply lie down in the snow to die.

Why do those who want to commit suicide favor this place? Apart from the practical reasons for concealment, the answer can be traced back a long time ago. In the 19th century, feudal Japan suffered severe famine, and Aokigahara was a place where poor families usually abandoned infants and dealt with elderly relatives. A writer named Matsumoto Seicho wrote a famous novel adapted into a film called "The Pagoda in the Waves," featuring a character who came here seeking death.

Another infamous book that sold well a few years ago, "The Suicide Manual," also recommended this place for suicide.

Among all Asian countries, it seems no country's people like to commit suicide as much as the Japanese do. In 1998, the number of Japanese suicides throughout the year exceeded the historical figure of 30,000 people, rising to 33,048 last year. About 12,000 children annually lose one parent to suicide. Among the suicides, 22,500 are men, mostly middle-aged. The image of suicides and the timing of their choices correlate directly with Japan's economic crisis, which began in the early 1990s when some companies were heavily impacted by restructuring, bankruptcy, or collapse.

To curb the trend of suicide, Tokyo subway stations have taken measures by placing mirrors along the platforms, intending to make those who contemplate suicide think twice upon seeing their own reflection. The Japanese Ministry of Labor and Welfare has requested more than two billion yen specifically to combat suicide. However, everyone knows that the only feasible method to reduce the suicide rate is to restore economic prosperity. Even during prosperous times, the number of Japanese people choosing suicide remains significantly higher compared to other countries.

Why do they commit suicide? This needs to be explained through various aspects of Japanese society: their cultural traditions and religious beliefs, their national character, and their social environment.

Let's start with their cultural traditions and religious beliefs. The Japanese are a homogeneous ethnic group who practice Shintoism. However, Shinto doctrine teaches that if you're unhappy in life, you can die and be reborn. Therefore, suicide in Japan isn't considered newsworthy. Sociologist Koyoko Ueno of Tokushima University points out that Japan has long been known as the "Land of Suicides." Social scientist Masahiro Yamada of Chuo University says: The Japanese do not view suicide as a crime but rather as a virtue. Although this is believed, the Japanese do not commit suicide at home; they choose beautiful places to do so, often tourist spots. Thus, beautiful lakesides have many signs advising against suicide, though not directly stated but rather subtly suggesting that with such beautiful scenery, is it really the end? I've seen relatives of suicide victims come here looking for fathers or sons. I've witnessed the immense pain and heavy impact people experience from losing loved ones. I want people to understand that suicide is a futile act that only causes grief.

Secondly, regarding their national character, the Japanese are very polite. Not only do they bow when meeting and bidding farewell, but they also continuously nod and bow during conversations. Statistics show that on average, a Japanese person bows more than 200 times a day. The Japanese are dignified in demeanor, nodding and bowing, even refraining from showing emotions openly. Their speech is humble and restrained, even meticulous. Even among family members, this politeness persists: parents say "excuse me" when visiting their children's homes and express gratitude for meals eaten there. Children treat their parents as guests. Excessive politeness can be seen positively as courtesy but negatively as hypocrisy. Due to this politeness, the Japanese rarely confide or communicate deeply with others, regardless of how close a friend they may be, whether Japanese or foreigner, or whether family members like spouses, parents, or siblings. They share nothing personal with others. Consequently, the Japanese endure significant stress, leading to a high suicide rate, affecting people from seventy-year-old men to seven-year-old children. In a country of over 100 million people, the annual number of suicides exceeds 30,000. One could say that suicide is part of Japanese culture, true both in ancient times and today. Japanese police statistics indicate nearly 34,000 Japanese committed suicide last year, the second-highest number historically.

Thirdly, prolonged economic sluggishness lasting a decade has left many middle-aged and older individuals disheartened. The emergence of suicide websites has provided repressed and fragile young people with companionship.

A decade of sluggish economic growth and changes in Japan's lifelong employment system have resulted in unemployment and financial distress for many middle-aged and older individuals. Among those who commit suicide in Japan, nearly 71% are men, with those aged forty and above accounting for over 73%. More than 57% are unemployed.

Masahiro Yamada says that for those who were once salaried workers but are now unemployed, suicide might be a rational decision. In Japan, if someone commits suicide, the beneficiary can still claim the deceased's life insurance money. If the breadwinner of the family dies, the insurance company will pay off their housing mortgage, even if the cause of death is suicide. Masahiro Yamada: If he dies, his family will get the money. If he continues living without finding work, they will lose their home.

The internet provides depressed young Japanese with suicide suggestions and connects them with others embarking on the same death journey. A few years ago, Japanese people committing suicide began gathering online. They drove to the outskirts, locked themselves in the rear compartments, and burned charcoal or coal briquettes to produce carbon monoxide gas for suicide. Juzo Tsunoda, a 43-year-old anti-suicide activist, says: They really want to interact with others, they go together to commit suicide.

Tsunoda was once an alcoholic and drug addict and attempted suicide ten times before the age of thirty. He claims the current wave of suicides is scarier than ever before. Hydrogen sulfide gas is dangerous not only for those who want to commit suicide but also for bystanders. The gas can infiltrate nearby apartment buildings and structures. In April this year, a 14-year-old Japanese girl used hydrogen sulfide gas to commit suicide in Kochi Prefecture, southern Japan. The gas injured 80 Japanese people in the building, and another 120 had to evacuate urgently. The police demanded that internet service providers block websites promoting suicide. This year, 517 Japanese people have used deadly gases made from common household chemicals to commit suicide, often harming innocent bystanders.

Geisha

Geishas are representatives of Japanese culture, but many ordinary people have misconceptions about geishas. In fact, geishas are not prostitutes; they sell art, not their bodies. The 'art' of geishas refers to the need for genuine talent and learning, impressing people with their skills. Therefore, becoming a popular geisha is not easy. Modern geishas are like inheritors of the Mei school of Peking Opera or like our giant pandas, a rare historical heritage. They are a declining art form that needs protection. There are very few modern geishas, and most are daughters following their mother's profession. Families with elders involved in this trade find it challenging to be a geisha. Training starts around the age of ten, learning tea ceremonies, calligraphy, musical instruments, dance, etiquette, etc. They cannot wear regular clothes but must wear kimonos and wooden sandals. Their hair must be specially styled and typically lasts about a week without washing. During this period, they cannot use pillows while sleeping but instead use a narrow, hard wooden support under their necks to prevent their hair from touching the bed and getting messed up. They train for twelve hours daily. Beginners attend training schools (geisha schools), learning everything from drumming to playing the shamisen, singing folk songs to classical music, and dancing modern to traditional dances. Students often practice outdoors in the cold until their fingers bleed and their voices are hoarse. Additionally, they learn tea ceremonies, pouring drinks, walking, sitting postures, and conversational manners. Typically, geishas have only two days off per month and often sleep inadequately due to late-night work, feeling tired and unwell. They are not allowed to call friends or watch TV and must obey seniors' orders at all times, having no personal time or privacy.

Initially, the expenses are covered by the owner of the geisha house, including training fees and daily costs. Young girls can charge these expenses to the geisha house. This arrangement aims to cultivate them into ethereal beings detached from worldly concerns, elevating them beyond the mundane. While learning art, apprentice geishas also serve as maids for senior geishas and the geisha house owners. Since geishas are the main income source for geisha houses, the owners cater to their whims. Apprentice geishas are subject to the commands of senior geishas. Typically, all aspects of a geisha's life are managed by the geisha house, with apprentices handling trivial tasks. Apprentices grow up observing geishas' expressions and behaviors and learn how to serve others obediently. Most geishas endure abuse from madams and bullying from senior geishas during their training, with no allowance for resistance, serving as a form of discipline.

At the age of eighteen, apprentice geishas undergo the sash-changing ceremony, offering sake to senior sisters, the geisha house owner, and dance teachers, drinking nine cups of rice wine to officially become geishas.

After rigorous long-term training, even geishas with ordinary looks possess talents and charm enough to captivate guests. Hence, the saying in the geisha world goes: As long as there are three strings, there's no worry about meals.

Upon completion of training, geishas leave the tea houses around 6 p.m. to meet clients arranged by their mothers. Geisha performance fees are generally calculated by time. In the past, incense burning marked the time, with one stick representing one fee, called one flower. Now, it's calculated hourly. Geishas earn high incomes, charging approximately 30,000 yen per hour (about 210 RMB), and can accompany people for drinking and socializing.

Geishas mainly entertain political elites and celebrities, but contrary to the general Chinese perception, they do not sell their bodies. They only engage in conversation and entertainment. However, with frequent interactions, it's inevitable that some develop affections. If multiple admirers emerge, a bidding process ensues, with the highest bidder hosting a ceremony. The female invites her sisters, while the male invites his friends and competitors for a banquet. Afterward, they spend the night together, but afterward, they resume their separate lives. Each July, Japan selects the most popular geisha of the year, and posters of her are displayed everywhere.

Mixed Gender Bathing

When talking about hot springs, let's discuss Japan's bathing culture - mixed gender bathing, where families bathe together irrespective of age or gender. This is a long-standing tradition in Japan, but in the early 20th century, they felt embarrassed as other countries practiced gender-separated bathing, considering themselves primitive and ignorant. Therefore, they legislated a ban in 1912. However, the power of tradition remains strong, and this custom persists in some remote areas today. Some hotels also offer this, with designated time slots, attracting mostly elderly patrons. Within families, daughters helping fathers scrub their backs is still common, regardless of the daughter's age. At the hotel we stayed in, regardless of whether it was the men's or women's bath, the cleaners were all female. Therefore, the tour guide advised that if men see women cleaning inside, they should not panic or feel shy, as it's a unique Japanese custom and culture.

Japanese people love soaking in hot springs, eating fish, and drinking tea, leading to a long lifespan. Men live on average over 70 years, women over 80 years, with 25,000 people over 100 years old, predominantly women.

Body Art Sushi

Originating over a thousand years ago in Japan, Body Art Sushi involves placing colorful sushi, sashimi, and other Japanese dishes on the naked body of a woman who has been thoroughly bathed, for diners to enjoy. Its intent is an expression of the fusion of human and culinary arts rather than a sexual service, though inherently it cannot escape associations with sexuality. Simply put, Body Art Sushi treats the woman's body as a vessel for food, combining dining with eroticism, symbolizing a disguised objectification and disrespect toward women, unique to Japanese culture.

When preparing, cream cakes are placed on the chest, particularly the breasts, resembling beautiful bras. Placing sushi on the body has scientific considerations: frogfish provide strength, placed on the heart; sailfish aid digestion, placed on the abdomen; eel enhances sexual ability, placed near the groin...

Not only does Body Art Sushi involve careful placement of food, but it also imposes strict requirements on the women. Firstly, they must be virgins. Secondly, as carriers of food, they must remain motionless for extended periods, enduring any indecent behavior from guests silently. Their principle is complete service, entertainment, and obedience to customers.

Regardless of whether Body Art Sushi is good or bad, its existence clearly reflects the low status of women in Japan.

Japan's Indigenous Comfort Women

Speaking of karma and the cycle of life and death in Buddhism, in our impression, comfort women were Chinese and Korean women serving the Japanese army. Who knew that when Japan was defeated, to prevent their own women from suffering the same indignity, they organized a massive comfort women corps to resist the American forces. Regardless, the ultimate victims were women. Below is an excerpt about Japan's recruitment of comfort women during that time.

On August 15, 1945, Japan surrendered, and General MacArthur's American troops prepared to occupy Japan. The news of tens of thousands of American soldiers arriving caused extreme panic among the Japanese. Social surveys showed that fears of violence and rape against women ranked highest among concerns about the occupation. This idea stemmed from propaganda that if Japan lost, men would be castrated, and women would be forced into prostitution. Additionally, the atrocities committed by the Japanese army in Asia became the primary reference for imagining the behavior of the occupying forces.

In response to this panic, the Japanese cabinet urgently discussed countermeasures, concluding that they should adopt the comfort women system established during the war, providing comfort facilities and sexual services to reduce American disturbances to civilian Japanese women. Establishing these facilities required an initial budget of 50 million yen, a huge expense for a defeated Japan. However, the Minister of Finance, Hayato Ikeda, quickly approved the funds, reasoning that using this money to preserve Japanese women's chastity and lineage was highly worthwhile.

On August 18, the Japanese Ministry of Home Affairs issued documents to local governments, requesting assistance from local police departments in establishing comfort facilities for the occupying forces. On August 26, the RAA Association, led by the Tokyo Metropolitan Police Department, was officially established. This association was specifically created to provide sexual services to the American occupying forces, referred to as a national prostitution agency by the Japanese. The association had departments for comfort, gaming, performing arts, special facilities, dining, and products, claiming grandiosely that its role was to act as a bulwark against the tide, preserving the purity of the nation and supporting post-war social order underground.

Thus, a nationwide comfort system for the American military began to be established in Japan. The first such comfort facility opened at Komachien Garden on the outskirts of Tokyo. Subsequently, other comfort facilities like Gokurakubou, Mutual Joy, Full River, and Maiden successively opened, reaching a peak of 70,000 RAA women engaged in comfort services.

Initially, the Japanese government tried to conceal its involvement in state-sponsored prostitution, requiring operators of comfort facilities to act individually rather than under government auspices. Comfort women were initially recruited from public and private prostitutes in Japanese society. However, unexpected difficulties arose during recruitment.

On August 21, the Tokyo Metropolitan Police Department convened a meeting with major brothel operators, requesting them to mobilize their prostitutes to provide comfort services to the Allied forces. The results were embarrassing. Representatively, Seiji Narukawa, the boss of the large brothel Yoshiwara, responded that while it might be possible to forcibly order prostitutes to serve yesterday's enemies today, whether the prostitutes could accept it was questionable.

Please allow us to consult. In fact, the bosses' concerns were justified, as the prostitutes reacted strongly to the command. Long-standing propaganda was one reason. Another circulating rumor claimed that Westerners' bodies differed from Japanese, and engaging in intercourse with them would split them in half, making it akin to a tragic sacrifice.

Consequently, the number of willing prostitutes fell far short of one-third of the initial requirement. Out of desperation, the Japanese government removed its mask and advertised publicly for virtuous women.

Foreign Affairs Club recruiting female staff, free meals, accommodation, uniforms, high salaries, limited to women aged 18 to 25.

When this advertisement appeared in major Japanese newspapers on September 20, 1945, over 1,600 people attended interviews that day, and within three months, 60,000 women applied nationwide.

At the time, Japan's unemployment rate was extremely high, with males prioritized for jobs, leaving many Japanese women widowed by the war on the brink of starvation. Thus, such advertisements naturally attracted numerous applicants. According to statistics, less than 20% of the applicants acknowledged they could engage in sexual services. However, under the combined pressure of the government and brothel owners, most eventually ended up as comfort women. In Etsuko Inagaki's book "Occupation Army Comfort Facilities," she states that voluntary participation did not exceed half.

Additionally, the advertisement was distributed by the Special Comfort Facilities Association (RAA), established with the participation of the Tokyo Metropolitan Police Department. Most applicants were unaware that their job would involve providing sexual services to American soldiers stationed in Japan. The government's public advertisements in national newspapers convinced them fully, yet their fate remains a page in Japanese history that is still unwillingly mentioned.
