Car spray paint decision, dust-free spray booth paint spraying

by de2sd41x on 2012-02-09 15:31:49

Choosing car spray paint, dust-free spray paint booth paint application. Experts often raise questions about how to deal with car scratches and repainting, so I have gathered some information and organized it for publication. This will also allow everyone to observe the entire process of car paint spraying, as well as learn about touch-up methods and simple maintenance knowledge.

Regarding car paint:

Car paints are usually baked-on finishes. In the factory, after the car frame and body are welded (sometimes manually repaired), the next step is painting. Generally, first (high-end cars use phosphating anti-corrosion technology) is the primer. The bare body is dipped into a syrup-like paint bath, then dried to cure the primer; then sent to a dust-free spray paint booth where electrostatic spray painting applies the topcoat. It is then baked at around 200 degrees Celsius. For more refined processes, another layer of clear coat may be applied. Thus, the paint process is complete. Common topcoats include: solid paint; metallic paint; and pearl paint. Solid paint doesn't need much explanation - resin, pigments, and additives. Metallic paint contains aluminum powder, making it look shiny. Pearl paint contains mica particles. Mica is very thin and reflective in a specific direction, giving it a colorful effect. If metallic paint is combined with a clear coat, the car's paint looks bright and beautiful. In terms of color, white is undoubtedly the cheapest. For example, world-class brand red pigment costs about 25 times more than titanium dioxide; and the amount of titanium dioxide used in white paint is about five times that of red pigment. Therefore, the cost of red paint is five times higher than white paint. This is why some domestic cars have so few color options: simple and cost-effective.

Regardless of the color of the paint, its pigments will fade under sunlight. In theory, not just paint, but any colored object can fade under strong sunlight. At this point, the additives in the paint become very important, such as light stabilizers and antioxidants. Unfortunately, good additives are very expensive. Often, the quality of the additives determines how the paint performs. People will definitely think that a paint that starts to fade significantly after two years is worse than one that remains bright after five years. But in reality, there is no paint that does not fade. Many times, the manufacturer focuses on: even fading. Try to prevent different parts of the car from showing color differences.

When you get a new car, the paint is the easiest part to damage. Careless washing can injure the paint. Even during production, when loading goods, paint can be damaged.

For minor scratches, if the paint surface has scratches that appear white, it means the paint surface has been scratched. No touch-up is needed. Light scratches can be polished with sanding wax or by applying wax multiple times. More severe ones can be polished. If the scratch is deeper and you can see the base primer color (often dark colors), check the affected area. Typically, bumpers, mirrors, and wheel arches on some cars are made of engineering plastics and won't rust. Besides being unsightly, it's not a big issue whether or not to repaint. For other areas, repainting is necessary to prevent rust, even if it's a small damage.

Regarding touch-ups:

First, nowhere, including the original factory, will they use "original factory paint" for touch-ups! If the repair shop tells you they use "original factory paint," they are lying. The reason is simple: As explained earlier, in the original factory painting process, after painting, the paint is baked at 200 degrees Celsius. How many components on a finished car can withstand 200 degrees? Would you dismantle the whole car and leave only the shell? Touch-up paint uses specialized repair paint, which is baked at a lower temperature, around 70-80 degrees Celsius.

Second, the color of repair paint is mixed before application. The reason is simple: Your car might have been driven for several years, and the paint has started to fade, though you might not notice it. If your car is parked outside, fading might vary slightly depending on the sun exposure. To match the original color as closely as possible, the color must be adjusted on-site. Also, to minimize color differences, even if only a small section needs touch-up, the entire panel is usually repainted. For example, if a door needs touch-up, either the upper or lower half of the door will be fully repainted. This way, if there is a slight color difference, it won't be noticeable.

Also, if you bought a pearl paint finish for aesthetics (fortunately, most domestic cars don't use it yet), prepare for disappointment during touch-ups. Matching the orientation of mica particles in the touch-up paint to the original is extremely difficult (or almost impossible). Even if the color matches perfectly, the touch-up will still be noticeable.

Nowadays, computerized color matching, when done properly by skilled personnel, produces paint that is indistinguishable to the naked eye from the original. You can be relatively confident about this. The key lies in the quality of the paint, equipment, and the worker's dedication.

Although original factory paint isn't used for touch-ups, under normal circumstances, car manufacturers do recommend specific brands of repair paint to authorized service stations. Good repair shops will use reputable brands like ICI or PPG. However, smaller shops or less formal repair stations might cut costs by using cheaper paints (or even counterfeit ones). Initially, the results might seem the same, but after three months, color differences will start to show. As users, we almost can't distinguish these differences, so it's best to stick to trustworthy repair shops.

Although repair paint is baked at a lower temperature, it doesn't mean baking or alternative methods aren't necessary. If you find a repair shop without a proper paint baking booth and instead uses "small suns" (iodine tungsten lamps) for heating, consider changing locations immediately unless you drive a public vehicle.

Additionally, in regular car manufacturing lines, painting occurs in dust-free spray booths. Although many purely domestic cars might not follow this practice, it doesn't mean it's unnecessary. If you see a repair shop that's dirty and dusty, I suggest finding another location. Otherwise, even if it looks fine initially, issues like bubbling and peeling of the paint surface may arise over time.

For touch-ups, apart from paying, there's probably nothing else we can do.

Regarding paint protection:

First, don't wash your car with just a bucket of water like a bus driver would. That's equivalent to sandpapering your car paint, albeit on a smaller scale. If it has rained or the dust is thick, avoid using a wax cloth or duster. Wash the car thoroughly instead.

Washing your car with plenty of water is undoubtedly necessary. However, many car wash attendants like to use high-pressure water guns aimed directly at the paint, which can also damage the paint. Additionally, using laundry detergent instead of dedicated car wash liquid is not advisable.

These damages might not be noticeable after one or two instances, but over time, they can accumulate significantly.

Also, given how quickly new cars are sold, the car you receive might have left the factory just days ago. Paint baking has a characteristic: it takes some time to fully harden and stabilize. Therefore, don't rush to wax your new car. Wait at least three months. After three months, try to avoid using hard wax. If the car has been repainted, avoid washing it within a week. Rain is unavoidable sometimes.

Furthermore, many new cars have an additional clear coat over the paint, enhancing brightness and protecting the paint surface. Don't fall for gimmicks like sealing or polishing the paint surface of a new car. Doing so will degrade your car's paint quality to that of a lower-grade vehicle.
