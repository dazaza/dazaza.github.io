Stock investors found the stocks bought by their father in 1954 were worth 120 million yuan

By Wang Yanping

"Who would have thought that just after liberation, my family was actually a billionaire!" Yesterday, Mr. Wu, an old stockholder from Hangzhou, joked with reporters as he took out the stock certificates his father purchased in 1954. However, after several decades, Mr. Wu has heard that it is unlikely these stocks can be redeemed. He wants to know how much they are worth for collection purposes.

More than 2,000 shares, valued at over 12 million yuan

Mr. Wu's parents both passed away not long ago. According to Mr. Wu, his father was a renowned doctor during his lifetime. A while ago, when he was sorting through his parents' belongings, he discovered these stocks at the bottom of a box. I counted them, and there were four 500-share certificates, four 50-share certificates, three 10-share certificates, and one 1-share certificate, making a total of 12 certificates and 2,231 shares. They were kept very clean and intact. The colors of the different shares varied, categorized by share size into yellow, green, red, and blue.

The top of the ticket printed "Public-Private Joint Venture Zhejiang Enterprise Stock Company Limited Stock". The company address was No. 477 Zhongshan Middle Road, Hangzhou City. "Operated businesses" included processing and marketing of local specialties, establishing mining enterprises, investment, accepting purchase and sales on behalf of others, and other commissioned matters. The company's total capital was over 14 billion RMB, with a total of 250,750 shares, each share amounting to 56,000 RMB. The company chairman was Shi Lieqing, vice chairmen Jin Dongzhang and Weng Yi'an. The registration date of the stock was January 1, 1954.

The registered shareholder name on the stock was Xiangtaichaji, with a shareholder account number 2305. On the reverse side of the stock, under the transfer table, it read: "Transferred on July 8, 1954." Calculating by 56,000 RMB per share, the market value of these stocks reached over 120 million RMB!

According to historical data, 10,000 RMB in old currency in 1954 was equivalent to only 1 RMB in new currency later. Despite this, after converting to new currency, the market value of these stocks still reached 12,493.6 RMB, indicating the substantial wealth of the Wu family.

Continuous dividends for 12 years, recovering most of the investment

"These stocks were quite standardized back then," Mr. Wu pointed out, referring to the interest payment records on the back. Carefully examining the interest payment records, the company paid interest annually from 1953 to 1956, paid interest three times in 1956 (for the first half year, third quarter, and fourth quarter), and began paying interest quarterly starting in 1957. The last interest payment was in the third quarter of 1966. Initially, the annual interest rate was 6%. Starting from 1956, according to central regulations, the fixed interest was calculated at an annual rate of 5%. Without considering compound interest, by 1966, most of the original investment of over 120 million RMB had been recovered.

What interested me most was the company's method of distributing dividends: After paying income tax, covering losses, and depreciation, the company's annual surplus would first allocate 20% as reserves, then distribute dividends. Among these, 60% went to shareholders' bonuses and remuneration for directors, supervisors, and managers, 15% to employee welfare funds and rewards, and 15% to safety and health improvement funds. The remaining 10% was proposed for use by the board of directors and approved by the shareholders' meeting. If there was a loss in the fiscal year, profits would be used to cover the loss before appropriately compensating shareholders.

"It seems that companies at that time placed great emphasis on returning benefits to shareholders and also focused on employees' safety and health," Mr. Wu remarked, comparing them to some current listed companies.

The company has become history; old stocks have certain collection value

After extensive inquiries by reporters, it was learned that "Zhejiang Enterprise Stock Company Limited" was the predecessor of Hangzhou Local Specialty Group Company Limited. It was established in 1954 and subsequently underwent transformations into a collective enterprise and a state-owned enterprise. In 2001, the enterprise was restructured into a limited liability company, and in 2008, the group was officially formed.

"In the late 1950s, after socialist transformation, the original public-private joint venture became a collective enterprise, and later turned into a state-owned enterprise (SOE). The company originally distributed dividends every year, but dividends stopped when the Cultural Revolution began," a staff member surnamed Wang told reporters. "Previously, there were individuals who came to us with those stocks asking if they had any value."

A stock collector in Hangzhou told reporters that China's stock market dates back to the Qing Dynasty, and many stocks were issued during the Republic of China era, possibly numbering in the thousands. Stocks issued after the founding of the People's Republic of China, however, are relatively fewer.

The collector said that stock collecting is similar to stamp collecting—rarer items are more valuable. "If the stock was issued during the Qing Dynasty or the revolutionary Soviet areas, it would be valuable. Stocks from the Republic of China era are generally worth a few hundred yuan due to their large quantities." Additionally, factors such as the issuing company's fame, whether the stocks form a complete set, and whether there are dividend or transfer records all contribute to determining the collection value of stocks. Judging from the situation of Mr. Wu's family's stocks, although the company's fame is low and it hasn't been reissued or listed now, its variety is relatively high, with complete dividend and transfer records, so it should still have some collection value.
