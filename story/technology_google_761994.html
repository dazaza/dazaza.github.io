Privacy Issues With Google Glass? There Are Plenty of Cues

by geekzhang on 2013-05-17 12:16:35

Google Glass was overlooked at this year's I/O conference, but that doesn't mean it hasn't drawn attention. Venturebeat found that nearly a third of people on-site looked like cyborgs (referring to those wearing Glass), showing just how popular this product is. In subsequent sessions, the topic of Google Glass was hotly discussed, with Steve Lee, the product director of Glass, and engineer Charles Mendis disclosing some of the latest developments on-site.As Larry Page said, the release date for Glass remains unclear. However, Lee revealed that all developers who registered at last year's I/O conference have received the product. The next step is to give priority purchase rights to 8,000 people selected for Glass events. This purchase will likely still be for a test version.To encourage developers, Lee and Mendis introduced many use cases to entice them. For example, Lee mentioned wanting a fitness app so he could check fitness messages without interrupting his workout. Other examples include making payments through Glass to free up your hands, or even singing karaoke. As an augmented reality device, its potential applications are very broad.On-site, Google set up a special studio to introduce how to hack Glass, hoping developers would explore more possibilities. The list of apps for Google Glass has expanded, including new additions such as Facebook, Twitter, Tumblr, CNN, Elle, and Evernote, in addition to the New York Times and Path. It can be seen that news and social media still dominate, and there are no dedicated augmented reality apps specifically designed for Glass yet.When discussing the sensitive issue of privacy regarding Glass, Lee showed no concern whatsoever. He believes that Glass has had sufficient safeguards built into its design from the beginning:Taking photos has obvious action cues: lifting your hand and speaking. This action is very noticeable to the surrounding crowd. Moreover, once Glass is in use, its display area lights up, not only visible to the user but also to everyone around. We ensured this from the initial design phase; any app must have a visible indicator when activated, otherwise it won't be allowed to pass.Lee stated that early versions of the product had the display area covering the eyeball, but the team quickly realized the importance of eye contact and moved the display area to the top. This way, if someone wearing Glass is paying attention to you, you'll immediately notice. If they're looking at Glass, they must look up. If Glass is recording you, the person must stare directly at you. So when such situations occur, you need to be cautious.In other words, Glass still gives indications when recording, such as actions and the display lighting up. However, what Lee said isn't entirely accurate; users can record without staring directly at someone, or even with their eyes closed. This action is similar to secretly taking pictures with a phone while pretending to be looking at it, just with the hand slightly raised. At this point, it's hard to blame Glass because there were enough previous warnings.Finally, the Glass team discovered that color is more important than imagined and began working on creating more cool colors. Glass currently comes in only five colors: black, gray, white, orange, and blue. As a wearable device, having more color options would be beneficial.The title image is from technotin.
