**Must-read for bosses: Points to consider when selecting work phones for employees**

When using "Enterprise Access," some bosses choose to let employees use their personal phones, and in return, give them a small subsidy for using their own devices. Some companies prefer to provide employees with unified company phones. Both methods are good. If you choose the first method, please pay attention to the following issues:

1. Smartphones on the market vary greatly. Although they are all smartphones, differences in operating system versions and manufacturers may lead to different effects when using "Enterprise Access." Please remind your employees that some off-brand (non-name brand) phones should be avoided as much as possible. These phones often have quality issues, and if problems arise while using "Enterprise Access," it will increase the difficulty of determining whether the issue is with the phone or the software system we provided.

2. Avoid using phones produced many years ago, such as smartphones with a resolution of 240x320. These phones may experience poor display effects and slow operation speeds when using "Enterprise Access."

If you choose the second method, i.e., the company provides employees with unified phones, sometimes companies will arrange bulk mobile services with carriers. This has several advantages:

1. The company doesn't need to pay for the phones directly but can get one as part of a consumption agreement.

2. The phone numbers belong to the company, so when an employee resigns, the number can be reused by the person who takes over the position.

3. Employees can make free calls to each other, and some carriers even allow free calls between mobile phones and the company's landline.

However, please also note:

1. The mobile signals from different carriers vary in different regions. In some areas, China Telecom has better signals, in others, China Unicom or China Mobile does. Therefore, try to consult your employees' opinions as much as possible.

2. China Mobile’s phones have relatively slower internet speed, and in many areas, only 2G signals are available without 3G. If you need to use "Enterprise Access," especially for uploading photos taken on-site, please test and check the speed beforehand. China Unicom’s phones sometimes have poor signals in remote areas, so if your employees frequently work in rural areas, consult their opinions as well.

3. Try to choose a carrier’s contract package for either 2 years or 1 year. Some carriers offer marketing activities where consuming for 3 years gives you a free phone. It is generally acknowledged in the industry that the lifespan of a smartphone is around 2 years. If you choose a 3-year consumption agreement, there is a high likelihood that many phones will become obsolete or damaged after 2 years.

Regardless of whether you procure phones through a carrier's unified consumption contract or not, here are some additional considerations when purchasing phones:

1. What happens if the phone breaks or gets stolen? It could directly affect business operations. For example, if no orders can be placed or sales reported for a day, it would be hard for the company to accept. Therefore, preparations must be made. We suggest that company bosses either reserve some backup phones, encourage employees to use their personal phones if the company-issued one fails, or ensure the logistics department can quickly purchase replacements.

2. Smartphones consume a lot of power. When selecting phones, try to pick ones with longer standby times. Generally, phone batteries come with specifications like 1500 mAh or 2000 mAh. A higher number usually means a longer standby time. If the phone can be equipped with two batteries, that would be even better.

3. Finally, after providing your employees with phones, it is recommended to equip them with silicone protective cases, which can definitely extend the lifespan of the phones!

After smartphones became inseparable from us, the influence of mobile internet began to permeate into enterprises. It’s time to include location information as a factor in enterprise management. "Enterprise Access," developed by Zhongtai Group, is a software that uses location information provided by mobile devices to enhance the efficiency of managing employees.

In traditional models, it was difficult for company managers to monitor the real-time locations of employees working outside the office. Data collected by these employees also had to be entered via phone calls or filling out forms upon returning to the office. After the development of smartphones and mobile internet, more convenient location-based management allows companies to manage field employees just like office employees.

Through a mobile app, employees can sign in more promptly and accurately, and transfer data collected during fieldwork to the company intranet in real time. Such needs are concentrated in industries with many field tasks, such as fast-moving consumer goods (FMCG) and logistics express industries. Statistics show that over 30 million company employees work outside the office nationwide. With the increasing penetration of mobile internet into daily life, the popularization of mobile office software is an inevitable trend.

In addition to basic attendance management, "Enterprise Access" also provides comprehensive mobile office services, such as managing/updating customer information via mobile phones, recording new business opportunities, uploading data to the company intranet, viewing company notices, receiving new tasks, writing work summaries, applying for expenses, and registering business trips through the mobile phone.
