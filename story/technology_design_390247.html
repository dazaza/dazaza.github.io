Farmers in a small town in Northern Jiangsu Province make over 10,000 yuan per month by operating online shops

In Shaji Town, Northern Jiangsu, with its population of 60,000, over 1,200 online stores have emerged, creating a large group of online merchants and leading farmers to prosperity through distinctive paths.

On August 20, the "2009 First Half Online Shopping Market Development Report" showed that in just the first half of the year, Taobao alone had an online transaction volume of 80.9 billion yuan, approaching the full-year total of 99.96 billion yuan in 2008. With 140 million registered members, representing more than 40% of China's internet users, the numbers are astonishing.

People from Guangdong, always at the forefront, contributed the most this time as well. In just six months, Guangdong consumers spent nearly 10 billion yuan on online shopping, firmly holding the "leading position." Meanwhile, Guangdong also has close to 20 million registered Taobao users.

However, in rural areas of Northern Jiangsu, the penetration of e-commerce and its vitality among the common people are equally surprising. In Shaji Town, Xuzhou, out of a population of 60,000, there are over 1,200 online stores. Traditionally, Chinese farmers were considered far removed from the internet—let alone e-commerce—but now, in their humble homes amidst barking dogs, they are typing away on keyboards, creating another miracle of the internet age.

On August 29, Yang Yang, 23, was focused intently on his computer screen, ignoring everything happening around him. He barely touched his lunch, eating only a few bites before putting it down. A delivery order form was kept nearby for immediate use.

Yang Yang's work enthusiasm is astonishing. From 9:00 AM to 12:00 PM every day, he stays online, waiting for business. No wonder, after only four months of running his online store, his monthly income has already reached close to ten thousand yuan.

Dialog windows kept popping up on the computer screen. Yang Yang typed letters with his two index fingers and used his left thumb for the space bar, achieving an astonishing typing speed. Locals call this method "three-finger Zen," which most have gradually mastered from "one-finger Zen."

A computer, scattered delivery forms, simple furniture, red brick houses, and black local dogs guarding the home—these stark visual contrasts make up the most ordinary households in Dongfeng Village, Shaji Town, Xuzhou, Jiangsu Province. Of course, the most important element is the villagers bent over their computers—people are even hesitant to interrupt them for fear of affecting their business.

Replication

Within a very short period, 10, 100, and then over a thousand online stores sprang up dramatically, replicating Sun Han's success more than 1,200 times.

Yang Yang is not satisfied with his current income; his role model is Sun Han, 27, from the same village—the first person in the village to open an online store, with an annual income in the hundreds of thousands. Dongfeng Village was once known as the "waste village," primarily dealing in scrap materials, but now, out of more than 500 households, over 400 online stores sell simple furniture. This transformation took only two years.

Everything started with Sun Han and a few friends' entrepreneurship. In 2006, Sun Han quit his job at the local mobile company, giving up a stable monthly income of two or three thousand yuan, insisting on starting a business and playing with computers all day.

His mother, Xia Cuixia, did traditional curtain business and could not understand her son's obsession with the seemingly intangible network, believing that her son was deceiving her. "Children who play computers don't do proper work." When Sun Han wanted to change his computer and was short by several thousand yuan, Xia Cuixia refused outright. Later, "he would pat my head like this and say, Mom, I'm using the computer to do business, real business, I'm not deceiving you." Xia Cuixia mimicked the scene at the time, but her expression was now filled with pride.

Unable to resist her son's plea, the family added over 3,000 yuan to buy a new computer. Sun Han began working vigorously. He registered an online store on Taobao, selling small accessories, sourcing goods from elsewhere, spreading them all over the house. It was only then that Xia Cuixia finally confirmed that her son had truly started doing business. A few days later, Sun Han completed his first transaction, worth dozens of yuan.

Because of his fondness for IKEA's style, around May 2007, Sun Han decided to switch to selling assemble-it-yourself simple furniture. The area lacked carpenters, wood, and channels, so everything had to be restarted from scratch.

It took an entire month to convert pictures into the first product. Fortunately, Sun Han succeeded. The products were highly welcomed online, and the people around him became interested. Some came to ask about the secrets of opening an online store, and Sun Han taught them, knowing the principle of "aggregating stores into markets," especially since "his own business was too busy to handle."

Within a very short time, 10, 100, and then over a thousand online stores sprang up dramatically, replicating Sun Han's success more than 1,200 times—out of the town's 60,000+ people, over 1,200 opened online stores.

The attitude shifted from initial hesitation to dramatic replication, and special products went from nothing to something—all within two years for the farmers of Shaji Town.

Similarly impressive scenes appear in Yandu Village, Yanji Town, dozens of kilometers away. The elderly men sitting together cooling off talk about topics related to computers. Yanji Town's traditional industry is flower and tree production, and in Yandu Village, over 700 households have opened more than 200 online flower shops.

Jiang Puhui, 31, still remembers the panic when she received her first online order. In March of last year, just 10 days after opening her online store, while taking care of her small convenience store and looking after her child, suddenly, the "bulging belly" computer in the corner rang—it was a customer from Zhejiang wanting to buy something.

"I panicked immediately, the customer asked me to send a link, but I didn’t know how, so I let him teach me. Luckily, the customer was kind and patient, telling me step-by-step what to do." Her first order amounted to over 300 yuan.

Business improved steadily, and this year, her husband, who worked as a plumber in Shanghai, stopped going out to work. Young people returned home one after another. Thinking about the hardships of raising a child and managing the store alone in the past, Jiang Puhui now feels genuinely happy.

Every afternoon, white mini vans shuttle through the streets and alleys of Shaji Town to collect goods. Yang Yang hands over the accumulated orders and goods of the day to the courier service, with destinations distributed across the country and even overseas.

At the beginning, there were only one or two courier companies in the town. If delivering to Jiangsu-Zhejiang-Shanghai region, the price for less than 15 kilograms was 10 yuan. Now, it has dropped to 6 yuan. More than a dozen courier companies compete fiercely—"it's painful" yet also joyful. Just EMS alone, according to estimates, daily revenue reaches 7,000 yuan. Previously ranking last in Jiangsu Province’s EMS rankings, it now ranks among the top three. Each year, the courier turnover in Shaji Town can reach several million yuan.

Previously unsold wood is now in high demand, and if unavailable locally, people buy from Anhui, Hunan, Guangxi, and other places. Carpenters no longer need to go to Shanghai or Guangdong for decoration jobs. Now, even at 80 yuan per day, there may not be enough willing workers.

All the simple furniture in Shaji Town requires customers to assemble themselves. Manufacturers only need to cut wooden strips and boards and prepare screws. In processing plants, people from nearby towns come to work, sanding wooden strips and packing, earning 30 yuan per day.

Walking along the main road of the town, on both sides of the street, whether selling phones, clothes, shoes, hair salons, or agricultural machinery stores, 80% have computers and run Taobao stores, selling all kinds of wooden home furnishings.

Sun Han said that each product, as soon as it comes out, gets copied instantly, including the promotional pictures on the webpage, which also get copied and circulated throughout the village. Taking pictures is interesting. Some families run photo studios, and their pictures look better and are naturally more popular. Whose furniture looks good will become the best choice for neighbors to take photos. Even children from the family are "borrowed" as models.

Shaji Town has over 1,200 online stores and 33 furniture processing factories. That means most online stores do not have their own processing plants but act as "brokers" for sales, sourcing goods from others. "For example, if this product is taken, sold for a profit of 10 yuan, the processing plant takes 7 yuan, and the seller takes 3 yuan."

In Yandu Village, villagers have come up with even more interesting methods. Power poles are scattered among lush green trees, and each pole has seven or eight loudspeakers facing all directions. Originally, if any household runs out of stock, they just turn on their loudspeakers and broadcast it across the whole village. During peak seasons, loudspeakers sound off continuously.

Zhou Xiao is a barefoot doctor with a clinic at home. As soon as you enter, you'll be surprised to see three computers on the table. "Three computers, three Taobao stores, one Alibaba store."

Zhou Xiao is proud. Every morning, he sits in the clinic as a doctor like in the past, and at noon, he starts managing the online store, receiving orders, packing, and shipping, becoming a true online merchant... dual roles, tradition and modernity, thus separated by the network.

Villagers all have tricks for running online stores. Zhou Xiao is proficient in computers and "cleverly" bought software that allows him to send messages automatically every day, ensuring that his online store is visible to customers from morning till night. When others ask, Zhou Xiao never hesitates to share. For those who are not skilled at typing, they can pre-type commonly used phrases, save them, and directly copy them when needed.

Prospects

As e-commerce rapidly gains popularity in rural areas of Northern Jiangsu, it will also bring issues unique to the region.

"First, sell goods at low prices to build credit ratings, then promote the website." Yang Yang clearly understands the practical effects of credit and "attention economy," but these insights don't necessarily come from books but rather from oral traditions and experiences.

Friedman once predicted the performance of developing countries in the information age: "(Developing countries) will first solve their own problems using cheap labor, high technology, and highly creative re-imagination of the future." At this moment, Chinese farmers are proving through action the enormous potential they possess.

Without the internet and without opening an online store, Zhou Xiaolong should still be repairing cars in Changzhou, earning 700 yuan per month, paying rent, and spending all his remaining money. Now, at 21, he lies back on a leather chair, his face slightly youthful, but trying hard to display the demeanor of a boss.

He is the "broker" for his parents, each doing their own business. He buys flowers grown by his parents and sells them online, earning the difference. By the end of August, his sales this year had already reached sixty-seven thousand yuan.

Unlike young people who desperately try to escape the countryside for the city, Zhou Xiaolong doesn't mind being called a farmer. "It's the result of my own labor." His words reflect an unusual calmness and stability for his age.

As e-commerce rapidly gains popularity in rural areas of Northern Jiangsu, it will also bring issues unique to the region. Over-replication leads to homogenized competition, outdated production methods reduce efficiency, and villagers sometimes even engage in price wars, reducing profits again and again.

Fortunately, Sun Han has already hired a designer to strive for more original works, introducing more advanced equipment, and strengthening his operations.
