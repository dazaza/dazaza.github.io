Amateur astronomers also make great discoveries.

In late July, Jupiter was hit by another celestial body, leaving a black scar near the south pole. A hole the size of Earth appeared in the atmosphere above the impact site. Interestingly, the first observer of this Jupiter collision was not a professional astronomer, but an amateur astronomy enthusiast from Australia named Anthony Wesley. He captured photos of Jupiter's collision through a telescope set up in his backyard and published them online. How could amateur astronomy enthusiasts surpass professional astronomers? In fact, amateur astronomy enthusiasts have their own advantages. They can track a celestial body for a long time, and they can also use a dedicated telescope. Therefore, as long as you have good luck in the vast universe, amateur astronomy enthusiasts can also make surprising discoveries.

Observing Jupiter's collision in his own backyard

The new hole that appeared on Jupiter was initially observed on the morning of July 20th by Australian amateur astronomy enthusiast Anthony Wesley. Subsequently, NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory in California conducted observations and used infrared space telescopes located in Hawaii to capture static images of Jupiter's hole and scars.

Wesley is 44 years old and works as a computer programmer. According to reports from Australia, Wesley has been interested in astronomical observations since he was young. The tool he used to discover the hole on Jupiter was a 14.5-inch catadioptric telescope set up in his backyard.

In fact, Wesley almost missed this precious opportunity. Just hours before he observed the collision, due to poor weather conditions, he almost turned off the telescope, but then changed his mind. He rested for half an hour himself, but did not let the telescope rest. The imaging device on the telescope continued to operate. When Wesley returned, he found a black spot on Jupiter's south pole region. "I believe I have observed something new. Two days ago, I took pictures of the impact area, and at that time, I didn't find any abnormal black spots, but now there are." Wesley wrote this in his shooting records published online. Wesley said that at first, he thought the "black spot" was Jupiter's "polar storm," but as Jupiter rotated, he found that the black spot was a three-dimensional hollow shape, so he overturned the "polar storm" guess and determined it as a collision mark.

Amateur astronomy enthusiasts have many advantages

Jupiter being hit and creating a hole the size of Earth is considered a relatively rare astronomical spectacle. People are curious why it wasn't a professional institution but an amateur astronomy enthusiast who first observed this astronomical phenomenon. What were those professional astronomers doing? Are their millions-of-dollars astronomical telescopes all just for show?

This is actually a very good example of positive interaction and synergy between professional astronomers and amateur astronomy enthusiasts. Professional astronomers have their own advantages, and amateur astronomy enthusiasts also have characteristics that professional astronomers do not possess. First of all, the number of amateur astronomy enthusiasts is much greater than that of professional astronomers, and they are distributed in every corner of the world. Secondly, for some astronomical observations with low technical thresholds but requiring long-term tracking, the advantage of amateur astronomy enthusiasts is more obvious. They can focus their telescopes on a certain target for a long time, as long as needed, while professional astronomers do not necessarily have a telescope each, different astronomers have different tasks, and they are less focused in terms of dedication.

There are countless similar examples

Besides Wesley, there are many similar examples, some discovered by chance, others quite professional. For instance, Caroline Moore, who discovered a super nova last November when she was only 14 years old, making her the youngest person to discover a super nova. And recently, astronomy enthusiasts in Connecticut, USA, identified a new galaxy cluster while using the SDSS image database, which has been named the "Green Peas." This discovery will help astronomers understand how stars were born and evolved into galaxies in the early universe.

According to reports, this galaxy cluster is named for its small size and green fluorescent light. Observers found that the star formation rate of the "Green Peas" is astonishing, the highest among all known galaxy clusters. Amateur astronomers, after long-term tracking, observed 250 galaxies in this galaxy cluster. They are 1.5 billion to 5 billion light-years away from Earth, about one-tenth the size of the Milky Way, but their star birth speed is ten times that of the Milky Way.

Astronomers introduced that such astonishing star formation rates were very common in various galaxies in the early universe, but are rarely seen today. By observing the light of the "Green Pea" galaxy cluster, we can determine how many new stars are being born in the galaxy cluster. Research on the "Green Pea" galaxy cluster will help people understand how planets were produced in the early universe and how many galaxies, including the Milky Way, developed into their current form.

Professional astronomical institutions are considering "recruiting" them

Because amateur astronomy enthusiasts can contribute to professional astronomers' observations, some professional astronomical institutions are considering "recruiting" amateur astronomy enthusiasts to participate in observations of some important projects, such as NASA inviting amateur astronomy enthusiasts to join lunar observation activities, which is part of NASA's Lunar Crater Observation and Sensing Satellite Program. The satellite will collide with a crater in the south pole of the moon in October this year. This collision will stir up a large amount of material, which may include water components. NASA has invited amateur astronomy enthusiasts to observe after the collision and provide photos and other observational data to NASA, thereby enriching the observational data obtained from professional observatories and space telescopes.

"The contribution of amateur astronomy enthusiasts may be very important," said Faith Villas, director of the MMT Observatory on Mount Hopkins in Arizona, USA. This observatory is managed by the University of Arizona and is equipped with a 6.5-meter aperture telescope, which will also participate in observing this collision action.

With good equipment, a good location, and a relatively clear sky, amateur astronomers will be in a relatively advantageous position to observe the collision. Once the dust stirred up by the satellite collides with the surface of the moon, it can be observed. The structure and shape of the dust will provide clues to determine the composition of the moon's material.

There are actually quite a few opportunities for amazing discoveries

In addition to the aforementioned project, NASA has also invited amateur astronomy enthusiasts to take pictures of the south pole of the moon, aiming to collect map data of this area, which will help determine the geological composition of the area.

Moreover, amateur astronomy enthusiasts have observational advantages in tracking near-Earth asteroids and stars close to the sun. They can help astronomers determine the orbit of asteroids and track changes in the light and radiation of stars.

"Today's amateur astronomy enthusiasts can provide various useful observational data, especially image data, and many of the data are impressive," said Brian Daye, an official from NASA responsible for contacting amateur astronomers. Adam Block, an astrophotographer, believes: "They are particularly prominent in some relatively long-term observational projects."

Therefore, for amateur astronomy enthusiasts, they have become a powerful force in the ranks of astronomical observations. Moreover, as long as they have good luck, there are actually quite a few opportunities for amazing discoveries.
