Emergency Tips for the Holidays

Throat Obstruction

Who who loves eating fish hasn't had their throat stuck with a fish bone? During the holidays, you definitely eat more seafood and aquatic products than usual, so the probability of getting your throat stuck with a fish bone is higher. Drink some vinegar? Eat a rice ball? Let them push down the fish bones, perhaps this is what you normally do. But this is not scientific, it might push the fish bone deeper, which could lead to infection and abscesses, it's no joke. Here's what you should do:

(1) Open your mouth wide, let your family press down your tongue and check where the fish bone is.

(2) If it's above the throat, bear with it, let your family use a small pair of tweezers to pull it out for you.

(3) If the fish bone is deep inside, then you'd better go to the hospital. For ENT doctors, this is really a piece of cake.

Burns from Explosions

Firecrackers are banned but accidents still follow. Every Spring Festival, hospitals always "receive" several patients whose eyes were injured by firecrackers. Once an explosion accident happens, you should immediately do the following:

(1) Do not rub your eyes, calm down first, and see if there is any bleeding in the eyes.

(2) When dust gets into the eyes, you can rinse with regular anti-inflammatory or antibacterial eye drops, then try to estimate your vision.

(3) Even if you don't feel pain in the eyes, it's best to go to the ophthalmology department of the hospital for a check-up.

(4) If the situation is serious, lie flat immediately, cover the injured eye with a clean bowl or cup, and rush to the hospital.

Tip

Explosive eye injuries, if given timely, correct, and reasonable treatment by an ophthalmologist within 24 hours, often result in better outcomes.

Scalds

This kind of thing is most likely to happen in the busy holiday atmosphere. In the hustle and bustle, the hot water bottle was knocked over, and the scalding water spilled all over the body; when bathing the child, the hot water was poured, but the cold water wasn't added yet. You turned around to get clothes, and the mischievous child had already sat down... What to do?

(1) Quickly move away from the heat source that caused the injury, if scalded on the body, quickly remove the clothes on the scalded area.

(2) Then, as quickly as possible, rinse the scalded area with cold water, generally rinse with tap water for 20-30 minutes, or even use ice water from the fridge.

(3) Then carefully observe the scalded area, if no blisters form, then it's nothing serious; otherwise, wrap the scalded area with a clean cloth and go to the hospital immediately.

Tip

Don't apply soy sauce or toothpaste, it's a big mistake, beware of infection!

Bites

In lively holidays and bustling environments, not only people get excited, animals also get restless, thus incidents of cats and dogs biting people occur frequently. The rabies virus takes this opportunity to invade the human body, posing a threat to life. Don't panic at this time, immediately:

(1) First, correctly and promptly treat the wound. Rinse the wound with clean water for at least half an hour.

(2) After rinsing, apply alcohol or 70% alcohol to the wound.

(3) Then, go to the municipal disease prevention and control center immediately to get the rabies vaccine.

(4) The earlier the vaccine is injected, the better. Generally, after being bitten, the vaccine should be injected within 15 days. However, if indeed too much time has passed, it's still better to "rather late than never", meaning "better to vaccinate than not".

Tip

Remember! The incubation period of rabies is between 10 days to more than two months after being bitten, usually 20-90 days, with some cases lasting even decades; after cleaning the wound, remember not to bandage it before going to the hospital; make sure to get vaccinated, don't take chances.
