Meizu MX3 continues to be "unconventional": innovation without breakthrough, hard to become a national phone

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 14:40:41

The battle of new smartphone releases in September has officially begun, and the first to pull the trigger is Meizu Technology from Zhuhai, a southern city.

Last night, Meizu introduced its upcoming product, the MX3 smartphone, in a 2-hour launch event at the Water Cube in Beijing. The venue, process, and last year's MX2 launch were exactly the same.

Similarly, the soul figure of Meizu, company founder Huang Zhang, was still absent.

However, compared to the MX2 launch, during the MX3 launch, neither Meizu CEO Bai Yongxiang nor Vice President Li Nan mentioned Huang Zhang much, focusing instead on the product.

As a company often compared to Xiaomi, the biggest difference between Meizu and Xiaomi lies in Meizu having its own factory, which helps Meizu better control the hardware and appearance of its products. But for the MX3, although there have been improvements in various hardware indicators compared to the MX2, there hasn't been a major breakthrough.

To highlight the characteristics of the MX3's hardware, Bai Yongxiang listed a large number of data and parameters, even having to frequently read parameters from a prompt board below the stage. However, apart from being the currently narrowest-bezel smartphone, the other hardware parameters of the MX3 are not very competitive. Moreover, excessive listing of parameters is hard to leave a deep impression, and ordinary consumers' perception of these parameters is also quite limited.

In terms of software, Meizu's deeply customized Android system, Flyme OS, has evolved to version 3.0. Li Nan introduced many features of Flyme OS 3.0, emphasizing the humanized functions designed for large screens as well as Meizu's cooperation with partners in cloud services, but overall, Flyme OS 3.0 is more of an evolution rather than too many breakthroughs.

In terms of pricing, the starting price of the Meizu MX3 is 2499 yuan, maintaining a relatively high position. A Meizu distributor told Tencent Technology that if the MX3 price was set too low, it would directly affect the sales volume of the current MX2 priced at 1999 yuan.

Hardware lacks innovative highlights

When the MX2 was released, large-screen smartphones had already started to become popular, but Meizu still insisted on making what Huang Zhang thought was the most suitable size for Chinese users at the time. However, Huang Zhang had also posted on the Meizu forum that he did not rule out the possibility of producing large-screen phones in the future.

Now mainstream Android phones have their screen sizes set at above 5.0 inches. For Meizu, making a large-screen phone is on one hand adapting to the trend of larger phone screens, and on the other hand, meeting user needs.

The MX3 is Meizu's first large-screen phone, and its tone is set as "a better large-screen phone," hoping to differentiate the MX3 from existing large-screen phones in the market.

For this purpose, Meizu deliberately retained the appearance design style of the MX2 and the seemingly strange 15:9 screen ratio, using a resolution of 1800x1080 for the 5.1-inch large screen. Additionally, it is equipped with Samsung Exynos 5410 1.6 GHz dual quad-core processor, Sony 8 million pixel back-illuminated camera, and a 2400 mAh built-in lithium battery.

Defining "easy-to-use" is a very difficult task. From a hardware perspective, easy-to-use means maintaining good grip while increasing the screen size, sufficient battery life, and proper heat dissipation; from a software perspective, it means providing a good single-handed operation experience and a system suited to large-screen phones.

However, these requirements are not only for large-screen phones but also directions any phone should strive towards. Nevertheless, Meizu made some innovations for the MX3.

In terms of hardware, Meizu actually uses the mindset of making small things to make big ones: while ensuring the screen size is large enough, Meizu tries to make the phone smaller to facilitate single-handed use, such as the narrowest bezel design. This is the aspect Bai Yongxiang spent the most time introducing.

At the launch, Bai Yongxiang also introduced that Huang Zhang required a rework because the holding arc of the engineering sample differed by 0.07 millimeters from his requirement. However, for consumers, details may just be bonus points, and whether they can perceive such subtle differences is hard to say.

However, in terms of hardware, the MX3 has changed in size compared to the MX2, which is a very easily perceptible point. "Switching to a phone with a larger screen" is enough reason for consumers to upgrade their phones, and if they recognize the Meizu brand, then the only choice is the MX3.

Flyme OS 3.0 still needs improvement

But to make consumers abandon other large-screen phones and choose Meizu, hardware is one aspect, and more differentiation might need to come through software. "An operating system designed for large-screen phones" is another major selling point brought by Li Nan for the Meizu MX3.

In the past, for the Android system, it was usually divided into phone operating systems and tablet operating systems, with significant differences between the two. However, on phones with different screen sizes, the Android operation experience didn't differ much, and Meizu took advantage of this in Flyme OS 3.0.

How far you can operate the phone with one hand is a key indicator for measuring the operating system of large-screen phones. To make single-handed operation more convenient, Meizu came up with the Smart Bar solution, adding an operation area below the screen for easier thumb touch.

Another noteworthy point is that Meizu attracted many partners to join in cloud services, such as cooperating with豌豆荚for app downloads, 多米音乐for online music, and also partnering with 新浪微博, 支付宝, 拉卡拉, 乐视and other companies.

Since Meizu is a hardware-based company, it doesn't have much experience in the Internet and cloud services. Introducing partners is the most effective way to solve cloud service issues. However, if core applications rely too much on third parties, it could pose certain risks for Meizu.

For Meizu, if hardware is its strength, then the Internet and cloud services are its weakness.

In terms of cloud services, Xiaomi understands the Internet better and does better than Meizu. But from the operating system perspective, both MIUI and Flyme are deeply customized Android systems, yet MIUI is stronger in influence and compatibility with models.

Due to more compatible models, MIUI has a large number of users, which helps Xiaomi build its own ecosystem around MIUI. Lei Jun once stated at the Internet Conference in August that MIUI already has nine modules including an app store, game center, cloud service, security software, music, video, reading, and browser. Currently, MIUI's monthly revenue exceeds 20 million yuan.

MX3 increases efforts to attract operators, while MX2 sales remain a mystery

In the sale of MX2, Meizu cooperated with Unicom, and this cooperation continued to the MX3, and seemed to go smoother. At the MX3 launch, Bai Yongxiang announced that the price of the MX3 Unicom contract phone would be 2699 yuan.

The biggest benefit of cooperating with operators is the ability to fully utilize the operator's nationwide network for phone sales. Although Meizu has its own offline stores, the number is still limited and cannot cover more areas nationwide.

Although e-commerce has now become an important channel for phone sales, Meizu has not shifted its sales focus to e-commerce. In the sales of MX2, Meizu cooperated with JD.com, and Meizu also has its own online store, but the proportion of e-commerce sales for MX2 was only a small part of the total sales.

According to Meizu Vice President Mo Cuitian's previous interview with Tencent Technology, only about 10% of Meizu MX2 sales came from e-commerce channels, and Meizu would control the proportion of e-commerce sales to around 15%.

The reason Meizu does this is largely due to its offline stores being able to serve as a good sales channel. However, this sales method is obviously geographically restricted, so e-commerce channels are also indispensable for Meizu.

But no matter what sales model Meizu adopts, its product sales always remain a question to outsiders. Meizu has never publicly disclosed the sales volume of the MX2, and this has led to speculation about the poor sales of the MX2. Now, with the MX3 expected to be launched in late September, the sales of the MX3 will become a focal point of attention.
