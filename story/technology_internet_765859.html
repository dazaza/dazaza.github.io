US Nobel economics laureate: Apple's problems are worse than Microsoft's

by anonymous on 2013-08-26 14:18:09

Famed American economist and Nobel laureate in economics, Paul Krugman

On August 25th, news broke that Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer announced his intention to retire within a year. As a result, Microsoft's stock surged by approximately 7%, indirectly indicating that investors did not fully endorse Microsoft during the "Ballmer era." In contrast, Apple has become a leader in the mobile industry through products like the iPhone, iPad, and App Store.

However, renowned American economist and Nobel Prize winner Paul Krugman (Paul Krugman) recently penned an article in The New York Times stating that Apple faces more severe challenges than Microsoft. He argues that Apple's product advantages are becoming increasingly less significant, and it is entirely possible for competitors to surpass them in the future. Below is the main content of the article.

Although I am not an expert in high-tech fields, upon hearing about Ballmer’s retirement, I have formed some thoughts of my own. I believe that Apple's current strategy resembles Microsoft at its peak, which also suggests that Apple's decline may be imminent.

Most people are familiar with Microsoft's story. Both Microsoft and Apple sold their operating systems in the 1980s, with Apple's system being superior to Microsoft's. However, Apple misinterpreted the market. They believed, "We have a better operating system, so we will only install it on our beautifully designed computers and sell them at a high price." Meanwhile, Microsoft was already licensing their operating system to multiple manufacturers, allowing them to produce affordable computers. Over time, Microsoft dominated the PC market, while Apple's system and computers became the choice of only a small group of users. This situation continues today, and many people use Windows not necessarily because they like the system, but because everyone else uses Windows, creating a herd effect that makes it difficult for people to accept Apple's computer products.

However, Microsoft's mistaken strategies in the mobile device sector temporarily gave Apple an advantage. I use the word "temporarily" because I believe the quality advantage of Apple's products is no longer significant. I used to be an iPhone user, but now I use a Samsung phone and find little difference between the two. I also once purchased an iPad 2 mainly due to its excellent screen quality, but when I wanted to buy a tablet that could fit in my pocket, I found that the iPad Mini wasn't much better than other Android tablets.

Unlike Microsoft, Apple has always pursued a "premium route," but currently, the advantages of Apple's products over similar products are not obvious. Why can they still be sold at high prices? I believe one of the main reasons is the abundance of apps available on Apple devices.

Microsoft excels in dominating markets, and their related products have almost dictated the development of the PC industry for decades, continuing even today. In comparison, Apple's dominance is not secure. One reason is that Apple relies on the loyalty of regular users, while Microsoft primarily depends on the loyalty of enterprise IT managers, who tend to be more conservative.

Apple's products largely reflect the spirit of Steve Jobs, who knew what was good for users. Because of this, Apple products rarely leave room for users to think independently. If you're a non-typical user, you might find that performing simple tasks on iOS can be unnecessarily complicated.

For example, I enjoy watching live performances on YouTube. If my device cannot connect to the internet, I download them in MP4 format onto my PC using various software. Additionally, I like watching these videos on a tablet. When I tried to transfer these videos to my iPad, I found it quite frustrating. Although syncing with iTunes isn't overly complex, after importing the videos, they don't appear in separate folders, which isn't an issue on Android as you can simply create a new folder.

It appears that Apple needs to take more cues from its competitors regarding certain details or consider consumer needs more thoroughly instead of stubbornly doing things their way. After all, in the highly competitive mobile device market, paying attention to consumer needs is truly the "right path."
