Existing Problems and Improvement Directions of Wind Turbine Rotor in Wind Power Generators

by ditan360 on 2010-07-01 16:55:27

Exclusive to Low Carbon Network. Abstract: This article points out three problems of low efficiency in the current wind turbine rotor of wind generators and introduces the improvement direction of self-adaptive rotor based on invention patents. It can be started under the condition of 1 meter/second wind speed. Its performance is as follows: under the condition of 3-5 meters/second wind speed (which is the starting wind speed for large turbines, and a common wind speed in most areas), compared with the existing rotors of the same diameter, the power generation capacity will increase by at least 50%. Under higher wind speed conditions, the growth rate of power generation capacity will decrease but still increase.

Nowadays, global oil and coal resources are facing depletion, rising prices, and serious pollution. Therefore, countries around the world are developing renewable clean energy. Almost all hopes are pinned on wind and solar energy projects. However, the cost of power generation from these two sources is much higher than that of hydropower and thermal power. In my observation, existing wind turbines, especially large-scale ones, are mostly shut down. Some are turning but generating very little electricity; some places have become symbolic projects. The key to the efficiency of wind turbines lies in the rotor. The topic of my speech today is: the existing problems and improvement directions of wind turbine rotors.

I. Main Problems of Existing Fan Rotors

The condition for existing fans to generate rated power is a wind speed of 9-13 meters/second (6th level wind). In Beijing area, there are only a few days each year when winds above level 6 blow. Often, a fan has a large rated power, but its annual average power generation is very small. It is said that the average annual wind speed in Beijing is 3 meters/second, which is exactly the starting wind speed for large fans. How to generate electricity in Beijing? The efficiency of existing fans (i.e., the wind energy utilization coefficient Cp, the ratio of the generated power to the total power of the air flowing through the rotor area) is low. The main problems of the current rotor are as follows:

1. The solidity of the rotor (the ratio of the total blade area to the swept circular area) is low, causing a small wind energy utilization coefficient.

Most existing rotors of various sizes have only 3 blades, which are thin and long, with a solidity of about 4%. Comparable to helicopter rotor blades, the number of rotor blades increases with the weight of the helicopter. For example, the Zhi-9 helicopter weighing 4 tons has 4 rotor blades with a diameter of 11.5 meters. The Mi-26 weighing 56 tons has 8 rotor blades with a diameter of 32 meters. The solidity of the helicopter rotor is about 8%, approximately twice that of the wind turbine rotor. The rotor is a key technology for helicopters, and huge investments have been made by various countries for research and development, making it a mature technology. The helicopter rotor is a reliable reference for rotor design. Compared with the helicopter rotor, the wind turbine rotor naturally has lower efficiency due to its low solidity.

2. The twist angle of the rotor blades is fixed, which is another reason for the low wind energy utilization coefficient.

The twist angle of the rotor blades is generally designed for a wind speed of 8 meters/second, at which point the wind energy utilization coefficient is the highest. Since the wind speed size is random, whether the wind speed is less than or greater than 8 meters/second, the blade efficiency (lift-to-drag ratio) will decrease. This is completely different from the constant-speed rotation of helicopter rotors. Most of the time, the rotor blades work in a non-design state, not only resulting in low overall efficiency but also making it difficult to start.

3. The width of the current rotor blades cannot be increased to raise the solidity.

We know that aircraft wings are designed with a large aspect ratio (span-to-chord ratio) to achieve a high lift-to-drag ratio and efficiency. If the blade width is simply increased, the resistance will sharply increase, and the lift-to-drag ratio will decrease. The current rotor blades cannot increase the solidity and the wind energy utilization coefficient by increasing the width. There are many other problems, which I will not enumerate here.

II. Technical Improvement Direction

As shown in the figure above, let me introduce an example based on my invention patent "Self-adaptive Rotor for Wind Power Generation" (Patent No.: 2008 1 01015693.5). This rotor consists of 3-4 blades, which are relatively wide, and each blade is composed of several feathers. The main advantages are as follows:

1. This rotor can start under a wind speed of 1 meter/second and generate electricity at the rated power under gentle wind conditions (level 3 wind: 3.4-5.4 meters/second). It can be used in areas with relatively low wind speeds such as Beijing, and has a wide range of applications.

2. The performance of this rotor: under wind speeds of 3-5 meters/second (which is the starting wind speed for large turbines and a common wind speed in most areas), compared with existing rotors of the same diameter, the power generation capacity will increase by at least 50%. Under higher wind speed conditions, the growth rate of power generation capacity will decrease but still increase.

3. The principle is: since the rotor blades have the ability to adapt to wind speed and rotational speed, the feathers will deflect along the wind direction during startup, making the angle between the feathers and the airflow (feather angle of attack α) close to the optimal angle of attack, thus reducing the starting wind speed. As the rotor speed increases, the air flow direction will deflect in the opposite direction of the rotor rotation, and the feathers will rotate back towards the rotating plane, making the feather angle of attack equal or close to the optimal angle of attack within a certain range. Within a larger wind speed range, the lift coefficient Cy of the blades (Y=Cy•0.5ρV²•S) is large, while the drag coefficient Cx (X= Cx•0.5ρV²•S) is small, resulting in a high lift-to-drag ratio. The resultant force of the lift and drag components on the rotating plane is large, making the rotor rotate powerfully with high speed and large power generation.

4. There are gaps between the feathers, similar to bird wings, which increase the area, improve the solidity of the rotor, increase the lift, but do not significantly increase the resistance, resulting in a high lift-to-drag ratio.

In summary, the solidity of this rotor is more than twice that of existing fans; within a larger wind speed range, the angle of attack of the blades equals or approaches the optimal value, with self-adaptive capability and high rotational power. The result is: low starting wind speed (1 meter/second); strong micro-wind power generation capability, suitable for a wide range of regions, can rotate every day, and can generate electricity at the rated power for more than 50% of the days. When the rotor diameters are the same, the annual power generation will be greatly increased compared to existing turbines, and the cost of power generation may drop to the level of hydropower and thermal power. From a technical perspective, this rotor is a product of combining aerodynamics theory with biomimicry of bird wings. Its development prospect may be revolutionary.
