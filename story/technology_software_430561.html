Several days before the centipede lays eggs, feeding should be stopped, especially during the hatching period of 40-50 days when feeding should be strictly prohibited. Otherwise, the centipede may eat food that sticks to the egg mass or embryo, eating the egg mass or embryo together. The hatching process of the egg mass is relatively slow. After 15-16 days, the egg forms a kidney shape and begins to crack in the middle, which is the first molting stage. After 20 days, the second molting occurs and it takes on a crescent shape with the initial larval form. After 35-40 days, the young body enters the third molting phase and can crawl up and down in the mother's embrace. After another 5-7 days, the exoskeleton of the young body gradually thickens, turning yellow-brown, living under the protection of the mother. After a month, it can live independently and forage on its own.

In breeding centipedes, attention should be paid to possible viruses that may affect the centipedes themselves. On the other hand, breeders should prevent being stung and poisoned by centipedes.

Preventing centipedes from suffering diseases: Due to poultry, rats, and stone dragons eating centipedes, especially ants being the main natural enemy of centipedes, one should often observe whether there are any harmful insects appearing inside and outside the centipede field, preventing these natural enemies from entering the pool and attacking the centipedes. To prevent ants from entering the pool, you can use roach and ant powder pens to draw several circles around the pool, or mix camphor balls, vegetable oil, and sawdust in a five-to-one ratio, then frequently sprinkle this powder around the outer wall of the breeding pool to form a circle of medicine line. Especially during the molting, egg-laying, or hatching periods, centipedes have very weak resistance and are easily bitten to death by ants. Once ants enter the pool, the centipedes should be quickly transferred and the ants boiled to death.
