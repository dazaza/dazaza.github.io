Coloring (Painting) Steps

1. Scribbling (Random drawing)

2. Proper grip for scribbling on paper (pea-grasping, clip exercises), (thumb, index finger, and middle finger hold the pen, small fish muscle close to the table)

3. Coloring within boundaries, from wide boundaries to narrow ones, from small areas to large ones, from geometric shapes to real-world shapes

4. Copy coloring. Position copy coloring, color copy coloring

Painting Steps

5. Coloring as instructed

6. Drawing (copy drawing). Lines, straight lines, geometric shapes, same-line geometric shapes, external solid shapes, situational composition

7. Completing figures

8. Independent painting

9. Painting as instructed (verbal instructions, written instructions)

10. Thematic painting (Beautiful Home)

11. Creative painting (imagination ability)

Writing Steps

The first three steps are the same as painting

4. In-domain line drawing (maze solving), figures from large to small, figure distance from near to far, from wide to narrow, path from simple to complex

5. Connecting dots, from two points to multiple points, dot distance from near to far, connecting straight lines to connecting curves, in numerical order

6. Connecting dots to form shapes, from geometric shapes to real-world shapes

7. Tracing (strokes, numbers, Chinese characters)

8. Independent writing, strokes from few to many

9. Writing as instructed

Skills required for writing:

1. Upper arm can rotate with some strength

2. Wrist can rotate

3. Fingers can coordinate activities

Common problems:

1. Incorrect sitting posture (parents assist by pressing down on the child's body from behind, face-to-face interaction is recommended)

2. Cannot hold the pen correctly

3. No boundary sense when coloring (best assisted hand-in-hand for dependent children)

4. Uneven coloring, only one spot colored

5. Eyes not focused

6. Proportions not accurate

Paper-cutting Steps

1. Practicing tearing paper (correct way: left-right holding paper and tearing front-back) along the line

2. Able to open and close scissors for arbitrary cutting, practicing gripping and opening fists

3. Learn continuous random cutting

4. Cutting along lines (lines from thick to thin, lines from short to long, from straight to curved to angular to semi-circular)

5. Cutting and pasting geometric shapes (from large to small)

6. Cutting and pasting real-world shapes

Assistance notes:

1. Both hands coordinated, eyes follow the scissors

2. Tear paper along the line, left-right holding and tearing front-back

3. Correctly hold the scissors

4. Best assistance method involves two people, one behind and one opposite the table

5. Stop at turning points

6. Scissors perpendicular to the paper

7. Clear goal, tell the child how many lines to cut before resting

8. Timely assistance and reinforcement

Puzzle Board

One, Select single-piece puzzles

1. Number of pieces taken out increases gradually, cannot take all pieces out at once

2. Shapes from regular to irregular, such as starting with squares, from simple to complex

3. Present different orientations to the child to learn rotation

4. Follow instructions for placement combined with cognitive abilities

5. Let the child place pieces with both hands simultaneously to practice bilateral coordination

Two, Combine shapes

1. Number of pieces from few to many

2. Patterns from simple to complex, such as individual circles, squares

Three, Matching practice according to patterns

Four, Geometric shape shadows

Stringing beads

One, Training purpose: Bilateral coordination, hand-eye coordination

Two, Stages

1. Hard wire, holes from large to medium to small

2. Soft wire, holes same as above

Three, Teaching methods

1. Partial imitation threading, matching pairs

2. Full imitation threading, including color, shape, quantity

3. Threading as instructed

4. Threading according to patterns, such as red-yellow-blue-white

Building blocks

One, Partial imitation building (matching pairs) you build one block, I build one block, distinguishing between "yours" and "mine"

Two, Full imitation building

Three, Memory imitation building, training visual memory

Four, Building according to a diagram, including 1:1 scale and reduced scale

Five, Games, purpose: taking turns, waiting, you build one block, I build one block

Precautions:

1. Place blocks on the side most convenient for the child

2. Maintain consistent direction

3. Start with colorful blocks

4. Don't just stack blocks, also build sideways

Clay modeling steps

1. Let the child recognize playdough. It's soft and used for playing.

2. Instill a desire in the child to play with clay.

3. Squeeze the clay, bring four fingers together with the thumb.

4. Roll the clay into a ball using both hands alternately.

5. Pinch the clay.

6. Roll it into a long shape.

7. Roll it into a round shape.

8. Flatten it (press with the palm).

9. Press a pit (use an item or finger).

10. Pinch it sharp, use the thumb and index finger to pinch.

11. Shape with tools.

12. Mold simple shapes with one piece of clay (imitation and following instructions).

13. Combine flat shapes.

14. Combine clay pieces to complete three-dimensional shapes.

Paper folding steps

One, Basic actions

1. Correctly hold the paper, left and right thumbs underneath, other four fingers on top pinching the paper.

2. Correctly flip the paper.

3. Rotate the paper.

Steps:

1. Press flat.

2. Can fold arbitrarily.

3. Imitate folding in half, including edge-to-edge, corner-to-edge, corner-to-corner, edge-to-corner.

4. Imitate two-step folding.

5. Imitate folding more than two steps.

6. Imitate folding simple shapes.

7. Independently fold simple shapes.

8. Imitate combining folded shapes (flat).

9. Independently fold combined flat shapes.

10. Imitate folding three-dimensional shapes.

11. Independently fold three-dimensionally.

Two, Completion action steps:

1. Place the paper properly.

2. Eyes focus on the paper.

3. Correctly hold the paper with both hands.

Notes:

1. Let the child recognize edges and corners.

2. Recognize midpoints and midlines.

3. Recognize the front and back of folded paper.

4. Parents' paper-folding direction should be consistent with the child’s, either both folding northward or southward, i.e., if the child folds outward, you fold inward.

5. You can give the child a pre-folded crease.

Language Development Steps

One, Action pronunciation stage

Two, Imitation speaking stage

1. Single sounds.

2. Repeated sounds.

3. Two-sound combinations, like a,y,y,f, (clothes, auntie).

4. Three-sound combinations.

5. Short sentences.

6. Long sentences.

7. Complex sentences.

Three, Response stage. Concurrent with imitation speaking at any time.

1. Acknowledging: "Hey."

2. Answering questions, specific: "What is this?"

General question: "Is this...?"

Yes/No: "Is this...?"

Choice question: "Is it...or...? Is...not...? Want...don't want...?" Children tend to prefer the latter option.

3. Abstract sentences. Answering why questions, answering if questions.

Four, Active expression stage

1. Situational expressions

2. Emotional expressions

3. Using questions, asking: "What is this?" "...where?" "What corresponds to...?" "Who...?" etc., to seek answers.

4. Descriptive language

Describe pictures/items in one sentence.

Use several adjectives to describe items.

Describe how to do something.

Describe a topic.

Describe similarities and differences of things.

Five, Communication stage

1. Exchange information

2. Relay information as instructed, using names instead of "you" or "he/she."

3. Emotional communication

Chapter Six: Social Skills Training for Students with Autism

Training Goals

6.1 Students with autism are not adept at learning general social behavior patterns through daily social activities and even less so in understanding others' expressions and actions. Therefore, teachers must systematically train students' social skills, helping them early on to recognize and follow social norms, enabling them to learn socially accepted behaviors, making it easier for them to be accepted, develop appropriate interpersonal relationships, integrate into communities, and live independently as they grow up.

6.2 Additionally, teachers should help them improve inappropriate social, emotional, and behavioral issues. How to handle students' behavioral problems will be detailed in the next chapter.

Training Content

6.3 The content of social skills training may include:

(a) Interacting with others and building relationships

- Enhance students' awareness of others' existence. For example, activities in front of students to attract their attention or games based on their preferences to stimulate interest and reactions;

- Guide students to make eye contact with others. Teachers can actively adjust to their eye level or touch their bodies to ensure eye contact with others. Once eye contact is made, immediately speak or make facial expressions/actions to elicit responses;

- Create opportunities where students need help from others. For instance, placing toys they like in places they can't reach or suddenly stopping activities they enjoy, prompting them to signal to the teacher for toys or to continue the activity, thus mastering the skill of seeking help from others.

(b) Training game skills to enhance foundational learning abilities

- Provide various toys and demonstrate ways to play with them, such as pushing cars, stacking blocks, etc., avoiding students playing with the same toy or object in only one way;

- Train focus, such as observing how others act or play with toys;

- Train imitation skills, such as mimicking others' speech or actions;

- Guide students to participate in group games, allowing them to learn simple game rules like taking turns and cooperation;

- Enhance imaginative abilities, such as pretending to be a teacher, doctor, etc.;

- Establish reward and punishment systems, helping students understand winning and losing rules, learning to face victories and defeats.

(c) Learning general social norms

- Learning social etiquette and language usage, such as saying "Good morning," "Goodbye," "Sorry," "Thank you," emphasizing maintaining eye contact while speaking or signaling;

- Following simple rules, such as listening, sitting quietly, waiting, following orders, not interrupting others' conversations, etc.;

- Learning to share and cooperate in activities, such as sharing food, playing group games, etc.;

- Learning appropriate social responses, such as responding to others' requests;

- Learning to initiate social interactions, such as greeting others proactively;

- Expanding social circles, arranging opportunities for students to interact with different people, enhancing and training their social skills to adapt to various social situations.

(d) Recognizing and controlling one's own emotions

- Recognizing and expressing different emotions, such as happiness, anger, sadness, joy, etc.;

- Learning how to appropriately express one's emotions to others, such as happiness, fear, anger, etc.;

- Learning early methods to seek help from others and self-relaxation techniques to relieve anxiety.

(e) Developing appropriate interpersonal relationships

- Learning to care for, help, respect, and respect others;

- Understanding that different relationships require appropriate social distances. Teachers should explain the differences between intimate behaviors and social etiquette, such as hugging, kissing, waving, shaking hands, etc.

(f) Understanding others' expressions and actions, comprehending others' thoughts and feelings

- Through games, watching videos, etc., guide students to discuss, helping them understand others' thoughts and feelings, cultivating empathy;

- Assist students in understanding the intentions behind others' actions and expressions, teaching them to make appropriate social responses, such as smiling greetings, waving goodbyes, etc.

(g) Cultivating habits for utilizing leisure time

- Learning to use community public facilities, such as parks, libraries, youth centers, etc.;

- Arranging participation in recreational and leisure activities;

- Cultivating good interests and hobbies.

Training Principles and Methods

6.4 When conducting social skills training, it should be progressive, first attracting students' attention to others, letting them know others exist, then creating opportunities, encouraging students to interact with others, and assisting them in mastering social interaction skills. Training methods can start from initially needing a lot of prompts in a passive mode, gradually transitioning to an active mode without prompts. When setting individualized training goals for students, teachers must determine the starting point based on the student's ability and needs.

6.5 Social skills training should not be limited to certain lessons or venues but should permeate every aspect of students' activity schedules. Teachers can provide intensive training individually or in pairs and arrange social activities in real-life contexts, such as group games, tea parties, etc., helping them learn and apply social skills. The training content should cater to students' underdeveloped social skills while repeatedly practicing newly learned social skills to consolidate what they've learned.

6.6 Considering the stubborn characteristics of students with autism, teachers should teach appropriate social skills from an early age, developing proper interpersonal relationships. Otherwise, once habits are formed, they become difficult to correct.

6.7 Use visual aids such as images and text to assist students in engaging in sustained social activities around specific themes, strengthening their social interaction skills. For example:

(a) Social stories;

(b) Comic-style dialogues - through simple character graphics, symbols, text, and colors, students engage in interactive paper conversations to express opinions and feelings.

6.8 Teachers can film students' behavior clips, allowing them to observe their own behavior and conduct social behavior assessments together. This helps students understand what constitutes appropriate or inappropriate social behavior, leading to necessary corrections.

6.9 Teachers can instruct students to observe and follow classmates during social activities or arrange peers as guides and demonstrators, allowing them to imitate and master appropriate social skills.

Community Integration Arrangements

6.10 To assist students with autism in integrating into the community, schools and parents must actively create opportunities for them to interact with society. For example:

(a) Arrange practical experiential learning activities to help students understand the community environment near schools and homes, making it easier for them to adapt to community life;

(b) Collaborate with linked schools or district schools to organize cross-school activities, expanding students' social circles and experiencing group life;

(c) Arrange service community activities for students, such as visiting the elderly, cleaning the community, cultivating a willingness to serve society, and promoting community members' understanding and acceptance of students with autism, enabling them to integrate into the community;

Tearing Paper: Game Objectives:

1. Exercise baby's bilateral coordination.

Game Preparation: New books, magazines, napkins, easy-to-tear colored paper, glue.

Game Process: 1. Mom shows the book, magazine, napkin, and colored paper one by one, “This is Mom's book, this is Dad's magazine, this is baby's napkin, put it on the table.”

2. Mom shows the colored paper, “This is colored paper, it can be torn, tear—tear—tear, big paper becomes small paper!”

3. Mom recites nursery rhymes while tearing paper pieces with the baby and helps the baby put small paper pieces in the box to avoid throwing them all over the floor. (Little baby, tears colored paper, tears—tears—tears, big paper becomes small paper.)

4. Mom shows the sun made of colorful small paper pieces, “Baby look, this is the sun, there are many small paper pieces, red ones, yellow ones.”

5. Mom says, “Baby has small paper pieces, let's make a sun.” Recite nursery rhymes while making the sun with the baby. (Little baby, paste the sun, paste—paste—paste, small paper becomes the sun.)

6. Mom points to the sun the baby has pasted and says, “Baby is really capable, pasted a lovely sun, just like the baby, red ones, yellow ones, very beautiful!”

7. Mom picks up the sun and hangs it in the “Baby Talent Column” while gently kissing the baby to the rhythm of the nursery rhyme. (Little baby, really capable, haha, Mom loves baby!)

Game Extension: Older babies can tear regular patterns with the baby, such as the sun, noodles, etc., or draw patterns on the paper and let the baby tear along the edges of the cute animal patterns.

Precautions:

1. For the baby's health, avoid giving the baby polluted or dirty paper, such as newspapers, which have high lead content. Since babies at this age like to put things in their mouths, try to avoid letting the baby come into contact with such paper.

2. When the baby starts learning to tear paper, it's a milestone in the baby's development, indicating a significant leap in fine motor skills. Moms shouldn’t stop the baby but should provide clean paper and tell the baby which paper can be torn and which cannot, helping the baby develop good habits.
