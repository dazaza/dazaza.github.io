Who dares to use a smartphone if it can be easily tapped?

by mylovehisa on 2007-07-30 16:14:25

Recently, a eavesdropping software named "X Undercover" that supports all Symbian and Windows Mobile smartphones has been sold at a high price online. If someone secretly installs the "X Undercover" (also known as Xwodi) software on your smartphone, anyone who dials your number will be able to listen to everything you say. The supported phones almost cover all models of Nokia and Dopod, as well as Samsung, Lenovo, HP and other brands that support Symbian and Windows Mobile. Be careful when using smartphones!

Carefully analyzing the technical principles of X Undercover should be simple. It's just that there was no such product before. If my analysis is correct, the so-called X Undercover technically installs a software that initiates three-party calls on your phone. Whenever you make or receive a call, X Undercover automatically activates the three-party call function and transfers your call to a specified third-party phone. SMS forwarding is naturally even simpler and almost any phone can support it.

Although the technology is simple and the software isn't complex, it is sold online for as high as 6800 yuan. The reason why it has received so much attention is that once your phone is being listened to by others, you will have no privacy left. Once X Undercover and similar software become widespread, its impact on society will definitely not be limited to the value of a single software, but will be multifaceted:

In the near future, software similar to X Undercover may be freely available online.

The technical difficulty is low, and the concept is simple. Engineers with some programming foundation and communication knowledge can write similar software. Perhaps in the near future, such software will be available for free download everywhere, and might even be easier than downloading a song.

The confrontation between morality and law will become a social hotspot.

Once privacy cannot be protected, confrontation with the law will be inevitable. Legal disputes caused by privacy issues will increase exponentially. Although developers can position the use of X Undercover within the scope of business competition, it can be certain that its use among family and friends will be far more common. After all, it's very difficult to install eavesdropping software on a competitor’s phone, whereas the chances of a family member's or friend’s phone being tapped are much higher.

Smartphone sales could be significantly affected.

Nokia and Microsoft probably didn’t expect that so-called technologically advanced smartphones would bring such trouble, becoming a platform for infringing on privacy. If this issue is not effectively resolved, many people will likely prefer traditional phones for peace of mind.

Phones will become confidential items like ID cards and bank cards.

People generally do not easily reveal their ID numbers, nor do they show their bank account details casually, simply because these involve personal safety and property. If personal privacy cannot be effectively protected, phones will become another highly confidential personal item, alongside ID cards and bank cards, which people are unwilling to show others.

After saying all this, if you use a smartphone, do you still feel safe? Would you still buy a smartphone?
