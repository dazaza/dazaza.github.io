1. Search Engine Marketing (SEM)

Search Engine Marketing (SEM) is the full English term, which refers to marketing services and products through search engines. SEM is mainly divided into two types: Search Engine Optimization (SEO) and Pay-Per-Click (PPC). SEO involves optimizing websites and pages so they appear in search results, while PPC involves purchasing clicks from search engines to direct users to your website. These clicks typically come from "sponsored links" on the search results page.

2. Backlink

A backlink is also known as an "incoming link" or "simple link," referring to a hyperlink from another website pointing to yours. Backlinks are crucial for SEO because they directly affect a webpage's PageRank and its ranking in search results.

3. PageRank

PageRank is a Google algorithm used to evaluate the importance of one page relative to others. The basic idea is that if Page A links to Page B, it can be seen as Page A trusting or recommending Page B. Therefore, the more backlinks a page has, and the higher the value of those links, the more important the page is considered by search engines, resulting in a higher PageRank.

4. Linkbait

Linkbait refers to content on a website or blog designed to attract as many backlinks as possible to increase the site's PR value. Typically, this content is textual but can also include videos, images, quizzes, or other popular content. Classic examples of linkbait are "Top 10" lists, which easily become hot topics on social networking sites.

5. Link Farm

A link farm refers to a group of websites (usually large in number) that form a workshop-like entity to boost each other's page rankings through mutual linking. While effective in the past, this method is now considered a disreputable cheating tactic and may result in penalties.

6. Anchor Text

Anchor text refers to the clickable text in a hyperlink within a page. The keywords in anchor text significantly aid in SEO because Google associates these keywords with your content.

7. NoFollow Attribute

NoFollow is an attribute used by webmasters to inform search engines that they are not endorsing the linked website. Such links might be user-generated content (like blog comments) or paid links (such as advertisements or sponsorships). When Google identifies NoFollow attributes in backlinks, it generally does not consider these links in its algorithms for page ranking and search result positioning.

8. Link Sculpting

Link sculpting involves webmasters setting properties for outbound links on their websites. For example, adding a NoFollow attribute allows webmasters to decide whether to help specific websites improve their page rankings or not. However, this practice is now largely ineffective as Google has decided to use its own algorithm to handle NoFollow attributes.

9. Title Tag

The title tag refers to the title of a webpage, which is one of the most critical factors in Google's search algorithm. Ideally, your title should be unique and include as many relevant keywords from your page content as possible. You can see a webpage's title at the very top of your browser when browsing.

10. Meta Tags

Meta tags, like title tags, provide additional information about your page content to search engines. They are located in the head section of HTML code and are invisible to visitors.

11. Search Algorithm

Google's search algorithm is designed to find the most relevant websites and pages for each search query. This algorithm considers over 200 factors (as claimed by Google), including PageRank, titles, meta tags, website content, and domain age.

12. Search Engine Results Page (SERP)

The full English term for SERP is Search Engine Results Page. Essentially, this is the page you see after entering a keyword in Google and hitting enter. The traffic your website receives from this page depends on your site's ranking in the search results.

13. Sandbox

In addition to its main index, Google has a separate sandbox index for newly discovered and indexed websites. While your website is in this sandbox, it will not appear in regular search results until Google confirms its legitimacy and moves it out of the sandbox into the main index.

14. Keyword Density

To calculate the keyword density of a specific page, divide the number of times a keyword is used by the total number of words on the page. Keyword density was once a crucial factor in SEO due to early search algorithms placing significant emphasis on it, but this is no longer the case.

15. Keyword Stuffing

Because early search algorithms heavily emphasized keyword density, webmasters would cheat and deceive search engines by artificially stuffing keywords onto their pages. This technique, called keyword stuffing, is now ineffective and may even result in penalties.

16. Cloaking

Cloaking involves using code to show different content to search engine bots than what is shown to human visitors. The purpose is to manipulate page rankings with certain keywords while actually promoting unrelated products or services. This is a deceptive tactic that could lead to severe penalties or even removal from search engines.

17. Web Crawler

A web crawler, also known as a spider, represents search engines browsing the web to discover new links and pages. This is the first step in the indexing process.

18. Duplicate Content

Duplicate content refers to substantial portions of a page being copied either from within the same site or from external sources, or pages being entirely identical or extremely similar. This is a situation that websites should avoid as it may lead to penalties.

19. Canonical URL

A canonical URL ensures that a webpage's link conforms to standard and established guidelines.

20. Robots.txt

Robots.txt is a simple text file located in the root directory of your domain. It provides directory structure information to crawlers and spiders, restricting access to certain folders and content or completely blocking them.
