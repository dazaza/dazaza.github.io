When it comes to qi and blood, most people think it's something mysterious. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), a person's standard of health is having sufficient qi and blood. The organs in the human body are like people; when they are full, they work with vigor. And blood is the "food" for the organs.

1. Look at the eyes: Looking at the eyes actually involves observing the color of the whites of the eyes. As the saying goes, "as one gets older, the pearls turn yellow," which refers to the whites of the eyes becoming cloudy, yellowish, with bloodshot veins, indicating insufficient qi and blood. If the eyes can always be wide open, it shows that the qi and blood are sufficient; conversely, large bags under the eyes, dry eyes, and heavy eyelids all represent insufficient qi and blood.

2. Look at the skin: Skin that is white with a pinkish glow, glossy, elastic, without wrinkles or spots represents sufficient qi and blood. Conversely, rough skin, lacking luster, dark, yellow, white, bluish, reddish, or spotted skin indicates poor physical condition and insufficient qi and blood.

3. Look at the hair: Black, dense, and smooth hair represents sufficient qi and blood, while dry, falling out, yellow, white, split ends indicate insufficient qi and blood.

4. Look at the ears: Nowadays, people's physical constitutions are getting worse. If you look carefully, you'll notice that people's ears are smaller, appearing stiffer, and their shapes seem somewhat deformed. Even children and young people rarely have round, plump, and full ears anymore. However, these big ears are quite common among the elderly, indicating that people in the past were physically stronger than modern people.

For children, observe the shape of the ears, while adults need to consider both the shape and the postnatal conditions, mainly focusing on color, presence of freckles, and any pain. If the ears appear pale pink, glossy, without black spots or wrinkles, and are full, it represents sufficient qi and blood. Dullness and lack of luster indicate a decline in qi and blood. Shrinking, dryness, spots, and many wrinkles on the ears indicate a decline in kidney function, which requires attention.

5. Feel the temperature of the hands: If the hands are warm all year round, it indicates sufficient qi and blood. If the hands are hot or sweaty, or icy cold, it indicates insufficient qi and blood.

6. Look at the fingertips: Whether for children or adults, if the fingertips are flat, thin, or the tips are narrow, it indicates insufficient qi and blood. Full fingertips with meaty elasticity indicate sufficient qi and blood.

7. Look at the veins: If there are blue veins visible on the index finger of an adult, it suggests poor digestive function in childhood, and this condition has continued into adulthood. Such individuals tend to have weak constitutions and deficiencies in both qi and blood. If there are blue veins on the little finger, it indicates insufficient kidney qi.

If there are many deep lines near the wrist crease on the palm, it indicates poor nutrition and a weak constitution in childhood, with insufficient qi and blood. In adulthood, such women are prone to gynecological diseases, and men are prone to prostate enlargement and gout.

8. Look at the half-moon shapes on the nails: Normally, half-moons should be present on all fingers except the little finger. On the thumb, the half-moon should occupy 1/4 to 1/5 of the nail area, and on other fingers like the index, middle, and ring fingers, it should not exceed 1/5.

If there are no half-moons or only on the thumb, it indicates excessive internal coldness, poor circulation, and insufficient qi and blood, meaning the blood does not reach the fingertips. If there are too many or overly large half-moons, it increases the risk of hyperthyroidism or hypertension.

9. Look at the longitudinal lines on the fingernails: These only appear on adults, not on children. When longitudinal lines appear on adult fingernails, it is a warning sign indicating dual deficiencies in qi and blood, representing bodily depletion and a sign of aging.

10. Look at the gums: This is mainly for adults, not obvious in children. Gum recession indicates insufficient qi and blood. If you notice larger gaps between your teeth where food easily gets stuck, it suggests your body is declining and aging is accelerating.

11. Look at sleep: Adults who fall asleep quickly like children, sleep soundly, breathe evenly, and wake up naturally indicate sufficient qi and blood. Those who have difficulty falling asleep, wake up easily, urinate frequently at night, breathe heavily, or snore are deficient in blood.

12. Look at physical activity: During exercise, if you experience chest tightness, shortness of breath, and fatigue that doesn't recover easily, it indicates insufficient qi and blood. On the contrary, those who feel refreshed and relaxed after exercise are in good health.

Are all cold foods unsuitable to eat?

Not all cold foods are harmful when consumed. As long as they match a person's constitution and the season, they can balance and harmonize the body. For example, in summer, the body sweats profusely, and eating an appropriate amount of watermelon, which is cooling, can remove heat and replenish water and sugar lost due to sweating. In this case, watermelon can harmonize and replenish blood. However, eating watermelon in cold weather can lead to blood deficiency.

Cold and hot foods should be eaten in combination. For example, when eating crabs, which are very cold, they must be paired with ginger, which is warm, to neutralize the coldness of the crab. This not only avoids harm but also aids in the digestion and absorption of the crab meat.

Are black foods necessarily kidney-nourishing foods?

We often see claims that black foods nourish the kidneys and replenish blood, such as black sesame seeds, black beans, black rice, black fungus, kelp, nori, and black-boned chickens. However, this isn't always true. Warmth nourishes, while coldness drains. Black rice and black-boned chickens are warming, and their blood-nourishing and kidney-nourishing effects are significant. Black sesame seeds are neutral and can nourish the kidneys, liver, moisten the intestines, and nourish hair. Black beans are neutral, nourish the kidneys, invigorate blood circulation, and detoxify. However, black fungus is cooling, and kelp and nori are cold. They can be eaten frequently in summer but should be avoided in winter. Whether a food is nourishing or not depends on its nature. Foods that are neutral or warming can benefit the body throughout the year. Cooling or cold foods should be avoided except in summer. If you must eat them, pair them with warming ingredients like ginger, chili, pepper, or Sichuan pepper. This way, you can intake the unique nutrients of these foods without harming the kidneys or stomach.

Popular family blood-nourishing recipes

In daily diets, there are many blood-nourishing recipes. Interested friends can try the methods introduced below or change things up to make them more appealing to family members.

Jujubes, longan, peanuts, red beans, brown sugar, white fruits, and wolfberries are commonly consumed foods that nourish blood and kidneys. Combining them creates excellent blood-nourishing dietary therapy recipes.

Jujubes are the most commonly used food for nourishing blood, and eating them raw or soaking them in alcohol is the most effective.

Jujubes can also be stir-fried in an iron pot until they turn black and then soaked in water to drink. This can treat stomach cold and stomach pain. Adding longan makes it a blood-nourishing and qi-supplementing tea, especially suitable for teachers, salespeople, and others who use their voices frequently. Adding 4-6 wolfberries can also treat constipation, but those with loose stools should not add wolfberries. Women who regularly drink jujube, longan, and wolfberry tea have clear skin and good beauty results. Don't add too many wolfberries; a few grains are enough. Six to eight jujubes and longans are sufficient. Drink a cup every morning at work, not only replenishing qi and blood but also improving eyesight, especially suitable for computer-bound workaholics.

Drinking plain jujube tea without stir-frying them in an iron pot until hard and black is ineffective because the outer skin prevents the nutrients from being released. After stir-frying, the jujubes crack open when boiled in hot water, allowing the nutrients to gradually seep out.

Cooking jujubes, peanuts, longan, and brown sugar together in water and slowly stewing them until soft provides excellent blood-nourishing effects.

Cooking jujubes, red beans, and sticky rice together into porridge is also a delicious blood-nourishing dish. Since red beans are harder to cook, you can boil them first until soft before adding the sticky rice and jujubes.
