Eight Mindset Viruses to Avoid in Job Interviews

by jintaimqr on 2012-02-22 17:42:41

Job hunting [http://www.jt077.cn/index.php?m=job] improperly affects many people who are looking for jobs. Not only can they not find work, but they also struggle in their job search in a state of confusion. In fact, avoiding certain factors during the job search process can lead to immediate success. We have summarized the following objective factors for reference by blind job seekers [http://www.jt077.cn/index.php?m=job].

Virus One: The falsity and restlessness of job interviews

Facing endless recruitment fairs, which university graduates don't attend every one, handing out resumes like distributing flyers. As the saying goes, floating inevitably shows, and restlessness leads to urgency. Thus, various [toxins] gradually seep into the talent market—fake diplomas, fake degrees, fake certificates, as well as beautifully bound false resumes and even superficially packaged talents. University students should have [rules] and [distance] when facing interviews. First, maintaining the essence of being a student is most important—introducing oneself clearly and concisely, answering questions accurately, and then candidly acknowledging one's shortcomings rather than avoiding them.

Virus Two: Professional ideals as an excuse

University graduates often blame their inability to find suitable work on studying something they do not love or cannot apply practically. When you cannot find work in your [interests], you must learn to find [interest] in work.

Virus Three: Career selection demanding "social equality"

Many people like to classify job choices by social status, such as Peking University and Tsinghua University corresponding to IBM and HP, etc., but this results in many feeling disheartened or even giving up when they don't achieve their desired positions. University graduates should not have such elitist views; heroes do not ask about origins. The door of career choice is open to every university graduate, but overestimating or underestimating oneself will both lead to feelings of loss.

Virus Four: Employment after graduation focuses only on taking

When choosing careers after graduation, university graduates generally have a psychological misconception: they believe that after years of hard study, it is time to "take" from society, feeling that they deserve and should gain rewards. This psychological positioning first causes maladjustment to the environment, such as stopping in the face of difficulties at work and later blaming others.

Virus Five: Talent "over-cultivation"

Nowadays, university graduates entering the workforce all say they feel too much pressure. Why does the taste of tomatoes not satisfy anymore? Why do the fresh cucumbers no longer fill the house with fragrance? The reason lies in the use of catalysts, or what could be called the modern version of "over-cultivation."

Virus Six: Inappropriate attire at job fairs

The dress code for attending job fairs should avoid two extremes: one is being overly casual, which shows a lack of quality; the other is "imitation," where dressing too formally appears unnatural.
