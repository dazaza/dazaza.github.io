First Article: About Earthquakes

The structure of the Earth is like an egg, which can be divided into three layers. The central layer is the "yolk" - the core; the middle layer is the "white" - the mantle; and the outer layer is the "shell" - the crust. Earthquakes generally occur within the crust. As the Earth continuously rotates on its axis and orbits around the sun, changes are also happening inside the crust. This creates forces that cause the crust's rock layers to deform, crack, and shift, resulting in earthquakes.

The place underground where an earthquake occurs is called the hypocenter (or focus). The point directly above the hypocenter on the surface is called the epicenter. The distance from the epicenter to the hypocenter is known as the focal depth. Earthquakes with a focal depth less than 70 kilometers are shallow-focus earthquakes; those between 70-300 kilometers are mid-focus earthquakes; and those exceeding 300 kilometers are deep-focus earthquakes. The deepest recorded earthquake was a 5.8-magnitude quake in 1963 off the northern coast of Papua Province, Indonesia, with a focal depth of 786 kilometers. For earthquakes of the same magnitude, differences in focal depth affect both the intensity and the degree of destruction caused to the ground. Shallower the focus, greater the damage, but the affected area will be smaller, and vice versa.

The distance from a location to the epicenter is called the epicentral distance. Earthquakes with an epicentral distance less than 100 kilometers are referred to as local earthquakes; those between 100-1000 kilometers are near earthquakes; and those over 1000 kilometers are far earthquakes. The farther the epicentral distance, the less the impact and destruction experienced.

The ground vibration caused by earthquakes is a complex motion, resulting from the combined effects of P-waves and S-waves. In the epicentral area, P-waves make the ground move up and down, while S-waves cause horizontal shaking. Since P-waves travel faster but decay quickly, and S-waves travel slower but decay more slowly, places further from the epicenter may not feel vertical jolts but can experience horizontal swaying.

The size of the earthquake itself is expressed in terms of magnitude, determined by the amount of elastic wave energy released during the quake. China generally uses the Richter scale. Earthquakes below 2.5 are classified as minor quakes; those between 2.5-4.7 are felt earthquakes; and those above 4.7 are destructive earthquakes. Each increase in magnitude corresponds to about 30 times more energy being released. For instance, a 7-magnitude earthquake equals the energy of 30 6-magnitude earthquakes or 900 5-magnitude earthquakes. A difference of 0.1 in magnitude equates to approximately 1.4 times more energy released.

When a large earthquake occurs, a series of aftershocks often follow within a certain period. The largest earthquake in this sequence is called the mainshock, those before it are foreshocks, and those after it are aftershocks.

Second Article: Earthquake Evacuation and Self-rescue

1. Emergency self-help at home during an earthquake

If you're at home when an earthquake strikes, do not rush out recklessly, especially if you live in high-rise buildings or densely populated apartment complexes. Instead, choose a safer spot indoors to take cover, such as under a bed or table. In unit-style buildings, you can hide in small rooms like bathrooms, kitchens, storage rooms, or against wall corners. These areas have smaller spans, reinforced concrete structures offering better overall stability, and often have support from water pipes, making them relatively safe. When rushing out hastily, falling railings, balconies, flower pots, and building facades might injure pedestrians.

After the earthquake, people should evacuate damaged buildings in an organized and orderly manner. Residents in high-rise buildings must never jump out of windows or use elevators when moving downward. Balconies and staircases are the weakest parts of a building in terms of tensile strength, while elevators can jam or deform during earthquakes, making jumping extremely dangerous. During the 1976 Liyang earthquake in Jiangsu province, among 654 severely injured victims, 524 were hurt just as they escaped outside and were struck by collapsing eaves walls. Among the 41 fatalities, 30 were killed under similar circumstances.

In rural areas, where there are fewer high-rise buildings and open spaces, you can escape outdoors during an earthquake. If there isn't enough time, it's best to seek shelter indoors, such as under tables or beds, relying on their support to block falling cement blocks and bricks. Stay away from windows. When evacuating, it’s preferable to wear something protective on your head like a quilt, pillow, or safety helmet. To prevent doors from deforming and becoming stuck during an earthquake, if your home allows for it, don’t close the door during the quake. At night, quickly move to a safe area. Many urban and rural houses fail to meet national seismic fortification standards and require renovation and reinforcement according to government requirements. In the event of a destructive earthquake, escape outdoors promptly. After an earthquake, immediately cut off power, shut off gas, and extinguish fires to prevent secondary disasters like fire and gas leaks.

2. Emergency self-help outdoors during an earthquake

(1) Domestic earthquake examples show that during the first few seconds to tens of seconds of an earthquake, entering or leaving buildings poses the highest risk of injury or death. We do not recommend indoor residents panicking and running outside. Similarly, if you're already outside, remember not to enter a shaking building to rescue loved ones. Control your emotions to avoid greater casualties. First preserve yourself so you can help rescue family and friends after the earthquake.

(2) During a major earthquake, cars behave as if their tires have deflated, making steering difficult. Pay full attention, avoid intersections, and pull over to the side of the road. Leave the center part of the road clear for evacuees and emergency vehicles. Most roads in urban centers will be fully closed. Listen carefully to car radios and follow police instructions if present. If evacuation is necessary, to avoid being caught in a fire, keep car windows closed, leave keys in the ignition without locking the doors, and act together with locals.

Train drivers should apply emergency brakes gradually to avoid derailment and other catastrophic events. Passengers (especially on trains) should firmly hold onto handrails, poles, or seats and beware of falling luggage. Facing the direction of travel, rest your arms on the seat cushion ahead, protect your face, lean towards the aisle, and shield your head with both hands. Backing the direction of travel, use both hands to protect the back of your head, lift your knees to protect your abdomen, and contract your body into a defensive posture.

(3) If you're walking on the street during an earthquake, glass fragments from tall buildings, chunks of concrete from building exteriors, signs, tin plates, and neon light frames could fall and injure people. It’s advisable to use a bag or soft item to protect your head or use your hands if no items are available. Quickly move away from transformers, utility poles, walls, narrow alleys, and head toward open, spacious areas.

(4) If you're in a chemical plant emitting toxic gases during an earthquake, run upwind away from the source of pollution. If a victim is suffering from chlorine poisoning, do not perform artificial respiration until they've been moved to a safe area for immediate treatment.

(5) Residents and villagers living near mountains should quickly evacuate to safe areas to avoid hazards caused by landslides, collapses, and mudflows triggered by earthquakes. If on a slope, do not run downhill with rolling rocks; instead, hide behind elevated ridges or crouch in ditches, protecting your head. Also, stay away from steep cliffs to avoid being harmed by landslides and mudflows.

(6) People living by the sea or on boats should be cautious of potential tsunamis if they notice seawater suddenly receding faster and lower than usual. Boat personnel should swiftly evacuate to land and move to higher ground.

In summary, many accidents occur outdoors during earthquakes, and each person should assess the situation and adopt flexible emergency measures accordingly.

3. Emergency self-help at work during an earthquake

(1) In office buildings, quickly hide under desks and then orderly evacuate via stairs to a safe location post-earthquake.

(2) Factory workers should immediately shut down machines, cut off power, and evacuate quickly.

(3) Underground miners should stop production immediately, avoid hazardous areas like tunnels or shafts, and seek shelter in supported tunnels. After the earthquake, evacuate to the surface in an organized and orderly manner.

(4) Personnel handling or producing toxic, radioactive, or bacterial substances should immediately follow procedures to halt operations and take swift measures to protect themselves from earthquakes. For example, quickly move one type of chemically incompatible substance outdoors or isolate it; extinguish flames; secure containers holding substances that ignite easily in air; and close valves controlling the production and storage of toxic gases. If necessary, neutralize toxic gases quickly to eliminate possible indirect losses or secondary disasters. After most personnel have evacuated, a few should remain in pre-reinforced, supported areas to monitor conditions and handle emergencies promptly.

(5) High-temperature, high-pressure workers should immediately cease production following established procedures and regulations, urgently closing and handling sources that could cause disasters, eliminating potential hazards, and ensuring absolute safety during the earthquake shock.

(6) High-altitude workers should immediately stop working, lower their center of gravity, or return to the ground and find a safe place to take cover.

(7) Ships at sea or fishing should stop navigation upon noticing unusual phenomena and dock nearby.

(8) Computer systems for automatic control, data storage, and important archives should have earthquake-resistant measures to prevent memory loss.

(9) Medical staff performing surgery or assisting childbirth should continue their duties amidst an earthquake, ideally reinforcing operating and delivery rooms beforehand with steel supports and dust covers.

4. Emergency self-help in public places during an earthquake

(1) Cinemas and large entertainment venues usually have thin-shell roofs with wide spans that are lightweight and unlikely to collapse easily during an earthquake. Audience members should immediately hide under rows of seats. Though individual plywood panels may not be very strong, the collective strength of multiple rows connected by cast iron frames and screws is significant. Additionally, under the stage and orchestra pits are good places to take cover. After the earthquake stops, organize an orderly evacuation through multiple exits.

(2) Sports arenas should immediately halt competitions, calm the audience, and prevent panic and crowding. After the earthquake, guide spectators out of the stadium in an organized manner.

(3) Students in class should hide under desks or next to lecterns if in sturdy, safe buildings. Those in teaching buildings can move to small rooms with pipe supports. Never let students run around or jump from buildings. When the earthquake stops, quickly evacuate classrooms under teacher guidance and gather in open areas to take cover.

(4) Children in kindergartens or nurseries should have their buildings reinforced beforehand. During an earthquake, caregivers should organize children to take cover under tables and lead them to safety afterward.

(5) In department stores, maintain composure. Panic and falling merchandise can obstruct escape routes. Hide near large columns or big goods (avoid display windows), or move toward unobstructed passages, then squat and wait for the quake to subside. Ideally, move to lower floors, but staircases are often weak points in buildings, so wait for the right moment to escape. Salespeople should guide customers to nearby shelters and evacuate safely afterward.

5. Emergency self-help if trapped under debris during an earthquake

If buried under rubble during an earthquake, with only a tiny space and darkness all around, do not panic. Stay calm, believe help is coming, and do everything possible to protect yourself.

After an earthquake, aftershocks may occur, potentially worsening your situation. To avoid new injuries, try to improve your environment. If an emergency kit is nearby, it can significantly aid your escape.

In this dire situation, use towels, clothes, or sleeves to cover your mouth and nose to avoid accidents. Try to free your hands and feet and use them along with any movable parts of your body to remove objects pressing on you. Use bricks or wood to prop up potentially collapsing heavy objects, expanding your "safe space" to ensure sufficient air supply. If conditions and physical strength allow, attempt to escape danger. If you notice a larger passage around you, try crawling underneath or sliding backward. Remember to remove your jacket and unbuckle belts to avoid getting caught on obstacles while moving toward areas with light and air. If several people are trapped together and the surrounding area is unstable, one person should escape first, reach a safe area, and then help others escape one by one. If the surroundings are stable, everyone can exit in an orderly line. Another method involves throwing a knotted rope or rough, easy-to-grab belt to someone waiting to be rescued, tying it around their body, and pulling them to safety.

If unable to self-rescue, conserve energy and wait quietly for rescuers to arrive. Call out or bang on objects only when hearing people outside to draw their attention and prompt timely rescue. The longer you survive, the greater your chances of being saved. After the 1985 Mexico City earthquake, over 40 babies were found alive in the ruins of a hospital a week later. Their survival may be due to various factors, including adapting to the environment, avoiding unnecessary fear and panic, and minimizing energy consumption, all crucial for survival.

During the time of being trapped, look for water or food substitutes, including urine, as the saying goes, "hunger makes one eat anything." Survival is paramount. For instance, during the Tangshan earthquake, a child trapped under rubble ate puffed millet from a pillow until rescue. Another resident survived by drinking undisturbed foot-washing water under his bed. A middle-aged woman drank her own urine and survived for over ten days before being rescued. Generally, people trapped in ruins hear sounds from outside clearly, but sounds from inside are hard to detect outside. Therefore, lie still to conserve energy, calling for help only when hearing people outside, or using methods like tapping on pipes or walls that can be heard externally, achieving better results.

6. Three Strategies for Earthquake Protection in High-Rise Buildings

Experts suggest that in metropolitan areas dominated by high-rise buildings, residents should consciously master some scientific and practical earthquake protection strategies.

Strategy One: Stay calm during the quake, then go outside afterward.

This is the international guideline for earthquake protection. Numerous earthquake cases worldwide show that the probability of being crushed is highest when entering or leaving buildings during an earthquake. Thus, experts advise choosing indoor protection if indoor conditions are good. If the building has poor seismic resistance, try to get outside as soon as possible.

Don't panic during an earthquake; keep your vision clear and mobile to act appropriately. Remember these key points: don't linger in bed; don't go to balconies; don't crowd into corridors; don't jump from buildings; don't use elevators. If in an elevator during a quake, leave as soon as possible. If the doors won't open, crouch down and protect your head. Immediately extinguish fires and cut off electricity to prevent burns, electric shocks, and fires.

Strategy Two: Position is critical.

For high-rise residents, based on the building layout and indoor conditions, assess the situation and seek safe spaces to take cover. Preferably, find a triangular space. Crouching near radiators is safer because radiators have a large load-bearing capacity, metal pipelines have a network-like structure and elasticity that resist tearing, even during severe shaking. Radiator pipelines provide good ventilation, reducing the risk of suffocation. Water stored in pipelines can extend survival time. More importantly, trapped individuals can tap radiator pipelines to send signals to the outside world. Radiators near exterior walls facilitate quicker rescue.

Special attention should be given when hiding in small spaces like kitchens or bathrooms. Stay away from stoves, gas pipelines, and fragile dishes. Avoid selecting kitchens or bathrooms located in awkward corners with thin partition walls as ideal earthquake shelters. Don't crawl into cabinets or boxes, as this restricts mobility, obstructs vision, ties up limbs, misses escape opportunities, and makes rescue harder. Lying down increases the likelihood of being hit fivefold compared to standing and makes it difficult to change positions.

Strategy Three: Near water, away from fire; closer to the exterior, farther from the interior.

This principle ensures timely assistance in urban earthquake disasters. Avoid proximity to gas stoves, gas pipelines, and household appliances; don't choose interior positions of buildings, stay near exterior walls but not under windows; stay close to water sources. Once trapped, establish contact with the outside world through cell phones, tapping on pipes or radiators, or turning on flashlights.
