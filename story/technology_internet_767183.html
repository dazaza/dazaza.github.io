Scientists plan to send robot snakes to Mars: to assist rovers

by anonymous on 2013-10-10 18:29:22

A research team from the Norwegian Institute of Technology and Industry has developed an experimental robot snake called "Wheeko." Currently, they are studying how to use this robot snake to collect samples on the harsh surface of Mars.

Scientists have proposed a concept that involves using traditional rovers, such as "Curiosity," to carry the robot snake and release it when it reaches areas difficult for the rover to access.

The research team envisions a cable that connects the Mars rover with the robot snake, providing power to the snake while also transmitting communication signals. Additionally, the cable could serve as a rescuer for the rover.

On October 8th, according to foreign media reports, snakes are highly intelligent animals that inspire fear. From the cold Arctic to arid deserts, snakes can be found in almost every corner of the Earth. Now, European space scientists plan to utilize the high intelligence of snakes to explore an even more severe environment — the surface of Mars. However, instead of real snakes, they will deploy robot snakes developed by Norwegian researchers.

The robot snake was developed by a research team from the Norwegian Institute of Technology and Industry in Trondheim. They are currently studying how to use the robot snake to collect samples on the harsh surface of Mars. Their research is part of a feasibility study conducted by the European Space Agency aimed at giving robots greater mobility than traditional rovers. Norwegian scientists have proposed the idea of using traditional rovers, like "Curiosity," to carry robot snakes and deploy them when reaching areas that are hard for the rover to enter.

To date, NASA has sent four rovers to Mars. These rovers are six-wheeled solar-powered robots equipped with mechanical arms capable of collecting soil samples and operating cameras. In 1997, the "Sojourner" rover landed on Mars. In 2003, the "Opportunity" and "Spirit" rovers landed on Mars. In the summer of 2012, the more advanced "Curiosity" rover landed on Mars. Pal Liljeback and Aksel Transeth from the Institute of Technology and Industry stated: "Improving the mobility and flexibility of robots is quite a challenge. The 'Spirit' rover ended its mission after getting stuck in Martian sand. Due to its limitations, the rover cannot enter many areas to collect samples."

Rovers can analyze collected soil samples using onboard equipment and then send the results back to Earth. This process takes some time. Even if data is transmitted at the speed of light, it still takes about 20 minutes to travel from Mars to Earth. The European Space Agency hopes to bring samples back to Earth for more detailed analysis. Robot snakes could help them achieve this goal. Currently, the Norwegian research team has developed an experimental robot snake called "Wheeko," studying how snake-like robots move on flat surfaces.

"Wheeko" consists of ten identical modules connected together through "joints." Each module has two degrees of freedom. These modules are covered with passive wheels, providing ground friction to allow the robot snake to move on flat surfaces. Transeth said: "We are researching various methods for the rover and robot snake to work together. The rover has a more powerful energy source and can provide power to the snake via a cable connecting the two. If the robot snake had to rely on its own battery, it would quickly run out of power, and we would lose it. One idea is to deploy the robot snake on the rover's mechanical arm. It can be connected or disconnected from the rover. After being lowered to the ground, the robot snake can act independently."

The research team envisions a cable that connects the rover with the robot snake, providing power while transmitting communication signals. Additionally, the cable could serve as a savior for the rover. Liljeback said: "The connection between the robot snake and the rover means that if the rover gets stuck, the robot snake can help it escape. In the event of the rover getting trapped, the robot snake will detach itself from the rover, wrap around a rock, and use a winch to pull the rover out." Another concept is to deploy the robot snake under or on top of the rover. This concept requires a lifting device to raise and lower the robot snake. The Norwegian research team, in collaboration with the Center for Interdisciplinary Space Research and the Norwegian Space Center, plans to publish a detailed research report in December, explaining the robot snake.
