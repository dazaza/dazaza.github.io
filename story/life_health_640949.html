The Potential "Poisoning" Risk of Apples: Coloring with Ripening Hormones

The danger of eating apples: Enlarging hormones make them bigger, ripening hormones enhance their color, and preservatives keep them fresh. Overuse of enlarging hormones, ripening hormones, and preservatives can harm the liver. Retail fruit vendors may also apply industrial wax to apples in order to retain moisture and give them a shiny appearance that is more appealing to customers.

What should be done about the wax on apple skins?

From a nutritional perspective, it's best to eat apples with the skin on because compared to the flesh, the skin contains more antioxidants. In fact, the wax on apple skins can be divided into three main types:

The first type is the natural wax that naturally occurs on the surface of apples. This is a lipid component that forms a protective layer for the plant. It effectively prevents external microorganisms and pesticides from invading the flesh and serves a protective function. This type of wax does not need to be removed.

The second type is the edible wax artificially applied to some imported apples. This "artificial fruit wax" is actually a chitosan substance, which is often extracted from crustaceans like crabs and shells. This substance is harmless to the body and is mainly used for preservation, preventing apples from rotting during long-distance transportation and long-term storage. Removing this layer of wax is simple; just rinse it with hot water.

The third type is industrial wax, which may contain mercury and lead that could seep into the flesh through the skin, posing a health risk to humans. The way to identify it is: if you can wipe off a faint red substance from the skin using your hand or a napkin, it might be industrial wax.

Apples Come in Different Colors with Varied Health Benefits

The color of an apple can predict its health benefits. For example:

Red apples: better for heart health, improving memory, and maintaining urinary system health;

Green apples: better for strong teeth and bones;

Yellow apples: can strengthen the immune system and help prevent certain cancers.

Traditional Chinese medicine believes that different colored apples have different health benefits.

Red apples benefit the heart: they have stronger effects in lowering blood lipids and softening blood vessels, protecting cardiovascular health, and are suitable for older people to consume more.

Green apples: have liver-nourishing and detoxifying effects and can combat depression, making them more suitable for younger people to consume. Additionally, green apples promote the growth of teeth and bones and prevent gum bleeding.

Yellow apples: are good for protecting vision, and those who frequently use computers can appropriately consume them.

The Adorable Apple - "General Practitioner"

As early as ancient Egypt, people did not only see apples as food but also as medicine. Canadian research shows that apple juice has a powerful effect in killing infectious viruses in test tubes, and people who eat more apples are much less likely to catch colds than those who do not eat or eat fewer apples.

Therefore, some scientists and doctors refer to apples as "all-round healthy fruits" or "general practitioners."

It's Best to Chew Apples Slowly for Fifteen Minutes

Apples are high in nutritional value and rich in various vitamins and acidic substances.

According to experts, eating apples should not be done too quickly. If chewed for a longer time in the mouth, more saliva and gastric juice can be secreted, which helps promote the absorption and digestion of apple nutrients by the human body.

From a nutritional standpoint, we do not advocate the "wolfing down" method of eating. People with poor gastrointestinal health especially need to pay attention to the speed at which they eat apples. Eating too quickly can easily lead to indigestion, causing discomfort.

Many people may have experienced this: when eating an apple over a long period, the bitten surface turns yellow, and the water content in that part of the flesh decreases, making it softer and less crisp. Fu Jinru explained that this is actually the result of oxidation in apples. Although it may not look as appealing and the taste may not be as good, its nutritional elements have not been lost, and generally speaking, consuming it will not cause harm.
