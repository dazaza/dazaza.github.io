How are Gold and Silver T+D transactions carried out?

Historically, transactions took place in trading halls through verbal bids by traders. Currently, most T+D transactions are completed electronically. During silver T+D trades, investors input buy/sell orders into the T+D computer system, which are matched by the exchange's matching system to complete the transaction. When buying or selling T+D contracts, both parties must pay a small amount of money to the settlement house as a performance guarantee, known as a margin. The first purchase of a contract is called establishing a long position, while the first sale of a contract is called establishing a short position. Afterward, the contracts held must be settled daily.

After establishing buy/sell positions (known as opening a position) in silver T+D, one does not need to hold them until expiration. At any time before the T+D contract expires, a reverse transaction can be made to offset the original position. This transaction is called closing a position. For example, if on the first day 10 lots of T+D contracts are sold, and on the second day, 10 lots are bought back, then the first transaction is opening a short position of 10 lots of T+D, and the second transaction is closing a short position of 10 lots of T+D. On the second day, if another 20 lots of T+D contracts are bought, this becomes opening a long position of 20 lots of T+D. If then 10 lots are sold, it is called closing a long position of 10 lots of T+D, leaving 10 lots of T+D long position. At the end of a trading day, the contracts that have not been closed are called open positions. In this example, after the first day's trading, the open position is a short position of 10 lots of T+D, and after the second day's trading, the open position is a long position of 10 lots of T+D.

Compared with stocks, silver T+D has several very distinct features, which are particularly important for stock investors:

(1) Time-wise, T+D has 10 hours of trading time per day, while stocks have 4 hours per day.

(2) T+D contracts are margin trading and must be settled daily. Silver T+D uses margin trading, usually requiring only about 10% of the contract value to buy or sell a contract. This increases profit potential but also brings risks, so daily settlement of gains and losses is necessary. Stock account profits and losses are not settled until sold. However, T+D is different; after trading, daily settlement according to the settlement price is required for the contracts held. Account profits can be withdrawn, but account losses must be replenished before the next day's opening (i.e., adding margin). Moreover, since it is margin trading, losses may even exceed your investment principal, which is different from stock trading.

(3) T+D can go short. T+D can easily go short, waiting for the price to fall and then buy back. Stock short-selling transactions can also go short, but it is relatively more difficult. Of course, once shorted and the price rises instead of falling, investors face losses.

(4) Higher market liquidity. Studies have shown that the liquidity of the T+D market is significantly higher than the stock spot market.

(5) T+D can be applied for withdrawal within any trading period.
