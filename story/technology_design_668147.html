In the past two years, a unprecedented collecting craze has arisen in China.

by fbfshtwk0423 on 2012-02-17 14:42:37

"The business in the antiques market was hard to do throughout 2012, and the industry should prepare for tough times," said a female boss at Guangzhou Lingnan Antiques City. Vice Chairman of the Guangdong Collectors Association Huang Mingwei, on the other hand, stated that 2012 would be a good opportunity for collectors to enter the market, especially in antique auctions, where "ancient porcelain has the potential to become very popular." This complex sentiment actually reflects a subtle change: compared to the mindset of "how much have my old items appreciated" at the beginning of 2011, the key word for 2012 should be: buy low.

Reviewing 2011: Ancient porcelain and huanghuali were the hottest in the first half of the year, with merchants regretting not having enough inventory. In 2011, the Guangzhou antiques market experienced "an exceptionally hot first half and an extremely slow second half," concluded the majority of merchants. A ceramic shop owner at Wenzhou Antiques City informed that "whether it was imperial or civilian kiln, ancient porcelain was particularly popular in the first half of last year, with a conservative estimate of a 50% increase, some even doubling or quadrupling in price." Mr. Li, who sold old redwood furniture, also agreed: "Redwood furniture prices surged two to three times in the first half of last year, with both new and old Hainan huanghuali furniture rising significantly, while old zitan furniture saw substantial increases as well." Not only did prices rise, but transaction volumes skyrocketed, leading many merchants to lament not having enough inventory.

An experienced businessman, Mr. Liu, who has been doing business at Wenzhou Antiques City for many years, explained the reasons behind the antiques craze: "Firstly, high-end ancient porcelain and Hainan huanghuali antiques have a certain level of scarcity; secondly, there are more wealthy people in today's society, some of whom want to own a few heirloom-worthy pieces for future generations; thirdly, the past few years have seen an unprecedented wave of collecting fever domestically, with many investments being made blindly; fourthly, it’s not that antiques prices rose quickly, but rather they were previously undervalued."

A major reversal in the second half turned into a situation of high prices but no buyers, which the industry saw as a sign of "rationalized investment." However, the noise of the first half seemed like false fire, just months later the antiques market suddenly reversed: despite still high prices, transaction volumes shrank dramatically. Vice President of the Guangdong Cultural Relics and Art Association and Expert Committee Member Luo Xiong had a deep understanding of this: "In the second half of 2011, domestic high-end cultural relics and art transactions fell by 20% to 30%, and in some cases, there were instances where confirmed auction sales remained unpaid and uncollected. Although some companies tried to create fake transactions to maintain market atmosphere, it was ultimately futile." He believed that the reason for the cooling of the market in the second half of 2011 was related to scandals such as the 'Forbidden City Gate,' fake appraisals, and fake auctions within the collecting community, combined with an oversupply of collectibles forcing the market to lower its head.

However, the antique dealers in Guangzhou did not see this as a reversal but positioned it as an adjustment: "Over the past 20 years, the price of ancient ceramics has risen 100 times; and in the past year alone, the increase reached 50%, too fast. A correction should naturally follow." Mr. Chen, owner of Lingnan Antiques City, talked about another side of the issue: "In recent years, many people have entered the field, spending lavishly, but soon discovered two things: one, they bought many fakes, severely damaging their confidence; two, they ran out of money; and three, it is easy to buy but hard to sell. Collectors became more rational, temporarily freezing their enthusiasm."

Looking ahead to 2012: The antiques market will continue to be deadlocked, focusing on buying low and eliminating fakes. Most interviewed merchants were prepared for 'winter', and expert Luo Xiong also believed that the overall market trend in 2012 would further decline, entering a consolidation period. That is, reducing the number of auction items and lowering prices slightly to achieve successful transactions. "With the decline in auction trends, the mentality of antique dealers has also stabilized, most will no longer demand exorbitant prices, and some may even sell at rock-bottom prices to liquidate assets. This is a good opportunity for rational investors to carefully choose and purchase goods."

Mr. Liu, owner of Wenzhou Antiques City, predicted: "Many people will remain in a state of observation in the first half of 2012, making the market slower, but it will improve in the second half." He gave his reasons: "High-quality, high-end items, their prices will only rise and never fall, and the market always buys when prices rise rather than fall. That is to say, official kiln products in ancient porcelain, and huanghuali in furniture, will continue to attract investor interest. Meanwhile, coarse items, like junk stocks, will gradually be abandoned by people. Discarding the coarse and keeping the fine, eliminating fakes and preserving the genuine is the inevitable trend."

He believes that even if market transaction volumes shrink further, antique prices will not drop, "Labor costs, rent, etc., are increasing, and those involved in this trade are all super-affluent individuals who do not lack money. It's okay for them to make a little less profit, but it is impossible for them to sell antiques purchased for 10,000 yuan last year for 9,000 yuan this year—they absolutely won't!"

Expert Predictions: Vice Chairman of the Guangdong Collectors Association Huang Mingwei: Ming and Qing porcelains have been over-hyped, now is the time to invest in high-antiquity porcelains. Vice Chairman of the Guangdong Collectors Association and Deputy Director of the Ceramics Professional Committee Huang Mingwei, when discussing the general trend of the 2012 collection market, expressed optimism about the "high-antiquity porcelain" market. High-antiquity porcelain refers to ceramics from before the Song and Yuan dynasties. Due to issues such as insufficient hardness, high water absorption, and ease of damage, this category has not been given much attention by collectors in recent years, and prices have remained relatively low. However, the auction prices of Chinese high-antiquity porcelains in Hong Kong and overseas are astonishingly high. In the eyes of Western collectors, Chinese official kiln porcelains from the Ming and Qing periods are considered high-grade crafts, while porcelains from the Yuan dynasty and earlier contain rich cultural and historical value, so they are willing to pay high prices to acquire them.

According to Huang Mingwei, professionals have already started collecting high-antiquity porcelains in the past two years, and prices began to rise last year. However, the current continuously rising private prices are still far from the intrinsic value of high-antiquity porcelains and international circulation prices.

Huang Mingwei analyzed: "Ming and Qing porcelains have been hyped for so long, prices have gone crazy, putting down 1 million yuan can hardly buy anything now, the investment cost is too high. Moreover, Ming and Qing porcelains have no room for price reduction because the collector base is expanding, but the quantity of fine ceramics is limited, and repatriation is decreasing. But if you use 1 million yuan to buy high-antiquity porcelains, you can get one or even several fine pieces. As a porcelain investor, one should closely monitor high-antiquity porcelains, which are performing stocks."

However, he also warned that the biggest risk in investing in ancient porcelain is "wrong buying," mistaking fakes for genuine items. He suggested that investors buy from reputable collectors or those with authenticity guarantees. If participating in auctions with large funds, one must be vigilant.

Investment Reminder: When buying antiques, focus on the item, not the story. Do not easily trust appraisals or certificates. There is a saying in the antiques market: true and false, selling false as true, is common. "Antique shops often sell fakes. Even collectors with a certain level and taste initially bought fake items in the antiques market." Industry insiders call this "taking medicine." It is said that a highly-valued entrepreneur spent over 6 million yuan on "tuition fees" before discovering that the treasures he brought home were all fakes. A female businesswoman from Fujian once spent over 10 million yuan on a batch of "series collections" as "tuition fees."

Expert Huang Mingwei frankly stated that one should not easily enter the market without understanding the interested collectibles or the antiques market. "Absolutely do not have a gambling mentality. If you are interested, find one or two trustworthy experts to help evaluate. At the same time, focus on the item, not the story. If the shop owner says it was passed down from ancestors or obtained through some special encounter, do not easily believe it. Also, do not trust so-called appraisals or collection certificates, as everything can be faked under market behavior." He directly stated that the chance of finding a bargain now is almost nonexistent, "There are no fools in the business of opening stores, ignorance leads to failure."
