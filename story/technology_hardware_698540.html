Exhibition Trip to the 2012 European Tombstone Supplies Show

by anonymous on 2012-03-09 10:02:26

The Journey of Participating in the European Cemetery Supplies Exhibition

Traveling to Europe to participate in the European Cemetery Supplies Exhibition was naturally an exhilarating event for us who have always stayed within our domestic borders. Moreover, this time we were going to France, a country renowned for its romance. Would we encounter a romantic journey while completing our exhibition work? Could we find our ideal partner? Would we have the chance to witness the breathtaking Parisian night views depicted in our impressions? What would the actual Gothic architecture look like? With these excited yet nervous thoughts, we began our preparatory work before the exhibition.

After the exhibition work commenced, I realized that everything was far from the beautiful image I had imagined. From selecting the exhibits, purchasing booths, to finding participating companies for visa processing, hotel accommodations, and exhibit shipping, a plethora of tasks overwhelmed everyone. Especially, the selection of intermediary exhibitors caused us significant headaches.

For our small stone-processing enterprise still in its developmental phase, the costs associated with traveling to Europe for the exhibition were squeezed out from tight budgets. The exhibition service providers were varied and numerous, with more than half being scammers. Fortunately, due to our rigorous screening process, we avoided being deceived. Here, I can share some methods to distinguish genuine from fake exhibition service providers.

Firstly, be cautious of those who proactively call offering services. Generally, popular European exhibitions are difficult to secure spots for; many exhibitions are booked a year or half a year in advance. Large-scale exhibition service companies already struggle to accommodate their fixed long-term customers, making it unnecessary for them to solicit business elsewhere.

Secondly, the internet can be a great help. First, we can check through online industrial and commercial systems whether the company exists, what its registered capital is, if it has its own website, and how professional the website appears. This is crucial. Imagine, a company without industrial and commercial registration or a professional website—how financially capable could it be, and how could it claim professionalism?

Of course, relying solely on the internet is insufficient. There's much homework to do offline as well. For example, verifying the phone numbers and faxes of the exhibition organizing companies to ensure they match, repeatedly confirming booth and itinerary reservations, and even directly communicating with their executives if necessary.

With all preparations completed, we were ready to set off. The host city for this exhibition was Lyon, France. Our flight required a layover at the Frankfurt airport. We traveled alongside two other exhibitors, making a group of five in total. Among them were a Polish couple, very friendly, who introduced us to various European customs and traditions along the way. In return, we proudly shared with them the brilliance of our five-thousand-year-old culture.

When we arrived in Lyon, it was already midnight. We went straight to the hotel to rest but slept poorly due to the time difference, waking up at around seven in the morning. Only then did we notice the details of the hotel. It had six floors, with not overly large rooms but clean and tidy interiors. The rooms were equipped with kitchens where we could prepare our own meals.

After freshening up, we headed downstairs for breakfast. Unlike most hotels in China where breakfast requires additional payment, European hotels generally include breakfast. According to the tour guide, three-star hotels in France mainly offer cold dishes for breakfast, four-star hotels provide hot food, and higher-end hotels serve gourmet breakfasts. We stayed in a three-star hotel, and although it only offered cold dishes, the variety was quite abundant: croissants, cakes, fruits, raw meat, smoked meat, milk, jams, etc. The croissants, freshly baked, were crispy on the outside and soft on the inside, with a unique texture. Even someone like me, who isn't particularly fond of bread, couldn’t resist eating several.

After satisfying our hunger, it was time to focus on the main task - heading to the exhibition area to set up our booth. Our booth wasn't in a prime location, on the right side of the entrance. This was unavoidable as good booths had been reserved long ago. Therefore, based on our experience, we advise others to book early when participating in European exhibitions. Our booth was a standard stall, equipped with one table, two chairs, three projector lights, and a header board (which only displayed the English version of our company introduction, no Chinese).

Setting up the booth took much longer than expected because our exhibits had to be shipped through customs. As they were heavy stone materials, we spent 20 euros in tips to hire a staff member from the exhibition center to help us arrange the display. Around noon, our local receptionist kindly delivered lunch to us, which turned out to be chicken fried rice from a Chinese restaurant. Being away from home made us truly appreciate the depth and breadth of Chinese culinary artistry; our cuisine indeed tasted better.

By around six in the evening, after a day’s hard work, the booth was finally set up. We finally had time to stroll through the streets of France. After dinner at a restaurant, we visited a supermarket to buy daily necessities, perfumes, small trinkets, and other souvenirs to bring back for our friends. By the time we returned to the hotel, we were exhausted and fell asleep immediately.

On the third day in France, the exhibition officially commenced. Since it was Friday, there weren't many visitors, but each booth still had some European guests asking about products. Nowadays, more and more domestic companies are involved in foreign trade, and foreigners also compare prices among different vendors. They usually ask questions of interest, leave their business cards or contact information, and then move on to the next booth.

There were quite a few Chinese companies participating in this exhibition, and some domestic exhibitors who didn’t speak French hired local Chinese as interpreters. After a day of communication with these burly foreigners, we collected contact information from over twenty companies. Some clients showed strong intentions to place orders, making us feel that all our efforts were worthwhile, and we could confidently report back to our boss.

With the first day’s experience, the subsequent two days of the exhibition went smoothly. As Saturday and Sunday approached, the number of visitors increased significantly. We skillfully navigated among these potential customers, vividly introducing our exhibits. Our efforts were recognized, and just on Saturday alone, we obtained contact information from over fifty European enterprises related to the stone industry. From our conversations with them, at least two to three expressed willingness to cooperate with us.

It wasn't until late afternoon on Sunday that the peak viewing period gradually subsided. At this point, most visitors were companies coming to reclaim exhibits. Given that transporting stone exhibits back to China was both inconvenient and expensive, we sold them at low prices.

By around six in the evening on Sunday, all exhibition-related work was basically completed. Taking advantage of the remaining time, despite our fatigue, we insisted on joining the local guide for a nighttime tour of Lyon, France. Without the pressure of work, we felt completely relaxed, indulging in the dazzling neon lights and enjoying the occasional witty remarks from our guide. Finally, we could laugh heartily and enjoy the moment freely.
