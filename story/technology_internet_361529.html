Dongguan toxic gas was poured into the sewer, questioning the conscience of the enterprise

by ditan360 on 2010-08-03 20:22:11

Special report by Yitan.net: On the morning of the 29th, within Dongguan City in Guangdong Province, an unknown chemical substance was poured into a sewer, producing a pungent gas with moderate toxicity that spread through the nearby air. Upon investigation, it was confirmed that the gas contained substances such as trichloroethylene and dichloroethane, classified as gases with moderate toxicity. The police have since launched an investigation, but it is still unclear who dumped the chemicals. The Dongguan Environmental Protection Bureau stated that the residents' domestic water supply has not been affected.

After rapid monitoring by the Dongguan Environmental Monitoring Station on the remaining liquid and contaminated soil around the source of the odor, it was preliminarily determined to be a chlorine-containing organic gas, including toxic and harmful gases such as trichloroethylene and dichloroethane. Detailed conditions are still undergoing further testing. The Environmental Protection Bureau stated that chlorine-containing organic gases belong to substances with moderate toxicity. Due to the low concentration of this sudden appearance of gas, it will temporarily not have a significant impact on the health of surrounding personnel.

According to available data, trichloroethylene was once used as an analgesic and metal degreaser, can be used as an extractant, bactericide, refrigerant, and dry-cleaning agent for clothes. Long-term exposure can cause symptoms such as trigeminal nerve paralysis. Dichloroethane is a colorless liquid at room temperature with a smell similar to chloroform, highly toxic, and potentially carcinogenic.

It is quite evident that the company or individual responsible for the illegal discharge could not have been unaware of the hazards of these toxic gases, yet they irresponsibly dumped them into the city's sewers, affecting more than 100,000 people. Although there were no casualties at the time, it caused great panic and unease among the residents.

This incident undoubtedly serves as another warning bell for local environmental protection work. Due to a lack of effective supervision, enterprises or individuals illegally discharging pollutants, even toxic and harmful substances, is not uncommon in the Pearl River Delta region. Guangdong is an economic powerhouse, but it is also a province with severe pollution. Since the reform and opening-up, the high-speed development of the economy in the Pearl River Delta is undeniable, but almost simultaneously came the issue of environmental pollution. As a result, the Pearl River Delta area has long been criticized for "prioritizing GDP over environmental protection."

Although the government is currently fully engaged in environmental governance, incidents where factories in the Pearl River Delta irresponsibly dump sewage or chemicals into sewers occur frequently. Not only groundwater, but the pollution situation of Guangdong's nearshore sea areas is also quite serious. The Zhujiang Estuary carries millions of tons of pollutants into the sea every year, causing severe pollution in the nearshore sea areas of the estuary. In May this year, due to marine pollution, there were multiple cases of scallop poisoning in Guangzhou, Hong Kong, Macao, and other places.

In fact, it's not just Guangdong. Over the past twenty years, while China has achieved rapid economic growth, it has also paid a heavy price, with a sharp increase in the number of environmental accidents. According to statistics from the Ministry of Environmental Protection, approximately 102 environmental accidents occurred in the first half of this year, compared to 171 last year. Especially in the past half month, we've seen the Zijin Mining pollution, followed by the Dalian crude oil spill, then the explosion at Nanjing's waste plastic factory, raw material drums from Jilin's chemical plant being washed into the Songhua River, and toxic gases being dumped into Dongguan's sewers... These consecutive environmental incidents not only warn us about the current environmental crisis but also reveal the serious lack of corporate social responsibility.

Under a market economy environment, a company's social responsibility and integrity are not automatically established; they must be constrained by strict legal frameworks. Both domestically and internationally, companies may hide unfavorable information or even collude with officials and manipulate the media to evade responsibility and punishment. In response to such situations, strong external constraints are necessary.

Following the Gulf of Mexico oil spill incident in the United States, pressure from the U.S. government and the public's "public opinion storm" forced BP to auction off assets to cover various aftermath and compensation costs resulting from the oil spill. The reason for this lies in the United States having a comprehensive set of "severe laws" for companies responsible for environmental pollution incidents. Comparing this to some disasters that have occurred domestically, although the involved companies are punished, we also see some disturbing phenomena: superficial accountability and trivial corporate compensation.

For a long time, China's economic development has followed a model of "getting rich first, cleaning up pollution later." Local governments prioritize GDP over environmental protection, often reducing regulatory efforts on companies due to fiscal revenue and GDP data considerations, or even allowing pollution. Unnecessary powers lacking proper constraints inevitably lead to misconduct, and the same applies to companies. With frequent major environmental pollution incidents, establishing a stringent and deterrent constraint mechanism is urgent.
