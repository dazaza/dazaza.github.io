Several key scientific issues in wind energy development

by lklkkgfeg5 on 2012-02-06 08:36:29

Several Key Scientific Issues in Wind Energy Development - Building Materials Knowledge - Industry News - Building Materials Network. According to a report on ScienceNet on May 14, 2008, the importance of wind energy development is indisputable, and large-scale wind energy development must first solve a series of key scientific issues. Luo Yong, deputy director of the National Climate Center and director of the Wind Energy Solar Energy Resource Assessment Center of the China Meteorological Administration, and Hu Fei, a researcher at the Institute of Atmospheric Physics of the Chinese Academy of Sciences, both emphasized this point in interviews with the *Science Times*.

So, what are the key scientific issues related to atmospheric science that wind energy resource development faces? Establishing a refined assessment model for wind energy resources: "Just as mineral resource development requires finding minerals first, developing wind energy resources requires finding wind first. However, wind energy differs from mineral resources; having wind does not necessarily mean that wind energy can be developed," said Hu Fei. A set of effective scientific methods must be established for detailed surveys of wind energy resources. These scientific methods include improving and developing existing international theories and methods for wind energy resource assessment, establishing a refined assessment model suitable for China's climate and topographical characteristics.

From the perspective of atmospheric science, wind power generation primarily utilizes the kinetic energy resources of wind in the near-surface layer (wind turbine hub height generally does not exceed 120 meters). Therefore, it is necessary to have a detailed understanding of the total reserves of wind energy resources within the range of turbine heights in China (below 120 meters) and where the macro-rich areas of China's wind energy resources are located.

Luo Yong introduced to the reporter that previous assessments of China's wind energy resources mainly relied on conventional wind data at 10-meter heights from meteorological stations and limited wind tower data. Due to factors such as the spatial distribution density of meteorological stations, short sequences of wind tower data, and the lack of refined assessment theories and methods adapted to China's monsoon climate characteristics and topography, it has not been possible to accurately provide the total reserves of wind energy resources at turbine heights and their refined regional distribution features (with horizontal resolution reaching 1 kilometer and vertical resolution reaching 10 meters).

To achieve large-scale sustainable development and utilization of China's wind energy resources, it is necessary to first understand the specific distribution areas of wind energy resources at turbine heights in China. Luo Yong further emphasized that to scientifically guide the micro-siting of wind farms within regions rich in wind energy resources, it is necessary to establish a micro-siting model suitable for China's monsoon climate characteristics and topographical features to achieve refined assessment of wind energy resources. The results can then be better applied to the design and construction of wind farms.

In order to conduct refined assessments of wind energy resources and also provide technical support for the dynamic design of wind farms and wind power forecasting, there is also a need for microscale numerical models at the scale of wind farms and turbines.

"In summary, our goal is to truly realize multi-scale wind field simulation and forecasting. This is also one of the key technical challenges we need to overcome," said Hu Fei.

Impact of Extreme Weather on Wind Energy Development

"Typhoon Dujuan in 2003, Typhoon Zhenzhu in 2006, and Typhoon Saomai caused severe damage to wind turbines at the Honghai Bay Wind Farm in Shanwei, Guangdong, Nan'ao Wind Farm, and He Ding Mountain Wind Farm in Cangnan, Zhejiang," said Luo Yong when discussing the impact of extreme weather on wind energy resource development. Previously, during the design of wind farms, discussions on the impact of extreme weather were limited. For example, how strong are typhoons along the southeastern coast, and what impact would they have on wind farms? Now, these questions have become key issues that must be addressed in wind energy resource development, especially considering the impact of extreme weather or climate... hazards such as severe sandstorms, strong winds, strong typhoons, icing, and lightning strikes.

Hu Fei also pointed out that guiding the macro layout and key regions of wind farm construction based on extreme climatic environmental conditions and scientifically assessing the climatic risks of wind farm construction in different regions (mainly the probability of destruction caused by extreme climatic environmental conditions) are currently important scientific issues in wind energy development. For example, tropical cyclones, tornadoes, explosive temperate cyclones, low temperatures, storm surges, and sea ice can seriously affect the safe operation of wind farms, among which tropical cyclones have the most significant impact.

Hu Fei gave an example: Currently, there is still insufficient understanding of the wind characteristics of tropical cyclones within the height range of wind turbines in the atmospheric boundary layer, making it impossible to provide reliable aerodynamic parameters for the design of coastal and offshore wind farms. The planning and construction of wind farms lack a basis for climate risk analysis.

"In the context of global climate change, only by scientifically recognizing the historical changes and causes of China's wind energy resources and making relatively accurate predictions about possible future trends can large-scale sustainable development and utilization of China's wind energy resources be achieved, providing a scientific basis for the macro layout and micro-siting of wind farms. For instance, how did China's wind energy resources change in the past? What will be the trend of future evolution? Can they be sustainably utilized?" asked Hu Fei.

Wind Energy Resource Monitoring and Forecasting System

With the widespread use of wind energy, more and more wind power enterprises require accurate forecasting services. The demand of wind power enterprises for short-term wind energy forecasts is: predicting power generation rather than wind speed; issuing hourly forecasts for 36-48 hours ahead one day in advance; providing forecast reliability; medium-term forecasts several days in advance are very important for planning and maintenance management.

"The current weather and climate models basically cannot make refined forecasts of wind speed distributions at the scale of wind farms," said Hu Fei. To build large-scale wind farms, it is necessary to improve the level of short-term wind power forecasting for already built wind farms.

Luo Yong also pointed out that due to the needs of grid electricity dispatching, wind power needs to estimate its potential power generation. In this aspect, China's existing wind farms mainly carry out some wind power forecasting work based on weather forecasts combined with local climate conditions. In June 2006, the China Meteorological Administration established the Wind Energy Solar Energy Resource Assessment Center. The center has started some work on wind power forecasting. "We are cooperating with two wind farms to construct and develop some wind power forecasting systems."

"However, this is far from enough. There is an urgent need to establish a specialized wind energy resource monitoring and forecasting system dedicated to the allocation of wind power online for wind farms," pointed out Luo Yong.
