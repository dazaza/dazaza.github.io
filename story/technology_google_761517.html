Is it really feasible to use Google for stock investment?

by geekzhang on 2013-05-03 17:00:50

Every day, countless keywords are entered into Google's search box by people. Although the purposes of these search behaviors vary greatly, to a large extent, they can help us determine the level of attention an event/item/concept receives worldwide. Can these keywords be used for investment prediction? Researchers in the UK conducted a seven-year study and have just released their report. The final result is that using keywords from Google Trends to predict market changes and conduct short-term investments helped them achieve a return of 326% during this period, whereas if you had simply bought all these stocks, you would have only achieved a return of 16% over the seven years.

Their method involved continuously collecting data on search volumes for keywords directly related to investment behavior such as debt, stocks, portfolios, unemployment, markets, and some less related keywords (art, happiness, war, conflict, politics) through Google Trends. They designed a multi-dimensional evaluation system which would help them predict what kind of investments should be made based on these keywords.

Among these keywords, debt carries a significant weight. Typically, when the search volume for debt increases, it indicates that stock prices are about to fall, while a decrease in search volume suggests that stock prices will rise.

So, does this mean we can use Google Trends for stock prediction now? I think this behavior is still difficult to measure.

Firstly, there’s the issue with the model system. This research only modeled 98 keywords, and the 326% return rate applies to the entire set of experiments. In other words, this successful investment was built on a small-scale keyword test model. What would the results be if each investment were analyzed individually?

Secondly, it is clear that some of these keywords must be meaningless. This meaninglessness comes in two forms: one where under any circumstances, it has almost no significant impact on the market, and another where it only works under specific scenarios/periods. For example, the word "conflict" broken down by different regions might have completely different positive or negative impacts on the market.

Finally, this experiment was based on the premise that people did not have the concept of judging the stock market through keywords. If this experimental report gains widespread attention, then everyone starts making investment judgments based on keywords, it is very likely that it would completely disrupt the weightings and trends of these keywords.

Therefore, using Google for stock investment is currently just a meaningful experimental result for reference. To apply it in real-world investment may expose you to greater risks.
