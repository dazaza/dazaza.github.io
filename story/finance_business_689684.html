AT&T Asks to Withdraw T-Mobile Bid, Will Sue FCC if Not Approved

by anonymous on 2012-03-04 13:57:34

Sina http://www.jgkaisuo.com/ Technology News, Beijing Time, early morning of November 26th - AT&T stated today that it would sue the Federal Communications Commission (hereafter referred to as "FCC") if the commission does not agree to withdraw its application for the $39 billion acquisition of T-Mobile USA. The company had been seeking U.S. government approval for the acquisition of T-Mobile.

In a statement issued on Friday, AT&T said that due to FCC Chairman Julius Genachowski's suggestion this Tuesday to hold a hearing regarding the acquisition of T-Mobile USA by AT&T, which indicated his intention to block the deal, AT&T and T-Mobile USA's parent company Deutsche Telekom decided on Thursday to submit an application to the FCC to withdraw the approval for the transaction.

The FCC originally claimed on Thursday that AT&T’s move to withdraw the approval request was done with ulterior motives. The U.S. Department of Justice has already filed a lawsuit to stop the acquisition of T-Mobile USA by AT&T, arguing that the merger would be detrimental to market competition. The court will hear the case in February next year. However, if AT&T withdraws the request at this time, it could avoid the trial next year.

AT&T General Counsel Wayne Watts issued a statement today pointing out that, "According to relevant rules and regulations, the FCC is obligated to approve AT&T's application to withdraw the acquisition of T-Mobile USA. We have the right to withdraw our application from the FCC, and the FCC has no authority to prevent us from withdrawing the application. Any attempt to block our withdrawal is an abuse of power, and we will take legal action to protect our rights."

A spokesperson for AT&T declined to comment on the statement, while a female spokesperson for the FCC did not respond.

AT&T's acquisition plan faces many difficulties, including scrutiny by the Department of Justice, lawsuits filed by the attorneys general of seven states, and opposition from competitors Sprint Nextel and C Spire Wireless. Currently, the FCC has approved the recommendation of its chairman, and AT&T must still undergo at least one hearing and one court trial.

AT&T currently needs the approval of both the Department of Justice and the FCC. The former is tasked with determining whether AT&T has monopolistic behavior, while the latter analyzes whether the merger would benefit the public interest.

At the same time, AT&T announced on Thursday that the company would prepare $4 billion in expenses for the last fiscal quarter of this year. This amount will be used to subsidize Deutsche Telekom in the event of a failed acquisition. Of this amount, $3 billion will serve as a breakup fee, and another $1 billion represents the anticipated residual book value, including car decoders, car jammers, and car unlocking tools.
