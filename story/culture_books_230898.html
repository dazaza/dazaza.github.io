Reinterpreting Chinese ancient history through the mouth of Yuan Tengfei, hailed as the "most awesome history teacher in history," transforms those originally dry and boring historical passages into lively and engaging narratives. The already interesting parts of history become absolutely enthralling. Readers can absorb a vast amount of historical knowledge while enjoying it like listening to crosstalk performances. This is a book that thoroughly endorses the saying "learning history enlightens one's mind." It’s an educational yet entertaining historical text filled with wit and wisdom.

Following in the footsteps of authors like Dan Yu from "The Ming Dynasty: Those Years" and speakers like Yu Dan and Wang Lique from "Lectures from the Hundred Schools," Yuan Tengfei emerges as another master storyteller of history, leading China into a new wave of national enthusiasm for studying, reading, and discussing history. His storytelling is logically organized, insightful, well-researched, focused, and packed with information, encompassing all the qualities a good history book should have. His writing style is fresh and unique, his words sharp and pointed, directly hitting the core of issues with humor and wit—qualities often lacking in traditional history books but present here.

In addition to the written content, the book includes numerous colorful images, printed in four colors, combining fun, knowledge, and readability.

Recalling the Prosperous Days of Kaiyuan

It’s estimated that the father of Emperor Jing of Northern Zhou might have been forced to marry Yang Jian's daughter, just as external relatives seizing power often do. Wang Mang, being the grandfather of the emperor, usurped the throne; Cao Cao, the father-in-law of the emperor, didn’t, but his son Cao Pi did, deposing his own brother-in-law to proclaim himself emperor. Similarly, Emperor Xian of Han was also forced to marry Cao Cao's daughter because the original empress had been eliminated. Yang Jian was ennobled as Duke of Sui in Northern Zhou, so after he deposed the emperor, he established the Sui dynasty, with the era name Kaihuang, and made Chang’an his capital. In 589 AD, Yang Jian sent his second son, Prince Jin Wang Yang Guang, with more than 500,000 troops by land and water to destroy Chen, unifying the north and south.

Your 'Bà' (霸) Comes, My Stage Begins

Our nation is relatively complex and diverse, which means there are more opportunities for mutual learning and cultural exchange, making the entire Chinese people smarter and our genetic makeup increasingly superior. Look at the Native Americans, who belong to the same Mongoloid race as us, but why were they still in the prehistoric civilization era when Columbus discovered America? One important reason is that they were too isolated, without communication. Among their over a thousand tribes, there were over two thousand languages, where Zhang Village couldn't understand what Li Village said, and Li Village couldn't understand Wang Village. They didn't try to unify their language, each developing blindly. The lack of communication resulted in situations where Zhang Village knew crabs were edible, but Li Village considered them poisonous; Li Village knew not to provoke crocodiles, but Wang Village kept feeding them.

The Final Han Dynasty

Yingtian is today's Nanjing, and Zhu Yuanzhang is the founding emperor of the Ming Dynasty. Look at how that guy looks, not a bit like someone who has the fortune to be an emperor. That face looks like a shoe horn. Yes, like a shoe horn, or a trowel, covered in pockmarks. This is the true appearance of Emperor Taizu of Ming. If you paint him like this, he'll kill you. If you beautify him, making him look like Qin Shi Huang or Emperor Wu of Han, with a full forehead and square jawline, he'll still kill you. So there's no way around it. The Ming Dynasty was the darkest dynasty in Chinese history, with emperors not attending court for 121 out of 276 years, and when they did attend, they didn’t do good things. Look at Zhu Yuanzhang, who didn’t do good things, a typical tyrant. He became emperor, being the most humble-born emperor in Chinese history.
