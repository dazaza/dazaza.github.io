Cheng Ming: Modern enterprises "fight the landowner"?

by ej6688tq on 2009-12-07 09:40:13

Saying No to the Winner-Takes-All Phenomenon: Defining Bubbles

The liquidity premium of modern enterprises comes from the capital market. This premium is related to a company's profit, but not linearly. The fact that the Growth Enterprise Market (GEM) saw a sharp increase at its opening indicates that the premium companies receive after listing mainly stems from investors' irrational frenzy. After all, the growth premium represented by corporate profits has already been reflected in the issue price-to-earnings ratio. Neither stock investment nor equity investment are driven by capitalists' or entrepreneurs' profit motives. Although their returns are predicated on the sustainable growth of the enterprise, stock investors focus more on transactional premiums, while equity investors focus more on liquidity premiums. I have previously stated that transaction value is the mainstream value in modern financial markets. So what is liquidity premium? It is a form of transaction value, which is also commonly referred to as an asset bubble. If I were to define an asset bubble, it would be: the liquidity premium generated based on expectations and transactions.

In the game of modern enterprises, it is not always winner-takes-all. Capitalists without "freedom" are likely to be shackled. The exit of equity investments involves trade-offs. After years of hard work cultivating a company, choosing to exit during its most glorious phase means not taking away the company's profits and no longer sharing in its growth. This mindset can be explained by rewriting a famous poem: "Profit is truly precious, the value of growth even higher; for the sake of freedom, both can be forsaken." Here, "freedom" refers to the liquidity of capital, and the price of obtaining "freedom" is neither past profits nor future growth, but rather the liquidity premium gained by the company after going public.

A company's development goes through three stages: the start-up period, the growth period, and the maturity period. Equity investors can choose to enter during the start-up phase and exit during the growth phase, giving up the pursuit of profit in exchange for liquidity premium. This behavioral pattern sometimes also influences capitalists, turning them into equity investors. In such a situation, the game of modern enterprises is not always winner-takes-all. Capitalists without "freedom" are likely to be shackled.

Equity investments pursue "freedom" far more than they do profit. In the choice between profit maximization and liquidity premium, equity investors prefer liquidity, while capitalists prefer profit maximization. Therefore, the game of "Landlord" is not always winner-takes-all. After a company goes public, capitalists have the highest paper asset premium, but due to lack of freedom, their paper asset premium fluctuates with stock prices. Capitalists must bear not only the operational risks of the company but also the transaction risks of stocks. After equity investors exit, stock investors will come and go like a revolving door, and entrepreneurs can also choose to leave after realizing equity incentives, except for the capitalists who don't have this freedom. In this round of "Landlord," the losers are the capitalists because the company's pursuit of profit maximization serves as both an incentive and a shackle.

After returning home, I learned about the game of "Real Money Landlord." In a three-person game where two teams fight against one, from a fairness perspective, it is very asymmetrical. This is very similar to the way modern enterprises operate. Capitalists, entrepreneurs, and equity investors sit together, issuing some shares at high premiums to stock investors. A year later, equity investments fade out, completing the entire investment process, and the company internally restores peace between the landlord and the manager.

Defining Bubbles

If this is the first round of the game, the winners are ranked accordingly: the capitalist has the highest paper premium, followed by the equity investor, then the entrepreneur, and finally, the stock investor bears the transaction risk. However, from the perspective of liquidity, equity investors exit at a premium and no longer bear the operational risks of the company.
