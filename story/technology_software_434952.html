Family civilization is one of the oldest cultural landscapes of human beings.

by was65752 on 2011-05-21 16:49:26

As an important component of traditional Chinese culture, it has its peculiar forms of expression and rich cultural connotations, involving a variety of disciplines such as anthropology, demography, biology, genetics, sociology, education, economics, history, and more.

People, as members of society, are first and foremost members of a family. All human activities begin primarily within the family or clan, which has a profound and lasting influence on an individual, affecting not only their entire life but also often being passed down genetically to future generations.

A family is a small group built on the foundation of marital relations, blood ties, economic relationships, and ethical relationships, characterized by close cooperation and shared living. A clan or lineage extends and develops from family relations, connecting several close-knit families through bloodline ties into a social group. Economic production, population reproduction, and ethical education form the basic premises and characteristics of families and clans.

Families and lineages, as the cells of society and the most basic organizational units, reflect from different angles and at different levels the political, economic, cultural, religious beliefs, ethical morals, customs, and other social conditions of various historical eras, ethnic groups, and countries and regions. They constitute a family culture with distinct era characteristics, serving as a microcosm of human society and an important window for analyzing historical evolution. Therefore, true historical materialists and social science researchers all start by dissecting and studying families. Engels' "The Origin of the Family, Private Property, and the State" is a representative classic work. Moreover, "Cultivate oneself, regulate one's family, govern the country, and bring peace to the world" is an ancient and unwavering principle for standing in life and a maxim for dealing with the world, summarizing thousands of years of traditional culture and crystallizing historical essence.

(1) The Origin of Families and the Formation of Clans

Family is the cell of society, the foundation, and also the product of social development, the result of human civilization's evolution.

In human history, with the development of the social economy, there have successively appeared four types of family structures: "bloodline family," "Punalua family (exogamy)," "pair marriage family," and "monogamous family." The primitive communal bloodline family of the early period represents the ignorance stage of the origin of the family. The exogamous Punalua family and the relatively stable pair marriage family during the clan society were major evolutions from the matrilineal extended family to the patrilineal family. Monogamous marriage based on male bloodlines was the product of the development of social productivity and private ownership, adapting to the needs of the civilized era.

With the development of the social economy and the continuous reproduction of the population, the core monogamous family gave birth to new families. Calculating bloodlines and property inheritance rights through the father became a socially recognized legal and customary standard. Thus, a family-based structure emerged, centered on the core family, networked by bloodline relationships, grounded in shared property, and regulated by ethical norms.

In ancient China, the composition of a family was traditionally referred to as "three clans" or "nine clans." The three clans refer to "paternal clan, maternal clan, and spousal clan," a general term for relatives beyond blood relations. The nine-clan system, structured along bloodline genealogies, vertically includes parents, grandparents, great-grandparents, high grandparents, oneself, children, grandchildren, great-grandchildren, and great-great-grandchildren. Horizontally, it includes oneself, brothers, sisters, cousins, second cousins, third cousins, and so on. Around these two-dimensional nine-clan structures, a nine-clan and five-servant family structure formed.

The cohabitation lifestyle and the inheritance system of "sons inheriting from fathers" allowed families to develop and strengthen continuously. Cohabitation provided the necessary material and economic foundation for newly separated families while avoiding conflicts caused by unfair property distribution. The shared bloodline relationship of the same ancestors and the common economic foundation strengthened the cohesion and binding force of the family, prompting the formation and development of various rules and regulations within the family, allowing family members to live harmoniously and expanding the power of the family. Therefore, many ancient records document phenomena like "a hundred-family clan" and "nine generations living together." Historically, a large, prosperous household was a sign of a thriving family, while separation was considered shameful and a sign of failure.

(2) Family Ethics and Cultural Characteristics

China is an ancient cultural nation with a long history, known as a "land of propriety." As the grassroots organization of society, families differ in their characteristics—some are scholarly, some agricultural, some martial, some commercial—but they all share cultural traits such as benevolence, filial piety, honesty, diligence, simplicity, bravery, progress, respect for ancestors, harmony among clans, patriotism, and dedication, reflecting the shared ethics, customs, and cultural features of the Chinese nation.

1. Family Ethics

Throughout Chinese history, whether in noble families or common households, great emphasis has been placed on the education of moral ethics and behavioral standards. "Lun" refers to the relationship between people, and "li" refers to the principles of reason and propriety. "Ethics" thus refers to the overall guidelines for handling relationships between people.

The scope of Chinese family ethics is very broad, encompassing loyalty, filial piety, benevolence, righteousness, sincerity, trustworthiness, familial affection, respect for elders, chastity, righteous conduct, harmonious neighborhood, mutual assistance, etc., but the most fundamental core is encapsulated in the words "filial piety" and "fraternal duty." An ancient Chinese saying goes, "Of all evils, lust is the worst; of all virtues, filial piety comes first." From ancient times to today, people have always believed that "filial piety" and "fraternal duty" are the fundamentals of being human. In family relationships, the affection between parents and children is most important, and the harmony and love between brothers form the basis for the unity and prosperity of the family. Therefore, every family emphasizes the interpersonal relationships and family atmosphere of "kind parents, filial children, friendly elder brothers, respectful younger brothers." Filial sons and virtuous grandsons are often rewarded by many families and clans, even advocating extreme acts of filial piety such as "cutting flesh to cure illness" or "lying on ice to catch carp." For ungrateful descendants, the strictest punishments under family laws and clan regulations are administered, sometimes leading to criminal prosecution or even expulsion from the clan. Daughters-in-law who disobey their in-laws may be divorced according to the "seven grounds for divorce," bringing great shame to their natal families. Many family laws and clan regulations emphasize that while the parents are alive, "separate registers and independent wealth" are not allowed, meaning that the family cannot be divided or personal wealth accumulated privately.

Since having many offspring is an important indicator of a family's prosperity, childlessness is considered a significant taboo. Therefore, ancient families generally believed that "there are three forms of unfilial behavior, and having no heir is the greatest," urging men to marry at an appropriate age. If a man remains childless into his thirties or forties, he should promptly take a concubine.

Many families also stress "brotherly friendship and respect," promoting harmonious coexistence. During property division, disputes over seniority or sibling rivalry leading to estrangement should be avoided. If the parents pass away early, older brothers should not separate from unmarried siblings. The saying "the eldest brother is like a father, the eldest sister like a mother" succinctly captures this brotherly relationship. Those who do not exhibit "friendship and respect" face strict punishment under clan rules.

Such education and advocacy result in family members respecting hierarchy, distinguishing intimacy and distance, properly handling interpersonal relationships, and preserving family unity and reputation.

Because "filial piety, fraternal duty, loyalty, and trustworthiness" and other ethical norms are the cornerstones of maintaining family, clan, and social order in imperial autocratic eras, successive feudal rulers promoted "governing the world through filial piety," highly praising and rewarding those who practiced "filial piety." They equated filial piety towards parents with loyalty to the emperor, integrating them into family ethics, making "loyalty and filial piety" inseparable. Hence, there has long been the saying that "loyal ministers come from homes of filial sons."

2. Family Education

Family education is a crucial aspect of clan culture. "Expecting one’s son to become a dragon" (hoping for extraordinary achievements) is almost every parent’s greatest wish. The concise saying "If a child is raised but not educated, it is the father's fault" clearly outlines the responsibilities and tasks of parents in educating their children. Throughout history, almost every clan has recorded family precepts and maxims in their genealogies, passing them down to future generations. Among the most famous are the "Yan Family Precepts" and the "Zhu Xi's Rules for Managing the Home."启蒙读物如《弟子规》、《女儿经》、《三字经》、《百家姓》等更是家喻户晓，流传至今。“孟母断杼”、“岳母刺字”更是被众人津津乐道，传颂不绝。

For a long time, the traditional morality, ethical relationships, and knowledge skills of the Chinese nation have been passed down to future generations through conversations and teachings. Therefore, parents are the first teachers of their children, and the family is the first classroom of life. This influence and transmission from the family is an important form of family education. The content of family education is quite extensive, covering almost everything, including moral, intellectual, physical, and aesthetic education. Summarized broadly, it mainly includes four aspects: self-cultivation and worldly conduct, moral ethics; reading and officialdom, seeking fame and fortune; establishing merit and career, glorifying one's family and becoming famous; respecting one's profession and staying true to one's roots, finding stability and purpose in life.

Of course, due to the limitations of the times, in ancient China, the content of family education was predominantly centered around Confucian thought, with parents and elders instilling feudal moral concepts such as "loyalty, filial piety, integrity, and righteousness" and the "Three Bonds and Five Constants" in the younger generation. To some extent, this formed a mental shackle constraining them. However, it cannot be denied that it also contained a wealth of the essence of Chinese traditional culture and excellent virtues, nurturing generation after generation of heroes, virtuous individuals, and patriots.

3. Family Rituals

China has long been known as a "land of propriety." The origin, development, evolution, and perfection of rituals have progressed almost simultaneously with the trajectory of family development, showcasing distinct era characteristics as well as regional colors and ethnic customs. Early human rituals originated from early totem worship, ghost and spirit sacrifices, and ancestor worship ceremonies, imbued with a simple sense of reverence and a desire for blessings. The central content was what was called "revering heaven and serving ghosts." However, as human society continued to develop, merely using ghost and ancestor worship as rituals could no longer meet people's needs. Thus, the scope and content of rituals expanded from various divine matters to various human affairs. Guo Moruo said: "Rituals arose from worshipping gods, later expanded to include humans, and further expanded to include auspicious, ominous, military, guest, and celebratory ceremonies." These rituals were modeled after the most exemplary behaviors in contemporary society, standardized according to value criteria, and evolved into a set of widely applicable, imitable, and referable behavioral processes.

The Book of Rites and the Book of Etiquette, compiled during the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods, summarize various ancient rituals and are classic works of comprehensive ritual. They cover wedding, funeral, capping, sacrificial, drinking, archery, banquets, guest visits, diplomatic missions, and viewing ceremonies, among others.

Among numerous rituals, a significant portion relates to court grand ceremonies or solemn diplomatic protocols. The rituals most closely related to ordinary families are weddings, funerals, celebrations, memorials, adult capping ceremonies, succession rites, and ancestral worship.

① Wedding Ceremony

Marriage is a significant event for both the family and the clan. The Book of Rites states: "A wedding ceremony unites two surnames, honoring the ancestral temple above and continuing the lineage below." Thus, wedding ceremonies are grand, elegant, diverse, interesting, meaningful, deeply symbolic, and rich in national traditions and local characteristics.

In ancient China, people had already noticed the harm of marrying close relatives, and many family laws and clan regulations prohibited marriages within the same surname, continuing the ancient tradition of "surname differentiation in marriage," reflecting scientific concepts of eugenics.

In choosing a spouse, emphasis was often placed on equal status and purity of lineage, without greed for wealth or beauty. Some families advocated "a daughter must marry someone better than our family, and a wife must come from a family inferior to ours," ensuring respect for the husband and devotion to the in-laws.

Regarding taking concubines, practices varied depending on the family situation. Generally, taking a concubine due to lack of heirs was understandable, but doing so despite having a son was discouraged. Wealthy and powerful families often tolerated concubinage without prohibition. Divorce was seen as a "great change in human ethics," and many families and clans opposed hasty divorces. Although there were "seven grounds for divorce" (circumstances under which a husband could divorce his wife), most family laws and clan regulations prohibited unjustified divorces, especially those motivated by wealth disparity. Widow remarriage was not strictly forbidden before the Song Dynasty. However, after Zhu Xi promoted the ethical norm that "starving to death is a minor matter, losing virtue is a grave matter," many families viewed widow remarriage as sinful, intervening harshly and glorifying chaste widows or those who died for their husbands, leading to tragic outcomes.

Since marriage is a major life event concerning lifelong happiness, morality, and social customs, normal families took it very seriously. It required "parental consent and matchmaker's word," along with three engagements and six witnesses, to gain family and societal approval. So-called "mutual love and free marriage" only began to appear at the end of the Qing Dynasty and the beginning of the Republic era, still facing criticism and being prohibited by most family laws and clan regulations until the establishment of New China brought about gender autonomy in marriage.

Due to the high importance given to marriage by successive dynasties and various families, a comprehensive set of rich, complex, and solemn wedding rituals developed.

Ancient Chinese wedding rituals consisted of six parts, known as the "six rites," which included betrothal and marriage stages. The "six rites" refer to nacai (proposal), wenming (inquiring names), naji (auspiciousness confirmation), nazheng (presenting gifts), qingqi (selecting a wedding date), and qinying (personal welcome).

"Nacai" refers to the groom's parents sending a matchmaker to propose to the bride's family. After acceptance, the groom's family sends an envoy with a goose as a gift to formally propose. The use of a goose symbolizes fidelity, as geese are loyal birds that do not remarry if their partner dies, symbolizing a woman's need to remain faithful to her husband.
