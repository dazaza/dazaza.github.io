Point: A Tumblr-like lifestyle information feed service

by daodao on 2011-02-22 18:03:34

DianDian is a life-streaming service developed by imitating Tumblr, where users can record the details of their lives with a usage frequency between traditional blogs and microblogs. DianDian is a life-streaming service developed by mimicking Tumblr, which users can simply understand as a notebook of daily life around them. They can casually record the bits and pieces of their lives on this platform. This approach of DianDian will be significantly distinct from microblogging and traditional blogging, allowing users to post various short and concise texts or other forms of rich media content in a mixed manner. It is understood that DianDian was created by Xu Chaojun, the former president of Shanda Bianfeng after his resignation, and the project has an investment background from Innovation Works.

Generally, I am not very willing to discuss the meaning of "innovation" in my blog or life. On one hand, it is indeed hard to define and describe this word most of the time. But more often, I feel that discussing this topic with some people in China would seem overly sentimental and superficial. However, over these years, while I have been in contact with thousands of Internet products at home and abroad and recorded my random thoughts through blogging, I actually still have some stubborn ideas about "innovation":

Firstly, I must admit that the U.S. Internet is at least three years ahead of our country. Therefore, to a certain extent, due to the existence of this gap in civilization and technology, the needs of users in their country are bound to far exceed the general needs of users in our domestic market. Thus, under this large environment, it is predetermined that our Chinese Internet products often follow and imitate the American way (this conclusion is based on the premise that I have basically agreed with the view that "user needs determine product form"). On this point, I think there is nothing wrong with it, and it is hard to change. So, comrades who are even more angry than me should not transfer this dissatisfaction onto our entrepreneurs.

However, the key issue is that I firmly believe that even under such a large environment, our entrepreneurs can actually create many innovative products with our own characteristics. This kind of innovation, I think, is at least a small innovation through functional borrowing and mixing, user interface reconstruction, and redefinition of information organization structure. If entrepreneurs lose these basic thinking abilities, who will drive the development of China's Internet?! Therefore, when I saw DianDian write in "About Us" that "As for us, we are just a group of silent Internet believers. We are young and full of dreams. We believe in realizing our ideas through our efforts and passion, making valuable products to change people's lives, which is also the original intention of us doing DianDian." I couldn't help but feel that this group of "believers" in the Internet were pretentious, artificial, and restless. In my eyes, true Internet believers find it difficult not to insist on integrating more of their own concepts and ideas into their products. Such pure Copy2China wins Chinese users and speculates capital markets but severely strikes the belief of startups that persist in innovation and loses sight of the value of entrepreneurship.

Of course, complete C2C also has its dual effects. Similarly, from the Innovation Works project, my evaluation of Zhihu is completely different. Although it is also thorough plagiarism and replication, regarding Zhihu, I have already completely ignored the design and planning of the product itself, instead focusing more on the social effects it brings. The value of the Zhihu platform lies in providing domestic Internet users with a better personal knowledge management platform and tool. It is believed that it will play an indispensable role in the sedimentation of knowledge and views for individuals and even the entire era. Therefore, I see such C2C favorably, rather than DianDian.
