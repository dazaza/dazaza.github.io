Mtime Bo guest article: To summarize the feeling that horror films from different regions give people with one word, the conclusion is that European and American horror films are "disgusting", Japanese and Korean horror films are extremely "abnormal", and Hong Kong's? "Not very scary, but somewhat detestable." Of course, this is a generalization. Hong Kong horror films can also be disgusting, for example, "Dumplings" in "Three Extremes 2". The highly acclaimed American production "Watermelon Sugar" released not long ago seems to follow the Japanese and Korean style. However, overall, Western horror films like to depict some nauseating and terrifying scenes, and zombies slowly walking is their repeatedly used trick. No matter how visually terrifying it gets, after watching too much, it becomes boring. Sometimes Westerners have this characteristic - they can be quite dumb. Japanese and Korean horror films are different. They focus mainly on creating an atmosphere, and the source of terror can often be traced back to the distortion of human nature. This makes many Japanese and Korean horror films profound, delving deep enough to make people shiver with fear. If after watching a zombie movie, we leave the cinema with a deep breath because of the eerie light in the room; then after watching a Japanese or Korean horror film like "Ring 0: Birthday", it's not so easy. That kind of terror lingers in your heart, occasionally scaring you.

So, what about border-crossing horror films? Many people may only have a vague impression of coastal horror films. We don't have as much money as Europeans to create grand special effects (although a certain famous director wants to try big productions, which is a separate discussion), nor can we distort things to the extreme like South Korea does, because everyone knows we are very materialistic. We cannot be too idealistic when making horror films. Distortion is even less favored. When it comes to breaking conventions, we always prefer to beautify things. Not just horror films, even in "The Warlords", Jet Li is portrayed as very bad, harming his brothers for power and fame, and we consider this very groundbreaking, thinking it realistically portrays human nature. It's unclear whether these people are really fake or just overly attached.

Speaking of which, let's return to horror films. There's a saying: where there are people, there is a江湖(jianghu - a term referring to the complex world of interpersonal relationships). Where there is ajianghu, there are hearts. Applying this logic, where there are no people, there are "ghosts". Ghosts exist in every population. Don't forget, horror is an innate instinct of every race and era. Although each race and era fears different things, the fear itself is undeniable. Western-style horror films precisely touch the nerve of fear in each era within such gaps. Its terrifying elements include both Eastern and South Korean-style audio-visual shocks, as well as imprints of specific eras' consciousness and historical scars. Horror films from each era, regardless of the method used, have their own set of scare tactics, either targeting superficial memories, grossing at the box office, or sparking discussions. Therefore, despite decades of changes in foreign-style horror films, the terror of each era still leaves a deep impression. Each will be discussed in detail below.

Let's not debate who made the first horror film. But when it comes to Chinese-style horror films, Ma Xuweibang is an unavoidable name. This person was a bit strange. First, he was multi-talented, capable of writing, directing, and acting, and also researched makeup and set design techniques. Secondly, like many geniuses, he was melancholic and often ill. Lastly, his background was a bit eerie. He had a chaotic early life and eventually died tragically in a car accident in an alley.

"The Inn of the Desolate Village" continues to use Ma Xuweibang's creative ideas, which is quite ironic.

In the currently playing "The Inn of the Desolate Village," there's a creative idea: if you sleep in a bed at the inn, you might hear the song of Yanzhi (a beautiful female ghost). This idea originated from Ma Xuweibang's representative work "Midnight Song." I'm not sure if it's because this work is too classic or if the creativity of later creators is too weak. In any case, through his research on makeup techniques over many years and referencing Eastern film styling and patterns, Ma Xuweibang created the image of a passionate male lead disfigured by the times. We previously summarized the classic opera "Wen Mao Fu": the new society turned people into ghosts, and the old society turned ghosts back into people. "Midnight Song" is the best representation of the former statement. Its gloomy, melancholic, and sorrowful style, along with its cry for freedom, greatly won the audience's approval. "Midnight Song" continuously played in over thirty places, setting a record for domestic film box office sales in the year.

Later, Ma Xuweibang directed other horror films such as "The Haunted House Diary," "Hot Moon Poetic Soul," "Leper," "Midnight Song Continues," "Mandarin Duck Tears," "Cold Mountain Rain," etc. Other directors also made films like "Paradise Probe," "Beautiful Corpse Revenge," etc., making horror films a recognized genre.

Spies! Around everyone

"Dark Sentinel in Sheep Village" poster

I recently heard that Chongqing Fengdu plans to invest seven billion yuan to transform Fengdu into a famous ghost city. For economic purposes, culture is used as a platform, with ghost culture being widely promoted. Compared to the early founding period, it's like night and day. Back then, during the Four Olds campaign, smashing and looting were rampant, and not only ghosts, but even gods had to "lower their heads and act as gods." At that time, a word replaced the position of ghosts, and that was spies. Spies are the positive term for spies. Not only positive, but in the high degree of ideological state propaganda, spies almost certainly equate to the synonym of terror.

In these silent anti-spy films like "Dark Sentinel in Sheep Village," "People's Giant Hand," "Railway Guards," "National Day Ten O'clock," "Ancient Temple Bell Sounds," the classic model of intelligence and courage battles between enemies and us, psychological confrontations (like "Inside Job") is placed. Spies wouldn't chat about love like in the remade spy TV dramas broadcasted recently (imagine, Beilingfei, this comedy-like romance and psychological exploration of characters is very attractive to audiences and TV directors, hence why they all fell into the same pattern). At that time, anti-spy films combined the stimulating narrative of suspense films with various elements and patterns of horror films. For example, in "Ancient Temple Bell Sounds," a young monk, a mute old monk, a gloomy ancient temple, mysterious bell sounds — this is the classic character composition and scene setting of horror films. Another example is in "Dark Sentinel in Sheep Village," where our side's covert personnel impersonates 209, interacts with spies known as "Great Immortal" and "Eight Auntie," and uses camera language and sound to create a mysterious and terrifying atmosphere. Also, in "Spring Shadow Case," the few segments of Xu Chunying's flashbacks before being shot are classic scenes of horror and suspense. In summary, the new society turned people into ghosts, and before the founding of the country, spies became ghosts. Anti-spy films borrowed elements and patterns from horror films, becoming another type of horror film after the founding of the country up until the 1980s.

Eastern Suspense, Chinese Horror

After the Cultural Revolution ended, "the spring breeze of reform and opening up swept across the land," haha, this is the opening sentence of most excellent college entrance exam essays in the 1980s. With the renewal of all things, Chinese films not only welcomed the renaissance of the fourth generation and the rise of the fifth generation, but also saw the emergence of various commercial genres. Horror films began to extensively learn and borrow from Eastern film techniques. Just from the film titles, "Hunt Word 99," "Black Triangle," "405 Murder Case," "Fog City," "Priest Peak Mist," "Snake Case," "Forest Mystery Case," "Valley Corpse Change," etc., it's clear they're fully dedicated to scaring people.

During this phase, horror films humbly learned from Eastern suspense narratives, making bold and meticulous studies and insights. The "405 Murder Case" begins with an ordinary murder case. As the young detective investigates deeper, unraveling the threads, it leads to a major political conspiracy, putting the young detective in danger. The film's plot twist is unexpected yet reasonable, with the details and pacing giving the story a fine quality.

"The Lonely Spirit of the Black Tower" (Liang Liang, Mu Denjin) uniquely explored the audio-visual language of horror films. This film, later considered experimental, was initially regarded by many as "the scariest movie in the coastal region." The film paid great attention to the creation of atmosphere through lighting and styling. Narrow hallways, ancient staircases, shadowy children, automatically opening doors, blood in the bathroom, and playthings encompass nearly all the classic elements of earlier horror films. In terms of sound, the director first used electronic sound effects and the creaking of rotten wood, adding a mysterious and eerie atmosphere to the film.

"The Lonely Spirit of the Black Tower" ends with special effects showing lingering resentment.

Huang Zhong's "Fog Mansion" carefully designs a ghostly and misty old mansion. The endless fog in the mansion, the deaf and mute old steward, the bloodstained plastic mannequin, and the blood water spraying out while bathing in the film all make the audience jump in fright.

During this stage, horror films generally learned from Eastern techniques without losing their own characteristics. Many horror films touched on the pain of the Cultural Revolution. The historical pain exposed in such a violent, brutal, and extreme way is probably an important point that cannot be overlooked in Cultural Revolution memory.

More hype than actual scares

Throughout the 1990s, horror films struggled to survive. In such difficult times, a "horror director" suddenly emerged (there was an interview with him titled "I am horror, I am the 'ghost director'"). His name is A Gan, maybe born to be an advertising director, thus having a market-oriented mindset and a stubborn spirit of success. He made "Ancient Mirror Strange Tales," "Spiritual Fury," "House Spirit," "Close Your Eyes When It Gets Dark," collectively known as "A Gan Series," claiming to occupy the territory of horror films and "take horror films to the extreme."

A Gan's film model can be summarized as low budget, second-tier stars, "big package wet" (he did everything himself, which was quite hard), and the most powerful move: hype.

"Shining Ferocity" claims to be "Mainland China's first horror film." There were numerous hypes like "alluding to Dou Wei and Wang Fei's relationship change," "Huang Chun dead role acted by Ye Tong leading the charge," "male lead Red Bean rumored to have affairs with male actors," "female lead's nude scene causing controversy," "Chinese version of 'Scream,'" "Director A Gan determined to challenge Feng Xiaogang's box office miracle," etc. An accurate evaluation of A Gan's "Close Your Eyes When It Gets Dark" is: "Chinese horror films still have a distance from international standards, but I believe the story of 'Close Your Eyes When It Gets Dark' reaches Pan-Asian standards, and the production reaches Korean standards."

Is there really any film that doesn't involve hype? When "Midnight Song" was released, there was fake news about an old child being scared to death by the film. The issue is that A Gan's films were too hyped, overshadowing the actual content. A film critic harshly said that A Gan's horror skills were "just a project of scenic spots, at the level of five yuan tickets for a quick walk around." After "Close Your Eyes When It Gets Dark" encountered the "Bun Tail Blood Case," A Gan seemed inspired and started producing comedy "short films" series. No matter how you look at it, it feels like A Gan is still making advertisements, with catchy slogans (like "Close your eyes when it gets dark," that phrase is quite clever), lacking creativity, fragmented segments, and films that are messy. There's a bit of market appeal, but no depth at all.

Similarly, films like "Suffocation," "Curse," "Seven Days," and "Door" mostly made a big splash but lacked substance, ending hastily. They weren't as innovative as the creators hoped.

Stills from "Believe Without Doubt" In recent years, there have been a few horror films, such as "Believe Without Doubt," "Ghosts in the Heart," "Deep Sea Find People," "Desolate Village Inn." Compared to previous stages, supernatural elements have increased, and the reasons for fear mostly come from mental abnormalities of the characters. The stories are weaker and can mostly be explained rationally. While frightening, they also reveal warmth.

At this stage, viewing these horror films as story films is acceptable, but as horror films, they feel unsatisfying. Because the directors put equal effort into both the story and the horror elements, their focus is divided. For example, in "West Side Ghost," once the audience knows who the ghost is, the fear diminishes, violating the suspenseful narrative law of horror films. In "Desolate Village Inn," the innkeeper is the creator of the terror, but this character is too ridiculous. Threatening the male lead with a knife, even cutting off a finger without stopping, and finally letting them go, giving them a chance to repent and rethink life, it feels more like a teaching moment. In "Deep Sea Find People," sometimes it's terrifying, sometimes it's touching, feeling like alternating between drinking soap water and juice. Actually, Coppola's "Bram Stoker's Dracula" successfully blended horror and romance, like a potent cocktail, but unfortunately, we lack such creativity.

Perhaps the prosperity of horror films really has a lot to do with the film rating system. Horror originally challenges normalcy, inevitably breaking new ground in unknown concepts and phenomena, offering new explanations. It must be unrestricted. So, where does the future of horror films lie?
