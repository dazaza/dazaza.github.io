A entrepreneur's own account: How I sell fish online

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:49:27

　　Actually, when I first started selling fish online, many friends around me thought I was crazy.

　　When everyone's eyes were on real estate, technology, mobile phones, and cars, I chose to sell fish. It was indeed a tough decision, but it felt solid.

　　How can O2O be done creatively? Should we abandon WeChat? Who is more awesome, Lei Jun or J.Wong? Can BlackBerry make a comeback? Can Cook take over from Jobs? I don't care about these things because they are too far away from us. Dreams may be big, but reality is small. People should still live a little more mundanely.

　　YouCai failed, and it was hard for them to accept. But I still have no doubt about the great potential of fresh produce in e-commerce.

　　In 2012, I started trying to sell fish online through B2C. YouCai failed, but I survived. Of course, this might be related to the fact that I am just a small-scale startup company with low operating costs and easier execution. However, some experiences I've learned along the way, I believe, have certain value, and I'm willing to share them with you all. Maybe they could give some inspiration to those who are starting businesses online selling fish, vegetables, or fruits.

1. Product Positioning

　　My product positioning is: never do cheap daily products, only focus on mid-range offerings. In the early stages, avoid vegetables and meats; instead, focus solely on frozen seafood.

　　Let me explain. I think your biggest competitor in e-commerce for fresh produce is the local farmers' market. Compared to them, I consider myself at a disadvantage. First, the traditional group of shoppers consists mainly of middle-aged and elderly people, most of whom would never have the habit of buying fish online. Second, you might argue that selling fish online saves tens of thousands in stall fees compared to physical stalls, but the harsh reality is: relative to vendors who restock almost every morning with near-zero inventory, your storage, labor, and delivery costs over a year will be much higher than theirs.

　　YouCai mainly failed here. Observing its product structure, there are almost no mid-to-high-end products, mostly common agricultural by-products found in farmers' markets, directly facing competition from local farmers' markets. You want to change the shopping habits of traditional aunties, which is as unrealistic as making cows stop eating grass and start eating meat.

　　Compared to supermarkets, I find my advantages quite clear: shorter procurement cycles mean fresher products (this refers to bulk goods cycles), prices nearly 40% cheaper (don't think supermarkets are cheap), and a younger customer base (I’ve observed that most supermarket shoppers are aged between 25-40), fitting my requirements well.

　　Seafood categories are also numerous, mainly divided into live seafood, chilled seafood, and frozen seafood (no need to explain each one here, if interested, search yourself). Live seafood has the highest storage requirements, and the price difference between a live fish and a dead fish can be around 50%, so it isn't suitable for small-cost operators like me. Frozen seafood, after preliminary processing, is the easiest to preserve with minimal loss. Generally, as long as the temperature is maintained, the shelf life is around one and a half to two years (Ding Jingtao said YouCai's fresh produce loss rate was 5%, I simply don't believe it).

　　Therefore, I positioned my competitors as supermarkets. I selected ingredients used in buffets, Western restaurants, mid-to-high-end Chinese restaurants, and Japanese cuisine. High profit margins, not easily available on the market, and easy to maintain quality. This way, I avoided the impact of farmers' markets, and the product itself enhances its own value.

2. Price Positioning

　　Unlike phones, cars, and houses, this market is extremely sensitive to price.

　　For example, for the same pound of cabbage or salmon, if your price is more than 5 yuan higher than the market price, customers won’t be interested. You can only attract them by being cheaper than the market price. But the profit from a pound of cabbage versus a salmon...well, you understand what I mean.

　　Regarding pricing, Ding Jingtao from YouCai once said: “Even for tomatoes, prices may vary 2 to 5 times. The quality of vegetables in the market varies greatly, and nutritional safety aspects differ. YouCai tries to source high-quality produce whenever possible. For instance, cucumbers can cost either 1.2 yuan or 1.5 yuan per unit, and we choose the 1.5 yuan ones.”

　　“So, the vegetable prices on YouCai are slightly higher than those in the farmers’ markets. Ding Jingtao emphasized that if the quality is the same, YouCai is definitely cheaper.”

　　To all of this, I say: Mr. Ding must have never sold vegetables himself.

　　90% of aunties absolutely won’t buy cabbage that’s priced higher than the market rate, no matter how much you hype it up, even if you claim it’s green, organic, super-duper amazing cabbage. Aunties are practical—wherever the price is lower and the quality acceptable, that's where they’ll shop.

　　If your product is positioned as agricultural produce, then even the slightest price fluctuation can significantly affect sales. If you follow Mr. Ding’s approach, you’d have to sell agricultural produce that barely passes quality checks but is much cheaper than the market price, running on volume. This allows you to capture a large market share with low profit margins. But for a startup without market experience or brand recognition, aiming to capture the market right off the bat is very risky.

　　Without sufficient gross margins, it’s hard to even conduct market promotions. Unfortunately, Mr. Ding tried to position YouCai as premium agricultural produce while expecting aunties to accept prices higher than the market and without allowing them to inspect the goods on-site. This undoubtedly pushed him into a dead end.

　　So, I set realistic prices for my products: they must be lower than the market price but still highly profitable. I don’t care about market share; I only care if my gross margin is high enough.

　　Would you rather earn 1 cent from 1000 people or 1 yuan from 100 people? I know how to do the math.

3. Channel Costs

　　A small suggestion from me: any industry must have its unique channels. These channels don’t necessarily monopolize the market, but relatively few product channels ensure your product has a certain uniqueness and scarcity.

　　While doing wholesale, I was thinking about this problem, and the solution was simple: first, try to avoid competitive first-tier cities. After all, how can you compete barefoot against someone driving a car? Second, choose less popular origins. My product origins are concentrated in three places: South America, Africa, and Northeast Asia. The weakness of giants (including other large aquatic companies) lies in their inability to pivot quickly—they're big but inflexible. All I need to do is observe the market, select the right products, and make decisions independently. By the time they get multiple levels of approval, my product channel foundation will already be firmly established.

　　YouCai positioned its channels domestically, which made the threshold too low. Wholesale principles are similar to group-buying—mainly dependent on volume. So today you can negotiate with farmers, and tomorrow they can do the same. Today you can create a YouCai, and tomorrow they can replicate it as "YouCai Flower."

4. Marketing Model

　　This is something we must discuss in detail.

　　YouCai positioned itself as "delivering vegetables like milk," attempting to build a closed ecosystem similar to iOS, where customers can enjoy doorstep delivery service without leaving home. This brings up two issues: 1. How to attract potential customers to your platform? 2. Why should customers stay? (Or as Mr. Ding puts it, increase customer stickiness.)

　　I initially noticed YouCai due to a Sina report. Since the products were similar, I clicked out of curiosity. When I opened its UI, I was disappointed. An outdated interface with no design sense, besides product types and price descriptions, there were no other relevant consultations. The variety wasn't rich, yet it included items like disinfectant and toothpaste that had nothing to do with "vegetables"...

　　I really couldn't figure out how YouCai planned to retain customers. Opening its webpage felt like walking into a chaotic roadside grocery store, making me forget that I originally came to look at "vegetables."

　　Relying solely on the unoriginal milk-style sales model of order-delivery-after-sales to attract customers was undoubtedly YouCai's biggest mistake.

　　So, before I started, I had my products prepared into various dishes. Just for one type of crab, I made soups, stews, stir-fries, etc. Then I hired professional photographers to artistically process the dishes, making them look as appetizing as possible.

　　Here, I borrowed IKEA's concept—enticing customers with finished products but only selling semi-finished ones. Letting customers fully experience the joy of achievement is much stronger than giving them a ready-made product.

　　Later, when many customers exchanged ideas on QQ, I simply talked to them about how to use my raw materials to cook, rather than focusing on how delicious the fish or crab was. Taobao doesn’t sell products; it sells pictures, and the same goes for fresh produce. Eventually, I stopped shouting about selling products and only chatted with foodies about how to eat the best way.

　　Targeting a younger customer base, many people like to eat, but they don’t know how. They actually want to eat, so you need to teach them how to make it taste good. (In this aspect, sometimes I even think Dianping would be more suitable for fresh produce).

　　Later, more and more customers gave positive feedback. I observed that 80% of them were foodies, and 20% came because of friends' recommendations. For this, I specifically created several QQ groups. As long as it didn't violate national laws, members freely shared their foodie experiences and compared other brands of seafood. Don't fear competition; the more competition, the more your product's uniqueness and price advantage stands out.

　　In terms of model, YouCai aimed to do an electronic + commerce model, but I personally think this was also a misstep. There are two paths: one is to become a platform-focused business like Taobao, building relationships with offline wholesalers without engaging in commerce itself. The second is to only do commerce, quickly establishing various regional distribution platforms, using options like SNS, regional BBS, Weibo marketing, WeChat marketing, etc., focusing on channel quality and price.

　　I prefer the latter because it allows direct contact with customers.

　　My current model focuses on offline wholesale, mainly supplying hotels, buffets, and restaurants. Online, I focus on retail, with prices slightly higher than offline. But I don't create my own platform; I affiliate with a few popular local BBS communities, leveraging their traffic to attract customers. If the customers react positively to the product, it spreads virally.

　　Sometimes, customers even upload photos of their self-cooked dishes to "show off." Usually, I offer some small extra rewards, and over time, almost no one leaves the QQ group. Communication increases, and by the second half of last year, I almost didn't need to promote myself online anymore—all reviews were done by customers themselves.

　　Another benefit of this approach is solving trust issues. Doing online fresh produce in China is challenging—not only is standardization difficult (which I'll talk about later), but also the public lacks trust in the hygiene of online fresh produce. Simply put, after drinking Sanlu milk and eating Sudan Red eggs, the general public has lost significant trust in merchants. But through word-of-mouth promotion, this issue can be resolved.

　　In terms of profitability, I adopt small-batch retailing. My minimum standard is five-pound packaging—I don’t retail one or two pounds. Prices are attractive, and volumes increase.

5. Warehousing and Logistics

　　I dare say that aside from procurement costs, warehousing and logistics are another major expense. This includes manpower + transportation + warehousing costs + losses.

　　YouCai adopted a method inspired by milk boxes—delivering door-to-door at fixed times daily. I see a big problem here: suppose I place an order for a pound of cabbage, two pounds of pork belly, and half a pound of ginger and garlic just before work ends. When I get home, I realize that what I saw online differs greatly from what I see in reality, and I decide not to take it. YouCai's customer service is great, and after communication, they agree to unconditional returns.

　　My question is: What do I eat for dinner tonight?

　　If I have such an experience once, I probably won't buy agricultural products on YouCai again to save a few bucks, because I'd starve...

　　What YouCai loses isn't just missed orders and customer churn but also product degradation (some products degrade overnight or after being out of storage for a period), plus repeated labor and transportation costs.

　　This is the biggest problem e-commerce faces in fresh produce: how to solve product standardization.

　　Frankly speaking, I currently don't have a good solution to this problem. I've heard complaints like "it doesn't look exactly like the online picture." All I can do is compensate with service—if dissatisfied, return it.

　　Because my company is small, I can only cut costs. Based on regional order volumes, I deliver at fixed times and check goods face-to-face. This way, I can explain to customers on the spot and eliminate misunderstandings (some misunderstandings arise because customers don't fully understand the product), and also avoid the repeated losses of secondary deliveries.
