A Management Thought Triggered by a Rubik's Cube Interview Joke

Is management as simple as "assigning the right people to do the right things?"

By Xu Shuguang, Chairman of GreenTree Hotels and www.519.com Wine Direct Sales Network

An interview question from a big boss for new hires

My old classmate is now rolling in money. (Super rich!!)

He founded a software company and developed a series of software products, expanding his business more and more.

Today, after arriving in this city, he immediately called me.

"Xin Han, it's me! Recognize me? Here's the thing, I'm here recruiting graduates from the university, and I'll be staying for five days. Let's take this time to catch up properly. My treat!"

Since he was treating, I naturally went along with it. He's the boss; why not enjoy the free meal!

I arrived at the hotel where he was staying and saw a person who looked like a college student being interviewed in front of him.

"Well," my old classmate said, "I have a Rubik's Cube here. Can you solve it so that each side shows one solid color?"

"Watch carefully, I'll give you a demonstration." Saying this, he began manipulating the Rubik's Cube. In no time, the cube was solved.

"Do you see?" he said, "Now it's your turn!"

The college student took the Rubik's Cube but looked puzzled. My old classmate then noticed me and turned to the student:

"If you haven't figured it out yet, you can take the Rubik's Cube home and think about it. I won't be leaving until Friday."

After the student left, I asked my old classmate: "What's this? Is this your original exam question?"

"Hmph! This guy has connections, and I feel obligated to hire him. So I gave him a test to figure out what position would suit him best."

"If I were you," I said, "I wouldn't be as smart as you are. I'd just disassemble the Rubik's Cube and reassemble it piece by piece."

"If he did that, it would mean he's bold and takes responsibility. That kind of person could handle market expansion work."

"What about other methods?"

"Nowadays, kids don't play with Rubik's Cubes anymore, so I don't expect him to solve it instantly."

"If he paints all six sides with different colors, it shows creativity, and he could work in the software development department."

"If he brings back the Rubik's Cube this afternoon, it means he's very intelligent and quick to learn. He'd be most suitable as my assistant."

"If he returns it before Wednesday, it indicates that he sought help from others, meaning he's well-connected and could work in customer service."

"If he brings it back before I leave, it shows he's hardworking and diligent, making him fit for entry-level programming jobs."

"If he eventually admits he can't solve it, it means he's honest, and he could handle inventory or finance roles."

"But if he doesn't bring it back at all, there's nothing I can do."

That’s how it goes!

---

The next evening, my old classmate invited me for dinner. Over the meal, I brought up the topic of the Rubik's Cube again.

This time, my old classmate seemed quite smug.

"I've decided to hire that college student! He returned the Rubik's Cube to me this morning. Guess what?"

"He bought me a new one!"

He continued: "I tried twisting the old Rubik's Cube every which way, but I couldn't restore it. So I bought a new one, and it's even bigger and smoother than your original one!"

"What does this indicate?" I asked.

My old classmate lowered his voice: "He's absolutely perfect material for producing pirated goods!"

I received this little story today, and I found it incredibly interesting. Perhaps this is what they mean by "placing the right people in the right positions." While it makes some sense, I believe this is merely the first step in a long journey of talent cultivation. Otherwise, if this were truly effective, there would be no issue of talent shortages, and all schools, including GreenTree's Hotel Leadership Management Academy, should close their doors.
