When You Encounter a Tick

What should we do when ticks appear around us?

First, avoid staying in forests and grasslands for too long. If you are an agricultural worker or work outdoors and must enter areas where ticks may be present, personal protection is essential. Wear a hat, long pants, and long sleeves, and tuck your pant legs into your socks or boots. If possible, use mosquito repellents containing DEET on your clothing and exposed skin. Such repellents can be purchased at any large supermarket. If not necessary, it's recommended to wear light-colored clothing. This isn't because light colors repel ticks but because they make it easier to spot ticks that land on your clothes.

After returning home from places where ticks might be present, check your pets first as they are more likely to be bitten by ticks than humans. When taking a shower, pay special attention to areas with skin folds such as the scalp, behind the ears, neck, armpits, groin, wrists, and knees, to see if there are any ticks biting you. Ticks' saliva contains components that can prevent you from feeling pain. Studies have shown that most harmful microorganisms carried by ticks are transmitted to humans only after the tick has been attached for over 24 hours. Removing ticks within 24 hours can greatly reduce the risk of contracting tick-borne diseases.

If you find a tick attached to your body, never squeeze, pull, or irritate it with fire or other objects. Doing so could cause the tick's mouthparts to break off inside the skin or stimulate the tick to secrete more saliva carrying pathogens, increasing the risk of infection. The best course of action is to seek medical help immediately. If circumstances force you to handle it yourself, use a pair of fine-tipped tweezers to grasp the tick as close to the skin as possible, then pull it straight out without twisting to avoid breaking the mouthparts. After removing the tick, clean the wound with alcohol or water. Do not discard the removed tick; instead, place it in a sealed plastic bag or container and freeze it in the refrigerator. In case you develop symptoms of a tick-borne disease later, this tick will assist doctors in identifying the cause more easily.

How to control tick-borne diseases? First, we need to understand that ticks are merely transmission vectors. In areas where human infections of tick-borne diseases occur, there must also be other animals carrying related pathogens, though they may not exhibit symptoms themselves. Since ticks are not picky about their hosts, various animals like rats, wild birds, poultry, livestock, and pets could all potentially be sources of tick-borne diseases. While actively treating human cases, investigations into relevant hosts and pathogen control must also be conducted. Secondly, if a tick outbreak occurs, create buffer zones between green belts and residential areas, similar to firebreaks during wildfires, by clearing weeds and trees to cut off the ticks' pathways towards residential areas. Using chemical insecticides is also a quick and effective method. Moreover, scientists have long been researching biological controls, and some methods of using insects to control insects have already been tested in practice.
