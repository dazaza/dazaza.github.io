Mother's experience

by dyy1501168885 on 2011-03-16 10:01:20

For many years, I have always wanted to write about my mother, but when I pick up the pen, I am still terrified. My mother's life has been full of hardships and misfortunes. I don't want to bring up painful memories for her and make her calm heart in old age be disturbed again.

In my mother's memory, the first thing that made her suffer was foot binding.

Since the Southern Tang Dynasty in China, women's foot binding has been practiced until it was abolished in the mid-period of the Republic of China, lasting for nearly a thousand years.

So-called foot binding is to take advantage of the bone development period when women are young, force the four toes under the sole, then use a cloth band about 1 meter long and 15 centimeters wide, start wrapping from the big toe, tightly tie like wrapping a zongzi (sticky rice dumpling), and wrap all the way to above the ankle. Even if the toes are twisted or broken, there is no pity, so day after day, the feet develop abnormally. Around the age of ten, a pair of "three-inch golden lotus" that meet people's aesthetic standards will basically be formed. Because at that time, women who naturally had big feet would find it difficult to find a good husband in the future.

When my mother was six years old, her grandmother started binding her feet. Mother said that the bound feet felt like being cut by a knife. When walking, she didn't dare to apply force on the front, but could only rely on the heels, and every step was excruciatingly painful. Often, as soon as the feet were wrapped, she would untie the bandage behind everyone's back. With the support of grandfather, grandmother did not insist, but sighed deeply. Mother often told me that fortunately, grandparents did not persist, which not only spared her much pain but also gave her a pair of healthy big feet that her peers envied later.

Mother was born in March of the lunar year 1934, one of seven siblings. Due to the family's poverty, when she was seven years old, her grandmother reluctantly sent her to a family in Jiaxiang, Jining. Saying that she was giving her away, it was actually finding a place for her to survive, so that she would not starve to death.

During the days of living under someone else's roof, mother endured countless tortures: this family never regarded mother as a human being. From morning till night, she had to take care of their children, wash clothes, cook meals, cut grass, and feed pigs, without a moment of rest. The hardest to endure was that not only the children hit and scolded her at will, but sometimes even the adults would beat her with fists and kicks. Her body was covered with bruises, often before the old wounds healed, new ones appeared. Mother lived in constant fear and abuse.

Three years later, my uncle, who was seven years older than mother, was dispatched by grandparents to visit mother in Jiaxiang. When he saw his emaciated and bruised sister, tears streamed down his face. He knelt before the elders of that family, begging them to let him take his sister away.

Perhaps the elder had compassion, or perhaps he was moved by my uncle's sincerity, or maybe to avoid criticism from neighbors, the elder agreed to let my uncle take his sister away.

After returning home, although life was very hard, mother was spiritually happy, so she worked especially hard. Mother said that was the happiest time of her childhood. Being together with parents and siblings was the most blissful.

At the age of seventeen, mother married father, and at nineteen, she gave birth to my brother. Since the day my brother was born was the Chinese solar term "Mangzhong", he was named "Mang".

According to what mother and the neighbors said, brother Mang was not only exceptionally handsome but also extraordinarily intelligent. He could learn things in one go, and remember everything he read. People said he was the reincarnation of the "Wenqu Star". However, in 1963, when he was nine years old, he got seriously ill. He suffered from headaches and sore throats day and night. When it was severe, he would bang his head against the wall while holding his neck with both hands. Grandmother said he had 'throat obstruction' (diphtheria), and mother thought it was a brain problem (brain tumor). At that time, the family was very poor, without enough food, and the neighbors were not well-off either. There was absolutely no ability to treat him, and my brother painfully held on for several months before dying in mother's arms. On the day of burying my brother, mother tightly hugged his body, refusing to let go no matter how others persuaded her. In the end, it was grandmother who placed me, who was only three months old and crying for milk, in mother's arms that brought her back to reality. Later, whenever mother mentioned this to me, her eyes were filled with tears, and she sighed for a long time. I think: this might be a way for mother to express dissatisfaction with my laziness and lack of interest in studying.

Because of treating my brother's illness, the family accumulated quite a bit of debt. Mother was an expert weaver and wanted to earn some income by selling cloth to repay the debt. Speaking of weaving, there are actually many procedures involved. First, cotton seeds are processed into cotton wadding using a "machine", the wadding is spun into threads, which are then processed into yarns through a spinning wheel. The yarns are hung on the loom, and after stepping on pedals and operating levers, the shuttle moves back and forth on the loom to weave the cloth. The finished cloth must then be washed and dried, and finally beaten with a wooden mallet to make it compact before folding for exchange or sale. At that time, according to policy regulations, all personal commodity transactions were considered speculation and profiteering, and individuals were not allowed to conduct business. Otherwise, they would be criticized or subjected to labor reform. But for the sake of the family, mother resolutely engaged in the business of weaving and selling cloth. To reduce trouble, and more importantly, to avoid being labeled as a speculator, mother mostly chose bad weather when no one would notice, traveling over thirty miles to Bai Shi market for transactions, often setting out at dawn and returning late at night. Sometimes, mother would carry me in her arms, and on her back, she carried dozens of kilograms of woven cloth or cotton seeds, walking alone on sparsely populated paths, facing sandstorms and frost clinging to her clothes. Actually, physical suffering was secondary; the main issue was mental. Each transaction had to be done secretly, like stealing. If caught, confiscation of the cloth or cotton seeds would be considered light punishment, and in severe cases, one would be detained for re-education or even labor reform.

One day in December of a certain year, the weather was fine in the morning, but suddenly a strong wind arose in the afternoon. After completing a homespun cloth transaction in Bai Shi, mother returned late due to the stormy weather obscuring the road. She could only grope her way home by memory. Stumbling in the icy darkness, she walked and walked until dawn and realized she had taken the wrong path, ending up forty miles away in Da Meng Market. At that time, mother was frozen to the point where her whole body trembled like sifting grain. She tightly held me in her arms, hiding in a corner to avoid the wind. Fortunately, an early-rising old man discovered her, hurriedly helped her into his home, lit firewood to warm her, and gave her ginger soup. After mother recovered, he sent his son to escort her home.

Whenever people refer to the history of the 1960s as a "hardship" journey, mother always shakes her head in opposition, saying that compared to the old society, that wasn't really considered hardship. If anything, it could only be called 'difficulty', because life was extremely difficult for everyone from top to bottom. Then she would remind me: Nowadays, life is too good, you must work hard, learn thrift, and deserve the good times we've hardly obtained under the leadership of Chairman Mao, General Zhu, and Premier Zhou.

At the age of sixty-four, mother suffered greater pain when my younger brother, twelve years younger than me, committed suicide by drinking poison due to marital issues.

Mother gave birth to us three sons in total. My brother died young due to illness, and my younger brother died at the age of twenty-four. The impact on her was unimaginable. In the hospital, facing my younger brother's body, she remained unusually calm. She first bent down and used her left hand to gently pry open her younger brother's eyes, staggered slightly, then straightened her body, and quietly said to me: "Let's take him home."

In the few days following the burial of my younger brother, my wife and I stayed by my parents' side. Mother lay quietly half on the bed, eating nothing, drinking nothing, sleeping nothing for three consecutive days, without shedding a single tear. Her eyes were filled with bewilderment and confusion, and despite the neighbors' repeated consolations, she did not utter a word. Until the fourth day, mother suddenly got out of bed, asked my wife and me to prepare some food for her, and pulled my daughter-in-law's hand, saying: "You two should go to work, public matters come first. Don't worry about family matters." Then she said to me: "Now I understand. Your younger brother is gone, but there's still you. For your sake, your father and I must persevere and live well!"

I choked up. In just four short days, my parents' hair turned half white, and they looked ten years older. The kind of pain and inner struggle they endured was indeed unimaginable for ordinary people.

Mother's life was full of difficulties, obstacles, and pain, but she was also strong, enduring various misfortunes. In her, the excellent qualities of diligence, strength, generosity, and simplicity of Chinese women are embodied, worthy of our respect, admiration, and learning.
