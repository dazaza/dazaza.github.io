Children's English games

by kayaedu on 2009-08-28 16:57:46

This is a game that trains students' ability to listen, write, and recognize letters. Each student prepares a piece of paper and draws a tic-tac-toe grid on it, dividing the paper into nine squares. Then the teacher randomly reads out nine learned letters. As the students listen, they fill in the letters in the squares, placing them in any square they choose. After the students have filled in their grids, the teacher again reads out the nine letters in a random order. The students listen and circle the letters they hear. When the circled letters form a straight line horizontally, vertically, or diagonally, the student can shout "Bingo" and raise their paper for the teacher to check. The first student to shout "Bingo" and who has written the letters correctly wins. This game can also be used for phonetic symbols, words, or numerals.

8 Passing Letters

Each column forms a group, and the class is divided into several groups. The teacher gives the last student in each group a piece of paper with a letter or a set of letters written on it (such as KG-PV). When the teacher says "start," the last student whispers the content on the card to the student in front of them, and this student then whispers what they heard to the next student... This continues until the first student writes the transmitted letter or set of letters on the blackboard. The fastest and most accurate group wins.

9 Follow Me

This is a game that trains students to memorize the letters in alphabetical order. Before starting, distribute letter cards to the students. Then say a letter (for example: M), and the student holding that letter card stands up and says: "I am M. Follow me, please." The student holding the letter N should immediately stand behind the student with the M card and say: "I am N. Follow me, please." And so on. Correct answers earn 10 points, incorrect ones earn no points. This game can also be played in reverse order, or by asking "I am M. Who is before me?" In which case, the student holding the letter L should immediately stand in front of the student with the M card.

10 Letter Order

This is a game that trains students to memorize the order of letters. The teacher calls a student's name and shows them a letter card. The called student immediately reads the letter and says a word containing that letter sound. Then the student before them says the previous letter and a word containing that letter sound, followed by the student after them saying the next letter and a word containing that letter sound. For example: called student: B-bag, the student before: A-apple, the student after: C-cat. Any mistakes or inability to continue results in a negative point for the group.

11 Letter Order Relay Race

Take two sets of 20 letter cards in both uppercase and lowercase, mix up their order, and stick them on the blackboard, one set per side. Then divide the whole class into two teams (or boys vs girls). The game begins with one student from each team coming up to the board in relay style, moving one letter at a time to rearrange them in alphabetical order. The team that completes the task fastest and most accurately wins.

12 Quick Say Letters Competition

Divide the entire class into several groups, and the competition takes place between two groups. After the teacher announces the start of the game, the first student in the first group immediately says a letter (for example C), and the first student in the second group must immediately say the next letter D. Mistakes or inability to quickly say the letter results in a negative point. The team with the least deductions wins.

13 Letter Lineup

Give each student a letter card. The teacher says a letter (for example C), and the students holding letter C and the four subsequent letters (DEFG) must immediately come out and line up in order. If the class size is large, the students can also be divided into two groups for competition.

14 Uppercase Finds Lowercase

The teacher posts a set of uppercase letters on the blackboard. One student from each group comes to the podium, and the teacher gives them a set of lowercase letters mixed in random order. When the teacher says "start," they must quickly arrange the lowercase letters according to the order of the uppercase letters on the blackboard. The team that sticks the letters fastest and most accurately wins.

15 Stool Grabbing

The teacher divides the students into two groups, giving each group 26 uppercase and lowercase letter cards, and arranges five stools in a circle in front of the stage. The game starts when the teacher says three letters, and simultaneously plays music. Six students from both groups holding these three letter cards must immediately come forward and jog around the stools. When the music stops, they grab stools. If all three members of a group grab stools, the group earns 10 points.

16 Letter Snowball

This is a game that trains students' memory of letters. Divide the students into several groups or boys vs girls. The game starts with the first student saying a letter (for example: B), the second student adding another letter arbitrarily (for example: BE), and so on (for example: B…BE…BEX…BEXG…BEXGL…). Students are eliminated if they make a mistake, and the group that accumulates the most correct sequences wins.

17 Guess the Letter

Divide the whole class into several groups. The teacher shows the students some letter cards (for example 10 letters), allowing them to read them. Then the teacher pulls out one card and places it behind their back, letting each group's students guess. The group that guesses correctly earns 10 points. The teacher then shows another card and continues letting the students guess. After the first row of students from each group has guessed, the second row continues. Finally, the group with the highest score wins.

18 Letter Writing Relay Competition

This is a game that trains students to write letters. Each row forms a group, dividing the class into several groups. The teacher gives the last student in each group a piece of paper with a letter written on it, only allowing that student to see the letter. When the teacher says "start," the last student writes the letter on the back of the student in front using their finger... This continues down the line. Finally, the first student writes the transmitted letter on the blackboard. The fastest and most accurate group wins.

19 Letter Writing Competition

Hang a letter chart on the blackboard. Two competing groups send four people each to the front, facing the entire class in a line. The game begins with the first student from Team A turning around to face the blackboard, while the first student from Team B points to any letter on the letter chart. After seeing it, the first student from Team A writes the letter on the back of the second student in their group using their finger, and then the second student says the letter aloud. Correct answers earn 10 points. The game continues with the first student from Team A pointing to a letter, the first student from Team B writing it, and the second student from Team B guessing the letter. The team with the most points at the end wins.

20 Find Vowels

The teacher gives the students some cards with both vowels and consonants. Each student holds one card. When the teacher says "start," the students holding vowel cards should immediately hold up their cards and line up in order. This game can also be done by dividing the entire class into two groups for competition, with the group that lines up correctly and fastest winning.

21 Find Partners

The teacher distributes letter cards to the students, with each student holding one card. Then the teacher asks students holding letters with the same phoneme to stand together. For example, if the teacher says a phoneme, students holding F, M, N, S, X, etc., should immediately gather together.

22 Pick Apples, Learn Phonemes

The teacher first hangs a picture of an apple tree full of apples on the blackboard, with each apple having a letter written on it. Then show several baskets, each labeled with a letter such as S, A (or K), E (or P). Next, select a few students to come to the front, pick the apples off the tree, and place the apples with the same phoneme into the corresponding basket. For example, place the apples with F, M, N, X, etc., into the basket labeled with the letter S, and place the apples with B, C, D, V, etc., into the basket labeled with the letter E (or P). The fastest and most accurate wins.

23 Quick Read Phonetics

Divide the entire class into several groups, then the teacher shows some phonetic symbol cards one by one. The students raise their hands to quickly answer. The teacher lets the first hand-raising student read out the phonetic symbol. Correct reading earns the group 10 points, and the group with the most points wins.

24 Quick Read Words from Phonetics

Divide the entire class into several groups, then the teacher shows some phonetic symbol cards for words one by one (such as [keik]). The students raise their hands to quickly answer. The teacher lets the first hand-raising student read out the word, spell it, and explain its meaning. Correct reading and spelling earns the group 10 points, and the group with the most points wins.

25 Phonetic Listening Contest

This is a game that trains students to listen to phonetic symbols. The teacher prepares two sets of phonetic symbol cards and divides the entire class into two groups, giving each student one card. The teacher quickly reads out phonetic symbols, and the student holding the corresponding phonetic symbol card should quickly stand up. The first to stand gets two points, the slower ones get one point, and those who don't stand get zero points. The group with the most points wins.

26 Listen to Words and Find Vowel Symbols

The teacher prepares several sets of vowel symbol cards, then lets five students from each group come up. Each student is given one card (such as five long vowels or short vowels). The game starts, and the teacher says a word, such as cake, then the students holding the card [ei] in each group should immediately stand up and raise their card. Fast and correct responses earn 2 points, slower ones earn 1 point, and incorrect or no response earns no points. The group with the most points wins. Initially, this game can be played separately for long vowels, short vowels, or diphthongs, and later combined.

27 Listen to Sounds and Pick Phonetics

This is a game that trains students to listen and recognize phonetics. First, the teacher writes the phonetics they've learned on cards, making two sets and sticking them on the blackboard. Then divide the students into two groups. The game begins with the first student from each group waiting in front of the blackboard. The teacher says a phonetic symbol, and these two students should immediately pick the phonetic symbol card the teacher said and place it on the podium. Correct and fast picking earns 2 points, correct but slow earns 1 point, and incorrect earns no points. When the teacher says the first phonetic symbol, the second student from each group should come up and wait. After the first student picks the phonetic symbol, the teacher immediately says another phonetic symbol, and the game continues. The group with the most points wins.

28 Listen to Words and Find Phonetics

This game is similar to game (27), but instead of phonetic symbols, the teacher says words on the blackboard, and the students should pick the phonetic symbol contained in the word.

29 Bingo (Bingo)

The gameplay of this game is the same as game (7). Each student prepares a piece of paper and draws a tic-tac-toe grid on it, dividing the paper into nine squares. Then the teacher randomly reads out nine learned phonetic symbols. As the students listen, they write the phonetic symbols in the squares, filling them in any square they choose. After the students have filled in their grids, the teacher mixes the order and reads out the nine phonetic symbols again. The students can then shout "Bingo" and raise their paper for the teacher to check. The first to shout "Bingo" and write correctly wins.

30 Guess Phonetics

Divide the entire class into several groups. The teacher shows the students several phonetic symbol cards (for example 10 cards), allowing them to read them. Then the teacher pulls out one card and places it behind their back, letting each group's students guess. Correct guesses earn the group 10 points. The teacher then shows another card and continues letting the students guess. After the first row of students from each group has guessed, the second row continues. Finally, the group with the highest score wins.

31 Pick Apples

The teacher first shows a hanging picture of an apple tree on the blackboard, with each apple having a word written on it, such as bed, desk, big, pig, etc., and shows several baskets, each labeled with a phonetic symbol, such as [e], [i], etc. Then select a few students to come to the front, pick the apples off the tree, and place the apples with the same vowel sound into the corresponding basket. For example, place the apples with bed, desk into the basket labeled with the phonetic symbol [e], and place the apples with big, pig into the basket labeled with the phonetic symbol [i]. The fastest and most accurate wins.

32 Find Friends

Divide the students into two groups. The teacher gives each student a word card (such as big), and in the other group, there is definitely one person holding a word card with the same vowel phoneme (such as pig). The game starts when the teacher says a phoneme, such as [i], and the two students holding the words with this phoneme (i.e., pig and big) should quickly stand up and read out these two words. The first to stand earns 2 points, the slower ones earn 1 point, and those who don't stand earn zero points. The group with the most points wins.

Word Games

33 Quick Read Words

This is a game that trains students to read and recognize words. The teacher divides the entire class into several groups and then shows some word cards or pictures one by one. The students raise their hands to quickly answer. The teacher lets the first hand-raising student read out the word and explain its meaning, or read out and spell the word on the picture. Correct reading, explanation, and spelling earns the group 10 points, and the group with the most points wins.

34 Guess the Word from Pictures

Compete by each vertical row as a group. The teacher first shows some word pictures and then collects them. Then pull out one picture and place it behind their back, letting the first student from each group take turns guessing. They can ask: "Is it a plane (bus, bike)?" Responses: "Yes, it is." or "No, it isn't." The group that guesses correctly earns 10 points, and then continue guessing. After the first row guesses, the second row continues. The group with the most points at the end wins. This game can also be led by a student who makes the others guess.

35 Write Words from Pictures

This is a game that allows students to review learned words. The teacher prepares 20 words drawn in simple pen sketches on a small blackboard or large white paper beforehand, not letting the students see them yet. Then divide the entire class into several groups of four based on their seating arrangement. The competition begins when the teacher hangs the small blackboard or white paper, allowing the students to look for one minute, then collects it. Give the students two minutes to write down the words they saw. The group that writes the most and correctly wins.

36 Same Initial Word Spelling Contest

Divide the entire class into several groups. The teacher says a letter (such as D), and the first student from the first group immediately stands up and says and spells three (can also be five or ten depending on the students' vocabulary size) words starting with the letter D, such as desk, dog, door, etc. Not being able to say or saying incorrectly results in deductions. After this student finishes, the teacher says another letter, and the second student from the second group says. Continue this way, and finally, the group with the most points wins. During this game, two groups' students can also take turns saying letters (such as the first student from the first group saying the letter, and the first student from the second group answering), thus making it a contest. Avoid saying Q, X, Z, etc.

37 Word Spelling Contest

Divide the entire class into several groups, and the contest happens between two groups. After the teacher announces the start of the game, the first student from the first group immediately says a word in Chinese (such as bicycle), and the second student from the first group should immediately say the word in English. Saying incorrectly or not being able to say quickly results in negative points. Finally, the group with the least deductions wins.

39 Same Vowel Word Spelling Contest

Divide the entire class into several groups, and the contest happens between two groups. The game begins when the teacher says a word (such as bike), and student A from the first group should immediately stand up and say a word containing the same vowel (i.e., the vowel [ai]), such as five, and explains its meaning and spells it out. Not being able to say, explaining incorrectly, or spelling incorrectly results in deductions. After this student finishes, the teacher says another word, and student A from the second group stands up and says. Continue this way, and finally, the group with the most points wins. During this game, two groups' students can also take turns saying words (such as student A from the first group saying a word, and student A from the second group answering), thus making it a contest.

40 Word Spelling Relay Contest

Divide the entire class into two groups. The teacher gives each student a letter card, and less commonly used letters (such as Q, Z) can be held by multiple students. The game begins when the teacher says a word, such as ship, or shows a picture of a ship. The students holding S, H, I, P letters should immediately stand in front of the podium in order. The group that lines up correctly and fastest wins.

41 Word Chain

Divide the entire class into several groups. Each group sends one student to write a word starting with a specific letter on the blackboard. The last letter of the previous word becomes the first letter of the next word. Within the specified time, the group that connects the most words wins. For example: pen-nice-eight-tea-an-no-or-right-teacher-radio-on-nor, etc.

42 Guess the Bagged Item

The teacher takes something out of a bag and puts it into an opaque bag. The first student from each group takes turns guessing. They can ask: "Is it a banana (an apple, orange)?" The group that guesses correctly wins.

43 Treasure Hunt

The teacher prepares some word cards (some with Chinese, some with English words) and pictures. Place the cards and pictures into an opaque bag. The game begins when the teacher says the bag contains many treasures, inviting students to take turns grabbing treasures. If they draw an English card, they translate it into Chinese; if it's Chinese, they translate it into English and spell it out; if it's a picture, they describe it in English.

44 Guess the Picture, Everyone Answers

The teacher first shows some word pictures and then collects them. Invite one student to the front to guess. The guessing student faces the whole class, and invite another student to stand behind them, holding a picture high. The guessing student can ask the whole class: "Is it a plane (ship, bike)?" The whole class answers: "Yes." or "No." After guessing correctly, replace the guessing student with another one.

45 Guess the Color

The teacher first prepares some word pictures, such as a white plane, a red car, black shoes, green clothes, etc. The game begins by inviting one student to the front to guess. The guessing student faces the whole class, and invite another student to stand behind them, holding a picture high and saying: "This is a plane (car). What color is it? Please guess." The guessing student can ask the whole class: "Is it red (black)?" The whole class answers: "Yes." or "No." After guessing correctly, replace the guessing student with another one.

46 What's Missing?

This is a game that trains students' memory. The teacher shows some objects on the podium, letting the guessing student look for half a minute, then turn their back to the podium facing the students. Then let another student come up and take away one item. Then say: "Please guess. What is missing?" The guessing student must say the missing item in English within 10 seconds.

47 Pass the Word

Each row forms a group, dividing the entire class into several groups. The teacher gives the last student in each group a piece of paper with a word written on it. After the teacher says "start," the last student whispers the word on the paper to the student in front, and this student whispers the word to the next student... This continues down the line. Finally, the first student writes the transmitted word on the blackboard. The fastest and most accurate group wins.

48 Find Pairs

The teacher sticks 20 word pictures on the blackboard and places 20 corresponding word cards face down on the podium. Then let the two competing groups' students come up one by one to draw cards. After drawing a card, they must show it to the students, then remove the corresponding picture from the blackboard and read out the word. Correctly finding and reading earns 2 points, correctly finding but misreading earns 1 point, and finding incorrectly earns no points. After all cards are drawn, the group with the most points wins.

49 Touch Your Nose

This is a game that trains students to listen to words and react quickly. After learning words like nose, ear, eye, leg, hand, arm, finger, etc., the teacher can quickly say these words, and the students touch the corresponding part upon hearing the command. The fastest and most accurate wins. When students become very familiar, the difficulty can be increased by requiring them not to touch a certain part when hearing a specific word. For example, if the teacher says "nose," the students cannot touch their nose but can touch their eyes, ears, mouth, etc. This game can be played competitively, with one student from each group coming to the front to act. Those who make mistakes are eliminated, and the last remaining one or two win.

50 Listen to Words and Act

This game is similar to "Touch Your Nose." After learning verbs and verb phrases like run, walk, sit, stand, swim, skate, play football, play basketball, etc., the teacher can quickly say these verbs or phrases, and the students act accordingly. The fastest and most accurate wins. This game can also be played competitively, with one student from each group coming to the front to act. Those who make mistakes are eliminated, and the last remaining one or two win.
