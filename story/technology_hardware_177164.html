The hospital version of the queue system and the lobby version are both referred to in the industry as On-Site Customer Management Systems, mainly providing on-site customer management, traffic management, and statistical functions for centralized service locations in public service domains. Depending on the specific service content and on-site conditions, the system is primarily divided into two major types: the Lobby Version On-Site Customer Management System and the Hospital Version On-Site Customer Management System.

From a structural perspective, the lobby version of the queue system is much simpler than the hospital version because the queue system in service halls generally does not have too much business interaction with specific service industries. For example, in bank queue systems, customers take a numbered ticket from the ticketing machine and then wait in the waiting area for the teller's call. The entire process does not interact with the bank’s business system, and the system's management of on-site customers is relatively simple. However, the hospital version of the queue system (such as clinic customer management, registration area customer management, pharmacy customer management) is different. Due to the special and complex nature of hospital business processes, which directly involve on-site customers, such as patients requesting priority, seeing a specific doctor, follow-up visits, or referrals, these require the system to have strong business processing capabilities. Many manufacturers, lacking sufficient capability to develop hospital-specific queue systems, simply use the lobby version of the queue system and apply it to hospitals. However, due to the characteristics of hospital business processes, which demand diverse functionalities from the queue system, if the system development unit lacks sufficient capability, issues such as unsuccessful HIS integration and media system call display not meeting hospital requirements may arise during project implementation. Ultimately, this leads to incomplete project construction, unsatisfactory results for the hospital, and project non-acceptance, causing irreparable losses for both the project party and the hospital.
