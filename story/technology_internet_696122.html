A Brief Discussion on Administrative Loyalty and Administrative Whistleblowing

by cxxzsty on 2012-03-07 20:49:00

**Keywords**: Administrative loyalty; Ethical dilemma; Administrative whistleblowing

**Abstract**: Administrative loyalty is a fundamental norm of administrative ethics and a moral quality that administrative subjects should possess. It is the basic requirement of a bureaucratic organization for individual bureaucrats and also serves as an institutional guarantee for the existence and normal operation of bureaucratic organizations. However, in real political practice, individual bureaucrats face severe loyalty dilemmas. The most effective way to resolve this ethical dilemma is arguably administrative whistleblowing.

In bureaucratic organizations, individual bureaucrats are required to obey organizational rules, fulfill their duties, and remain loyal to the organization. Yet, in actual political practice, when organizational interests conflict with public interests—especially in the face of serious rent-seeking and corrupt behavior within the organization—as a bureaucrat, should one be loyal to the organization or to the public? This represents an ethical dilemma currently under discussion. How to resolve this ethical dilemma has become a focal point of attention.

**I. The Connotation and Necessity of Administrative Loyalty**

Administrative loyalty refers to the obedience, responsibility, and reverence shown by administrative subjects toward superior power entities and higher-value entities. The starting point of loyal behavior is obedience: following the will and orders of superiors and the organization, acting according to the requirements and guidelines set by superiors and the organization, without defiance or evasion. Moreover, this obedience is not passive but sincere, involving wholeheartedly executing the instructions of superiors and the organization, striving to achieve the goals of superiors and the organization, and fully maintaining the authority of superiors and the organization.

As a behavioral norm in administration, loyalty is an inherent requirement of the administrative organizational system and governance model. The administrative organizational system is an orderly hierarchical structure formed through step-by-step authorization, creating a vertical power relationship between superiors and subordinates. The power of subordinates comes from the authorization of superiors, and their duty is to achieve the goals set by superiors. In this bureaucratic organizational structure, the relationship between superiors and subordinates is unidirectional and irreversible, with superiors holding a dominant position and subordinates occupying a subordinate role. Given the specific nature of administrative organizations and governance methods, it is inevitable that subordinates must obey superiors, and employees should respect leadership. From an organizational perspective, within administrative organizational relationships, administrative personnel exist as roles within the organization rather than as natural individuals or citizens. They are components of the organizational machine or tools for achieving organizational goals, lacking independent status and value. Of course, this does not deny the independent personality of administrative personnel as individuals or their rights as citizens. However, when they act as roles within the administrative organization to handle relationships with the organization and upper levels, they (whether male or female) can only obey and remain loyal to the organization, rather than emphasizing personal freedom and independence. Otherwise, the administrative organizational system would be unable to sustain itself, and administrative personnel would lose their value as administrative roles.

John Kenneth Galbraith once explained the reasons for individual obedience using his "twin-peak symmetry" theory of power effects, highlighting the importance of obedience in achieving administrative goals. The "twin-peak symmetry" theory posits that "[an organization can only gain external compliance when it secures internal compliance with its goals. The size and reliability of its external power depend on the degree of internal compliance]." [1] Therefore, individual obedience within the organizational system is a fundamental guarantee for the effective exercise of power, and the subordinate position of individuals is a decisive factor in the effect of power. A top-down hierarchical order is essential for the efficient operation of organizational power and necessary for the government to achieve its organizational goals. According to Galbraith's theory, "[the 'twin-peak symmetry' of internal and external power is the primary condition for an organization to make people comply with its goals]." [2] As individuals or subordinates, the basic responsibilities and tasks of administrative personnel are to execute the orders of their superiors, serving merely as parts of the overall organizational arrangement. "[Individuals must submit to the common goals of the organization, and this internal exercise of power enables the organization to assert its will externally. Internal factors play a critical role in exercising any organizational power.] [3] Within the collaborative system of the organization, the basic role of individuals can only be one of obedience and loyalty.
