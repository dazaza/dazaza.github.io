At 20, you compete on physical strength; at 30, you compete on expertise; at 40, you compete on your network.

by dang000 on 2009-07-31 15:01:44

As the saying goes: in your teens, you compare intelligence; in your twenties, physical strength; in your thirties, expertise; and at forty, social connections... I don't know what to compare when you're older.

First, establish your value.

Here, "value" can be more aptly described as "the value of being utilized." Before tallying up your network of relationships, calmly ask yourself: are you useful to others? If you cannot be utilized by others, it means you do not possess value (for example, career planning is nothing more than enhancing your "value of being employed"). The more useful you are, the easier it is for you to build strong social connections.

Think about Mr. Yu's famous saying? Few people can establish genuine social connections with those whose status is vastly different from their own. Therefore, even if I were lucky enough to connect with Li Ka-shing through Wealink today, he would not be interested in me (it would be more valuable to know the VP or CEO of Cheung Kong Holdings or Hutchison Whampoa). Just like building a brand, instead of hastily spending energy making friends aimlessly, one should first determine their own value positioning and then target specific audiences for targeted communication.

At each stage of life, depending on one's abilities and goals, there are different value positions. When you're still a college student, your value might lie in having excellent grades, or being particularly good at soccer, or perhaps being handsome, making people feel honored to be with you. If all else fails, you can have great stamina and enthusiasm, willing to run errands; the worst-case scenario (but not necessarily a bad thing) is being rich and always happy to pick up the tab... After you start working, maybe you're a computer expert, or a branding specialist, always ready to offer advice, or perhaps you know many media professionals and are skilled at using your pen to solve problems for others, or you have extensive experience in production and manufacturing. Of course, if you have a lot of money, congratulations, you're at the top of the food chain, as capital is the most valuable.

Second, convey your value to others.
