Transcript of Microsoft's conference call on Nokia phone business acquisition deal

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 15:53:51

Microsoft recently announced that it will acquire Nokia's mobile phone business for $7.2 billion, along with a large number of patent portfolio licenses. Subsequently, both companies held a telephone conference at 8:45 AM local time on September 3 (8:45 PM Beijing Time on September 3) to detail the transaction content and accept questions from analysts and media. Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer, CFO Amy Wood, Executive Vice President of the Operating Systems Division Terry Myerson, and Nokia Executive Vice President of Devices and Mobile Steven Elop attended this call.

Below is a transcript of the Q&A portion of the call:

A series of new tablet computers will be launched during the Christmas shopping season

Q: Regarding tablets. How does this acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business by Microsoft influence the strategy formulation for its future tablet computer business? Will Microsoft further increase its efforts in proprietary hardware in the tablet market?

Myerson: We need to continue with both mobile phones and tablet computers. We see that the screen sizes of mobile phone products are continuously increasing, and the user experience of our mobile phone products can now also be realized on Windows tablets. Our application platform connects mobile phones and tablet computers. I think that our consumers expect us to launch an excellent tablet product with a mobile phone experience, and our strategy will continue to evolve in this direction.

Ballmer: As you know, we do indeed have our own tablet products. We see opportunities and will continue to work in this area. In this field, we also have very close project cooperation with OEMs, especially in basic products such as Intel Atom processors. I believe people will benefit from this. A series of new products will be launched during the Christmas shopping season.

Affordable phone products are highly strategic

Q: Since Nokia has a strong low-end feature phone business, what space does Windows have for expansion? Microsoft has already reduced the specifications of Windows phones to allow Windows to run on affordable devices. Does Microsoft have plans to further expand into the vast affordable phone market?

Myerson: Our primary goal is to enable as many global users as possible to use Windows. From the perspective of launching the next new Windows phone, affordable phone products are highly strategic, but currently we won't reveal too much.

Q: What impact does this transaction have on Microsoft's restructuring? Given that hardware and software are two different fields, which department will Nokia's mobile phone business be merged into, the hardware department or the hardware/content device department?

Ballmer: The restructuring plan remains unchanged. The hardware department has a larger scale and new opportunities, and Julie Larson-Green is fully committed to running it. We have a lot of work to do in the coming months, and Julie and her team will be responsible for planning and integration. Julie is very excited about taking charge of the device side. However, most of the acquired groups are in the mobile phone sector, and Elop will be responsible for operating this part and will take appropriate measures. Julie will work with Elop to find a suitable integration solution.

Q: What expectations do you have for the proportion of Windows Phone among OEMs in the future?

Ballmer: Currently, 80% of Nokia's products are Windows Phones. In the short term, I don't think there will be significant changes. But as the market grows, the proportion of Windows Phones among OEMs will increase. It's still too early to make predictions now.

Better operation as a single company

Q: Regarding cost rationalization, Nokia has 32,000 employees, while Microsoft has 99,000 employees. What plans does Microsoft have in this regard?

Ballmer: I want to emphasize that many hardware manufacturers usually outsource production, but Nokia is internalized. Nokia has a relevant strategy for this, and they have executed it very well.

Wood: Among the 32,000 employees, about 18,000 are directly involved in manufacturing. I think Nokia's scale and opportunities do not lie here. In terms of integration, I think Ballmer and Elop have performed excellently, after careful consideration, and have been very cautious in rationalization, so that we can become a brand and team with good execution ability and efficiency.

Q: Management talked about partnerships and launching excellent products. So, through this deal, what can Microsoft achieve that partners cannot? Can you give an example? Or is the focus actually strengthening Microsoft's position in the smartphone sector? Is it to ensure Microsoft's long-term development in the smartphone market?

Ballmer: The latter question is completely correct. We believe that it is better to operate as one company rather than two, for three reasons: First, we mentioned one brand and a unified way of entering the market. I think we can do better than names like Nokia Lumia Windows Phone 1020. Because Nokia and Microsoft are completely different businesses, we are powerless against such brand names. Through this transaction, we can simplify our cooperation model with carriers and make consumer brand information more concise.

In terms of innovation, we have done a lot of work, but as two independent companies, there will still be some different perspectives on innovation. The Lumia 1020 excels in photography and imaging, but I think if we were one company, we would invest more in this area, developing better software and applications, and increasing investment in core hardware platforms.

Third, I think our business becomes flexible. As two companies, we are in different countries, with different decision-makers making investment decisions that are contrary in price and goals, which, in my view, reduces operational efficiency and makes the business lack flexibility. In summary, I believe we can achieve remarkable progress.

Promoting an increase in the number of applications

Q: Achieving a 15% market share within the next few years is a very aggressive target. To achieve this goal, developers' support is needed, and developers already have iOS and Android platforms. Microsoft has always said that HTML 5 will grow in the next few years. How will Microsoft convince developers to develop applications for Windows Phone?

Myerson: For developers, Windows provides a large number of installation platforms, from PCs to mobile phones to tablets, and even Xbox One. The HTML 5 applications or native applications developed by developers can be installed on all these devices, allowing developers to reach more users, such as enterprise users and gaming console users, providing developers with a unique platform for commercialization.

Ballmer: One of the keys is promoting an increase in the number of applications. We have differentiated products. Now, the number of applications in our app store has reached 160,000. However, I know we still have a long way to go. The key is to launch our own applications and hardware, encouraging consumers to purchase them, driving sales volume growth, and then attracting more developers to this ecosystem. The characteristic of HTML 5 is neutrality, and I hope all platforms can be compatible.
