Hackers' attack target shifts from Windows to Android

by anonymous on 2013-08-20 14:49:19

As a big player in the mobile field, Android is increasingly becoming a target for hackers, just like Windows in the PC market. Interestingly, the methods hackers use to attack Android are similar to those used for Windows malware.

According to a report by the American tech blog TechHive, a new study shows that the functions of malware targeting the market-leading Android platform are very similar to the Trojan programs that have been making money off Windows PC users for years.

In the latest report, the "Security Threat Evolution Report" released on Thursday, Kaspersky Lab claims that Android, with nearly 80% of the mobile market share, is equivalent to Windows in the PC market, holding an absolute leadership position.

The difference between Windows and Android malware lies in the fact that the latter evolves much faster, as criminals have gained extensive experience from attacking PCs. "The development speed of Android malware is much faster than that of Windows malware," said Roel Schouwenberg, senior researcher at Kaspersky Lab.

Android malware resembles Windows programs more

To date, the pinnacle of Android malware development is Backdoor.AndroidOS.Obad.a, which Kaspersky called the most advanced mobile Trojan to date in June. Its functions include opening a backdoor for downloading files, stealing phone and app information, sending text messages to premium-rate numbers, and spreading malware via Bluetooth.

Moreover, the Obad Trojan has reached new heights in using encryption or software encoding concealment techniques. In addition, it exploits three previously unknown vulnerabilities in Android. After studying the Obad Trojan, Kaspersky found that it looks more like Windows malware than typical Android programs.

Today, the vast majority of malware still primarily targets the more profitable Windows PCs. However, the trend in malware development indicates that a new generation of developers is trying to break into smartphones. In 2011, smartphone sales surpassed those of PCs. "That's the future they believe in," Schouwenberg said.

In the first half of this year, the number of malware samples collected by Kaspersky exceeded 100,000 for the first time. Throughout 2012, security providers collected about 76,000 malware samples. However, the infection rates of these malware are extremely low. For example, during three consecutive days in June, Kaspersky observed the performance of the Obad Trojan and found that its infection rate was only 0.15% of the total infections.

Increased channels for malware distribution

A partial reason for the low infection rate of this malware is the scarcity of its distribution channels. Today, most infections occur due to downloading apps from web-based app stores (rather than the official Google Play store) that contain malicious code.

Most third-party app stores are located in Asia and Russia. In the U.S., smartphone users tend to favor Google Play because it scans for malware.

As other distribution methods develop, the infection rate of mobile malware is expected to increase. Currently, hackers typically spread viruses through spam emails containing links to malicious websites, and the volume of spam is expected to rise.

In addition, Kaspersky has found that an increasing number of ads within mobile apps directly point to malicious sites. Moreover, many websites equipped with the BlackHole Exploit Kit can download malware onto users' phones when accessed via Android devices.

Another troubling trend is the emergence of mobile malware with backdoors. While most smartphone infections involve Trojans that send text messages to premium-rate numbers, Kaspersky has also discovered an increasing number of mobile malware with backdoors connected to command and control servers. "When the backdoors and Trojans start working, you realize how skilled these people are," Schouwenberg said.
