Problems in the Development of Black Peanuts

The problems in the development of black peanuts are provided by Jindi Agricultural Black Peanut Planting Base:

Currently, black food is very popular. Black peanuts are favored due to their rich content of protein, fat, unsaturated fatty acids, and trace elements such as selenium. In recent years, some agricultural production and sales departments, seed groups, and individual holders have also been actively promoting and recommending black peanuts, making them skyrocket in value for a time, even becoming fashionable gifts. Now, from Lianyungang in the east to Sichuan in the west, Hainan Island in the south to Inner Mongolia in the north, there are reports of successful research and development of new varieties of black peanuts across the country. Those who plant black peanuts indeed gain some benefits; there is a pioneer in Heishan County, Liaoning Province near the author's location. Many farmers are also eager to try, even willing to pay high prices for seeds. To guide farmers to correctly approach new things, we have introduced 14 varieties of black peanuts from all over the country since 2002, summarized corresponding cultivation techniques, and conducted various appraisals on the introduced varieties. It was found that there are still many problems with the currently planted black peanut varieties and during the production and sales process of black peanuts. Some problems even require various scientific research methods to be solved.

I. Variety-related problems

1. The color of black peanut seed coats is not easy to stabilize

The color of the seed coat is generally based on the color when peeling after harvest and drying. After a period of storage, the color of the seed coat will change. Therefore, observing the color of the seed coat should be done simultaneously with seed examination. Among the 14 introduced varieties, the seed coat color of Hunan black peanuts is relatively ideal. About 70% of the seeds in the current planting generation are dark purple, while 30% are brown-red, light purple, and gray-purple colors. In the second year, dark purple and brown-red seeds were planted separately, and the offspring of dark purple seeds still showed a 7:3 ratio separation. And about 60% of the offspring of brown-red seeds were dark purple, with the rest being mixed colors ranging from brown to gray-purple. Continuing to plant according to this did not improve the situation. Therefore, it is difficult to obtain pure black or entirely dark purple seed coat varieties simply by purifying existing varieties.

2. Black peanut seeds are uneven

Among the more than ten introduced black peanut varieties, they mostly belong to large-seed ordinary varieties. The seed shape is elliptical or conical. The characteristic is scattered pod formation, with poor uniformity of pods and seeds. Multiple random samples were taken, with an average hundred-pod weight of 140~165g and hundred-seed weight of 65~80g. After selecting large seeds, the hundred-seed weight can reach 130g, while the hundred-seed weight of small seeds of the same variety is only about 25g, and there are many shriveled seeds. The proportion of large, medium, and small seeds is approximately 25:55:20. By improving cultivation techniques, such as mulching, foliar fertilization, and removing late flowers, the uniformity of seeds has improved somewhat, but the improvement is not significant.

3. Black peanuts aren't "black"

Among the 14 black peanut varieties, no truly pure black peanuts were found. The best color performance is the Hunan black peanut series, where the majority of the seed coat color is dark purple, but there are still parts that are brown-red. As for Liaoning and other places' black peanut varieties, only 50%~60% of the seed coats are dark purple, with quite a portion being light red or pale purple. There is also a large difference in the color of the seed coats between different pods on the same plant. After several generations of selective planting, the results have not changed much. Therefore, what is currently referred to as black peanuts can only be said to be a vague term for all deep-colored peanuts, including purple, dark purple, brown-red, and light purple.

The uniqueness of black peanuts mainly lies in their higher zinc and selenium content compared to regular peanuts. The selenium content is positively correlated with the depth of the seed coat color; the darker the seed coat, the higher the selenium content. Therefore, solving the problem of black peanuts not being black is also a key issue in improving the quality and variety value of black peanuts.

4. Large differences in black peanut plant types and pod types

Plant morphology and pod type are two important markers for defining varieties. Pods are generally divided into common type (including axe-shaped), gourd type, string-of-pearls type, and hockey-stick type. Among the 14 introduced varieties, only a few varieties from Zhongyou, Shandong, and Jinzhou have relatively neat pods, but about 10% are other types of pods. Some varieties almost contain all types of peanut pods when harvested. Investigating the plant morphology, most of the 14 varieties belong to ordinary creeping or semi-creeping types with many lateral branches, some even having third-order lateral branches, with strong growth momentum. However, upright types also account for a certain proportion, and some even exhibit wild peanut plant types and leaf shapes. Taking Shandong black peanuts as an example, the impurity rate in 2003 was 7.5%, in 2004 it was 7.2%, and in 2005 it was 8.6%, making it difficult to eliminate impurities. The impurity rate of other varieties reached up to 12.5%. The color separation of stems and leaves is also significant, with green, dark green, purple, and red. Flower colors include yellow, orange-red, and light yellow separations. Therefore, these black peanut varieties cannot yet be considered very mature and stable varieties.

II. Reflections on black peanut varieties

1. Positioning the inheritance of black peanut seed coat color

Black foods are popular mainly because they contain trace elements such as zinc and selenium that are beneficial to human health. The content of these trace elements mainly depends on the depth of the product's color. Therefore, the positioning of black peanut seed coat color should ultimately aim for black. According to data, peanut seed coat color is controlled by three pairs of genes, which may be linked. Therefore, improving the existing black peanuts so they become truly black requires detailed genetic trait research and practice, using advanced breeding techniques including biotechnology to gradually make the seed coat color homozygous, and breeding truly "black" peanuts. Relying solely on simple natural selection methods is unlikely to achieve the goal.

2. Positioning the plant type and seed uniformity of black peanuts

Existing black peanut varieties mostly belong to large-seed ordinary types with longer growth periods. Due to the lack of uniformity in plant type and seeds, it brings great difficulty to the production and sales of black peanuts. To meet export needs, Northern spring-sown peanuts are mainly short-growth-period pearl bean types, which is the fundamental reason why the BaiSha 1016 series varieties remain popular. Given the continuous expansion of domestic and international black peanut markets, the breeding goals for black peanut varieties should be positioned as upright and compact plants, continuous flowering with concentrated flowering periods, round or peach-shaped seeds, smooth seed coats, and a growth period of around 120 days. Especially in drought resistance, disease resistance, and other stress-resistant properties, they should be superior to existing varieties.

3. Positioning the dormancy of black peanut seeds

The length and strength of peanut seed dormancy directly affect peanut production management, storage and transportation, and product quality. Too long dormancy results in slow seed germination, slow seedling growth, weak growth momentum, and susceptibility to pests and diseases during the seedling stage. Short dormancy, if not harvested in time, will cause sprouting on the plant, affecting yield and directly lowering product quality. Therefore, the dormancy period should be positioned between 50 to 70 days, too long or too short will affect the entire process of peanut industrialized production.
