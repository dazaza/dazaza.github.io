Samsung's curved screen phone may be different from what you think.

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:14:52

The evolution of new technologies always captivates the imagination, leading people to desire their immediate application as soon as they are unveiled, often without considering the long journey from technology development to commercialization.

Flexible screens have recently become a hot topic. With Samsung's announcement of a forthcoming curved screen smartphone, it seems like the next revolution in mobile phones is just around the corner. Some people overreact, believing that flexible screens will be shatterproof, high-strength, and capable of free-form changes. However, with current technology levels, commercially viable products can only achieve fixed-curved screens rather than ones that change freely. Thus, calling them "curved screens" is more accurate than "flexible screens."

In fact, such products have already appeared on Android devices. Samsung’s Nexus S and Galaxy Nexus both feature slight screen curves, which might go unnoticed due to their subtlety. Additionally, in smart TVs, many curved screens have been applied, marketed under the premise of enhancing visual experience.

Looking at Samsung's past innovative products, such as the Galaxy Note and Galaxy Zoom, Samsung has primarily focused on leveraging technological advantages. Therefore, this curved screen will likely also highlight certain technical features, such as larger-angle curved screens designed to better fit the palm or face, or side displays for notifications as shown in mature product solutions presented at CES.

Nonetheless, achieving screens that can freely change curvature is still far off. Current perceptions of curved screens generally fall into two categories: one being the fixed-curvature screens mentioned earlier, and the other being the idealized flexible screens envisioned by many—screens that can change curvature and are shatterproof. Companies like Samsung, LG, and E Ink are researching such products and have demonstrated prototype models. These products are lightweight and sufficiently strong (though not immune to heavy impacts), making them durable enough to withstand falls.

However, in practical applications, achieving screens that can freely change curvature is almost impossible. This is because, aside from the screen itself, other components such as the frame, internal motherboard, battery, and back cover cannot bend freely. As a result, when manufacturers demonstrate flexible screens, they usually showcase the screen alone, connected to the motherboard via a few wires—a setup that wouldn't work in an actual product.

Phones using E Ink flexible screens

Freely bendable phones, tablets, and displays remain largely wishful thinking on the part of consumers and are unlikely to be realized in the near future.

Moreover, when manufacturers develop screens, their goal isn't necessarily to create products that constantly bend but to focus on thinness. Flexible screens use plastic or fibers instead of glass, significantly reducing weight. For example, E Ink's Mobius flexible screen uses TFT technology but weighs 50% less than traditional TFT glass screens and can be as thin as 0.8mm.

Flexible screens may have a promising future in a new field—wearable devices, such as smartwatches and skin patches. However, the ultimate goal remains centered on achieving thinness rather than free bending.
