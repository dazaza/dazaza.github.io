Overview of rhinoplasty

by 865512 on 2008-12-19 10:06:17

Rhinoplasty is one of the most common procedures in cosmetic and plastic surgery. The shape of the nose plays a very important role in the overall appearance of the face. Over the years, from observing a large number of patients who have undergone the procedure, it has become clear that the key to a successful rhinoplasty lies in the preparation of the nasal implant model. If the model is well-designed and crafted, it can be like adding the finishing touch to a masterpiece, giving the patient a harmonious and natural beauty. On the other hand, if the design is inappropriate and the craftsmanship poor, it can resemble an unnecessary addition, appearing clumsy and unnatural, making it immediately obvious that the person has had cosmetic surgery.

The preparation of the nasal implant model is a crucial step. Surgeons must take into account the specific condition of the patient’s nose as well as other facial features around the nose: such as the distance between the inner corners of the eyes, the condition of the cheekbones and forehead, the size and shape of the face, etc. For example, there was a case involving a female patient with a round face. Aside from a slightly low nasal bridge, her other features were relatively well-proportioned, and she looked sweet and gentle when smiling. If the nose were augmented to create a straighter line, although the nose itself might look better than before, the overall effect would not be entirely harmonious. Additionally, the sweetness in her smile would be lost, replaced by a more serious expression. In cases like this, surgeons must proceed with caution, discussing the design of the nasal model with the patient beforehand to understand her requirements and aesthetic preferences. When necessary, computer imaging can be used to map out the patient's nasal profile, allowing for multiple design options to be considered and selected jointly by both the surgeon and the patient. Following this process, the model created often yields satisfactory results.

In addition to the aforementioned considerations in creating the nasal model, attention should also be paid to the fixation of the model, the angle and shape of its connection with the forehead, as well as the condition of the nostrils and nasal wings. If there is thickening or drooping of the nasal wings, they may also need to be reshaped to ensure overall harmony and proportionality of the nose. These are relatively common cases of low nasal bridges. Among the patients we have encountered with collapsed noses, there is also a more severe type, usually caused by disease or injury, such as syphilis, which can lead to perforation of the nasal septum, causing serious collapse of the nasal bridge and tip, resulting in a short-nose deformity where the tip is upturned. Moreover, the base of the nose and adjacent bones, i.e., the middle third of the face, present a concave dish-like depression. From the side view, the height of the nasal tip is lower than that of the upper lip. The surgery for such patients is more complex and requires hospitalization. It is not only necessary to elevate the nose and increase its length but also to pad the base of the nose and correct the concave deformities around it. The transplant materials used during the surgery can only be autologous cartilage or bone, which is shaped and then implanted in the nasal bridge, tip, and the surrounding concave areas of the middle third of the face. Only through this method can satisfactory results be achieved.
