Natural World: A Highland Haven This stunningly beautiful film reveals the unique wildlife of the Scottish Highlands, seen through the eyes of filmmaker Fergus Beeley. Based for a year at Loch Maree and the surrounding hills in Scotland's far North West, Beeley presents his personal view of the shy animals whose lives are ruled by the rains. He follows the fortunes of rare black-throated divers and white-tailed sea eagles, which both breed there, while capturing the red deer and salmon whose lives also revolve around the loch. With an evocative score provided by local musician Phil Cunningham, this enchanting film captures the magic of a very special place.

Oliver Sacks is a neurologist and writer, his representative work "Awakening" was published in 1973, telling the story of how Sacks used a new drug to wake up patients who had been comatose for decades, and it was later filmed in 1990, starring Robin Williams and Robert De Niro, nominated for the Oscar. Throughout his career, Sacks has encountered many weak patients. Although the symptoms of these patients were different, they had one thing in common: music therapy. Nova Musical Minds documented four cases (two of which were Sacks' cases, even using MRI to scan Sacks' brain), exploring the mysterious and inexplicable effects of music on the brain.

In Sacks' new book "Musicophilia", he tells us that anatomists cannot easily distinguish the brains of painters, writers or mathematicians, but can immediately identify the brains of musicians, as the areas responsible for movement, hearing, and vision in the cerebellum of musicians, along with the corpus callosum connecting both sides of the brain, are all larger than those of ordinary people. Compared to language, music affects more parts of the brain. It is obvious that humans are musical animals. Can music tell us about the functions of the brain? And can the brain tell us about the functions of music? Finally, can music help those with severe neurological disorders? The Nova team, together with BBC reporter Alan, visited cases in places like New York, the UK, and Ireland, just like the people described in Sacks' "Musicophilia", telling their private yet extraordinary stories.

Of the four stories, three involve neurological disorders: Tourette Syndrome (Tourette's Disorder, Multiple Tic Disorder, Tic Disorders with Coprolalia), autism, and amusia. Amusia is like color blindness to colors, unable to distinguish music. The last story's protagonist is a surgery doctor who loves rock music. After being struck by lightning while on the phone, he became obsessed with classical music. Apart from the amusic patient, the other three have, to a certain extent, miraculously developed their brains into musical geniuses, which is unimaginable.

Nova takes a first look at the world-famous neurologist's brain on television. Researchers used MRI to record Sacks' brain activity, including the brain activity generated when Sacks listened to music and thought about music, as well as the brain activity when listening to music by different composers (Bach and Beethoven). With Sacks' help, "Musical Minds" documents how those suffering from neurological disorders successfully cope with and transcend life. As Sacks said, music brings vitality back to life.

On the morning of December 26, 2004, a section of the Earth's crust beneath the sea slipped along a 700-mile-long fault off the coast of Sumatra. One of the worst natural disasters on record, the ensuing earthquake triggered a series of destructive waves or tsunamis that claimed more than 250,000 lives. Barely a minute after the quake, computers at the Pacific Tsunami Warning Center in Hawaii picked up seismic signals and automatically alerted scientists. They issued an emergency bulletin for the Pacific but watched helplessly as the tragedy unfolded, since no similar detection network exists in the Indian Ocean. Fifteen minutes after the quake, a colossal wall of water struck the northwest coast of Sumatra, surging miles inland and obliterating everything in its path. Over the next few hours, gigantic waves raced across the Indian Ocean, killing tens of thousands of people in Thailand, Sri Lanka, and India. Now NOVA tells the minute-by-minute story of the 2004 tsunami, featuring the testimony of lucky survivors and gripping eyewitness video footage. In an authoritative look at tsunami science, NOVA presents the first in-depth exploration of what made the waves so destructive. And with the help of a massive wave tank, scientists assess the risk that a similar or even bigger catastrophe might one day strike U.S. coastlines.
