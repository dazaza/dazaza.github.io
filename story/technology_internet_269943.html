Some small insights on stock trading

by gpgp6486 on 2009-11-22 13:32:29

Some small insights on stock trading: Grasping the direction of the market: Even the best individual stocks will not completely deviate from the long-term operation of the overall market. Focusing on individual stocks rather than the overall market is just a behavior for short-term traders. To study the trend of the market, one needs to look at macroeconomic factors, monetary policy, interest rates, exchange rates, politics and other fundamentals, as well as technical analysis trends. For mid-term research on market direction, the main focus should be on the 30-day moving average line. Although the 30-day moving average line lags behind the market index, every time it flattens out, it's a sign of a change in the intermediate trend. The development of the market generally goes in the opposite direction of the previous stage, and the stock index is very likely to turn into a longer-term mid-term trend. In this sense, as a stock trader, whether successful or not in the past, one should spend more time studying wave theory when free.

Stock selection: Choosing stocks is like choosing a spouse, looking at the weekly chart. Stock selection is the most important and primary work in stock trading. What stocks to buy depends on the fundamental analysis, and one should be familiar with all aspects of the company's operations. If you can predict the performance trend of the next quarter or half year to a year, that would be ideal. Human energy is limited, and good stocks keep coming up, so listing all the potential stocks of listed companies is unrealistic. Therefore, the number of selected stocks should not be too many, generally around ten carefully selected ones are enough. Stock selection principles: high performance, small cap, avoid poor-performing stocks and rarely touch large-cap stocks. High-performance stocks have low risk and high returns, they rise faster than the market and fall slower than the market, even if unfortunately trapped, the opportunity to recover capital is far greater than other stocks; small-cap stocks have strong growth potential, active stock nature, and more opportunities. Choose industry leaders and product monopolies, choose stocks with independent innovation and autonomous pricing ability, and choose stocks with policy and theme attention.

Buying and selling timing: The timing of buying and selling should be based on technical analysis. Technical analysis is the basic skill of stock trading, and spending time studying it can yield twice the result with half the effort. The stock market has certain regularities, and moving averages also have a certain inertia. Generally speaking, when the short-term moving average crosses above the long-term moving average, it's a buying point, and vice versa it's a selling point. When the moving average just turns back, the opportunity to buy and sell stocks is always greater than the risk. The commonly said "buying high and selling low" if done rationally, why not? The buying and selling timing definitely won't be the lowest or highest price of the stock. From a time perspective, this point has the characteristic of being slower than the stock price, and from a sensitivity perspective, choosing whether the 5-day and 10-day moving averages cross each other is relatively reasonable. In practical combat, people tend to hold onto stocks when the price drops from a high, and become timid when the price rises from a low. Knowing that the technical analysis has changed but still unable to take action, therefore, watching the moving average should avoid being indecisive. If one can consistently adhere to the principle that the crossing of moving averages is the buying and selling point, the opportunity will definitely be greater than the risk.

Position size: No matter how good an expert is, it's impossible to fully predict the bottom and top of the market, so don't fantasize about buying or selling stocks at the lowest or highest price. What can be done is to adjust your position size based on the highs and lows of the market and stock prices to control risks and returns. Gradually increasing your position when prices are low and gradually reducing your position when prices are high is the only way. Thus, for individual stocks, although you're not buying at the lowest point and selling at the highest point, the average price won't be bought too high or sold too low, always keeping most of the profits inside; for total returns, assets may not be maximized, but risks won't be maximized either, and absolute returns are still quite substantial. If the position is well controlled, even if you don't get the meat, you definitely won't starve or drink clear soup, and you'll still have enough rice to eat.

Types and combinations: Don't put all your eggs in one basket, this is another principle of controlling risk. Different stages of the stock market should have different combinations of varieties. A combination of high-quality blue-chip stable types (white horse stocks), high-risk high-return types with certain themes (black horse stocks), and small-cap rapidly growing types (colorful stocks) should be invested in, and the proportion between them should be adjusted accordingly according to market changes. Generally speaking, stable types have a long-lasting trend and should not be easily swapped out; high-risk high-return types come and go quickly, and once technical indicators change, do not linger; patiently pay attention to colorful stocks, knowing that gold will always shine.

Mindset: Everyone has the idea of entering at the lowest point and exiting at the highest point to make the most money, precisely this mindset blurs our judgment and makes us lose initiative in the stock market. Always remember the following sentence: When building positions, spend a little more money to actively buy; when unloading, earn a little less money to actively sell - always maintain the initiative. Stock trading requires patience, sometimes doing something different from the norm may allow you to win money from others' pockets. If your market judgment is wrong, admitting and correcting it makes you a good comrade, but denying it after making a mistake is compounding the error.
