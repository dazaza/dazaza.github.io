What to do for hemorrohoidal bleeding: Experts from the Nanjing Armed Police Force Hospital's Anorectal Center introduce that a perianal abscess refers to an acute suppurative infection occurring in the soft tissues around the anal canal and rectum or their surrounding spaces, which forms an abscess known as an anal and rectal abscess. The characteristics of perianal abscesses are spontaneous rupture or the formation of an anal fistula after surgical incision and drainage. Perianal abscesses are common anorectal diseases and also represent symptoms of internal hemorrhoids during the acute phase of inflammatory pathological processes of the anus and rectum, while anal fistulas occur during the chronic phase.

Harms of not timely treating perianal abscesses:

1. Harm of perianal abscesses: If not treated in time, low-positioned abscess infections can worsen and spread, transforming into high-positioned abscesses.

2. Harm of perianal abscesses: Delayed treatment can lead to the expansion of the abscess range and increased number of locations, causing greater pain to patients and increasing the difficulty of treatment.

3. Harm of perianal abscesses: Untimely or inappropriate treatment easily forms anal fistulas or anal fissures, bringing additional surgical pain to patients.

4. Harm of perianal abscesses: Abscess diffusion, bacteria entering the bloodstream, producing toxins, leading to sepsis, pyemia, and subsequently forming infectious shock.

Once deep perianal abscesses form, the internal pressure gradually increases. At this point, early surgery is recommended, not only to facilitate earlier healing of the abscess cavity and prevent its spread but also to avoid the formation of anal fistulas. Even if an anal fistula does form, it will be easier to treat. Experts from the Nanjing Armed Police Force Hospital's Anorectal Center point out that if the abscess misses the opportunity for early surgical treatment, the infection focus is very likely to shift to nearby gaps, forming a larger abscess. The pus can spread subcutaneously to the ischioanal fossa, from the affected side through tissue gaps to the opposite side, then enter the anus through the internal and external sphincters, upward breaking through the levator ani muscle to enter the pelvic cavity, forming peritonitis; or eroding adjacent tissues such as the rectum, bladder, vagina, urethra, etc., resulting in various types of complex anal fistulas. Therefore, after the formation of a perianal abscess, if early incision treatment is not performed in time, it not only increases invisible pain but also poses serious harm to the human body.
