An Lin coexistence? There has never been a positive reaction in NBA history between a Point Guard (PG), Small Forward (SF), and Power Forward (PF) where each player's offense far exceeds their defense. Setting aside the halo of the "Lin-sanity," a large part of the Knicks' recent strength should be attributed to Tyson Chandler, Josh Harrellson, Landry Fields, and partially Iman Shumpert. They are like the defensive unit of an American football team, quietly doing everything for their star quarterback and running back. Since the Suns era, D'Antoni's teams have never played such united and intelligent defense. However, unavoidable conflicts still exist: D'Antoni's system does not need a super side-winger, or rather, players with complex roles at the 2 and 3 positions are hard to fit into any team primarily based on pick-and-roll plays by the point guard.

Hall-of-fame level point guards do not need Jordan, Kobe, or James just as the latter would never actively recruit the former. Joe Johnson's departure did not hinder the Suns from reaching their peak in 07, and those who accompanied John Stockton, Gary Payton, Jason Kidd, and Steve Nash during their glorious times were Dennis Rodman, Nate McMillan, Sam Perkins, and Brent Barry. The offensive choices of Jeff Hornacek were as restrained and self-aware as Ray Allen’s during his Celtics era, while the Dallas duo of Vince Carter and Shawn Marion led the 2 and 3 positions to the extreme principle of reducing the ball control burden on the point guard. During its prime, the Kings team did not purely rely on the point guard to lead the offense but also adopted a strategy of functionalizing the wings. Peja Stojakovic, Doug Christie, and Hedo Turkoglu did not handle the ball much; the offense was orchestrated by the inside-out coordination among the 1, 4, and 5 positions. In the Timberwolves, Adelman's promotion of Ricky Rubio to the starting lineup meant that the best one-on-one player, Michael Beasley, became a substitute.

Even before Carmelo Anthony's return, there had been rounds of discussions in the U.S. media and public circles about whether An Lin could coexist. The world was anxiously waiting to see if the return of a superstar would mess up a team, a height even Arenas needed to look up to. SI commentator Thompson represented the most optimistic view, saying not to worry too much, Paul Pierce at 27 also achieved nothing amidst a flood of bad reviews, and the style-similar Melo is standing at the same crossroads as Pierce eight years ago. He will change himself for higher goals, giving up the ball. But Thompson obviously ignored many details. Jeremy Lin, Amar'e Stoudemire, Kevin Garnett, and Ray Allen are completely different teammates, D'Antoni and Doc Rivers are completely different coaches, and New York and Boston have completely different team cultures. Therefore, I more agree with Yahoo's Mr. Wang's view: For the Knicks to play well, Carmelo must understand that this is a team led by Jeremy. This may sound vague, but it is not. It is not a similar dog-blood topic as "who deserves to pass the ball to Yao/Yi." Discussing whether Lin and Anthony can coexist is not premised on Lin being arrogant. Essentially, it is a question of whether New York has already found the right model. Here, Lin can simply be replaced by "a point guard who can execute D'Antoni's tactical intentions."

Regardless of whether that person is Lin, a considerate point guard is a necessity for D'Antoni's system. Before Felton left and Lin appeared, you couldn't see a Knicks team bearing D'Antoni's mark. Chauncey Billups doesn't care about this stuff deep down, acting as a cancerous tumor, exposing all the bad habits of point guards that D'Antoni hates—inefficient shooting, suppressing pace, ignoring the interior. Douglas is both selfish and stupid, Bibby now is a version of Jason Kidd without defense, passing, or three-point shooting, and Shumpert should be playing shooting guard from any perspective. Out of desperation, D'Antoni made a foolish decision: handing over the ball control power to Anthony. The previous "passing endpoint" set a career-high in assists, becoming the team's assist leader before Lin's eruption. A similar dumb thing was done by Avery Johnson during the 06-08 seasons, letting Dirk Nowitzki hold the ball pretending to be Chris Webber, which cost the system confusion and poor results. Learning from history, successful cases are always about subtracting star functions, like Wilt Chamberlain and Kevin Garnett evolving from aliens to blue-collar leaders, Phil Jackson coaxing Jordan and Kobe to play triangle offense, Jason Terry switching from point guard to dedicated shooter, Wade wishing little James to triple-double every night so he could summarize... forcing stars to take on additional responsibilities essentially means making your best player do what they are least proficient at. And in games where they got dominated by the Heat, you'd see everything revert to its original form—the Knicks don't have an organizing forward or pick-and-roll offense.

New York Chaos

The player who reacts most strongly against Carmelo Anthony and Hedo Turkoglu is the S-team. His firepower plummeted drastically, strictly speaking, he has been miserable since the big trade, often unable to touch the ball in the final moments of games. Whenever Stoudemire gets angry about this, Anthony sincerely tells reporters, "If Amar'e thinks he doesn't get enough shots, it's my fault," and then continues as usual. But this is only one aspect of the problem, insufficient to explain all the reasons for Stoudemire's confusion. Even in games where Lin uses his passing to activate the whole team, Stoudemire still lacks the efficiency and vitality of old. This super rim rocker, who relied on offense to five times make the NBA All-Star Team, now struggles to score in close-range confrontations under the basket. If you say this is due to his approaching thirties, how do you explain his true shooting percentage this season being only 34.1%?

I believe among all statistical data, shooting percentage has the least deception. Making 5.42 out of 10 attempts per game, a 38-year-old man's name is mixed among Chandler, Bynum, Howard, James, and Griffin, being the only guard in the top ten shooting percentages this season. By the end of the season, he might become the first point guard after a 25-year-old Stockton to have a shooting percentage over 55%, or maybe not, he's tired. Even if you don't know his training plan every summer, just looking at the numbers he leaves behind year after year can give you a clue about the story behind them.

Paul Shirley, a wanderer who briefly played in the NBA and is now a published author of several books, said that Nash is the person with the top character and professional ethics he has ever seen. Leaders like Kobe would require you to keep up with him, but Steve wouldn't tell you "Hey, let me make you do this," instead walking over after you've done something and saying "Thank you for what you've done, I appreciate it," while simultaneously being the best player, hardest worker, and best person on a team, perhaps only Duncan and Nash have achieved this. It is precisely this power of example that keeps the Spurs and Suns as a whole even after their rosters have dwindled.

Shirley also mentioned that Stoudemire wasn't a kid who liked to train, as long as others rested, he would slack off too. But after Nash joined the team, the entire atmosphere changed, thus we witnessed Amare later expanding his offensive radius despite severe nearsightedness, transforming from a beast-style player to an all-around hand. It was Nash's urging that turned him into a miracle who made the All-NBA First Team after undergoing knee microsurgery. Stoudemire knows how to be a captain. When he first arrived in New York, he undoubtedly practiced what he preached, enjoying the MVP cheers at Madison Square Garden, scoring 30+ points in nine consecutive games to secure eight victories, rescuing the Knicks from the losing streak quagmire—a plot identical to today's Linsanity. But when the player regarded by George Karl as the laziest and least responsible player on earth took charge of the team, why bother practicing extra when you can just clock in as a hundred-million-dollar man? One wrong trade, one wrong power shift fundamentally destroyed New York, and Linsanity could do nothing about it. The rule of locker room politics is always older point guards saving younger teammates, not the other way around.

Diligence and self-discipline are the most important legacies left by the Suns captain to his juniors, and also the easiest wealth for Jeremy Lin and Ricky Rubio to inherit. No matter where their future lies—in New York, Minnesota, or elsewhere, serving under D'Antoni, Adelman, or other coaches—at least this can ensure that their fate won't fall below the limit.
