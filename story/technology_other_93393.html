Learn to lower your head and bend

by wisdom on 2008-09-20 01:12:44

A butterfly flew into the room through an open window, fluttering around in circles, somewhat panic-stricken. Apparently, it was lost. After many attempts to find its way out, it still failed to leave the house.

The reason why the butterfly couldn't fly back out the same way was that it always searched for an exit at the top of the room and refused to fly lower — where the open window was located. Even several times, it had flown to a position just two or three inches above the window's top but still wouldn't go any lower! In the end, this butterfly that refused to fly lower exhausted all its energy, landing weakly on the table like a lifeless leaf.

Salmon are even more rigid than that butterfly, running straight down one path until they hit a dead end. Salmon are beautiful, with silver skin, swallow tails, and big eyes. They usually live in deep seas but return upstream to spawn during the spring and summer transitions, drifting with the tides to shallow waters. Fishermen have a simple method to catch them: use a coarse bamboo curtain with iron weights attached to the bottom, place it in the water, and let two small boats drag it to intercept the fish schools. Salmon have strong "personalities" and don't like turning; even if they swim into a net, they won't stop. Thus, one after another gets stuck in the holes of the bamboo curtain. As the holes tighten, the salmon become more agitated, bulging their eyes and raising their fins, struggling desperately forward, only to be firmly trapped, caught by the fishermen.

Often, people complain that their lives are getting narrower, unable to see hope for success, yet they stick to old ways, refusing to change and continuing on the same old paths. Isn't this somewhat similar to the butterfly and the salmon? In fact, everyone has their own purpose in life. If the east doesn't shine, the west will. If we adjust our goals and change our thinking, we can certainly discover new horizons and endless beauty.

Benjamin Franklin, known as the father of Americans, once visited a respected elder when he was young. Back then, full of youthful vigor, he walked in with his chest puffed out and long strides. As soon as he entered, his head collided painfully with the doorframe. Rubbing his sore head while looking at the much shorter door, the elder who came to greet him chuckled and said, "That must hurt! But this will be your greatest takeaway from today's visit. To live peacefully in this world, you must always remember: know when to bow your head. This is what I want to teach you."

Franklin regarded the lesson he learned from this visit as the most significant gain of his life and made it one of his lifelong principles. Franklin benefited immensely from this principle throughout his life, later achieving great accomplishments and becoming a remarkable figure. In one of his conversations, he said, "This insight helped me greatly." The implication being: a person should not lack integrity, but neither should they always keep their heads held high when doing things.

Perhaps, in real life, we should try learning how to bow our heads, to admit defeat. It's not difficult. Just understand that when you're dealt a bad hand, don't expect to win this round. Only fools would tell themselves that with a bad hand, effort will surely lead to victory. Learning to bow your head means knowing how to get up promptly when you fall into a quagmire and move far away from it. Only fools would tell their muddy shoes that they remain unstained despite the mess. Learning to bow your head also means getting off the wrong bus when you realize you've boarded the wrong one and taking another. Lei Mo once said, "Bowing requires courage." Indeed, otherwise, how could there be gamblers who stubbornly persist even when they know they'll lose? Throughout history, countless emperors have killed those who dared to offer advice due to a lack of such courage. Around us, numerous mistakes are made because of a lack of this courage.

On the journey of life, we often lose our way due to dazzling things, persisting with unyielding determination and unwavering spirit, only to lose ourselves in the end. So, with a peaceful mind, learn to bow your head. This might be the most basic common sense of life. In the valley, snowflakes fall heavily, covering the branches of the snow pines. When the snow accumulates to a certain extent, the flexible branches of the snow pine slowly bend downwards, allowing the snow to slide off bit by bit. This process repeats: accumulating, bending, and shedding. After the storm, the snow pines remain undamaged. Other trees, lacking this skill, have their branches broken under the weight of the snow, leading to their destruction.

A flood washed a pile of boulders onto a grassland, trapping a patch of grass underneath. To breathe fresh air and enjoy the warm sunlight, the grass changed direction, curving and twisting through the gaps between the stones, emerging beyond the obstruction. In life, we should endure external pressures as much as possible. When endurance reaches its limit, it's not wrong to bend temporarily, like the snow pine yielding a step to avoid being crushed; or like the grass, flexibly finding a way around to avoid being stifled.

Bending is not compromise; it is a rational tolerance to overcome difficulties.

Bending is not falling; it is standing stronger and firmer.

Bending is not destruction; it is stepping back to achieve greater freedom and a bright future; it is about maintaining a face that can laugh last in life.

Learning to bend gives one a cheerful attitude towards adversity.

Learning to bend teaches one to face life's hardships with a sense of beauty.

Learning to bend enables one to perceive the vicissitudes of life with higher wisdom.

Bending, indeed, is an art of life.
