by miage543 on 2011-04-11 00:26:04

College Student Designs Mini Overpass; Illustration by Yang Fangfang

In response to increasingly severe traffic congestion, college student Li Xu, majoring in transportation, spent two years designing a "mini overpass." In his design, vehicles at crossroads do not need to stop, and it also saves space. The design has sparked heated discussions among netizens. Some believe that this plan could help alleviate Beijing's traffic congestion. Li Xu also stated that his solution is suitable for Beijing. However, some experts pointed out that if implemented, it might only worsen the congestion. The "mini overpass" has also drawn attention to Beijing’s existing overpasses, with some residents pointing out design issues causing traffic jams.

According to reports, recently, the design diagram of the "mini overpass" appeared online, drawing numerous questions from netizens. “Can Beijing's overpasses adopt this design?” “This plan has no traffic lights, allowing all vehicles to go straight, turn, or make U-turns.”

Netizen Opinions:

Amy0311 believes that current overpasses, such as the Beitaipingzhuang Bridge, allow mainline vehicles to pass without stopping at traffic lights, but turning vehicles must wait. The "mini overpass" design can solve this problem.

The release of this design sparked criticism of Beijing’s existing overpasses. Netizen Ttyin mentioned that the Beichen Overpass lacks a connecting bridge from north to east linking the eastbound section of North Fourth Ring Road. The Wanquanhe Bridge on North Third Ring Road and North Fourth Ring Road lacks a connecting bridge from south to west linking the westbound section of North Fourth Ring Road, forcing westbound vehicles to make U-turns under the Yiyuanlu Overpass and detour. Other netizens pointed out that many interchanges connecting with the Fifth Ring Road either cannot exit or enter the Fifth Ring Road. Netizens expressed that these problems lead to congestion around the overpasses.

Netizen Shaozi pointed out that the Shuangjing Bridge has design flaws: concentrated bus stops, subway stations, and shopping centers cause intersections between loop road exits, bicycles, and pedestrians, leading to chaos. He hopes the "mini overpass" plan can help alleviate Shuangjing Bridge congestion.

Designer’s Perspective:

Beijing’s Wide and Flat Roads Suit This Plan

Li Xu is optimistic about whether this plan is applicable to Beijing. He said that this plan particularly suits Beijing because Beijing has many intersections, and the roads are relatively wide and flat.

Li Xu mentioned that making left turns on Chang'an Avenue is a big challenge, and congestion on Chang'an Avenue is becoming increasingly serious. However, Li Xu's plan has been questioned by some citizens. After reviewing the plan, citizen Mr. Li believed its design merely merges outwardly expanded left-turn loops into the internal main road. Such a design would suddenly reduce the number of lanes on two main roads, potentially causing significant congestion at intersections requiring deceleration.

Currently, Li Xu has submitted materials to apply for a patent for the "mini overpass."

Expert Opinion:

This Design Would Worsen Beijing’s Congestion

Ma Lin, Deputy Director of the Urban Traffic Engineering Technology Center of the Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural Development, stated that the spirit behind the university student's design proposal is commendable. However, the "mini overpass" lacks feasibility and is unsuitable for Beijing. If adopted, Beijing would become even more congested.

Ma Lin believes that the plan lacks an engineering foundation. If strictly following the design standards of overpasses, such as slope and turning ramps, all these factors considered, the implementation will no longer be "mini" but massive.

Ma Lin noted that this design did not consider traffic flow. According to this plan, vehicles would necessarily slow down when turning, which, in a city like Beijing with heavy traffic volume, would exacerbate congestion once speed decreases. Moreover, the design features steep slopes, making driving difficult in rainy or snowy weather. Additionally, the design has blind spots regarding sight distance, with insufficient distances between four up/down left-turn lanes and U-turn lanes, inadequate turning radii, and insufficient clearance height under the bridge. Overall, low indicators may easily interfere with visibility and increase the likelihood of accidents.

Dialogue:

Father Lost Business Due to Traffic Jam, Sparking Design Inspiration

The 22-year-old Henan native, Li Xu, spent two years designing the "mini overpass" and applied for a patent. He believes his plan is superior to traditional overpasses and could alleviate Beijing's traffic congestion. On the previous day, our reporter interviewed Li Xu.

Beijing News: What was the initial motivation behind designing the "mini overpass"?

Li Xu: My father came to Beijing for business in 2007 and lost a big deal due to traffic jams. At that time, Beijing had already started experiencing traffic congestion. My father thought many overpasses in Beijing were poorly designed, such as the complex Xizhimen Overpass. I received strong support from my father in designing the "mini overpass." My father is interested in drafting, and some of the work was completed by him.

Beijing News: The "mini overpass" has drawn attention from netizens. How do you feel about it?

Li Xu: I didn't expect it to attract so much attention, which indicates the severity of the traffic congestion issue and the imperfections of the existing overpasses. Netizens have personal experiences, which draws their attention.

Beijing News: Many netizens criticized your "mini overpass" for not considering pedestrians, calling it a "fatal flaw." How would you address the interaction between vehicles and pedestrians and the issue of pedestrians crossing the road?

Li Xu: This is indeed a defect, and I've considered this issue. Perhaps it could be resolved through overhead bridges or underground passages.

Beijing News: Netizens have raised several defects about the "mini overpass," such as too many extra exits prone to congestion and difficulty in correction if a wrong turn is made. Experts also believe your plan lacks feasibility. What do you think?

Li Xu: Design diagrams are not rigidly fixed when applied but should be adapted according to actual conditions, including traffic flow. Expert opinions have been very helpful, such as sight distance blind spots and vehicle-pedestrian interactions, which were previously overlooked areas.

Will the "Plan" Clear Up the Shuangjing Bridge Congestion?

Designer Li Xu believes the "mini overpass" is suitable for Beijing. Some citizens suggest applying this plan to diagnose congestion points like the Shuangjing Bridge to see if it can solve the congestion.

Li Xu stated that although Beijing's road characteristics suit his plan, he cannot immediately provide a definitive answer for specific locations. Research is needed on local traffic flow, road characteristics, and surrounding environments. "We can find time to simulate using software first."

[On-site]

Multiple Congestion Points Around the Overpass

Yesterday afternoon, reporters conducted an on-site inspection of the Shuangjing Bridge. In the northeast corner of the Shuangjing Bridge, vehicles turning left from Liangguang Road face multiple bus stops just after crossing the bridge, including nearly 20 bus lines such as routes 28, 37, and 57. Because buses enter the station too close to the bridge, crowds waiting for the bus spill onto the bicycle lane and mix with cyclists, creating one congestion point. In the northwest corner of the bridge, there is a large shopping center where some vehicles are parked randomly on the side of the road, occupying the bicycle lane, forcing cyclists onto the motor vehicle lane and worsening the congestion.

To the west of Shuangjing Bridge, heading south from Guomao Bridge, vehicles going to Liangguang Road need to exit the main road before reaching the Shuangjing Bridge. Exiting vehicles mix with auxiliary road vehicles, bicycles, and pedestrians. Also nearby are bus stops and subway stations, where heavy pedestrian and vehicle flows mix, forming another congestion point. Nearby newsstand owners say that during peak hours, traffic often becomes completely gridlocked, with minor accidents happening almost daily. A 200-meter stretch of road might take half an hour to traverse by car.

Additionally, from the Shuangjing Bridge eastward to the intersection of Guangqu Road and Xidawang Road, a mere three-kilometer stretch, there are four traffic signals set up, slowing down vehicles and causing queues.

Citizen Ms. Zhang believes that if Li Xu's design is applied here, traveling from Liangguang Road to Guomao would eliminate traffic lights in any direction, making left turns very convenient. Multiple entrances and exits on the overpass would disperse traffic flow without significantly reducing speed, naturally eliminating congestion points.

[Commentary]

Traffic Relief Must Solve Vehicle-Pedestrian Mixing Problems

After reviewing Li Xu's plan, Zheng Xueshin, a civilian designer who has studied overpasses for over a decade, believed that the design does not effectively resolve vehicle flow intersection problems, making it unsuitable for high-traffic areas like the Shuangjing Bridge.

Zheng Xueshin thinks that the biggest problem with traditional overpass designs lies in vehicle flow intersections and vehicle-pedestrian mixing. These intersection points reduce road capacity. Additionally, when vehicles and bicycles share parallel lanes and turn, vehicle-pedestrian mixing occurs, causing chaotic vehicle flow intersections. Li Xu's design does not address these problems.

[Call for Suggestions]

Please Offer Suggestions on Overpass Traffic Relief

Since the construction of the first overpass, the Zhenxingmen Bridge in 1974, Beijing currently has nearly 200 overpasses, yet they struggle to alleviate increasingly severe traffic congestion. Many citizens believe that some overpasses prioritize form over function, with designs becoming increasingly complex and limiting passage. Some citizens also believe that overpasses themselves create congestion points.

Which overpasses do you think have unreasonable designs leading to congestion? Which overpasses make you take unnecessary detours? What opinions or suggestions do you have about Beijing's overpasses?
