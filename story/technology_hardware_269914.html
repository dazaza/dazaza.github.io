Last night, my daughter had trouble falling asleep.

by vrfdesnm on 2009-11-22 12:53:10

On Sunday night, after removing the release paper, we had to return to the county town to prepare for work the next day. As usual, my daughter clung to her mother's arm with a look of reluctant parting that made my heart restless.

The next day, my daughter also had school, and at 8 p.m., she would go to bed on time as always. Normally, she would fall into a deep sleep soon after going to bed, but this night, she couldn’t fall asleep for a long time. My wife stayed fully clothed beside her, comforting and protecting her to help her relax.

At 9 p.m., I checked on my daughter’s sleeping condition. Although an hour had passed, my daughter was still tightly hugging my wife's arm. I saw that my daughter had her eyes closed and wasn’t moving, so I thought she was asleep. However, my wife signaled to me that my daughter was not actually asleep.

Since my daughter stopped breastfeeding, she has been taken care of by my parents who teach in the town middle school. Due to various reasons, she hasn't been able to stay with us regularly. The county town is not far from my parents' home; it takes about ten minutes by car. To ensure that our daughter always feels our endless love and grows up healthily and robustly, every weekend, we return to my parents' house to be with her. On weekdays, if we have time, we try our best to go back and be with her. During winter and summer vacations each year, we simply bring her to stay with us.

Even so, we and our daughter always face brief separations repeatedly. I remember when our daughter was still a toddler, she was very well-behaved and quiet whenever we parted. Perhaps because she was too young at the time to understand the attachment to family or the pain of separation. But every time we parted then, I always felt a sourness in my heart, unable to express the deep nostalgia I had for her.

Some people say that men's emotions are more rugged than women's. But regarding my feelings for my daughter, I find myself even more delicate than my wife, who is a woman. Every time I leave my daughter, I am always reluctant to part, looking back again and again. After saying goodbye to her at home, I walk outside and look back through the window one more time before leaving resolutely.

Even though each separation from my daughter only lasts a few days, the feeling of missing her always strongly occupies my heart. Whenever I have free time, I always miss and worry about her. As long as I am separated from her, no matter where I am, I will call her every day without fail. Only after hearing her familiar and crisp voice on the phone, and having a warm conversation with her, can I feel at ease and satisfied.

As my daughter grows up and gradually becomes more understanding, I also give her countless warnings regarding her personal safety, such as telling her to enhance her safety awareness, prevent traffic accidents, and beware of human traffickers. Sometimes, just giving her instructions isn't enough, so I personally call my parents, repeatedly urging them to pay attention to my daughter's personal safety at all times.

Now, as my daughter grows older, she begins to understand the attachment of family affection more deeply. Recently, whenever we separate from her, she always shows extreme attachment to us, unlike before when she would straightforwardly agree to let us go.

Perhaps at this age, my daughter is especially attached to her parents, or maybe as an only child, she fears loneliness and hopes her parents are always with her. Perhaps, as my daughter grows up, she starts to have her own thoughts, and her inner world becomes richer. However, "the moon has its phases, and people have their joys and sorrows." We cannot be with our daughter all the time, although the separation is short, it is something we and our daughter must face. In fact, compared to the millions of left-behind children around the world, our daughter is quite lucky because we often take time to be with her.

Joys and sorrows, reunions and separations, are the normal state of life. Maybe one day, when our daughter grows up and flies away, it will be us who truly need someone to accompany us. Understanding the impermanence of life, we can face it calmly and deal with it peacefully.

When we separate from our daughter, we won’t be too sad because it is a normal state of life; when we are together with our daughter, we will cherish the time we spend together and strive to make up for the regrets caused by separation.

These years, I have been doing just that: finding ways to be with my daughter, cherishing the time we spend together, being close and harmonious, to compensate for the deficiencies and regrets brought by repeated separations.
