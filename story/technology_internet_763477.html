Google's Reason for Launching Moto X: Reducing the Risk of Hardware Partners Defecting

by anonymous on 2013-08-02 10:21:28

Introduction: On Tuesday, the American technology news website ZDNet published an article titled "Moto X: Can Google deliver on its promise to strengthen the Android ecosystem?" The article suggests that despite Android's current smooth sailing, Google's decision to invest resources in developing the Moto X smartphone is a precautionary measure aimed at minimizing risks associated with hardware partners potentially defecting.

The following is the full text of the article:

Fulfilling Promises

In August 2011, when Google first announced its plan to acquire Motorola Mobility, the company promised that this acquisition would "strengthen the entire ecosystem." Two years later, Motorola Mobility is about to launch its next-generation flagship smartphone, the Moto X. Is this Google’s way of fulfilling its original promise, or does it represent a complete deviation from that commitment?

Motorola Mobility refers to the Moto X as the first smartphone "designed and assembled" in the United States, though it did not mention where the components were produced. Motorola claims that by having engineers and designers work together to accelerate the innovation process, the Moto X has gained a competitive edge.

Motorola also claims that the Moto X will be the "first smartphone that users can fully customize according to their own preferences," although this simply means that users can only choose the color of the phone and engrave text on the body; they have no say in internal components. Motorola stated that by the end of summer, it expects to add 2,000 employees at its Fort Worth, Texas factory to ensure full production of the Moto X.

The Moto X will officially debut at a New York event later this week, but Google has already revealed some details to build anticipation. Google Executive Chairman Eric Schmidt was seen using the Moto X at an event. At the D11 Conference, Motorola Mobility CEO Dennis Woodside briefly described some features of the Moto X.

He said, "Motorola has always been adept at managing device power consumption. Additionally, Motorola excels at managing ultra-low-power sensors, gyroscopes, and accelerometers, keeping them running optimally so that the device understands different usage states: knowing it's in my pocket, knowing when I take it out to do something — like taking a photo. It anticipates my needs. Imagine being in a car, the device knows if the car is started and traveling at 60 miles per hour, and it responds appropriately to allow for safe interaction."

Significant Acquisition

In June 2011, Google announced plans to acquire Motorola Mobility for $12.5 billion, a transaction completed in May 2012. Google CEO Larry Page then stated that acquiring Motorola Mobility would "further strengthen the Android ecosystem," stimulating competition in mobile computing.

At the same time, Google sought to reassure other Android hardware manufacturers, stating that the Android operating system would remain open. Google Senior Vice President of Mobile, Andy Rubin, remarked, "Our vision for the Android platform remains unchanged. Google remains fully committed to its open platform strategy and to building Android into a vibrant open-source community. We will continue to collaborate with all key Android partners to develop and release innovative Android devices."

However, while Google executives emphasized the mobile business, most industry observers believed that the acquisition was primarily aimed at obtaining Motorola Mobility's portfolio of mobile technology patents to counter patent litigation against the Android ecosystem, with the mobile phone business being merely an "add-on." Through this deal, Google acquired 17,000 patents and 7,500 patent applications.

Monopoly

Since completing the acquisition, Google has sold Motorola Mobility's set-top box business and production facilities. In March, Google CFO Patrick Pichette stated that Google had inherited a 18-month product line from Motorola Mobility and needed to "exhaust" it before releasing products that met Google standards.

Various signs indicate that Motorola Mobility urgently needs to find its innovative genes: Market research firm comScore data shows that in May, Motorola Mobility's share of the U.S. smartphone market was only 7.8%, down from 8.4% three months earlier, and in May 2012, when the acquisition was completed, its market share was 12%. In comparison, Apple's share of the U.S. smartphone market is 39.2%, and Samsung's is 23%. Samsung is the largest Android hardware manufacturer.

Given this, it is indeed difficult to understand why Google would still want to create its own mobile phone business through Motorola Mobility. Microsoft ventured into the hardware market with the Surface tablet because it was extremely dissatisfied with its hardware partners' lack of innovation. Conversely, the Android ecosystem is currently thriving, with Samsung's Galaxy S4 smartphone impressing users and generating strong interest in Android phones.

Preparation for the Future

So, why does Google invest resources in developing the Moto X amidst the flourishing Android ecosystem? Ovum's Chief Analyst of Devices and Platforms, Tony Cripps, believes that although Android is currently the dominant player in the mobile operating system market, highlighting Motorola Mobility's position allows Google to offset risks brought by hardware partners switching to other operating systems.

Cripps said, "Strategically, Android remains a crucial part of Google's product line. Seeking to protect Android's position as much as possible, achieving this goal through its own hardware business is undoubtedly a good choice." Given Samsung's success with Android devices, the possibility of hardware manufacturers abandoning the Android platform seems negligible, but who puts all their eggs in one basket?

Take Samsung as an example, which dominates half of the Android phone market. Although Samsung benefits greatly from the Android platform, Google's preparation for potential defections remains significant. The mobile industry has witnessed notable cases of defection in the past, and Samsung continues to show interest in Tizen, a competitor to Android. Even though hardware manufacturers may not reject Android in the near future, Google must prepare ahead of time to minimize the risk of splitting from its hardware partners.

In fact, in the technology sector, Google is known for its long-term vision, meaning that the success or failure of a single phone product won't significantly impact the company's long-term strategy. As Larry Page said upon completing the acquisition of Motorola Mobility, "People tend to overestimate the impact of technology in the short term and underestimate its long-term importance, which is a well-known fact. Among today's internet users, many may never use desktops, and this shift will have profound implications."
