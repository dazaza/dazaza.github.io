The backstory of how the iPad became an iconic design

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:56:00

iPad Prototype

　　The newly released book this Thursday, "Jony Ive: The Genius Behind Apple's Greatest Products," tells the stories behind the development of multiple Apple products. The following excerpt reveals the untold story of how Apple's iPad became an "iconic design."

　　As Jony Ive's design team secretly worked on the iPad, Apple co-founder Steve Jobs publicly claimed that Apple had no plans to release a tablet computer. He sarcastically remarked at the time, "Tablets only appeal to the rich who already own PCs and other devices."

　　However, Jobs' statement was actually a feint. Apple executive Phil Schiller once stated, "Jobs was always interested in developing a tablet." In fact, during the development of the iPhone by Ive's team, they were also working on a tablet. Jobs was simply waiting for the right moment to introduce Apple's tablet to the market.

　　One of Apple's motivations for developing a tablet was the emergence of netbooks. Netbooks entered the market in 2007, characterized by their smaller size, lower price, and lower energy consumption compared to traditional laptops. This product quickly impacted the sales of regular laptops. By 2009, netbooks accounted for 20% of the laptop market share. However, Apple never seriously considered producing a netbook. Jobs said at the time, "Netbooks aren't much good; they're just cheap laptops." Nonetheless, there were several discussions in Apple's executive meetings about whether to develop a netbook.

　　At a high-level executive meeting held by Apple in 2008, Ive expressed that his team was developing a tablet that could serve as a powerful weapon for Apple to respond to the challenge posed by netbooks. Ive noted that tablets are essentially netbooks without physical keyboards. This suggestion piqued Jobs' interest, leading to the command for Ive to turn the prototype into a real product.

　　What was crucial was the significant progress made in mobile technology since the launch of the iPhone. As a result, the tablet prototype developed in 2004 appeared bulky. With the advent of new display and battery technologies, tablets could be made thinner and lighter. One of the reasons the production of the iPad was not initially approved was the immaturity of components like displays and batteries.

　　Subsequently, Ive ordered his team to create 20 products of varying sizes and dimensions. These products were then placed before Ive and Jobs, allowing them to decide on the most suitable size. This method was similarly employed when determining the appropriate sizes for Mac mini and other products. A former Apple technical engineer said, "Jobs and Ive liked to do it this way—they would first compare a bunch of different-sized products and then choose the model they were satisfied with."

　　However, another former Apple executive indicated that the choice of iPad's size was strongly influenced by another factor: standard paper size. He stated, "The size of the iPad is actually the same as a sheet of paper. Since the product was positioned as a notebook-like tablet, standard paper size became the appropriate dimension. Its target users were educational institutions, schools, and it could be used for reading."

　　Ive's ultimate goal for the iPad design was to create a "highly simple, beautiful, heart-stirring, and easy-to-operate" product—one that people would immediately like and could use intuitively. Achieving this required a significant investment of time and creative thinking from Apple's R&D personnel.

Jony Ive

　　Device Production

　　Similar to the design approach for the iPhone, Ive's team also explored two different design directions for the iPad. The first was based on the Extrudo design, which resembled the iPod mini but larger and smoother. Chris Stringer, who was responsible for this design, had previously worked on the Extrudo iPhone. In this design, sharp corners were not overly considered, as most people wouldn't use the tablet close to their face.

　　Ive's team also experimented with some "picture frame" models, which were slightly larger than the iPad prototype. Apple's designers found that, similar to the Extrudo iPhone, the Extrudo iPad had its shortcomings: too prominent edges. Ive wanted users to focus entirely on the screen without any distractions. Meanwhile, another designer proposed using bright white plastic based on the experience from the Sandwich iPhone. During this process, the iPad design evolved towards being thinner and lighter. However, Jobs still felt something was missing: the iPad seemed not casual enough.

　　Ive identified the issue: to make the iPad more friendly, it should be easily picked up with one hand. So he thought about adding handles to the iPad but soon realized that wouldn't work either and had to find another solution. After the design work was largely completed, the Apple design team encountered manufacturing problems with the iPad. Since the back cover of the iPhone 3G was small, there weren't many manufacturing challenges. However, the larger size of the iPad presented difficulties in producing the back cover. Thus, Ive's team revised the back cover design.

　　After all these efforts, when the team saw the produced iPads, they were incredibly excited. To Stringer and others, the new iPad looked completely fresh—a brand-new product.
