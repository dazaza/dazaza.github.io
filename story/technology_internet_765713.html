Matias Duarte Talks Android Design Guidelines

by anonymous on 2013-08-19 15:24:24

　　English Original: Android Stencil Kit by UI Stencils

　　Should app developers follow the design guidelines of their platform? This is a complex question. On one hand, they need to consider user needs, helping users get started quickly, and following design guidelines is undoubtedly a shortcut. On the other hand, they also need to consider brand requirements, making users feel unique and fresh, which often means breaking the design guidelines. Therefore, the process of app design is one of compromise, weighing various options to find the most suitable solution.

　　Before Android 4.0, there were no design guidelines for Android app development. This seemingly gave developers full freedom, but the result was that, in the absence of standards, developers doing their own thing did not lead to beautiful apps; instead, it caused inconvenience and confusion for users. Additionally, since iOS was often the preferred platform, some developers simply copied the iOS interface, leading to even more chaotic interfaces and operations in Android apps. Now, the situation has fundamentally changed. With the release of Android design guidelines, app design now has a new standard. The quality of Android apps has begun to improve significantly. For developers, as long as they follow the official design specifications, they can create attractive apps in a short period of time.

　　Of course, the various choices developers face still exist. When developing an app, should they follow the design specifications? In what situations can they break the rules? Regarding this, Matias Duarte, the head of Android User Experience, shared his views on Google+.

　　Matias first discussed the significance of design guidelines. Why does a platform need design guidelines? Because developers face various complex choices when designing apps, and these choices influence each other. "Some choices may seem fine individually, but when you consider the other choices made in the design, they turn out to be bad choices."

　　Therefore, consistency becomes very important. "The whole point of consistency is to make it easier for users to understand and use something."

　　Let's take a simple example. Suppose you are designing a lid for a jam jar. You have many options, but it would be best not to design it to open clockwise. Why? Because everyone turns lids counterclockwise. If you insist on showing product uniqueness this way, you will drive everyone crazy.

Consistency and rules are there to help users. They enable users to accomplish tasks without thinking about how to do them. Design guidelines help you understand consistency and rules, especially when you're new to it.

　　This doesn't mean developers need to rigidly adhere to the rules. It just means that when designing a product, developers need to consider whether their innovations provide unique value to users, not being different just for the sake of difference. Furthermore, developers need to understand that not all rules are equally important. Visual rules, such as color, font, skeuomorphism or flat design, are issues related to emotional acceptance and don’t affect basic usability. However, rules related to user behavior are another matter. Even for the sake of branding or cross-platform consistency, these rules shouldn’t be broken because doing so can frustrate users.

　　Ultimately, whether the design of an app is successful depends on whether users approve. Regardless of the platform, each will have its own unique design that influences user habits. For app developers, design guidelines can help them avoid unnecessary detours and gain user recognition more quickly. On this basis, bold innovation by developers is not only beneficial but can also advance the design guidelines themselves. For example, the navigation drawer (Navigation Drawer) in Android apps originated from the community and was later officially recognized and added to the design guidelines. Following and breaking rules aren't entirely contradictory; sometimes, to achieve breakthrough innovation, you must first understand the existing rules.
