Experts from the Yangzhou Armed Police Hospital's Anorectal Center point out that anal fissures, as a common anorectal condition, may not be a serious illness but are quite difficult to cure. Patients often experience recurring anal fissures. Recurrent anal fissures are closely related to drug treatments and failure to treat constipation.

The main factors that induce anal fissures are as follows:

**Reason One: Constipation**

Among the causes of anal fissures, constipation is the biggest culprit. The long-term retention of feces in the rectum can not only induce rectal cancer but also affect blood circulation. When patients strain to pass hard stools, it inevitably puts significant pressure on the anus, causing tears, swelling, and a series of pathological changes. Additionally, if anal fissure patients do not treat their constipation during treatment, worsening constipation can re-induce anal fissures, leading to recurring episodes.

**Reason Two: Poor Defecation Habits**

Many people like to read or look at newspapers while squatting on the toilet, which prolongs both squatting and defecation time. This habit can easily cause blood stasis in the anus and rectum, inducing disease. Smoking during bowel movements can buffer the brain's defecation reflex, easily leading to constipation. Straining too hard during defecation, regardless of whether the urge to defecate is strong, blindly forcing oneself will only increase unnecessary burden and local blood stasis in the rectum, anus, and pelvic floor muscles, promoting the occurrence and development of anal fissures.

**Reason Three: Infection Factors**

Acute and chronic anal sinusitis, anal papillitis, internal hemorrhoids, and polyps are the main causes of infection. Infectious pathogens enter the anal glands through the gland openings, forming abscesses in the subcutaneous tissue of the anal canal, which rupture to form ulcers. Small superficial thrombosis due to infection can cause thrombophlebitis, also leading to the occurrence of anal fissures.

**Reason Four: Human Anatomy Factors**

The external sphincter of the human anus extends forward from the coccyx to the posterior part of the anal canal, dividing into two parts that wrap around the anal canal along its sides until they meet at the front of the anal canal. This leaves gaps at the front and back of the anal canal. Most of the levator ani muscles attach to the sides of the anal canal, with fewer attachments at the front and back. The anterior and posterior parts of the anal canal are less robust than the sides, making them more prone to injury. The anal canal angles downward and backward, creating the anal-rectal angle, where the posterior part of the anal canal experiences greater fecal pressure. The posterior midline of the anal canal lacks sufficient blood supply and elasticity, all of which contribute to the formation of anal fissures.

**Reason Five: Other Factors**

Besides the above four factors, unreasonable dietary habits, trauma, chronic diseases, and physiological factors in humans can also trigger anal fissures.

**Reason Six: Incorrect Treatment Leading to Recurrence**

In clinical practice, many anal fissure patients initially hope for conservative treatment, such as diet and medication, which often leads to worsening conditions. This is another important reason for the recurrence of anal fissures.

Experts from the Yangzhou Armed Police Hospital's Anorectal Center remind patients that after treatment, they must follow medical advice strictly and should not assume everything is fine after treatment. It is best to have regular hospital check-ups.
