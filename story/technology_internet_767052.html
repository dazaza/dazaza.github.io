How do iOS 7 developers interpret the design of iOS 7?

by anonymous on 2013-10-02 17:25:11

After the official launch of iOS 7, the App Store began prompting that many apps needed updates, and the biggest improvement for these apps was "optimized for iOS 7." Then, their icons were seen to have been "flattened," with the UIs of the apps not decorated with too much texture, and animation being more emphasized.

But is this all there is to the design of iOS 7? Let's take a look at what developers and designers think.

"(iOS 7) no longer imitates real-world visual elements, but rather how they function, their movement, and how other elements interact with your fingers," said Jeff Broderick, co-founder and creative director of software design studio Collective Ray. "Finding good ways to use physics and parallax is key to designing great apps." For iOS 7, "looking great" is no longer the only goal; it places even more emphasis on feeling great when in use.

Jeremy Olson, developer of the soon-to-be-released app Hours, believes that iOS 7 creates an illusion of "direct manipulation." "The idea (of iOS 7) is to give people the feeling of directly manipulating functions on the screen in real time, rather than clicking a button and seeing feedback somewhere else." Apple has always placed great importance on interaction, and in iOS 7, the status of "interaction" has greatly increased, becoming a philosophical system, or even more.

"If you deviate too far from the regular built-in apps of Apple, users may not adapt as quickly, and they might even feel repelled by the 'differences,'" said Michael Simmons, designer of the quick calendar software Fantastical on OS X. He believes that iOS 7 strips away unnecessary visual elements and places content in such a central position. "I see a new method of app design that no longer hides behind decoration — no longer hides behind false design." What truly matters is what is placed upfront and at the center.

"Simply calling iOS 7 'flat' design completely misses what Apple is really doing," said Justin Williams, developer of Elements. "The greatest feature of iOS 7 is using layers to convey deeper transitions of content." Apple's thinking about the system is "motion"; they use motion to indicate screen transitions and also simulate real-life motion elements to inform users of which interface they are currently on.

Pasquale D'Silva asks, "Why not provide your brain with some clues about what's happening right now?" In his work *Transitional Interfaces*, he mentions a point: providing some clues within apps or interactions can reduce cognitive cost. In a previous interview with BusinessWeek, Jonathan Ive explained the design changes in iOS 7, "Creating layers is not an aesthetic viewpoint. It is a way to try and handle information that already exists on different levels and hopefully make you feel where you are."
