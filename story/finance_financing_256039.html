Growth Enterprise Market warning: Do not disturb unless sincere

by lay1987 on 2009-11-04 19:34:19

On the first trading day, prices surged across the board, followed by two days of widespread declines. More than 200,000 individual investors were trapped, and nearly 20 billion yuan in market value evaporated... This was the report card presented to all investors after China's Growth Enterprise Market (GEM), which had been delayed for nearly a decade, made its spectacular debut last Friday.

Dubbed "China's NASDAQ," the GEM's performance was, it must be said, still within the predictions of the media and the public, and did not come as a great surprise. What was surprising, however, was that while a stock price collapse is generally not something to celebrate, many netizens expressed their "joy." Why? Because with the sharp decline over these two days, the personal shareholders who had become instant billionaires due to the launch of the GEM also saw an equivalent drop in their net worths. Watching their wealth being dragged down by the green stock prices brought some comfort to many onlookers.

Among them, the most representative group consisted of shareholders from Huayi Brothers (300027), including celebrities like Feng Xiaogang, Huang Xiaoming, and Li Bingbing. These individuals were doing well in the entertainment industry with high salaries, and at the same time, became multimillionaires or even billionaires thanks to their status as founding shareholders riding the wave of the GEM. However, the cost of the wealth reduction for these star tycoons was the collective trapping of thousands of small and medium-sized investors who bought shares on the first day, with little hope of breaking even in the short term.

"The stock market carries risks; enter with caution." This phrase has long been familiar to Chinese investors. However, judging from the overheated trading on the first day of the GEM's listing, its market risks have yet to be fully recognized by small and medium-sized investors. Compared to mainboard companies, GEM companies are characterized by smaller scale, lower thresholds, and being predominantly private enterprises. For investors, these features imply the possibility of higher returns but also greater risks.

The risk lies in the fact that according to the GEM system design, GEM stocks have a "direct delisting" rule, without the warning transition phases such as ST or *ST seen on the mainboard. Mainboard companies can still undergo restructuring through shell buying or borrowing when facing continuous losses, but this is not possible on the GEM. Once a company fails, it will be directly removed from the market. Investors who buy delisted stocks could lose all their capital. In addition to the risks associated with the system design, GEM companies may also face technological risks. Given that we live in an era of rapid technological development and increasingly shorter product life cycles, the global technology market is fiercely competitive. If a GEM company loses its technological edge, it will face significant risks.

Moreover, GEM companies also face moral risks. The rapid expansion of the owners' wealth due to the company's listing could conversely fuel their greed for wealth. Therefore, experts foresee that violations such as product quality falsification, financial fraud, and false information disclosure may follow the GEM market closely. Once these misdeeds are exposed, they could trigger a domino effect, causing the GEM stock prices to plummet sharply and wealth to vanish. There have already been vivid examples of this happening in foreign GEM markets.

Therefore, investors who lack the ability to bear risks, and those who cannot afford to lose but are eager to get rich quickly, should not rush into trading GEM stocks. Otherwise, they might end up like Hong Kong stock guru David Cao Renchao warned: "The GEM is like a boy, and you need to beware of the day he suddenly runs away."
