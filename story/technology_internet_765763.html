Sina Weibo's value has been overestimated, Sina may be shorted

by anonymous on 2013-08-20 15:57:30

　　The atmosphere of concern about the future of Sina Weibo is becoming increasingly intense, and the voices predicting its decline are also growing louder.

　　Barron's columnist Shuli Ren published an article today titled "Weibo May Be Overvalued Relative to WeChat," stating that investors have placed overly high expectations on Weibo.

　　Currently, Sina’s valuation has reached 40 times its projected earnings per share for 2014, making it one of the most expensive stocks in the U.S. According to some analysts' estimates, Weibo is valued at $3.7 billion, which accounts for two-thirds of Sina's market capitalization.

　　However, the short sellers have already caught a whiff of something. Even as Sina's stock price rose by 24%, investor interest in shorting Sina increased by 63% in July compared to June.

　　Earlier this year, a data report released by a London market research firm showed that Sina Weibo's active user count dropped nearly 40% last year. Charles Chao, CEO of Sina, revealed that Weibo had "burned" through $280 million over two years with a severely imbalanced return on investment.

　　The alliance with e-commerce giant Alibaba gave Sina new ways to monetize Weibo, but these methods seem ineffective.

　　Due to still not finding an efficient business model, Weibo remains unprofitable. In the long term, if the losses continue, the value created during its product lifecycle may be very low or even negative, which would significantly compress Weibo's valuation.

　　In addition to uncertain business models, Sina Weibo also faces three major challenges: competition from WeChat, policy uncertainties, and user disengagement.

　　Shuli Ren pointed out in her article that if we assume each WeChat user is worth the same as each Weibo user, WeChat would be valued at $2.1 billion, which only represents 2.5% of Tencent's market capitalization. Once Tencent starts commercializing WeChat, its valuation could undergo significant changes.

　　Ren also mentioned that although Sina has 500 million registered users, WeChat currently has 300 million registered users but is ready to profit from mobile business. Given that WeChat is China's most widely used mobile software, its value will one day surpass Weibo's.

　　According to FactSet data, nine analysts have upgraded Sina's rating, with a median target price of $95 (the target price range is between $85-$100), which is more than 10% higher than Sina's latest stock price.

　　Nomura Securities gave the highest target price of $100 among the nine institutions. The bank used a sum-of-the-parts analysis method.

　　Analysts at Nomura used the 2011 valuation of Twitter, where each user was valued at $15, as a benchmark and halved the value for Chinese users, valuing each Sina user at $7, thus arriving at a Weibo valuation of $3.7 billion.

　　Despite Weibo's valuation accounting for two-thirds of Sina's total, its second-quarter revenue only accounted for one-quarter of Sina's total revenue, showing that investors have overly high expectations for Weibo.
