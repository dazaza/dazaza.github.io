Can potatoes really help with weight loss? Perhaps your first reaction is disbelief. Potatoes, which are rich in starch, should be enemies of weight loss, right? In fact, potatoes contain very little fat, have low calories, and are rich in dietary fiber, a key element for weight loss, making them standout members of the weight loss food family.

Weight Loss Highlight 1: Dietary Fiber

According to data from the U.S. Food and Drug Administration, a 148-gram (nearly three liang) baked potato with skin contains 3 grams of dietary fiber. This amount meets 12% of people's daily needs, which is close to the content of dietary fiber found in apples. Therefore, potatoes have earned the nickname "underground apple." The reason dietary fiber is called a star element for weight loss is because it helps the body digest and absorb better, gives a sense of fullness, and reduces the intake of excessive food. Additionally, multiple health studies have shown that dietary fiber helps reduce the risk of colon cancer and heart disease.

Weight Loss Highlight 2: Low Fat Content, Low Calories

Potatoes contain only 0.1% fat, which is something all satiating foods can't match. Each 148-gram potato produces only 100 calories. Compared to eating other foods, eating more potatoes every day can reduce fat intake and help metabolize excess fat.

Weight Loss Highlight 3: Nutrient-Rich Healthy Weight Loss Food

In addition to helping you lose weight, potatoes can also provide other vitamins and minerals needed by the human body. A 148-gram potato can provide 45% of the daily vitamin C requirement and 10% of the daily B6 requirement. Since they grow in the soil, potatoes naturally contain abundant minerals and can provide 21% of the potassium required daily by the human body and 6% of phosphorus. Even some American nutrition experts say that each meal consists of whole milk and potatoes, and you can get all the nutrients the human body needs.

Why do we gain weight? Because hunger drives us to keep eating. The dietary fiber contained in potatoes can give us a sense of fullness; carbohydrates can also provide us with energy. (Editor-in-Charge: Deng Qi)
