Will there be a Chrome OS phone this year?

by geekzhang on 2013-05-14 22:35:18

As the saying goes, "When the mountain rain is about to come, the wind fills the building." We are still savoring the flavors of last year's Google I/O, and this year’s event is just around the corner. What topics will Larry Page and Sergey Brin create this time?Last year’s Google I/O had two highlights worth revisiting: a new type of smart device called Google Glass, and Android 4.1 which brought significant design changes. To this day, Google Glass remains a highly discussed device, with mixed reviews from the public. However, for this year’s Google I/O, it is already an artifact of last year and cannot serve as the main focus again. Moreover, in the first half of this year, Google has continuously released updates on Google Glass, detailing its basic product form. Unless a second-generation product or a major technological breakthrough is announced, Google Glass is not well-suited to reprise its role as the star of this conference.As for the Android operating system, it naturally remains one of the main attractions at the conference. However, based on recent news, unless its version number advances from 4.x to 5.0, it won’t easily attract attention. In fact, after several years of development, smartphones have become commonplace, reducing their ability to generate buzz. From a product perspective, both Android's hardware and software have reached a high level of maturity, making it less likely for any dramatic surprises.So, what could be the next big surprise? It might be Chrome OS, a product that has consistently been undervalued.Looking back at the news over the past six months, there has been no shortage of developments related to Chrome, offering considerable room for imagination. For example:We can see that Google’s approach is similar to Apple’s, integrating the features of desktop and mobile operating systems to provide a unified user experience. However, unlike Apple, Chrome OS and Android are entirely different operating systems, differing significantly in design philosophy, user experience, and system kernel, making unification challenging. After all, Android was something Google acquired, while Chrome OS is its native creation.But with Andy Rubin no longer responsible for Android's development, it will be increasingly difficult to ensure its independence, and its future direction will gradually move away from Rubin's personal influence. Some believe that Android and Chrome OS will eventually merge. Given that Android already exists, there’s no need for Google to reinvent the wheel; they can simply refine the product gradually, transforming it into something more aligned with Google’s core values.Last year, Google introduced Chrome Packaged Apps, allowing web applications developed on Chrome to possess local characteristics, along with cross-platform development capabilities, simplicity in development, and ease of deployment. In other words, the network-based operating systems that Firefox OS and Ubuntu advocate, aiming for a unified desktop and mobile experience, are within the capabilities of Chrome OS.In reality, Rubin's successor, Sundar Pichai, has always been in charge of Chrome, and he will unify the experience between Android and Chrome. However, after five or six years of development, it would be impossible to completely change Android overnight, unless Android were scrapped and a new operating system developed from scratch. But Google has an advantage in that it doesn’t need to start over; it can simply take Chrome OS and adapt it for use on phones. Google certainly has the capability to do this; the only question is whether it will choose to do so.Perhaps, in the future, Android will become more like Chrome OS, using cloud and web applications to transform the internet into a large operating system. By then, it might be worth asking if Android should even be called a Chrome OS phone? Regardless, the trends surrounding Chrome OS at this year’s Google I/O are definitely worth keeping a close eye on.
