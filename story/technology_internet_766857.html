The Sense of Time in iOS 7 - How the Digital Age is Growing Old Slowly

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 22:18:11

The designer with nostalgic tendencies, Brendon Dawes, once sentimentally mentioned a tea stain on one of his booklets in his book *Analog In, Digital Out*, which explores the creativity of interaction design. The kind of accidental discovery that makes one sigh momentarily and lament the passage of time has become a swan song in the digital age. Digital files are always cheerfully fresh to look at; they do not dog-ear, yellow, or accumulate dust with the old-world charm.

Not aging is indeed a form of progress, yet it subtly deprives humans of one of their favorite melancholic pleasures. Likewise nostalgic, the designer Robert Hoekman slightly elaborates on this topic in his book *The Beauty of the Moment*, exploring how to display the sense of the passage of time, which only the physical world possesses, on digital web pages. He made an attempt by borrowing from Google Reader's tag cloud, using the size and color of fonts to represent the newness or oldness of articles in a list.

Robert Hoekman merely dabbled in these environmental cues showing the passage of time and also expressed doubts about the significant effects produced by such designs. To be honest, this is actually a relatively elegant but useless design. Therefore, it is no wonder that almost no websites or digital products make substantial efforts for humanity's elegantly useless nostalgia. Especially in today's purpose-driven modern people, indulging in nostalgia seems luxuriously superfluous.

A sudden change occurred after I sent my first text message upon updating to iOS 7.

The color of the text messages gradually changes from deep blue to light blue over time, like an echo fading as it travels back and forth between two walls. Although this is helpful in distinguishing importance, the sense of time created intentionally or unintentionally (I tend to think it’s intentional) far outweighs its minor functionality for me. Whether it's useful or not, I feel good.

From this perspective, the entire iOS 7 is like a moment in people's growth when childhood suddenly ends after a long period, and time begins to pass swiftly, making the act of reminiscing commonplace.

Firstly, the representative of time—the clock—has changed. The clock in iOS 7 no longer remains eternally frozen at 10:20, the release time of the first generation iPhone, but starts rotating without pause.

Next, in the calendar and photo album sections, iOS 7 provides users with the opportunity to trace back and reminisce.

Before iOS 7, the photo album already had clear markers for location, but it handled the time dimension rather sloppily. Photos only existed in sequential order, and their exact shooting times could not be obtained before importing them into a computer. Compared to iOS 6, the photo album in iOS 7 truly shines. The time dimension is divided into three levels: Moments - Collections - Years, closely integrated with the spatial dimension of geographical locations. For the representation of the time dimension, iOS 7 draws inspiration from spatial positioning methods, similar to looking down from above on Google satellite maps, zooming from a complete Earth to your residential neighborhood. This is the design logic running through iOS 7.

The provision of this macro perspective, along with the process of switching between macro and micro perspectives, consciously or unconsciously triggers the floodgates of memory, especially since photos themselves are carriers of memories. Just like realizing how fast time passes during year-end summaries, every switch between years and moments emphasizes that these photos are evidence of the passage of time, while also enhancing feelings of reminiscence and nostalgia.

To preserve the integrity of this process, iOS 7 even sacrifices some task-completion shortcuts. This is where some users have complaints. For example, switching between Years - Collections - Moments - Collections - Years in the photo album must be done level by level. Similarly, after opening an app from a folder and pressing the Home button, you still need to return to the folder interface first, then exit to the main screen by tapping blank areas or pressing the Home button again.

Lewis Mumford said in *Technics and Civilization*, "The key machine of the modern industrial era is not the steam engine, but the clock." The clock helped establish the existence of mechanical time, which "unfolds as a series of mathematically isolated moments," "that can move forward or backward like the hands of a clock or individual frames of animation." On the other hand, human, organic time is different; it "can only move in one direction, going through the cycle of birth, growth, development, aging, and death. The past that has died is relative to the future that has not yet been born, and we always face the present."

In the digital age we are currently in, mechanical time still exists, while organic time has been diluted. The effort iOS 7 puts into creating a sense of time provides a possibility for reviving organic time in the digital age. It wants to awaken our nostalgic nerve. Although it feels a bit like a heartless robot trying hard to say 'I love you'—stiff though it may be, it's quite touching.
