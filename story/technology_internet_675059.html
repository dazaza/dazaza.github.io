One-year-old child does double eyelid surgery, "beauty" starts from infancy

by childrenstory126 on 2012-02-24 17:36:30

Yesterday, at the plastic surgery department of the city's emergency center, a beauty-conscious mother, fearing that her daughter would lose in the looks department right from the starting line, actually brought her one-year-old daughter to the hospital for double eyelid surgery. The doctor flatly rejected and criticized the mother's actions. Experts say that plastic surgery must conform to the objective laws of human physiology, and is generally only performed when the body has fully developed. Moreover, plastic surgery reflects the aesthetic awareness of the individual, and even parents have no right to impose their own aesthetic concepts on their children.

When history entered the 21st century, times changed, and people's thoughts changed accordingly. It became fashionable for three-year-olds to study economics, two-year-olds to communicate bilingually, and one-year-olds to learn dancing to music. Despite experts repeatedly exposing the proposition that "children cannot lose at the starting line" as a false advertising gimmick by merchants, parents stubbornly cling to it as gospel, treating their child's future as the ultimate interest, blindly sacrificing other things in its name, leading to one bizarre "starting line" drama after another.

There is no limit to how bizarre these scenarios can get. If parents enrolling their toddlers in early education classes or extracurricular activities out of fear of them "losing at the starting line" still reveals some current educational inequalities and resource allocation issues, then this incident of a mother bringing her one-year-old baby for double eyelid surgery shows us another interpretation of "cannot let children lose at the starting line."

Some say that men conquer the world to win women, while women conquer men to conquer the world. I suspect this parent deeply understands this saying and has personal experiences related to it. According to reports, Ms. Xiao, 30 years old, lives in a high-end neighborhood in Yubei. As a true beauty herself, she participated in multiple beauty pageants and worked as a model for fashion magazines during her university days and after entering the workforce. After marrying two years ago, she became a full-time housewife, devoting herself to family life. Interestingly, her own beauty was also the result of cosmetic surgery: according to Director Wu Yi, around seven or eight years ago when she was still in university, Ms. Xiao underwent rhinoplasty at this department. Later, she had five or six additional surgeries including cheekbone reduction and liposuction. It was precisely because of Ms. Xiao's obsession with plastic surgery that led her to consider having double eyelid surgery for her baby still in swaddling clothes.

Having conquered a wealthy man with her beauty, living in a high-end neighborhood, and achieving a high-end lifestyle, it’s unsurprising that she insists on performing cosmetic surgery on her one-year-old daughter. This undoubtedly adds a new dimension of reflection to our already restless and confused society: previously, "knowledge is power," but now does "beauty is power" hold true?

The decision to perform cosmetic surgery on a newborn due to the belief that "children cannot lose at the starting line" stems not only from the mother's personal reasons but also societal influences. Many parents emphasize preschool education and strive to provide their children with the best educational resources out of a desire for knowledge and to lay a foundation for their children's future ability to compete in society. However, harsh realities tell them that women find it difficult to realize good dreams through knowledge or ability alone. Nowadays, the prevailing trend of "it's better to marry well than to study well," various beauty pageants, and the fiercely competitive art exams all stimulate the emotions and thinking of parents. Additionally, universities offering "ladylike classes" aimed at turning female students into rich wives or even mistresses further reinforce the idea among perplexed parents that "beauty is power." Consequently, they believe that a girl's future and happiness depend on her physical appearance!

Nowadays, urban families usually have only one child, and the pressure of "not being able to afford losing" follows them everywhere. Children not only bear the hope of becoming successful but also carry the future of the entire family. How can we help parents who are already overwhelmed by fierce social competition? I think this is a question that requires deep reflection from the entire society!
