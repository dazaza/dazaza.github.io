"Silent as the Sea": Here, no one realizes, who has fallen in love with whom. People with insight on Tianya Blog know.

by bhjdfghjsf41 on 2012-02-09 21:19:11

Music. Countryside. Seaside towns. Those buildings with a bit of history, and the gravel roads. Bicycles, grasslands, stones by the roadside, and occasionally swaying trees. In the French film "As Quiet as the Sea" directed by Jean-Pierre Melville, there is a moment when you want to forget about the war. Although the military vehicles coming and going, German soldiers in uniform, guns, and even a few arrests that occur all remind you that it is wartime, specifically the period when Germany occupied France during World War II, you really want to forget that the movie ultimately portrays war.

The quiet style of the film conceals something akin to thunderous currents within; deep, calm, and almost imperceptible emotions flow beneath the surface, carrying the wounds of war. The cruelest reality is wanting to love but being unable to, wanting to escape from war yet constantly floating in its oppressive atmosphere. It's truly tormenting.

A few days ago, I received a postcard sent by a friend from Xitang. Light ink, like traces. The film is beautiful; every frame of "As Quiet as the Sea" resembles such a postcard. Japan's "5 Centimeters Per Second" also seems to have this effect, except that each still frame from "5 Centimeters Per Second" can be used as a computer wallpaper for display and appreciation, while every still frame from "As Quiet as the Sea" should be kept in one’s heart or used as a bookmark, tucked away in the pages of time, becoming the wind chimes in the rain.

It's hard to find beauty like Nianna's anymore, like daisies in the wind. When she pedals her bicycle through the quiet countryside and the not-so-quiet small town, this beauty settles into a specific era. There is a similar feeling in "Lovers of Lushan Mountain," only Nianna has more freshness and naturalness compared to Zhouyun. This kind of freshness and naturalness seem to be born only in a naturally romantic country like France.

Did Nianna fall in love with Varnay? I've never seen anyone like Varnay. He himself is a German officer, representing invasion and destruction. Ordered to live in Nianna's home, he is courteous, trying his best not to disturb Nianna and her grandfather's lives. He says goodnight every time he returns home, even more gentlemanly than most. Varnay is handsome, was a musician before joining the army, and shares the same favorite piece of music as Nianna. If placed in a normal era, they would probably fall in love at first sight. However, the war artificially places them on opposing sides.

From the day Varnay moved into Nianna's house until the day he left, over several months, Nianna never spoke a word to Varnay. Most of the time, Varnay returned from the military camp each evening and delivered profound monologues to himself. He spoke alone while Nianna and her grandfather remained silent. Under the lens of the cinematographer, the three formed a triangular relationship with aesthetic significance. The fireplace burned brightly during winter nights between this triangle. Varnay, who loves music, was forcibly conscripted, while Nianna and her grandfather harbored the pain of their invaded homeland deep in their hearts. Deep down, all three were cold, yet in a strange situation, there emerged a mutual warmth where only Varnay spoke.

This kind of warmth is a pitiful spark and light created by the friction of their gazes amidst an uncontrollable tide of events.

They did fall in love. When Nianna saved Varnay's life with her piano playing, they had already deeply fallen in love. But such love might not even have the courage to be acknowledged by Nianna and Varnay themselves. In the 88-minute film, Nianna only spoke two words to Varnay. This happened when Varnay was about to be transferred to the Russian battlefield. In a hurried farewell, Nianna walked out of the house, approached Varnay who was already seated in the car, and whispered with tears held back: "Goodbye..." Hearing these two words, Varnay smiled at Nianna. For them, a simple "goodbye" was enough. Everything was contained in this brief phrase—love or inability to love, hope or despair, goodbye or perhaps never meeting again.

Quiet music, frame after frame of quiet images. Nianna, Varnay, and an old man.

Suddenly, I thought of a song by TWINS.

"Winter farewells / In the deep night of Moscow / A series of armies / Strictly guarded on the streets / No one here notices / Who has fallen in love with whom / Because suffering does not allow people to collapse"

And after Varnay left, the pot of geraniums on Nianna's windowsill symbolized resistance.
