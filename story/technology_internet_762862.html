Dianping: A Bone Too Tough for Giants to Crunch, Living Outside the Comfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:45:53

CEO of Dianping.com, Zhang Tao

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com, has postponed the company's IPO once again.

In 2011, Dianping secured a $100 million Series C round from Sequoia Capital and others, valuing the company at $1 billion. According to convention, it should have gone public within the next one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in its Series D round.

Given Dianping's scale and development trajectory, going public would not be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that an IPO now would be too costly. He deconstructs the big internet (PC + mobile) into four elements - people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point offers opportunities for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is where the fourth giant could arise. Since 2003, Dianping has been working hard to accumulate data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, which merges "location" and "service." Following Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting for the image of the fourth giant than Dianping.

But outsiders don't see it that way. In recent months, with BAT's frequent acquisition strategies, Dianping has become a presumed acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; regardless of which family name it adopts from BAT, it would help them achieve great success early on. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it's too early to predict whether it can become a giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, all are targeting Dianping's territory - local life services centered around dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two things, Dianping's IPO would equate to a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs time to prove that what Dianping does now, BAT cannot do. Second, Dianping is about to enter the final battle of group buying, and if it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." For all the speculation and rumors, Zhang Tao turns a deaf ear. He is very clear: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes potentially acquired companies into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is better suited to develop within the ecosystem of a giant, such as maps and email. These products can easily attract large numbers of users but struggle to find independent business models. Others, like browsers, may make money, but the revenue from navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money maintaining an email product because it helps with branding and user stickiness, but I've never heard of a successful standalone email company." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type involves companies facing financial difficulties selling out to survive.

Now, Meituan has been brought under Alibaba's wing. In theory, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated by its competitor's rapid expansion fueled by capital. However, Zhang Tao says, "The giants offer us limited help. We do need users, but execution is even more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities and might even marginalize us within a large company." Furthermore, he aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. If Dianping gets too close to any one of them, it would be equivalent to "choosing sides," making it an enemy of the others.

Thus, logically speaking, acquiring Dianping becomes a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why should we take their money? We're not short of funds. What we need are resources. If a giant is willing to invest a small amount and give us many resources, I might consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only hold a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Hard Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for companies doing pure internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities come from those combining points with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that lifestyle services mainly revolve around dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure. Other niche markets like ride-hailing can form decent business models, but there's little chance for any of them to grow into giants.

For dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure, user demand is simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of lifestyle services, precisely where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, later entrants find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship contention phase, and Dianping plans to battle Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as two strong players coexist, the industry's gross margin will be pulled down, unable to form stable profitability.

Another visible future lies in reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but they must be built on the foundation of Dianping having absolute advantages in information and discounts; otherwise, building a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began trying out reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Both users and merchants have given good feedback so far, but if we were to do reservations alone, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, lifestyle services also present some heavier opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid demand for users, and merchants can increase sales. In the past few years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet" have flourished. However, the core of food delivery is delivery, meaning it must be delivered within a very short time frame, and issues like soup spillage must be resolved. The logistics requirements are even higher than those for fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If executed well, food delivery still has opportunities, but it's harder dirty work, and what Dianping is doing now is already dirty and exhausting enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, which Zhang Tao refers to as "dirty and hard work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but past pain has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, aside from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the lifestyle services field.

However, he still needs to be wary of BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct; Baidu Maps has over 100 million users, allowing users to search for nearby dining and other lifestyle services using maps. Tencent, considered to have secured its ticket to mobile internet, has launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest physical goods trading platform, and expanding into services naturally follows. Besides, it has included Meituan and AutoNavi into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there are no signs of explosive growth in reality and data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users think of first when they want to dine, drink, entertain, or relax.

Moreover, in the vast field of lifestyle services, competition among giants will certainly intersect; no one can completely monopolize the field. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that this makes Dianping safer instead, "In细分fields like car rental and business travel, the massive user base directed by giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in AutoNavi, invading Dianping's territory won't be that simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, no matter how peculiar, they can always be categorized. A user's dining experience depends on various factors such as the environment on the day, waitstaff, and chefs. Managing merchants cannot be done using systems and tools like Alibaba; it requires a 'ground force' managed by 'people.'

"The three giants all want to go down this path, and there is room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing lifestyle services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, which BAT lacks the genes and capability to manage offline teams; second, they lack the willingness. Lifestyle services require focus and cannot be accomplished by just dispatching a small team, and the profits are slow. "A company finds it hard to excel in two important things simultaneously, let alone they need to consolidate their advantages in their original fields."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Are Easier to Maneuver

In 2011, when Dianping secured a $100 million financing round, Zhang Tao told the world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogies fell from grace.

He wasn't overly anxious about it. "They didn't become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they all want to be America's Dianping."

The current path was gradually explored by Zhang Tao himself. When he felt that selling keyword ads and discount coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform, one deal per day, similar to the Americans. But as orders increased, he realized that group buying had the characteristics of a trading platform. Thus, he saw the future of Dianping as a trading platform for lifestyle services. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were used for Dianping's transformation from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this "gene mutation," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth seat among the giants today. But gene mutation sounds simple, in reality, it's akin to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, an extremely painful process.

First, compared to selling ads individually, the gross margin of a trading platform decreases, requiring stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, a trading platform demands higher cross-departmental communication skills.

In order to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and sales team were completely separated. Zhang Tao even deliberately set up a "firewall" between departments - even for important clients, the information on the platform couldn't be modified for sales purposes, which once led to mutual distrust between the content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication to achieve a good marketing plan. Planning is one thing, but the editor's cooperation is essential. He said, "Maintaining information quality remains important, but there's no need to reinforce animosity anymore; we need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors can accept this logically, individuals' behaviors and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, the inertia becomes even greater. To address this, Zhang Tao specifically held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. After a few months, an editorial manager casually told a sales manager, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also underwent gene mutation. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team a dual identity. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants, not only selling products but also helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants are the company's procurement agents. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety of goods must also be abundant. Marketing consultants should aim to help users procure group-buying products. Of course, dual identities will inevitably lead to a series of complex issues. Frontline personnel may find it quite headache-inducing, having to balance the scale themselves.

In substance, Dianping's "dual identity" is very similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to the front lines allows the company to respond faster, but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffered more after the gene mutation.

Each year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, then senior executives further break them down layer by layer. However, breaking down isn't simple arithmetic. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds that unmet merchant demands stem from sales issues, but affect overall performance. Therefore, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists who gather information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change, and everyone feels uncomfortable when faced with it. All employees must go through a period of discomfort, including myself."

Numbers reflect the "weight" of Dianping's execution team. Out of the current 3,000+ employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground force is responsible for directly communicating with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group-buying, and integrated marketing solutions.

For this core team that resists BAT's reach, Dianping has a unique management and evaluation system.

They aren't called "sales"; from the first day, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the era of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging at least three to five years behind Taobao merchants in terms of internet knowledge. Marketing planning consultants must tell merchants what online users need and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing consultants wouldn't recommend a group-buying package for abalone or shark fin because online users and offline users have different needs."

An outstanding marketing consultant must be a versatile player. They need to explain to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the fundamental differences between discounts and group-buying and determine which discount aligns better with their positioning. If dealing with a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant must know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products have rising costs during certain seasons, and which have falling costs, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users prefer.

This places high demands on marketing consultants, requiring them to be not only internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every consultant to be a double expert, so we've set up star ratings." Similar to hotels, marketing consultants range from one-star to five-star, with the best being called Super Star.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping established a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and relevant cases that both new and experienced employees can browse. If encountering a new issue, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, and thousands of consulting advisors nationwide will help answer them.

A large number of knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experience. Dianping set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base; employees who help others the most each quarter receive rewards. Every quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, with results directly linked to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to acquire than internet knowledge. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after decades of trial and error in the industry. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that hair salons should avoid offering wash-cut-blow packages because men lack motivation to buy, while women mostly blow-dry their hair without needing a full wash-cut-blow service. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never have thought of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are not only divided by city but also by industry, such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is even subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with a marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, ensuring merchants don't resist when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing session: what difficulties a certain type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, what solutions the company provides, and what effects they achieve. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case per week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition - Entrepreneurial Winner. Within the first three months of joining, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products, but listening to merchants share their experiences. If responsible for a hot pot restaurant, after three months, they must submit a commercial plan PPT titled "If I Were the Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees defend their presentations on-site, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, with the company providing appropriate rewards.

From a strategic perspective, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, what they need to do is quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with basic industry knowledge, and their words sound alike. "The era when telling stories and creating concepts could lead to success is gone. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
