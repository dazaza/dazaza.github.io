American universities are not the only bridge for studying abroad.

by ab8v34t565 on 2012-03-05 21:43:25

With the opening of the U.S. study abroad market, more and more universities are accepting applications from students without language test scores, including some universities ranked in the top 100 in the United States. This policy is undoubtedly good news for students who don't have time to prepare for exams like TOEFL, SAT, or IELTS. However, as a result, many students place all their hopes on English courses in the U.S. and abandon English learning and preparation at home, which can be quite dangerous.

This public comprehensive university located in Dickinson, North Dakota, in the central northern part of the United States, has had issues with the files of 743 international students participating in its international program since 2003. Among the 410 students granted joint degrees, only 10 completed all required courses; among the 120 currently enrolled students, only 39 finished all courses and were eligible for a degree; and among the 584 students, most had previously obtained other certificates or degrees.

Nowadays, studying abroad is not difficult, nor is gaining admission to foreign universities. But we must remind our students and parents that truly good universities, world-class institutions, maintain high admission thresholds, such as requiring high language scores and good high school performance.

Not long ago, I was invited to attend an event organized by the Cultural and Educational Affairs Office of the U.S. Consulate General in Beijing. When I asked officials from the University of California, Berkeley, if they had any joint programs in China, they firmly replied no. Truly top-tier American universities generally do not offer joint programs because they maintain very high admission standards.

Last Tuesday (April 14), after the "Study Abroad Industry Index Survey" was launched in our newspaper's "Study Weekly," it received significant attention from many prospective international students and their families. From the feedback forms, it is clear that the U.S. remains the top destination for aspiring international students. Regarding concerns, questions such as how to choose the right American university and major, how to apply to suitable American universities, what conditions and qualities are necessary for studying in the U.S., how much does it cost to study in the U.S., and which universities and majors offer higher scholarships have been raised by parents and prospective students. Therefore, this follow-up report on the "Study Abroad Industry Index Survey" will analyze studying in the U.S. and provide an in-depth interpretation of American education.

Recently, the exposure of the diploma mill scandal at Dickinson State University in the U.S. has shocked many prospective international students and their families planning to study in the U.S. In this incident, the school administration bears undeniable responsibility, but the underperforming international students who failed to meet academic requirements also cannot escape blame. Currently, relevant officials from the Ministry of Education have requested our educational office at the Chinese Embassy in the U.S. to investigate the situation.

Warm Reminder:

B Expert Interview

Guests: Huang Yongju, Director of the U.S. Program at Hua Ying Study Abroad; Yu Linquan, Project Director of the International Department at Shu De High School:

It is unnecessary to rush into studying in the U.S.

Parents and students should consider not only the admission requirements, majors, scholarships, and geographical locations when choosing U.S. universities but also verify the list of universities recognized by the Chinese Ministry of Education’s Overseas Study Supervision Network. Don’t blindly choose schools just because they promise quick processing times or lower costs.

Such situations in U.S. universities are inevitable. The number of Chinese students applying to U.S. universities doubles every year. In fact, since the financial crisis, U.S. universities have seen two trends: one is that top schools are becoming increasingly harder to get into, with the acceptance rate of international students at the top 100 U.S. universities dropping from 20% to 10%, raising the threshold continuously; the second is that lower-ranked U.S. universities, for economic benefits, keep lowering their admission standards to attract students.

A Event Recap

Recently, the "diploma factory" scandal at Dickinson State University in the U.S. has come to light.

Who could believe that a legitimate U.S. university would issue graduation certificates to underperforming Chinese international students? This seems to go beyond the traditional concept of a "diploma mill."

According to statistics, about 95% of the unqualified students are from China. Dickinson State University has signed cooperation agreements with several schools in China. The exposure of this scandal has shocked many prospective international students and their families planning to study in the U.S. Currently, relevant officials from the Ministry of Education have requested our educational office at the Chinese Embassy in the U.S. to investigate the situation. The Chinese Embassy's educational office has already contacted the school administration and relevant Chinese students to understand the situation and ensure the legitimate rights of Chinese students.

For example, there are over 3,000 universities in the U.S. alone. We recommend parents refer more to the USNEWS magazine rankings in the U.S. Generally, universities ranked in the top 100 are all excellent choices.
