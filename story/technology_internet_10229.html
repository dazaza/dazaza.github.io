Expression sparks the last round of technical debates this year.

by dexter_yy on 2006-12-09 09:06:57

Last month, Microsoft started showing off the legendary Expression. This product holds strategic significance for Microsoft and is much more important than IE7. To understand its importance, you first need to understand Microsoft. What Microsoft values most isn't necessarily the end-user experience, even though this year you've seen them tirelessly launching IE7, Office 2007, and Vista... as if they were busy catering to the needs of the general public. What Microsoft values most are developers. In recent years, despite lagging behind on the Internet and stagnating with their main software products, what has allowed Microsoft to remain at the top of the industry? .NET, Visual Studio, and MSDN—these are the three "sacred treasures" that Microsoft relies on to survive. As long as they can capture a large number of developers with these tools and dominate the technical market, they can continue to maintain their monopoly. Simply put, Expression is: the successor to FrontPage, Microsoft's new "three musketeers," used to counter Adobe's trio. If Microsoft can defeat Adobe and win over web front-end and RIA developers, adding the followers of the old trio, Microsoft could then unify the world. In fact, World War II in web development technology has already begun. Looking back at this year, most people have been frantically learning Ajax, while traditional Flash developers have been eagerly studying Flex 2 and ActionScript 3. Of course, some have defected to other camps. These two major combatants share many commonalities: both are rich client (RIA) technologies, both emphasize cross-platform compatibility, and both are essentially XML + scripting languages. The difference is that the former enjoys massive support from open standards and open-source communities, making it fast and efficient, while the latter benefits from comprehensive development tools, support from top-tier commercial companies, and is easier to understand and get started with. Although Microsoft's Expression is a late entrant into the fray, if it really gains momentum, we may see a battle between three forces, akin to those commonly seen in Blizzard games, as early as next year or the year after. While this might confuse many people, once you think about it, you'll find that getting involved in—or even just observing—the chaos can be quite interesting...
