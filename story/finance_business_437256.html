Different beers have different temperaments and different ways of enjoying them. This article explores several postures of drinking beer.

In recent years, fashion-conscious urbanites have flocked to the embrace of red wine and Italian coffee, leaving beer somewhat neglected.

While you may be aware of the basic knowledge required to appreciate red wine and the steps necessary to brew a good cup of coffee, have you ever considered that pouring beer also requires technique, and that there are standards for consuming beer?

For the Nordic people, beer is equivalent to the position of wine in the dietary habits of southern Europeans, an essential beverage in daily life. Wine is more common in southern European countries, as these regions are major grape-producing areas. The climate in northern European countries is unsuitable for growing grapes but is conducive to growing barley, which is the basic material for brewing beer, hence the prominence of beer culture in these countries. Take Belgium as an example; its history of beer brewing can be traced back to the 13th century. This country has 150 beer breweries and offers over 800 varieties of beer flavors.

Different brewing methods and materials give each beer a unique taste and aroma. Based on production methods, beer can be divided into two main categories: mass-produced and specially crafted.

Mass-produced beer, in terms of ingredient selection, not only follows traditional recipes but also uses other substitutes to simplify the brewing process and save production costs. However, we cannot say that mass-produced beer is necessarily inferior; the taste will definitely differ.

In ancient times, food storage equipment was not as advanced as it is today. To avoid wasting grain, people brewed barley into beer for longer preservation. Beer was then a significant source of nutrition for people. Nowadays, people drink beer more as a leisure habit. When it comes to tasting beer, Europeans are much more particular than Singaporeans.

The way Singaporeans drink beer is very similar to that of Australians and Londoners. Their requirements for beer are simple: as long as it's icy cold, it's good enough. However, the way true Europeans enjoy their beer is highly particular. They require a layer of foam about two fingers wide on top of their beer for optimal taste. It’s similar to drinking Irish coffee; we don't break up the foam on top of the Irish coffee but let the coffee pass through the foam and into our mouths, where the foam softens the texture of the coffee and enhances its aroma.

Beer vs. Glassware

Just like with wine, every type of beer has a specifically designed glass. Belgian beers from the same manufacturer, Interbrew, such as Stella Artois, Hoegaarden, and Leffe, generally come with designated glasses. Manufacturers believe that each beer has a distinct personality, and different glass designs help better bring out their flavors.

Quality Beer

How do you identify quality beer? Its color should match its variety, free of sediment, clear in appearance, with abundant foam that rises quickly. Gently shake the glass, take a deep breath inside the cup, and you'll smell the hop aroma. A good beer tastes clean without any off-flavors.

Beer vs. Cuisine

Which beers pair best with which foods? You need to understand the general flavor profiles of beer to make decisions.

Hoegaarden, popular among women for its rich antioxidant beauty-enhancing properties, has a refreshing fruity aroma that pairs best with sour, spicy, fried foods, reducing the guilty feeling after indulgence. Stella Artois, Belgium's best-selling beer, has a light taste with a hint of bitterness, making it ideal for pairing with bland or chicken and seafood dishes. Leffe, once exclusively brewed by monks, has the strongest flavor profile, with a full-bodied texture and a subtle apple aroma, pairing well with sweets or richer dishes.

Belgians believe that the deliciousness of beer is closely related to the pouring process, so they have followed a set method for a long time.

a) To ensure the purity of the beer, the first step is to thoroughly clean the beer glass. There is a specific way to clean it.

After washing with soapy water, rinse again with clean water. Pay attention to how you hold the glass; your fingers should only touch the lower half of the glass to ensure cleanliness and hygiene.

b) Beer must be ice-cold to taste good. Before pouring, chill the glass with ice water.

c) Before pouring the beer, make sure to release any beer remaining in the tap (which has been exposed to air and contaminated). While pouring, avoid letting the tap touch the glass.

d) After filling the glass, use a knife to cut off any overflowing foam.

e) Rinse the outer layer of the glass with water.

f) Dry the bottom of the glass with a sponge.

g) When presenting the beer to guests, remember to smile politely. End.

When pouring beer from a bottle, follow the above steps. Additionally, do not pour all the beer at once. When the beer in the bottle is less than half, shake the bottle to mix the sediment before pouring it into the glass.
