Our children's clothing store (10) ----- bargaining vs. non-negotiable prices (repost)

by tonzhuanyin0y on 2012-03-01 01:33:13

My wife is not good at bargaining with people or playing psychological games. Not long after opening the store, she decided to adopt a "fixed-price" policy, clearly marking the price on all goods and then posting a notice: "Our prices are firm, no haggling allowed. If you wish to bargain, please don't bother." It was an attitude that seemed to keep people at arm's length! As regular customers increased and relationships deepened, sometimes customers would ask for discounts or rounding off the bill. Out of politeness, my wife had to yield repeatedly. Eventually, the four words "firm price, no negotiation" became empty talk. Of course, I am very supportive of her fixed-price policy. However, for a small roadside shop, if you want to truly implement a "firm price" and gain the understanding and support of customers, it is extremely difficult. After all, whoever it is, everyone has the mentality of wanting a bargain.

Let's take a look at what kind of stores in the actual market can achieve "firm price, no negotiation": branded specialty stores! Large malls! Why can these large retailers offer fixed prices? Their employees have no authority to offer discounts, and the bosses who do have the authority to offer discounts are someone we can never reach. And as consumers, it's hard for us to establish solid emotional connections with their sales staff. The operation of specialty stores doesn't rely on maintaining emotional ties with customers; instead, they establish a series of rules and regulations to standardize the operations of each store. From top to bottom, everyone follows the procedures. You get the discount according to the store's regulations based on how much you buy, not something that can be promised to you because of personal feelings. Therefore, even if we have a friendly relationship with a certain store clerk, we cannot obtain additional "bargaining rights," let alone the fact that, like soldiers in a camp, store clerks come and go. Who knows when the clerk you know might be transferred or quit?

In contrast, our small business shops start from the very beginning with the owner personally taking charge, using our warm and friendly relationships to drive sales and encourage customers to return regularly. Over time, as regular customers increase, our relationships become more solid, and customers inevitably develop the mentality of "since I come often and give face, give me a discount." If they are refused, out of embarrassment, they may never set foot in your store again. After all, our goods are sourced from wholesale markets, especially in situations where we cannot control the supply, why should customers necessarily come to our store? Isn't it because the boss can talk well and understands others' feelings? If such a small favor isn't granted, paraphrasing a saying: "Who do you think you are?"

Therefore, bargaining is human nature, and there's absolutely no need for us to regard customers' haggling as a provocation and dwell on it. Even though my wife prominently displayed a sign in the store announcing the fixed price, customers still didn't comply: "So expensive? What brand is this? Is this a specialty store? No bargaining? They can bargain at department stores too! Aren't these just prices you made up yourself?" Every time my wife mentioned customers' lack of understanding, she would sigh. In the early years, she would often argue with customers over this matter, only to end up being criticized for having a bad attitude, and she could only come home and cry to me about her grievances.

Thus, my wife once wavered, thinking maybe they shouldn't label prices anymore and hide the wholesale price on the tag, casually quoting a price. But what happens when there are many customers? How does one deal with so much haggling? Besides, children's clothing is full of trivial items; what exorbitant price can you quote? For a piece of clothing bought at 29 yuan, would you dare to arbitrarily mark it up to 109 yuan? Scaring away customers would be surprising! Children's clothing isn't a highly profitable business; wholesale markets only allow for so much profit margin. Children's clothes generally cost just a few dozen yuan, so what kind of price can you offer?

After many years, through continuous psychological adjustment and experience accumulation, my wife learned many subtleties about pricing. In previous years, all our goods were priced after purchase based on the procurement price, setting a retail price ourselves. Generally, new customers wouldn't negotiate prices or would get a 90% discount; regular customers would usually get an 85% discount, with bulk purchases possibly getting rounded down or a small pair of socks or trinket as a token of goodwill. But if a customer opened negotiations, my wife would definitely decline! Her implicit meaning was: giving a discount is fine, but it's not up to the customer to decide the amount. For example, she might say to a customer: "No problem, even though it's your first time here, I'll treat you as a regular customer and give you an 85% discount, but you must pay the exact change!" Customers will definitely agree. When dealing with new customers, my wife generally enforces the principle of "no bargaining" strictly. "You must eliminate any notion of bargaining in customers' minds, otherwise, you'll never recover. It's better to stubbornly hold on at the start, and when customers become familiar, gradually lower the discount." This trick, in my opinion, is worth considering. However, the process of holding on might be very challenging.

For long-term patrons, my wife's rewards are rich and varied. On Children's Day, store anniversaries, Spring Festival, or even when a child gets excellent grades, my wife prepares gifts and sends them promptly. My wife is a very generous person; as long as customers understand and support her, she's willing to give her heart away. She doesn't forget online shopping either; seeing something nice or fun, she impulsively buys it for the children and mothers at the store. Most memorable are the expensive Transformers she gave out a couple of years ago, purchased at a cost of 59 yuan per unit! She got ten units back, attracting kids so much that they insisted their moms buy clothes! The moms came back telling us that outside stores sold them for over 300 yuan! Of course, my wife clearly knows how to use these ten Transformers to secure substantial business. (Regarding what kind of gifts we should choose and when to give them, my wife is quite particular, and we will write another article about it later.)

To cut to the chase, in recent years, customers generally felt that discounts of 90% and 85% were too little. This prompted my wife to carry out "pricing reforms" in the second half of 2009 on the most fashionable medium and large-sized girl's clothing series in the store. Instead of self-determined prices followed by discounts, she switched to direct tag price discounts. We all know that the prices marked on children's clothing tags are usually quite high, so we took advantage of these high prices to offer relatively low discounts. When we purchase goods, branded children's clothing prices are already discounted based on the tag price, so why should we set a low price ourselves and then offer discounts? We simply offer discounts based on the unified retail price on the tag, which significantly reduces the workload. In fact, observations show that customers don't care about the final price they pay; they care more about how much discount they receive, or rather, how much they perceive themselves saving. Thus, although the surface discount appears lower, the actual price remains unchanged. The effect, however, is entirely different.

The new discount method quickly gained the understanding and support of customers, bringing many benefits! There's no need to painstakingly calculate after purchasing goods, laboriously marking each item individually, and ultimately receiving complaints despite the effort. Now, I see her handling things much more easily; she hands the goods over to the store clerk and goes home. Customers don't care about your basis for discounting; hearing that the discount is fairly low, and with the possibility of rounding down, they feel satisfied and pay more willingly. In the end, the amount customers pay is the same as before, or even slightly higher in some cases. Customers are happy, the shopkeeper is happy, achieving a win-win situation! Why not do it? Previously, arguing with customers until faces turned red was unnecessary. Customers shop for peace of mind, right? Why can't we satisfy their vanity? By making them happy, they willingly open their wallets, handing over reasonable profits with joy, and next time they'll remember your kindness. The real price is still the real price; it's just presented differently.

A couple of days ago, before going to bed, my wife stared at the ceiling and suddenly said she had thought of a new measure that would leave customers without a reason to haggle. But immediately issued an order, stating that it was her insight from ten years of operation, and she wasn't ready to reveal it online yet. She would wait until she successfully practiced it before sharing! Following her command, we respect her "intellectual property" and temporarily keep her secret. In the future, if our leader successfully practices it, I will certainly announce it to the world. Let's look forward to it!

As my wife says, persistence is victory! To defend her "firm price, no negotiation," my wife racked her brains, shed countless tears, and poured endless grievances into my ears. But for ten years, she clenched her fists, gritted her teeth, and persevered. Now, regular customers generally no longer haggle endlessly. When encountering new customers, my wife seriously explains: "We've been operating here for ten years, no bargaining!" If she encounters unreasonable customers who randomly slash prices, my wife absolutely retaliates without hesitation. I once tentatively asked her, aren't you offending some customers? Unexpectedly, our leader showed utter disdain and confidently argued: "If a customer thinks our children's clothing is expensive, it simply means she's not our customer. Go ahead, it's not regrettable! Frankly speaking, we don't have a price advantage, our rent is higher than others, and we get goods at the same discount as everyone else..." "Our business strategy isn't about competing on price. I'm confident in our products and prices. I haven't made immoral money. All my profits are extremely reasonable, there's no hint of excessive profit. Even if customers check other places and come back asking me to lower my price, I still refuse. I run a store to make a profit, not to engage in vicious competition. If you don't buy mine, that's fine, you can buy elsewhere, I have no objections, I won't scold you. But if you expect to buy my stuff at the cheap price of others and deliberately disparage my products, that's disrespect to me! I don't have to be polite to her!"

Thus, I don't know from which day it started, but I suddenly noticed a new sign in the store: "You can choose not to buy! But please do not haggle! Understanding forever!" Especially the four words "you can choose not to buy" stood out prominently! Ha, our leader's stubbornness really makes me sweat profusely! For this "firm price, no negotiation" principle, our leader has really racked her brain.

Below is a dialogue between my wife and a customer recorded from one day last month:

Customer: How much is this sweater? (Priced at 89 yuan)

Wife: (Pretending to check) Oh, this special price is 89.

Customer: So expensive? And it's still a special price!

Wife: Oh yes, our original price hasn't been removed yet, see it here (pointing to the tag), yesterday if you came, we sold it for 159, even our regular customers with 85% discount would still pay 135 yuan. Today, I just adjusted the price since only one piece is left. If your child fits this size, it's a great deal! Only 89, don't mention transportation costs, buying stock wouldn't cost less!

Customer: Are there any new ones?

Wife: Good grief, if I had new ones, how could I sell them at this price? Wholesale markets have sold out. If tomorrow I find stock at the wholesale market, I'll sell it at the original price immediately, there's not enough supply!

Customer: It's the last one, can you give a discount? 80 yuan.

Wife: Sorry! We've been doing business here for ten years, and from the start, we've never negotiated prices (pointing to the sign "firm price, no negotiation"). If it's new merchandise, we'd give you a discount, but for sale items, we can't go lower. Also, please check for quality issues before buying, as the last piece is being cleared at a loss and will not be returned. (Her attitude is neither arrogant nor humble, making the customer feel that this 10-year-old shop has some history.)

Customer: (Checking quality, paying)

Not long after the customer left, while tidying up, my wife sighed and said: The sweater earlier was actually sold at the original price, without a discount. If I told the truth, the customer would definitely pressure me to lower the price. Actually, the profit margin on this sweater was very low. Look at the world, it's forcing honest people to resort to dishonesty. A perfectly reasonable price, and she insists on cutting it drastically to feel comfortable, so to make them feel comfortable, I had to lie kindly. (In life, my wife is a genuine person, alas, business has made our leader cunning.) They actually all buy at the same price, but they cleverly assume that getting a discount means getting a bargain. What kind of mindset is that?! Isn't this a case of cleverness backfiring?

But, can we blame the customers? In reality, who has disrupted this market? Is it the customers? No! It's us merchants! Our random pricing forces customers to fiercely haggle. When we slash prices toward customers, have we considered how they feel after being slashed? We spent ten years building the "firm price, no negotiation" pricing model. Honestly speaking, this "rectifying chaos" process was really tough. I estimate that not all shopkeepers can withstand it. The key is that this "no negotiation" is implemented based on the shopkeeper's pricing integrity. It's not just any arbitrary fixed price used to exploit customers. When we have short-sighted visions and earn huge profits from customers, we shouldn't complain about their vigilance and resistance. Who broke the rules first?

Finally, let me add a "profound saying" from our leader: To truly implement "firm price, no negotiation," you must first ensure your products are excellent and always guard against greed for excessive profits! Set a reasonable price with a clear conscience. If your firm price exceeds reasonableness, you'll completely offend customers! Such a firm price is like lifting a stone to drop on your own feet. Don't treat customers as fools! Only by respecting each other and putting oneself in the other's shoes can your "firm price" gain credibility.
