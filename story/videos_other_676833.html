Uncovering the Smuggling Den of Second-hand Luxury Cars: Hiring Hawkers as Foreign Enterprise Representatives to Deceive Quotas

by asljw9004 on 2012-02-26 16:04:07

Using false foreign enterprise information to register a "Beijing representative office" at the industry and commerce bureau, then taking advantage of customs policies to import "used cars for corporate use" under the company's name. Once they had possession of the vehicles, they would resell them on Beijing's second-hand car market for profit.

Among these smuggled cars, Mercedes-Benz made up the largest number, accounting for 30% of the total; Land Rover followed closely behind, making up over 20%. In addition, the smuggled cars included high-end brands such as BMW, Cayenne, Porsche, Cadillac, Infiniti, Chrysler, Audi, Hummer, Touareg, and more.

In order to defraud import quotas, those involved even hired kebab vendors to act as chief representatives of foreign enterprises. It was reported today that the Second Intermediate People's Court recently concluded this major case, sentencing four defendants including Zhang Jun to prison terms ranging from three years to one year and four months for illegal business operations.

**Importing Cars Overseas**

Deceiving Quotas Under the Name of Foreign Enterprises' Beijing Representative Offices

According to the "List of Prohibited Import Goods" and the "Regulations on the Supervision of Public Items Entering and Leaving the Country by Resident Institutions," used cars are prohibited goods in China. However, resident institutions of foreign enterprises are allowed to import a certain number of vehicles based on their personnel size.

The court found that between July and December 2008, Zhang Jun provided false registration materials for foreign enterprises' Beijing representative offices to handle customs filing procedures, deceiving customs into approving three documents authorizing the import of used cars. Between 2008 and January 2009, Zhang Jun sold the registration materials of foreign enterprises' Beijing representative offices to Ban Yongzhi and Wang Jing (both separately handled), assisting them in registering false foreign enterprises' Beijing representative offices with the industry and commerce bureau, handling customs filing procedures, and deceiving customs into approving twelve documents authorizing the import of used cars.

Zhang Jun then sold these documents for profit, allowing fifteen used cars from abroad to be smuggled into the country.

It was reported that Zhang Yu is one of Zhang Jun's subordinates who started working for him in August 2008. Zhang Yu said that Zhang Jun either found agencies to register foreign enterprises' Beijing representative offices or spent 40,000 yuan to buy the procedures for such offices from others. Afterward, Zhang Jun would order used cars overseas.

Once the used cars arrived at domestic ports, he would bring invoices for the used cars mailed from overseas along with the registration materials for the foreign enterprises' Beijing representative offices to apply for used car quotas at the Beijing Customs Non-Trade Goods Supervision Center.

After the used cars reached Beijing, Zhang Jun would first go to the Beijing Customs Non-Trade Goods Supervision Center to exchange for a license plate notification letter, then inspect the vehicle, pay the purchase tax, buy insurance, and finally register the imported used car at the Vehicle Management Office and obtain a license plate. A month later, it would be possible to process the deregistration of the imported used car at the Beijing Customs Non-Trade Goods Supervision Center. Subsequently, these cars would be sent to the second-hand car market for sale.

**Assembling Their Own Team**

Even Kebab Vendors Became "Chief Representatives" of Foreign Enterprises

According to Zhang Jun's driver Mo, Zhang Jun eventually formed his own team to register foreign enterprises' Beijing representative offices. These representative offices were merely empty shells, only used to "obtain quotas." The so-called "employees of foreign enterprises" who went to customs to handle procedures were all people recruited by Zhang Jun's subordinates.

Gao is an unemployed person who posted on a forum in early 2008 hoping to practice English conversation with friends. He met Xia (one of Zhang Jun's subordinates). After meeting, Xia expressed the wish to use Gao's ID to register a foreign enterprise's Beijing representative office and appointed Gao as the "Chief Representative."

Tian is an employee of a real estate intermediary company and a friend of Qi (a partner of Zhang Jun). In September 2008, enticed by a 1,000 yuan bonus, Tian and Qi went to customs to handle the procedures for a "foreign enterprise's Beijing representative office."

The most amusing case was Wu, who was originally a kebab vendor. Because Zhang Jun's driver Mo often ate at his stall, they became acquainted, and Wu was also recruited to become the "Chief Representative" of the "Beijing Representative Office of Canada Sartem Sales Co., Ltd."

In addition, these so-called "Chief Representatives of Foreign Enterprises' Beijing Representative Offices" also included pool hall employees, taxi drivers, and others.

**Selling Quotas**

Each quota was marked up by 5,000 yuan and sold to second-hand car merchants.

Ban Yongzhi and Wang Jing were specialized quota traders. The court found that between 2007 and 2009, the two deceived customs into approving 33 documents authorizing the import of used cars, which they then sold to others. Between 2008 and 2009, Wang Jing also independently sold seven quotas.

In October 2007, the two met Zhang Jun and began obtaining quotas from him. Zhang Jun priced each quota between 50,000 and 35,000 yuan.

The costs of registering representative offices and hiring chief representatives were borne by the two, which amounted to nearly ten thousand yuan. According to Ban Yongzhi, "all the key steps were in his hands, so we had to pay the fees. The foreign representatives were all people they found; if we submitted applications ourselves, customs wouldn't process them."

Their regular supplier was Wang Weidong. Wang Weidong was the manager of Beijing Jingxi Times Old Motor Vehicle Brokerage Co., Ltd., specializing in second-hand car business. According to Ban Yongzhi and Wang Jing, they mostly sold the quotas they obtained to Wang Weidong at a markup of 5,000 yuan each.

**Smuggling Luxury Cars**

Imported second-hand luxury cars, buyers were mostly executives.

From June 2007 to April 2009, Wang Weidong, Wang Jing, Ban Yongzhi, and others declared the importation of 23 old motor vehicles through ports such as Guangzhou Huangpu and sold 21 of them domestically for profit.

It was understood that the illegally imported second-hand cars were all high-end sedans and SUVs such as BMW, Mercedes-Benz, Land Rover, Porsche, and Cayenne. According to the investigation records, all the car owners questioned were corporate bosses. Although these were second-hand cars, their selling prices were generally around one million yuan.

The pre-trial dossier showed that a car owner surnamed Liu, who was the chairman of a group, told investigators that he bought a Cayenne SUV for 900,000 yuan from Wang Weidong in the first half of 2008. He agreed with Wang Weidong to "inflate the invoice amount," which ultimately read 1.5 million yuan because "it would be convenient for offsetting accounts in the future."

**Court Verdict**

Unable to clarify the tax amount, unable to sentence according to smuggling crime.

The judge stated that when investigators initially received the report, they found no illegal activities in any part of the suspects' actions. When the case hit a deadlock, investigators suddenly discovered that the involved foreign enterprises' Beijing representative offices were all "empty shells," leading to the final resolution of the case.

After reviewing the case, the court believed that Wang Weidong, Wang Jing, Ban Yongzhi, Zhang Jun, and others violated national regulations by reselling customs-approved vehicle import permits, constituting illegal business operations.

The judge noted that a necessary condition for the crime of smuggling ordinary goods is the need to ascertain the amount of evaded taxes. In the aforementioned case, the defendants had already paid part or all of the taxes when the vehicles passed through customs. Moreover, since the investigation department could not determine the actual price of the imported vehicles, they could not accurately calculate the taxes, thus preventing sentencing based on the crime of smuggling ordinary goods.

It was learned that the criminal facts recognized for Ban Yongzhi and Wang Jing were "jointly reselling 33 import second-hand car quotas, with an illegal operating amount reaching 1.84 million yuan"; the criminal facts recognized for Zhang Jun were "reselling 15 import second-hand car quotas, with an illegal operating amount exceeding 800,000 yuan"; the criminal facts recognized for Wang Weidong were "purchasing quotas, declaring the import of 23 old motor vehicles through ports such as Guangzhou Huangpu, with a total taxable price of over 6.6 million yuan."

**Customs Statement**

Smuggling imported used cars yields a minimum profit of 30%.

A customs official who wished to remain anonymous introduced that in previous years, similar smuggling cases occurred in various places.

These self-brought cars mainly entered through taxed imports because the customs supervision period for taxed self-brought cars is short, allowing transfer after one year of completing vehicle registration procedures with traffic management departments. On the other hand, duty-free imported motor vehicles have a six-year supervision period starting from customs release, with strict annual inspections and complex procedures for deregulation upon expiration.

He also mentioned that imported self-brought cars are primarily used cars. This is because new car prices are uniform, and importing via self-brought cars does not differ in price from general trade imports. In contrast, used cars abroad are very cheap, being only 2-3 times discounted compared to new cars, leaving significant profit margins even after paying relevant taxes and fees.

It was learned that at the time, the profit margin for each imported used car was at least 30%. Some mid-to-high-end used cars cost between $10,000 and $20,000.

After arriving at Chinese ports, after paying various taxes and transportation fees, as well as purchasing quota fees, the total cost was less than 300,000 yuan, but they could be sold for 500,000 yuan on the market. For example, a BMW X5 was priced between $15,000 and $30,000 in the local U.S. used car market. Including shipping, tariffs, and other fees, the import price should be between 280,000 and 550,000 yuan RMB, yet the actual selling price could reach 400,000 to 700,000 yuan RMB.

**Latest Developments**

Luxury cars registered under foreign enterprises' Beijing representative offices have disappeared.

It was learned from the customs system that starting from July 1, 2010, customs implemented new regulations: except for resident institutions and personnel following relevant intergovernmental agreements that allow duty-free entry of motor vehicles, and specially introduced high-level talents and experts by the state, other resident institutions and personnel may not enter used motor vehicles. Customs will not accept applications for the entry of used motor vehicles.

It was understood that in recent years, customs authorities in Wuhan, Tianjin, Taiyuan, and other places successively cracked major automobile smuggling cases using methods identical to those described in this case. Relevant analysts believe that the customs' newly issued policy will effectively curb the above-mentioned smuggling activities.

Recently, after multiple visits to several second-hand car markets in Beijing, it was discovered that "second-hand high-end cars registered under foreign enterprises' Beijing representative offices" have vanished from Beijing.

●News Inquiry

If buyers know the car is smuggled, do they commit a crime?

Spending millions to buy stolen cars, would these cars be confiscated by relevant authorities once the case breaks out?

Regarding this, Judge Tian Guiqing of Daxing Court's Huangcun Tribunal, who has handled many disputes involving the purchase of stolen cars, stated that according to legal provisions, if the buyer knows the vehicle is a smuggled car, not only will the car be confiscated, but the buyer will also constitute the crime of concealing or covering up the proceeds of crime or the benefits thereof.

According to Article 312 of the Criminal Law, knowingly acquiring property derived from crime or its proceeds shall be punished with fixed-term imprisonment of not more than three years, criminal detention, or public surveillance; if the circumstances are serious, the punishment shall be fixed-term imprisonment of not less than three years but not more than seven years.

If the buyer genuinely did not know the car was smuggled at the time of purchase, and the transaction price was not significantly lower than the market price, it constitutes a good faith acquisition under civil law, and the transaction behavior is legally protected. Not only does it not constitute a crime, but the vehicle will not be confiscated and will still belong to the buyer.
