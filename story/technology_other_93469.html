Former President of China HP, Sun Zhenyao, Talks About Work and Life

I have an interesting observation: there are many white-collar workers aged 25-35 in foreign companies, but very few employees over the age of 40. The young employees in their twenties and thirties at foreign companies are full of confidence, but the managers around the age of 40 in foreign companies are in a rather awkward position. Most of the foreign company managers I know who are about 40 years old have been constantly job-hopping, eventually ending up in private enterprises, for example, Tang Jun. The success of foreign company employees largely depends on the success of the company, not necessarily on personal success. Siemens is indeed bigger than Gome, but that does not mean that the manager of Siemens China is better than the boss of Gome; in fact, one could say they are far apart. People working in foreign companies often fail to understand this early on, attributing 90% of their success to their own abilities. In reality, randomly replacing the General Manager of China Operations in a foreign company would not significantly impact performance. Now the problem arises: when these managers are in their forties, their salary requirements become quite high, yet their talents are not particularly outstanding. If you were the boss of a foreign company, how would you choose? Some may prefer younger individuals who don't demand high salaries, are sharp, energetic, and ambitious. Others might wonder, why should we still hire you?

From the above example, we can see our career trajectory. In our twenties and thirties, the pressure of life is relatively small, our health is good, our parents are still healthy, and we don’t have children yet. We don’t need to pay off mortgages or worry about our children's college education. Being a white-collar worker in a foreign company seems glamorous, and the income, though not substantial, is enough for expenses. However, everyone will eventually get married, have children, and grow old. By the time we reach 40, our parents are elderly, needing medical care, medication, and someone to look after them. We also have mortgages to pay, basic living expenses to cover, and children to raise... At that point, it becomes crucial to consider how much money we need to earn to sustain our lifestyle. Therefore, when viewing work, it is important to have a long-term perspective. Temporary fluctuations in status do not necessarily mean anything.

From this perspective, I don’t support overly focusing on the salary of one’s first job, nor is there any need to compare the salaries of first jobs, which is quite common among students just out of school. On average, a person works for about 35 years, akin to a marathon race. Unlike real marathons, this race has no professional athletes, and each individual gets only one chance. It should be known that many people don’t even make it to the finish line. Most people end up walking to the finish line, while only a few run past it. Therefore, trying to grab the leading position at the start doesn't have much significance. When entering society, if one joins a Fortune 500 company, the monthly salary might range from 3k-6k RMB, with some highly technical talents possibly earning up to 8k RMB per month. However, the question remains: what will the salary be after five years? Probably between 5k-10k RMB, which is already quite remarkable. Although the starting point may be high, the growth rate is limited, and the pressure from younger generations catching up increases over time.

The other day, I asked one of my salespeople: the skills you have, a newcomer can learn within two years, but the salary requirement of the newcomer is only half of yours. So, what will you do then?

A career is like a sports competition, with preliminary rounds, semi-finals, and finals. During the preliminary round, everyone is just entering society, and most are ordinary individuals. At this stage, putting in a little more effort and being diligent quickly sets one apart from the rest. Thus, some people become managers in their twenties, while others eventually win the preliminary round in their thirties. Then comes the semi-final. Those participating in the semi-final are all winners from the preliminary round. Everyone has certain capabilities, intelligence is not an issue, and standing out at this stage is not easy. A little extra effort and diligence are no longer sufficient. One needs great perseverance, the ability to rely on team strength, the skill to win hearts, and a long-term vision...

Winning the semi-final appears challenging, but it’s not that difficult. This world has a rule: giving a bit of success while fostering arrogance and complacency. Those who just won the preliminary round often don't realize they've merely won the preliminary round. With a little achievement, most people become arrogant and complacent, believing they already know everything and no longer need to put in effort or continue learning. They believe that their inability to progress further is no longer due to themselves. Although they remain formidable opponents, they lack patience, tolerance, and clear long-term vision. Like an angry bull, although fierce, they will ultimately lose. Those who win the semi-final are like matadors, calm and composed, following their own rhythm, gradually exhausting their opponent's patience and energy. After winning the semi-final, one probably becomes an extraordinary professional manager, serving as the general manager of a medium or small-sized company, or the vice president of a large company, overseeing businesses worth tens of millions to hundreds of millions of yuan annually.
