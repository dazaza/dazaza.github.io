Benchmark score: iPhone 5s outperforms some Android flagships

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 22:28:52

Unlike some Android manufacturers who arm themselves with benchmark scores, Apple rarely advertises the benchmark results of iPhones. During its product launches, the company usually compares the new iPhone with its previous generation to highlight the progress made.

The newly released latest generation of iPhones sold 9 million units in its first weekend, setting a new historical record for the same period. Compared to the iPhone 5c, the flagship model iPhone 5s showed overwhelming sales superiority.

Perhaps you're not an ardent follower of benchmark scores, but it's still worth taking a look at how the iPhone 5s outperforms several well-known Android flagships.

PhoneArena selected the iPhone 5, Xperia Z1, LG G2, HTC One, Galaxy S4, and iPhone 5s for benchmark comparisons. Apart from the two Apple products, the rest are this year's Android flagships. To provide a more comprehensive reference, I have organized the release dates of the above phones as follows:

iPhone 5: September 2012

HTC One: March 2013

Galaxy S4: March 2013

LG G2: August 2013

Xperia Z1: September 2013

iPhone 5s: September 2013

The first test item is Sunspider Javascript Benchmark (the lower the score, the better). The test results reflect the JavaScript performance of the phone. SunSpider was developed by Apple and has now become a very popular browser JavaScript testing platform.

In the SunSpider test, the iPhone 5s easily beat all the Android flagships, followed by last year’s iPhone 5 in second place, while Samsung’s Galaxy S4 ranked at the bottom.

Mozilla Kraken is another JavaScript performance testing benchmark (the lower the score, the better). According to Rob Sayre, an engineer at Mozilla, Kraken focuses on real workloads and advanced applications. In this test, the iPhone 5s once again came out on top. However, the iPhone 5 performed poorly, ranking fifth, though slightly better than the HTC One, which came in last.

For GPU performance testing, we started with Basemark X 1.0 (on-screen, 1080p off-screen). Basemark X is a professional game engine testing tool developed by Rightware, reflecting the gaming graphics performance of the phone. Basemark X contains a large number of graphics designed to push the device's graphics rendering capacity to its limits, so the scores won't be too high because they represent the actual frame rates when rendering similar games.

The test results (higher scores are better) showed that the iPhone 5s ranked first, followed by the iPhone 5, with the HTC One scoring the lowest.

GFXBench is used to test graphics card performance (resolution affects graphics card performance; note that the resolution of the iPhone 5s differs from the aforementioned Android phones). In this project, PhoneArena first tested Fill rate (higher scores are better), showing that the performance of the iPhone 5s is nearly twice that of the iPhone 5 and far exceeds other Android flagships.

Next is GFXBench - T-Rex HD (higher scores are better), which reflects the image performance of devices running heavy games. The iPhone 5s is the only device in the test that exceeded 30fps on-screen, reaching 35fps on-screen. The Galaxy S4, HTC One, and iPhone 5 were all below 20fps. It's worth noting that the performance of the iPhone 5s in this category is 2.7 times that of the iPhone 5.

In the T-Rex HD offscreen test (higher scores are better), the iPhone 5s still ranked first, while the iPhone 5 performed poorly, coming in last. The Nexus 5 has yet to be released, and its score comes from previously leaked information, which has not been verified.

Summary

In all the tests mentioned above, the iPhone 5s consistently led the pack. In some tests, the Android flagships couldn’t even see the tail lights of the iPhone 5s.

Clearly, the A7 processor and PowerVR G6430 graphics chip have elevated the iPhone 5s to a whole new level.

The first-ever application of a 64-bit processor in a smartphone ensures that the iPhone 5s can handle complex data. Although there aren't many apps currently supporting 64-bit, Apple's move undoubtedly pushes the entire app ecosystem forward. By the time the company equips its entire series of mobile products with 64-bit processors, the App Store should already have a sufficient number of apps supporting 64-bit. Additionally, a 64-bit processor allows the phone to support up to 4GB RAM, even though the iPhone 5s only comes equipped with 1GB LPDDR3 RAM.

Therefore, the application of the A7 processor lays the groundwork for the future of Apple's mobile products.

The PowerVR G6430 graphics chip enables the iPhone 5s to reach the iPad 4's graphical performance of 76.8GFlops (one GFlops equals one billion floating-point operations per second). For comparison, Sony PS3 has a value of 230GFlops, while the Galaxy S4 and HTC One are around 50GFlops.

Finally, it is worth mentioning that benchmarks can reflect device performance to a certain extent, but they do not represent everything.
