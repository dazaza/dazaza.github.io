Chapter Two: Three Agreements, Section 27: Theory M of Humanized Management (2) "The Doctrine of the Mean in Management" by Zeng Shi Qiang

What is the Doctrine of the Mean? It means that everything should be reasonable, which is the way of the Doctrine of the Mean. Therefore, management is about managing reasonably, loving reasonably, and restricting reasonably. Restricting everything is useless; not restricting anything is equivalent to not caring for him. We always say that children are naturally lovable, but I don't believe this statement; many people say that children are angels, but I also don't believe it. Children are born destroyers; if you leave them alone for two hours, they will make a mess of your home—that's what children do. If your child tidies up your house cleanly after being left alone for two hours, then that’s a monster. When you give a child shoes, strangely enough, they always put the right shoe on the left foot and vice versa, with a probability of 50%. But that’s how they are—what can you do? This is why children need to be taught. If children were naturally capable of everything, parents wouldn't have to educate them at all. Why do we insist that education is important? It is because children are naturally destructive. When a child takes out a book, they will throw each one down one by one. To get them to tidy up afterward requires adult guidance. A child will urinate wherever they feel like unless you teach them to go to the bathroom. Nowadays, many people are afraid to discipline their children, thinking that this is fashionable and trendy. In fact, this notion is completely wrong and stems from a lack of understanding of education. Because they haven’t grasped the nature of children, they simply assume that new ideas are correct, which leads to complete failure.

Managing reasonably is humanized management. What does "reasonable" mean? Explaining "reasonable" with four words makes it clearer—it means "just right." This is difficult to grasp and has its challenges.

Three, The Doctrine of the Mean indicates "just right."

For a long time, we've been confused by the idea that "not leaning to either side is called 'zhong', and not changing is called 'yong.'" For a long time, many people mistakenly believed that a person should lean to neither side, which actually represents fence-sitting and stubbornly unchanging attitudes.

When two people argue, if A comes to you saying B is wrong, and you say "Yes, B is wrong," A leaves happily. Then when B comes saying A is wrong, and you say "Yes, A is wrong," B also leaves happily. Your wife, hearing this, might ask, "You're strange; you say A is right when A comes, and B is right when B comes. Who is really right?" You would say the wife is most right. Does this person have any sense of right and wrong? This is called "xiang yuan," or hypocrisy, and is just foolishness, not reasonableness. Confucius despised such people who have no sense of right and wrong—"xiang yuan"—and these overly accommodating "good people."

Frankly speaking, if one wants to be a good person, they must never be an overly accommodating "good person." A person cannot sway both ways with the wind; that is having no stance, being smooth, cunning, and sinister—Chinese people detest such individuals the most. If moving in one direction is reasonable, we move in that direction; if moving in another direction is reasonable, we move there. Wherever it is reasonable, we go there—not where there are benefits. That is unacceptable.

In fact, the Doctrine of the Mean is an expression of "just right." Zhu Xi said, "All his actions are done appropriately, thus nothing he does is unreasonable." Nothing being unreasonable means every matter is handled perfectly, in accordance with the Doctrine of the Mean. There is nothing in the world that is unreasonable, and this is the Doctrine of the Mean, which is highly challenging. But I emphasize repeatedly that precisely because it is difficult, it is worth our attention and pursuit as a goal. Moreover, once learned, we can use it for a long time. Therefore, learning earlier while young is more beneficial for this reason.

Whether something is reasonable depends on many factors, including people, events, time, place, and things, and these factors change constantly, which is very important. Therefore, the principle of management remains unchanged, but the measures of management vary. Whether something is legal is fixed, but whether something is reasonable is not fixed—it changes. This is the difference between "law" and "reason." Law is rigid text, while reason is a flexible response measure. Therefore, the four characters most admired by Chinese people are "adapt flexibly." Adapting flexibly to achieve the most reasonable solution at this time and place is the "Doctrine of the Mean."

Anyone needs to learn how to adapt flexibly. However, we must remind everyone that "adapting flexibly" has a twin brother, identical in appearance, called "taking shortcuts." How do we distinguish between "taking shortcuts" and "adapting flexibly"? The easiest distinction for Chinese people is this: if a person adapts for the public interest, we consider it adapting flexibly; if a person adapts for personal gain, putting money into their own pocket and only taking care of their small family, then it is definitely taking shortcuts. So one is public-oriented, and the other is private-oriented, which is the difference between "human heart" and "moral heart." If you adapt based on "moral heart," it is adapting flexibly; if you adapt based on "human heart," it is taking shortcuts.

We must remember to let your subordinates, employees, and everyone adapt flexibly absolutely and avoid taking shortcuts. As a Chinese person, as long as you remember this sentence, everything will be reassuring.

Four, Humanized Middle Way Management

The Doctrine of the Mean is abbreviated as "Middle Way," representing the path of the Doctrine of the Mean. Management that conforms to the Doctrine of the Mean is called Middle Way Management, aiming to seek appropriateness to stabilize people. Middle Way Management manages according to "the nature given by heaven," meeting the needs of humanity, making it humanized management.

From a modern perspective, Middle Way Management is "reasonable, appropriate, and lawful" management. Emotion, reason, and law, with "reason" at the center, seek both appropriateness and legality, focusing on reasonableness.

In recent years, voices opposing "emotion, reason, and law" have risen. People seem to fear "emotion" and dislike it, trying instead to elevate "reason" or "law," advocating "reason controlling emotion" and "law suppressing emotion," often turning it into "law, reason, and emotion," forgetting that "a person without emotion is not human," which is a very bad thing. This one-sided denial attitude shows a misunderstanding of the true meaning of "emotion, reason, and law" arranged by our ancient sages. In fact, management based on emotion, reason, and law will never become outdated.

First, we need to point out that the focus of "emotion, reason, and law" is not on "emotion" but on "reason." The most prosperous branch of Chinese philosophy is Neo-Confucianism from the Song and Ming dynasties, originating from works like "The Book of Songs," "The Book of Changes," "The Great Learning," "The Doctrine of the Mean," "The Analects," and "Mencius." However, our ancient sages already recognized the independent existence of "reason." Before the Neo-Confucianists of the Song and Ming dynasties, Confucius, Laozi, Mozi, Mencius, and others preferred to talk about "the way," because "the way" is the necessary path for the formation of things, essentially a principle. Zhu Xi said, "The word 'way' encompasses much, and 'reason' consists of many veins within the word 'way.'" "Reason" can be spoken of with words, but "the way" must be practiced in reality. The sages hoped not only that we talk about reason but especially that we value practice, doing everything according to reason.

Since "the emphasis is on reason," why isn't it sequentially combined and arranged as "reason, emotion, law" or "reason, law, emotion"? This involves the Chinese-specific sequence view of "seeking harmony." Our "spirit of moderation" places "reason" in the middle among "emotion, reason, law," so "great harmony" is what we value.

Chinese philosophy has a fundamental tendency, affirming that "change is a fundamental fact in the universe." Confucius stood by a great river and sighed, "The passage of time is like this! Unceasing day and night!" Laozi said, "It (the way) is vast and endless, flowing continuously, extending far, and returning to its origin." All explain that "the universe is an infinite process of continuous change." In this ever-changing context, Chinese people deeply understand the principle of "extremes inevitably reverse," leading to the principle of "moderation brings harmony." Even now, when taking group photos, we habitually offer the central position to elders of higher rank or older age, proving this point.

Observing from real life, it is not difficult to prove that among "emotion, reason, and law," Chinese people value "reason" the most. We only say "study to understand reason" and never "study to express emotion." A common saying goes, "With reason, one can travel the world; without reason, one cannot take a single step." Any company reviewing gains and losses usually focuses on evaluating "reason" rather than openly seeking "emotion."

"Reason" is "face," and "emotion" is "dignity." Chinese people "cherish dignity" even more than "face." Insulting someone as "without face" is a very serious matter, and "without face" means "irrational," disliked by everyone!

However, "reason" is not easy to understand. Zhang Zai of the Song dynasty said, "Heavenly reason is merely timely appropriateness." "Timely appropriateness" means suitability, and heavenly reason is not fixed but adaptable. Chinese people value flexibility, using change to achieve communication. Confucianism emphasizes both principles and flexibility. Chunyu Kun posed a dilemma to Mencius: men and women should not touch each other directly, a principle society should follow. But in special circumstances, like a sister-in-law drowning, what should be done? Mencius told him to act out of benevolence (compassion) and rescue her immediately. Normally, "no direct contact" is reasonable, but in special cases, "rescue with hands" is also reasonable.

Too many examples, from www.fenghuangpingtai.com, lead Chinese people to think "you said it," because "the husband says he is right, and the wife says she is right," everyone claims to be right, but the reasons they state differ, showing that reason is constructed by people. Such reasoning often leads to "having reason means having spirit," "spirit full means reason correct," forming "the strong have reason." Chinese people dislike "power is reason" and thus evolve into "higher officials have reason."

If an employee in a company commits a mistake and you tell them, "This action was wrong!" they probably won't directly concern themselves with whether the action was wrong but rather with "who said it," demonstrating the psychology of "the strong have reason." Just telling them, "The chairman said it," significantly increases the likelihood of submission.

Reason is relative; there is up and down, this and that. Westerners therefore use law to determine reason, judging everything based on legal standards, where legality equates to reason. Since legal texts are relatively fixed and rigid, discussing reason based on law is simpler and more convenient.

Our ancient sages did not disregard law. Some claim that Confucianism disregards law, only Legalism values law, which is not true. Valuing rites and laws was one of Confucius's most important doctrines. Xiao Gongquan, a pioneer in Chinese constitutional theory, believed Confucius's political thought started with "following Zhou." "Following Zhou" means "adhering to Zhou's classic systems," specifically advocating "correct naming," adjusting the rights and obligations of rulers and subjects according to the Zhou system. Confucius's study of "Zhou rituals" was not limited to coronations, weddings, funerals, sacrifices, or ceremonies, encompassing the entire social system. He advocated "self-discipline and restoration of rites," often advising against "unritualistic seeing, listening, speaking, and acting," clearly valuing legal norms.

"Law" in enterprise management is referred to as "institutionalization," which is necessary but not highly sophisticated. Overemphasizing institutionalization today easily leads to the misconception that institutionalization equals good management. Regulations are universal forms, impossible to apply universally across all special situations when formulated, especially since individuals often find new ways to implement practices unforeseen beforehand. Confucianism advocates that in special situations, one need not adhere strictly to the formality of rites but should base decisions on humanitarian principles to enhance the efficiency of normative adaptation. Humans are masters, not slaves, of regulations. Laws are made by humans and must change according to reason to fit the times. We often see some supervisors loudly advocating institutionalization yet frequently breaking rules with justification, revealing the influence of these concepts, powerful and profound.

Xunzi said, "Adapt according to righteousness, knowing when to bend and straighten." He believed that people should adapt flexibly to situations, varying methods according to different circumstances to suitably respond. In special environments, one may sometimes violate clear and established rules to make decisions. Doing so doesn't signify rejecting the rules entirely but following another layer of rules (reason) after abandoning or violating the original ones (law).

Using law to discuss reason is indeed simple and clear but practically incapable of handling "either-or" or "exception" cases, inevitably leading to becoming "unreasonable" due to insistence on "law." Zhu Xi said, "The shame of conscience is inherently good, but overdoing it leads to cruelty." Now some righteous individuals, witnessing many lawbreakers in society, develop a sense of shame and advocate using severe laws indiscriminately to eliminate evil and violence, unknowingly becoming cruel. Using law to discuss reason has good intentions but results in many unreasonable actions.

Thus, our ancient sages placed "discussing reason according to law" at the second level and elevated "entering reason through emotion" to the first level. "Entering reason through emotion" is called "offering wine," and "discussing reason according to law" is "punishment wine." Chinese people prefer "eating punishment wine" to "offering wine," meaning "when discussing reason nicely, you'd better be reasonable; otherwise, enforcing law will still make you reasonable," with the ultimate aim still being "discussing reason."

This explains that Chinese people keep "law" in their stomachs, mentally aware. I won't break the law, but I won't talk about it. Because discussing law with Chinese people harms feelings. In airports, customs, and many places, you can hear that when enforcement officers tell someone they can't do something, they'll ask, "Why can't I?" When the officer answers, "It's our regulation!" they immediately retort, "Don't scare me with regulations!" Chinese people most disdain regulations and laws. So they verbally emphasize emotions, "No problem," "Won't be an issue," but mentally prepare to handle matters legally when the time comes. Chinese people speak loosely but execute strictly.

Some people verbally stress legality but mentally think about reasonable solutions, rarely acting purely legally. Implementing legality in Chinese society is highly difficult because Chinese people specialize in doing things beyond legal boundaries, refraining from actions once legally defined. Thus, we often arrest people without finding legal grounds, unsure which article to punish them under. We mostly resolve issues reasonably rather than legally, though we verbally emphasize "acting legally."

Middle Way Management aligns with heavenly mandate and human nature, a "heaven-human unity" management style. Law is a last resort; Chinese people first enter reason through emotion, first "offer wine." When emotional reasoning fails, they turn their faces and "eat punishment wine," then act legally. Once legality is enforced, all emotions are set aside. We often talk about "legal exceptions," which is "selling favors." When law appears, there is no room for emotion, indicating a true understanding of emotion, reason, and law. First, I give you face; will you listen? If you listen when given face, excellent—I give you face. If you don't listen, I still give you face, and you still don't listen. When I turn my face, there's no favor left, and I enforce the law regardless of how much you protest.

Middle Way Management represents Chinese "entering reason through emotion" in management with a human touch. Understanding emotion, reason, and law is truly understanding management; otherwise, we often get entangled in law and human sentiment. Foreigners criticize us for not abiding by laws, which is quite unreasonable.

"Reason" has front and back sides; following it is correct, going against it is wrong. Managers "talk reason" best by following these principles to achieve correctness:

1. Establish consensus: For all colleagues who "value face" (reason), we must give them "dignity" (expressing "respect," "cherishing," "caring" in moderate emotions). For those who "don't value face" (irrational), we certainly won't give them dignity (expressing "disdain" in coldness).

2. Gradual progression: The order of "emotion, reason, law" prioritizes "reason," but "reason is the moderate part of emotion." Therefore, implementing management is best done by first touching emotions, using the concept of "caring for employees" to resonate, making employees naturally emit appropriate emotions and act reasonably. Managers should naturally "uphold principles (reason)," but before that, they should "build good relationships (emotion)." Using "building good relationships" to "uphold principles," employees will willingly comply. Building good relationships daily fills employees with confidence, understanding that managers are rational. When upholding principles, employees won't emotionally distrust, making it more conducive to discussing reason.

3. Obey the law yourself. Upon encountering irrational employees, you must not easily overlook them; otherwise, you're destroying discipline yourself. Management starts by touching emotions, then explaining reason. If it still doesn't produce positive effects, it must enforce law, letting them know the consequences of being irrational, with no possibility of avoidance or escape. Managers often find many excuses, even blaming it on "emotion," but in reality, they themselves don't abide by the law, failing to effectively maintain the authority of the law. Once enforcement becomes lax, powerlessness follows.

The arrangement of "emotion, reason, law" aims to genuinely discuss reason. Managers discussing reason, following the principle of "emotion first," "reason paramount," and "if emotion and reason fail, enforce law," should find no situation unmanageable.

Five, Theory M as Middle Way Management

Since Chinese-style management is "Middle Way Management," we can use Theory M to represent the Chinese approach to management. M stands for "man" (human), "medium" (moderation), and "management." Humanized management values humans, centered on people, pursuing conformity with the Doctrine of the Mean. Such management, naturally, relies mainly on Theory M.

Especially observing the shape of M, it is balanced and symmetrical, fitting well with the characteristics of "moderation." Careful observation reveals three basic requirements of Chinese people: first, symmetry; second, balance; third, dynamics. Ancient Chinese architecture has a central line, symmetrically balanced on both sides, maintaining dynamic movement rather than static rigidity. Unfortunately, many architects today ignore these elements, lacking a central line, unbalanced sides, asymmetry, and disrupted feng shui. I must clarify that feng shui should be scientific, not superstitious; if superstitious, humanity has no future. The shape of M fits well with the three basic requirements of Chinese people, appearing stable and balanced.

M's stability and balance symbolize the favorite "non-toppling figure" spirit of Chinese people, standing invincible. Chinese people love a toy called "non-toppling figure." We played with it as children—a round object that doesn't resist when pressed but bounces back when released. Press it left, it tilts left; press it right, it tilts right; release it, and it stands up again. This is the standard spirit of Chinese people. People under the eaves don't resist too much; when you press me, I obey, looking compliant, but as soon as you release me, I rise again. Look at Hong Kong people; during British colonial rule, they spoke English and imitated everything British. After Hong Kong's return, they revived as Chinese.

Theory M differs from "X Theory" and "Y Theory," not overly optimistic or pessimistic about human nature, embodying the unbiased Doctrine of the Mean. We know humans can be shaped, with the golden period being from three to six years old. At three, one's future tendencies are visible; at six, one's lifelong traits are evident. After seven, it's too late. Actually, whether a person has self-confidence is determined between ten months to eighteen months after birth. After eighteen months, lack of self-confidence is irreversible. We must use appropriate methods to shape reasonable human nature, which is called humanized management.

Before the age of twenty, almost everyone is similar, with little difference. After twenty, some progress quickly, others slowly. Almost all changes occur after twenty. Twenty is the age when one enters the workplace, growing through work and modeling leaders. Therefore, a leader has two responsibilities: educating and requiring growth, and providing opportunities for good performance. This is humanized management.

So, encountering a good boss leads to rapid growth; encountering a bad boss leads to misfortune, diminishing capability despite initial potential. An employee's growth depends on having a good leader. A good leader's greatest responsibility is ensuring smooth employee growth, the highest virtue. Making them earn money actually harms them. Success too early makes life tough later.

I offer two phrases for consideration: first, "rather succeed late than early"; second, "rather fail early than late."
