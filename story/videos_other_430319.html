The Laughter and Tears of Thailand's Ladyboys

When the plane arrived in Bangkok, Thailand at dawn, Thailand implements a visa on arrival. In the Suvarnabhumi Airport terminal, there were people of all races—blacks, whites, yellows—queuing for visas. Bangkok is truly an international metropolis. Among these crowds, not only do their skin colors differ, but their clothing does as well, with clothes from all seasons present, though summer wear predominates. We knew it would be hot here, so we were prepared, taking off our down jackets and putting on sweaters, but we still felt a bit bloated. Compared to the locals who wore tank tops, big shorts, slippers, and had skin clearly darkened by the sun, we obviously stuck out like ducks among chickens.

After completing the visa process and exiting the terminal to board the bus that greeted us, Thai ladies placed garlands of fresh flowers around our necks and greeted everyone with hands pressed together saying "SOWALICA" (Thailand's highest form of respect), which immediately improved our impression of Thailand. The government's support and emphasis on tourism are evident from this gesture alone.

On the bus, a male and female tour guide came over. They referred to the men in the group as 'Old Mama' or 'PP', 'Big P', and the women were called 'Crystal Crystal', 'Watery Watery', and 'Dry Dry' depending on age. Then they exchanged baht for us at a rate of 1:3.9. However, later we found out that Renminbi (RMB) was just as widely accepted as baht, even more so, whether in large stores or small stalls, at a rate of 1:4.3. The male guide called us young ladies 'Crystal Crystal', so we called him 'King PP'.

Although we haven't left Asia yet, Southeast Asia is part of the Sinic cultural sphere, but upon arriving here, everything feels different from home—the plants on the ground, the birds in the sky, the goods sold on the streets. Truly, we have arrived in a foreign land. Language and script have also changed. If there weren't so many other domestic tour groups around, you would really feel how strange this place is—with its dense coconut groves, strangely shaped and foul-smelling durians, dark-skinned faces, worm-like scripts similar to Arabic, and hordes of white tourists. All this indicates that this is another place. Buddhism may be familiar to us, but here the Buddha statues are slim like the local people, with tall pointed towers on their heads, some even having noses curled like elephants'. Apart from the color of their robes being golden yellow, all other details are different. This indeed verifies what a certain philosopher said: gods are created by humans, reflecting humanity itself.

Thailand is a country steeped in Buddhist culture. On the streets, one frequently sees offering platforms about 1.5 meters high and 1 meter wide. Not only are they present in public places like hotels and shops, but also more so in front of schools and government institutions. According to the guide, this is both custom and legally mandated. As common on the streets as these offering platforms are portraits of the current king, King Rama IX. Royal authority and religious power have special status here. Especially Buddhism, from the ruling class to the common people, both in infrastructure and spiritual aspects, one can see the influence of Buddhism everywhere. For example, the greeting ritual among ordinary Thai people is not the Eastern-style handshake or bow, but rather the monk-like gesture of pressing palms together. It's similar to how during the Jin Dynasty in China when Buddhism was prevalent, everyone greeted each other with 'Amitabha'. All Thai men must serve in the military once in their lives, but they must also enter the monastery once. Staying in the monastery can be as short as 7-10 days or as long as a lifetime. Afterward, it's very casual; one can enter the monastery anytime and leave anytime, drink alcohol, eat meat, and hang out in bars as long as no one reports them and the police don't find out. Once discovered by the police, they must be forcibly disrobed and cannot re-enter the monastery again. Despite this, there are still 300,000 monks in Thailand.

Thailand is a country with 60 million people and a history of over 700 years. It was ruled by the Khmers for over 200 years and did not have its own writing system until later. There have been nine kings in history, with the fifth king having the greatest impact because he studied in Europe and absorbed advanced democratic ideas there, much like Sun Yat-sen in China. He was dedicated to benefiting the people and transforming his own country. He abolished the then-existing slavery system and did many things to benefit the people, so the people loved him dearly. Commoners burn three incense sticks when worshipping Buddha and deities, but they burn five incense sticks when worshipping the fifth king. The current living king is King Rama IX, who has been on the throne for 60 years and has three daughters and one son. But the crown prince has not yet inherited the throne because of his bad habits like drinking, gambling, and womanizing. It is rumored that he once forcibly assaulted a famous female singer, so the Thai people do not like him, nor does King Rama IX. Among his three daughters, the eldest and youngest princesses are married, while the second princess is capable and diligent. The people dislike the crown prince but like the second princess. King Rama IX intended to pass the throne to the second princess, but the crown prince found out and sent hitmen to kill her. She was saved by bodyguards, and now King Rama IX plans to pass the throne to his grandson when he turns three years and eight months old.

We visited shortly after Thaksin had stepped down, and domestic tourists had just been allowed to travel to Thailand again. However, since the political situation was somewhat unstable, the guide repeatedly warned us not to leave the hotel alone at night because of possible robberies and such. According to the guide, on the second day of the coup, there was a soldier every five steps and a tank every ten steps on the street, with extremely strict vigilance. Thaksin was a very talented politician who made many contributions to the country early on, but later became corrupt, selling the national telecommunications operating rights to Singapore and embezzling billions of dollars. King Rama IX had always wanted to replace him but didn't dare to act easily. This time, during Thaksin's absence abroad, the coup was launched.

Thailand is rich in natural resources and has seven provinces, four of which produce red, white, and blue gemstones, especially red gemstones. Therefore, the walls of the Grand Palace of Thailand are all built with red gemstones. At first, I didn't quite believe the guide, but after personally seeing the magnificent, unparalleled Grand Palace, touching the walls painted with gold powder and inlaid with glass and gemstones, and seeing the giant jade Buddha, I truly believed it all. I believe that no matter how splendid any building in the world might be, none can match the beauty and rarity of the Grand Palace of Thailand. No wonder there are so many European tourists here, and people of all races can be seen. Rarity is valuable, and people gather because of rarity, just like how flowers attract bees, stench attracts flies, and sweets attract children. People are attracted by the rare and precious.

What draws many tourists to visit are not only the Grand Palace but also the ladyboys. In terms of fame, Thailand's ladyboys are probably even more famous than the Grand Palace of Thailand. This stems from human curiosity. A country that produces the world's only ladyboys is indeed a very peculiar nation. If ladyboys appeared in a country with loose morals and open-minded thinking, it wouldn't be surprising, just like how homosexuality appears in America, where it doesn't seem particularly odd or abrupt. But they emerged in a Buddhist country known for asceticism, combining abstinence with indulgence, conservatism with eccentricity—a unique combination that can only be seen in Thailand. Such opposing elements existing in the same place is a great spectacle, also illustrating that any extreme leads to its opposite. Just like in China's Tang Dynasty, where the social climate was extravagant and people's thoughts were very open, leading to situations like Emperor Xuanzong marrying Yang Guifei, who was originally his daughter-in-law. But by the Song Dynasty, it swung to extreme conservatism, resulting in incidents where a woman would cut off her arm if touched by a man. Historically, there were the most chaste and virtuous women. Buddhism believes that desire is the root of all evil, hence the practice of asceticism. But when desires are forcibly suppressed to the extent of suppressing human nature, it leads to the opposite. Thus, when those stunningly beautiful ladyboys, considered the epitome of femininity, press their palms together to greet you in the Buddhist way, you're unsure whether it's a demon becoming enlightened or a saint turning wanton. All this can be summed up in a local saying: "Visit temples during the day, watch spirits at night." And there's a folk joke: "In Thailand, implement the three-light policy: strip naked, run out of bullets, and get slapped by your wife when you return home." This shows the degree of openness in Thailand, where in a country known for asceticism, the essence is actually more open and liberal thinking.

Thailand's nightlife is exceptionally rich and diverse, almost unmatched in the world. Although the Thai government repeatedly bans some places, the guide still led us there. I didn't want to go, but standing on the street for two hours would be even more boring, so I went too. Upon entering, it was like stepping into a cave of demons, a bizarre place filled with supernatural beings. Although the Moulin Rouge falls into the same category, I haven't seen it, but compared to Thailand's ladyboys, it seems less managed, more raw, and more open. When Adam and Eve's fig leaves are torn away, everything is just like that—Adam's lower body can beat drums and blow up balloons, and Eve's body can keep goldfish and shoot darts. Seen with an appreciative eye, it feels like a stunt show. From a moral perspective, it feels immoral, boring, and uncomfortable. But no matter what, things under the sun are ultimately healthy and lasting, while things in the dark are inherently dark and cannot withstand the light. Exposure to darkness makes one appreciate the value of light even more.

We use the Chinese term 'Ren Yao' (ladyboy) to refer to men turned into women, and the character 'Yao' (妖) is indeed most appropriate. During the day, when we see cheerful, angelic, and charming ladyboys, we like them. But at night, when you see someone on stage with a woman's upper body and a man's lower body, you lose your sense of direction, unsure of where you are, whom you're seeing, or what year it is. When you can't determine if the person in front of you is a man or a woman, calling them 'Yao' seems most fitting. This character 'Yao' is truly brilliant.

There are two types of ladyboys in Thailand. One type has all their physiological structures transformed into females except for lacking a uterus and the ability to bear children; they are psychologically completely transformed ladyboys. The other type is incompletely transformed, half-man and half-woman ladyboys. However, regardless of the type, they are all artificially created, not naturally formed. They usually start injecting estrogen at around eleven or twelve years old, and as adults, they undergo surgeries like breast augmentation and reproductive organ modifications. Their lifespans are very short, and health issues begin to arise after forty, requiring medication to sustain life. Their collective mindset is: life lasts a thousand years, but eventually ends, so life should be brief but without regrets. Although their income is relatively substantial locally, they are mutated humans, not natural or normal ones. While their bodies suffer damage, mentally, they are also in a difficult position of being looked down upon and treated as curiosities. Yet, despite such living conditions, the number of boys choosing to become ladyboys in Thailand is not few but amounts to 100,000—a significant population. Why is this? Monks may become monks out of faith, but these individuals do so due to poverty and for money. What does this group of 100,000 bring to Thailand? We will discuss this later. Because ladyboys are ubiquitous, in Thailand, tall and beautiful women are often mistakenly identified as ladyboys, leading to many amusing misunderstandings.

Out of a total population of 60 million, Thailand has 100,000 ladyboys and 300,000 monks. This disrupts the balance between men and women, so Thailand practices polygamy. Our two guides, one male and one female, illustrate this. The female guide, around forty years old, has a husband with two wives, and she is the first wife. After working all day outside, she reportedly massages her husband's back at home. The male guide, only twenty-five years old, also has two wives—one works outside and the other stays home to take care of the children and manage household chores. This pattern of one wife managing the home and the other working is quite common. Even so, due to the openness of thought, many men still seek relationships outside, so the two wives must work together to keep an eye on the husband. Women in China are fortunate to live in a monogamous society with higher social status. The female guide flaunted this to us. However, unaware to us, in the Sichuan-Tibet border area of China, the ratio of men to women is 5:1, so the polyandrous system where several brothers share one wife is also a common scenario. Both positive and negative examples show that having too much or too little of anything isn't good.

Both guides come from the Golden Triangle region in northern Thailand near Yunnan, being the third generation descendants of remnants of the Kuomintang army. Only these descendants of the Golden Triangle remnants can speak both Mandarin and Thai. Back then, those abandoned Kuomintang troops, like children without parents, could neither return home nor find a foothold abroad, so they could only lease land in the Golden Triangle, a no-man's land, to grow drugs to survive. Children couldn't attend school, adults had nothing to eat or wear, and even lacked proper housing. Under such destitute conditions, they struggled to survive without nationality or recognition, living in self-built shacks, borrowing food from local Thais, and leading primitive lives. Visiting the Golden Triangle, the tools resembling those from the Stone Age and the emaciated children beside the shacks made us shed tears involuntarily. A couplet at the exhibition hall read: "No home anywhere, yet home everywhere; hard times every year, yet survived every year." To gain recognition and obtain Thai nationality, they gave up drug cultivation, sending teenagers to fight for the Thai army, earning bloodshed and sacrifice to secure nationality and land for their children and future generations. After decades of development, today's Golden Triangle has become a bustling town. However, most of its residents are Chinese.

Thailand is rich in natural resources, as they say, with treasures on the mountains (gems), snakes on the ground (venomous snakes), and fish in the rivers (crocodiles). We deeply understand this. Are gems abundant? Look at the walls of the Grand Palace. Crocodiles? In the Ratcha Zoo, crocodiles are piled one on top of another densely, more numerous than pigs in pigpens. So we bought many crocodile wallets and belts in Thailand. Snakes? We didn't see them in the wild, nor did we wish to, but in the snake house, they were also densely packed. According to the guide, a bite from a regular venomous snake can kill a person within days, a bite from Australia's tiger snake can kill within three minutes, and a bite from Thailand's green pit viper can kill in less than two minutes. Therefore, the venom of Thai snakes is several times more valuable than gold. Snake venom treats diseases through the principle of using poison to counteract poison, with effects like stopping bleeding, detoxifying, and beautifying. Snake bile clears liver heat and improves eyesight. According to the Thai guide, if bitten by a snake, if the wound is flat with a row of teeth marks, it's harmless, but if there are two fang marks, it's a venomous snake bite. You need to quickly make a cross-cut, burn it with a lighter, or tie it with a cloth—not too tight or too loose—and loosen it every twenty minutes before rushing to the hospital. Venomous snakes have strong survival abilities, surviving without eating for two to three years. Thai snakes mate for 36 hours and continue laying eggs for two years afterward, so many people believe that snake whip has benefits for boosting yang energy and kidney health.

During the few days in Thailand, traffic jams were frequent, so wherever we went, we had to act early. The reason for frequent traffic jams is that Bangkok is both the capital and a tourist city with a large floating population. Moreover, it's related to their lifestyle. Thai people have a slow pace of life, starting work at 10:30 AM and finishing at 3 PM, with a half-hour break for snacks in between. Therefore, Thai people are poor, but their welfare is good. Education and healthcare are free for citizens, with no cost for medical treatment or schooling. In old age, they can stay in nursing homes with a monthly allowance of 1,000 baht. Thus, during our seven-day stay in Thailand, we didn't see a single beggar or homeless person. This greatly improved my impression of Thailand. I've always believed that if people are compared to eggs, the important thing is not whether the eggs are evenly sized, but ensuring a protective layer at the bottom. If the social security is poor, many small eggs at the bottom will be shattered and scattered everywhere, making the sight at the bottom unbearable. Too many broken eggs are detrimental to society. Therefore, ensuring eggs don't break is more important than ensuring they are evenly sized. What is a harmonious society? The rich are delightedly rich, the poor contentedly poor, the rich don't bully the poor, and the poor don't hate the rich—that's harmony. Society is not afraid of luxury and excess, but of starvation and death. Providing a basic line of survival and reliance for the disadvantaged can prevent many desperate actions.

Thailand, with such a thin foundation, can still provide guarantees. What about others? Where does all the welfare funding in Thailand come from? Everything comes from tourism. The prosperity of tourism brings prosperity to temple offerings, and temples use the income from donations to fund many public welfare projects. Many schools are funded by temples. In this thriving tourism industry, ladyboys have made indelible contributions. They endure hardships themselves to benefit the country, benefiting the elderly and the majority of the youth. Therefore, Thailand's ladyboys are very prosperous, and their existence has positive significance.
