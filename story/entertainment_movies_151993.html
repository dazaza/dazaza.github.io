The Nanjing Massacre atrocity, which was carried out by the invading Japanese army in December 1937, is one of the most heinous crimes committed by the Japanese fascists in China. It is an extremely tragic page in modern Chinese history. Although many of those who were in power in Japan at the time and later tried to deny that such an atrocity occurred, attempting to conceal the truth, facts are facts, and there have been continuous testimonies from people (including Japanese soldiers at the time) who experienced the massacre, providing materials that reveal the truth of the atrocity. The *Diary of John Rabe* is a true record made by a German friend who personally witnessed the Nanjing Massacre while in Nanjing. At the 60th anniversary of the Marco Polo Bridge Incident, which marked the beginning of China's full-scale resistance war, this long-sealed yet historically valuable material was published for the first time, making it a very significant event.

The author of this book, John H. D. Rabe, was born in Hamburg, Germany, in 1882. In 1908, he came to China and soon worked as an employee of the German Siemens company, engaging in commerce in places like Beijing, Tianjin, and Nanjing. Rabe and his family lived in China for nearly 30 years; his children and granddaughter were all born in China. They formed close friendships with the Chinese people. He was a devout Christian who had deep sympathy for the Chinese people suffering from internal strife and external threats in the 1920s and 1930s. On the eve of the Japanese attack on Nanjing in 1937, he and more than a dozen foreign missionaries, professors, doctors, and merchants jointly initiated the establishment of the Nanjing Safety Zone and served as the chairman of the International Committee of the Safety Zone. He and some international friends, under extremely dangerous and difficult wartime conditions, tirelessly protested and did their best to prevent the atrocities committed by the invading Japanese army against the Chinese people. The Nanjing Safety Zone they established provided temporary refuge for approximately 250,000 Chinese civilians. In his own home and small garden (now within the Gulou campus of Nanjing University), more than 600 Chinese refugees found shelter under his protection. He also documented in his diary and other writings the horrific crimes committed by the invading Japanese army in Nanjing. After returning to Germany in April 1938, he continued to hold seminars, submitting written reports to the German authorities, further exposing the crimes of the Japanese army in Nanjing. Even after being questioned and warned by the German police and the Gestapo, he still secretly organized his diary and relevant materials, leaving these historical records for future generations.

The *Diary of John Rabe* is the most extensive and well-preserved historical material discovered in recent years regarding the Nanjing Massacre. This diary contains personal experiences, observations, and accounts of Rabe, being very specific, detailed, and authentic—no one can deny its credibility. While writing the diary, Rabe carefully preserved more than 80 photographs taken on-site, along with thorough explanations of these images. These texts objectively and fairly evaluate the actual situation and political rights and wrongs of both sides during the Sino-Japanese conflict. As a member of the Nazi Party and the head (acting) of the Nanjing branch of the Nazi Party, Rabe's narratives carry unique significance that others cannot replace. Of course, since Rabe’s activities were mainly within the Nanjing Safety Zone and only included what he and those around him saw and heard, this diary could not provide a complete and systematic judgment or estimation of the entire process of the Nanjing Massacre. This should not be overly demanded. Nevertheless, the *Diary of John Rabe* still holds great historical value. It is a blood-stained accusation of the horrifying massacre orchestrated by the invading Japanese army and serves as strong evidence of the serious crimes committed by Japanese militarists.
