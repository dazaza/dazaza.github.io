Analysis and Repair: In normal use, the working time of a refrigerator's compressor should be shorter than its downtime. If the working time exceeds the downtime, it indicates that the refrigerator is malfunctioning. The main causes of this fault are as follows:

(1) Refrigeration System

① The compressor has been used for a long time, with significant mechanical wear. During operation, the casing temperature is high while the relative temperature of the high-pressure exhaust pipe is low. Checking with a pressure gauge at the process pipe can reveal that the low-pressure value is too high.

② Excessive refrigerant has been charged, increasing the compressor’s workload. The condenser is hot at the top and near room temperature at the bottom. Too much refrigerant leads to incomplete evaporation in the evaporator, reducing the cooling capacity. A pressure check at the process pipe will show that the low pressure is higher than normal.

③ Insufficient refrigerant charge results in frost on the evaporator after prolonged operation, preventing defrosting and failing to lower the internal temperature. Additionally, leaks in the refrigeration system piping cause insufficient refrigerant in the circulation system, leading to the same issue. A pressure check at the process pipe reveals pressure below normal levels.

④ Thick frost in the freezer increases thermal resistance, affecting cold air conduction.

⑤ Improper placement of the refrigerator or excessive dust accumulation on the condenser affects the condenser's heat dissipation.

(2) Electrical System

① Malfunction of the temperature controller, or excessive temperature-sensitive agent inside the sensing tube causing higher pressure, results in prolonged contact closure time. The contacts only open when the temperature drops below the normal set point, leading to longer contact closure time than opening time, resulting in a lower-than-normal internal temperature.

② The sensing tube of the temperature controller is too far from the evaporator surface, installed improperly, which reduces its sensitivity. This prevents the mechanical transmission part inside the temperature controller from functioning, causing prolonged compressor operation.

③ Setting the temperature controller to too low a temperature extends the compressor's operating time.

④ Damage to the fan motor in an indirect-cooling refrigerator prevents proper airflow, preventing the temperature controller from reaching the set temperature and extending the compressor's operating time.

⑤ Damage to the defrost motor prevents timely defrosting. You may hear the fan running but feel no cold air at the outlet. A check of the defrost motor’s DC resistance shows infinite resistance.

⑥ Damage to the defrost heater or defrost temperature protection fuse causes abnormal defrost circuitry, leading to thick frost in the freezer.

(3) Troubleshooting Methods

① Replace with a new compressor of similar power.

② Based on the pressure gauge reading at the process pipe, reduce or replenish the refrigerant; for refrigerators with leaks in the refrigeration system, first check and repair the leak before performing pressurized and vacuum leak detection. After ensuring no leakage, add the appropriate amount of refrigerant.

③ Reinstall the temperature controller based on its performance (installation varies depending on the type of refrigerator).

④ Place the refrigerator in a well-ventilated, dry environment.
