During this period, although some women have joined the competition against men and become dominant in certain fields, the situation is still one of "a single red flower amidst a sea of green." In the workplace, without a doubt, leaders at all levels are mostly men. So, if you haven't yet joined the ranks of those decisive and commanding career "women powerhouses," then like most women bustling about in the workplace, you likely encounter male superiors on a daily basis. Although it's common for a few female colleagues or friends to gather in private circles and comment critically about their male superiors, male superiors might also benefit from listening to what their female subordinates have to say, which could help improve their management.

### Type 1: Dedicated and Professional

Interviewee: Yu Qing, Profession: University Teacher, Age: 38, Years of Work Experience: 15

After graduating from university, I was assigned to work in my hometown, but due to dissatisfaction with that job, I worked hard and got accepted into a graduate program. After much effort, I was reassigned to my current university. Due to my varied experiences, I've encountered many male superiors, but the one I've interacted with the longest is my current department head. The best word to describe him would be "dedicated and professional." Our department is research-oriented, and he is an accomplished researcher in his academic field. He continuously improves his skills, often publishes academic papers in professional journals, and has authored more than one academic monograph. To us younger ones, he acts as a mentor, guiding us in our work and supporting us academically. Like many scholars, after years of work, his position remains unchanged. However, with his sincerity and down-to-earth approach, he firmly leads our department, and no one can compete with him. Having such a superior is truly a blessing for subordinates.

### Type 2: Opportunistic

Interviewee: Xin'er, Profession: Secretary, Age: 23, Years of Work Experience: 1

There's an old saying: Men fear choosing the wrong profession, and women fear marrying the wrong man. But women also fear entering the wrong field. Talking about my boss fills me with resentment. My workplace is a local branch of a certain organization, where there aren’t many people; including the driver, there are only eight of us, and I am the only female. Although my title is secretary, in reality, I do various odd jobs. Normally, I draft documents, send faxes, help other colleagues type manuscripts, and clean up after work. The work is relatively relaxed. When I first joined the unit, the station chief gave a grand speech about providing equal growth platforms for everyone, saying that as long as we work hard, we will have limitless opportunities to soar. The next day, however, instead of building platforms, a pile of rules regarding punctuality for clocking in and out was handed to us.

Over time, I realized that staying in such a place offered a bleak future. I just went through the motions, maintaining a certain distance from my boss while observing how he dealt with others. When facing those who held power and money, he flattered them excessively, changing his demeanor based on their expressions. But when dealing with us subordinates, he took on a "high-and-mighty" attitude, issuing orders. His wolfish and sheep-like nature was fully displayed. Working under such a boss lacks security; you never know when you might fall victim to his schemes. If it were just that, for survival's sake, it might be forgivable, but unfortunately, he also exhibits inappropriate behavior towards women, which is disgusting!

My plan is to quickly find another job. I’ve already signed up for the civil service exam, and hopefully, I’ll soon escape this predicament.

### Type 3: Mature and Steady

Interviewee: Yan Yuhuang, Profession: Copywriter, Age: 22, Years of Work Experience: Half a year

I started working at my current company last June. A few others joined around the same time, but they had several years of work experience, so they adapted quickly, whereas I felt quite lost. During my first meeting with the boss, I was nervous, like a newlywed bride meeting her mother-in-law, unable to relax. Seeing the boss dressed casually yet exuding authority, I felt a sense of distance, thinking that this boss must be rigid and harsh, and someone like me who values freedom would definitely have a tough time.

Later, I found out my initial intuition was wrong. In terms of work, the boss is extremely responsible and serious, maintaining a strict style. But outside of work, he is actually quite humorous. Some colleagues even called him a "two-faced person." For example, during lunch, he often tells jokes that make us lose our appetite, while he enjoys his meal thoroughly and asks us why we're not eating, claiming he’s treating us! The boss is quite considerate and caring towards his subordinates. Once, because clients from another region were coming the next day for business negotiations, we had to prepare materials the night before, which meant overtime work. No one knew how late we'd have to stay. Everyone skipped dinner and worked intensely until 1 a.m., finally finishing the task. The entire time, the boss stayed with us, and once the work was done, he heated up his food and ate with us, which was very touching.
