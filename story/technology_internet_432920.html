Comedy film "Mei Lanfang" fights alone in Berlin, Wang Quan'an's new work ultimately not selected - Chinese film

by letaoehv on 2011-05-18 16:19:04

The organizers revealed that stars including Clive Owen, Kate Winslet, Tommy Lee Jones, Steve Martin, Renée Zellweger, and Demi Moore will all walk the red carpet at the Berlin International Film Festival. The jury will announce the winners of the Golden Bear and Silver Bear awards on February 14.

Political elements are beginning to emerge as a highlight.

Of the 26 films selected for the international competition section of this year's festival, apart from "Forever Enthralled" from China, the others come from countries such as the UK, Germany, France, Greece, Denmark, Spain, and Poland. As Dieter Kosslick, the director of the Berlin International Film Festival, explained when discussing the criteria for selecting competition films: "We have chosen films that almost all explore issues within a globalized world. They deeply affect the lives of ordinary people. These works narrate experiences with profound implications or tragic undertones; they reflect international conflicts or bring hope to the world."

Directed by Chen Kaige, "Forever Enthralled" is the only Chinese-language film among the 26 competition films in this year’s festival, while Wang Quan’an's "Textile Girl," which was earlier expected to be included, ultimately did not make the cut.

Opening film addresses the financial crisis

At the press conference, Dieter Kosslick, artistic director of the festival, said that economic recession has not affected the emergence of films. This year's festival received entries from 6107 films. Many films reflect the impact of globalization on people's lives. He believes that the global financial crisis has brought about some films with new meanings, such as the opening film of the festival, "The International." Dieter Kosslick said, "This was a rational choice. If we had discovered this film six months ago, many people wouldn't have believed it because the financial crisis had just begun. Now this film almost serves as a documentary of the financial crisis."

From the list of competition films provided by the Berlin International Film Festival, many details can be seen showing the determination of the youngest of Europe's three major film festivals to achieve commercialization. However, looking at the announced list comprehensively, there are still many works worth anticipating, such as Costa-Gavras' "Eden Is West," François Ozon's "Ricky," and the film "09 Germany" co-directed by 13 renowned directors. Meanwhile, Theo Angelopoulos, known as the father of Greek cinema, will bring "The Dust of Time." The director's persistent exploration of history and the allegorical plots intertwined within are expected to attract attention.

However, in other sections of the festival, there are still several spots reserved for Chinese films. For instance, Hong Kong director Ann Hui's "Intimacy," and mainland director Zou Peng's "Northeast, Northeast" have been selected for the Panorama section; Hong Kong up-and-coming director Kit Hung's "Silent Wind Chime" has been selected for the Forum section.

In 2009, the Berlin International Film Festival attracted a large number of popular European and American directors' films, but paid less attention to regions like Asia, Africa, and Latin America. From the nomination list of the 59th Berlin International Film Festival, one can easily see that the festival chairman Dieter Kosslick is deviating from the traditional European art film route. Films such as "Tears of Joy," "Cold Light in Electric Fog," "The Reader," "Wrath," "The Messenger," and "The Private Lives of Pippa Lee" all contain Hollywood elements such as family warmth, crime thrillers, anti-war themes, and political topics, gradually becoming highlights of the festival.

Dieter Kosslick said that moviegoers hope to temporarily escape the global economic crisis, and the Berlin International Film Festival will fulfill this desire of the audience. Compared to the past, this year's festival will offer more feel-good movies and gradually discard those darker and more difficult films from before, showcasing more comedies, romances, and other optimistic films.
