Behind the Scenes

Band Introduction:

The name of The Rolling Stones comes from Muddy Waters' song "Rollingstones Blues." They are one of the greatest bands in today's music scene, one of the most outstanding bands in the history of music, one of the most important bands in pop music history, one of the most influential bands in rock history, and one of the best live performance bands. The Rolling Stones began performing at the Marquee Club in London at the end of 1962, thus starting their extraordinary musical journey. Early works of The Rolling Stones followed the folk and rhythm & blues route. In 1965, the first chart-topping song "Satisfaction" established the basic musical style of the band. Moreover, this masterpiece had a significant impact on Western society at the time, leading to rock music evolving into a cultural expression with social significance. By the late 1960s, The Rolling Stones released several outstanding albums like "Beggar's Banquet," "Their Satanic Majesties Request," and later "Let It Bleed," all of which achieved great success and became classic masterpieces in rock history. The Rolling Stones themselves established a distinctive and appealing image. At its peak, The Rolling Stones could vividly perform blues music and their mature and rich hard rock style could be exhilarating. The exaggerated and unrestrained musical characteristics formed by The Rolling Stones made them one of the founders of classic rock. Together with The Beatles, The Kinks, and The Who, The Rolling Stones pioneered a new era of British rock culture in the 1960s. In the 1970s, The Rolling Stones released excellent albums such as "Sticky Fingers" and "Exile On Main Street." Their concerts around the world allowed countless fans to experience the infinite charm and immense joy of rock music. The Rolling Stones indeed influenced the creation and growth of many outstanding bands that followed. In the 1980s and 1990s, The Rolling Stones remained active in the music scene. Their 1988 album "Steel Wheel" caused a great sensation. The 1994 album "Vooddo Lounge" and the 1997 album "Bridge To Babylon" received good reviews from critics and impressive sales figures.

The Rolling Stones was formed in the UK in 1963, with five members: singer Mick Jagger, guitarist and singer Keith Richards, guitarist and singer Brian Jones, bassist Bill Wyman, pianist Ian Stewart, and drummer Charlie Watts.

Compared to another great band of the same era, The Beatles, The Rolling Stones were more aggressive and rebellious — they were born bad boys, wild and unruly, exuding sharp and cunning vibes everywhere, representing absolute "self-centeredness" in the later "hippie" movement. In a sense, The Rolling Stones indeed had a devilish side; otherwise, their classic work "Their Satanic Majesties Request" wouldn't have seemed so aptly named. Dark and mysterious, "Their Satanic Majesties Request" is a good work, but when it comes to the band's pinnacle achievements, the 1968 "Beggars Banquet" and the 1969 "Let It Bleed" stand out as the top picks. After the prosperous 1960s, entering the 1970s, The Rolling Stones' rebellious image diminished somewhat, even though excellent works like "Knock You" emerged, but they couldn't recreate the grandeur of the past.

Band Journey:

The Rolling Stones are one of the most successful and longest-lasting bands in rock history. The Rolling Stones band has over 40 years of history and is considered one of the definers of rock 'n' roll. Due to the deep influence of blues artists like Chuck Berry, Howlin' Wolf, and Muddy Waters on the band's core members Mick Jagger and Keith Richards, The Rolling Stones have consistently followed the traditional blues-rock path for decades.

The Rolling Stones were formed by Mick Jagger and Keith Richards. This golden duo first met in elementary school in Dartford, Kent. In 1960, they reunited through a mutual friend, Dick Taylor. Dick Taylor was not only Keith Richards' classmate at Sidcup Art School but also Mick Jagger's bandmate in "Little Boy Blue and the Blue Boys" while studying at the London School of Economics. Soon after, Keith Richards joined Little Boy Blue and the Blue Boys upon his old friend's invitation. In July 1961, Jagger and Richards performed together for the first time at a local bar. Over the next year, Jagger and Richards got to know the multi-talented bad boy Brian Jones. Jones was proficient in guitar, saxophone, clarinet, and many other instruments and quickly became friends with Jagger and Richards. Soon, Brian Jones decided to join forces with Ian Stewart, a keyboardist he recruited during his personal development period, and joined Jagger and Richards' camp. In April 1962, Jagger, Richards, and Jones officially announced the renaming of their band to "The Rolling Stones," inspired by Muddy Waters' song "Rollin' Stone."

On July 12, 1962, The Rolling Stones performed for the first time at the Marquee club in London. At this point, the band lineup included Jagger, Richards, Jones, keyboardist Ian Stewart, drummer Mick Avory, and Dick Taylor. Just a few weeks after this performance, Dick Taylor announced his departure from The Rolling Stones, and his position was taken by Bill Wyman, the bassist of the Cliftons band. After Mick Avory left and Tony Chapman proved unsatisfactory, The Rolling Stones' drummer position was finally filled by Charlie Watts. With a fixed lineup, The Rolling Stones held multiple performances at Crawdaddy club over the following months, not only gaining a large number of fans but also catching the eye of producer Andrew Loog Oldham. Although Andrew Loog Oldham didn't understand music, he was very skilled at promotion and decided to package The Rolling Stones as the bad boy image to rival The Beatles. Keyboardist Ian Stewart was asked to leave the band, but he didn't truly depart and continued to tour and record with The Rolling Stones until his death in 1985. Therefore, he was also called the "sixth stone."

In May 1963, with Andrew Loog Oldham's help, The Rolling Stones signed with Decca Records. In June, the band released its debut single, a cover of Chuck Berry's "Come On," which reached number 21 on the UK charts. By the end of the year, the band released another single, a cover of Lennon and McCartney's "I Wanna Be Your Man," which entered the top 20 in the UK. In early 1964, The Rolling Stones released another single, a cover of Buddy Holly's "Not Fade Away," which set a record by reaching third place on the UK charts. Meanwhile, "Not Fade Away" became the band's first single to enter the US charts, reaching number 48. In May 1964, the band's debut album "The Rolling Stones (England's Newest Hitmakers)" was released. Subsequent singles like "It's All Over Now" gave The Rolling Stones their first UK chart-topper. That summer, the band embarked on a large-scale tour of the United States, attracting numerous American fans. Later, their cover of Howlin' Wolf's "Little Red Rooster" became another UK chart-topping single for the band. Despite their great success, Andrew Loog Oldham wasn't satisfied and decided to have Jagger and Richards start writing their own songs. The band's first original single "Tell Me (You're Coming Back)" became The Rolling Stones' first single to enter the top 40 in the US charts. Following this, the band released a series of singles with good chart performance. In the summer of 1965, the band's single "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" topped the charts for four consecutive weeks, confirming Jagger and Richards' songwriting abilities. The Rolling Stones no longer covered traditional old blues songs; instead, their signature became strong blues riffs and rebellious, alternative lyrics, marking the beginning of the band's glorious era.

In 1966, The Rolling Stones released their first fully original album "Aftermath." Brian Jones' penchant for Eastern music was fully displayed in this album, where the rhythm guitarist Jones played the sitar in the famous track "Paint it Black." In 1967, The Rolling Stones released their most pop and glamorous album "Between the Buttons," receiving widespread acclaim. Tracks like "Let's Spend the Night Together" and "Ruby Tuesday" became representative songs of The Rolling Stones. In February 1967, Jagger and Richards were arrested for drug possession. Three months later, Brian Jones was also arrested for the same offense, but fortunately, all three were quickly released. To compete with The Beatles, The Rolling Stones released the psychedelic/experimental album "Their Satanic Majesties Request" after being released. Despite mixed reviews from the media and fans, the album ultimately entered the top three of the charts.

In early 1968, The Rolling Stones fired their former manager Andrew Loog Oldham and hired Allen Klein as their new manager. The band's next album, "Beggar's Banquet," originally scheduled for release in August, was rejected by Decca Records due to its cover featuring a dirty, graffiti-covered bathroom wall, forcing the release date to be postponed until November. This album, characterized by the band's signature blend of blues rock and country rock, received high praise, but a disaster was quietly approaching. During the recording of "Beggar's Banquet," Brian Jones, increasingly addicted to drugs, became very dissatisfied with the overly "authoritarian" Jagger and Richards. On June 9, 1969, Brian Jones left The Rolling Stones. Less than a month after leaving the band, the 27-year-old Jones was found dead in his swimming pool. The coroner ruled the cause of death as "accidental drowning," but various rumors about Jones' death circulated over the next two years.

At the time of Brian Jones' unexpected death, The Rolling Stones had already invited Mick Taylor from John Mayall's Bluesbreakers as their new guitarist. In the band's 1969 album "Let It Bleed," since Brian Jones had already participated in the recording of some songs before his departure, Mick Taylor only contributed to the recording of a few tracks on this album. Despite major changes in the band's lineup, "Let It Bleed" remains one of The Rolling Stones' finest albums. The same year, the band held their first U.S. tour in three years. During this tour, The Rolling Stones were first referred to as "the world's greatest rock band." The band broke box office records, but there were also discordant notes during this period. Among these, the band held a free concert at Altamont Speedway. Upon the recommendation of The Grateful Dead, The Rolling Stones decided to hire the "Hell's Angels" to maintain order at the concert. It turned out to be a wrong decision, as the entire concert descended into chaos that day. While the band was performing on stage, a young black man named Meredith Hunter was killed by Hell's Angels, known as the infamous "Hell's Angels" incident.

In the fall of 1970, the band released their last live album on Decca Records, "Get Yer Ya-Ya's Out!" Subsequently, the band founded Rolling Stones Records under Atlantic Records. In 1971, one of the band's classic albums, "Sticky Fingers," was released on the new label. To avoid taxes, The Rolling Stones collectively fled to France after the release of "Sticky Fingers." During their time in France, the band completed the recording of a double album. This famous "Exile on Main Street" was officially released in May 1972, and this album would later be rated by many fans as The Rolling Stones' most classic album.

In 1973 and 1974, The Rolling Stones respectively released the relatively melodic albums "Goats Head Soup" led by the ballad "Angie" and "It's Only Rock 'n' Roll." Although both albums topped the charts, they did not leave a deep impression on fans. After the recording of "It's Only Rock 'n' Roll," Mick Taylor left The Rolling Stones. The band was once again forced to search for a suitable guitarist, and several guitar virtuosos, including Jeff Beck, came into consideration. Ultimately, The Rolling Stones chose Ron Wood, the former guitarist of The Faces and Rod Stewart's band. In 1976, The Rolling Stones released their first album with Ron Wood, "Black n' Blue." In February 1977, Keith Richards and his girlfriend Anita Pallenberg were arrested in Toronto for drug possession. In 1978, the band released an album blending punk, new wave, and disco styles, "Some Girls," once again receiving positive feedback from fans. Both "Some Girls" and the lead single "Miss You" from the album helped the band regain the top spot on the charts. In 1980, The Rolling Stones released "Emotional Rescue," which again topped the album charts. In 1981, the band's album "Tattoo You" achieved commercial and critical success, but this album could be seen as the end of The Rolling Stones' glorious era. Starting with the preparation for the album "Undercover," Jagger and Richards had significant disagreements over the direction of the band, with Jagger believing The Rolling Stones should adapt to the times and Richards insisting on maintaining a traditional approach. Due to the discord between Jagger and Richards, the mediocrity of the 1983 album "Undercover" was entirely expected. Since Jagger had already decided to go solo, after the release of the 1986 album "Dirty Work," Jagger did not arrange any tours. Jagger released two solo albums in 1985 and 1987, neither of which were satisfactory, whereas Richards' debut solo album "Talk Is Cheap" in 1988 received good reviews and became a gold record.

By the end of 1988, Jagger and Richards finally reconciled and re-established The Rolling Stones. In early 1989, The Rolling Stones were inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, with Jagger, Richards, Ron Wood, and Mick Taylor attending the award ceremony in New York. That year, the band released "Steel Wheels," and the subsequent promotional tour for the album broke multiple records, generating a total box office revenue of $140 million. In 1991, the band released the live album "Flashpoint," documenting the grandeur of this tour. After the release of "Flashpoint," Bill Wyman officially left the band. The Rolling Stones did not immediately find a replacement for Wyman. It wasn't until 1994 that the band hired bassist Darryl Jones, who had previously accompanied Miles Davis and Sting, to record their latest album "Voodoo Lounge." This album became the best-reviewed album by The Rolling Stones in a decade, and the subsequent tour was even more popular than the "Steel Wheels" tour. What made "Voodoo Lounge" even more remarkable was that The Rolling Stones won their first Grammy Award with this album, winning the Best Album of the Year. In 1997, the band released their second album with producer Don Was, "Bridges to Babylon." On September 6, 2005, nearly ten years later, The Rolling Stones released their latest studio album "A Bigger Bang."
