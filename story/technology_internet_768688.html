IBM: The century-old store welcomes new challenges

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:55:50

　　If nothing unexpected happens, 2013 will be recorded in IBM's history books - the year when the blue giant encountered new challenges; the year when many IBM employees abandoned familiar ways of working to uncover undiscovered businesses.

　　In China, this source of IBM innovation and growth, Chairman and Chief Executive President of IBM Greater China Region Daniel Chan and his IBM Greater China team are still trying to endow this century-old company with new vitality.

　　Smart commerce and business intelligence

　　In the past year, even though he entered the office before seven every day, Daniel Chan often had no time for lunch. The leaders of these new businesses—"data analysis," "cloud" and "smart planet"—occupied more and more of his time.

　　Compared to the mainstream products IBM sold in China—hardware servers—these new business sectors have driven higher-quality revenue streams, improving revenue structure and profit margins. The performance map of IBM Greater China is taking up an increasingly large proportion. But it requires more attention. More importantly, these businesses are challenging IBM's accustomed sales model.

　　On October 15, at the fourth-quarter sales rally of IBM Greater China, Daniel Chan asked his over 300 salespeople: "Find the clients in your heart, integrate today’s big data, cloud computing, and social media technologies to help customers realize their dreams."

　　This was not a routine sales mobilization. Prior to this, through an online discussion involving 400,000 IBM employees worldwide, nine practices placing the customer first were proposed, including "the client's matter is our own," "foresee the future of the client," "continuous improvement and sharing of expertise," "constantly reinvent oneself," "dare to propose and implement new ideas," "harmony without uniformity," "repeated practice," "do together, now," and "establish long-term relationships."

　　These methods were established as behavioral norms, printed on each IBM employee's identity card. But in many ways, this global "self-reflection movement" seemed to have come late.

　　IBM's third-quarter financial report showed that due to a 17% drop in overall hardware revenue, the company's revenue fell by 4%, exceeding expectations. This report led eight brokerage firms to lower their stock price forecasts for IBM.

　　Previously, IBM announced layoffs of 8,000 people in the second quarter of 2013, accounting for 1.9% of its total workforce of 430,000. The Chinese market was also included. As the head of IBM Greater China, Daniel Chan spent most of his time meeting with decision-makers at critical moments to expedite transactions entering the approval and confirmation process.

　　Over the past few decades, IBM's mainframes and servers quietly infiltrated the key businesses of major Chinese enterprises: either installed in banks' core systems, or used to provide essential services to insurance and government industries, or helping those e-commerce companies needing occasional brilliance to speed up order processing.

　　Today, there is no Chinese bank's back-office department where IBM products cannot be found, and stories about IBM using its own technology and products to transform Chinese enterprises are becoming less surprising. Some old IBM customers began to feel: "IBM needs to adjust its culture and style, otherwise it cannot adapt to the dynamic demands of the market and customers." An unnamed old IBM customer said, "IBM appears increasingly impatient in its sales strategy."

　　Data shows that China accounts for about 5% of IBM's global business, of which approximately 40% comes from hardware, but the growth of the hardware market is slowing down. In China's rapidly growing internet and cloud computing market, IBM needs to accelerate. As local prices rise and data center space decreases, enterprise customers become less likely to build new data centers and continuously procure hardware equipment, making this a thing of the past. When new business models move from one-sided markets, vertical closed supply chains, towards a diversified open network system, it means that IBM can no longer use its past sales model and needs time to adjust itself. Daniel Sabbah, then CTO and General Manager of the Next Generation Platform at IBM, one of the key figures behind IBM Watson, remarked reflectively: "IBM cannot foresee everything and achieve anything in one step. We grow while learning. When the internet first arrived, we successfully developed WebSphere application server, which became a very successful product and family of products later. I led this project, and looking back, many steps were quite terrible, basically two steps forward and one step back. It took several years to understand what the market is, what customers truly need, and what enterprise users want. This is a trial-and-error process."

　　IBM adjusted quickly. In fact, to prevent losing orders due to sluggish responses, IBM Global CEO Ginni Rometty announced a new rule in the first half of 2013: "If the client has any request or question, IBM must respond within 24 hours." She emphasized that salespeople should always consult managers and leadership teams to resolve issues encountered during the sales process. She specifically launched an internal account called "think together," allowing all employees within the company to communicate with her about events inside and outside the company.

　　Rometty stated: "For IBM, change means reorganizing high-value products, opening new markets, reshaping core values, developing new skills, and building smarter enterprises. Smart enterprises will not only focus on efficiency but will become unique professional centers dedicated to delivering higher value and gaining trust from different groups."

　　Currently, the social (social) work mode adopted globally by IBM stimulates employee vitality, connecting each employee's terminal via the network, supplemented by company value standards jointly formulated and recognized by IBM's global employees. Different insights are obtained from more "unique" employees often referred to as "wild ducks," seeking improvements and establishing connections between unrelated fields, eliminating any policies and processes that do not directly contribute to creating customer value.

　　The IBM board has established corresponding regulations to ensure corporate strategy, establish risk management processes, and crisis communication plans. The internally released "Social Computing Guidelines" advises employees of this tech group: "In online social networks, the boundaries between public and private, personal and professional are blurred. By identifying yourself as an 'IBM person' within a social network, you connect with colleagues, managers, and even IBM clients." Daniel Chan recounted a recent event: IBM had just completed a contract with a partner, and instead of using traditional email posters to unilaterally announce the good news to employees, the executive responsible for internal communications immediately wrote a blog post himself, sharing thoughts with employees. Another executive responded to other employees' comments under the blog with dozens of replies. Such small stories indicate that IBM's corporate culture is undergoing a subtle yet significant transformation.

　　Daniel Chan said, "This is not the kind of anytime communication you might imagine. It has emotion, and there is no distinction between superiors and subordinates. We simultaneously extend this to clients, making our relationship with client management tighter."

　　In his view, the changes IBM is undergoing: focusing on customer experience rather than just customer relations. The difference is that IBM can perform these functions in a wiser way, delivering value to specific individual customers rather than segmented markets.

　　On October 17, IBM announced at the 2013 IBM InterConnect Conference that it would use cloud computing as a "transformation engine," combining capabilities such as analytics, social, and mobile to achieve a complete transformation of its business.

　　Previously, IBM's "Think 40" plan required each employee to complete at least 40 hours of learning annually. The company's human resources department's strategy was to achieve "distinctive customer experiences" through talent recruitment, management, skill training, and leadership enhancement. "Without a doubt, for IBM, the best representation of IBM's brand and value is always IBM's people," said Daniel Chan.

　　Moving forward in challenges

　　Mao Xinsheng, IBM China Development Center's Chief Technology Officer, is a perfect technical evangelist. At every suitable occasion to discuss technology, he tirelessly lists all the advantages of IBM's product technology to reporters: leading methodologies, excellent performance, comprehensive software and hardware capabilities, business-first, value creation. He sees the infinite possibilities of IBM product applications in various places: seamlessly integrating into various industries as a system platform, fitting perfectly into this era of ubiquitous data; helping enterprises rethink their existing IT models; achieving business transformation centered around the customer. Mao Xinsheng said, "It is difficult for us to predict the full panorama of IT technology applications, but it is easy for us to foresee its direction."

　　He is a top opinion leader in China's cloud computing field, an expert coveted by many cloud computing vendors for "poaching," and the "stabilizing force" in rumors and negative information. When Amazon snatched a $600 million deal from IBM with the U.S. Central Intelligence Agency, colleagues worriedly asked him about his views after being "defeated" by online messages. He calmly replied, "Amazon is doing what they do, making their money, but IBM is helping clients become the next Amazon." Colleagues leaving his office felt like they had been "re-energized." A month later, IBM became the primary cloud service provider for the U.S. Department of the Interior, securing approximately $1 billion in orders over the next 10 years.

　　When technical "experts" ask him some perplexing questions, such as whether IBM has already lost out in the cloud computing field to domestic internet newcomers? Over the past few decades, IBM has created many immortal classic cases. IBM's mainframe became the preferred choice due to its outstanding functionality, but now, competitors' products can win in selection as long as they are fast enough and cheap enough. What to do?

　　He answered, "They cannot do what IBM does."

　　"Alibaba is doing something very easy, it has only one customer, that is you and me, all technology revolves around the same scene. IBM meets the needs of thousands of enterprises, some with hundreds of years of history, requiring different technical characteristics and workload types, requiring the entire industry to work together to meet them, IBM needs competitors." He said.

　　When Mao Xinsheng explained all this to his colleagues and clients, the so-called "new business scenarios" had already changed significantly from the past.

　　Almost overnight, a series of technological and marketing innovations—such as ubiquitous broadband networks and data from terminal devices completely disrupted the technology market. Change is happening, and its banner reads "mobile internet," "internet of things," "big data," "cloud computing," "social." The users faced by enterprises (rather than the enterprises themselves) are the ultimate users of products and services.

　　IBM needs to inject business into technology, injecting landable, operable, and profitable business. This is like turning technology into a lump of dough, shaping it into whatever the user needs. In this process, IBM is not earning money from customers but earning money from the market together with customers, creating a better conversation foundation and forming a new ecosystem. The key is how to establish a new "communication" model.

　　Mao Xinsheng said, "This is a new model based on an ecosystem. The game has just begun."

　　Like Mao Xinsheng, IBMers who grew up on this land in China have profoundly influenced the change in IBM's business model.

　　Go to the front lines

　　As the General Manager of the Cloud Computing and Mobile Internet Business Group of IBM Greater China, Zhu Shaokang is surprisingly young.

　　This typical post-80s generation doesn't know "low-key" and chose to be interviewed in the meeting room at the entrance of Pan Gu Grand View Building 25th floor, where the room offers a broad view facing the Bird's Nest and Olympic viewing platform, adjacent to Daniel Chan's office, rarely opened to the public.

　　But what he wants to talk about is indeed so—"unlimited scenery"—this is a new source of income. In the two years since the launch of cloud computing business in China, annual revenue has grown from zero to tens of millions of yuan.

　　Mobile operators, governments, third-party companies, software developers, and every link in the new commercial closed loop will benefit. It is provided by all IBM's software and hardware equipment, deployed by partners for enterprises, making data centers land, operate, and profit. Leading the charge is Liaoyuan, an old industrial base in Northeast China; Gansu Mobile followed closely, integrating 20 million individual customers and 100,000 group customers, earning money through "cloud." Ren Shou County in Meishan, Sichuan Province, expects to generate 1 billion yuan in output within a year through the "IBM Southwest Excellent Cloud Computing Center," with an industrial output of 6-8 billion yuan within three years.

　　So, were these new business models painstakingly thought out in the meeting room?

　　Zhu Shaokang shakes his head, "Not possible." He admits that the continuous heating up of cloud computing centers in various places in recent years was an important catalyst. "Cloud is valuable because it can bring things that cannot be done without cloud, forming new business value and models." But if only resource integration is done on the original business model, today's business would be impossible to talk about.

　　Before the cloud solution landed, IBM's cloud computing in China was "too highbrow." Many data centers were filled with machines, their servers virtualized and connected, but there was no business. "Pursuing new business reveals many interesting things," said Zhu Shaokang.

　　He talked about local government officials in Liaoyuan facing enormous pressure due to urban migration of farmers, truck drivers in Hubei crying over lost goods and empty runs, and chief veterinary officers hoping to monitor the entire process from pig farming to slaughter transportation. Each case is a story of a group of people, exploring information, data, profits, and new business models.

　　Zhu Shaokang and his team did many things others were unwilling to do, dealing with people from all walks of life, even those who seem "down-to-earth." No one understands the nuances of the Chinese economy better than them.

　　He said, "I think this is also a point for reflection, are we spending too much time in the office? Our understanding of the Chinese economy is too superficial, our understanding of the local economy is too superficial. They taught me a lot."

　　In 2012, IBM's cloud revenue grew 80% compared to the same period last year. In the first three quarters of 2013, IBM's cloud business achieved 70% growth, with single-quarter revenue exceeding $1 billion. Such business cannot be compared with the substantial returns from hardware and software sales, and some cases ended without results because IBM's business system is still in the process of transformation, and most partners can only serve the original business model.

　　Over the past few decades, IBM has offered numerous classic products to enterprise users, proving the foresight of many concepts. These contributions can be described as "concept first," whether e-commerce, on-demand, smart planet... all were first proposed by IBM and then widely followed.

　　Nowadays, IBM people generally accept this idea: IBM has the responsibility—to explore customers' new business scenarios and existing business scenarios, and the necessary technical forms for their perfect combination, and under possible circumstances, let IBM customers achieve new "milestone victories" in their industries due to adopting IBM's technology.

　　This is IBM's fourth transformation in its century-long history, the outcome unknown, but if enterprises solve structural problems and aspects where leadership is not conducive to innovation through policy, then the daily management expenses and bureaucratic systems of large companies may give way to a new culture, a culture not only willing to change itself but also the entire industry.

　　More information is guiding IBM to gain another way of survival. Ultimately, the new service system will "completely turn around," new investments in software, hardware, and business models will bring IBM and its partners continuous new revenue. All accumulations just wait for the relationship to be smoothed out, plug in and activate.
