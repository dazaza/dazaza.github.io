Who is it that has been poisoned by the alluring love flower?

by da2j4g9z5o9 on 2012-02-24 17:55:35

In "The Return of the Condor Heroes," there is the poison of the passion flower, which ironically afflicts Yang Guo and Little Dragon Maiden - two characters who are rather detached in matters of love. And only the heartbreak herb can counteract this poison. Look at this ingenious metaphor: the beautiful and alluring passion flower has a fatal poison. Once you come into contact with it, you will experience immense suffering and even death. Only through the torment of having one's intestines broken bit by bit, only after going through life and death, can one resolve the entanglement of emotions and regain life.

To elaborate, love has always been this way since ancient times, causing physical and emotional pain to those caught in its web. The deeper the feelings, the deeper the poisoning. The Book of Songs said thousands of years ago, "Since Bo went eastward, my hair is like unkempt tumbleweed. Is it that I lack ointment and lotion? For whom should I adorn myself?" When the loved one is not by your side, where is the mood for makeup and dressing up? The love depicted in poetry is fragrant and enchanting. In our youth, we savor its sweetness and purity, filled with beautiful dreams. As we grow older, we add more beautiful reminiscences but also melancholy and sorrow.

What harms our beautiful love? Aging beauty or money? There is a currently popular TV series called "Touch Anything But Not Love" that bluntly reveals the modern urban attitude towards love. You can do anything, marry a young wife even when old and frail, or a middle-aged man can marry an elderly woman, just don't label it as love. Those who fall in love are considered pitiful, shameful, and destined for a miserable end. This value system or moral view reflects a kind of ideological crisis; they pride themselves on manipulating love within their grasp, being ashamed to speak of love, weary of speaking of love, and unable to articulate love.

At this point, we ask, what is love? Perhaps many people are no longer willing to answer, and if they do, their responses are often self-righteous insights mixed with worldly shrewdness.

We calculate reality and practicality so clearly, weighing "can I" and "is it worth it" in the palm of our hands. Hearts that once eagerly yearned, hearts that once got drunk from certain glances, gradually become tough, cold, and calculating. Yet, love often coexists with softness, warmth, and a slight foolishness. Too clever people often refuse to endure the bitterness of love and, consequently, cannot taste its sweetness.

In novels or movies, we may be moved to tears, but in real life, few people are willing to be like Yang Guo, waiting sixteen long years for a timeless love.

Therefore, the story of Liang Zhu is performed again and again, from movies to animated films to TV series, never tiring the audience.

Thus, I say, it is intelligence that harms love. Look at those intelligent people who would rather toy with love than get deeply involved in it. They are transcendent, steering their own ships, enjoying endless scenery along the way.
