Zhengzhou workers outside the production line

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 18:05:22

Labor costs are a major part of business expenditure. For profit-driven enterprises, if they want to cut labor costs, they either lay off employees or relocate their company to places with lower salary levels. Foxconn is doing just that - moving from Shenzhen to Zhengzhou, where Zhengzhou is gradually becoming a new manufacturing hub. In the local area, factories operate 24 hours a day producing international goods like the iPhone. Here, young Chinese workers refuse to become mere worker ants.

Near the factory, there's a bar where people of all sorts come in the evening to listen to loud disco music and dance. Lian Yulong (音), a 19-year-old inspector who works on the iPhone motherboard at Foxconn, is a regular visitor to the bar. "Dancing helps me vent my anger and stress. Whenever I'm here, I forget everything else." He uses dancing to forget the monotonous and dull reality of his daytime job.

An increasing number of recreational spaces attract workers back to the assembly line. These workers produce items that others desire but endure hardships themselves. Because opportunities are rare, their rest time is precious, and now they endure the present for a better future. Each worker eagerly awaits the weekend, though some have simple plans. For example, Bai Saihai (音), a 24-year-old worker, plans to indulge in online gaming and then call his girlfriend far away.

Now, the foremen in the factory begin to see the benefits of vacations. In recent years, "worker suicides" have been an ongoing shadow over companies like Foxconn and Samsung. After several reports about "sweatshops," facing external pressure, Foxconn improved worker benefits and promised not to make them work overtime. However, after another suicide incident at the Zhengzhou factory, Foxconn initiated a "silent mode," disallowing workers to discuss non-work topics during shifts. Despite Foxconn’s claim of canceling this rule under external scrutiny, workers say everything remains the same as before.

The domestic factory environment cannot compare to foreign ones. Overseas workers enjoy high allowances with benefits such as haircuts, climbing walls, table football, etc., and even luxurious office settings. Domestic workers wear uniforms and endure routine scolding and yelling from supervisors. As for dormitories, rooms contain eight hard metal bed frames and a combined bathroom/toilet room, nothing more.

Perhaps because of this, there's a large market in front of the factory gates. When the sun sets, Mandarin pop music drifts out from hair salons, DVDs are sold on the street alongside sliced watermelons and roses covered in glitter. A flatbed truck full of different colored teddy bears catches the attention of young girls. Some boyfriends, following their girlfriends' instructions, immediately take out 10 RMB to buy one. This market has vans labeled with artistic tattoos and large arcade games. Young workers drink light beer while continuously smoking cigarettes.

Occasionally, elaborately dressed singers will perform on a nearby stage. As for the youth, they find sufficient entertainment in strong liquor. Luo Haojie (音) left the factory in May, quitting his job making iPhone 5 components, saying, "Our factory supervisor is vicious." Talking about the unbearable food in the factory cafeteria to a circle of drinking friends.

Ultimately, he will look for another job, but for now, he enjoys his youth, drinking with friends and dating girls. Luo says, "I stayed because of the brothers. Without them, I would be miserable."

Summer is the low season for Chinese factories, allowing many workers to enjoy weekends off, sometimes even double days. The various forms of entertainment near the factory satisfy their needs. In the evenings, mobile food stalls are set up with names resembling KFC. Acrobats perform, someone blows fireballs. Workers say it would be better to come a few months earlier when the acrobatics troupe had real lions and tigers.

On Saturday, Zhou Pengzheng (音), another iPhone 5 motherboard tester, wearing roller skates worth thousands of RMB, carefully avoided plants and began spinning. These skates cost him a third of his monthly salary. He said, "It feels like I'm flying." Before the crowd grew, he quickly skated another round.

Roller skating culture is gradually forming among those striving at Foxconn. They've formed several teams that, on weekends, put on their skates and glide through the city. Fang Timaa (音), 17, joined the hundred-member "Shadow" skating team shortly after starting work at Foxconn. However, due to her age, she couldn't work overtime and thus left the factory. She said, "I used to come to the skating rink twice a week, now I come every night."

At 11 PM, the performers have gone, and clock hotels start getting busy. "We work every day, so why not go on dates?"
