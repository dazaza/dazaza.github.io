The Eight Categories of Hysteria: Hysteria, also known as hysteria, is one of the most complex diseases in psychiatry. There are mainly eight types of hysteria, and below is an expert analysis of the classification of hysteria.

1. Hysterical amnesia: Partial or complete amnesia for events that were or are traumatic or stressful.

2. Hysterical wandering: In a state of wakefulness, wandering without plan or purpose; during the wandering, basic self-care can be maintained, and simple interactions with strangers (such as hitchhiking or asking for directions) can occur. Superficial brief contact does not reveal any signs of mental abnormality. There is an identity recognition disorder, but it is not hysterical multiple personality. Afterward, there is amnesia. The start and end are sudden.

3. Hysterical identity recognition disorder: Primarily characterized by identity recognition disorders, loss of self-identity, double personality or multiple personalities; lack of awareness of the surrounding environment, narrow peripheral consciousness or unusually narrow and selective attention to external stimuli, which is associated with the altered identity of the patient; no hallucinations, delusions or other psychotic symptoms.

4. Hysterical psychosis: Repeated episodes of fragmentary hallucinations or delusions based on imaginative life scenarios, clouded consciousness, exaggerated actions, childish and confused behavior, or stupor. Social and daily living functions are impaired. Insight into the disease may be impaired, showing indifference to the illness.

5. Hysterical possession disorder: Primarily characterized by identity recognition disorders such as possession by gods, demons, or the spirits of the dead, which replace their own identity, reaching the level of delusion. This occurs under a strong religious or superstitious background and is a culturally related hysteria triggered by obvious psychosocial factors. Clinical manifestations primarily involve possession by deities, ghosts, or spirits. Patients often have a hysterical personality, and some have a history of previous hysterical episodes. It should be differentiated from possession states induced through rituals like prayers or sacrifices performed by witch doctors or shamans.

6. Hysterical motor disorders: Triggered by psychosocial factors; there are somatic motor dysfunction disorders, such as limb paralysis, inability to stand, or inability to walk; clinical manifestations lack a neuroanatomical and physiological basis.

7. Hysterical seizure attacks: Sudden and unexpected seizure attacks resembling certain forms of epileptic seizures, but without loss of consciousness, tongue biting, severe injury, or urinary incontinence. Attention should be paid to differentiating between co-morbid conditions with epileptic seizures.

8. Hysterical sensory disorders: Triggered by psychosocial factors; there are somatic sensory dysfunction disorders, such as aphonia, blindness, deafness, or partial or total loss of normal skin sensation (touch, needle prick, vibration, heat, cold, etc.) in part or all of the body; clinical manifestations lack a neuroanatomical and physiological basis.

Hysteria mainly has eight types, and the main symptom phenotypes of various types of hysteria vary, which helps doctors provide targeted treatment to patients.
