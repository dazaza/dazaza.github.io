Preface: In today's gaming market, when it comes to the choice of 3D game graphics, most domestic players tend to favor the lavish character style of Japanese and Korean games. A significant portion also shows interest in American-style graphics under the backdrop of Western fantasy. However, looking at the currently popular 3D games, the most distinct and influential style remains the Japanese and Korean aesthetic. If the "Kimchi-style" of Korean games can be considered a characteristic feature, then the style that seems to lack the most backbone and uniqueness appears to be what is called Chinese originality. In 2012, a large number of new Korean 3D games emerged, with more than half of Chinese players still preferring Korean online games. The deep influence of Japanese anime and the early dumping of Korean online games are the main reasons for the popularity of the Japanese and Korean art styles. The exaggerated character designs and facial features; the grandiose action moves; and the dazzling skill effects are the most prominent marks of Korean online games. This has planted a hidden suggestion in the minds of domestic players - "any Korean game must have a lavishly beautiful visual appearance." Korean developers have seized upon the soft underbelly of Chinese players, which is their endless pursuit of graphics.

AION continues to dominate the South Korean internet cafe rankings. Since the beginning of 2000, Korean online games have reaped huge benefits in the Chinese market. However, with the rise of domestic online games, the market share of Korean games has drastically shrunk. Korean developers have realized the lack of content and gameplay. In recent years, we can see in the 3D blockbusters from Korea that the graphics have shifted from meaningless exaggeration towards more refined detail processing. Although the graphics have basically reached the next generation standard, the gameplay and depth seem to remain stagnant. The most fatal issue of external cheats has never been resolved. The series of games by the veteran Korean company NCSOFT, including Lineage II, Aion: The Tower of Eternity, and Blade & Soul, best illustrate this problem.

From the perspective of graphics, perhaps only NCSoft can surpass itself? Former lead artist of NCSOFT and current lead artist of Sky City, Pang Zhe, pointed out: Korean games' pursuit of detailed graphics has reached its peak, sacrificing mid-to-low-end configuration players for the sake of graphic effects. However, in terms of gameplay implementation and world-building, there is uneven quality, ignoring the overall gaming experience.

Western Magic Epic + Western Aesthetics, the main reason for the success of Warcraft-style online games

World of Warcraft can be considered a typical example of Western art style. If we were to judge solely on aesthetics, most people initially thought World of Warcraft was "ugly beyond description." However, we cannot overlook that WoW is a highly realistic world where every mountain, water body, grass, and tree make you feel real and familiar. The personality of the characters is portrayed vividly. The artists have perfectly recreated a grand and rigorous worldview. One could say that everything visible in World of Warcraft is filled with individuality, brought to life by the game's narrative.

In contemporary online games, a large amount of Western magic elements are adopted, from professions to monsters, scenes, and costumes. Systems like faction systems and reputation achievement systems are essentially imported. From an objective standpoint, I believe that whether it's plagiarism or independent innovation, one must not lose their own style due to borrowed elements. After all, everyone would find it ridiculous to see so-called xianxia (immortal heroes) and wuxia (martial arts) characters throwing fireballs and ice arrows, wielding lightning spells, and riding mammoths.

Regarding genre classification, fantasy, high-fantasy, and science fiction basically belong to Western styles. In my memory, genres that belong to China include wuxia (martial arts), xianxia (immortal heroes), and fantasy. Many settings in fantasy works originate from Chinese metaphysics, creating a fantasy world based on Eastern elements. Domestically, whether in terms of graphics or themes, the only one that seems to have achieved some success is wuxia.

Does domestic online gaming have any backbone?

After analyzing Korean-style and Warcraft-like 3D games, we can basically identify the issues. Chinese online games lack "backbone" in their graphics. This is no longer just a problem of graphic style and individuality. Using movies as a metaphor: Korean online games are like commercial blockbusters, where you remember nothing but the visual effects. Western fantasy RPGs are like ethical dramas, making you think and imagine (for example, Avatar). As for Chinese online games, I can only describe them as "Ten Ambushes"... Graphics and substance, easy to talk about but hard to achieve. The benchmark for whether an online game possesses "beauty" is undoubtedly the combination of both. Domestic online games have been struggling to find a way out in terms of Chinese and ethnic styles. In recent years, we have already seen a large number of Chinese mythology and wuxia-themed online games faithfully adapted from original works. It can be said that Chinese developers are taking the hardest path, trying to create a truly Chinese-style online game.
