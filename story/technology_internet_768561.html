The target for Tizen should not be smartphones, but the Internet of Things.

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 19:44:19

Is the Tizen system important for Samsung phones? Very important. This is a platform war, but the longer it drags on, the less meaningful this competition becomes. The idea of building its own smartphone operating system was discussed two years ago. As of now, having Tizen hasn't significantly improved Samsung's phone business; in fact, Android has proven to be just as capable.

However, Samsung has never abandoned Tizen and has continued to enhance it by supporting the industrial chain and improving development tools, indicating there must be a strategy behind it.

At this year’s Tizen Developer Summit, one of the most significant announcements was the substantial increase in partner companies. In addition to existing partners like Samsung, Intel, Huawei, Fujitsu, NEC, Docomo, etc., eBay, Here Maps, Sharp, and Konami were among 36 new partners added. If the original partners were mainly terminal manufacturers and operators, this time the scope expanded to include all aspects of the mobile ecosystem, such as game developers, e-commerce payments, and mobile software providers.

The battle in mobile platforms is about ecosystems—only when all links have committed players can the system function effectively. Moreover, Tizen has attracted developers and platform providers at multiple levels, such as supporting programming with HTML5, CSS, and JavaScript, offering greater flexibility. Manufacturers adopting the Tizen system do not need functional authorization and can maximize customization of the system.

A better development environment and greater freedom are areas where Tizen does not lag behind Android and may even surpass it. However, the release of Tizen-powered phones has been repeatedly delayed, with the latest news suggesting they won’t launch until 2014. Given the current dominance of Android and iOS, this delay might mean waiting until it's too late.

The competition in smartphone OS has already reached a conclusion, and Samsung likely knows this. The repeated delays in launching Tizen-powered phones suggest that Samsung has its own considerations. In fact, this developer summit has hinted at signs of Tizen's transformation.

One notable piece of information from the conference is that Tizen introduced a streamlined version with a maximum resolution support of 480 x 320, requiring only 512MB ROM and 256MB RAM. Would this configuration appear in a smartphone?

Additionally, there are two other pieces of noteworthy news: first, Samsung announced that the NX300M digital camera released in October uses the Tizen system, making it the first consumer device powered by Tizen, albeit "disguised"; second, Tizen is collaborating with Toyota, Jaguar, and Land Rover to plan the launch of an infotainment system based on Tizen.

It's worth noting that Intel, another key ally of Tizen, launched the Quark chip platform targeting wearable devices and the Internet of Things (IoT) in September, with plans to expand computing chips to all electronic devices.

Samsung may very well have no intention of competing against Android on smartphones and instead seeks breakthroughs in other electronic devices, which could represent a much larger market. Since the competition in mobile smart terminals is nearing its end, Samsung must focus on the next wave, which is likely IoT and wearable devices. To penetrate these traditional industries, significant support from partners is needed. Tizen's extremely low configuration requirements and high degree of freedom offer advantages over Google and Apple, seemingly targeting this market specifically.

Samsung Digital Camera NX300M

It is said that the system of Samsung NX300M was disguised, so no one knew it was the first Tizen device before. This shows that for cameras, home appliances, and cars, the brand of the operating system isn't important. As long as the experience is excellent and the functions are complete, consumers will still buy it.

Samsung's extensive involvement in home appliances and other traditional industries helps promote Tizen, opening a door to the Internet of Things for Tizen.
