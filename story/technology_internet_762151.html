Dianping: The bone that giants can't bite, living in the discomfort zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 13:59:49

CEO of Dianping.com, Zhang Tao

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com, has postponed the company's IPO once again.

In 2011, Dianping received a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and other institutions, valuing the company at $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an appropriate time to go public within the following one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in a Series D funding round.

Given Dianping's scale and development momentum, going public would not be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that listing now would be too costly. He deconstructs the big internet (PC + mobile) into four elements—people, information, goods, and services—with each connection point presenting an opportunity for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is the seat for the fourth giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where smartphones merge "location" and "service." According to Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the image of the fourth giant.

However, outsiders don't see it this way. In recent months, with frequent acquisitions and strategic moves by BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; regardless of which of the BAT companies acquires it, it could help them achieve great success earlier. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it's still too early to predict its rise to giant status. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after acquiring Gaode and Meituan, all are directly targeting Dianping's territory—local life services centered around food, drink, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two points, Dianping's IPO would be akin to "selling at a discount." First, Zhang Tao needs some time to prove that what Dianping is doing now is something BAT cannot do. Second, Dianping is about to enter the final battle of group buying. If it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Zhang Tao ignores all speculation and rumors, stating clearly: "The internet has indeed entered an era of great integration, but Dianping will never be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is better suited to develop within the ecosystem of a giant, such as maps and email. These products can easily attract large numbers of users but struggle to find independent business models. Some, like browsers, can make money but the revenue from navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to spend money nurturing email products because they help with brand building and user retention, but I've never heard of any standalone email company succeeding." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type consists of companies facing financial difficulties, selling out for survival.

Now, Meituan has been brought under Alibaba's wing. Theoretically, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated by a competitor gaining capital and expanding rapidly. Zhang Tao, however, says, "The giants' assistance to us is limited. We do need users, but execution is even more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution capabilities and we might even get marginalized by a larger company." Furthermore, he aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would be tantamount to "choosing sides," making Dianping an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically speaking, the acquisition of Dianping is a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why would we take their money? We're not short of funds. What we need are resources. If a giant is willing to invest a small amount and provide us with many resources, I could consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only hold a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty Work, Hard Work

In the mobile internet era, opportunities for companies focusing solely on pure internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities come from those that integrate with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that the core of lifestyle services is mainly food, drink, entertainment, and leisure. Other细分 markets, such as ride-hailing, may form good business models, but it's hard to imagine them growing into giants.

For food, drink, entertainment, and leisure, user demands are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of lifestyle services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, newcomers will find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Now, group buying has entered the championship battle, and in 2013, Dianping must duel with Meituan. This is a winner-takes-all market. As long as there are two strong players, the industry's gross profit margin will be pulled down, making stable profits impossible.

Another visible future is reservations. Reservations can form a true online-to-offline closed loop, but they must be built on the foundation of Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts; otherwise, creating a closed loop is difficult. In 2012, Dianping began experimenting with reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, both users and merchants provide positive feedback, but if we focus solely on reservations, it’s hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, lifestyle services present some heavier opportunities. For example, food delivery meets users' rigid demands and helps merchants increase sales. In the past couple of years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet" have thrived. However, the core of food delivery is delivery itself—delivering meals within a very short time frame while solving issues like soup spills. The logistics requirements are even higher than those for fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "Food delivery, if executed well, does have opportunities, but it's harder dirty work. What Dianping is doing now is already dirty and tough enough."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has painstakingly accumulated merchant information and user reviews, which Zhang Tao refers to as "dirty work, hard work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but past pains have become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the lifestyle services sector.

Still, he must remain vigilant against BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users who can use the map to search for nearby dining and other lifestyle services. Tencent, considered to have secured a ticket to the mobile internet era, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and expanding its offerings from physical goods to services is natural. Moreover, it has incorporated Meituan and Gaode into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there hasn't been an explosive growth in usage or data yet," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to eat, drink, entertain, or relax.

Moreover, in the field of lifestyle services, with immense imaginative potential, competition among giants is bound to be cross-sectoral, and no one can completely monopolize the area. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that in this way, Dianping is actually safer. "In niche areas like car rentals and business travel, the massive user base directed by giants could easily overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in Gaode, invading Dianping's territory won't be easy either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can be categorized no matter how peculiar. A user's dining experience depends on various factors such as the day's environment, waitstaff, and chef. Managing merchants can't rely on systems and tools like Alibaba's; it requires establishing 'ground forces' managed by people.

"The three giants all want to walk this path, and there is imaginative space, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road." Zhang Tao believes that first, providing lifestyle services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, which BAT lacks the gene and capability to manage offline teams; second, they lack the willingness. Lifestyle services require focused effort, not just dispatching a small team, and making money is slow. "A company finds it hard to excel at two important things simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate its advantages in its original field."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Turn Easily

In 2011, when Dianping received a $100 million investment, Zhang Tao told the public that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, the world changes fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogous companies had fallen from grace.

He wasn’t overly worried about it. "They couldn't become big companies because they lacked offline teams, now they want to be the American version of Dianping."

Today's path was gradually explored by Zhang Tao. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying brightened his eyes. Initially, like Americans, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform—one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized group buying had the characteristics of a trading platform. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a trading platform for lifestyle services. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used to transition Dianping from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this "genetic modification," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth giant's position today. But genetic modification sounds simple; in reality, it's equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process filled with extreme pain.

First, compared to selling ads individually, trading platforms lower gross margins and require stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, trading platforms demand higher cross-department communication capabilities.

Previously, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and sales team were entirely separate. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments—no matter how important the client, sales couldn't alter platform information for sales purposes, leading to some mutual hostility between content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, editors and sales teams need deep communication to create effective marketing plans, requiring editorial cooperation beyond mere planning. He said, "Maintaining information quality remains crucial, but there's no need to reinforce animosity anymore; we need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors logically accept this, individuals have behavioral and value inertia, and when combined across the entire company, the inertia becomes even greater. Zhang Tao specifically held several meetings to encourage more interdepartmental communication. Months later, an editor manager casually told a sales manager, "I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague."

The sales department also needed genetic modification. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team dual roles. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants are the company's buyers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must product quality be good, but the variety must be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Of course, dual roles inevitably lead to a series of complex issues. Frontline staff find this headache-inducing, as they must balance the scale themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual role" is quite similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to the frontlines allows for faster response times but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's post-genetic modification management team experiences more torment.

Every year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, then senior executives further decompose them layer by layer, but this decomposition isn't simple arithmetic. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping responsible for the sales team, sometimes finds unresolved merchant demands due to sales issues affecting performance. Therefore, Li Jing created a new position, setting up communication specialists to gather information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change, and everyone feels uncomfortable when encountering it. "All employees must go through discomfort zones, including myself."

Numbers reveal the weight of Dianping's execution team. Among the current 3,000+ employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground force is responsible for directly communicating with merchants, selling coupons, keyword promotions, group-buying, and integrated marketing solutions.

Dianping has a unique management and evaluation system for this core team that resists BAT's encroachment.

They aren't called "sales"; from day one, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the phase of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging at least three to five years behind Taobao merchants in understanding the internet. Marketing planning consultants need to tell merchants what online users need and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, the marketing consultant wouldn't suggest a group-buying deal for abalone or shark fin because online users and offline users have different needs."

An excellent marketing consultant must be a versatile player. They need to explain to restaurant owners or barbershop owners the essential differences between discounts and group-buying and determine which discount aligns better with their positioning. If facing a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant should know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise during this season, and which costs decrease, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users prefer.

This places high demands on marketing consultants, who must not only be experts in internet marketing but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every consultant to be a double expert, so we rank consultants with stars." Like hotels, marketing consultants range from one-star to five-star, with the best being called Super Star.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't elite technical graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping established a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and relevant cases accessible to both new and old employees. If encountering a new issue, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, where thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer.

Many knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees combining practical experience. Dianping set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, with employees helping others the most rewarded each quarter. Each quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, with results directly tied to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to obtain than internet knowledge. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after decades of experience in their respective industries. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that hair salons shouldn't offer wash-cut-blow dry group-buying packages because men have no incentive to buy, and women mostly just blow dry. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are not only divided by city but also by industries such as catering, beauty, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, making merchants less resistant when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a certain type of merchant encountered, what marketing needs they had, what products from the company were used to formulate the solution, and what the effect was. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition—Startup Winner. Within the first three months of joining, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If they are responsible for hot pot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a commercial plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees defend their plans on-site, with industry supervisors evaluating and rewarding the winners.

Strategically, whether Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, the tasks they need to accomplish are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are common knowledge for anyone familiar with the industry, and the words spoken are almost identical. "The era where storytelling and concept creation could lead to success is gone. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
