From Diao Si to Fallen Aristocrats: Memories of the Nokia Arcade

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 13:51:32

Recently, a media outlet published an investigative report titled "A China Market Investigation with Earthy Insights," claiming to have finally uncovered the reasons behind Nokia's declining sales. One particular statement in the article—"The perceived value of Nokia phones among the vast majority of respondents is between 100 to 300 yuan, and very few would choose Nokia if the price exceeds 500 yuan"—stirred up significant controversy. For many Nokia fans, especially those who cherished the "non-conformist" N9 and the "no shell-change" Lumia, Nokia was once a king that deserved flagship pricing. This raises an intriguing question: How could the same Nokia brand leave such a polarized impression in China?

Which model—N97 or 5230—better represents Nokia's brand image?

What phone represents Apple? The iPhone! What about Samsung? Galaxy! However, Nokia's past strategy was to dominate across all market segments—high, medium, and low-end—with its philosophy rooted in frequent cosmetic changes. When reflecting on Nokia's iconic products, one might recall the luxurious 8800, the high-end N and X series, as well as mid-range E, 5, and 7 series. The 8800 and N97 were priced over 6000 yuan upon release, while the most popular 1010 eventually sold for just double-digit figures. Therefore, when discussing Nokia's brand image, it’s essential to identify the concrete models that truly represent it. Which phone best embodies Nokia's brand identity?

In 1998, Nokia ascended to the top of the mobile phone market, becoming synonymous with premium devices alongside Motorola. Before 2005, Motorola's V3 and Nokia's N70 were unattainably expensive for most domestic users, leading to high market shares for local brands like Bird and Amoi, along with PHS (Personal Handy-phone System) phones. In 2005, the launch of Symbian 9.0 marked the rise of Nokia's smartphones, though feature phones still dominated the Chinese market. By 2007, as the domestic smartphone market began to take shape, Nokia's N73 and 5700 became bestsellers, even as most Chinese consumers were still using domestically produced feature phones and knockoffs priced around 1000 yuan.

The year 2009 was a turning point, with the concentrated release of HTC HD2, Motorola Droid, and iPhone 3GS overshadowing Nokia. Despite this, the consumer market still saw solid sales of N97 and 5800. In 2010, the iPhone 4 and HTC's expansive lineup completely overshadowed Nokia, prompting a mass migration of high-end users to iOS and Android. Nokia's response was the simplified version of the 5800—the 5230. This phone achieved legendary status, consistently outselling the iPhone in the Chinese market for several months. With the rise of "diaosi" culture last year, the 5230 came to symbolize "poverty, ugliness, and mediocrity," marking the complete reversal of Nokia's once-premium image.

The Explosion of Smartphones and Nokia's Decline

In the smartphone era, Nokia had at least one or two models each year that became ubiquitous "street phones." These included the 5700 in 2007, the 5800 in 2008, the 5230 in 2009, and the C5-03 in 2010. Street phones were typically mid-to-low-end models, affordable with sufficient functionality, combined with Nokia's reputation for durability, making their popularity inevitable. The trend is clear: starting from 2008, the price of Nokia's street phones steadily declined. While they managed to maintain a price above 2000 yuan before 2009, by 2011, the C5-03 had become a sub-1000-yuan device.

In a mature and stable market, fluctuations in a brand's perception do not usually lead to a "split effect" among consumers. However, Nokia's decline coincided with an explosive growth phase in China's smartphone market. For the small group of mid-to-high-end users who owned smartphones before 2009, Nokia was a fallen aristocrat. Faced with the homogeneity of Android and iOS, Nokia's current persistence resonates with them, as does Fan Bingbing's "non-conformist" advertisement. On the other hand, for many younger users, their purchasing power was insufficient when Nokia reigned supreme. After the 2010 smartphone popularization wave, Nokia's image was firmly tied to budget phones, leaving no trace of its former high-end allure.

A similar situation has occurred among HTC users. The distinctive appearance and performance of Diamond and HD series left a deep impression on early Dopod users, while the popularity and pricing of Desire and Incredible series influenced Android pioneers. By 2012, however, HTC's best-selling model on the mainland was the New Desire, priced around 1000 yuan. In the hearts of Chinese "diaosi," the only "rich and beautiful" options were Samsung and Apple. Balancing these two types of potential users poses a significant marketing challenge for both HTC and Nokia, both of which aim for revival.

Nokia Needs Greater Focus in Its Rebuilding Efforts

Even today, Nokia's product line continues to fragment its brand positioning, ranging from the 5999-yuan 1020 down to the 160-yuan 1050. Such a wide price gap would make even Samsung bow in respect. In China, with its pronounced wealth and urban-rural disparities, this creates a significant difference in how Nokia is perceived. During its peak, this differentiation helped boost performance and market share, much like Samsung's broad appeal and Apple's introduction of the iPhone 5C. However, for a rebuilding Nokia, this presents a heavy burden in terms of decision-making and marketing.

In Nokia's first and second quarter financial reports this year, the feature phone business showed continuous decline, with profit margins far below those of the Lumia series. This is an unpromising market. Perhaps by narrowing its product range and focusing on users interested in Nokia's core values, Nokia can accelerate its path out of losses. However, the reasons behind Nokia's decline cannot be simply attributed to any single survey. For a brand that once touched a billion lives, it would be unfair to equate the opinions of some users with the entire market's attitude. Regardless of how Nokia's brand positioning evolves, having a clearly defined competitor in the market is undoubtedly beneficial for consumers.
