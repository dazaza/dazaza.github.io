Olympiad math problems stump international math masters

by longersing on 2009-05-08 08:02:23

Just as I participated in the Gu Bai You competition, I also enjoyed participating in other competitions before. I have participated in the Mathematical Olympiad many times, especially during my elementary school days, winning awards multiple times with my best result being a second prize nationwide. Today, seeing this question, I can't answer it immediately either. If you can solve it, please don't be arrogant because this is just an elementary school Math Olympiad problem!

Question: What is the sum of the first 500 numbers in the sequence 1, 2, 3, 2, 3, 4, 3, 4, 5, 4, 5, 6...?

Answer: The first 500 terms of this sequence are: 1, 2, 3; 2, 3, 4; 3, 4, 5 ...; 165, 166, 167; 166, 167, 168; 167, 168.

Solution One: This sequence contains one "1", two "2"s, two "168"s, three each of "3", "4", "5"... "165", "166", and "167". Therefore, the required sum = 1 + 2×2 + 2×168 + 3(3+167)/2×165 = 42416.

Solution Two: Write 1, 2, 3; 2, 3, 4; 3, 4, 5…; 165, 166, 167; 166, 167, 168; 167, 168 as three sequences: 1, 2, 3, 165, 166, 167; 2, 3, 4, 166, 167, 168; 3, 4, 5, 167, 168. Thus, the sum of the required sequence equals the sum of these three sequences, i.e., (1+167)/2×167 + (2+168)/2×167 + (3+168)×166/2 = 42416.

What is the sum of the first 500 numbers in the sequence 1, 2, 3, 2, 3, 4, 3, 4, 5, 4, 5, 6...? This is an elementary school Math Olympiad question that even a renowned mathematician couldn't answer right away. Yesterday afternoon, Fields Medalist Andrei Okounkov visited Nanjing University and expressed his confusion over Chinese elementary students' intense focus on Math Olympiads. He himself has never participated in such mathematics competitions. Those overly difficult and tricky questions might very well harm children's interest in studying mathematics.

Okounkov is a Russian mathematician who won the most prestigious global mathematics award, the Fields Medal, in 2006. The difficulty of winning this award surpasses even the Nobel Prize, with only 50 people receiving it from 1936 to 2006. Some refer to it as the "Nobel Prize" of mathematics.

However, this globally renowned mathematical master revealed that he had never attended any Math Olympiad classes nor participated in any mathematics-related Olympiads. In Russia, there are no institutions specifically training students for Math Olympiads, and he himself has never attended any special schools or received specific mathematical training. "In fact, when I was in high school, I was more interested in social sciences. I participated once in a German language Olympiad and twice in economics Olympiads, but I never participated in any mathematics ones."

Regarding the current trend of Chinese elementary students focusing intensely on Math Olympiads, Okounkov strongly disapproves. "Attempting overly difficult problems could damage children's interest in learning mathematics." It's like bicycles—while they're good transportation tools, not everyone should be forced to like riding bikes, must ride bikes, or be compelled to participate in bike races.

He also told reporters that he has two daughters, whom he has never particularly cultivated an interest in mathematics. His younger daughter often encounters difficult math problems, and both he and his wife help her solve them, but they've never deliberately made her tackle hard problems. "Interest sometimes comes naturally, without needing external influence or cultivation."

After chatting about Math Olympiads for a while, the reporter pulled out a prepared elementary school Math Olympiad question for the mathematician to try. "Surprise!" Upon hearing the reporter wanted him to solve the question on the spot, he burst into laughter, showing no intention to refuse.

"The question is this: 1, 2, 3, 2, 3, 4, 3, 4, 5, 4, 5, 6... What is the sum of the first 500 numbers?" After listening briefly, Okounkov smiled easily, "Oh, that's simple." The reporter handed him the question along with a piece of scratch paper. He read the question several times carefully, lips pursed, head down, yet hesitated to write anything. A few minutes passed, and the reporter still hadn't seen him calculate the answer. Just as the reporter was puzzled, Okounkov laughed somewhat sheepishly, "Can I skip this question? My thoughts seem a bit confused..."
