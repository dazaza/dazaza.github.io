Identification of top-grain leather and genuine second-layer leather

by yangliu435sh on 2012-02-21 17:41:33

Leather can be divided into three types: genuine top-grain leather, genuine second-layer leather, and composite leather. Genuine top-grain leather and genuine second-layer leather are from the cow's skin and retain the original skin characteristics such as pores and clear skin texture. They consist of a dense and thin fiber layer and a slightly looser transitional layer that is tightly connected to it, featuring good strength, elasticity, and craftsmanship plasticity. Composite leather, also known as second-layer leather but not from the cow's body, is made by crushing the scraps of genuine top-grain leather and genuine second-layer leather, adding polyethylene materials, and re-laminating them together. Then, chemical material spraying or covering with PVC or PU film is applied on the surface to create it. It still retains the characteristics of cowhide but lacks the durability and strength of top-grain leather.

Genuine top-grain leather can be further categorized into premium top-grain cowhide and regular top-grain cowhide, while second-layer leather can be divided into genuine second-layer leather (i.e., crushed leather) and composite leather.

Types of Leather:

(1) Top-grain leather includes soft grain leather, hard grain leather (also called smooth cowhide), soft corrected-grain leather, lightly corrected-grain leather, and soft leather. The classification is based on price, with higher-priced ones listed first and lower-priced ones last. Genuine leather surfaces have natural scars and blood vein marks, occasionally with knife cuts during processing and low-utilization belly areas. Imported top-grain leather may also have cattle identification numbers branded on it. Full-grain leather can be distinguished by pore size and density to identify the type of animal leather.

(2) There are various types of cowhide, such as dairy cowhide, beef cowhide, pasture-raised cowhide, cowhide, bullhide, uncastrated bullhide, and castrated bullhide. In China, there are yellow cowhide, water buffalo hide, yak hide, and hybrid cowhide. Water buffalo hide has coarser and sparser pores, while yellow cowhide has finer and denser pores.

(3) Sheepskin has even finer and denser pores with a slight slope, mainly divided into two categories: mutton sheepskin and goat skin.

(4) Pigskin has clusters of 3-5 hairs distributed, making it easy to distinguish. Generally, domestic pigskin is used, along with wild boar skin. South American wild boar skin is famous for its distinct pigskin pores and grain characteristics. Due to its special collagen fiber structure, it can be processed into very soft clothing leather or glove leather, which is highly valued.

(5) Other leathers like ostrich skin, crocodile skin, short-nosed crocodile skin, lizard skin, snake skin, bullfrog skin, marine fish skin (such as shark skin, cod skin, ray skin, eel skin, pearl fish skin, etc.), freshwater fish skin (like grass carp and carp skin), fur-covered fox skin (silver fox skin, blue fox skin, etc.), wolf skin, dog skin, rabbit skin, etc., are easily recognizable and cannot be made into second-layer leather.

Species Classification:

Grain Leather: Among various leather types, full-grain leather ranks at the top because it is processed from high-quality raw hides with fewer defects. The leather surface retains its natural state with a thin coating, showcasing the natural pattern beauty of animal skin. It is not only wear-resistant but also has excellent breathability.

Corrected-grain Leather: This is made by lightly sanding the surface with a grinding machine, followed by applying a coating and pressing the corresponding pattern. Essentially, it is a "facelift" for leather with defects or rough natural surfaces. This type of leather almost loses its original surface state.

Characteristics of Full-grain Leather: Divided into soft leather, wrinkle leather, and front leather. The characteristics include fully retaining the grain, clear, fine, and compact pores with irregular arrangement, a full and delicate surface, elasticity, and good breathability, making it a high-end leather. Products made from this cowhide are comfortable, durable, and aesthetically pleasing.

Half-grain Leather Characteristics: During production, it is processed and sanded down to retain only half of the grain, hence called half-grain leather. It retains some of the natural leather style with flat, elliptical pores arranged irregularly. It feels hard and is usually made from lower-grade raw hides, thus classified as mid-range leather. Due to its unique process, it has no defects or scars on the surface and has a higher utilization rate. Its products do not deform easily, so it is generally used for larger briefcase-style products.

Corrected-grain Cowhide Characteristics: Also known as "smooth cowhide," the market also calls it matte or glossy cowhide. Its characteristics include a flat and smooth surface without pores or grain patterns. In production, the surface grain is lightly sanded, and a colored resin is sprayed on the leather to cover the surface texture. Then, a water-based transparent resin is sprayed, making it a high-end leather. Especially glossy cowhide, with its bright and dazzling, noble and luxurious style, is a popular leather for fashion accessories.

Special Effect Cowhide Characteristics: Its production process is similar to corrected-grain cowhide, except that pearls, aluminum metal, or copper elements are added to the colored resin for comprehensive spraying on the leather, followed by rolling a layer of water-based transparent resin. The finished product has various glosses, bright colors, elegance, and luxury, making it a currently popular leather, classified as mid-range leather. Embossed Cowhide Characteristics: Using patterned plates (aluminum or copper) to apply heat and press various patterns onto the leather, creating a leather with a certain style. Currently, the market has popular "lychee-pattern cowhide," which uses a plate with lychee-pattern designs, hence named "lychee-pattern cowhide."

Distinguishing Between Top-grain Leather and Second-layer Leather:

Top-grain leather is used to make full-grain leather or corrected-grain leather, while second-layer leather undergoes a series of processes such as coating or laminating to produce second-layer leather. It has poor durability and wear-resistance and is the cheapest type of leather in its category.

Second-layer Cowhide Characteristics: Made by crushing the scraps of top-grain cowhide and then coating a layer of PU resin on the surface, so it is also called laminated cowhide. It is cheaper and has a high utilization rate. With changes in the process, it can be made into various grades of products, such as second-layer cowhide, which, due to its attractive appearance and low price, has been adopted by some counterfeit brands.

Method to Distinguish Between Top-grain Leather and Second-layer Leather:

An effective method to distinguish between top-grain leather and second-layer leather is to observe the longitudinal cross-section fiber density. Top-grain leather consists of a dense and thin fiber layer tightly connected to a slightly looser transitional layer, featuring good strength, elasticity, and craftsmanship plasticity.

Second-layer leather often requires spray coating or polishing before it can be used to make leather goods. It retains a certain degree of natural elasticity and craftsmanship plasticity but is less durable than top-grain cowhide.

Smell Test:

The higher the grade of leather, the fewer residual chemicals in the tanning process, resulting in a more mild smell. Generally, second-layer cowhide has a stronger smell, while domestic top-grain yellow cowhide has only a faint smell, and imported top-grain yellow cowhide has the natural fragrance of the leather itself.

Check Size:

Top-grain leather selects better leather blanks than second-layer leather, with larger dimensions, mostly over 3 meters in diameter, and uniform leather surfaces without gadfly injuries or knife cuts. Second-layer leather has a diameter between 2 meters to 2.5 meters, with many gadfly injuries.

Tear Resistance:

According to the requirements of automobile manufacturers, genuine leather should have a tear resistance of 40 kilograms per square centimeter. Specifically, second-layer leather can be easily torn by an adult after cutting a small opening. However, after cutting a small opening in top-grain yellow cowhide, it requires effort even for male car owners to tear it.

Color Observation:

Top-grain leather will not have color fading on its surface. The surface color, back color, and cross-section color of top-grain leather are all the same; while the back color and cross-section color of laminated second-layer leather appear white.

Distinction Between Top-grain Cowhide and Second-layer Cowhide:

1. Top-grain Cowhide: Composed of a dense and thin fiber layer and a slightly looser transitional layer closely connected to it, featuring good strength, elasticity, and craftsmanship plasticity. Top-grain cowhide includes soft grain leather, hard grain leather (also called smooth cowhide), soft corrected-grain leather, lightly corrected-grain leather, and soft leather. Full-grain leather can be distinguished by the fineness and density of the pores to identify the type of animal leather. Imported top-grain leather also has cattle identification number brands. Full-grain leather can be distinguished by the fineness and density of the pores to identify the type of animal leather.

2. Second-layer Cowhide: Made by crushing the scraps of cowhide and adding polyethylene materials for re-lamination, then spraying chemical materials or covering with PVC or PU film on the surface. It still retains the characteristics of cowhide but lacks the wear-resistance and strength of top-grain cowhide. Bags made from top-grain cowhide and bags made from second-layer cowhide differ significantly in quality and price. Although top-grain cowhide does not match crocodile skin or ostrich skin, it still qualifies as mid-to-high-end.

3. What is Calfskin? It refers to the slaughter of cows when they are young, with each piece of cowhide not exceeding 15 square feet (equivalent to the size of a car engine hood, each square foot equals 144 square inches, each foot equals 30.48 cm). The leather is soft and smooth, with limited production, so it is mostly used to make smaller leather goods (bags, wallets, etc.). Authentic calfskin appears clearly visible under a magnifying glass, feeling as soft and smooth as a quilt.

When identifying, check the material first to determine the type of leather. For example, if using "genuine leather" where the grain layer is unmodified and retains original natural characteristics: cowhide has tiny irregular pores; goatskin has pores resembling "tiles"; pigskin has three holes grouped in a triangular shape. Yellow cowhide has fine fibers, while water buffalo hide is coarse with large pores. For modified grain layers, carefully classify based on processing techniques.

Leather treated with aniline dye is called aniline leather, characterized by a crystalline luster, bright but not glaring, with no plastic feel.

Natural leather processed into leather generally has a thickness of 5-6mm, with the outer layer being the grain layer, having fine and tight fibers, smooth and beautiful, with good strength and wear resistance; the inner layer is the suede layer, with coarse fibers and large gaps, with a velvety surface. Top-grain leather is smoother than second-layer leather, which appears slightly rougher. After coating, moving the film or laminating, the second-layer leather may look smooth, but the cold resistance is poor, prone to wrinkles at low temperatures. Smooth second-layer cowhide, after embossing, shows no obvious defects, while second-layer pigskin often reveals groups of three pores, appearing dull in those areas.

To distinguish between corrected-grain leather and front leather, note that corrected-grain leather destroys the original grain layer and then presses patterns, mimicking cow, goat, or pig leather. These patterns lack pores and float on the leather surface, whereas front leather retains pores that can be identified upon close observation.

Synthetic leather is made by pressing organic fibers and then coating and embossing. Regenerated leather is made by grinding up leather scraps and fibers, pressing them with adhesive to form sheets, slicing them to the desired thickness, and then coating them to give them certain leather-like properties, widely used for luggage and leather items, and sometimes for shoes. To distinguish between genuine leather and artificial materials, examine the appearance first. Genuine leather lacks a base layer, while artificial materials have one. Touch it with your hand; artificial materials have a strong plastic feel, shiny, and feel cool in winter, whereas genuine leather feels smooth without a cool sensation. Press the softer parts with your thumb; around the thumb, genuine leather will show many fine, uniform patterns, disappearing when the thumb is lifted. Artificial materials may show no patterns or coarse lines that don't disappear when the thumb is lifted, indicating that the surface grain layer and the underlying mesh layer have separated.

To identify genuine leather versus synthetic leather, observe the cross-section. Genuine leather is composed of irregular fibers. Scraping off some fibers with your fingernail leaves no noticeable change in the cross-section. Genuine leather has irregular textures in different areas and smells of a fishy odor when sniffed. Synthetic leather smells of plastic or rubber, with consistent textures across all areas.

Laminated leather is a synthetic leather made by attaching a man-made surface layer onto the loose meat fiber layer of natural leather. It cannot be called "genuine leather" but rather synthetic leather using the inside layer of natural leather as a base.

Shoe Care Tips:

1. How to care for shoes that get wet in the rain? On rainy days, if shoes get wet, first remove dirt and absorb moisture inside the shoe. Use a desiccant (if none available, cardboard can be used instead) stuffed into the shoe and place it in a ventilated, cool area to dry. Replace the desiccant every half hour. After drying, apply raw chicken oil, then polish the shoes once. Leave them for two days and polish again.

2. How to store shoes that are temporarily not in use? Shoes have seasonal usage. Off-season shoes need to be stored and preserved. Focus on preventing dampness, mold, and deformation of the shoes. Clean the shoes thoroughly, removing dirt, mud, and dust. Repair any cracks on the shoe surface, then polish them. When storing, do not apply too much shoe polish because it has volatility and dryness. Applying too much and storing for a long time can cause the leather surface to crack. It's best to apply a little raw chicken oil, raw pork fat, or glycerin to keep the leather soft and prevent deformation.

3. Besides applying shoe polish, what else should you pay attention to when maintaining shoes?

(1) Avoid exposing shoes to direct sunlight or fire. Leather shoe surfaces fear exposure to direct sunlight because sun exposure and baking can quickly evaporate the oils inside the leather, making it stiff and causing the leather fibers to break easily. If the shoe has heavy sweat and moisture, it can be dried in a shaded area away from direct sunlight.

(2) Avoid oil immersion for shoes. Rubber soles of most shoes are oil-sensitive because rubber swells and deforms when immersed in oil. Although plastic soles and rubber-plastic soles have better oil resistance, they should not be soaked in oil to prevent glue separation in glued shoes. Do not add too much or too frequent oil to the leather surface to avoid migration of oils to the bonding areas between the upper and sole, affecting adhesion strength.

(3) Avoid contact with acids and alkalis. Leather has weak acid and alkali corrosion resistance, especially alkaline corrosion. Alkalis can destroy the coating layer on the shoe surface and burn through the leather fibers.

(4) Pay attention to moisture prevention and mold prevention for shoes.
