Google's story in the fight against aging and death has a new chapter.

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:14:24

Google previously announced its entry into the realm of human health with the creation of Calico, a company aimed at combating aging and death. However, over the past month, this company has remained shrouded in mystery.

Larry Page outlined Google's stance on fighting aging and death in an open letter, appointing Arthur Levinson, the former chairman of the board of Genentech, as the CEO of the new company. Yet, no specific details about any plans were revealed afterward.

Many people are curious: what kind of brainstorming will this company, always ready for a Moon Shot, bring us this time?

Recently, Dan Primack, senior editor at Fortune Magazine, gained significant insights during his trip to Silicon Valley, where he learned some further details about Calico's plans from some informed individuals in the area.

From these, we can understand the background of Calico's establishment and some of the concepts behind its stance on combating aging and death.

Firstly, the birth of Calico cannot be mentioned without recognizing the driving force behind the scenes: Bill Maris, strategic partner of Google Ventures.

Primack revealed in his article that Maris, who had served as the head of biotechnology investments at Investor AB in Sweden, has always been very interested in life sciences. However, he noticed that most pharmaceutical companies currently focus on drugs related to curing diseases, meaning that our approach to healthcare is still based on "treating illness when it occurs" to extend life.

But why isn't anyone truly focusing on the origin of numerous human diseases? Another way to put it is that aging and disease are caused by the decline in cellular activity within the human body, which is largely related to genes. Since the human genome map has been largely completed, Maris believes that it is entirely possible to find the most fundamental factors causing this decline at the genetic level. So, could we then develop targeted drugs to fundamentally prevent aging and disease?

This differs from the official statement that "Google will assign a small number of employees to work on experimental projects and attempt to achieve initial technological breakthroughs." This time, Primack learned from informed sources about some of Maris' thoughts regarding this project.

For example, Maris believes that we could find commonalities in the genes of healthy elderly people over 90 years old around the world, or conversely, identify their differences from others. Even if research does not yield methods to extend life, it at least hopes to improve the quality of life for people in their later years through disease prevention.

This idea subsequently turned into a real project, and Maris began lobbying high-level executives in biotech companies and venture capitalists to raise funds. Moreover, his lobbying skills were evident, as he frequently used the following analogy (modified according to context):

Although we don't know whether our lifespan is 3 days or 3,000 days, we all hope that this number is as large as possible. Since our lifespans are inherently limited from the start, shouldn't we invest in gaining more life when making investments?

Of course, Maris did not forget to consult Sergey Brin, co-founder of Google and director of special projects, who has always been highly interested in the investment field. Later, with Larry Page also joining the conversation, the three unanimously agreed that having Google itself drive the entire project was the best way to put this idea into action, leading to the creation of Calico.

While Google is still unable to disclose its investment in Calico (though we may see some details in next week's quarterly earnings report), Primack revealed that the current stage of investment has already reached hundreds of millions, or even billions, of dollars.

Initially, after Calico was quietly established, Maris intended to retain his position at Google Ventures while personally leading this new company. However, he did not abandon his search for suitable talent externally.

At this time, Arthur Levinson expressed interest in Calico and volunteered himself. Levinson was undoubtedly a very suitable candidate, as he held key positions in the biotech company Genentech for 15 years and currently serves as the lead director on Apple's board. Thus, he has extensive experience in bioengineering and project development. The two parties quickly came to an agreement.

According to Primack, Levinson is actively seeking suitable candidates and persuading them to join this promising plan. Meanwhile, Calico also shows great interest in some potential research teams in Silicon Valley and biopharmaceutical startups.

Although Calico's operations are still in a very early stage, at least this project, which has the potential to influence the future of humanity, has gotten off to a good start.
