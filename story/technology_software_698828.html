It wasn't until recently, when she had difficulty holding chopsticks and walking, that she realized the severity of the problem and went to a hospital specializing in spinal diseases. Although the doctors here had treated various complicated cases of cervical spondylosis, her condition still shocked them: her cervical spondylosis was very severe, and any misstep could lead to paralysis. She also had complications of hypertension and diabetes, which might result in heart problems or serious infections during or after surgery. But the most difficult issue was the stone bone disease, an extremely rare genetic-related disease where the structure of normal bones throughout the body is lacking, making them as hard as stones and difficult to heal after fractures. According to previous surgical methods, there might be difficulties in spinal cord decompression, or accidental spinal cord injury leading to paralysis during surgery, or non-healing after surgery causing secondary paralysis. After consulting experts in the province, they had not dealt with patients with such complex lesions either. Through extensive research, the doctors devised a posterior cervical single-door laminoplasty plus lateral mass internal fixation procedure and formulated detailed contingency plans. The surgery was successfully completed, and the postoperative recovery was also smooth.

Cervical spondylosis surgery, carefully performed by doctors and well-cooperated by patients, rarely leads to complications. Common complications include vagus nerve damage, recurrent laryngeal nerve damage, and superior laryngeal nerve damage. These damages can easily cause:

1. Choking on water, swallowing difficulties, hoarseness, unclear speech, etc.

2. Thyroid middle artery and thyroid upper artery damage may occur, as these two arteries often accompany the recurrent laryngeal nerve and superior laryngeal nerve to supply blood to the thyroid gland, and also participate in the blood supply of the cervical spinal cord. Therefore, their damage may cause abnormal functions of the thyroid and spinal cord, leading to a series of clinical manifestations.

3. Vertebral artery damage, especially during the removal of bone spurs, often causes massive bleeding and reduced blood supply to the brain, sometimes also affecting the blood circulation of the cervical spinal cord.

4. Nerve root and spinal cord injuries may occur when removing intervertebral discs with a trephine and scraping the posterior osteophytes of the vertebrae.

If choking, swallowing difficulties, or hoarseness and voice difficulties occur after surgery, do not panic or be afraid, because this function is innervated by both sides of the nerves. The function of the opposite side's nerve is normal, and generally, such nerve injuries are mostly traction injuries without nerve rupture, so there is a possibility of recovery. With appropriate training, swallowing difficulties will gradually improve, and hoarseness will gradually disappear. If vertebral artery damage causes uncontrollable massive bleeding, ligation surgery can be performed. Although it temporarily affects the blood flow to the brain and spinal cord, the opposite side will gradually compensate, and all damaged functions will eventually recover gradually. As for the chance of spinal cord injury, it is also very low. Timely use of high-dose hormones during surgery will reduce spinal cord injury and prevent further aggravation of postoperative neurological symptoms.

Cervical spondylosis refers to a clinical syndrome caused by degenerative changes in the cervical disc, hypertrophy of the cervical spine, neck injuries, etc., leading to cervical osteophyte formation, or protrusion of the intervertebral disc, thickening of the ligament, stimulating or compressing the cervical spinal cord, cervical nerves, and blood vessels, producing a series of symptoms. Mainly manifested as neck and shoulder pain, dizziness, headache, arm numbness, muscle atrophy, severe cases with lower limb spasm, walking difficulties, even limb numbness, urinary and fecal disorders, resulting in paralysis. It is more common in middle-aged and elderly people, with a higher incidence in men than in women.
