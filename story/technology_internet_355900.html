[Lead] I got married in a daze, and then had a child in the same state. When she was only a few months pregnant, the contradictions brought by marriage began to show up one by one.

Narrator: Qin Feiyang, Male, 27 years old, Civil Servant

One Saturday morning, I met him at the café he had chosen. He was a handsome young man with thick eyebrows and big eyes, and his smile was very cheerful. Before I arrived, he had already ordered some snacks and a pot of tea. I sat down across from him and smiled while exchanging pleasantries, trying not to notice his two legs and the crutches leaning against him.

Qin Feiyang described all his experiences in one sentence. He said, "I'm like someone who accidentally fell into a well and was lucky enough to be pulled out by someone. When I was lying on the edge of the well and took a look outside, that person kicked me down again. This made me feel really broken."

I didn't know if his metaphor was appropriate, so I remained silent and continued to listen. I knew that everything I was about to hear could explain all the doubts in my mind, including those crutches that kept catching my eye from time to time.

They say, "Marriage is the grave of love." Qin Feiyang hadn't even tasted much of love when he stepped into the "grave," making him feel extremely unbalanced.

My story is actually quite vulgar too. To put it bluntly, it's the story of a married man who divorced for another woman, but ended up losing both. But I think everyone's experience must be different.

I am a very immature person, which might be related to growing up in a comfortable family environment. I've been carefree and naive for a long time, and there are some things I find hard to get over, otherwise, I wouldn't have ended up where I am today.

Let's start with my ex-wife. We met when I was in senior three, and she was one year older than me and already working. At that time, the girl I secretly admired chose someone else between me and her, which made me feel very inferior, unable to focus on studying, and just wanted to play. I met her when I was playing with friends. She was beautiful, but her personality was completely different from what I liked. However, I still pursued her fiercely. Looking back, I felt like I was just trying to prove that I was still liked by some girls.

Anyway, I managed to win her over, and I thought at least she liked me. After dating for a while, the college entrance exam approached, and I suddenly wanted to study hard. At that time, I wanted to break up with her, but couldn't find any reason, so I just drifted apart. For a month before the exam, I didn't contact her, and after a period of intense studying, I surprisingly got admitted to university.

During my four years at university, I lived a rather pale life, just getting a graduation certificate, and none of the experiences others had were mine, such as falling in love. After entering university, I continued our relationship, but except for occasional meetings, I hardly felt the emotions of being in love. I always wanted to break up, but never found the courage, especially after we became physically intimate, breaking up seemed even harder.

It happened during the second semester of my first year at university. One day, she came to me asking if I could help her borrow 20,000 yuan. Her father was sick and needed surgery. I knew her family situation; her mother was laid off, her father was in poor health, and their financial condition was relatively bad. The first time I went to her house, I was very surprised, as if I had traveled back to the 1980s: cement floor, wooden bed, small TV, small refrigerator, everything was more than twenty years old. If I hadn't seen it with my own eyes, I would hardly believe that in the 21st century, people still lived like that. But at that time, I didn't think this had anything to do with my relationship with her.

When her father was ill, her family borrowed money from all their relatives, but they were still short of 20,000 yuan. With no other options, she turned to me, and I asked my parents for the money and handed it over to her. When she received the money, she looked relieved, but also somewhat disheartened. She invited me to stay for dinner and prepared some wine. That night, I stayed over, and under the influence of alcohol and hormones, we became physically intimate. The next morning, she cried and told me that it wasn't because she wanted to repay me, but because she really wanted to marry me. However, she also said, "You've seen our family conditions. If you want to break up, it's fine." Her words made me feel that no matter what, I couldn't abandon her, or I would be just like the heartless men in movies.

I decided to take responsibility for what I had done, but of course, I had to pay the price. The deeper our relationship grew, the more I interacted with her family, the more I realized that our families were from two different worlds. Her parents could argue over a few cents, even call the police, and ask her to call me to "mediate" disputes. Once, I rushed over and saw a large crowd gathered around, with her parents shouting loudly. I stood on the outskirts for a while and then quietly left. Honestly, I was embarrassed.

Since our relationship became more serious, I have been solving problems for her family. In the year I graduated from university, her parents got into a fight with their neighbors, and she cried and came to me for help. What could I do? I could only beg my parents, and her parents moved into our vacant house.

At that time, my parents, like me, did not approve of my relationship with her, but I was too indecisive, always feeling that I shouldn't let her down. Thus, we started talking about marriage. Getting married was like biting the bullet; there was no sense of happiness, and even smiling was forced.

Marriage is like a scale, with each spouse on one side. If both parties contribute equally, peace can be maintained; however, if one party feels that their contribution far exceeds what they can bear, the balance will inevitably be lost.

So, I got married in a daze and soon had a child in the same way. The contradictions of marriage began to emerge when she was just a few months pregnant.

At that time, her parents were still living in our house, but my grandmother's place was being demolished, leaving them without a place to live. My parents asked if we could vacate the house. I discussed it with her, and instead of saying yes or no, she replied, "You decide." There was nothing I could do; both sides were elders, and I was caught in the middle, unsure what to do. What should I do? Finally, I gritted my teeth and said, "Let's buy a house!" I used my savings, and her parents contributed some money, and we bought a single apartment. When it came time to register the property, her mother cried in front of me, saying, "After living half a lifetime, I haven't even managed to own a house. How is this supposed to be good?" I have a soft heart, and I couldn't bear seeing an elder cry, so I ended up putting her father's name on the deed.

Although at the time I willingly did that, looking back, I always felt uneasy. For so many years, ever since I started being with her, I have been constantly contributing to her family, even sacrificing my parents' interests and using up all my savings. But in the end, what did I gain? Yes, she was grateful to me, and she was determined to live with me, keeping the house clean. But she thought all my efforts were something I should do. A common phrase she used was, "Marrying a man means clothing and food. Since I married you, you should help my parents." These words made me feel even more imbalanced, and extremely so.

But despite the imbalance, life went on, and I increasingly avoided going home. Before the child was born, I passed the civil service exam and met Xiaobai at my new workplace.

Xiaobai was a girl about my age. The first time I saw her, I felt like I was meeting a college classmate. Her innocence and youthful vitality gave me a familiar, relaxed feeling. Most importantly, in her, I saw the shadow of my first love. Every man has a dream lover in his heart, and when reality doesn't meet expectations, the cherished shadow in his heart becomes particularly precious. That's how I was. Xiaobai's appearance turned the shadow in my dreams into a real person, as if I had finally met the person I was supposed to meet.

Xiaobai and I quickly became good friends. At that time, I often went out with her and her friends, simply because I didn't want to go home. We'd eat together, then sing karaoke, and afterward go to a bar. Initially, I really didn't think beyond friendship. I knew Xiaobai had a boyfriend she had dated for eight years, and although they had broken up, she hadn't completely forgotten him. Many people were pursuing her at the time, and I often cared about her future from a friend's perspective.

I frequently asked her, "Xiaobai, what kind of person are you looking for? Let me introduce you."

Sometimes she would tease me by saying, "A divorced man isn't bad either. They say a divorced man is a treasure."

I just thought she was joking and never considered it applied to myself.

One day, after staying out late, Xiaobai seemed upset and drank quite a bit. She said she didn't want to go home and asked me to find a place where she could stay a little longer. So, I took her to one of our empty houses. We sat on the sofa and talked. I asked her, "Do you actually like anyone?"

She said, "Yes."

"Who is it? Do I know him?"

"You do. It's someone from our office."

I mentally went through all the single men her age in the office and asked her about each one, but she shook her head at all of them. I laughed and said, "So you're just messing with me?" She looked at me sadly and asked, "Don't you know?" I said, "Know what?" She said, "The person I like is you." I was shocked, my brain momentarily blank. Though I appear clever, I actually have low emotional intelligence and couldn't react quickly at the time.

I looked at her, and she looked back at me seriously, not like she was joking. As I recovered from my surprise, I felt an indescribable excitement. At that moment, a thought suddenly popped into my mind: "I should marry her."

Next, I kissed her. Then, we spent several passionate hours in that empty house. Just before I was about to fully possess her, I asked her, "I'm a married man. Will you regret this?" She said, "I absolutely won't regret it." So, I had her.

After reluctantly sending Xiaobai home, I returned to my own home. It was already 1 a.m., and I woke my wife up and said, "I want a divorce."

To this day, Qin Feiyang admits that he had overly simplified everything at the time. Marriage isn't playing house, and divorce certainly isn't a game. And as everything slowly settled, new issues arose between him and Xiaobai.

Once I actually proposed it, I realized that divorce isn't easy. Of course, I couldn't say it was because of Xiaobai's existence; I just told my wife that I felt imbalanced by all she had done for me. But my wife didn't believe me. My parents strongly opposed my divorce too, mainly considering my child.

Faced with obstacles to the divorce, I was disappointed. I could only tell Xiaobai, "Sorry, divorce is too difficult. Just consider me as having toyed with you. Let's end it." She burst into tears upon hearing this, crying so bitterly that my heart broke. Seeing a girl cry like that for me, I thought she truly loved me. I couldn't help but tightly embrace her and say, "Maybe we can only be together if a miracle happens, but I'm willing to try." I decided that for her, I had to take a chance.

I started causing trouble at home every day, leading to chaos in the household. My wife refused to agree to the divorce outright, but she never pleaded softly; instead, she retaliated in her own way. The Chinese often say "fighting for divorce," and I think the word "fight" is incredibly apt. Divorces in China require "fighting"; it seems like you can't get divorced without it. First, my wife checked my phone bill and confronted me with Xiaobai's number. I denied everything. Then, she hired private detectives to follow me, turned on the gas to suffocate me, and attempted to move all her belongings out when no one was home, only to be stopped by my parents. Later, she took all her jewelry, the money at home, and everything belonging to the child. Initially, we fought over "why we were divorcing," but later, it turned into fights over "how to divide the assets." My wife relentlessly pushed me, and the things she did made me feel like I didn't recognize her anymore.

Throughout this period, only Xiaobai's love supported me. Without her, I might have surrendered long ago. After fighting for two years, last year, I filed for divorce in court. Just as things were nearing an end, Xiaobai and I had a major argument over the custody of the child.

Originally, Xiaobai hadn't paid much attention to the child, possibly due to concerns raised by her parents. By then, I had already met her parents, and they had a good impression of me. But Xiaobai mentioned that her parents worried about potential conflicts between her and my child in the future. Later, she emphasized that I mustn't take the child if I divorced. Actually, I also wanted to give the child to my ex-wife because maternal love is crucial for young children. However, considering the economic situation of my ex-wife's family and the behavior of her parents, I didn't feel secure entrusting the child to them.

Xiaobai was furious upon hearing my thoughts, stating that neither she nor her parents could accept the child. "If I was merely after your wealth, I could accept the child; but since I truly love you, I can't accept a child you had with her!" Her words deeply moved me, yet I still hoped she could understand my predicament. After extensive communication, she reluctantly agreed to try accepting my child. I confidently believed that as long as we genuinely loved each other, she would come to love the child too.

In early spring this year, I finally divorced. I gave my ex-wife the car, the house, and the money, and the child was awarded to me. Immediately after receiving the divorce judgment, I called Xiaobai, wanting her to know that I could finally be with her openly. However, she wasn't thrilled, only inquiring about the child's custody. Upon learning that the child was awarded to me, she was deeply disappointed. Honestly, I wasn't ecstatic either. Regardless of the reasons, divorce is a sorrowful event. From now on, my lifestyle would undergo fundamental changes, and whether these changes would be for better or worse, no one knows. Such complex emotions are hard for others to comprehend.

I tried to convince myself that the future was bright since I could finally be with the person I truly loved. Yet, the harmony between Xiaobai and me seemed irretrievable. She acted as if possessed, constantly monitoring my interactions with my child. After the divorce, I entrusted my child to my mother. I felt extremely guilty towards my child and missed him dearly. However, whenever I visited my mother's place, Xiaobai would bombard me with questions: "What are you doing? Are you hugging your child again? Are you missing your ex-wife again?"

Her behavior irritated me greatly. During that period, we constantly quarreled, with minor arguments every three days and major ones every five. Sometimes, I wondered: Why did I bother with all this?

Some say that love also has its limits. If love turns into mutual torment, perhaps not loving each other is a wise choice. Qin Feiyang tried to let go, but failed.

For those few months, I was almost besieged on all fronts. My child missed his mother and cried daily, my mother incessantly nagged me over the phone, and Xiaobai kept arguing with me. I had no outlet for my frustrations, reaching the peak of depression.

I contemplated letting go of Xiaobai. Since we weren't happy together and I couldn't meet her demands, it would be better to break up. I told her, "I'm divorced and have a child, facts that cannot be changed. Therefore, the kind of happiness you desire is something I cannot provide. Let's break up."

She first reacted angrily, sending many hurtful text messages, then regretted and called me the next day to apologize, saying she didn't want to leave me. Actually, I also couldn't let her go. Hadn't I sacrificed my family and marriage just to be with her? When she apologized, I softened, and we reconciled quickly. But even after reconciliation, we still argued unless we didn't speak. As soon as we talked, mentioning the child would inevitably lead to arguments. One time, after dropping her off at her building, we started fighting again. She loses control when angry and says many harsh words when enraged. In anger, I proposed breaking up again. Her face immediately changed, and she ran upstairs shouting, "I'll jump off the building and die for you to see!" Terrified, I held her tightly and consoled her with many kind words. This wasn't the scariest incident. Another similar situation occurred where I couldn't endure her arguments and said, "If it can't work, let's end it!" She started sobbing uncontrollably, suddenly stiffening her body and clenching her fists, fainting. I was so frightened that my legs went weak, and I had to massage her chest vigorously until she regained consciousness and started crying again. At that moment, I believed she truly loved me and genuinely wanted to be with me. Otherwise, why would she be in such unbearable pain? I even resolved to treat her better and never hurt her again.

However, no matter how well I treated her, I couldn't pretend my child didn't exist. Whenever the child was mentioned, our arguments seemed endless. Living like this exhausted me. I looked forward to seeing her every day, yet dreaded meeting her, always on edge, filled with sorrow.

Finally, one day, she said she wouldn't argue anymore. She sent me a text message: "Feiyang, do you know what happiness is? When I lie in your arms, I feel the happiest. You must never give up on me!" This long-lost tenderness and confession warmed my heart. I rejoiced that the hardest times were finally over, and clear skies awaited us.

After so many events, Qin Feiyang, utterly drained, only wanted to hold onto what little he had left. But could this affection still belong to him? Even he couldn't answer that question.

We enjoyed a rare period of tranquility. Xiaobai began going out frequently with her friends, perhaps as a way to adjust her mood. I understood.

But later, something seemed off. I sensed she might be seeing another man behind my back, and she always came home late. One night, I waited for her downstairs and saw her get out of a car with a strange man. Overwhelmed with emotion, I approached them and asked her, "Who is he? Where did you go so late at night?" She looked surprised and awkward but didn't respond. I pointed at the man and repeated my question. She glared at me, remaining silent. Losing patience, I raised my voice again. She snapped, shouting, "He's my boyfriend!"

We stared at each other defiantly. I broke the tension first, asking her to get into my car so we could talk. She refused, yelling at me, "I can't accept your child!" I hadn't expected her to still cling to this issue, deflated instantly. Dazed, I turned to walk away. She followed, crying and staring at me with tear-washed eyes, making my heart ache and chaotic.
