by dy5200 on 2012-02-25 07:01:03

The carefree joy akin to that of "The Smiling, Proud Wanderer" might be the greater impression this intense and thrilling martial arts film left on me after its re-release.

Movies that have been aired over and over again on television are often watched, albeit passively. Each time I watch them, they bring more excitement than the new but uninteresting bad films. However, what stays with me is just the overt and covert struggles in "New Dragon Gate Inn," the sandstorms that sweep across the desert, and the red hues revealed amidst the yellow sand. I don't remember much about the movie's story; it seems somewhat like a Wong Kar-wai film—abstracted scenes and fragments, merely for the interactions happening within the inn. There’s an unknown guest who appears at the inn and exchanges moves with the alluring madam before defeating the villain. Some plot points are truly classic and leave a deep impression, such as the madam singing on the roof, using the New Dragon Gate Inn flag as clothing, and the claw that was whittled down to white bone when the villain was defeated. Watching it, one simply seeks joy and exhilaration!

This time, after "New Dragon Gate Inn" was restored and re-released, I went ahead to see it through a screening event organized by Time Cinema. Alright, this was the first time I watched the complete story of the movie. The people from the Eastern Factory killed loyal officials and then used children as bait to lure those with military power into a trap to wipe them out. The Dragon Gate Inn was a crucial checkpoint for crossing the border. The movie starts with a scene where the Eastern Factory tests their new killing weapons on ministers. Hooves kick up sand as prisoners ride horses in a desperate escape, only to become the hunted prey. The backstory unfolds, and the premise of the movie is set: the villains are very evil. The appearance of the Eastern Factory personnel has been color-restored vividly; amidst the yellow sand, small touches of red in the groups of people moving around draw attention. The makeup design is very meticulous, with rouge applied to the eunuchs from the Eastern Factory—it's quite fun and highly commendable.

Basically, most of the conflicts in the movie take place in the New Dragon Gate Inn, this notorious establishment. Various factions come and go, observing, taking action, fighting. The regulars are mostly bandits and fugitives, not good people. There are the eunuchs sent by the Eastern Factory, the group led by Zhou Huai'an and Mo Yan who are being pursued, the gang led by the madam, and the soldiers led by the district captain who frequently appear. All the main conflicts of the movie happen in the New Dragon Gate Inn, making it very concentrated. Coupled with the rain that keeps guests staying, it adds a lot of dramatic tension. At this point, the movie feels similar to the methods used in suspense or detective movies—the solving of a locked room murder case, isn't it done this way?... It's also like a play, with all performances taking place on one stage, showing great dramatic tension. Meanwhile, the New Dragon Gate Inn is still an inn, a desert inn, allowing new forces to constantly join in. With storms and shifting sands, the scenes change quickly.

Watching this movie leaves many scenes etched deeply in one's memory. The first encounter between both sides in the inn shows Zhou Huai'an and the head eunuch laughing and chatting jovially in the picture, while below is the confrontation of swords, highlighting the tense situation. Such scenes are quite common. There's also warmth, like the conversation between the knife-wielding Tartar and the two children on the roof when he offers them fruit, which is warm and adorable.

I think the most outstanding part of this movie is the brilliant rivalry between the two women. One is the seductive yet stubborn and ruthless Jin Xiangyu, and the other is the traditional yet graceful Lin Qingxia, who show mutual respect and appreciation. The scene where the two women fight over clothes makes it seem as if Dongfang Bubai is once again displaying his charming demeanor, smiling with captivating grace. Zhang Manyu's singing on the roof displays her rascally freedom, full of righteousness, daring to love and hate, fully devoted to deep affection. Everything else is secondary.

Later, Zhou Huai'an tells Jin Xiangyu that she is braver than him, able to face the loneliness and solitude of the desert and run a Dragon Gate Inn. Yes, these two women are marvels in the desert, blooming beautifully, persistent and direct.

The entire movie has a fast pace, like the drum beats in Peking Opera, sometimes fast, sometimes slow, at times like galloping horse hooves, and at others like the sparring in Peking Opera, gently pushing and pulling, slowly dragging out. The movie flows smoothly and has many comedic elements. I imagine that Tsui Hark and the team had a lot of fun. What background stories, officialdom, and martial arts world are all cast aside, leaving only the carefree grudges and grievances of this small New Dragon Gate Inn. The martial arts are spectacular, but watching the restored version of the New Dragon Gate Inn, or possibly because there's a review to write for Time Network, it always feels like looking through a mist or a veil, not satisfying enough. But I think all these are fleeting clouds. Just like the backstory and plot can be discarded, "killing one person every ten steps, leaving no traces for a thousand miles," martial arts give us a sense of exhilaration and impulse. Like Mo Yan being swept into the quicksand, she loves, acts, and will definitely have no regrets. Perhaps what we seek is just that kind of free-spirited martial arts spirit. This night, we are all in the江湖 (jianghu).
