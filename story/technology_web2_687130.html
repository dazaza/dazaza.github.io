The earliest businessman who used the "free lamp" strategy was H.H. Kung (one of the four major families of Chiang, Song, Kung, and Chen) in 1912, China. After returning from the United States, Kung observed that kerosene had become a necessity for residents to light lamps at night in Shanxi. Therefore, operating a kerosene business would be highly profitable. He then established the Hsiang Company with his fifth uncle, Kung Fan-hsing. According to the Shanghai Management Consulting Company, they paid a substantial deposit to the British company Asiatic Petroleum Company and obtained the exclusive agency rights to sell Shell kerosene throughout Shanxi Province. This exclusive operation right brought considerable profits to the Kung family every year and made H.H. Kung a comprador merchant. In the early days of the business, Kung used the "buy one get one free" strategy, meaning if you buy a year's worth of lamp oil, you get a lamp for free, resulting in the great success of Hsiang lamp oil sales. Song Ailing, while serving as Mr. Sun Yat-sen's secretary, praised Kung for his modest character and lawful way of making money, saying he seemed to have a natural talent for finance. A year later, she married Kung.

Byproduct Free Promotion Method: After Kodak launched the "Polaroid" camera in 1964, the pricing was quite low, with the cheapest model selling for only $13. Among the eight models, half were priced below $50. More surprisingly, when Kodak cameras became very popular and sales soared, Kodak announced that anyone could imitate the Kodak camera. To ensure the quality of global manufacturers' imitations, Kodak provided its technical drawings, which took 10 years to research, for free to competitors. Not long after, people realized that initially most thought Kodak's boss was crazy. However, Kodak had already considered that with the increase in camera sales, there would definitely be a greater demand for film and developing services. Thus, when global manufacturers rushed to produce "Polaroid" cameras, Kodak focused on producing film and developing services. Indeed, as global camera manufacturers increased production, the sales of "Polaroid" cameras increased by 90 times, and Kodak film sales grew by 300 times. So many Kodak cameras would "consume" large quantities of film daily, and the developed film would need to be printed. Consequently, Kodak monopolized the global printing market. Using the same method, after the launch of the "miniature" Kodak camera, prices were lowered so that everyone could afford it. As a result, the sales of Kodak film, cameras, and related equipment skyrocketed. Despite Agfa, Fuji, and Sakura drastically reducing prices, they could not match the sales volume of Kodak film.
