Twitter Zhen Huan Story: The "hard-to-tell" stories of its founders

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:21:41

From left to right, Evan Williams, Noah Glass, and Biz Stone

You might think that "palace intrigue" only happens in TV dramas like "Empresses in the Palace," but that's not true. Wherever there is an organization, there will be political maneuvering, even in high-tech companies in Silicon Valley.

If the 2010 hit movie "The Social Network" wasn't enough to illustrate this point, Nick Bilton's new book "Hatching Twitter: A True Story of Money, Power, Friendship, and Betrayal" may convince you.

For example, Noah Glass is one of the founders of Twitter, but you've probably never heard of him because he was kicked out of the company as early as 2006. Actually, the name "Twitter" was thought up by Glass. So how much did Glass get after Twitter went public? The same amount as Dorsey's secretary at Square, which means pitifully little.

Glass's story is heartbreaking, and you might already see a resemblance to Concubine Hua in his story. But in this "Empresses in the Palace of Twitter," who would be the winner? Please read on slowly. But remember, these are just claims from Bilton's book; the truth may only be clear to those involved.

The Forgotten Glass: Like Empress Hua, the first rival of Empress Zhen, who met an unfortunate "unnecessary death"

Glass initially founded a podcast platform called Odeo, which faced difficulties with the launch of Apple iTunes. So Glass and his colleague Jack Dorsey pitched the original idea of Twitter to Ev Williams. Williams, who was then an investor in Odeo, accepted the idea and decided to give it a try. Then, Glass began to fully dedicate himself to the creation of Twitter.

In a 2011 interview, Glass said that Twitter was the result of "unpaid, voluntary work," and creating this service took "a tremendous amount of effort and energy."

When Twitter had taken shape, a power struggle emerged between Williams and Glass. Glass was very protective of his ideas, and he increasingly suspected that Williams wanted to kick him out of the company. Meanwhile, Glass was going through a divorce, which also affected his personal life.

Glass became increasingly anxious. At this time, he confided his fears to his friend and fellow Twitter co-founder Jack Dorsey. What Glass didn't know was that the person who wanted to drive him out of the company was actually Dorsey. Dorsey had once threatened that if Glass insisted on his stance, he would resign.

Sure enough, in July 2006, Glass was expelled from Twitter, and Dorsey became the CEO. In the end, Glass only received a small amount of cash and stock.

Not long after Glass was expelled, Dorsey started erasing Glass's name from Twitter's history during interviews. He also did not credit Glass for naming Twitter. Now, many people feel that Dorsey is the living symbol of Twitter. In the story of Twitter, Glass has become synonymous with "the victim of Silicon Valley power struggles and politics."

Bilton’s book describes a poignant encounter between Glass and Williams:

Just a few days before the announcement of Twitter’s IPO, Glass was walking down the street... and ran into his old friend Evan Williams. After an initial moment of shock, the two men, now both in their forties, chatted warmly. They reminisced about the days when they lived next door to each other, became very close friends, and then started businesses together. Politely, they agreed they should meet up again for coffee sometime. Then they parted ways.

The Ambivalent Dorsey: Seen as Empress Zhen by outsiders but truly as cunning as An Lingrong (another antagonist in the drama)

As the founder of both Twitter and Square, Jack Dorsey is widely respected. But his actions aren’t always above board.

In Bilton’s book, Dorsey is portrayed as indecisive and inexperienced as a manager, more interested in learning fashion design than running Twitter. It’s said that Williams once gave Dorsey an ultimatum: either be a clothier or be the CEO of Twitter, but not both.

Despite Dorsey’s divided loyalty towards Twitter, he was deeply hurt when Williams ousted him from the CEO position.

Hurt so much that he almost threw in his lot with Twitter’s biggest rival, Facebook.

Mark Zuckerberg once wanted to acquire Twitter; after Dorsey was fired from Twitter, he privately told Zuckerberg about this. Naturally, Zuckerberg invited him to work at Facebook, though the position was not specified. Dorsey worried that joining Facebook wouldn’t offer a high enough role, making it seem like a demotion.

Bilton writes:

While considering Zuckerberg’s proposal, Dorsey began pondering the consequences... The truth might come out, and worse still, he feared he might end up with a meaningless position at Facebook. How would that look? “Let’s keep in touch and see if there’s a suitable position for me,” he told Zuckerberg. “I need to think about it.”

The Indecisive Williams: Once in command like the Empress

Bilton’s book makes it very clear: Twitter’s early management was a complete mess. Of course, such situations aren’t uncommon in high-tech startups.

Jack Dorsey was Twitter’s first CEO. There are many examples illustrating his limitations as a CEO; Bilton mentions one detail: “Dorsey managed expenses with his own laptop and sometimes couldn’t even do the arithmetic correctly.”

Williams eventually forced Dorsey out of the company and took over as CEO himself. Williams had previously founded Blogger and sold it to Google, so he did have some management experience.

Unfortunately, Williams wasn’t much better as a CEO.

Although the number of Twitter accounts and tweets increased during Williams’ tenure, the company was shaky in many areas. Some employees felt Twitter would collapse like MySpace.

Williams' problem was indecision and sluggishness. He struggled to hire outstanding managers for key positions like CTO or COO. But when he finally hired someone, it was often a friend rather than necessarily the best candidate for the job.

This issue drew criticism from Twitter investors. Bilton writes:

This investor wanted Bill Campbell to serve as an advisor to Twitter. Campbell had served as the CEO of Intuit, head coach of Columbia University's football team, and had mentored Jobs, Google’s Eric Schmidt, and many other executives. During their first meeting, Campbell was quite concise. When Williams asked, “What is the most serious thing I can do as CEO that could harm the company?” Campbell replied, “Hiring your friends!”

Williams was eventually forced to step down, and Dick Costolo, who had entered the company as COO, replaced him as CEO. Dorsey returned to the company and became a symbol of Twitter's "creative" image. Williams remains one of Twitter’s largest shareholders.

During Costolo's leadership of Twitter, the company seemed to stabilize. Employees who previously thought Twitter would follow MySpace’s decline began to relax.

Essentially, Twitter was a complete mess during its growth phase. But its concept was excellent, so despite various problems in its early stages, Twitter survived as a useful tool.
