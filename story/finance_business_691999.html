ASEAN Wants to Strengthen Internal Economic Integration

by anonymous on 2012-03-05 20:38:49

On February 24, the Thai Ministry of Commerce held a "2012 Seminar" in Bangkok on the theme of "ASEAN Economic Community 2015 and Future Prospects." More than 500 officials, scholars, and businesspeople from ASEAN countries gathered to deeply discuss issues related to achieving economic integration as scheduled. Participants, when interviewed by our newspaper, generally expressed that only by accelerating ASEAN economic integration could ASEAN secure a favorable position in future trade negotiations in the Asia-Pacific region.

In the 1990s, ASEAN proposed establishing a unified free trade area to enhance its overall strength. In 2007, ASEAN signed an economic partnership agreement (TPP) negotiation process in Singapore, which began to accelerate, making ASEAN increasingly aware of the importance and urgency of speeding up its own economic integration construction. Currently, among the ten ASEAN countries, four—Singapore, Malaysia, Vietnam, and Brunei—have participated in TPP negotiations.

Not long ago, Surin emphasized in an interview with the Nikkei that ASEAN economic integration takes precedence over the TPP. He said ASEAN should prioritize internal economic integration, as control over regional trade liberalization being dominated by the U.S. could lead ASEAN's integration efforts astray.

Analysts believe that the economic and trade cooperation pattern in the Asia-Pacific region has begun profound changes in recent years, with overlapping bilateral and multilateral free trade areas appearing. However, within this overlap, two vaguely visible main lines can be discerned: one is ASEAN with China, Japan, and South Korea ("10+3"), and the other is the TPP. The Obama administration is pushing the TPP, which includes non-economic trade conditions with obvious American political coloring and intentions to dominate the trade landscape in the Asia-Pacific region, thus also being seen as a "free trade alliance established against China." Some ASEAN countries clearly feel the pressure brought by the adjustment of the U.S.'s Asia-Pacific strategy, realizing that only by increasing internal cooperation can they better maintain their own positions.

Mohammad Ariff, a professor at the Malaysian Institute of Economic Research, told our reporter that although ASEAN countries will ultimately choose to join TPP negotiations, ASEAN countries should seek to act as a whole to have greater influence in TPP negotiations. Ariff said that the economies of ASEAN countries are relatively small, and it would be difficult for any single country to have an impact in the TPP, so ASEAN needs to expand cooperation to form a larger, more unified market. Building ASEAN's unified market is clearly more urgent and important than TPP negotiations. Ariff said ASEAN countries must make good use of the TPP, but not as individual entities; rather, as the ASEAN integration entity, issuing a common voice.

Internal economic integration still faces obstacles such as insufficient domestic demand.

Malaysian Trade Minister Mustapa recently explicitly stated that Malaysia's foreign economic and trade relations, ranked by closeness, are: ASEAN, "10+3," "10+6," and TPP. Mustapa said that Malaysia's economic development foothold lies in ASEAN, and the TPP is the fourth priority in economic work. However, some people still express concern about this official ranking in some countries.

Some scholars attending the seminar believed that ASEAN countries have different political, economic, and legal systems and inconsistent levels of development, presenting many difficulties in coordinating economic integration efforts. There is also the consideration that some countries might use TPP negotiations to strengthen their economic and trade relations with the United States to gain more benefits, which is a concern for some ASEAN countries.

Surin emphasized at this meeting that ASEAN countries must achieve the goal of reforming and harmonizing laws and regulations by 2015 to promote trade and investment within the region.
