The simplicity is key: How photographer Peter Belanger shoots Apple products

by geekzhang on 2013-05-09 11:59:30

Who is Peter Belanger? You may have never heard of his name, but you must have seen or even possessed his photographic works; he is the photographer behind those striking and individualistic Apple product photos. As one of the top product photographers in San Francisco, Peter also counts eBay, Nike, Pixar, and Square among his clients.Your current work mainly focuses on product and advertising photography. How did you get into this industry?When I was studying photography, my focus was more on conceptual learning rather than technical aspects, with the final projects mainly intended for museum exhibitions. One day, I wanted to learn how to apply photographic techniques in a commercial environment, so I applied for an internship in San Francisco. I felt there were many opportunities because of the numerous innovative companies in Silicon Valley. In an era where computer products are constantly emerging, there are plenty of challenges and opportunities, and I enjoy working with my clients to address these challenges.What photographic equipment do you use most often?The Canon 5D Mark III is the camera I carry with me at all times. The commonly used lens is the 24-70mm, which is suitable for most situations. The depth of field achieved by this f/2.8 aperture lens impresses me greatly.Millions of people see your work every day, yet many may not have heard of you. In New York City, I see your photographs every day. How did you end up collaborating so extensively with Apple?Initially, I was just a freelance photographer with an Apple account. Over time, some intermediaries developed, and many designers and photographers entered Apple's network. Because I maintained good relationships with them, I was fortunate to receive these opportunities.Is there anything different about shooting for Apple products?Apple's team usually designs the shooting plan and drafts beforehand, and my job is to turn these sketches into photographs. We first select the product's shooting angle, then place it under photographic lighting. The materials of Apple products are carefully selected and processed, so the lighting needs to be meticulously considered to accurately showcase the product's quality. I start from one angle and then think about how to present the craftsmanship and material of the product.After this process is completed, I move on to the next step. This is the most challenging part. I need to precisely control each angle and composition to meet the client's requirements for highlighting product features. It's like processing images in Photoshop. You can't solve all the problems in one layer. I view the combination of different levels of light as different layers to achieve the best effect.When you're creating, how do you balance the actual photographic effects with post-processing effects?I try to achieve the desired effect with the camera as much as possible, but every photo inevitably requires post-processing. Regardless of how precise the product's manufacturing process is, when you magnify the image 100 times, you'll always see something that's imperceptible to the naked eye. Some surfaces that appear complete and smooth will reveal tiny scratches and defects upon magnification. Sometimes I use a method called self-destruction: I shoot from different angles separately and then combine the results in Photoshop. This method is very effective when I need to avoid certain real-world conditions or create effects that the camera cannot achieve. Last year, when designing the cover for The Broken Music Box, I combined carefully crafted images into a single composition with excellent lighting.In your works, the technical details of the products often hover between simplicity and comprehensiveness. Sometimes, outside the range covered by the flashlights, the product characteristics cannot be displayed. How do you handle these issues while photographing?The biggest determining factor actually depends on how many pieces I need to complete in a day. I try to control the number of photographs I take each day to ensure each product has enough shots. Ideally, I would first consider the material of the product and then choose the most appropriate lighting. Sometimes, to achieve the best effect, I remove the product's casing. When a product consists of many complex components, each part needs precise control of light and shadow.If time is limited or the product itself restricts, I can only simplify the control of lighting during the shoot. Once, when photographing a pair of high-end leather shoes, there was a real snake coiled around the product. At that moment, I could only use relatively simple lighting for the scene because the snake kept moving, and I had to quickly capture each shot.Is there any artist who has given you the deepest influence?It's hard to specify just one, but I really enjoy flipping through magazines. Often, I come across some highly creative pictures and compositions. Photographs or images that are completely different from my usual style often bring me a lot of inspiration and unique feelings. Of course, movies and TV also have a deep impact on me.What do you like to photograph the most?My children. It might sound boring, but it's full of fun for me. Taking pictures of them is entirely different from work-related photography; it's more like a break for me. I don't have to worry about clients' or others' opinions at all. With no pressure, I can conduct various experiments. During my kids' baseball games, I also bring my 400mm lens. My children often tell their friends, "My dad does this, ignore him." Every year-end, I show my children albums with 365 photos pasted inside. I've already taken 10 of them. Some of my family photos have even been used in Apple's marketing.Whose photographic works do you generally look at?I really like Anto Corbijin's works. I've always been a fan of his, especially since he frequently collaborates with singers I like, such as Depeche Mode and U2. His works tend towards a documentary style, and the aura of his clients is completely different from mine. Even simple images often carry strong personal styles and stories within them.What tools and software do you use in your work?In terms of software, I use Aperture to convert Canon's RAW format photos, CaptureOne to convert Phase One's RAW format photos, and Photoshop. xScope is a small but efficient software. Evernote and Dropbox help me achieve paperless office work and organize materials, while Blinkbid is used for bookkeeping. Usually, I keep my photographic equipment updated. Until last year, I only had Profoto gear, and then I added Broncolor equipment. In the studio, I use Phase One's digital back with a Sinar X camera module and the Phase One 645 camera system. Outdoors, I generally use the Canon 5D Mark III.What is your favorite movie?True Romance is one of my favorite movies. It's full of passion, showcasing people's pursuit of true love, blurring the boundaries of right and wrong. I originally wanted to name my child Alabama (one of the main characters), but my wife disagreed.What is your dream photographic subject?Products that haven't hit the market yet, products that haven't been photographed tens of thousands of times. I prefer to figure out how to photograph them myself rather than create according to someone else's will.VIA:theverge.com
