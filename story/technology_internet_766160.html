Former Nokia Executive: Windows Phone is Too Dull, Should Be Renamed Lumia OS

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:24:28

Recently, after Ballmer announced his retirement plan, many analytical articles considered one of the key reasons for Microsoft's decline was its unwillingness to abandon the "Windows" mindset and brand when facing the new mobile Internet market. Recently, a former Nokia executive in charge of developer relations suggested that the name "Windows Phone" is outdated and should be changed to "Lumia OS".

This statement was made by Richard Kerris, Nokia's former executive responsible for developer relations. Kerris is a seasoned veteran in Silicon Valley who has been in charge of developer relations at companies such as HP, Apple, Palm, and Nokia.

Kerris stated that after Microsoft acquired Nokia's phone business, it should abandon the "Windows Phone" operating system brand. This name evokes traditional desktop computers, which could make consumers think, "Why should I buy something my dad used years ago?"

Kerris indicated that Lumia is an excellent brand, and Microsoft can continue to use Lumia. Windows Phone could be renamed to "Lumia Phone" or "Lumia OS" (abbreviated as LOS).

Kerris also cited the example of the video game console Xbox. He pointed out that the Xbox did not adopt the "Windows Game Box" brand, and its business team operates relatively independently within Microsoft. The Xbox has achieved commercial success. Microsoft should emulate the policies and practices towards Xbox in its phone business and give them sufficient autonomy.

Kerris also discussed Microsoft's formidable rival, Apple. He said that if the iPhone, launched in 2007, had followed Microsoft's thinking and adopted the "MAC Phone" brand, it would not have had the same brand impact today.

Since Ballmer introduced the "devices + services" strategy, the outside world has believed that Microsoft embarked on a path copying Apple. Previously, Microsoft's self-developed Surface tablet received lukewarm responses. After acquiring Nokia's phone business, Microsoft will establish a complete product line in its business structure, including phones, tablets, mobile operating systems, and Internet services, leaving no gaps compared to Apple.

Kerris pointed out that in building a third-party developer ecosystem, Microsoft should not copy Apple, which releases numerous applications, leaving no room for third parties. Kerris said that Microsoft should prove to the world that it owns an excellent mobile operating system and powerful developer tools, welcoming third-party developers to create various applications. Kerris cited Facebook as a counterexample, pointing out that instead of acquiring Instagram and competing with third-party apps, it would be better to remain neutral and let third-party developers create their own impressive photo-taking and sharing applications.
