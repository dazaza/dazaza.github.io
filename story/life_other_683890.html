Methods for Handling a Splinter Piercing the Finger

In daily life and work, fingers are often pierced by splinters, bamboo slivers, or needles. Sometimes wooden or bamboo splinters break off and remain under the nail or in the soft tissue of the finger, causing unbearable pain. The presence of residual splinters can lead to wound suppuration. Moreover, the deep and narrow nature of such wounds facilitates the invasion, reproduction, and infection by tetanus bacteria. Therefore, it is necessary to immediately remove the splinter and eliminate potential risks.

1. If the splinter has been completely removed, gently squeeze the wound to expel any accumulated blood, reducing the chance of infection. Then disinfect the area around the wound with iodine once, followed by wiping twice with alcohol, and finally dress the wound with sterile gauze.

2. If there is still a splinter left in the wound, after disinfecting the surrounding area, use tweezers sterilized by fire or alcohol to attempt removing the splinter intact.

3. If the exposed part of the splinter is too short for the tweezers to grip, use a sterilized needle to open the outer layer of skin around the wound, appropriately enlarging the wound to expose more of the splinter. Then use tweezers to gently grasp and pull out the splinter. Disinfect the wound again and dress it with clean gauze.

4. To prevent inflammation, it's best to take antibiotics for 3 to 5 days after the splinter has been removed.

5. If the splinter pierces under the nail, visit a hospital where a doctor can cut the nail into a V-shape before removing the splinter.

Tips:

Remember! After being deeply pierced by a splinter, one should go to the hospital to receive a tetanus antitoxin (TAT) injection as a precaution.
