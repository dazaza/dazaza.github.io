Alibaba Launches Instant Messaging Service "Laiwang", Social Ambition Lives On

by anonymous on 2013-10-01 21:32:46

In the current market dominated by WeChat, Alibaba has not given up on its ambition to create a successful social IM tool, and today it launched an instant messaging app called "Laiwang". Similar to Yixin that was released not long ago, Laiwang is also used for friends to send text messages and pictures, making it a WeChat-like product.

Laiwang was introduced by the newly established Network Communications Division, which is directly managed by Jonathan Lu. At the launch event in Hangzhou today, Jonathan Lu also appeared on site, showing the importance of the entire group.

What is lesser known is that Laiwang is actually a communication tool with a history of two years, when WeChat and Miliang were just starting out. Laiwang was mainly used internally within Alibaba, initially hoping to be developed into a relationship circle between sellers and buyers, but after WeChat's rise, Laiwang gradually realized that it must go beyond e-commerce and embrace mobile internet.

According to insiders at Alibaba, the company has high hopes for Laiwang and has invested many resources in promoting this product. Many activities and lotteries within the company are completed on Laiwang, and employees with the most friends are heavily rewarded, all aimed at encouraging more people to use this product. Jack Ma has always been fascinated with instant messaging, and now under Jonathan Lu's leadership, they have still not abandoned this territory.

Tragically, Laiwang, which already has a history of two years, only managed to gain users in the millions after being opened to the public in April last year, with many people only finding out about this product today. This is partly due to Alibaba's low-key promotion, and partly because users do not know what needs Laiwang fulfills.

According to official statements, Laiwang operates independently from e-commerce, unlike Wangxin and Wangwang, focusing on social networking among acquaintances without importing Taobao or Alipay users. Compared to WeChat and Miliang, Laiwang has added some selling points in terms of functionality:

- Self-destructing messages: voice messages sent by users automatically delete after being listened to, providing safer communication;

- Group discussions: similar to interest-based groups in WeChat’s Moments and Douban groups, non-group members cannot view information;

- 240 free emoticons, group chats for 500 people, sound photos, shake-to-add-friends, etc.

These selling points indeed offer stronger functionalities compared to WeChat, but they may not necessarily make Laiwang stand out. Innovation in social products does not necessarily mean the more features the better; rather, fulfilling a certain need and triggering large-scale usage and dissemination among users can be more effective, such as WeChat’s “Shake” feature or viewing nearby friends, as well as Momo’s stranger dating. Under the circumstances where WeChat has already garnered a large number of users, the success rate of such innovations would be lower.

Yixin focuses on free SMS and voicemail, but despite being launched for some time, aside from being relatively popular during the initial period, Yixin has not posed any substantial threat to WeChat.

On the other hand, Alibaba’s e-commerce genes are both a blessing and a curse for Laiwang. Any association with e-commerce tends to make instant messaging apps more tool-like, greatly diluting their social attributes, as seen in Wangxin and Wangwang. However, on the other hand, Alibaba’s vast resources are an irresistible temptation.

Most products later launched or invested in by Alibaba are more or less related to e-commerce and local life, such as TVs and the Taobao version of Weibo launched after investing in Weibo. According to Zou Mengrui, the head of Laiwang, although Taobao tries not to associate with e-commerce and does not import relationships from Taobao or Alipay, on the other hand, Laiwang has to introduce high-quality lifestyle service applications from Ali's ecosystem, including price comparison apps, Alipay, Taobao, etc.

Just like Alibaba’s TV, it has been stuffed with various contents from Ali’s ecosystem. But in fact, Alibaba has yet to prove whether these things are truly needed by users?

The launch of Laiwang seems more like a defensive move. The "BAT" companies are all laying out strategies in mobile internet, investing or acquiring in areas such as maps, communications, social networking, e-commerce, video, etc., in order to have more strongholds for future battles. Not long ago, WeChat encroached on Taobao’s transactions, forcing Taobao to block WeChat, further proving that Alibaba needs Laiwang.

But for users, why should they use Laiwang? Alibaba needs to first figure out this question.
