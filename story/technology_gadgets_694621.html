Power Distribution Equipment Network (www.dq86.net) shares: We know that the current transformer (CT) primary winding has fewer turns, and during use, the primary winding is connected in series in the circuit being measured. The secondary winding has more turns and is used in series with measuring instruments and relays, etc., which have very low impedance current coils. Therefore, during normal operation, the CT is close to a short-circuit state. The size of the CT secondary current is determined by the primary current, and the magnetic potential generated by the secondary current balances the magnetic potential of the primary current. If the secondary side opens, its impedance becomes infinitely large, the secondary current equals zero, and its magnetic potential also equals zero, thus it cannot balance the magnetic potential produced by the primary current. As a result, the entire primary current will act as excitation, causing serious saturation of the core. Magnetic saturation increases iron loss, heats up the CT, and the insulation of the CT coil may be burned out due to overheating. It will also produce residual magnetism on the core, increasing the error of the transformer. The most severe consequence is that due to magnetic saturation, the sinusoidal wave of alternating flux turns into a trapezoidal wave. During the instant when the flux changes rapidly, a very high voltage will be induced in the secondary coil, reaching peak values of several thousand volts. Such high voltage acting on the secondary coil and the secondary circuit poses serious threats to both personnel and equipment. Therefore, at any time, the CT must not operate with an open circuit on the secondary side.

So, how do we detect CT secondary open circuit faults? Generally, these can be checked and judged from the following phenomena:

(1) Abnormal indication of loop instruments, usually reduced or zero. An open circuit in the current loop for measurement will cause inconsistent indications on the three-phase current meters, reduced power meter indications, and slow or stopped rotation of the metering meter. If the meter indication fluctuates intermittently, it might indicate a semi-open circuit state (poor contact).

(2) Whether there are noises, uneven vibrations, severe heating, or smoking from the CT itself. Of course, these phenomena are not obvious when the load is small.

(3) Discharge and sparking at the terminals and component wire ends in the CT secondary circuit.

(4) Misoperation or failure of protection relays. This situation can be discovered and handled during incorrect tripping or over-level tripping.

(5) Smoke and burning of watt-hour meters and relays. If reactive power meters, watt-hour meters, transmitters of remote control devices, and protective relays are burned out, it will not only cause the CT secondary to open but also lead to the PT secondary short circuit.

The above are just some basic clues for checking CT secondary open circuits. In fact, during normal operation, if the primary load is not large, the secondary has no work, and it's not the current loop for measurement that is open, it is difficult to detect the CT secondary open circuit fault. This requires us to accumulate experience through practical work.

When checking and handling CT secondary open circuit faults, try to reduce the primary load current to lower the voltage in the secondary circuit. Pay attention to safety during operations, stand on an insulating pad, wear insulated gloves, and use well-insulated tools.

(1) Upon discovering a CT secondary open circuit, first determine which current loop is faulty, the phase involved, and whether it affects protection. Report to dispatch and disable protections that might misoperate.

(2) Try to reduce the primary load current. If the CT is severely damaged, transfer the load and handle it after power-off.

(3) Quickly set up a good shorting wire near the test terminal according to the diagram to short the CT secondary, then check and handle the open point.

(4) If sparks occur during shorting, the shorting should be effective, and the fault point should be in the circuit below the shorting point. Further checks can be conducted. If there are no sparks during shorting, it may indicate ineffective shorting, and the fault point might be in the circuit before the shorting point. Shorting points can be gradually shifted forward to narrow down the range for inspection.

(5) Within the fault range, check terminals and components prone to faults. For detected faults that can be handled independently, such as loose or poor contact external components like connection terminals, immediate handling should be done followed by re-engaging the disabled protection. If the open point is at the wiring terminal of the CT itself, it should be handled after power-off. If the fault cannot be handled or identified independently (such as within relays), short the CT secondary first and report to superiors.
