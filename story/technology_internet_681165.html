10 Great Tips to Expand Your Baby's Oral Language Skills

by lmxbbwudi23 on 2012-02-28 11:47:24

Language ability is a crucial part of the growth and development process for children. After developing initial oral expression skills, they can further engage in social behaviors such as communication and interaction. Infancy is the best time for babies to practice speaking. If parents can pay attention to this period of their child learning to speak and guide them correctly, it will lay an educational foundation for improving their language abilities.

1. Create a Rich Language Environment

Language development has a close relationship with the environment. Therefore, parents should talk to their infants as much as possible while taking care of them. For example, in daily life, you can say things like "[This is an eye]" or "[Where is the nose?]" Starting from when the baby is six months old, you can read together and tell stories, allowing a large number of words to enter the child's ears. When the child actively asks their parents to read a story, the parents should respond to the child's request and repeat it for them to listen, helping the child develop an interest in reading.

2. Pay Attention to Speaking Techniques

Use standard Chinese pronunciation and avoid dialects or baby talk. This is because Chinese connects more smoothly with future reading and language learning after the child starts school. When the child begins to learn to speak, the way parents speak to their child should be clear and standard, providing the child with a correct learning model.

3. Listen to Children's Songs and Watch Cartoons

Children love rhythm and moving images very much. It is suggested that parents can encourage their children to sing and dance along with the music or pictures. Although babies may not necessarily understand the meaning of the lyrics, they will enjoy learning about rhythm immensely. This also helps children distinguish between characters, words, and syllables, which will greatly assist their pronunciation learning in the future.

4. Answer Questions

Engage in more question-and-answer sessions with the child. Ask questions like "[What is this?]" or "[Do you like mom and dad?]" Encourage the child to answer using complete sentences instead of short answers like "yes" or "no." This helps the child develop the ability to listen carefully and learn a large number of words.

5. Shopping

Take the child out shopping and let them participate in selecting items. Even if there are only a few items, it can give the child a sense of participation, help them understand product names, and learn more words. This can also increase the child's memory and observational skills.

6. Outdoor Activities

Children are very curious about new things outside. You might want to take your child out more often so they can learn more things. After returning home, parents can discuss the events that happened during the outing with their child. Children like outdoor activities and will remember special things like outings vividly. Repeated actions allow children to learn more words.

7. Storytelling

Parents can tell children stories about things they have done before, even if the child doesn't remember. For instance, how they "moved" inside mom's belly, cried loudly at birth, or when they first called their parents. This enriches the child's imagination and improves their expressive ability. Without picture books, the child will listen more attentively.

8. Help the Child Say Complete Sentences

When a baby is babbling, what they say usually consists of a few simple words but contains a lot of content, such as "want cookies" or "see daddy." They hope adults can understand their meaning. Caregivers should help the child express their thoughts in full sentences and then have the child repeat them, teaching them complete ways of expression.

9. Timely Encouragement

When the child uses new vocabulary, don't forget to encourage them immediately. Try to use different words, which will also motivate the child to want to learn more vocabulary.

10. Use Conceptual Words

For children over one and a half years old, you can let them understand the meaning and usage of conceptual words at any time according to daily life events. For example, "up and down" can be explained by saying "put the apple on the table"; "middle" can be explained by saying "the baby sits between daddy and mommy"; "fast and slow" can be explained by saying "the car runs very fast," etc. Once the child has a basic understanding of these concepts, they can use words more appropriately to narrate.

Most of a child's language ability is shaped by the environment. Parents should provide as many words as possible when the child is capable of absorbing them. The more you talk to your child, the earlier they will learn to express themselves. Enhancing a child's linguistic intelligence can start from birth. Only with care and patience can children learn faster and better!
