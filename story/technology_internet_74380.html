The memory technique of the ancient Romans - the Roman Room Method [Qiaozhi Forum]

by cozhi on 2008-06-16 07:55:16

Although the ancient Romans did not achieve as much in memory theory as the ancient Greeks, they placed greater emphasis on practical application. The ancient Romans invented many memory techniques, among which the most famous is the "Roman Room Method."

In the time of ancient Rome, the elders of the Senate needed to quote classics and memorize a large amount of data for speeches and debates. However, at that time, there was neither paper nor pens available for convenient recording. Moreover, even if it were convenient to record, a speaker holding notes would be less charming compared to one who could deliver a long speech fluently without notes. Therefore, an orator had to be eloquent and remember a large amount of legal texts and data to remain invincible. Thus, they gradually developed a set of memory methods that could accurately and extensively memorize information.

So, how did they memorize? The Romans noticed that the items, furniture, and many utensils in their homes were usually fixed in one place. If these items were used as media and associated with the content to be memorized, wouldn't recalling the items also recall the content? Additionally, since it was their own home, they were familiar with the placement of items, so the order would not get confused. By recalling in sequence, the order of the memorized content would also not get confused. This solved both the ability to "remember" and the ability to "remember in order," achieving two goals at once!

For example, if an orator had to debate with three people the next day, he would need to prepare different materials for each person. How could he remember the materials and not confuse the targets? He thought about his home situation: upon entering, there was a table, then behind it was a bed... Thus, he associated the door, table, and bed with those three people. The first was a thin man, the second was a fat man, and the third was very tall. He began imagining: the door and the thin man – a certain someone is so thin that he can squeeze through the crack in my door; he never needs me to open it when he comes over. The table and the fat man – a certain someone is so fat that he just sat on my table and broke it; he even imagined the "crack" sound and the fat man's cry of pain. The third, the tall man and the bed – a certain someone is so tall that when he lies on my bed, his feet still stick out.

This way, the three people were associated with the door, table, and bed. Next, the materials to be memorized were associated with the door, table, and bed. The next day during the debate, seeing the thin man reminded him of the bed, and through the bed, he recalled the content prepared the night before; seeing the fat man reminded him of the table, and through the table, he recalled the material... Thus, he spoke to each person in an orderly manner without getting confused.

Another example: A Roman's house layout is like this: there are two big pillars at the entrance, a lion pattern on the door, inside the house to the left is a sculpture, next to it is a pot of flowers, to the left of the flowers is a sofa, in front of the sofa is a table, on which there are wine glasses, bowls, etc.

The owner wants to go out and buy the following things: a pair of slippers, sharpen the sword, buy a slave, prune the grapevine, polish the helmet, talk to the child, etc. How to remember? Just associate the order of the furniture arrangement with the items to be purchased.

The two pillars at the entrance with slippers and sharpening the sword: imagine one pillar hanging full of slippers, colorful and various styles, you can just take a pair when you want to wear them; the other pillar is specifically for sharpening swords, so whenever you see the pillar, you remember it's time to sharpen the sword.

The lion and the slave: the newly bought slave is strong enough to tame the lion, and you even imagine the thrilling scenes of watching their fight.

The sculpture and pruning the grapevine: my sculpture needs to be decorated with grape leaves, so when I see the bare sculpture, I think it's time to prune some grapevines.

The flowers and polishing the helmet: use flowers to polish the helmet, making it shiny, so I like using flowers to polish the helmet, and growing flowers is just for polishing the helmet.

The sofa and talking to the child: generally, conversations with children take place on the sofa.

This way, he can recall what he needs to do according to the order of the furniture in his home.

The advantage of the Roman Room Method is that it allows you to remember the content you need to remember and also remember it in order. Many encoding methods, mailbox methods, and location coding methods described in books today are continuations and developments of the Roman Room Method.
