Parallel World: Silicon Valley begins to realize that chat apps might be the alternative to Facebook-like SNS.

by geekzhang on 2013-05-06 09:38:14

We are all familiar with the concept of parallel worlds in science fiction: beyond this dimension, there may exist another world that runs completely parallel to ours. The two worlds may be unaware of each other's existence, but they share many subtle similarities and differences. In movies, parallel worlds often feature some critical differences. For example, a character might be dead in one world but alive in the parallel world. However, apart from these plot devices, we often find that the parallel worlds depicted in films share far more similarities with our world than differences—similar physical laws, languages, and characters.

Why do parallel worlds have so many similarities instead of being entirely different? Let’s take a real-world example. The convention for naming mathematical theorems is usually to use the name of the first mathematician who provided the proof, such as the Pythagorean theorem. However, many mathematical theorems discovered during the Cold War were jointly named by two or more mathematicians. This is because during the Cold War, the scientific and academic communities of Europe and America were completely separated from those in the Soviet Union. Despite this, mathematicians on both sides independently discovered many of the same theorems. After the end of the Cold War, many mathematical theorems had their names changed. There are many things that, even when evolving and developing in two completely isolated parallel worlds, ultimately lead to the same result.

The ecosystem of China’s internet has always felt like a parallel world to that of the United States. Baidu-Google, Alibaba-Amazon+Ebay+Paypal, etc. These two parallel worlds clearly have many similarities, but what may be more noteworthy are their differences. One of the most obvious differences is that China does not have an SNS product similar to Facebook in terms of model and user coverage.

Renren Network has a model similar to Facebook, but Renren's user coverage is much smaller than Facebook’s. Monthly active users (MAU) are a relatively accurate measure of active users, but for a social network site (SNS), it is a very sensitive number and is sometimes not disclosed externally. We can estimate Renren's user coverage through daily active user data from sites like Alexa. Even among the age group that should be most actively using its services (students), Renren's user coverage is only around 10%. In contrast, about 41.6% of Americans are active Facebook users.

TechCrunch recently interviewed Aaref Hilaly, a partner at top Silicon Valley venture capital firm Sequoia Capital, discussing messaging apps, including WhatsApp, which Sequoia has invested in:

"For us, this is a major trend change. We believe WhatsApp is a reimagining of the SNS model. What does your real social graph look like? Is it the people you chat with via text messages, or the hundreds of people on Facebook whom you barely know? The answer is obviously the first group, and that's the value WhatsApp captures."

TechCrunch questioned whether Facebook-style SNS platforms and messaging apps represent a generational gap on the Internet — that is, whether the generation that grew up using Facebook is approaching thirty, while Facebook is not widely popular among today's teenagers. Hilaly's response was:

"There are some generational factors here, but more importantly, it's geographic differentiation. Many of these messaging apps first took root in countries outside the U.S., which is why our reaction to this trend was somewhat slow."

Hilaly is right; this isn't a generational gap issue. In the late 1990s, before Facebook was born, the U.S. already had widely used desktop messaging applications, such as ICQ, which AOL acquired and later renamed AOL Instant Messenger, and Microsoft's MSN Messenger. However, since AOL and Microsoft were companies that didn't understand the Internet well, and their main businesses were unrelated to messaging and social networking, these applications did not survive in the U.S., and Facebook miraculously expanded to become the core social network in the U.S. In China, however, in a parallel Internet world, the Web 1.0 era messaging application survived and has evolved into a highly accepted mobile Internet product. I'm referring, of course, to Tencent's QQ and WeChat.

I previously said that China doesn’t have an Internet product similar to Facebook in terms of model and user coverage. While QQ and WeChat differ in model from Facebook, belonging to the category of messaging apps, their user coverage in China is closest to Facebook's coverage in the U.S. Just as Facebook relies on network effects for dissemination and to enhance user stickiness, messaging apps are also a type of network-effect-driven Internet product. Therefore, the biggest danger facing messaging apps is market fragmentation. If you go to Korea now and have local Korean friends, you’ll find that their most commonly used messaging app is Kakao, and the social graphs of different messaging apps are clearly disconnected. This is also why Tencent heavily invests in other Asian markets in this field. In this domain, the ultimate global victor may be only one, and all these temporarily existing parallel worlds will inevitably merge.

Two parallel lines will never intersect, unless they exist in a projective space, in which case they would intersect at infinity. Two parallel worlds may have differences at a certain point on the time axis, but some fundamental things, like those mathematical laws, will eventually converge at a single point.
