The Origin of Playing Cards

It is said that as early as the Chu-Han Contention period at the end of the Qin Dynasty, General Han Xin created a paper card game to relieve the homesickness of his soldiers. Because the cards were only the size of leaves, it was called "Leaf Game". ("Leaf Cards", about two fingers in size, 8 cm long and 2.5 cm wide, made of silk and paper, with patterns printed by woodblock printing). It is said that this was the prototype of playing cards.

In the 12th century, Marco Polo brought this card game to Europe, immediately sparking great interest among Westerners. At first, it was just a luxury for the nobility, but due to its low cost, diverse gameplay, and ease of learning, it quickly became popular among the common people.

The French interpreted the four suits as spears, squares, clover leaves, and red hearts;

The Germans understood the four suits as leaves, bells, acorns, and red hearts;

The Italians interpreted the four suits as swords, coins, canes, and cups;

The Swiss understood the four suits as acorns, bells, flowers, and shields;

The British interpreted the four suits as spades, diamonds, clubs, and hearts.

Why these four patterns were chosen as the suits of playing cards has been debated for a long time. There are two main theories:

One theory suggests that these four suits represent the four major professions of society at the time: spades represent spears, symbolizing soldiers; clubs represent clover leaves, symbolizing agriculture; diamonds represent bricks used by craftsmen; and hearts represent the clergy.

Another theory suggests that these four suits originated from the symbols used in ancient European divination, where spades represent olive leaves, symbolizing peace; clubs represent clover leaves, symbolizing luck; diamonds represent diamonds, symbolizing wealth; and hearts represent red hearts, symbolizing wisdom and love.

The explanation of the 54-card deck is also very intriguing:

The Joker represents the sun, the small joker represents the moon, and the other 52 cards represent the 52 weeks of the year;

The four suits—hearts, diamonds, clubs, and spades—symbolize the four seasons: spring, summer, autumn, and winter;

Each suit has 13 cards, representing the 13 weeks in each season.

If J, Q, K are counted as 11, 12, 13 points respectively, and the jokers as half a point, the total points of a deck of cards add up to exactly 365 points. In leap years, if the jokers are each counted as 1 point, the total becomes 366 points.

Experts generally believe that the above explanations are not coincidental, as the design and invention of playing cards have intricate connections with astrology, divination, geography, and calendars.

There is a legend that Henry VIII was the model for the pattern on the four Kings. On the portraits of the four Kings on the oldest existing British playing cards, they all have the same curly sideburns and beards as Henry VIII. Another legend suggests that the portraits on the four Queens might likely be Elizabeth, Queen of the House of York, who was also Queen to Henry VII. In poker, J (JACK) was originally referred to as KNAVE (rogue), a term that was quite popular in Britain and continental Europe until the 1940s. Nowadays, it is rarely used, but in some parts of Europe like Sweden, it is still in use. One reason why JACK replaced KNAVE and quickly gained public acceptance is that when recording or reporting card examples, using abbreviations, or describing the card-playing process, the first letter J of JACK can be conveniently used. Previously, when using KNAVE, Kn had to be used, and using only K would cause confusion.

In 1840, after the Opium War, foreign playing cards entered China along with foreigners. At that time, imported playing cards mainly came from the United States and Japan, with Japanese playing cards being popular due to their low price. In 1931, a nationwide movement against Japanese goods arose, advocating for domestic products and industrial development. Huang Jinsheng from Shanghai opened the Cuihua Card Factory, producing China's first batch of playing cards, the Red Lion brand.

Playing Card Games

Percentage "Play Percentage" requires 4 players, divided into 2 teams, competing against each other. The Joker is the highest, followed by the small joker, then A, and so on in order. Without 2s, 2 is the smallest (with 2, 2 is greater than A). Each of the 4 Kings and 4 Tens counts as 10 points, and each of the 4 Fives counts as 5 points, totaling 100 points. During play, teammates sit opposite each other (i.e., "opposite partner," "opposite door"). Each person gets 12 cards, leaving 6 cards. Bidding (or "requesting cards") begins with the dealer starting from 60 points (or both sides decide), and you can decline if your hand is bad. Bidding proceeds counterclockwise, with each bid increasing by at least 5 points, potentially reaching 100 points. When bidding reaches a certain score (e.g., 70 points) and others do not bid further, bidding stops. The person with the highest bid takes the bottom cards and exchanges them with their hand. Before discarding (or "burying cards," "hiding cards"), choose one suit as the trump suit (or "main suit"), and then play begins. The last person to call plays first, and subsequent players must follow the same suit as the first player. If there are no matching suits, a trump card (like big or small jokers) or a secondary card can be played. After each round, if the non-calling team wins, the scoring cards (K, 10, 5) are taken out. The winner of this round gains the right to play the next round.

After all cards are played, if the calling team bids 80 points and the non-calling team picks up 25 points, the calling team is "broken," scoring negative points (-80), while the non-calling team scores positive points (+25). If the non-calling team picks up 20 points or less, the calling team is "successful," and both teams score positively. If the non-calling team picks up no points, the round scores zero, and the calling team scores 200 points for this round. If the non-calling team picks up 50 points or more, the score can be doubled during calculation; if the non-calling team beats the opponent with a trump card in the last round, it is called "bottoming," adding the picked-up score with the bottom card score multiplied by 3, counting as the non-calling team's score. Accumulate until the first team reaches the designated score (500 or 1000) to win.

Four Hundred Points

The principle is similar to the aforementioned "percentage" game, but the bidding and scoring methods differ. Four players are divided into two teams for competition. After dealing, each person gets 12 cards (big and small jokers are not used). The remaining 4 cards are placed aside to be exchanged after bidding. Then each person decides their bid based on their hand and the partner's bid. Starting from 250 points, bidding rotates to the right, with each subsequent bid needing to exceed the previous by at least 10 points. When one side bids the highest and the other three do not bid further, this score becomes the contract score for this round, and the bidder becomes the contractor. The contractor and their teammate form the contract team. The contract team's task is to strive to win scoring cards, making the score reach or exceed the contract score. The contractor has the right to exchange cards, taking the middle 4 cards and exchanging them with 4 cards, placing them aside on the table. After exchanging, the contractor decides the trump suit. After deciding the trump, the contractor leads the play, and after each round, whoever has the highest card wins that round. Winning cards of the same team are stacked together. After all 12 cards are played, both teams score based on the cards they won. In the four hundred-point game, there are 6 scoring cards: A and K, each worth 25 points; Q worth 20 points; J worth 15 points; 10 worth 10 points; 5 worth 5 points, totaling 400 points. Scores are calculated based on the contract score. For example, if the contract score is 300 points and the winning cards total 255 points, the contract is not completed, and the contract team scores zero while the non-contract team scores 45 points. If the contract team just reaches 300 points, it is considered the completion of the contract, and neither side scores. Bidding 350 points and completing the contract is called "small slam," with a bonus score of 100 points; bidding 400 points and completing the contract is called "grand slam," with a bonus score of 200 points.

The game ends when either side reaches the agreed-upon score.

Long-term Battle

Each round involves 4-6 players using a full deck of cards. For 4 players, each gets 6 cards; for 5 players, each gets 5 cards; for 6 players, each gets 4 cards. The dealer reveals their last card to everyone, and the suit of this card becomes the trump suit for this round. The remaining cards are placed in the center of the table for replenishment.

After dealing, the player to the right of the dealer leads by playing one card. Others must follow the same suit if possible. If not, they can play other suits or trump cards to win. Whoever has the highest card wins the round, draws one card, and leads the next round. This repeats, and since only winners can draw cards, the number of cards each player holds becomes unequal over time. Once any player runs out of cards, the game ends, and the others continue until only one player remains. This player is the winner of this round.

Level Up

Four players participate, sitting opposite their partners. Each player draws one card, and the one with the highest points starts. Usually, the game starts with 2s. The first player to reveal a 2 gets to play first, and the suit of this 2 becomes the trump suit for this round. Each player draws 12 cards, and the remaining 6 cards go to the revealer for exchange, replacing 6 unimportant cards (sometimes requiring showing the bottom cards to the other three players). Then the game begins. After playing, if the opponent scores 40 points or more, your team loses, and the next round goes to the next player; if the opponent scores 80 points or more, they advance one level, e.g., from 4 to 5; if the opponent scores 100 points, they advance two levels. If the opponent scores less than 40 points, your team wins this round, and the next round continues, advancing one level, e.g., from 2 to 3. If your team plays and the opponent scores nothing, it is called "shaving the opponent," and your team advances two levels, e.g., from 3 to 5. If the opponent plays a trump card larger than yours in the last round, it is called "bottoming," and your team loses, with the next round going to the opponent. If the opponent scores over 40 points and also "bottoms," they advance one extra level, e.g., from 5 to 6. Continue playing, and the first team to reach A wins.

Pig Capture

There are different ways to play in various regions, here introducing one of the more popular ones. A deck of cards without big and small jokers, each player gets 13 cards. Among them, the Queen of Spades is called "pig." After one round, the player who gets the "pig" scores negative 100 points; the Jack of Diamonds is called "sheep," and getting "sheep" scores positive 100 points; the Ten of Clubs is called "transformer" (shortened as "change"), and getting "change" doubles the score in hand; all Heart cards (except 2, 3, 4) are negative points, but if all 13 Heart cards are obtained, they become positive points, where A, K, Q, J are sequentially 50 points, 40 points, 30 points, 20 points, and 10 to 5 are each 10 points, totaling 200 points. Before playing, players holding Red Ace, "pig," "sheep," and "change" can sell these cards based on their hand, or choose not to sell. After selling, all scoring cards double in value, i.e., "pig" scores negative 200 points, "sheep" scores positive 200 points, and Red Ace to 5 totals 400 points. Selling "change" multiplies the score of Ten of Clubs by 4. Selling can be done openly or secretly; open selling displays the sold cards on the table, while secret selling keeps the cards hidden, making it harder for others to guess. After selling, the game begins, starting with the Two of Spades, and the player who gets the "pig" leads the next round. Players must follow the same suit if possible, otherwise, they can pad with other suits. After each round of four cards, scoring cards are taken out and given to the winner of that round. The goal is to avoid negative scores or minimize them while striving for positive scores. After one round, each player's score is calculated. If one side gets all 13 Hearts and "change," "pig," "sheep," they score +1000 points, and others score zero. For example, if a player ends with Red Ace, Red Seven, "pig," "sheep," "change," their score is [50+(-10)+(-100)+100]x2=-120 points. If "sheep" and "change" were sold, their score becomes [-50+(-10)+(-100)+200]x4=+160 points.

The game ends when players reach the agreed-upon score (usually negative). The more negative the score, the bigger the loss.
