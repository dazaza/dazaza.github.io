Stock Index Futures Launch - Hexun Exclusive Interview with Analyst Yuan Lei of Hengji Investment

by huaxun892 on 2010-04-18 12:22:34

Interview time: April 16, 2010, 14:55-15:20. Host: Now we are broadcasting the series of interviews on stock index futures by Hexun.com. Today’s analyst is Mr. Yuan Lei from Hengji Investment. Hello, Mr. Yuan! (14:57)

Yuan Lei: Hello, netizens and investors. Hello, host! (14:57)

Host: Let's get straight to the point. Has our market seen a round of stock index futures trend? (14:57)

Yuan Lei: First, I think the trend is not very clear. We can see from today's market that the linkage between stock index futures and the A-share market is not obvious. The market is currently undergoing a smooth transition. After all, it has just been launched, and the number of accounts opened for stock index futures is not particularly high - only 9,137 at present. Although there may be some increase in interest compared to before, overall, we have not yet reached a truly mature or good level. This will require a process. The most important thing now is stability. (14:58)

Host: Why haven't financial legal entities entered the market yet? (14:58)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (14:58)

Host: What do you think about the management's decision not to allow financial legal entities into the market? (14:58)

Yuan Lei: It might be to protect the interests of small and medium-sized investors, which could affect their investment and participation methods. Current policies and strategies are not yet perfect, so they may wait until they are perfected before letting them in. This would be a more stable and positive approach for investors. (14:58)

Host: Is the risk relatively higher before financial legal entities enter the market? (15:01)

Yuan Lei: Risks definitely exist. There is a view in the market that the introduction of stock index futures marks the arrival of an institutional era. At this time, more investors may worry whether after the listing of stock index futures, us small and medium-sized investors won't have any opportunities left. I don’t think that's the case. In this market, participating involves significant risks, and one needs to enhance their own professionalism. Nowadays, not only is the risk greater, but participating in stock investments also requires a certain level of expertise; blind investment is impossible. (15:01)

Host: The biggest issue brought by stock index futures is that the 5 million yuan threshold locks out 90% of individual investors. Do you think having stock index futures increases or decreases the risk for those who cannot participate in them? (15:02)

Yuan Lei: I believe their risk is reduced. The 5 million yuan is just a threshold, and many investors being locked out isn't solely due to these 5 million yuan. For investors, many small and medium-sized investors with 5 million yuan in investment capital aren't a big problem, but why are they still locked out? Because stock index futures have their particularities, such as the leverage principle. After investing 5 million yuan, the amount required for future investments will far exceed just these 5 million yuan. (15:02)

Host: It's not the threshold itself but the subsequent investment requirement behind the threshold that locks them out, right? (15:02)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:02)

Host: Let's continue discussing the stock index futures market. You mentioned that the divergence between today's spot and futures markets indicates no stock index futures trend, but from 2890 points, the rise was mainly led by "two" stocks while "eight" stocks were still falling sharply a few days ago. Doesn't this count as a kind of stock index futures trend? (15:02)

Yuan Lei: Personally, I believe this should still be based on the inherent operating rules of the A-share market, and the influence of stock index futures is very limited. Previously, the A-share market had regional investments, including the Expo concept and the whole "map trading" concept. Small-cap stocks and concepts performed well, and the "eight" among "two-eight" performed well, followed by a slight shift. At the beginning of the year, I thought there were at least three attempts, but none were successful. This time, it quietly shifted, and at the beginning of this week, the heavyweights rose again for a day, then fell back, especially real estate and finance clearly leading the market down. The "two-eight" effect was not particularly evident. Overall, the best method for the market is that stock index futures will have more long-term effects rather than immediate ones. (15:02)

Host: If stock index futures cannot change the existing market structure, do you expect a rising, falling, or stable market trend for the rest of the year? (15:02)

Yuan Lei: I think it will be stable because the A-share market has its own independent operating rules. The weight of the "two" in the market is very obvious, including bank stocks and real estate stocks. A straightforward investment strategy is that the more they fall, the more we should buy, because their valuations and prices are low, and the advantages are very clear. The performance of listed companies themselves is excellent, so we focus on investing in them. On the other hand, policy-wise, recent policies regarding real estate have been successively issued, and they are becoming increasingly tight, which will surely limit the upward trend of real estate stocks. Here we have both bullish and bearish factors, and the final result of the tug-of-war between bulls and bears is likely to be a smooth transition because the forces on both sides are evenly matched, making it unlikely for a major market movement to occur. (15:02)

Host: It seems like another factor unfavorable to the bulls is that market liquidity doesn't support the "two" stocks' rally. (15:09)

Yuan Lei: True, the volume isn't sufficient. (15:10)

Host: What level of volume do you think is needed to sustain a continuous rally in the "two" stocks? (15:10)

Yuan Lei: I think it needs to exceed 20 billion yuan. (15:10)

Host: Two markets or one? (15:10)

Yuan Lei: Two markets. Without sufficient volume, the "two" need further observation. (15:11)

Host: Many bullish people shout loudly, believing there should be a rally in heavyweight stocks, but they may not have noticed the impact of trading volume on the market. Do you think that after the introduction of stock index futures, in terms of trends in the spot market, stock index futures will not significantly affect the spot market trend? Will there be a bigger impact on profit models and arbitrage models? (15:11)

Yuan Lei: I think there will be arbitrage models where some companies will make certain profits. Investors need to improve their professional skills. Older investors who previously traded both spot and futures markets, although the targets have changed, at least the mode remains the same, allowing them to find techniques. For new investors, the professionalism in this area might be less developed. Before this, I think improving professionalism is crucial. Afterward, arbitrage hedging operations are feasible. (15:12)

Host: You seem neutral on the market. If we use the Shanghai Composite Index as a benchmark, what is the range of market fluctuations? Below 3000 points or above? (15:13)

Yuan Lei: Optimistically speaking, it should be between 2900 and 3300 points, which is a relatively large range. Pessimistically, it could adjust to 2800 or 2700 points. I believe the possibility of a sharp rise or fall is not very high. (15:13)

Host: So the fluctuation range isn't very large either? (15:13)

Yuan Lei: Yes, probably within a 200 to 300-point trend. (15:13)

Host: In the history of A-shares, such a rise represents only about a ten percent space. It has already remained steady for eight months. Do you think it can stay steady for another eight months within the year? (15:13)

Yuan Lei: Not eight months, but five to six months is possible. (15:15)

Host: Neither the bulls nor the bears can break through directionally? (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:15)

Host: If external forces change this balance, what do you think could change it? (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Policy. China is a policy-driven market, and we cannot ignore the attention to policy. We must first have policy support before we can have volume support. (15:15)

Host: What kind of policy support? The end of real estate regulation? (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Currently, house prices are too high, so this is not very realistic. If the RMB appreciates... (15:15)

Host: Do you think RMB appreciation would be beneficial? (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:15)

Host: The short-term possibility of RMB appreciation is not high either. (15:15)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:16)

Host: You talked about breaking through upwards, but what about downwards? (15:16)

Yuan Lei: That depends on policy factors. We don't know what bad policies the government might introduce. (15:16)

Host: If the government does not introduce any bad policies, there won't be any downward breakthroughs, right? (15:16)

Yuan Lei: There might be an equilibrium state. (15:16)

Host: Would adjustments in overseas markets drag it down? (15:16)

Yuan Lei: The A-share market is a relatively closed market. Many people in the market believe that if U.S. stocks fall, A-shares will also fall, and if U.S. stocks soar, A-shares will also rise. However, I completely disagree with this theory. A-shares have their own unique characteristics, and we are a relatively closed market. Recently, U.S. stocks have risen significantly, but A-shares have not moved much. This shows that there is some influence, but the trend of overseas markets is not an absolute determinant of the A-share market direction. Last year, when U.S. stocks kept adjusting downwards, A-shares showed an independent rebound. In fact, I think there was a divergence between them, which is not normal interactivity. Actually, A-shares adjusted earlier than U.S. stocks. When A-shares adjusted fully, U.S. stocks just started to adjust, and they should have rebounded. The lack of clear interactivity appeared during the adjustment period. (15:17)

Host: Do you think changes in the external market will not alter the market trend? (15:17)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:17)

Host: When the Shanghai Composite Index was at 6124 points, U.S., A-share, and Hong Kong markets bottomed out simultaneously in October 2008. Of course, the Hong Kong market experienced a secondary bottom. Historically, they have never interacted so well. This round of rebounds saw A-shares rebounding before U.S. stocks, but A-shares also adjusted earlier than U.S. stocks. Adjustments lasted longer, much longer than the starting times. Can you explain the interactivity between them, from adjusting together to rebounding sequentially? (15:18)

Yuan Lei: Markets exhibit phenomena such as peaking together, bottoming out together, and rebounding sequentially. Sometimes, U.S. stocks rise while A-shares fall, or A-shares rise while U.S. stocks fall. Therefore, the theory that external markets directly determine A-shares is invalid and unreasonable, given the lack of valid evidence. This matter, I believe, should be viewed dialectically and divided by time windows. The divergence between A-shares and U.S. stocks occurs at different time windows, leading to different trends. (15:18)

Host: Now mainstream opinions believe that after the introduction of stock index futures, the volatility of A-shares will decrease, as if part of the risk has been hedged. How do you explain that despite having stock index futures, the Dow Jones Industrial Average and Hong Kong stocks experienced severe declines after the financial crisis, especially Hong Kong stocks? The decline in Hong Kong stocks was similar to ours, with state-owned enterprise shares falling by seventy percent. We did not observe stock index futures reducing volatility. Historically, a seventy percent drop in Hong Kong stocks is extremely rare. How do you view this issue? (15:18)

Yuan Lei: Stock index futures in the U.S. were introduced early, in 1987, and initially had little impact on the U.S. stock market. They became more mature after improvements. Currently, A-shares have always been a one-sided bull market, and only now has the process of completing a one-sided bull market finished. This improves the mechanism of the entire A-share market, which includes hedging and risk avoidance. The purpose of stock index futures is to avoid risks, which is the fundamental reason. However, when the overall market and large economic environment, such as the U.S. subprime mortgage crisis, financial crisis, or Hong Kong Asian financial crisis, appear, it depends on the dynamics and mentality of funds. They might believe that hedging risks is ultimately unachievable regardless of the side, and another result is temporarily leaving the market to avoid risks. Therefore, after significant economic issues arise, corresponding adjustments will occur in both markets because funds and institutions withdraw, believing they are unsafe anywhere. This results in simultaneous declines without achieving risk avoidance. (15:18)

Host: Risk hedging by stock index futures also has conditional restrictions. (15:18)

Yuan Lei: Yes. (15:18)

Host: Another interesting perspective is that after the introduction of the Dow Jones Index in 1987, it rose 15 times over 30 years, not much less than A-shares. Do you think stock index futures cannot change the mechanism of a one-sided market? (15:19)

Yuan Lei: Stock index futures have just been introduced, and at this stage, the number of people doing short selling exceeds those doing long buying. After all, we have been in a one-sided market for so long, and the trend has not completely changed yet, requiring a process. Initially, there will be more long positions than short positions, which might drive the market upwards. Gradually, after two or three months of maturation, our thinking matures, and short positions start to appear. The combination of both forces might stabilize the market. Subsequently, we must judge according to the large economic environment, policy environment, and intrinsic market rules, determining which force dominates—short or long—at different time periods, thereby judging the market trend. (15:19)

Host: We just considered the normal and extreme states of the market. There is an extreme situation where the market forms a consistent opinion, unanimously bullish or bearish. Do you think the futures market would lose liquidity? Stock index futures require counter-parties. If everyone is bullish and no one is bearish, long contracts cannot be established, and vice versa for short contracts. In such cases, how would stock index futures hedge risks? (15:19)

Yuan Lei: This belongs to an extremely extreme phenomenon. I can confirm that this phenomenon is impossible. Because under any circumstances, each investor's direction will definitely have disagreements, and it is impossible to reach complete consistency. (15:19)

Host: Let's say a relatively extreme situation where most people look one way and a few look another. What happens if they cannot find enough counter-parties? What if someone wants to go long or short but can't? (15:19)

Yuan Lei: I think the market will be in a temporarily calm stagnation phase. When the A-share market is active, the volume is large, and when it is inactive, there is no volume. At that time, the market obviously lacks money and volume, but the market will not disappear; it can only stop. (15:19)

Host: Just now, I discussed the stock index futures turnover with colleagues, and after calculating, it turns out that today's actual turnover might only be several billion yuan, which is very low. If the turnover is so low, indeed, no one would want to go long or short for just several billion yuan—it's not worth it. Does the sluggish turnover mean that, as some people say, the market will remain sideways for a while, satisfying enough turnover before choosing a direction? Otherwise, people think it's not worth investing unless they treat it not as a hedging or arbitrage tool but as something else. To make it worthwhile, they need to patiently build up the necessary volume and establish positions before proceeding. (15:20)

Yuan Lei: Today's turnover is not very large, but this is a normal phenomenon. There are only over 9,000 accounts opened, and not all 9,000 accounts will be active. I believe at least half of the people will be in a waiting state, observing the trend for today or next week. After identifying the trend, they will act. It's just the beginning, so they won't sell lightly. Some people will move, and we need to wait for the real volume to come up, gradually becoming more active, to achieve arbitrage or risk avoidance, thus truly realizing these goals. (15:20)

Host: I am concerned that the market will eventually have more short sellers than buyers. Management finally requires public mutual funds to hedge. With such a large market value, who can be the counter-party for public mutual funds? If public mutual funds want to hedge, they need to open short contracts. How large a group of buyers can absorb them? Without absorbing the large contracts from public mutual funds, how large a contract is needed to support them? (15:20)

Yuan Lei: There must be a sufficiently strong counter-party; otherwise, it cannot be realized. Public mutual funds hedging can only lead to the market not rising, possibly moving downward or staying flat, with a greater likelihood of moving downward. However, public mutual funds cannot all engage in hedging. (15:20)

Host: Currently, only 20% hedging is allowed. (15:20)
