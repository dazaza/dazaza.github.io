Keyboard follows the finger: Dryft aims to redefine tablet keyboard

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 14:45:01

Tablets are becoming increasingly popular. However, one of their flaws still leaves the commercial market dominated by laptops - input functionality. It must be said that the poor input experience offered by tablets cannot compare with the tactile feedback provided by a laptop's physical keyboard. But at this year's Disrupt SF 2013, an Android keyboard app named Dryft aims to change this situation.

Dryft was founded by Randy Marsden, co-founder of the once-popular Android sliding input method Swype, and Rob Chaplinsky, co-founder of Bridgescale Partners.

In addition to lacking tactile feedback, virtual keyboards on tablets also lack the small bumps on the "F" and "J" keys that help with positioning on physical keyboards. As a result, the speed and accuracy of typing on tablets are relatively low, leading many content creators using tablets to resort to external physical keyboards.

Dryft's solution is a keyboard that moves with your fingers. Unlike traditional fixed-location virtual keyboards on tablets, Dryft's keyboard only appears when the user places their fingers on the touchscreen. Wherever the fingers are placed becomes the home row. Once the fingers are in a stationary position, the home row keys appear underneath them, followed by the other keys unfolding sequentially. In essence, this is a virtual keyboard that actively adapts to the user rather than requiring the user to adapt to it.

Of course, despite the simplicity of this idea, it involves quite a bit of technical wizardry behind the scenes. In addition to tracking the user's touch inputs, Dryft also utilizes the tablet's accelerometer to measure the sensitivity of touches, thereby determining whether the user intends to type or simply rest their fingers there.

The keyboard itself is not the only thing that can move; Dryft will also attempt to guess which letters you intend to type and then move the keys to where it believes you need them. Thus, under your guidance, this keyboard can become one tailored specifically to your habits. The beauty of this input method lies in the fact that users do not need to adapt to various different keyboard layouts on different tablets; they just need to follow their own habits.

Dryft is certainly not the first company hoping to reinvent tablet input methods, but it may be the most ambitious. Marsden's goal is to make Dryft the de facto standard for text input on tablets. However, he also acknowledges that achieving this will require external assistance, such as low-level access to the accelerometer, meaning Dryft needs the help of hardware manufacturers and OEMs.

Considering that Marsden co-founded Swype, some of the tactics used to popularize Swype will likely be applied to Dryft as well. For example, partnerships bundled with OEMs. However, one of the most powerful companies won't buy into it—Apple.

Nevertheless, although Apple does not allow changes to the keyboard layout on iPad devices, Marsden believes the situation is beginning to change. The breakthrough is not with Apple, but with app developers. Marsden says that Dryft is developing SDKs for Android, iOS, or Windows Phone versions, and some app developers have already expressed interest in licensing Dryft for use in their apps.
