Keywords: Reform of computer fundamental education in applied undergraduate institutions

The computer fundamentals course is a required course for all non-computer science students at applied undergraduate institutions. In modern society, obtaining, storing, transmitting, processing, and applying information all depend on computers. Mastering the basic knowledge of computers is increasingly becoming a fundamental survival skill and a necessity for adapting to an information-based society. For non-computer science students studying computer fundamentals, this course serves as an entry point into the world of computing within an application-oriented institution. It can cultivate students to meet the demands of an information society, ensuring that they have strong hands-on skills, innovation, and entrepreneurial spirit after graduation. Therefore, this course plays an important role in the teaching process for non-computer science majors, laying a solid foundation for students' computer application skills and their future career development paths.

Considering the actual situation of students after enrollment, we have discovered through a period of teaching that the new entrants' grasp of computer fundamentals varies greatly. Some students are already proficient in using the Windows operating system, word editing, the internet, and email, while others may have never even touched a computer. This is mainly due to the uneven普及 (popularization) of computers and information science in high schools. We believe that in computer-based learning, it is necessary to recognize individual differences among students and teach according to their aptitudes so that each student can develop fully. Given that this is a highly practical and application-oriented course, overemphasizing classroom teaching while neglecting computer operations can lead to poor teaching outcomes. Some students pay close attention to lectures but fail to complete experimental tasks on the machine or solve practical problems they encounter.

On the other hand, some students quickly master relevant operational skills by using computers to complete tasks. Additionally, spending too much time on theoretical aspects of computer fundamentals can make students feel bored, leading to a lack of engagement. The widespread penetration of information technology across various disciplines requires more computer-based training for students entering professional fields. This training emphasizes basic computer skills and the application of new technologies, such as proficient keyboard input for Chinese characters, text processing capabilities, data handling, information acquisition, and the ability to send and publish information. Mastery of computer fundamental and application skills will directly impact students' learning in other specialized courses. Therefore, computer fundamentals in application-oriented institutions serve as an important course for non-computer science students, emphasizing the cultivation of practical operational skills. The rapid update of computer fundamental content means that the speed of content renewal is getting faster, from the initial DOS system to Windows, covering an increasing amount of knowledge. What was once considered fundamental due to the widespread use of computers has now changed. With the continuous emergence of new theories and technologies, teachers should continuously learn and update their own knowledge to keep up with the needs of the curriculum and adapt to modern educational technology.
