High School Student's Winter Vacation Essay: The Migrant Workers of the School

They are temporary workers hired by the school, specifically responsible for sorting and disposing of the school's garbage. They have scruffy beards, gray temples, cracked fingers, and wear yellow shoes. Their faces are kind, though sometimes furrowed with worry. They move around every corner of the school, calmly facing over 3500 teachers and students.

One of them is relatively short, with disheveled gray hair, a face longer than average. Every time he comes to pick up paper or drink bottles from the trash bins at the classroom entrance, some male students shout, "Here comes the monkey, here comes the monkey." The old man remains silent, smiling as he picks up the drink bottles thrown by the students.

One day, after coming out of the store with a good friend, we saw him dragging a cart pass by in front of us. A senior boy beside us said, "Hey! Did you see that? He's even wearing a branded item!" Following the boy's finger, I saw that the old man was wearing a pair of "Nike" sports shoes. But what kind of branded shoes were they! I think they should have been white, but now they are unrecognizable, with a patch on the toe of the left shoe, and the shoelaces made from knitting yarn. Immediately, those three burst into laughter because of the "branded shoes." However, the old man smiled and calmly pulled the cart away. That figure seemed to carry a sense of "self-innovation" and "satisfaction," but perhaps more of a tolerant mindset. Those three had walked far away, and my friend and I stood there for a while. My friend said, "Like you, I also feel very sad."

The other one is a bit taller, about 1.6 meters. What touches me most are his eyes, bright and spirited, just like the old Red Army soldier playing the flute in "Hard Times." Regardless of wind or rain, he always wears a pair of yellow military shoes. The wrinkles on his forehead are deep, like the plow tracks of spring. His fingers are often wrapped with tape, and his hair is also gray and disheveled.

Their relatively fixed workplace is the garbage pile at the school gate, helping to dispose of garbage, clean garbage bins, and manually sort the garbage. Every time I go to throw away the garbage, I try to do it myself. But once, they actually came over to "grab" the garbage can, emptied it, wiped it clean, and handed over two pieces of clean paper for us to take away. I don't know if others feel it's taken for granted or feel sorry when faced with all this. But what wells up in my heart is a strong sense of self-reproach and shock.

In fact, there are many farmers across China who are even more weathered and destitute than them. In one issue of "Reader's Digest," in the "China Impressions" exhibition, there was a photo called "Years." The subject of the photo was an elderly farmer with bronze skin, deeply lined face, and mud-stained ears of grain in his hand. His eyes, filled with hope for life yet powerless to change it, moved me deeply. Every time I think about these things, seeing those two garbage-picking elders, thinking about those so-called post-80s youth writers who only vent their melancholy and first love sorrows, my heart fills with indescribable sadness.

Where have millions of children of farmers gone? When will someone write a book about the great and simple Chinese farmers? Dear classmates, when will we start throwing garbage into the garbage pit? When will we start confronting our own souls?

In front of them, we are actually quite insignificant.
