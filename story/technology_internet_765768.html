The Dilemma of Programmers

by anonymous on 2013-08-20 15:58:20

Programmer’s dilemma

Translator: cssmagic

Recently, I interviewed over a dozen candidates for a kernel programmer position. These candidates all came from some well-known large companies that are famous in the chip or embedded operating system fields. Most of them claimed to have over a decade of on-the-job experience in kernel development. Their resumes looked very impressive—full of relevant projects, technical terms, and awards...

However, almost none of them could answer a very basic question: What happens in the kernel when we call the standard malloc function?

Don’t be shocked just yet. When I asked one of the candidates to write a simple LRU (Least Recently Used) cache framework based on glib's hash functions, he first said he had never used glib—something I had anticipated. So, I helped him by opening the glib hash API documentation page and explaining the APIs in detail. After about an hour, he managed to write only a few lines of messy code.

I don’t know if this situation exists in other countries, but in China—or more specifically, in Beijing—this is the reality. Those "senior" programmers who have worked for many years in well-known large companies cannot demonstrate their abilities on simple, fundamental questions.

What is going on here?

The more I thought about this issue, the more I became convinced that it is not just their fault—it also lies with the companies they work for. These companies often provide a stable codebase that may go years without major updates. The proprietary technologies of these codes confine people's skills into a fixed pattern, so much so that they only need to follow existing paths and do not need to exercise creativity. If you happen to work on such code and have been isolated from the outside world for a long time, one day you will find yourself in a sorry state—inside the team or company, they call you an "expert," but unfortunately, you cannot find a job with similar compensation on the market.

This is called the "expert trap." Day by day, programmers yearn to become experts within their teams or companies. However, when that day finally comes, we are already trapped in our own cocoon. The deeper we delve into existing code, the deeper we get stuck. The existing code is so stable (so vast, so useful) that we gradually lose the ability to independently write complete projects from scratch. Worse still, if our main job is to maintain this existing code and rarely develop new features, sooner or later, no matter how much code we study, we will discover that we can no longer write code—even for a task as simple as a final-year university project. This is the programmer's dilemma: We make a living through coding, but the big companies that sustain us inadvertently erode our survival skills.

How to break out of this dilemma?

For individuals:

First, create your own private projects. You need to continuously hone your craft. If your job itself does not help you achieve this, pick up problems that interest you and tackle them in your spare time. Through this method, you should learn something new. If you release your private projects, for example on GitHub, you might meet some people who can help you take great leaps forward.

Do not stay in the same team for more than two years. Force yourself to move around, even within the same company, so you face new challenges and new technologies. Try to interview for jobs every 18 months. You don’t necessarily need to change jobs, but it will help you see the real market demand and how to stay relevant.

For teams and companies:

Put pressure and challenges on employees. Implement a rotation system so "experts" have the opportunity to expand their skills. Launch new projects and use battles to temper your warriors.

Periodically host hackathon events. This helps foster a corporate culture that values innovation and creation. People will be inspired by their peers—"Damn, this guy can write such a beautiful framework in 24 hours? I need to step up my game!"
