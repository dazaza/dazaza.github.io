The Top Ten Tweets That Twitter Will Never Publish

by anonymous on 2013-11-16 17:48:23

1. "You say we're a social media site, we say we're an ad delivery system."

Anticipation among investors and fans finally paid off when Twitter submitted its IPO application in September this year. Last Thursday, the microblogging site announced that its stock price range would be $17-20 per share, valuing the company at approximately $11.1 billion. It is set to become one of the most sought-after stocks in recent years, not only because its user base has surged dramatically—from 49 million three years ago to 232 million by the end of the third quarter this year—but also due to its extensive influence spanning from politics to entertainment.

Social networking experts claim that Twitter might make a series of adjustments post-public listing. First, it may target ads more precisely. In July, Twitter announced that it would soon attempt to launch advertisements in the U.S., "showcasing promoted content from brands and businesses that users are interested in." Twitter gave an example in a statement: retailers could provide Twitter with information about which users visit their websites, and Twitter would then display promotions from these retailers via the users' Twitter accounts. (Users who wish to protect their privacy and avoid receiving such promotional content can uncheck the "promoted content" box in their account settings.)

In September, Twitter announced the acquisition of mobile advertising company MoPub. Acquiring MoPub will help Twitter deliver more targeted ads on smartphones and tablets based on user habits within games. In its prospectus filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission (commonly known as Form S-1, containing the company's overall business model and financial information), Twitter stated it plans to develop MoPub to "expand advertising services across the entire mobile ecosystem." Jason Keath, CEO of SocialFresh, a social media training company, said this acquisition will push Twitter deeper into the advertising sector, "making it more like an advertising company rather than a social network with ad products."

According to Twitter's S-1 filing, advertising is its main and growing revenue source. In 2012, the company's revenue was approximately $317 million, with 85% coming from ads. In the nine months ending September 30 this year, 89% of its total revenue came from advertising.

A Twitter spokesperson noted that the company had no comment due to being in the "quiet period" before going public. The SEC prohibits companies from making any statements that could affect their stock value. However, the spokesperson pointed out that there are relevant policies on the Twitter website.

2. "We know a lot about our users—not just what they do on Twitter."

Twitter collects relevant information based on users' behavior on the platform—whom they follow, their geographic location, and topics they frequently engage in. But its reach extends far beyond that. Keath mentioned that many websites now have Tweet buttons that allow users to quickly share links to webpages on Twitter and also act as tracking devices, letting Twitter know which sites users are visiting. (Of course, Twitter isn't the only company with this capability; for instance, Facebook’s 'Like' button serves a similar function.) Moreover, even if users don't click the Tweet icon on these sites, Twitter (like Facebook) can still collect this information.

Consumers can easily limit the tracking of their online activities by various companies, including Twitter. For example, they can select the "Do Not Track" option in their web browsers. A Twitter spokesperson declined to comment but pointed to the company's policy on user account settings: for instance, after logging into Twitter, users can uncheck the box under security settings that says "Personalize Twitter based on my recent website visits."

Furthermore, Twitter's data collection capabilities place it within the realm of data collection industries. In different clauses of Twitter's user agreement, the company retains the right to share, reutilize, and dispose of user activity information. This information can also benefit Twitter. According to its S-1 filing, in the first nine months of 2013, Twitter earned $47.3 million from data licensing, accounting for about 11% of its total revenue, up 36.3% from the same period last year. The site also allows other companies to pay for access to tweets and other user data. In its S-1 filing, the company stated that its data partners might use Tweets to "identify user sentiment, influence, and other trends." One developer cited used Twitter content to provide research reports for brands, media companies, and political campaigns, informing them how people on Twitter perceive them.

3. "Looking for a job on Twitter? That's your own risk."

An increasing number of employers are using social media to decide whether to hire a candidate. According to a survey conducted by the Society for Human Resource Management in 2011, 18% of employers used social networks like Twitter, Facebook, and LinkedIn to screen candidates during the hiring process, compared to just 13% in 2008.

Even employed Americans aren't exempt from employer scrutiny. From late 2010 to early 2011, 33% of surveyed companies indicated they would take disciplinary actions against employees violating their social media usage policies. Employees often create two separate Twitter accounts—one for personal life and one for work. However, this offers little protection. Kate Kennedy, a spokesperson for the Society for Human Resource Management, stated that different companies have varying social media usage policies, some of which extend to personal accounts. Even if employees post content unrelated to work, inappropriate content can still lead to punishment.

A Twitter spokesperson said every user should be responsible for their posts.

4. "Thieves are watching your status updates."

A survey conducted by MetLife Auto & Home revealed that 15% of Americans post updates on social media when they're away from home. Twitter users often post vacation photos while traveling. A 2011 study in the UK found that such updates help thieves identify their next targets.

Of course, Twitter isn't the only site where users inadvertently expose important personal information. Most social media platforms allow users to share their locations, and if thieves track their posts, they can easily determine when users are away. A concern for Twitter users is that anyone can see their updates unless their accounts are locked (experts note many users choose not to lock their accounts as doing so significantly reduces their follower count). Twitter claims its geotagging feature is off by default and automatically removes geolocation data embedded in photos uploaded to the site.

Meanwhile, law enforcement agencies have begun using social media to catch fraudsters. In 2011, New York City Police Department established a "social media unit" specifically to monitor sites like Twitter and Facebook, targeting those boasting about thefts or other criminal acts.

5. "We know where you live—and so does everyone else."

Some Twitter users may not mind exposing their location information, but others may not even realize they've done so. Such exposure can lead to significant trouble. For instance, ex-boyfriends or potential stalkers could use this information to closely monitor their movements.

In September, the University of Southern California published a report analyzing 15 million Tweets over a week. The study found that about 6% of Twitter users once allowed the service to list their location with each Tweet. Even users who didn't enable geotagging could unintentionally disclose their location. The research also showed that 17% of Tweets without geotagging still leaked location information, sometimes down to the city level. Chris Weidemann, a graduate student in the USC Spatial Sciences Institute, noted that Twitter users often reveal their location through their Tweets or photos. "Most people don't realize how much information they're leaking," he said.

In extreme cases, Twitter users can expose their exact locations. According to the study, over 2% of Tweets—or around 4.4 million daily—expose users' exact locations. This typically happens when users enable geotagging and simultaneously post updates indicating they are home, thereby revealing their home addresses.

Twitter claims geotagging is off by default, and it removes geolocation data from photos uploaded to the platform. However, it cannot remove identifiable landmarks in uploaded photos, which could still reveal users' locations.

6. "We won't really stop cyberbullying."

According to a survey released by Piper Jaffray in October, Twitter has surpassed Facebook as the most popular social site among teenagers. 26% of teens primarily use Twitter, while 23% mainly use Facebook and Instagram. However, wherever there are teenagers, there's likely bullying. On Twitter, this behavior can be particularly distressing because if someone searches for the victim's name, negative posts about them may appear. Additionally, victims trying to block bullies from following them often cannot prevent the bullies from continuing to post defamatory messages.

Justin Patchin, a criminology professor at the University of Wisconsin-Eau Claire and co-director of the Cyberbullying Research Center, said the popularity of social sites among teenagers places them at risk of cyberbullying because teenagers can freely express themselves on these platforms without regulation. Based on several surveys conducted by the center over the past three years, about 20% of teenagers aged 10-18 reported experiencing cyberbullying. An independent study by the University of Wisconsin-Madison found that over 15,000 bullying-related posts are made on Twitter every day.

Cyberbullying is not unique to Twitter. The site allows users to set their pages as private to prevent others from reading their posts. Twitter suggests on its website that users unfollow people they dislike to end interactions. If the harassment continues, users can consider blocking bullies or reporting them to Twitter. If users feel physically threatened, they can print or screenshot evidence of the bullying and present it to the police. Twitter even hinted that if users persistently post threatening content, the site might close their accounts. The company's policy also states that its service is not intended for children under 13.

7. "Stock information is abundant but not necessarily reliable."

Some analysts claim that Twitter can help predict stock price movements, offering guidance to investors. In 2011, a paper jointly published by professors from Indiana University and Manchester University claimed that Twitter users' sentiments could predict short-term (1-4 days later) stock price changes. For example, when people's posts indicate increasing anxiety, the Dow Jones Industrial Average tends to fall; conversely, when posts show increasing relaxation or calmness, the Dow Jones rises.

Many seem to agree with this finding. Many companies now analyze Tweets to anticipate stock price movements. Once they understand Twitter users' sentiments, they inform their clients promptly, allowing them to buy when prices are expected to rise and sell when prices are expected to fall. Igor Gonta, founder and CEO of Market Prophit, said that on August 13, billionaire Carl Icahn tweeted that his company had acquired a large stake in Apple, believing the stock was undervalued and mentioning a conversation with Apple CEO Tim Cook earlier that day. Immediately afterward, his company informed clients that Apple's stock price might rise. Indeed, Apple's stock closed nearly 5% higher that day, reaching a six-month high.

In its S-1 filing, Twitter claims that some of the data it sells "may relate to users' portfolios." The company provides "actionable data" access to data partners to help them "find clues that may impact economic decisions."

However, looking for stock movement signals on Twitter carries risks. For instance, during the Federal Reserve meeting preparation period in September, many Tweets incorrectly predicted that the Fed might taper its bond-buying program, causing many investors to sell prematurely before bond values appreciated.

8. "We're losing ground in the fight against spammers and hackers."

Not all Twitter users are genuine. Some have malicious intent. Twitter estimated in its S-1 filing that "fake or spam accounts" account for about 5% of its monthly active users, roughly 11.6 million. The actual number could be higher. Spammers include those who send misleading links via email or Tweets, tricking users into downloading malware. For general users, if spam Tweets or emails carry viruses, they can cause significant damage. In its S-1 filing, Twitter said the continuous growth in user numbers may encourage spammers to abuse the platform further; Twitter plans to "fight spam, including suspending or terminating accounts it deems spammy, and adjusting algorithms to focus on combating relentless activities."

However, hackers have successfully bypassed Twitter's security measures. In the first quarter of 2013, Twitter's system was hacked. The company claimed that hackers might have obtained the usernames, email addresses, access tokens (unique identifiers assigned by the server when users visit a website), and encrypted passwords of approximately 250,000 users. Twitter stated on its website that upon discovering a hack, it takes immediate action, including resetting passwords, invalidating related account access tokens, and notifying affected users to create new passwords promptly. In August, the Syrian Electronic Army hacked one of Twitter's domains, twimg.com, changing the profile pictures of certain Twitter users. Twitter stated in a statement that it restored the domain within less than two hours of the attack, and user information was not compromised.

9. "We're hot, but not profitable."

Despite its popularity, Twitter remains unprofitable—its losses continue to widen. According to Twitter's S-1 filing, its net loss in the first three quarters of this year was $133.9 million, compared to $70.7 million during the same period last year. Moreover, the growth rate of Twitter's user base is slowing. In the third quarter ending September 30, the average monthly active users were 231.7 million, an increase of 6.13% from the end of the second quarter. In comparison, the quarterly growth rate was 10.6% during the same period last year and 17.9% in the corresponding period in 2011.

In the past few years, although Twitter's revenue has been growing, its costs have also been rising daily. Experts point out that the company has been actively investing in research and development, which should help it become profitable in the long run. In the first three quarters of this year, it invested over $199.1 million in its R&D department, an increase of 153% compared to the same period last year.

Twitter expects its costs to continue rising in its S-1 filing: it plans to expand its domestic and international operations, develop its technical infrastructure, and invest in R&D.

10. "Our stock is already trading—internally."

Investors may be thrilled to finally have the chance to buy and sell Twitter's stock. However, according to Twitter's S-1 filing, its stock trading has actually already started. Before the company goes public, insiders—such as founders or angel investors—already hold partial shares and can sell them in private markets or various online trading markets. Shareholders need the company's approval to conduct such transactions. Many startup tech companies do this to retain employees. (Many tech companies give their employees large amounts of private stock, and if employees need money urgently to buy homes, they can sell these stocks in private markets.)

During this private trading process, stock prices often appreciate, especially when the company is preparing to go public. "The earlier investors get involved, the lower the stock price, and the more likely they are to make big money," said Igor Gonta of Market Prophit.

However, many investors cannot access these potential gains. Only accredited investors can buy the stock. According to SEC regulations, these individual investors must have a net worth exceeding $1 million or annual income over $200,000 in the past two years. Sometimes, even this income level isn't enough. Companies choosing to trade stocks on secondary markets usually select investors capable of purchasing private shares. In most cases, they prefer institutional investors, such as venture capital funds and asset management companies, over individual investors.

The only way for regular investors to buy Twitter stock is to purchase publicly traded funds holding Twitter shares. For instance, as of September 30, Firsthand Technology Value Fund held approximately 11% of Twitter's shares; and as of June 30, GSV Capital held approximately 15% of Twitter's shares. Of course, investors buying these funds may see diluted returns from Twitter's growth since these funds often invest in many other areas, some of which may fail.
