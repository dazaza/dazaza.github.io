Various Aspects of Postpartum Recovery

Having gone through pregnancy and childbirth, I have lost another great title in this world—mother. Because pregnancy and childbirth cause significant changes to all organs of the body, there needs to be a period after delivery for rest and recovery. This period is called the puerperium, or commonly known as "sitting the month." The puerperium usually lasts 42 days. By the end of this period, perinatal health care requires that mothers go to the hospital for a health check-up.

1. Recovery of All Systems:

After pregnancy and childbirth, the placenta is expelled from the uterine surface, ending the placental circulation. A large amount of blood returns to the systemic circulation, increasing the burden on the heart. Gradually, over a period of 2-6 weeks, blood volume gradually returns to pre-pregnancy levels. Due to the need for hemostasis and repair of the uterine cavity's large wound, the blood is in a hypercoagulable state, with relatively high levels of white blood cells and platelets, which are beneficial for repairing the uterine wound. Similarly, the urinary system and digestive system gradually recover; the bladder contraction and slowed intestinal motility caused during childbirth also gradually return to normal within 4-6 weeks.

2. Reproductive System Recovery:

Uterine Involution: The most significant change during pregnancy is the enlargement of the uterus, which can accommodate a baby weighing around 7 pounds along with the placenta, umbilical cord, amniotic fluid, etc. After these contents are expelled during childbirth, the size of the uterus gradually decreases, shrinking from 1000g to its pre-pregnancy state of 50g within 6-8 weeks. The endometrial surface becomes rough after the placenta detaches, and the decidua undergoes degenerative changes. The necrotic decidua forms lochia, which is expelled from the body. The uterine segment also gradually contracts, and the cervical os gradually shrinks. Due to lacerations at the 3 o'clock and 9 o'clock positions of the cervix during childbirth, the internal cervical os heals into a “--” shape. The vagina, due to compression by the fetal head, experiences edema, hardening, disappearance of folds, and improved elasticity, all of which gradually return to their pre-pregnancy state during the puerperium.

Due to excessive stretching of the pelvic floor muscles during childbirth, elasticity decreases, which is one of the main reasons for future uterine prolapse and vaginal laxity.

The most noticeable postpartum phenomenon is the discharge of lochia, which contains blood and necrotic decidua. There are several types of lochia:

- Bloody Lochia: Bright red in color, it contains a large amount of blood, is abundant, and sometimes has small blood clots. It also contains small amounts of amnion and necrotic decidua tissue. Bloody lochia persists for 3 to 4 days, after which the amount of uterine bleeding gradually decreases, and serous fluid increases, transforming into serous lochia.

- Serous Lochia: Pinkish-red in color, it contains a large amount of serous fluid and some blood but more necrotic decidua, cervical mucus, uterine cavity exudate, and bacteria. Serous lochia lasts about 10-14 days, gradually decreasing in volume, with increased leukocytes, turning into white lochia.

- White Lochia: Thin and pinkish in color, it contains a large amount of white blood cells, necrotic decidua, epithelial cells, and bacteria. White lochia typically clears up after about 3 weeks.

Normal lochia has a bloody smell but no foul odor, lasting 4 to 6 weeks, with a volume of approximately 250ml-500ml. Some women clear up in 20 days, while others may continue up to 40-50 days, with significant individual differences.

We mainly observe the amount, color, and odor of lochia to understand whether the uterus is recovering normally. If, after two weeks postpartum, the lochia remains bloody, is abundant, and accompanied by a foul odor, sometimes discharging flesh-like substances or placental-like materials, it indicates poor uterine involution. In this case, it should be considered that there might be residual placenta or membranes in the uterus, which could lead to massive bleeding at any time, necessitating immediate medical attention. Foul-smelling lochia accompanied by uterine tenderness might indicate puerperal infection, leading to endometritis or myometritis. Symptoms include fever, lower back pain, increased lochia with a foul odor. Sometimes, the lochia not only has a foul odor but also appears turbid, dirty, and brownish. In such cases, timely medical attention is necessary to avoid delaying treatment.

Generally, lochia clears up within three weeks postpartum. If it continues beyond three weeks, doctors often diagnose it as "persistent lochia," which is mostly due to poor uterine involution. Taking medications that promote uterine contraction and expulsion of lochia can resolve the issue. Another situation involves sudden increase in lochia after early postpartum, with dark-colored blood or repeated fresh blood flow, indicating late postpartum hemorrhage. Causes of late postpartum hemorrhage include retained placenta, poor uterine contraction, coagulation disorders, cesarean section or other injuries and infections. Immediate medical attention is required to identify the cause and stop the bleeding. Do not mistakenly assume it is menstruation, as it could lead to life-threatening complications.

Lochia can truly be regarded as the barometer of postpartum recovery. Therefore, we must pay attention to genital hygiene, as it is the place where urine, feces, and lochia mix. The cervical os is still open and not fully restored, so neglecting cleanliness can lead to ascending infections. Wounds from perineal tears or episiotomies during childbirth can also affect wound healing.

Therefore, we must learn to observe lochia ourselves. We also need to adjust our activities and diet postpartum. Due to the fatigue of pregnancy and the exertion of childbirth, recovery is needed during the puerperium. Simultaneously, the discharge of lochia, lactation, and breastfeeding consume a lot of physical strength and energy. Therefore, adequate rest and nutritional supplementation are essential during the puerperium.

3. Recovery of Ovarian Axis Function:

Around one week postpartum, the endocrine system gradually returns to its pre-pregnancy state. Two weeks postpartum, human chorionic gonadotropin is no longer detectable. Placental lactogen disappears within six hours postpartum, and follicle-stimulating hormone and luteinizing hormone gradually recover by six weeks postpartum. During pregnancy, the ovaries are in a resting state. Normally, postpartum breastfeeding inhibits ovulation and menstruation. However, some women resume ovulation 42 days postpartum, so contraception is necessary when resuming sexual activity.

The timing and amount of the first postpartum menstrual period vary among individuals. When breastfeeding, increased prolactin inhibits the secretion of gonadotropin-releasing hormone, causing the ovaries to lack an endocrine cycle and thus no ovulation or menstruation. However, when breast milk production decreases, the child suckles less effectively, or mixed feeding occurs, hormonal suppression weakens, resulting in ovulation and menstruation.

Due to the recovery of the endocrine system, the first menstrual period might be heavy, with irregular cycles for the first few months before gradually returning to normal.

If there is a significant amount of bleeding after a short period without menstruation, it could be due to the recovery process of the endometrium. The prolonged duration of menstruation might result in coffee-colored secretions due to acidity. This is generally not alarming unless it persists for too long, in which case medical advice is recommended.

Breastfeeding does not affect lactation even if menstruation resumes. At this time, the mother's body consumes more energy, so nutritional foods and soups should be consumed to ensure sufficient breast milk supply.

If menstruation does not return after weaning, it could be due to the larger size of the uterus during breastfeeding, requiring time to recover. Alternatively, consulting a doctor for appropriate adjustments is advisable.

4. Breast Care:

The most significant change postpartum occurs in the breasts. After pregnancy, the levels of progesterone decrease, and under the influence of prolactin, milk secretion begins, providing the best food for the baby. Proper breast care ensures ample breast milk for exclusive breastfeeding up to six months, laying the foundation for lifelong health for the child. Preventing mastitis is equally important.

5. Psychological Adjustment:

As a new mother, facing a lively and adorable new life, one must adapt to the presence of the child and coordinate relationships between oneself, the child, and other family members. Raising a child requires full dedication, so having a mindset of willingness to endure hardship, take responsibility, be strict, and remain optimistic is crucial for dealing with all postpartum matters. Childbirth is a joyful psychological process. We call upon society, family members, and mothers themselves not to treat mothers as patients. Excessive protection and distrust can cast abnormal shadows on a mother’s psychology, potentially triggering postpartum depression, which is detrimental to both her mental and physical recovery and the healthy growth of the infant.
