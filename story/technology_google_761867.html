Will Google come out with an Android notebook?

by geekzhang on 2013-05-13 21:15:29

The release of Android 3.0 allowed Android to extend its battleground into the tablet market. However, Android, which has been triumphant in the mobile sector, seems to have even greater ambitions to counterattack on the desktop. Previous reports indicated that Android 5.0 would provide optimizations for laptops, and there were also reports that Samsung intended to act preemptively again by releasing an Android laptop before the launch of Android 5.0.Is an Android laptop a good idea?From an external perspective, the PC industry is declining, with mobility and touch becoming the mainstream of the future. However, this does not mean the end of the PC market; the demand for laptops still exists. Yet, despite making significant breakthroughs in portability and interaction, ultrabooks have not seen much success, partly due to their high prices. On the other hand, low-priced Windows RT has been coldly received by the market due to reasons such as a lack of software and poor compatibility. In other words, there is still a market for PCs, and the key lies in whether it can meet basic user needs while ensuring a low price.Clearly, price control is never an issue for Android. Due to the free system, ARM-based Android laptops will inevitably be cheaper than ultrabooks, and manufacturers are fully capable of keeping the pricing between $200 and $300, which is a huge advantage.Productivity is a necessary condition that distinguishes laptops from smartphones and tablets. Productivity can be reflected in two aspects: one is the number of applications, and the other is operability. With previous accumulations, even if hundreds of thousands of applications cannot all be transplanted, some will still be usable. Of course, quality will be a big issue. Android 3.0, optimized for tablets, was released over two years ago, yet the applications available on Android tablets remain far from satisfactory. However, compared to Windows RT, being worse is still better than having nothing.However, Windows RT has its unique advantage - the Office suite. In fact, this is a huge advantage; document processing is one of the most important functions of desktop systems. What does Android have? Google Docs, but clearly, the current Docs is too lightweight to handle desktop-level work.Evidently, to enter the desktop market, Google's transformation work will be a considerable project, whether it be the user interface, multitasking, or system-level applications like Chrome and Docs. Google may even need to rebuild a desktop environment? However, if Google truly intends to incorporate PCs into Android's future development plan, these issues do not seem insurmountable.Besides, from the perspective of market and user recognition, Android has already been widely applied and recognized in the smartphone field. Extending this halo effect to the desktop market might encounter less resistance.Looking at internal possibilities, Google already has Chromebooks. Data shows that Chromebooks have already sold 500,000 units, with an estimated 1 million sales within this year. In fact, before the launch of Chromebook Pixel, Chromebooks were synonymous with affordable laptops. Does Google need to start another stove in the same house?Prior personnel adjustments within Google saw Sundar Pichai, responsible for Chrome applications, taking charge of both the Android and Chrome departments. The industry almost unanimously believed that this move, to some extent, revealed Google's plans regarding the relationship between Android and Chrome, possibly signaling the prelude to the integration of the two systems. In reality, although these two platforms share a series of Google online services, as Schmidt previously stated, they solve different problems. The fundamental reason lies in the opposite logic of the two: Android represents the appification of the Internet, while Chrome represents cloudification.In reality, Android is at its peak, whereas Chrome OS appears somewhat immature and ahead of its time. Integrating an immature product into a mature one obviously carries great risks. From a short-term interest perspective, rather than actively suppressing one party to promote a single concept, it might be better to follow trends and let both develop. Google certainly will not abandon Chrome OS, but Android is evidently more suited to make a bold push under the current circumstances.
