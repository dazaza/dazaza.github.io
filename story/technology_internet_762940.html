A entrepreneur's self-narration: How I sell fish online

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 18:09:04

　　In fact, when I first started selling fish online, many of my friends around me thought I was crazy.

　　When everyone's eyes were on real estate, technology, mobile phones, and cars, I chose to sell fish. It was indeed a very difficult decision, but it felt solid.

　　How can O2O be done creatively? Should we abandon WeChat? Who is more awesome, Lei Jun or J.Wong? Can BlackBerry make a comeback? Can Cook take over from Master Jobs? I don't care about these things because they are far away from us. Dreams are big, but reality is small. People should live more down-to-earth.

　　Youcai failed, and its demise was regrettable. But I still have no doubt about the enormous potential of fresh produce in e-commerce.

　　In 2012, I began trying B2C by selling fish online. Youcai failed, but I survived. Of course, this might be related to the fact that I was just a small-scale start-up company with low operating costs and easy execution. However, some experiences I've gathered along the way still hold certain value, and I'd like to share them with all of you. Perhaps they could give some inspiration to those who are starting businesses online selling fish, vegetables, or fruits.

1. Product Positioning

My product positioning is: never do low-priced daily necessities; focus only on mid-range products. In the initial stage, avoid vegetables and meat, and focus exclusively on frozen seafood.

Let me explain. I believe your biggest competitor in e-commerce for fresh produce is the local farmers' market. Compared to them, I consider myself at a disadvantage. First, the traditional group of shoppers is concentrated among middle-aged and elderly people, most of whom will never develop the habit of buying fish online. Second, you might say that selling fish online saves tens of thousands or even hundreds of thousands of yuan in stall fees compared to individual vendors, but the harsh reality is: compared to vendors who restock almost every morning with nearly zero inventory, your warehousing costs, labor costs, and delivery costs over the year will be much higher than theirs.

Youcai mainly failed here. Observe its product structure: there are almost no mid-to-high-end products, mostly common agricultural by-products found in farmers' markets, which directly faced competition from local farmers' markets. Trying to change the purchasing habits of traditional aunties is as unrealistic as expecting cows to suddenly stop eating grass and switch to meat.

Compared to supermarkets, I found my advantages quite clear: shorter procurement cycles mean fresher products (referring to bulk goods), prices nearly 40% cheaper than supermarkets (don't think supermarkets are cheap), and a younger customer base (I observed that most supermarket shoppers are aged between 25-40), which fits my requirements well.

Seafood has many categories, mainly divided into live seafood, chilled seafood, and frozen seafood (I won't explain each one individually here; if you're interested, you can look it up yourself). Live seafood has the highest storage requirements, with price differences of about 50% between live and dead fish, so it's not suitable for small-cost operators like me. Frozen seafood after preliminary processing is the easiest to preserve with minimal loss; generally, as long as the temperature is maintained, the shelf life is about one and a half to two years (Ding Jingtao said Youcai’s fresh produce loss rate was 5%, which I find hard to believe).

Therefore, I positioned my competitors as supermarkets. I selected some raw materials used in buffets, Western restaurants, mid-to-high-end Chinese restaurants, and Japanese cuisine. These products have high profit margins, are not easily found on the market, and are easier to preserve. This way, I avoided the impact of farmers' markets while enhancing the quality perception of my products.

2. Price Positioning

Unlike luxury items like mobile phones, cars, and houses, this market is extremely sensitive to price.

For example, if a pound of cabbage or a piece of salmon is priced more than 5 yuan above the market price, customers won't be interested unless your price is lower than the market price. And the profit margin on a pound of cabbage versus a piece of salmon—well, I don’t need to elaborate, you get it.

Regarding pricing, Ding Jingtao of Youcai once said: "Even tomatoes can vary in price by 2 to 5 times. The quality of vegetables on the market varies greatly, and their nutritional safety differs as well. Youcai always tries to choose high-quality produce. For instance, cucumbers may cost 1.2 yuan or 1.5 yuan per unit, and we would choose the 1.5 yuan ones."

"So, the vegetable prices on Youcai tend to be slightly higher than those at the farmers' market. Ding emphasized that if the quality is the same, Youcai is definitely cheaper."

To these statements, I would say: Mr. Ding must have never sold vegetables himself.

Ninety percent of aunties absolutely won’t buy cabbage that costs more than the market price, no matter how much you hype it up, whether it's organic green super-invincible cabbage or not. Aunties are practical; they'll buy from whoever offers lower prices with acceptable quality.

Since the product is positioned as agricultural by-products, even the slightest price fluctuation can significantly affect sales. If Ding follows his approach, he can only sell agricultural by-products that are barely qualified but much cheaper than the market price, aiming to increase volume. This allows him to capture a large market share with low profit margins. However, for a start-up company without market experience or brand recognition, aiming to capture the market right from the beginning is very risky.

Without sufficient gross profit margins, it's difficult to even conduct marketing promotions. Unfortunately, Ding's mindset was to create premium agricultural by-products, yet expect aunties to accept higher-than-market prices without being able to inspect the goods on the spot. This undoubtedly boxed him into a corner.

Therefore, I set realistic prices for my products: they must be below market prices but highly profitable. I don’t care about market share; I only care if the gross profit margin is high enough.

Earning 1 fen from 1000 people versus earning 1 yuan from 100 people—I know how to calculate that.

3. Channel Costs

A personal suggestion: any industry should have its own unique channels. These channels aren’t necessarily monopolistic, but relatively rare in terms of product sourcing, giving your product a certain uniqueness and scarcity.

While doing wholesale, I pondered this issue, and the solution was simple: first, try to avoid first-tier cities where competition is fierce. After all, how can you compete barefoot against someone driving a car? Second, choose less popular origins. My product sources are concentrated in three places: South America, Africa, and Northeast Asia. The weakness of giants (including other large aquatic companies) lies in their inflexibility despite their size. All I need to do is observe the market, select the right products, and make decisions independently. By the time they get multiple levels of approval, I’ll have already established a stable channel for my products.

Youcai positioned its channels domestically, which had too low a barrier to entry. Wholesale principles are similar to group buying, relying mainly on volume. Today, you can negotiate with farmers, and tomorrow, they can just as easily do business with others. Today, you can launch a Youcai, and tomorrow, they can replicate another “Yancaihua.”

4. Marketing Model

This is something we must discuss in detail.

Youcai positioned itself as “delivering vegetables like milk,” attempting to build a closed ecosystem akin to iOS, where customers could enjoy doorstep delivery services without leaving home. This raises two issues: 1. How do you attract potential customers to your platform? 2. Why would customers stay? (This is what Mr. Ding referred to as increasing customer stickiness.)

I first noticed Youcai through a Sina report. Since the products weren't much different, out of curiosity, I clicked to check it out. Upon opening its UI, I was disappointed. An outdated interface with no design appeal, besides product types and prices, there were no other relevant consultations. A limited variety of products also included items like disinfectants and toothpaste, which had nothing to do with "vegetables"...

I really couldn't understand how Youcai planned to retain customers. Opening its webpage felt like walking into a roadside store filled with miscellaneous goods, making me forget that I was originally looking for "vegetables."

Relying solely on an uninnovative milk-style sales model of orders-delivery-after-sales service to attract customers was undoubtedly Youcai's biggest failure.

So, before I started, I had people prepare my products into various dishes. Just for one type of crab, I made it into stews, soups, stir-fries, braises, etc. Then I hired professional photographers to artistically process the dishes, making them look as appetizing as possible.

In this regard, I borrowed from IKEA's concept: tempting customers with finished products while only selling semi-finished ones. Letting customers fully experience the joy of accomplishment is much stronger than simply giving them a finished product.

Later, when many customers exchanged ideas on QQ, I would simply talk to them about how to use my ingredients to cook, rather than discussing how delicious the fish or crabs were. Taobao actually sells pictures, not products, and fresh produce is the same. Eventually, I stopped promoting products outright and instead chatted with food enthusiasts about how to eat them best.

Positioning towards a younger customer base, many people want to eat but don’t know how. Teaching them how to cook can be more effective. (In this aspect, sometimes I even think Dianping might be better suited for fresh produce.)

More and more customers gave positive feedback. I observed that 80% of them were food enthusiasts, and 20% came due to referrals from friends. For this reason, I specifically created several QQ groups where members could freely exchange food experiences (as long as it didn't violate national laws) and compare seafood from other stores. Don't fear competition; the more competition, the more it highlights the uniqueness and price advantage of your products.

In terms of model, Youcai aimed to do electronic + commerce mode, but personally, I think this was also a mistake. There are two paths to choose from: one is to become a platform like Taobao, building relationships with offline wholesalers, where the platform itself doesn't engage in commerce. The second is to only do commerce, quickly establishing various regional distribution platforms, utilizing many options such as SNS, regional BBS, Weibo marketing, WeChat marketing, etc., focusing on channel quality and price.

I lean towards the latter, as it allows direct contact with customers.

My current model is primarily wholesale offline, mainly supplying hotels, buffets, and sushi restaurants, etc. Online, I focus on retail, with prices slightly higher than offline. But I don’t create my own platform; I merely affiliate with several popular local BBS communities, using their traffic to attract customers. If the product gets a good response, it spreads virally.

Sometimes, customers even upload photos of their own cooked dishes to "show off." Generally, I offer small additional rewards, and over time, hardly any customers leave the QQ groups. Exchanges increase, and by the second half of last year, I almost didn't need to promote online anymore; customers helped evaluate the products themselves.

Another benefit of this approach is resolving trust issues. Doing fresh produce online in China faces challenges beyond standardization (which I'll discuss later); another point is the lack of trust in the hygiene of online fresh produce. Simply put, after drinking Sanlu milk and eating Sudan Red eggs, the general public has lost significant trust in merchants. However, word-of-mouth can solve this problem.

In terms of profit model, I adopt small-batch retailing. My minimum standard is five pounds per package, and I don’t retail single or double-pound packages. Prices are attractive, and the volume increases accordingly.

5. Warehousing and Logistics

I dare say, aside from procurement costs, warehousing and logistics are definitely another major expense. This includes labor + transportation + warehousing fees + losses.

Youcai adopted a method inspired by milk boxes, delivering door-to-door at fixed times each day. I think there's a big problem with this approach: assume, I order a pound of cabbage, two pounds of pork belly, and half a pound of ginger and garlic just before finishing work today. When I get home, I realize that what I see online is very different from what I see in reality, so I decide not to keep it. The Youcai customer service is great, and after communication, they agree to unconditional returns.

My question is: what am I supposed to do for dinner tonight?

If I have an experience like this once, I probably won't shop for agricultural products on Youcai again just to save a few yuan, because I'll end up hungry...

And Youcai not only loses out on cancellations and customer retention but also faces losses (some products degrade in quality overnight or after being out of the warehouse for a while), plus the repeated labor and transportation costs incurred from returns.

This is the biggest challenge e-commerce faces when dealing with fresh produce: how to solve product standardization.

Honestly speaking, I currently don't have a good solution for this problem. I've heard complaints like "it doesn't look exactly like the picture online," and all I can do is compensate with service—if they're not satisfied, I'll refund them.

Because my company is small, I can only save costs. Based on the number of regional orders, I deliver at fixed times and verify the goods in person. This way, I can explain face-to-face to eliminate misunderstandings (some are caused by the customer's lack of understanding of the product) and avoid the repetitive losses of re-delivering goods.
