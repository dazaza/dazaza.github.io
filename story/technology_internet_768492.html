The latest IDC data shows that Chromebook has suffered a major setback.

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 18:55:29

Ever since the launch of Chromebooks over two years ago, Steven J. Vaughan-Nichols from tech blog ZDNet has been tracking their sales figures. In June 2011, he boldly declared that Chromebooks were a "Windows killer" and predicted that "Microsoft was about to face real trouble in the PC market."

By April last year, there were predictions suggesting that Chromebooks might be struggling. However, recently Steven countered with the claim, "Over the past year, Chromebooks have been as popular as hot cakes on a cold morning."

This surprised me enough to delve into the latest statistics provided by market research firm IDC. What I found was that there had been little change in Chromebook sales since the start of this year. In fact, these statistics even suggest that Chromebooks have suffered a major setback.

Rajani Singh, senior research analyst at IDC, said: "With the exception of Samsung, other manufacturers haven't sold many Chromebooks. Even Samsung's Chromebooks are merely seen as replacements for aging netbooks, gaining some traction only in K-12 (kindergarten through 12th grade) schools. In Q3 2013, Samsung sold approximately 652,000 Chromebooks globally. Other suppliers like Lenovo, Acer, and HP also shipped units, but in very small quantities. Samsung continues to dominate the market."

In contrast, during Q3 2013, global PC sales reached 81.6 million units; tablet sales totaled 47.6 million, including 14.1 million iPads and 33.5 million other tablets. According to Rachel King from ZDNet, in Q3 this year, "The PC market isn't as bad as people think," mainly due to increased demand for business PCs in enterprise and government markets. She added, "IDC concluded that the consumer market is 'at best lukewarm'."

The combined sales of PCs and tablets amounted to 129 million units. During this period, Samsung, despite being the dominant supplier, sold just 652,000 Chromebooks. Assuming other suppliers with "small shipments" collectively sold half as much as Samsung, total Chromebook sales still fall short of one million units. This roughly equates to the number of Surface RT units Microsoft sold this quarter.

In other words, during Q3 2013, Chromebook sales accounted for at most 1.2% of the PC market; and only 0.76% of the combined PC and tablet market.

This hardly qualifies as success. Chromebooks have hit the same wall that caused netbooks to crash and burn.

Over the past one or two years, the netbook market has shrunk drastically while business PC sales have remained relatively stable. In Q3 2013, Lenovo, Dell, and HP saw their market shares grow globally, driven primarily by business PC sales. Over the years, Acer and Asus focused heavily on the consumer market, investing significantly in netbooks and affordable PCs. In Q3 this year, their market shares dropped by about 34% year-over-year.

Stephen Baker, vice president of industry analysis for consumer technology at NPD Group, stated that Chromebooks "maintained a 20-25% share of the entry-level consumer notebook market in 2013." If true, this reflects how weak the consumer PC market, especially the low-end segment, truly is.

Based on the above Chromebook sales figures, the entire entry-level consumer notebook market didn't exceed 4 million units, representing less than 5% of the overall PC market.

IDC analyst Rajani Singh pointed out that the current impact of Chromebooks is greatest as a "replacement for aging netbooks" in K-12 schools. There is no indication yet that consumers are genuinely interested in Chromebooks. In price-sensitive low-end markets, consumers are rejecting anything resembling a laptop, opting instead for iPads, Kindles, and budget Android tablets.
