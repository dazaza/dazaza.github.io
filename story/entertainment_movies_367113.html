Boring movie, perfect audiovisual experience —— A brief review of the Blu-ray version of "Vantage Point"

by diglog on 2010-10-07 05:15:06

In the DVD era, *Vantage Point* was one of the more disappointing discs for me. It had a unique but boring storyline and subpar audio quality that felt uninspired. The multi-perspective storytelling approach has become increasingly common in recent times, but it's a risky strategy. If not executed well, it can become tediously redundant, and unfortunately, this film largely falls into the latter category. Seeing Dennis Quaid’s face repeatedly appear in the same venue over and over again was truly a torment. Oscar-winning actor Forest Whitaker's performance in this movie felt a bit exaggerated—too many expressions, overly dramatic reactions, and an unnecessary look of anguish that left me somewhat perplexed. The only actor I liked, Ashton (the President), didn’t have enough screen time. The pacing of the movie improved somewhat in the second half, so I found the latter part more engaging than the first.

Despite my dislike for the plot, the Blu-ray disc's outstanding picture quality brought me immense joy. As expected, as long as there are no obvious digital compression flaws, the visuals of new films usually don't disappoint. Of course, we can't rule out some poorly converted releases. Rich details, vibrant colors, and a fiery depiction of Spain (despite some scenes being shot in Mexico City)... Many close-up shots showcased clear facial details, and the debris flying everywhere during the explosion scenes were impressively rendered. The contrast was appropriate, with no overly dark scenes or overly exposed daylight sequences. The visual quality was nearly flawless and worthy of praise.

The English Dolby TrueHD surround sound completely erased my "psychological shadow" about the audio quality from the DVD version. The explosions that sounded like flatulence on the DVD finally regained their proper impact. The street car chase scenes provided rich surround sound effects that left a deep impression on me. The story immediately became more gripping, with solid and powerful gunshots and explosions. The sound of shattering cars felt like it was happening right in front of you.

There wasn’t much exciting content in the extras. A 50-minute standard behind-the-scenes featurette made up the bulk of the material. One interesting extra was the GPS tracking feature. When selected, a mini map would display during the main feature showing the positions and movements of other characters during "meanwhile" scenes, which was quite fun. This feature requires a compatible Blu-ray player to function. I bought this disc mainly for its fresh and excellent audio-visual experience, but I wouldn't strongly recommend it otherwise.
