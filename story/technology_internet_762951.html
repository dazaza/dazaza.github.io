"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 18:16:03

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of senior executives, was hit again. On July 17, it was reported that according to insiders at Yougou.com, the company's COO Zhang Xiaojun is set to leave and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun's departure is particularly significant, indicating that Yougou.com is indeed facing serious crises.

Let's take a look back at the e-commerce journey of Belle International, the parent company of Yougou.com:

Belle quietly experimented with e-commerce several years ago, developing steadily through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed via Taoxiu.com). Led by veteran group member Xie Yunli, an initially modest e-commerce team started by selling products from Belle's own brands, gradually introducing exclusive online models and eventually launching new online brands. This development path was considered a successful case of a traditional brand entering e-commerce more solidly than Li-Ning before 2011.

However, as Letao.com and Haolemai gained over $100 million in financing and rapidly developed in 2010, Belle became restless. On one hand, there was concern about being constrained by distribution channels; on the other hand, after evaluating its potential, Belle realized it had significant advantages in the e-commerce sector, including:

1. Numerous brands: In addition to Belle's own major women’s shoe brands, its agency for internationally renowned sports brands like Adidas and Nike saw astonishing growth in online sales.

2. Easier category expansion: For Belle, which specializes in women’s shoes and well-known sports brands, achieving absolute market dominance through multi-brand and multi-category strategies was their main focus. Expanding offline stores proved too slow, while their ongoing e-commerce business could easily add brands but struggled to quickly expand categories.

3. Financial advantage: During cooperation with e-commerce enterprises, Belle discovered that e-commerce is capital-intensive. With financial strength, Belle could develop without relying on external capital, allowing it to outlast many vertical e-commerce companies dependent on funding.

4. Talent advantage: After years of e-commerce practice, Belle had cultivated a highly professional and capable e-commerce team, ready to scale up or even go further.

5. Supply chain advantage: This excited Belle immensely and attracted many high-level executives from e-commerce platforms or brand e-commerce. For e-commerce, supply chains are always fascinating because they seem to be the critical factor beyond capital determining survival. The supply chain includes product research and development, production, warehouse management, delivery, and after-sales service—a series of “business” contents. In traditional businesses that prioritize “commerce” over “e-commerce,” this is seen as a superior competitive advantage compared to pure e-commerce enterprises.

With these advantages, Belle decided to make a big push into e-commerce. First, it purchased the domain name "yougou.com," abandoning the slightly copycat "topshoes.cn" Taixiu website and redirecting traffic. All distribution sites stopped receiving supplies, though direct third-party stores on Taobao and Tmall were retained, albeit with reduced investment and attention. Belle recruited top talents from JD.com and Vancl to serve as CMO and COO respectively, while sending a seasoned veteran to act as CEO, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to build a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing multi-brand strategies and expanding into apparel and other categories to realize the dream of a department store empire impossible to achieve offline!

All sounded perfect, but every dream needs to face the harsh reality. The realities were:

1. Higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Located in Shenzhen headquarters, Belle couldn’t attract more excellent technical talents, and outsourcing services were scarce. However, technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C involves dynamic technology, different from relatively static technologies like ERP, making it a challenge for many traditional enterprises.

2. Increased management difficulty with category expansion: Someone once said that excelling in women’s shoes doesn’t guarantee success in men’s shoes, which Belle likely experienced during its male shoe category expansion. Expanding into women’s clothing and accessories within a year undoubtedly caused backend difficulties. Traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous calculations struggle with rapid expansion tactics that worked for Vancl in special circumstances. Although Belle hired Vancl’s COO and JD.com’s CMO, whether they could understand and execute with "exaggerated costs" remains questionable.

3. Institutional barriers: Many people outside e-commerce and traditional enterprises find it hard to understand these differences. Simply put, organizational structure, approvals, finance and budgeting, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, and procedural systems form a company’s "mechanism." The conflict arises when e-commerce attempts to persuade traditional teams to accept its system, and vice versa, leading to inefficiency and instability in execution teams. Ultimately, the chairman or board might say, "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have their strengths and weaknesses, and the future will see integration, so you must integrate in these areas...". If such "integration without harmony" occurs, it becomes a so-called management innovation, resulting in the bizarre "CEO rotation system" appearing in a company like Belle!

Initially, it seemed Yougou.com would lead the pack, with senior executives awaiting the glory of an IPO and enjoying success and wealth, setting examples for countless traditional enterprises succeeding in the e-commerce era. Instead, they found themselves entangled in these fundamental issues.

After all, whether it’s the veterans who grew with Belle or the recruited e-commerce professionals, their departures weren't due to lack of ability. JD.com VPs returning to JD.com continue to excel, and Vancl VPs have the courage to start their own ventures. Are these the three core issues? Or are these not just Belle's problems but also common challenges for many traditional enterprises venturing into e-commerce?
