Behind the Low-Priced Moto G: Google's Android Aims for Both Offensive and Defensive Strategy

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 20:54:59

Just as Motorola's Moto X is struggling in sales, the company recently launched the Moto G smartphone. Its unlocked, no-contract version priced at $179 (approximately RMB 1090) has drawn significant attention within the industry.

Despite Google's claim that Motorola Mobility would operate independently after its acquisition, Google spent $12.5 billion on the initial purchase and has since borne approximately $1 billion in losses from Motorola Mobility. Given Motorola Mobility’s current performance in the smartphone market, it is likely that Google will continue to invest in the company. In such a situation, how could the astute Google simply be a passive spectator throwing money into the venture? The so-called non-interference is merely rhetoric for the outside world and media, meant only to be heard.

Therefore, when evaluating Motorola Mobility, one must consider Google. Evaluating Motorola Mobility in isolation no longer holds much significance. As the saying goes, "The mantis stalks the cicada, unaware of the oriole behind." Every move made by Motorola Mobility represents a shift in Google's Android strategy.

IDC's latest global smartphone statistics report shows that in the previous third quarter, Google Android's market share reached about 80%, far surpassing other competitors. If nothing unexpected happens in the future, Google Android's lead will be difficult to challenge. So why does Motorola still launch the extremely low-priced Moto G based on Google's native Android applications?

In the same IDC report, Microsoft's Windows Phone achieved a year-over-year sales growth of 156% globally in the third quarter, with a staggering 367% growth in the U.S. market. This propelled Windows Phone to fourth place in the U.S. smartphone market, surpassing Motorola. A major contributor to this growth was Nokia's low-cost Lumia phones. For example, in the U.S. market, Nokia shipped the most units of the low-cost Lumia 520. This indirectly validates Motorola CEO Dennis Woodside's statement during the Moto G launch event regarding the growth opportunities in the low-cost smartphone segment, including even the U.S. market where Apple dominates.

Theoretically, given its market share, Google should not worry about Microsoft's Windows Phone impact. However, the functional phone market retained by Nokia after being acquired by Microsoft is something Google cannot ignore. Although Nokia's overall mobile business declined last quarter, they still shipped 55.8 million feature phones, undoubtedly forming the foundation for future Windows Phone users. To encourage more functional phone users to switch to smartphones running Android, price becomes a critical factor.

IDC's statistics show that in the previous third quarter, the global smartphone ASP (average selling price) continued to decline, dropping by 12.5% year-over-year to $317. In contrast, Nokia's Lumia smartphones had an ASP of just €143 ($193), which is about 60% of the global smartphone ASP. This clearly demonstrates how Microsoft's Windows Phone strategy of capturing emerging markets through low prices is being implemented via Nokia's Lumia phones.

We must not underestimate Microsoft's capabilities. Recently, there have been reports that Microsoft plans to spend $450 million on marketing during the upcoming shopping season to achieve a target of selling 16 million Windows tablets. This highlights their determination to compete in the mobile internet (smartphones and tablets) system platform market. It is also possible that Microsoft may increase investment in the smartphone market, even allowing Nokia to burn money post-acquisition, given Microsoft's revenue and profit growth last quarter, indicating they are fully capable of doing so.

Aware of the strong pull and growth potential of price-sensitive emerging markets and the opportunities they present to competitors, Motorola launched the Moto G at a mere $179. This price is competitive with Lumia, ensuring that when Nokia and other feature phone users transition to smartphones primarily based on price considerations, Google Android does not want to give Microsoft's Windows Phone another opportunity to break through via pricing.

Of course, achieving this goal solely through Motorola's efforts would be insufficient. Google understands this well. Thus, the Moto G serves more as a symbolic device, meaning Google hopes to lower the ASP of Android, and even the entire smartphone market, by a price range through the Moto G, thereby accelerating Android's adoption.

Previously, analysts suggested that the reason Google continues to fund Motorola's operations is to gradually reduce the overall price of smartphones through Motorola's devices, encouraging more users to adopt smartphones. Google's profit model and logic are quite straightforward: the more users who use Android phones, the more they will go online, the more they will use Google services, and the more money Google will make through these services. Whether Motorola phones themselves can make a profit is secondary to Google.

Besides accelerating the adoption of Android smartphones and slowing down Microsoft's transition of feature phone users (leveraging Nokia's feature phone users), the release of the Moto G serves other purposes, namely hoping to close the window of opportunity for other system platforms to enter the smartphone market.

It is widely known that since the beginning of the year, open-source mobile operating systems like Firefox OS, Sailfish OS, and Ubuntu OS for Phones have gained momentum, though largely more talk than action. However, because these open-source system-based smartphones are positioned in the low-price entry-level smartphone market, they have caught Google's attention. Among them, Samsung and Intel's collaboration on the Tizen system stands out.

Recently, Samsung showcased the Tizen 3.0 operating system at the Tizen Developer Summit, jointly with Intel. The biggest highlight was that the Tizen operating system supports various sizes and different screen dimensions of devices, with varying specifications across different size ranges. The system supports smooth operation on devices with as little as 256MB of RAM. This means that Tizen-based smartphones are also targeted at developing countries such as India and other Asian and African markets, which are also areas where Google Android aims for rapid growth and fierce competition.

Given Samsung's dominant position in the smartphone market, even if they continue using Android in the future, adopting Tizen in the low-end market could inevitably affect the further spread of Android. Therefore, Google preemptively set a price barrier with the low-cost Moto G before these open-source system-based low-end smartphones officially hit the market, increasing the difficulty for such smartphones to enter the market and putting pressure on their profitability. Ultimately, the goal is to ensure Android's dominance in the low-end market.

Lastly, it is important to note that Moto G is not merely setting a price benchmark for Android manufacturers but also striving for a balanced ratio of price, performance, and experience in terms of cost-effectiveness. According to relevant tests, the Moto G performs better than Samsung's Galaxy S4 in some smartphone applications. More accurately, Google is establishing an image of high cost-effectiveness in the low-end market through Motorola's Moto G, lowering the smartphone market's selling price on the basis of cost-effectiveness.

In summary, I believe the core purpose of launching the Motorola Moto G is for Google to lower the ASP of the entire smartphone market through Android-based phones, accelerating the adoption of Android, especially in emerging markets, while slowing down Microsoft Windows Phone's penetration into the low-end market and raising the threshold for new systems entering and competing in the low-end market. This approach is both offensive and defensive.
