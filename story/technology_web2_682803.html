Art investment: a dazzling array of options can be quite confusing

by anonymous on 2012-02-29 09:32:31

Art Investment: A Bewildering Array of Flowers Gradually Attracting Attention □ Wu Xue'an/Article

People often say "prosperous times for collecting." [Calligraphy and Painting Wholesale]. Whenever inflation expectations are strong, hot money flows, and the prospects for the stock market and real estate market are unclear, the calligraphy and painting collection market will continue to see sky-high auction items, and the group participating in calligraphy and painting collections is also expanding. However, under the backdrop of excessive liquidity and enhanced inflation expectations, the speculative atmosphere in the calligraphy and painting collection market is intense, and it's inevitable that some people will take advantage of the chaos to "inflate" the bubble. When the attributes of calligraphy and painting collections are overshadowed by investment attributes, whether one can remain rational undoubtedly tests the wisdom of investors.

The Chinese once had the saying "culture does not talk about profit." And under the context where the cultural industry has been elevated to a pillar industry of the national economy, culture and capital have become unprecedentedly "close." Cultural products, chased by capital, have become increasingly valuable. Artwork auctions frequently exceed hundreds of millions, box office revenues have broken through the 10 billion yuan mark, and the cultural sector has become a new favorite in the stock market. Borrowing Zhang Yimou's words: "The Chinese culture bed is particularly warm." However, due to the inherent "profit-seeking" nature of capital, while bringing prosperity to art investment, it also brings concerns.

Sky-High Art Auction Items Appear Suddenly

With the global economic recovery and ample liquidity, this year, the hammer sounds of various auction houses and the sky-high art auction prices that keep setting new records have repeatedly caught people's attention. Especially after a brief rest following the financial crisis, art investment mainly focused on Chinese calligraphy and painting has once again shone brilliantly, with the market full of confidence.

Looking back at the "investment" market of 2010, there was more of an impression of "speculating on calligraphy first," and under the background of rising CPI and strong investment desire of private capital, the uncertainty in the real estate market and others led many capitals to choose alternative investments. In previous years, an art piece being auctioned for over ten million yuan would already be considered news. But in 2010, Chinese art prices soared comprehensively, with artworks priced in the tens of millions becoming commonplace, and individual works frequently surpassing hundreds of millions in auction price, marking the entry into the boiling "hundred-million era."

As the Asian auction market's weathervane, Sotheby's struck the first gavel of Hong Kong's spring auction on April 3, 2010, with a total transaction amount nearing HK$2 billion, refreshing Hong Kong Sotheby's highest single-season total transaction record. On May 18, China Guardian Spring Auction achieved a total transaction amount of RMB 2.128 billion, creating the highest single-season auction record in mainland China. On June 5, 2010, Beijing Poly's fifth anniversary spring auction had a total transaction amount exceeding RMB 3.3 billion, creating the highest single-season auction record for Chinese art. Among these, Huang Tingjian's "Dizhu Ming" from the Song Dynasty, owned by a Japanese collector, was sold for RMB 390 million, plus a 12% commission, reaching a total transaction price of RMB 436.8 million, far surpassing the world record of RMB 230 million set five years ago for the "Yuan Blue-and-White Gui Gu Descends the Mountain Large Vase" in London, UK, instantly stirring up society's wealth nerve.

Before people could calm down from their astonishment at the sky-high price of "Dizhu Ming", one hundred-million-yuan record after another followed quickly: Zhang Daqian's silk painting "Ai Hen Hu" was sold for RMB 100.8 million, setting a new record for contemporary Chinese calligraphy and painting; the famous Northern Song dynasty guqin "Songshi Jian Yi" started bidding at RMB 16 million and finally sold for RMB 136.64 million, breaking the world instrument auction record... According to incomplete statistics, there were more than a dozen art pieces that exceeded RMB 100 million in value throughout the year.

At the end of last year, during the Beijing Poly auction, Ming dynasty Wu Bin's "Eighteen Arhats Picture" was estimated at RMB 20 million but sold for RMB 169 million, increasing eight times over its estimate. This painting was purchased 18 years ago by the Ullens couple in New York for $620,000, and its price increased by 40 times in 18 years.

Not only did individual art pieces set new highs, but the entire art market's transaction amount also "ran fast." The total transaction amount of large auction companies such as China Guardian and Beijing Poly in 2010 reached RMB 11.604 billion, nearly double the same period of the previous year. By the end of the year, autumn auctions in various places were still in full swing, with Poly alone achieving a total transaction amount of RMB 5.28 billion, setting a single-season auction record for Chinese art.

In the "hard to find a painting even if gold is easy to get" art collection market, due to the lack of an authoritative pricing mechanism, it is easily manipulated and speculated, making it a cultivatable "black land." At the Beijing Jia De autumn auction, Wang Xizhi's "Ping An Tie" copy was sold for RMB 308 million. Industry insiders joked that Chinese art investment has truly entered the "hundred-million era." The annual global art market monitoring report by the European Art Foundation shows: China's art auction transaction amount has quintupled in three years, ranking third in the world. According to statistics from the China Auction Association, China's total art auction amount was RMB 2.5 billion in 2003, but it broke through RMB 26.8 billion in 2009.

Hidden Concerns Behind Art Speculation

Where does the driving force come from for art auction items to repeatedly set sky-high prices? The reason "a large amount of funds withdrawn from the real estate and stock markets entering the art market" has been recognized by all parties. In 2010, the state twice intervened to regulate the real estate market; the stock market remained in a consolidation and oscillation state, with the price center continuously declining. A large amount of real estate and stock speculation funds had to seek new ways to make money, and many capital tycoons moved their funds to collection accounts, turning art into another platform for speculation and investment after the real estate and stock markets.

The rise in art prices often occurs during periods of high economic growth and high inflation. Due to an increasing amount of anti-inflation hedging funds entering the collection market, art as an asset allocation will become a consensus among investors. With ample funds chasing scarce items, prices naturally keep setting new highs. In the past, ancient paintings were not valuable, or even less valuable than contemporary calligraphy and painting works. However, the scarcity of art is the key determinant of value. Chinese ancient calligraphy and painting, after thousands of years of cultural accumulation and disasters, have limited quantities surviving, and their scarcity and uniqueness determine that they should have reasonable values. Many works that were lost overseas early on are now flowing back, and their values begin to be reflected in circulation, which is also a kind of cultural recognition and value return.

The biggest risk in investing in Chinese calligraphy and painting is purchasing fakes. Under the background of strong investment desires and accumulated idle funds, the temptation and bewilderment brought by frenzied speculation leading to explosive wealth effects are very strong, drawing more and more investors into it. However, the art market has always been unpredictable. Once you buy a fake or a work with low artistic value, getting stuck at a high position and waiting for someone to take over is unavoidable. In the collection market, every dynasty of Chinese calligraphy and painting has had fine imitations and reproductions. Now, ancient Tang, Song, and Ming dynasty paintings that are auctioned for very high prices, sometimes reaching hundreds of millions, have survived for hundreds or even thousands of years. Relying on simple preservation conditions in the civilian sector, having "true, fine, and new" "high-quality" works sometimes seems incredible. Especially under the drive of interests, using "high-fidelity calligraphy and painting prints passing as genuine" as a forgery method is quite common in the market, with many prints flooding into the collection market. For example, in Beijing, there was a lawsuit where "a collector sued a certain auction company for selling calligraphy and painting prints passing as genuine fraudulently." Some unscrupulous calligraphy and painting merchants collude with auction houses, splitting profits after selling forged works, and the international convention of auction houses not guaranteeing authenticity means that buyers who purchase fakes can only swallow their losses silently.

Famous collector Ma Weidu once said a very insightful sentence: Art as consumption is the top consumer good in life, and as an investment, it is also a top-risk investment product. The calligraphy and painting collection market is chaotic and filled with fish eyes masquerading as pearls. This small-market driven by huge interests requires professional experience. Due to severe information asymmetry, the participants' eye for detail, scholarly cultivation, and other comprehensive abilities will determine whether what they buy is waste paper or treasure. Because of cultural background issues, overseas funds lack recognition of the Chinese calligraphy and painting market, so this market mainly gathers domestic funds.

In the past, the mainstream funds in the art market mainly came from Beijing, Shanghai, Shanxi, and Hong Kong-Macau. In recent years, the proportion of Jiangsu-Zhejiang private funds investing in art has been increasing. Among them, there are both prescient real estate speculators "changing fields" rushing in and huge funds from Shanxi coal bosses entering the market. These investment and collection funds are all aimed at appreciation, and most investors have only a superficial understanding of calligraphy and painting collection knowledge, bringing a certain degree of blindness and profit-seeking. Therefore, how to open up more investment channels, guide idle funds towards more reasonable investment directions, and calmly face the "overheating" of the art market is what every rational investor should actively consider.

As the art transaction records are constantly refreshed, various voices are swirling within the market, including the cheers of investors and the doubts of observers. Although many collectors believe that China's rapid economic development has boosted the art market, revealing its due value; some industry insiders also think that whether sky-high-priced artworks really sell is questionable, and the art market will accumulate more and more bubbles. Over time, the inflated prices will inevitably fall back. Currently, the two viewpoints are opposing each other fiercely, and who is right or wrong will eventually be answered correctly by the future market.
