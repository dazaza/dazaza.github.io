How to get things done, and better?

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 11:57:50

Editor's note: The author of this article is Sam Altman, the original title is "How Things Get Done".

I've heard many methods about how things get done and done well, and I am personally very interested in this topic. Among all these methods, I think the best one is: focus + networking. This was first told to Paul Graham by Charlie Rose, and then he passed it on to me.

This method is extremely accurate. Personally, I have a lot of things I want to do, but for some reason, they never make it onto my to-do list. Ironically, the items that I do schedule always get completed quite well. A small startup has the capacity to work on three things at the same time, and these are usually the things its CEO focuses on. Of course, for a startup with limited time and energy, anything not ranked as the first or second priority is hard to accomplish.

Most early-stage entrepreneurs don't do well when it comes to focusing; they don't let their companies focus on just one or two, or even three things. They like chasing any flashy new ideas. This isn't surprising. Entrepreneurs generally prefer trying new things rather than maintaining strict focus. But as you know, ruthless focus and rejecting distractions are very important. Deciding to take on a project because it seems like it won't take much time is often rash. The result is either wasting a lot of time or realizing after completion that it wasn't worth the effort. Founders know what they should do, but rarely know what they shouldn't do.

The "YC version" of focus is "write good code and talk to users." Startups usually don't have many people or things to focus on, and most things are actually a waste of time. For whatever reason, many founders spend their time on unimportant matters — worrying about the company's organizational structure, making the accounts look as good as possible, etc. All these actions pretending to run a company actually go against what a real startup should be doing. The best startups will spend hours discussing their products, how to move forward, how to become faster, and get excited about creating new features according to user needs. The worst companies endlessly discuss other things... over and over again.

In terms of networking, most people find it difficult to build an excellent product alone because there are too many issues to address. Successful startups usually realize that hiring is their biggest problem — to "recruit and promote excellent people," while also maintaining good relationships with those they work with.

I've been trying hard to find talented people. Besides being interesting and fun, the most important thing is their ability to complete tasks beautifully. These are the people I want to hire, and I believe many others feel the same way. I also think working with your friends is a good solution. Smart people always want to collaborate with other smart people. This certainly isn't nepotism. The most successful hiring cases I've heard about or witnessed usually involve hiring friends of friends.

Finally, spending time on networking may seem to conflict with focus, but this is an exception, and the process can be quite enjoyable. When you combine extreme focus with a great team, wait and see, miracles will happen.
