Baidu

by qiaocui8317 on 2012-02-16 22:57:13

Baidu, everyone on Qian Street knows. Who is Lin Shu? Perhaps many know him -- he's Lin Qinan, a key translator for the Commercial Press’s "Lin-translated novels," the first person in modern times to translate European and American literature into classical Chinese. He translated Shakespeare's play stories into "Yinbian YanYu," introducing the story of La Dame aux Camélias to Chinese literati for the first time — oh, it turns out Duan娘 had traveled abroad! Baidu and Lin Shu, these two were born in different times, and their deaths are likely unrelated. Searching for Lin Shu on Baidu should give you a brief introduction to his life or references to related reviews. But Lin Shu would probably not find any trace of Baidu, right? What relationship could there be between an individual and a search engine? Could it be that Mr. Li, the CEO of Baidu, has some connection with Lin Shu? Stretching the imagination, Li and Lin could indeed be connected— at least, they both have "wood" radicals in their names, both relate to trees that grow without moving, and the plum tree can form a forest of plum trees. But this is not my reason for writing about them.

On March 18, 1919, the newspaper "Gong Xing Bao," run by the Anhui clique of Beiyang warlords under Duan Qirui and Ji Shuzhen, published an open letter titled "To Cai Heqing." The letter begins respectfully: "Mr. Heqing Tai Shi, ... You admire new learning, yet still think of those who uphold old customs, standing alone in defense of traditional teachings, like a thread barely holding together, hoping you will protect and cherish them." After the flattery comes the sharp criticism: "Recently, rumors have been spreading... Some people, in their zeal, have gone too far. They do not understand that to save the world, one must speak words that others can say, and blunt truths must be delivered to those who can bear them. If one goes against convention and speaks nonsense, then it is like serving poisoned porridge, with dead rats nearby; or lighting fires in the dark, with insects dying beneath."

It also contains such harsh accusations: "Since ancient times, when people's hearts have become blind, it has never been beyond redemption. Now, with strange and extravagant ideas being spread, causing unrest among the masses, young people half-educated are easily swayed, and there is no one who does not rush to support them, while the fate of Western nations hangs by a thread."

Finally, it ends with veiled threats: "If you use your position and power to promote strange and eccentric teachings... This is what I warn you against. The country is drowning, the blessings of the regional lords are near, and yet you allow such things as North-South disputes." Even the seemingly sincere conclusion feels strange: "You are respected by southern scholars, and should weep and cry to help bring peace, allowing the people to rest; instead, with your pure and upright character, you let critics attack freely. I deeply regret this for you. After this letter, you need not show it to anyone, but I hope you consider the direction of the citizens."

This sender of the letter was none other than Cai Yuanpei, then president of Peking University, and the writer was Lin Shu himself, known as Lin Qinan. He was furious at the promotion of vernacular Chinese writing by Chen Duxiu and Hu Shi, who taught at Peking University, seeing their call to change the tone of writing as a betrayal of ancestral rules. With his gaze as sharp as electricity, he feared that the vernacular would rise, moral norms would collapse, and the fate of the nation would face calamity. In response, he fiercely defended tradition. Shortly before this letter, Lin Shu also published allegorical works, "Jing Shi" and "Yao Meng," in the Shanghai-based "Gu Wen Bao," portraying Chen, Hu, and Qian as demons and imagining Peking University as an "Oral Language Academy" — with a wrong couplet hanging on its gate: "Vernacular language is divine, Dream of the Red Chamber and Water Margin are unimaginable masterpieces; Modern essays are disgusting, Ouyang Xiu and Han Yu are nothing compared to this." In the story, several large ghosts strut around proudly, eventually provoking the wrath of Jing Shi and the Asura King. The former captures and eats them, turning them into small pieces with a fragrant smell. Contemporary observers and later researchers believed that Lin Shu's actions hinted at urging the Northern government to use its authority to crush the new cultural movement.

This letter was carefully crafted: Lin and Cai were acquainted, yet this public letter was neither registered nor personally delivered to Cai's residence but directly published in a government-controlled newspaper—this "Gong Xing Bao" held a weight comparable to today's "People's Daily" or "Guangming Daily." It created the impression of honest advice while placing the intimidating force of the state before the reader—this is official content, after all! First point; secondly, the new cultural movement was led by professors like Chen, Hu, and Qian from Peking University, yet this letter was not addressed to any of them individually but rather to Cai Yuanpei, their superior, clearly identifying him as the leader of their unruly behavior, challenging his ability to "tolerate inclusively." Thirdly, it stirred up concerns about societal order and political stability: the established laws and morals of ancestors are crucial to social harmony, and the radical views of these "base and incompetent scholars" are akin to poisonous gruel and hidden fires, corrupting the youth and misleading the common people. Moreover, amidst the instability of north-south tensions and regional conflicts, their strange and eccentric teachings only added fuel to the fire, threatening to catalyze another American Civil War, endangering the lives of the people!

How Cai Yuanpei responded is left aside for now, but the history of the new literary movement records the outcome of this debate: Lin Shu's plans failed. However, history also shows that the pioneers of the new cultural movement and their successors did not achieve complete victory either.

In mid-September 2008, Baidu, reputedly the largest Chinese search engine, accepted 1.2 million RMB to help Sanlu Group conceal information about the melamine scandal. When this was revealed, it faced fierce criticism online, and Baidu temporarily became "Badu." Subsequently, due to its ranking system promoting a series of fraudsters to the top, it was exposed by a certain well-known television media much larger than the "largest global" claim. It is said that Mr. Li, the CEO of Baidu, fell into the "professional low point" of his life—though without poisoning children, it did not fall into the vulgar valley described by Eastern poets with fine wine and beautiful women. At this time, a highly dramatic event occurred. On November 15, a news item appeared in Baidu News: "Google Exposed by CCTV." Then, while in the "low point," Li Yanhong sent out an open letter saying that as industry leaders, they inevitably face more cruel—even vicious—market environments. By shifting blame onto the "harmonious society" environment of a declining era, netizens commented: "From Baidu's response after the exposure, we only feel evasion of responsibility. What seemed like an apology letter turned out to be so justified. Compared to the excuses given by the government, which often involves temporary layoffs and fake forgetfulness, Baidu's performance is not outstanding. ... It's dragging Google into the mix to shift the blame."

Naturally, Google, which was often manipulated by certain forces and used awkwardly, was reported by users multiple times. On June 18, 2009, the same television station exposed Google under the guise of spreading pornographic and vulgar content. In front of the television-watching populace, entering the word "son" into Google's search menu magically produced results like "the inappropriate relationship between son and father-in-law"—very yellow and violent! The entire nation was shocked: how could it hide such filth? Two days later, a home computer engineer debunked the magic trick: Google's keyword suggestions are automatically generated based on the most popular recent keywords. Someone exploited this by searching for pornographic terms en masse on Google a week before the TV program aired—was this a conspiracy?—trapping Google. The engineer presented Google's search trend charts and third-party statistics, comparing the data sources for the search terms "weather forecast" and "Japanese pornography," concluding:

1. Someone deliberately searched for pornographic terms on Google, increasing daily searches by 5950%, with monthly totals rising thousands of times compared to the previous month.

2. All these searches originated 100% from Beijing.

3. These search volumes rose almost linearly, whereas actual sudden search spikes should follow a normal distribution and be sporadic, meaning this was intentional.

Beijing residents, whose hormones surged similarly a week earlier, angrily questioned: who did this? Further investigation revealed that "illegal relationships" is such a legally precise term, strangely formal yet colloquial! If I were a petty official in an ideal situation seeking to flex my tension-inducing powers, I'd aim straight for "nakedness," "human art," or even "sex" with direct and effective visual appeal—who would indulge so obediently in lustful thoughts, shrinking back timidly? Was this the act of overworked legal professionals seeking relaxation, or bored office clerks with altered interests? Although the engineer couldn't provide exact data pinpointing the location within Beijing—a specific floor of a building on a certain street—such as whether it was the 10th floor of the Southern Ideal Building on the eastern section of North Fourth Ring Road—it was widely speculated online that this was undoubtedly the work of Baidu, a member of the Internet Elegant Organization of the People's Republic of China. Believe it or not, as you wish! I remain firmly committed to not spreading or believing rumors.

This is because, facing matters with ulterior motives, I have only one reason for making my decision. The great writers Lin Shu hoped for—or even the Asura King—are unreliable, just as a bestseller once showed: "Authority is unreliable," and governments are unreliable too. As Natsume Sōseki wrote in his essay "My Freedom": "Even if countries speak grandly and formally, morality and conscience are another matter entirely. Fraud, deception, and manipulation—anything goes." Therefore, whoever regards the state—as represented by its self-proclaimed government—as a standard or wraps it in a package or label, suffers from low or even non-existent morality, while individual morality always surpasses them. They have their own inherent logic and will, and cannot be moved or made to smile by the instigation or stimulation of a single person or event. Lin Shu's letter did not stir the latent abilities of the Beiyang Duan government, but seven years later, on the same day, the Duan government unexpectedly fired upon protesting students without warning. Repairing Baidu's reputation, some TV channels silently spoke about Google, likely not because of "improper relationships between sons and fathers-in-law." Whether Baidu played a role similar to Lin Shu is meaningless.

I have only one reason: beneath Lin Shu's loyal and steadfast belief in the value of classical Chinese over the translated "Yinbian YanYu," lies his personal moral stance, which makes me continue to enjoy reading his works and thinking about him. And I have only one reason: to immediately banish from my internet life the unrighteous money-makers of Baidu, who do wicked deeds and proclaim themselves as leaders. Since September 16, 2008, when I learned of its heartless actions for the Sanlu Group, I have regarded it as something ostentatiously hypocritical, immediately ceasing to use Baidu and continuing to avoid it, never contributing another positive click to any of its rankings.

Written in cyberspace on the afternoon of July 1, late 2009.
