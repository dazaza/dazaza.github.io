Full experience of Lumia 1020, Nokia's strongest one is none other than you.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 17:12:43

Not long after the official release of Lumia 1020, foreign media outlets have gradually got their hands on the actual device, and reviews have been subsequently published. For a smartphone, there's not much to criticize about the Lumia 1020; its overall strength is worthy of Nokia's strongest title. As a camera, the Lumia 1020 is also fully qualified. Alright, let’s take a look at The Verge's full review.

A Shape That Still Captures Hearts

If we say that Lumia 925 is the delicate lady among Nokia's Lumia flagships, then Lumia 1020 is a robust man. Nokia has no pursuit for thinness like Huawei or Vivo, but its bulky size does not hinder its beauty.

The unibody polycarbonate design used since the Nokia N9 remains stylish even today. Just from the photos, you can feel the quality of the Lumia 1020. Compared to Lumia 920, the Lumia 1020 is slightly lighter and thinner, but we can basically consider Lumia 1020 as an enhanced camera version of Lumia 920.

The only flaw might be the somewhat protruding camera lens on the back, which creates an angle when the phone is placed on a table due to the lens sticking out. And when holding the phone, the uneven back may make the grip feel a bit strange.

The 4.5-inch screen with a resolution of 1280 x 768 displays well, reaching a PPI of 334. Thanks to ClearBlack display technology, the Lumia 1020 can still offer good visibility in sunlight, as seen from the cover image. This screen's precision may not match the current crop of 1080P Android devices, but the Lumia 1020's screen is crisp, with accurate color reproduction. Its only drawback might be that the whites are slightly dark and some gray tones are off. Overall, the Lumia 1020's screen won't disappoint you.

Unprecedented Camera

The impressive camera function of the Lumia 1020 amazed people at its launch. It can be said that, among current smartphones, the camera capability of the Lumia 1020 has no rival, even surpassing its predecessor, the 808. In terms of photography, The Verge gave the Lumia 1020 a perfect score of 10. Moreover, the Lumia 1020 can outperform most digital cameras in its price range of $299. According to the reviewer, he wanted to find flaws in the sample photos taken by the Lumia 1020 but failed, as if the Finns had cast a magic spell on the phone.

The 41 million-pixel lens of the Lumia matches the 808, but its 1/1.5-inch sensor is smaller than the 808's 1/1.2-inch sensor. Due to Nokia's Superpixel technology (different from HTC's Ultrapixel) and oversampling algorithm, the circular "image circle" can freely adjust between 4:3 and 16:9 ratios according to user needs, producing photos with resolutions of 7152 x 5368 (38 million) and 7728 x 4354 (34 million), respectively. Then, through an algorithm that merges seven pixels into one, it becomes a high-quality 5-megapixel photo.

Another advantage of high pixel count is the ability to perform 6x optical zoom. Unlike digital zoom, optical zoom does not blur the image and can preserve image details well, as demonstrated by the needle-in-a-haystack demonstration at the launch event.

The new Nokia camera software Pro Cam comes with the new lens. Here, you can adjust white balance, zoom focus, ISO sensitivity (up to 3200), shutter speed settings (from 1/16000 seconds to 4 seconds), etc. Foreign media have given this software positive reviews except for some delays and lag.

It's worth mentioning the $79 Camera Grip, an interesting accessory. The Camera Grip also features a polycarbonate material design but looks more rugged. It includes a two-stage shutter button, universal tripod socket, and a 1020mAh battery. Again, it receives positive reviews, at least making the Lumia 1020 look and operate more like a camera.

Below is a series of sample photos, including comparisons with the iPhone 5, Lumia 920, and Nokia 808.

Although I haven’t personally handled it, based on the sample images, the camera performance of the Lumia 1020 is indeed excellent, capable of handling both daytime and nighttime scenes. It excels in detail representation and image purity, though there seems to be some noise in the night sky during nighttime shots, but these minor flaws do not overshadow its strengths.

As for video recording, three key advantages stand out: oversampling technology ensures clean and accurate images, excellent stabilization, and superior microphone audio quality. To be fair, Lumia 1020's advantage in video recording isn't as significant as in photo taking. Xenon flash continues to receive praise.

In short, there's nothing to criticize about the camera on the Lumia 1020; foreign media have showered it with praises. The iPhone 5, Lumia 920, and Nokia 808 are all left behind.

Soft Software and Hard Hardware

What happens after taking photos with the Lumia 1020? Is it just for personal appreciation? Most people would choose to upload and share them, and this is where the Lumia 1020's awkwardness comes in—not just for the Lumia 1020, but across the entire Windows Phone lineup. However, the powerful camera capability amplifies this awkwardness: the number and quality of social photo sharing applications on WP8 are not satisfactory.

Lumia 1020 can help many people get rid of digital cameras and SD cards, and WiFi makes uploading photos fast, but there's always something missing.

The most popular Instagram hasn't landed on the Windows Phone 8 platform, nor have Google+ and Flickr. Foursquare, Evernote, Facebook, and Twitter are available, but their experience doesn't match iOS and Android. However, there is some good news—Path, Vine, and Hipstamatic apps are coming, and so might Flickr.

There's no denying that Outlook / Office / SkyDrive have unique appeal, but the issue of third-party applications has been over-discussed. Even with excellent hardware and a brilliant camera, the ecosystem gap leaves Nokia powerless.

Hardware-wise, there's not much to say—apart from the memory increase to 2GB, there's not much difference compared to the Lumia 920 and 925. Fortunately, WP8's hardware requirements aren't high, so there's no need to worry about the smooth operation of the Lumia 1020. The only noticeable lag or delay is in the previously mentioned Pro Cam, where there was even an instance of system freeze.

The phone's battery life seems decent; the 1.5GHz Snapdragon S4 dual-core processor doesn't consume too much power. Under conditions where frequent photography isn't necessary, the 2000mAh battery can last the reviewer a day to a day and a half, but frequent photography drains the battery quickly, and the phone heats up. Don't forget, the Lumia 1020 also has the Camera Grip nearby, which can serve as a small external power source.

Nokia's traditionally tough panel glass has been upgraded this time, capable of breaking walnuts, resisting bricks, and stopping bullets effortlessly. For the Windows Phone 8 platform, the Lumia 1020 has already achieved the best possible at this moment.

Finally, The Verge gave the Lumia 1020 a respectable score of 8.1. For comparison, the iPhone 5 scored 8.7, HTC One scored 8.3, Samsung Galaxy S4 scored 8, and Lumia 920 scored 7.9. Below are the scores for each sub-item. The low score of 5 for the ecosystem dragged the overall score down, but this isn't Nokia's fault.

In summary, the Lumia 1020 is a well-rounded phone with distinct strengths. If you have high requirements for a camera, you can consider purchasing it. Even if you don't value the camera function as much, it is still an excellent Windows Phone 8 smartphone.
