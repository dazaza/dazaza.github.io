Experts from the Nanjing Armed Police Hospital's Anorectal Center introduced that internal hemorrhoids grow above the dentate line and are formed by the expansion and tortuosity of the submucosal internal hemorrhoid venous plexus, creating a soft vein mass known as internal hemorrhoids. Another perspective is that internal hemorrhoids result from the pathological enlargement and displacement of anal cushions. Internal hemorrhoids protruding outside the anus are clamped by the sphincter muscle, causing obstructed venous return while arterial blood continues to flow in, enlarging the volume of the hemorrhoidal mass until the artery vessel is compressed shut, forming a thrombus, leading to hardened, painful hemorrhoids that are difficult to retract into the anus.

Symptoms of internal hemorrhoids:

1. Bleeding during or after defecation, bright red in color. Sometimes there is a small amount of blood on the surface of the stool or it stains the toilet paper red, sometimes dripping or spurting blood. Due to feces rubbing against the mucosa, or excessive force during defecation, increased pressure within the vessels causes the dilated veins to rupture, resulting in jet-like bleeding during defecation. If there is long-term repeated bleeding or multiple large bleedings, anemia may occur.

2. Protrusion: As the size of the hemorrhoidal mass increases, it is squeezed by feces during defecation, gradually separating from the muscular layer and protruding out of the anus. Sometimes one or two hemorrhoidal masses protrude simultaneously, sometimes all the hemorrhoidal masses along with the rectal mucosa protrude together. Initially, only during defecation do they protrude, then they can spontaneously reposition themselves. In more severe cases, manual repositioning or bed rest is required for repositioning. In even more serious cases, not only during defecation but also when exerting force, walking, coughing, sneezing, or squatting, protrusion may occur. The protruded hemorrhoidal masses are extremely prone to infection, often causing inflammation, swelling, pain, and incarceration, making repositioning difficult.

3. Pain: Simple internal hemorrhoids generally cause no pain, sometimes only a feeling of heaviness or difficulty in defecation in the anal area. If inflamed and swollen, a thrombus forms inside the hemorrhoid or it becomes incarcerated, causing pain; if not promptly repositioned after protrusion, the pain worsens; if incarceration occurs, ulceration and necrosis may happen, causing inflammation and swelling around the anus, leading to severe pain, making the patient uncomfortable sitting or lying down.

4. Secretion flow: Long-term stimulation of the rectal mucosa by the hemorrhoidal mass causes increased secretions; in late-stage internal hemorrhoids, due to the relaxation of the anal sphincter muscle, secretions often flow out of the anus. Mild cases see secretions during defecation, while severe cases have natural secretion flow without defecation, contaminating underwear, greatly inconveniencing the patient. During the protrusion of internal hemorrhoids, secretions increase.

5. Itching: Due to the stimulation of secretions or protruded hemorrhoidal masses, the anal area remains moist and unclean, leading to eczema and itching. Sometimes itching is caused by the reflex action of protruded internal hemorrhoids.

6. Internal hemorrhoid attacks: Internal hemorrhoids usually have mild symptoms and little pain. If there is constipation or diarrhea, or overexertion, the condition suddenly worsens, referred to as an internal hemorrhoid attack. During an internal hemorrhoid attack, the hemorrhoidal mass suddenly swells, protrudes, feels hot, painful, pulsates, and gives a sensation of being filled with a foreign object. Due to compression by dry feces, it easily ruptures and bleeds, causing tenesmus. The attack lasts 3-5 days, and if treated properly, the swelling gradually subsides, the thrombus is absorbed, and the hemorrhoidal mass softens and shrinks. Sometimes the swelling does not subside, due to infection, abscess formation, ulceration, or obstruction of blood circulation, the hemorrhoidal mass may necrose.

The main feature of internal hemorrhoids is bleeding. Experts from the Nanjing Armed Police Hospital's Anorectal Center introduce that internal hemorrhoid bleeding is quite severe but almost painless. Therefore, many people neglect it for a long time, worsening the condition and delaying treatment. In cases of dripping blood, some experience bleeding every time they defecate, others intermittently. When there is heavy fresh blood loss, hemostasis is difficult, often requiring continuous bleeding for two or three days, and in cases of large blood loss, some may become anemic.

Experts at the Nanjing Armed Police Hospital's Anorectal Center remind everyone that internal hemorrhoids are the most common disease among all types of hemorrhoids, mostly occurring on the right front, right back, and left side of the anus. Initially, the symptoms of internal hemorrhoids are not obvious and are only discovered during physical examinations. However, as the hemorrhoidal mass gradually increases in size, the symptoms will also gradually worsen. Therefore, once internal hemorrhoids are detected, treatment should be sought as early as possible to avoid delaying the condition.
