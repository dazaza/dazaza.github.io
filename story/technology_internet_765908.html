Apple is the biggest victim of the patent wars

by anonymous on 2013-08-29 16:30:35

According to the American tech blog TechHive, a Silicon Valley lawyer recently stated that over the past five years, Apple, HP, and Samsung have become the biggest targets for "patent trolls." However, recent legislative activities may weaken the destructive power of these patent trolls.

At the Hot Chips conference held at Stanford University on Tuesday, Michael Brody, Vice Chairman of Intellectual Property at the globally renowned law firm Winston & Strawn, said during his speech that in 2009, patents accounted for 5% of the total U.S. export value, approximately $89.8 billion. This is a huge business, and since the Industrial Revolution, the number of patent applications has been growing at an unprecedented rate.

Companies most frequently sued for patents

It is unsurprising that semiconductor and software companies file the most patents. Since 1990, the number of chip patents filed annually has increased from 15,000 to over 65,000, accounting for more than 25% of all patent applications each year. Moreover, about 125,000 software patents are filed annually.

This lucrative pie naturally attracts sharks.

What are patent trolls?

"Patent trolls," also known as "non-practicing entities" (NPEs), do not use their technology to produce products. Instead, they either develop their own technology and then patent it, or purchase patents from others and subsequently license them to others. The key point is that NPEs build their own patent portfolios and then obtain patent licensing fees from targeted companies through litigation. Many people claim that this practice has stifled the development of many startups in Silicon Valley.

Types of lawsuits brought by "patent trolls"

"Ultimately, patents equate to the right to sue others," Brody said, "Therefore, we see patents that could have created economic value now being used as a privilege to sue others and profit from it."

As a result, the largest tech companies often become the most frequent targets for litigation. Brody noted that over the past five years, Apple, HP, and Samsung have been the biggest targets for patent trolls. In 2012, over 4,200 companies or individuals were sued by troll companies.

It is unsurprising that large companies face frequent lawsuits. However, the majority (about 63%) of companies involved in lawsuits are small companies with annual revenues of less than $100 million. Small companies get caught up in one or two lawsuits each year. In contrast, in 2012 alone, large companies with revenues exceeding $50 billion were involved in an average of 7.3 lawsuits.

(Most lawsuits take 30 months to resolve, so many companies opt for out-of-court settlements. The average licensing fee for judicial judgments is $7.5 million, while the average licensing fee for out-of-court settlements is $29.75 million.)

Lawsuits against large companies typically demand higher damages. This means that the cost of resolving lawsuits, including damages and settlement costs, is $7.5 million for companies with revenues exceeding $1 billion, and $7.8 billion for companies with revenues exceeding $10 billion.

The cost of defending each lawsuit is: $800,000 for startups, and an average of $7.9 million for large companies with annual revenues exceeding $50 billion.

Harm caused by patent trolls

Brody pointed out that patent trolls pose a threat to both small and large companies. Although the defense cost for companies with annual revenues of less than $100 million is approximately $200,000, those startups lack sufficient revenue to sustain their defense efforts.

In total, the annual defense cost for lawsuits against NPEs is $1.04 million for companies with annual revenues of less than $1 billion, and approximately $57.67 million for companies with annual revenues exceeding $50 billion.

This has a significant impact on small companies, potentially leading to delayed product releases or redesigns. Brody noted that 13% of small companies, after dealing with NPE lawsuits, either shift to other business models or shut down altogether.

Why are patent trolls so keen on filing lawsuits? Because it's profitable. The success rate for an NPE lawsuit is 24.1%, achieved through settlements, victories, or appeals. The law firm Winston & Strawn estimates that the "net present value" of an NPE lawsuit is $800,000 — meaning that once a lawsuit is filed, the case itself is worth at least that amount.

"This is a good deal. That's why many people indulge in it," Brody said.

However, Brody also pointed out that this does not mean NPEs will continue to thrive indefinitely. Now, the cost of defending against patent lawsuits has decreased, and the U.S. Congress is targeting "patent trolls." Perhaps the biggest deterrent is that obtaining injunctions from courts to ban product sales, or obtaining orders from the International Trade Commission to ban product sales and imports, is very difficult.

Brody pointed out that the phenomenon of patent trolls is just a bubble that will eventually burst. However, this bubble is highly corrosive. Any patent lawsuit entails significant expenses. But before patent trolls are reined in or the patent system is improved, they will remain a potential threat.
