Childhood Memories - Fishing and Shrimp Catching

by zxcdm331dl on 2011-08-26 13:24:36

In childhood, living in the countryside, leading a carefree life. In leisure time, I liked to catch fish and shrimp. Now looking back, it was both a tempering of character and a small improvement in food.

In those days, in rural towns, there were no specialized shops selling fishing gear. Only hooks and fishing lines were sold at general merchandise stalls, suitable for professional fishermen, not for children's use. Children in the countryside had no money, nor would they buy them. We made our own very simple fishing gear using local materials. Different types of fish required different fishing gear.

Fishing gear consists of a combination of hook, rod, line, sinker, and float. Back then, we didn't care much about sophistication; simplicity and convenience were key.

Fish Hooks: For different types of fish, we made different hooks. For small fish, we used sewing needles to make hooks. The method was simple: use pliers to hold one end of the needle where the eye is, heat the tip over a kerosene lamp until red-hot, then use pointed pliers to bend the heated needle tip into a hook shape. After shaping, reheat the hook and dip it in oil to maintain its hardness. For larger fish, steel wire was used as material. Sharpen one end, heat it red-hot, then bend it into a larger hook.

Fish Rods: Fishing rods were all made from bamboo, which was abundant in the countryside. Select bamboo that is relatively straight and elastic. The length and thickness of the bamboo should be determined by the type of fish you are targeting. For smaller fish, a bamboo pole about 1.5 meters long and as thick as a thumb is sufficient and convenient to use. Soft bamboo is best because soft rods have good elasticity, providing a better feel when a fish bites, making it less likely for the fish to escape. For larger fish, thicker and longer bamboo is needed, referred to as "long rod, short line." However, these rods are stiffer, with less sensitive reactions and poorer feel. It’s also harder to cast and fish, especially in windy conditions. The length of the fishing rod also depends on the height and distance of the riverbank where you are fishing. Usually, various rods are prepared for different fishing spots.

Fish Line: Used to connect the fishing rod and the hook. One end of the line is threaded through the hook's eye, the other end is tied to the bamboo rod, ensuring convenience and firmness. The thickness of the line should be chosen based on the size of the fish targeted. Generally, cotton or silk thread used for sewing at home suffices.

Sinker: Made from soft tin or lead, the sinker serves to weigh down the hook. Normally, the weight of the hook plus bait is enough to sink it underwater, so a sinker isn’t always necessary.

Float: The float can be made from a one-inch piece of hollow wheat straw or from solid small bamboo cut into sections with a small hole drilled in the middle, resembling beads on an abacus. These can be tightly strung onto the fishing line and serve as floats. When fishing, adjust the position of the float according to the depth of the water.

Thus, a simple set of "handy" fishing gear was created, and next comes the art of fishing. Fishing is a common method of catching fish. Usually, I fished in the freshwater river behind the house or in nearby village ponds. In fishponds, only wild fish such as crucian carp, snakehead, inch-long minnows, and small fry could be caught. Cultivated fish like bighead carp, grass carp, and common carp were off-limits. If a cultivated fish was hooked, it had to be returned to the pond. Common catches were small fish, snakeheads, and crucian carp, with minnows being the most frequent.

Bait: Bait selection is important. Fish respond differently to bait depending on the species, season, and water body. Common baits include earthworms, rice grains, flies, maggots, small fish, and tadpoles. Different fish require different baits.

For minnows (we called them inch-length minnows), after school in the morning or afternoon, I fished in the river behind the house using maggots as bait. After my family harvested rice in autumn and winter, we kept enough for a year's supply, milled it into rice, and stored it in straw nests. Straw nests were homemade from rice straw, either round or square. Ours was square, large, as tall as me, storing enough rice for six to seven people for a year. To prevent the rice from breeding maggots, dried chili stalks were placed inside during construction. Nevertheless, white maggots still appeared the following year, which were perfect as fish bait.

For snakeheads, use large hooks and bait with small fish or tadpoles. Tadpole bait is hooked from the rear towards the head; small fish are hooked through the backbone. Once the bait is placed in the water and gently moved, it mimics live movement, easily attracting snakeheads.

For crucian carp, use small hooks and earthworms as bait. Crucian carp are very common and have delicious meat. When cooking, place minced meat inside the fish and add an egg, then steam it above a pot of boiling water for a memorable taste. Crucian carp reproduce quickly and adapt well to changing environments, thriving in both deep rivers and shallow ponds. They are excellent targets for fishing throughout the year, biting more or less depending on the season. Regardless of when you cast your line, you will always catch something.

Fishing with a spear: Spears can be found at blacksmith shops in town. Spear teeth vary in length from three to seven inches. Spears from blacksmiths are heavier, while homemade ones are lighter. Making a spear is straightforward: use five or seven steel wires, sharpen one end, bend them into prongs, tie them tightly with thin wire, and fix them to a bamboo pole. Spears are attached to bamboo poles 1.5 to 2 meters long, with a long line connected at the back. When spearing, throw the spear forward while holding the line to retrieve it afterward. Spearfishing requires waiting for fish to surface. Estimate the distance of the fish from the surface, calculate the exact moment the spear hits the fish, and how far it may dart. Aim slightly ahead of the fish's head and thrust forcefully. My success rate gradually improved from ten percent to around forty percent. Spearing snakeheads after they spawn was especially fun. Baby snakeheads resemble tadpoles and move in groups near the surface. Approach quietly, as any heavy step will cause the group to dive. Aim the spear at the group of baby snakeheads and thrust downward accurately and forcefully to hit the larger snakehead underneath, usually weighing about 1.5 pounds. Typically, a second snakehead could be speared from the same group the next day.

Net fishing: An adult once bought a silk net, cutting it into two sections as requested. The ends of the net were tied to a longer line with floats attached. If the net is dragged by fish, the float can still be retrieved to pull back the net. Floats are attached to the top of the net, and weights to the bottom, allowing the net to spread out and ensnare fish. The net can either float on the surface or sink deeper, depending on the configuration of floats and weights. Small minnows can be netted on the surface, while larger fish can sometimes be caught from the river bottom, occasionally even a three to four-ounce crucian carp. Unfortunately, the silk net broke after a few uses, making it too "expensive."

Weaving our own nets was more durable. Nets were woven into a basket shape and attached to long bamboo poles. This setup could catch fish, shrimp, and snails. Practically every day, we would cast the net several times, collecting a large bowl of snails, which need to be soaked in clear water for two days before eating to remove mud and sand. Therefore, we kept three basins of snails at home, allowing us to eat snail meat daily. Eating snail meat is labor-intensive, as each snail's tip must be clipped to facilitate sucking out the meat. At the time, we ate snail meat almost every noon. Snails from the river behind our house had soft tips and fresh meat. Snails from fish ponds had harder tips requiring pliers and tougher meat. Toronto Chinese supermarkets also sell snails, with harder tips called stone snails. Concerned about digestion issues, we avoided trying them.

Nest fishing: Creating a spawning nest to catch fish. Nest fishing is a unique fishing method used under special environmental conditions. I learned this technique from old farmers, and it was a playful trick during childhood, not considered sophisticated. I don't know if this method is used elsewhere. Making a fish nest is simple: an old straw shoe and two pieces of earthenware tiles (old-style semi-circular tiles) can create a fish nest. Farmers commonly wore straw shoes, especially in southern China, which were woven from rice straw and resembled cloth shoes. Collect worn-out straw shoes, plug the broken parts with some rice straw, insert two tiles, and secure them with a straw rope to form a fish nest. Place this nest near aquatic plants or tree roots in the pond, securing one end of the rope to a wooden stake on the shore. A maximum of nine nests can be placed, checking them morning and evening. During the spring fish spawning season, there is always a harvest. This method is highly seasonal, yielding little outside of the appropriate season.

Shrimp catching: The most enjoyable yet tedious task. At the water station (stone steps) by the river behind our house, sunny mornings saw people fetching water, washing rice, vegetables, and clothes in shifts. At noon, with no disturbances, it became quiet. With the sun shining directly, the shallow waters revealed moving fish and shrimp. This was the perfect time for shrimp catching. Use horsehair to make a live loop, attach it to a small bamboo stick, and hold the loop in your right hand while holding snail meat on a line in your left hand as bait. When the shrimp approaches the bait, carefully loop the shrimp from behind, lifting it up when the loop reaches the midsection. Each shrimp caught is lively and "large," with fifteen to sixteen captured in an hour, boiled into a bowlful for dinner.

I miss my childhood days, and even more, the beautiful environment of that time. With societal development, those days are gone forever.

Nowadays, rural industries have developed, economic benefits have increased, and families live in new houses. However, environmental pollution has worsened. The clear river where I used to catch fish and shrimp behind the house is now a foul-smelling swamp breeding flies and mosquitoes. Many ponds in nearby villages have been filled to build residential buildings and factories, transforming the land of fish and rice. People here grow up surrounded by toxins. Initially, they dug wells for drinking water, but over time, pesticide-contaminated water from rice fields seeped into the wells, making well water unsafe. Tap water was introduced, adding another expense for each household. Polluted river water is used to irrigate vegetables and other plants, containing varying levels of toxins. To combat pests, chemical pesticides and insecticides are applied, making long-term consumption of these plants equivalent to slow poisoning. Despite adapting to or developing resistance to the toxic environment, locals seem to live comfortably. Yet, strange diseases appear, affecting younger populations. Outsiders entering this area may experience gastrointestinal discomfort due to unfamiliarity with the environment.
