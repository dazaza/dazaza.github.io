2. Neuro-psychological Development

At 4 months: When supported in a sitting position, the baby can hold his head up steadily and turn it freely from side to side, thus expanding the range of vision. When placed on the stomach, he can lift his head and shoulders at a 90-degree angle and look around. The eyes can focus on objects and moving objects for a long time. When supported under the arms to stand, the baby can support part of his body weight. He will bring his hands together and play with them, often putting them in his mouth. The baby will often suck on his hands with great enjoyment, sometimes even putting his whole fist into his mouth. His engagement is so intense that it brings a smile to observers. When seeing a toy, the baby will express happiness, grab the toy, and hold it with a thumb-and-four-finger grip. The baby will show interest in surrounding events with immediate smiles. When spoken to, the baby will make "goo goo" and "ga ga" sounds. The baby can recognize his mother and familiar objects and begins to interact with others, especially enjoying being held upright by his parents to look around like an adult.

At 5 months: The baby's muscle functions gradually strengthen, allowing the head to be held up steadily and moved freely; the limbs move quite frequently and freely. The baby likes to grab adults' noses and when grabbing things, either shakes them or puts them in his mouth to suckle. The legs like to kick around, often kicking off the covering blanket. When held on the lap, the baby can stand slightly supported and bounce up and down. When unhappy, the baby may stiffen his body completely straight. For babies born in spring or summer, some may already be able to roll over at this stage. Therefore, if a child is left alone in bed without supervision, it can be very unsafe. Babies at this age sleep less than before, enjoy looking around while awake, and actively pay attention to things around them. They often laugh out loud, enjoy babbling, and begin to clearly show a willingness to interact with adults. They can distinguish between familiar people and strangers.

At 6 months: The baby may be pulled up from a sitting position and sit independently for a while when set down. Most babies will skillfully roll over, and when supported by both hands to stand, they can slightly bend their hips and knees, making bouncing movements. They can skillfully transfer objects from one hand to another, and the reaching motions increase significantly. Regardless of what is in front of them, they reach out to grab it, sometimes using both hands simultaneously, and also transfer objects from one hand to the other to play with. They purposefully move forward to grab things they want but cannot reach yet. Moreover, the baby begins to be able to use hand-eye coordination to eat small biscuits themselves and even feed cookies to their parents. When called by name, the baby quickly turns his head, showing a happy and focused expression. When excited, the baby can emit joyful cries and high-pitched squeals, and when crying, makes "m, m, m" sounds. At this time, the baby's emotions change very quickly; just moments ago, they were crying intensely, and suddenly they are laughing uncontrollably. The baby also starts to show clear signs of stranger anxiety, recognizing familiar people and smiling at them, but showing stranger anxiety towards unfamiliar people, knowing shyness. Upon hearing their name, they respond, such as laughing or turning their head, and they laugh heartily, showing strong initiative.

This period is crucial for establishing a close attachment relationship and trust in the surrounding world. A good attachment relationship promotes active exploration of the environment by the child. They are interested in various items around them, like touching and knocking on things, and placing anything in their hands in their mouths to taste its flavor and texture. This phase sees the emergence of food preferences, and any changes in food are met with highly sensitive reactions. There may be fear of new foods, reluctance to try new foods, and some babies who have been accustomed to breastfeeding may initially refuse when switched to cow's milk.

3. Nutrition and Feeding

Continue breastfeeding. However, after 4 months, the secretion of digestive enzymes becomes increasingly perfected, and to supplement the insufficient nutrients in milk, meet growth and development needs, exercise chewing functions, and prepare for weaning, auxiliary foods should be gradually added starting at 4 months, whether the baby is artificially fed, mixed fed, or breastfed. Auxiliary foods should be fed with a spoon, avoiding placing them in a bottle for sucking, allowing the baby to learn and get used to using a spoon, fully exercising chewing and swallowing functions. Wash your hands before feeding, put a bib or small towel on the baby, and prepare a damp small towel to clean dirty objects at any time. Feeding should be basically scheduled and quantified, have a fixed eating place, and finish feeding at once. Do not eat a little and then play for a while, and then eat later.

4. Care and Safety Protection

When entering the fourth month, the baby’s head can remain upright more stably, liking to be held upright, but the duration should not be too long, still requiring adults to frequently support the baby’s head with their hands or shoulders. As the baby’s activity increases continuously, clothing styles should also change accordingly. Tops can be slightly longer, changing from short monk collars to collared shirts. Except during summer, loose overalls can be worn on the lower body for warmth and free movement. Elastic waistbands should be avoided as much as possible, and they should not be too tight or high to avoid constraining chest development and affecting thoracic and pulmonary development in the baby.

Around 4 months of age, the salivary glands develop relatively perfectly, but the baby has not yet learned how to swallow, leading to frequent drooling. Some babies start teething, which exacerbates drooling. Therefore, several sets of clothes should be prepared for the baby at this stage, or the baby can wear a smock and a water-absorbent bib.

As the baby's grasping ability improves, special attention should be paid to toy safety and environmental safety. Medicines, cleaning products, and other toxic substances must not be placed where the baby can reach and touch them to prevent accidental ingestion and poisoning. Hot porridge, soup, etc., freshly prepared should not be placed where the baby can reach them to avoid scalding. Toys that the baby often grasps should be washed and disinfected regularly.

During this stage, the baby's waking time gradually increases. They can flip over independently in bed; their hands like to feel around and then put things in their mouths. They cannot sit steadily alone yet, but when supported to stand, they like to jump up and down. They are active but cannot move freely. If there is no dedicated caregiver, accidents can easily occur. At this point, consideration can be given to using a baby walker. Placing the baby in a walker allows them to practice sitting and provides toys for independent play. Parents can then do other tasks without needing to stay constantly by the baby's side. However, the baby should not be kept in the walker for extended periods. The correct approach is to let the baby sit for a while, then be held by an adult alternately.

5. Baby Teething

Teeth are related to chewing, digestion, and development, and to some extent, can reflect a child's health condition. Baby teeth are the chewing organs of infants, and stimulation of chewing function can promote the development of the jawbone and dental arch. Maintaining normal development of the jawbone and dental arch is an important prerequisite for the proper alignment of permanent teeth. However, the development and calcification of baby teeth are directly affected by maternal health conditions such as nutrition, diseases, and genetics. The calcification and development of permanent teeth mainly occur during infancy. Inorganic salts, vitamins, and trace elements like fluoride in drinking water are closely related to tooth development. Poor care of baby teeth, leading to early decay and loss, often results in misaligned permanent teeth or even tooth deformities, affecting chewing function, causing dietary inconvenience, and impacting facial appearance. Therefore, dental care should start from the eruption of the baby's first tooth.

Teeth are one of the indicators of health, but the timing of tooth eruption is unrelated to intelligence. Some diseases like rickets, malnutrition, cretinism, and congenital idiocy can delay tooth eruption and result in poor tooth quality. Mothers should observe the baby's tooth eruption and dental condition at all times.

Tooth development requires multiple vitamins (such as A, D, C) and minerals (such as calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, fluoride). If mothers lack these nutrients during pregnancy, it can affect the development of fetal tooth buds, causing enamel hypoplasia. To ensure good teeth for the child, mothers should consume more fresh vegetables, fruits, milk, eggs, fish, lean meat, and other nutritious foods during pregnancy to ensure the normal development of fetal deciduous teeth and jawbones. Pregnant women should prevent colds and fever during pregnancy to avoid affecting the development of fetal deciduous tooth buds; they should also be cautious about medication, especially avoiding tetracycline drugs to prevent the baby from developing tetracycline-stained teeth after birth. Infant tooth bud development requires large amounts of calcium and vitamin D to promote calcium absorption. If timely supplementation of cod liver oil and calcium is not provided after birth and the baby does not get enough sunlight, rickets may develop, delaying tooth eruption. Vitamin C deficiency affects enamel growth, and fluoride deficiency makes teeth prone to cavities, but excessive fluoride causes brownish spots on the enamel and brittleness. The main source of fluoride intake is water, so it is necessary to understand the fluoride content in local water. Regular consumption of tetracycline antibiotics by children can turn their teeth brown and make them prone to cavities, so the use of such antibiotics should be avoided.

Babies aged 4-6 months may sprout their first pair of teeth, or they may sprout their first tooth between 4-10 months or even at one year, all of which are considered normal. The number of baby teeth for different ages = age in months - 4 (or 6), meaning an 8-month-old baby should have 2-4 baby teeth.

6. Importance of Adding Complementary Foods

After 4 months, infants need timely addition of complementary foods for the following reasons:

(1) After the infant reaches 4 months, the nutrients obtained from breast milk or cow's milk no longer meet the needs of growth and development. It is necessary to add some foods in time to supplement the insufficient nutrients in breast milk or cow's milk, helping the infant grow up healthily. For example, infants aged 4-6 months have almost exhausted the iron obtained from their mothers, and the iron content in breast milk or cow's milk is insufficient. Therefore, children in this stage are prone to iron-deficiency anemia, and attention should be paid to adding iron-rich foods when introducing complementary foods.

(2) Adding complementary foods also prepares the infant for weaning, so the infant's food is also known as weaning food. Its meaning is not entirely about the food consumed during weaning but refers to the food added during the transition from exclusive breastfeeding (or cow's milk feeding) to complete weaning.

(3) To train the infant's swallowing ability. Infants accustomed to consuming milk (liquid) need to gradually transition to future solid food consumption, requiring an adaptation process that takes half a year or longer. The infant can start with soft, finely textured foods and eventually adapt to foods closer to adult solid foods.

(4) To train the infant's chewing function. As the infant grows older, the mucosa of the gums gradually hardens, especially after the front teeth erupt. Providing soft semi-solid foods encourages the infant to chew using the gums or teeth and then swallow. Thus, timely introduction of complementary foods helps train the infant's chewing function, promoting jaw development and tooth eruption.

7. How to Add Complementary Foods to Your Baby

Eating is simple for us adults, but for a newborn baby, it is a new attempt and exercise. Therefore, there are many issues regarding adding complementary foods to your baby.

Firstly, when should complementary foods be added to infants? Generally, complementary foods can be introduced to infants between 4-6 months, but for each individual infant, the specific time to add complementary foods should be determined based on different situations. 1. Based on the infant's energy needs, complementary foods can be introduced when the infant's weight has doubled since birth. For example, a child weighing 6 kg who is fed 8-10 times daily but still appears hungry frequently. At this point, a small amount of complementary food can be added to satisfy physiological needs. 2. Maturation of the infant's development, such as in motor development, requires the infant to be able to sit with support, raise their head and chest while lying on their stomach, and support their body weight with their elbows. In terms of sensory perception, the child can intentionally place their hands or toys in their mouth to explore the shape and texture of objects placed inside. This indicates the child is ready for complementary foods. 3. During feeding, when the spoon touches the lips, the infant exhibits a sucking action and swallows the food. When the infant touches the food or the feeder's hand, they show a smile and open their mouth, indicating hunger. Conversely, if the infant turns their head or body away or closes their mouth to refuse food, the feeding speed should be slowed down. Parents should respect the feedback from the infant.

Secondly, how specifically should complementary foods be added to the baby? Complementary foods should be introduced when the baby is in good condition, the mother is emotionally stable and relaxed, and a pleasant eating atmosphere is formed. A friendly attitude and joyful emotions should be used to influence the baby, making them willing to accept complementary foods. New complementary foods should be added after feeding half of the breast milk or formula milk, when the baby is partially full, making it easier for the baby to accept. Each new food should start with one spoonful, placing a small amount in the spoon and gently placing it on the middle of the baby's tongue. The food temperature should be kept at room temperature or slightly higher. For some children, a new food may need to be tried 15-20 times before being accepted by the baby. Attention should be paid to observing the baby's reaction and body language during feeding. If the baby is hungry, they will be delighted upon seeing the food, leaning forward and opening their mouth. Conversely, if the baby is not hungry, they will close their mouth, turn their head away, or close their eyes and fall asleep.

Principles for Adding Complementary Foods to Infants

Infant gastrointestinal development is not yet mature, and improper addition of complementary foods can easily cause digestive dysfunction or even diarrhea. Therefore, the following principles should be followed when adding complementary foods to infants:

(1) Allow the infant to gradually adapt. Start with one complementary food (such as rice powder), and after 3-7 days of adaptation, try another (such as wheat powder), gradually expanding the variety. During the "trial eating" stage, pay attention to whether there are any allergic reactions (such as skin rash, diarrhea, vomiting), and if there are allergies, temporarily stop feeding.

(2) The consistency of complementary foods should progress from thin to thick, and the taste from bland to flavorful. Initially, mix the rice powder thinner so the infant can easily swallow, and gradually increase its thickness as the infant adapts.

(3) The quantity of complementary foods should progress from small to large. Once a type of complementary food has been adapted to, the amount can be gradually increased.

(4) The texture of complementary foods should progress from fine to coarse. Fine-textured foods are easier to swallow and digest, such as starting with mashed vegetables for the infant, and gradually chopping the vegetables coarser into diced pieces.

8. Complementary Foods That Can Be Added During This Stage

(1) Rice Porridge or Thin Porridge: Exercises the baby's chewing and swallowing abilities, promoting the secretion of digestive enzymes.

(2) Egg Yolk: High in iron, it can supplement iron and prevent iron-deficiency anemia. Preparation: Cooked egg yolk 1/4, mixed with rice water or milk to form a paste, fed with a small spoon.

(3) Fish Puree: Supplements protein, iron, and various vitamins. Preparation: Remove the internal organs of fresh fish, wash thoroughly, steam or boil until cooked, remove all bones and mash into a puree, which can be mixed with rice porridge for consumption.

(4) Fruit Puree: Supplements vitamins and fiber. Preparation: Scrape the fruit into a puree with a spoon, gradually increasing the amount.

(5) Vegetable Puree: Wash fresh dark green vegetables (such as spinach, Chinese cabbage, mustard greens, etc.), finely chop into a puree, steam until cooked in a covered bowl; root vegetables like carrots, potatoes, sweet potatoes, etc., should be boiled or steamed until soft and mashed; add a little vegetable oil, stir-fry quickly on high heat.
