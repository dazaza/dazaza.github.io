The Ten Easiest Mistakes for Entrepreneurs to Make

by mading on 2007-05-06 19:24:03

If you want to benefit from this article, then please read it all at once.

It's been 14 years since I became an entrepreneur, and I have talked about the mistakes I made in business. I also advised many people to start their own businesses and found similar mistakes during their entrepreneurial processes. This article is intended to provide some advice to small business owners, especially those who are just starting (or about to start) a business.

One, selling products to people who will never buy your product.

Although sales are very important for any business, there is no need to sell your product to everyone, including your friends and family. Moreover, trying to sell products to people who obviously don't need them is a waste of your time.

Selling to the wrong people includes selling to anyone. Some customers are easier to sell to than others. For example, my wife provides online consulting to small businesses, and she finds that some customers are more difficult to deal with than others. If a potential customer is overly concerned about every dollar they invest, if they want to build a website but don't know why, or if they don't understand the Internet at all, they won't become your long-term customers. Don't hesitate to say "NO" to these consumers; serving them will only cause you more trouble. Let your competitors sell to them, which will save you a lot of headaches and give you more time to serve key customers.

Just because someone is interested in doing business with you doesn't mean you should accept. In my first year of doing business, I said "YES" to about 50% of the people who wanted to establish potential business relationships with me, which wasted a lot of my time chasing more customers. I had to deal with lunches with clients who just wanted to "see if we could do something together," and I hardly got a penny from them. If you think a meeting might be meaningless, don't meet with strangers just because you think you should strengthen cooperation. Today, when I receive such invitations, if they can't immediately spark my interest, I usually decline or ignore them. Many relationships are not worth building, and it is very important to learn to say "no" to bad opportunities and "yes" to good ones.

Two, spending too much money

Until you have a stable cash income, don't spend your valuable startup capital unless it's absolutely necessary. I started my computer game company with $20,000 of my own money, and it grew quickly; soon after, I began using loan financing. Unfortunately, the company's business model didn't work, and it took me five years to start making a profit. I quickly realized that every dollar spent would eventually need to be recouped through sales.

In 2004, I started this personal development business with just $9, even though I could have spent more on it. No fancy logos, no trendy website design, no business cards or stationery - I only paid for the domain name, which was my entire investment and all I wanted to spend before having a stable income. All other expenses came after I had a cash flow.

Your business should first put cash in your pocket, and then you "invest" in your business, clearly knowing how to recover the funds you invested.

Clearly, some businesses require a lot of capital at the beginning, but in the Internet era, you can easily start a profitable business.

Three, spending too little money

Being too stingy with your money is also a mistake. Don't let frugality hinder the efficiency of your business. Hire skilled workers who are more efficient than you to do specific tasks, and purchase equipment that you clearly know is worth the cost. You don't need to overspend on flashy equipment, but the equipment you have should increase your productivity. When you can use better software, don't stick with old versions, as it will allow you to complete work faster.

It takes a long learning process to know when you're holding onto cash too tightly or too loosely. Therefore, if you're just starting out, it's best to focus on the second point: "spending too much." Often considering this will help you make clear and correct decisions. If you can't confirm an expense, it may be a mistake. On the other hand, some situations are hard to justify without cash expenditure.

Four, fabricating a fake team

Many sole proprietors refer to their companies as "we," which is a common mistake among new entrepreneurs, and it's unnecessary. Especially today, there's nothing wrong with running a company alone. I referred to my game company as "we" for many years, but I always called my personal development company "my company." My wife refers to her VegFamily business magazine company as "we" because she has employees working for her, but she calls her online consulting company "my company." If you run a business alone, calling your company "my company" is perfectly fine. Conversely, if you falsely claim your personal company as "we," it's somewhat foolish because it won't earn you any respect. Promoting yourself as "my company" may be more effective today because others will realize you're not pretentious, and if you make a promise to them, you'll take full responsibility for it alone. Conversely, promises made by "we" aren't always trustworthy.

If you're a newly established sole proprietor, it's best not to falsely call your company "our company" or anything else. Most importantly, set a price level that matches the products and services you offer and your personal skills and abilities. Some sole proprietors think they must be controllers and market their products as if they're daydreaming. Deceiving your customers will only get you into trouble. If you falsify things to grow your business faster, then you shouldn't start your own company at all; if you can't provide genuine products or services, don't start a business yet. First, improve your technical skills.

Five, It's wise to let go of some contracts

I've made this mistake many times. I signed contracts with some well-known companies, but when their CEOs broke them for some reason (even dishonorable reasons), they wouldn't acknowledge the contract anymore. Obviously, I was in the right, but should I sue them? No, I wouldn't, I'd rather do something more meaningful.

A contract is just a piece of paper; what truly matters behind it is the business relationship. If the business relationship behind the contract ceases to exist, that contract does nothing for the commitments you both made. The significance of a contract lies in clearly defining each party's role and obligations in the transaction, but it is merely such a relationship, not a legal tool to force compliance. Once I realized this, I focused more on maintaining the business relationship hidden behind the contract rather than worrying about what was written on it, which made my transactions smoother. Once you focus on the contract itself, it means your transaction will become more difficult. Creative (profitable) deals often don't align with what's written in the contract. A lawyer who worked in the game development industry for over a decade told me that none of the deals he handled were entirely done according to the contract, some were even completely unrelated to the contract, but these were often large deals. Business relationships are like interpersonal relationships; they can change at any time and place.

Signing contracts is still necessary, especially with large companies where personnel come and go, but perhaps they don't value the business relationship with you. Don't make the mistake I mentioned; a contract is just the shadow of your transaction, the real transaction is your business relationship. Maintain good business relationships, and you won't worry too much about what's written in the contract.

There are many scoundrels in business transactions, and many of them are "CEOs," "Presidents," or "Chief Financial Officers." This is both sad and realistic. Some people seem to care about nothing but money; they lie, cheat, and steal to gain more wealth. In recent years, some extremely despicable businessmen have played with fire and got burned, leading to lawsuits (or court proceedings), but there are still plenty of "verbal tricks." For instance, in the computer game industry, some major game publishers pretend to be interested in a developer's new game, deceiving them continuously. This is quite common. They give developers indicators without signing contracts, and all the developers get are delays or false verbal promises. In fact, these publishers just want the game not to appear on the market so they don't face competition, allowing them to publish the game under their names. They hope the developers miss the next peak season, thereby paying them less. This really happens; in business, especially in the entertainment industry, the timid cannot survive.

Six, not trusting your intuition

Intuition is just as important in business as it is in other contexts. You'll be surprised by how many major deals are influenced by a CEO's intuition. While some situations fit logical business language, they're often impractical. If you strictly follow logic in business while ignoring intuition, you're likely to struggle in the business world.

Humans weren't originally logical thinkers; we simply don't have enough data to make fully logical decisions. Real business involves human factors, and we lack an accurate logical system to predict others' behaviors. Our inability to foresee others' future actions is a significant flaw in our logic, but intuition fills this gap. Others' actual actions either facilitate or disrupt a business partnership. Assuming everyone will act as expected is highly unrealistic, and no deal proceeds perfectly.

When your intuition tells you, "This business will fail," it's hard to say "no" to an opportunity that seems very attractive, but things often turn out differently. Later, I saw many pieces of evidence proving my intuition was consistently right. Sometimes, someone disagreed with me, and a few years later, several people I knew complained about being deceived by that person.

Intuition plays a crucial role in a company's decision-making process. Since business transactions rely on relationships, you need to understand others' perspectives on your transactions. If they disagree with you, ignore them; if they agree, remain cautious.

Seven, being too rigid

I reiterate, business is built on relationships. There are specific procedures in certain situations, but in most business settings, being too rigid is more harmful than beneficial. Business relationships must be based on friendly, humanized connections.

I believe being too rigid before establishing new business relationships is also a mistake. If an email sent to me starts with "Dear Mr. Chaotic Student," and then explains a commercial proposal at length, I usually consider it spam, especially when the word "we" is frequently used. A good email should start with "Hi, Chaotic," and then casually ask if I'm interested in collaborating. This saves time and directly establishes a genuine interpersonal relationship.

Treat your business relationships like friendships (or potential friendships). Being too rigid creates a wall between you and others, hindering the establishment of good business relationships. No one is loyal to a wall... (except the one in China – original text, no translation needed, up to the reader to understand).

Rigidity makes people feel boring. Everyone wants to enjoy their work; if someone treats me like a computer, I'll respond similarly – delete. But if someone shows personality and humor, it's much easier to establish a connection.

Eight, sacrificing your personality

In the early years of running my game company, I was overly serious and assumed I had to be "efficient"... whatever that meant. As a sole proprietor, I took on heavy responsibilities, and others judged me – was that right?

I started my game company in my twenties, a time when young people should be energetic. But I thought it was inappropriate for a company owner to be too active. Therefore, most of my business letters and emails sounded like they were written by the same person who created Microsoft's EULAs. My job title, "President," was deeply ingrained in me. I learned how to work without showing any personality.

After many years, I finally decided to be myself, especially after my game company became profitable. Now I am a blogger, and my personality and experience continue to strengthen. My unique traits add a distinctive charm to the blog; if I remained serious and wrote formal articles, the blog would become very ordinary, and I would lose most of my readers.

Being uniquely yourself and infusing your personal charisma into your company is excellent, especially when you're in your teens or twenties. Don't pretend to be someone else; if you can attract various customers and partners willing to collaborate with you, you'll ultimately enjoy your work more.

If some people can't handle your eccentricity, that's their loss; focus your energy on those who can accept you.

Nine, neglecting value creation

It's easy to fall into the trap of thinking the purpose of business is solely for money, but the true purpose of business is to create value. In the short term, you can make money without creating value, but in the long run, that's unsustainable. Even criminal organizations must "create value." When you find your company is merely absorbing value from others without reciprocating, it diminishes your self-respect and makes running your company less enjoyable.

Why does your company exist? Its existence is to create value for you and your customers. The clearer you understand what value you're trying to produce, the better you can focus on it. My game company creates the value of brain games, and StevePavlina.com creates the value of personal growth. Often, company owners don't clearly understand what value they're creating. They just want to sell more and make more money, which is an unsuccessful business model. The world doesn't lack sales or products, but it often needs truly valuable creations, which is where you should focus your efforts.

Currently, this website contains over 400 articles, representing much of the value I've created. Thousands of people visit this site daily to benefit from it. Helping others grow is the primary goal and value of this website.

Ten, not striving for perfection

While value creation is essential for a continuously developing company and you should focus on how to create value, it also means you need to spend remaining time striving for perfection. You can build a company that provides great value but still runs at a loss. As the owner, you should find a suitable way to convert that value into benefits. Your initial product attempts might be subpar, and you'll spend a lot of time, money, and resources improving its value – and that's the right approach. Many companies develop this way, but don't let your company just do that; strive for perfection.

Once a business process is in place, break it down step by step and optimize it in every aspect repeatedly, finding multiple ways to make it more efficient. Can you complete the task in less time and with less money? Can you perform it regularly?

I once manually managed orders at my game company. In 1994, when I started the company, whenever I received an order via mail or my website, I used some software to input and manage orders through a modem. At the end of each month, I manually recorded sales. This was convenient when sales were low, but as sales increased, it became increasingly burdensome. A few years ago, I upgraded the software to automate online records, including game download speeds. Each record was stored in a database, and I could browse real-time reports to see how each product was selling. Doing this required some time, but it was worth it. That automated system saved me a lot of time and effort, and I didn't have to spend a lot managing it.

Don't fall into the trap of letting old methods automatically handle routine tasks, including inventory management, promotion, accounting, order processing, communication, and sales. If you find yourself doing the same repetitive work every month, ensure you invest some effort into optimizing it. Not optimizing is like wasting time and money; saving money and time is much simpler than creating them.

Internet operation optimization becomes simple with numerous opportunities. Trying new things online and predicting their outcomes is easy. After running this website for a year, I gained much insight into Google Adsense. Some people dislike the ad layout on this site, but it's the most effective layout I've tried so far. I use it because it works now. Adding a donation page was also an optimal choice; some people click ads, some donate, some do both. Still, my main goal remains to create value; this is just non-profit revenue to maintain development. More money means more resources invested in value creation, so value creation and optimization can proceed simultaneously.

Building a successful company requires a lot of effort, but it's also a process of gaining experience. I know many people quit their jobs to start their own businesses, and although not everyone achieved what they hoped, I haven't heard anyone say they regret taking the risk. The reins of destiny are in your control.
