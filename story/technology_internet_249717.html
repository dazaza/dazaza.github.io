Kaifu Lee: Google China failed to surpass Baidu because of its insufficient speed

by longersing on 2009-10-20 13:49:56

Li Kai-Fu: Google China did not surpass Baidu because it was not fast enough. IT legendary hero Li Kai-Fu is currently on his longest "business trip" since resigning as President of Google Greater China - a 10-day tour through 8 cities and 15 universities. Last Friday, during a break from his speeches at Fudan University and Shanghai Jiaotong University, he gave an exclusive interview to the Morning Post, addressing three major questions of concern. He said, "During my tenure, Google China's market share doubled, but we fell short in terms of speed, which is why we were at a disadvantage compared to Baidu."

Question 1:

Was Google's performance unsatisfactory during his tenure?

After Li Kai-Fu resigned, some analysts had already drawn conclusions about his performance at Google China. There were even speculations that one potential reason for his resignation was Google China's inability to surpass its rival Baidu over the years. "During my tenure, Google's market share has already doubled. When other multinational companies have completely failed in the Chinese Internet market, only Google has survived," Li Kai-Fu said. Regarding Google's successful foothold in the Chinese market, Li Kai-Fu has his own perspective. In his view, other multinational companies suffer from four problems: insufficient humility, lack of understanding of Chinese user needs; insufficient delegation, too many decisions made by the U.S. headquarters; excessive eagerness to make money; and slow action, while Chinese Internet companies move very quickly, making it difficult for multinational companies to keep up.

Li Kai-Fu pointed out that the reason Google China could survive alone was because it performed well in the first three areas, but fell short in terms of speed, which is why it was at a disadvantage in the competition with Baidu.

Question 2:

Are lectures and blogging considered neglecting one's main responsibilities?

Li Kai-Fu officially resigned on September 4, and since then, his packed schedule seems to have little to do with his new job at Innovation Works - the launch of his new book on October 11; campus tours from October 13 to 22; publishing ten blog posts since his resignation, and also leaving messages on public welfare websites. Is Li Kai-Fu neglecting his duties, or has he reached the ultimate use of time?

"There's still plenty of time for people," Li Kai-Fu smiled and told reporters when asked how to balance social work and his main responsibilities, "I'm just using fragmented time, doing things during my spare time, whenever there's time, I'll do it." Wang Zhaohui, the media liaison for Innovation Works, also mentioned that Li Kai-Fu is very good at utilizing fragmented time, "On the way to the company by car, waiting for flights, or waiting for friends, he will take advantage of these moments."

Question 3:

Is helping youth just a cover for business?

In the eyes of many young students, Li Kai-Fu is a mentor and friend, even a spiritual leader. However, some suspect that his image as a youth mentor has ulterior motives. Is Li Kai-Fu trying to achieve success while helping young people, or is he helping young people while achieving success?

"Helping millions of young people is my ideal. But my current goal is to succeed and give high returns to my investors," Li Kai-Fu clearly defined the above doubts.

Li Kai-Fu once wanted to establish a private university, but it came to nothing. When he talked about this again, Li Kai-Fu said that the priority now is to run Innovation Works well and give returns to investors. The idea of founding a university will be considered later. [Reporter's Notes]

At universities, he receives star-like treatment.

At 8:30 am on the 16th, half an hour before the speech began, the movie classroom at Fudan University's Zhangjiang Campus was already full. Many students stood at the back of the classroom for two hours just to catch a glimpse of Li Kai-Fu. Even without the title of President of Google Greater China, his popularity among young students remains undiminished.

Dressed in a suit and tie, with a neatly parted hairstyle, this seems to be Li Kai-Fu's standard appearance when facing the outside world. However, beneath this seemingly rigorous exterior lies a humorous heart. Especially during the student Q&A session, his witty and funny answers elicited laughter from the audience.

"I haven't been to the toilet at the company for a long time recently," Li Kai-Fu said when answering whether "Innovation Works" accepts good ideas from the outside, "Because every day outside the company door, there is a large group of people with good ideas blocking me from going to the restroom, fortunately, the company has a back door." The audience burst into laughter.

When talking about his memorable failures, Li Kai-Fu gave three examples: one was being rated as the worst teacher when he was teaching; another was launching a product that he thought was cool but failed to gain market success, eventually selling the product and causing the team to become unemployed; and another was his lawsuit with Microsoft.

"If today I had the chance to choose again, I would not hope that these three things didn't happen," Li Kai-Fu indicated that it is precisely through setbacks that one can learn more. "From my lawsuit with Microsoft, I learned not to waste a second on unnecessary things. At that time, there were many rumors about this matter in the media. If I looked at each one and cared about each one, I wouldn't have time to do anything else. After this incident, I learned perseverance, focus, and how to judge people, who are true friends."

After the speech, Li Kai-Fu was surrounded by students rushing forward for five minutes straight, asking for autographs and photos, receiving treatment akin to an idol star.

Morning Post reporter Zhou Kai, interns Kang Feicheng, Liang Kaiqi
