Shuguang Village's Undercover Battle Against "PetroChina Impostor"

by 0brq212j1x on 2009-12-01 13:03:21

The "sacred land" of Liushui Pit Fishing Village in Zhejiang, which welcomed the dawn of the new millennium on the mainland, saw an oil depot erected just hundreds of meters from residential houses 9 years ago. In 2008, villagers of Liushui Pit commissioned a survey, and the results shocked them: these several dozen massive oil tanks were actually a counterfeit version of "China National Petroleum Corporation (CNPC)." The best scenic spot in "Dawn Village," Liushui Pit Village - "Diaoren Bingwan Bay" - was occupied by over a dozen giant oil tanks. On November 15, the oil depot was hit by a landslide. Villagers worried that an explosion at the oil depot would endanger the village. What would happen to the nearby villages if the oil tanks exploded? The water between them could carry oil and fire into the village, leading to "a chain reaction of fires." Every day, the sun rises. In the year 2000, the first ray of sunlight of the new millennium on the Chinese mainland shone upon Liushui Pit Fishing Village under the jurisdiction of Shitang Town, Wenling City, Taizhou City, Zhejiang Province.

Villagers said that at the easternmost part of the village, there is a place called "Diaoren Bingwan Bay," which is where the first ray of sunlight fell. It is a seaside terrace where the sea crashes against rocky coasts, and beneath the rocks, the sea abounds with oysters, seaweed, and laver.

This area has relatively low terrain and a narrow topography; hence, the "China Millennium Dawn Monument" built in 2000 was located on a hill behind it, about 200 meters above sea level.

Also, in that same year, township and village cadres required the villagers to "sacrifice small families for the greater good of the nation," and thus, a large oil depot named "China National Petroleum Corporation's Third Oil Depot in Eastern Zhejiang" was established in Diaoren Bingwan Bay.

Thus, more accurately speaking, the first rays of sunlight hitting the Chinese mainland every day illuminate the tops of the towering oil tanks, each marked prominently with the Chinese and English logos of "China National Petroleum Corporation."

In 2008, the villagers of Liushui Pit commissioned a survey, and the results startled them: these dozens of mammoth oil tanks turned out to be a counterfeit version of "China National Petroleum Corporation." —— Who do they belong to?

"CNPC" Arrives

A large project landing in a small fishing village caused concerns among the villagers, but most supported the construction of this "national project."

At the easternmost end of Wenling City in Zhejiang Province lies a peninsula fishing village surrounded by sea on three sides. Liushui Pit Village is named after a stream pit over 200 meters long that runs through half the village. The entire village consists of more than 300 households and nearly a thousand people. For hundreds of years, the men have gone out to sea to fish during the fishing season, while the women have managed household chores and crafts at home.

At the eastern end of the village lies a terrace about 40 meters above sea level, covering nearly 80 acres. Fishermen grow vegetables and sweet potatoes on the mountain, and the seawater below produces abundant oysters, laver, and other seafood. Elders in the village say that the place receiving the first ray of sunlight is a feng shui treasure land.

In 2000, after accepting the first ray of sunlight shining on the Chinese mainland, the so-called "Dawn Village" of Liushui Pit received visitors. Under the introduction of the Shitang Town government and village cadres, an oil depot project would be implemented here.

According to publicity from the town officials, this was the state-owned enterprise CNPC's "Third Oil Depot in Eastern Zhejiang," and the villagers deeply believed this. In September 2000, the Wenling Zhongyou Petroleum Sales Co., Ltd., self-claimed as a subsidiary of "China National Petroleum Corporation," signed an agreement with the then Liushui Pit Village Committee, paying a one-time compensation of 350,000 yuan for the land use. This piece of land was precisely "Diaoren Bingwan Bay."

Massive oil tanks rose from the ground. Although they were less than a kilometer away from the nearest houses, causing some worries among the residents, under the reassurance of the town and village cadres, the fishermen said that with the spirit of "sacrificing small families for the greater good of the nation," most people supported the construction of state-owned enterprises and national projects.

After the completion of the oil depot, the Wenling Zhongyou Petroleum Company also built a port along the coast. In the following few years, the business of the oil depot grew larger, and they acquired land twice within the village. The massive oil tanks lined up along the coast, expanding several times in scale, separated only by two or three hundred meters of water from the easternmost houses in the village.

Recalling this, villager representative Zheng Jufang said, "At that time, the village chief and secretary handed out blank papers for the villagers' representatives to sign, saying that after signing, they could collect subsidies from the township government. We didn't know beforehand what the cost of these signatures would be—losing our land."

There were also some people in the township who questioned how a prestigious CNPC could build an oil depot on collective land without going through the requisition process, but no one could explain exactly why. At that time, the relationship between the Liushui Pit villagers and the oil depot was generally peaceful.

New Village Officials Reveal Inside Secrets

As the fishing industry gradually declined, the oil depot became an obstacle to the development of tourism in Liushui Pit Village.

This apparent peace was broken in 2008 due to a brawl.

On July 13th of that year, two young men from Liushui Pit Village attempted to enter the oil depot premises to fish on the sea reef and had a verbal altercation with the management staff. The current chairman of the oil depot, Wu Gang, explained that it was done out of safety considerations.

The verbal altercation escalated into a physical fight. After the incident, the whole village of Liushui Pit was in turmoil. Hundreds of villagers blocked the only road connecting the oil depot to the outside world, setting up tents and taking turns guarding day and night.

On July 19th, leaders from the Shitang Town government rushed to the scene to mediate. The oil depot side brought in dozens of security guards to try to drive away the villagers. The town officials insisted that the public should not interfere with the normal production and operation of the large state-owned enterprise "China National Petroleum Corporation." Soon afterward, a working group sent by the municipal committee entered the village, and relevant leaders promised to resolve the issues raised by the villagers before the end of the year. On August 6th, all the villagers withdrew from the oil depot area and roads.

The conflict between the village and the oil depot thus became publicized. And the stubborn attitude of the newly elected village head, who took office in May that year, gradually revealed many secrets about the oil depot that had been kept confidential.

The newly elected village head was named Lin Guifeng. During the village head election, she declared her intention to protect the interests of Liushui Pit Village and its villagers, earning her the position.

Whose interests in Liushui Pit Village and among the villagers have been infringed upon? The villagers believed that the primary offender was the "China National Petroleum Corporation" oil depot located in "Diaoren Bingwan Bay." Apart from being frequently driven away by the oil depot managers when fishing, collecting laver, or gathering oysters, the emerging "Fisherman's Fun" activities were also severely affected by the presence of the oil depot.

According to some commercial developers, "Diaoren Bingwan Bay" is worth over 100 million yuan. For Liushui Pit Village, whose marine fishing industry has gradually declined and can only find a way out in tourism, the oil depot has become an obstacle.

Clusters of stone houses, ancient stone fortresses, and stone wells make Liushui Pit Village's scenery increasingly known to the Chinese public. In the works of Guo Xiaolu, a female writer from Shitang Town living in the UK, her English novel "Village of Stone" ("Stone Town") uses Shitang Town as its prototype, and Liushui Pit Village is recognized as the most beautiful village in Shitang by many foreigners.

In 2006, Liushui Pit Village was the first in Taizhou to establish a "Fisherman's Fun" program, gradually gaining popularity among tourists from surrounding areas. However, the best viewing areas in the village are filled with oil tanks, leaving many tourists feeling regretful.

After Lin Guifeng became the village head, she began attempting to address this issue. The first question was about the compensation for the oil depot's occupation of village collective land.

The villagers believed that the previous land-use compensation agreements, which were not discussed at a village meeting, should be considered invalid. Under Lin Guifeng's leadership, members of the village committee began negotiating with the oil depot operators.

"There are many rumors about this oil depot," Lin Guifeng said. "From the attitudes of some municipal officials during the road blockage, I also sensed something strange: How could CNPC accept the former village head as a shareholder? Are there shares owned by higher-level government officials?" Lin Guifeng said, "Otherwise, why does the government allow them to defy orders when asked to dismantle illegal buildings and return the occupied land?"

Lin Guifeng began shuttling between the Wenling municipal government, law firms, and other institutions, but she did not get answers. Some people initially showed great enthusiasm, but eventually remained silent.

Many invisible hands were also trying to stop her. The "Fisherman's Fun" in Liushui Pit could no longer see high-ranking officials from Taizhou Wenling. Previously, here was almost the preferred place for the government to entertain guests.

"My house windows have been broken several times. Someone tried every means to prevent me from investigating further," Lin said.

This oil depot is not simple.

The oil depot is privately owned. Over the years, it has managed to survive amidst the tide of nationalization with its own survival strategies: first, it emptied the "poverty alleviation project," and second, it counterfeited CNPC.

Several new village committee members secretly went to Shanghai and Hangzhou to seek support from legal experts. Friends in Shanghai helped lawyers obtain business registration information of related companies in Wenling, Taizhou, Ningbo, and Shanghai.

Lin Guifeng asked others to study the materials, and the results were shocking.

At the end of the 1990s, as China's WTO negotiations approached their conclusion, the country began to seek mergers and control over finished oil wholesale and retail enterprises by CNPC and Sinopec; prior to this, local governments and private capital-sponsored oil enterprises mostly needed to rely on these two giants to survive.

Relevant industry insiders said that around this time, the prices of oil depots and gas stations soared. The sudden emergence of the Liushui Pit Village oil depot occurred right before the skyrocketing prices of oil enterprises. At that time, due to the tightening of provincial departments' approval policies, "private enterprises had almost no chance."

The first entity to propose the establishment of an oil depot project was the fully locally state-owned Wenzhou (Jingning) Fubang Ethnic Development Co., Ltd. In May 1998, the aforementioned company applied to the relevant provincial authorities for the establishment of an oil depot project.

Jingning She Autonomous County, one of the impoverished counties in Zhejiang Province in the 1990s, had a counterpart support agreement with the economically developed coastal county-level city of Wenling. The oil depot project was soon approved by the Energy Department of the Provincial Planning and Economic Commission. Some village cadres outside heard that the oil depot was a "mountain-sea cooperation" poverty alleviation project. Sea referred to Wenling, and mountain referred to Jingning.

It is worth noting that the approval document clearly stated that the location for building the oil depot was Hengyu Island, nearly 10 kilometers away from Liushui Pit Village. This island is still uninhabited today and lies off the Shitang Town fishing port.

After the oil depot project was approved, in June 1999, the equity structure of "Wenling Zhongyou Petroleum Sales Co., Ltd." (hereafter collectively referred to as "Liushui Pit Oil Depot") changed. According to industrial and commercial records, Su Xiaomin, who was unemployed at home that year, became a shareholder of the company and served as its legal representative. Thus, the originally state-owned enterprise that applied for the project quietly transformed into a private enterprise.

In May and July 1999, the General Office of the State Council and the National Economic and Trade Commission issued successive documents to clean up and reorganize the refined oil market, namely the "State Council General Office Document [1999] No. 38" and the "National Economic and Trade Commission Document [1999] No. 637."

These notices emphasized that enterprises engaged in gasoline, kerosene, and diesel wholesale must have registered capital of no less than 5 million RMB. Non-state-owned enterprises need to undergo restructuring through methods such as transfer, joint operation, participation, or acquisition by the two state-owned petroleum groups to continue operations.

Accordingly, the "Liushui Pit Oil Depot" increased its registered capital to 5.18 million RMB; additionally, the oil depot found Ningbo Bonded Area Zhongyou Petroleum Products Sales Co., Ltd. A certificate issued by the latter to the Wenling Petroleum Market Rectification Leading Group in June 1999 stated that the Liushui Pit Oil Depot was its joint venture partner.

"But in fact, their cooperation with Ningbo Zhongyou did not constitute a legal joint venture. You could say they just found a talisman," a knowledgeable person said. The Wenling Petroleum Market Rectification Leading Group recognized this "problematic talisman."

Before the initiators of the oil depot, Wenzhou (Jingning) Fubang Ethnic Development Co., Ltd., relinquished control, they did one more thing —— applied for relocation and obtained approval from relevant departments. The oil depot moved from the inconvenient Hengyu Island to Liushui Pit Village. Six departments stamped their seals within two days. According to administrative regulations reviewed by the reporter, counties should not have corresponding powers, requiring approval from the original approving department step by step.

In July 2000, the operating entity of the oil depot changed to Taizhou Huadong Zhongyou Petroleum Sales Co., Ltd. According to industrial and commercial registration data, Taizhou Huadong Zhongyou Petroleum Sales Co., Ltd. has two legal shareholders: Wenling Zhongyou Petroleum Sales Co., Ltd., with Su Xiaomin as the legal representative; and Shanghai Huadong Zhongyou Petroleum Sales Co., Ltd., with Gao Zhenhuai as the legal representative.

Previously, Wenzhou (Jingning) Fubang Ethnic Development Co., Ltd., which applied to the competent authority for the construction of the oil depot, had completely withdrawn, transferring its shares at the original price of 100,000 RMB to Su Xiaomin at the time of registration.

Legal professionals pointed out that by 2000, this share price should have multiplied many times, making the cheap sale by the initiators puzzling.

Industrial and commercial materials show that Shanghai Huadong Zhongyou Petroleum Sales Co., Ltd. was originally the East China Branch of China National Petroleum Corporation Sales Company. In 1999, according to the requirements of the national rectification of finished oil wholesale enterprises, it changed into an independent legal entity under China National Petroleum Corporation. However, from 2001 onward, the company stopped selling finished oil products and no longer had retail licenses.

In October 2001, in order to let state-owned enterprises fully control the oil market before the end of the WTO transition period, the General Office of the State Council issued the "Opinions on Further Rectifying and Standardizing the Order of the Finished Oil Market." This opinion again clarified that finished oil sales in China must be handled by CNPC and Sinopec.

At that time, due to obtaining the investment and joint operation of Shanghai Huadong Zhongyou Petroleum Sales Co., Ltd., a subsidiary of CNPC, the Liushui Pit oil depot survived in the "strangling" of private enterprises by the two major oil giants.

Was it really recruited by CNPC? The reporter checked relevant industrial and commercial data and found that in the annual "external investment situation" of Shanghai Huadong Zhongyou Petroleum Sales Co., Ltd., there was no investment behavior in "Taizhou Huadong Zhongyou Petroleum Sales Co., Ltd." During its operation, the company publicly invested in dozens of enterprises, none of which were willing to be minority shareholders.

"Corporate external equity investments should have complete records in industrial and commercial data," said a lawyer in Shanghai.

According to the WTO agreement, the country fully opened the finished oil market in December 2006, making the "China National Petroleum Corporation" talisman unnecessary. The following May, "Shanghai Huadong Zhongyou Petroleum Sales Co., Ltd." withdrew, selling its shares at the original price of 780,000 RMB to a natural person named Wu Gang.

At this point, whether it was truly invested or not, "Taizhou Huadong Zhongyou Petroleum" had nothing to do with CNPC anymore. But in the Liushui Pit Village oil depot, the operators sprayed the "China National Petroleum Corporation" and "CNPC" marks on the oil tanks and gas stations to proclaim their affiliation with CNPC.

According to reports in the "China National Petroleum News," in 2003, due to the rampant illegal use of "China National Petroleum Corporation" trademarks by oil product dealers, CNPC's East China Sales Branch conducted a cleanup and reorganization of enterprises illegally using "China National Petroleum Corporation" and "CNPC" marks in East China and South China regions.

According to industry insiders, CNPC mainly operated in the north, while Sinopec operated in the south. These southern oil companies bearing the CNPC brand mostly wanted to avoid being acquired and simultaneously deceive consumers.

The Liushui Pit oil depot escaped this inspection. After maneuvering for six years, the current Party Secretary of Shitang Town, Xiang Genfa, still insists to the reporter that this is CNPC's oil depot.
