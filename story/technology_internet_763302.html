Interesting numbers in App Store vs Google Play comparison

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 18:23:17

We've seen this kind of comparison many times:

The number of apps on Google Play exceeds 1 million, surpassing Apple's 900,000; the total app downloads on Google Play have reached 5 billion, aligning with App Store figures.

In reality, Google Play and App Store are quite different in terms of their app store models, and there are other interesting comparative dimensions. Mobile cloud service company Kinvey created a set of infographics that persistently examine these two app stores from an "interesting" perspective.

App Store generates a lot of money daily

Over the past two years, Android has been expanding globally, and currently, Google Play holds a 74.4% share in the "app store" market, while Apple's App Store only accounts for 18.2%. The remaining market share belongs to Windows Phone and BlackBerry World.

Despite Google Play securing the majority of the market share, its daily earnings are only one-fifth of the App Store's. The App Store generates $5.1 million in revenue daily, whereas Google Play earns just $1.1 million.

"Widgets" are very popular on Google Play

Due to Android's openness — and also because of its imperfections — personalized small customization tools are very popular on Google Play. These kinds of widgets rank second after "games and entertainment," accounting for 12% of apps.

There are two commonalities between App Store and Google Play: both have "games and entertainment" and "books and literature" among their top three categories. However, the second most popular category on App Store is not "widgets," but "education," suggesting that iOS device owners might be more studious, while Android users appear more geeky and enjoy tinkering with their devices?

Top app publishers: App Store far outpaces Google Play

This chart combines the download volume of apps developed by publishers and the total number of apps they have developed. In terms of downloads, there is no doubt that apps officially produced by Apple and Google are highly downloaded, making them the top app publishers themselves.

However, when it comes to the number of apps developed, the top five publishers on App Store are far superior to those on Google Play: Gameloft has launched 266 apps on App Store, followed by Disney (144), EA (126), and Google (23).

On Google Play, the publisher with the most apps is Go Dev (135), followed by Google (60) and Adobe (31). This aligns with the earlier observation that Android users are willing to tinker with their phones. It also shows that Android serves as an important carrier for Google services, embodying Google's efforts on the mobile platform.

Google Play apps have various pricing methods

In this survey, the pricing methods of the top 100 apps were examined. Among the top 100 apps on Google Play, there are 29 different pricing tiers, whereas App Store's top 100 apps only have 7 distinct pricing methods.

The reason is straightforward: App Store prices generally end with ".99," and within one dollar, there is only one pricing method. Google Play does not have such restrictions, hence its diverse pricing strategies.

The "most expensive apps" are all on App Store

The "most expensive app" on App Store costs up to $999.99, while the most expensive app on Google Play is priced at $200.

Among the top five most expensive apps, App Store is more "premium" — their prices range between $399-$999. Meanwhile, the top five most expensive apps on Google Play are evenly distributed between $159-$200.

Google Play is more vibrant than App Store

In this set of statistics, the proportion of new apps' total income among the top 300 is nearly 20% for Google Play, while App Store's proportion is less than 15%. This indicates that new apps don't make as much money on App Store.

The percentage of newly added publishers among the total publishers is roughly the same for Google Play and App Store. This means that as the Android user base grows, many developers release products on both platforms simultaneously, ensuring that Google Play isn't neglected by developers.

Google Play maintains a good level of vitality, and newcomers can earn decent rewards rather than having their income monopolized by established publishers like on App Store. This bodes well for Google Play's future as an app store. This is more significant than simply surpassing App Store in terms of app numbers or download counts. The future of education lies with children, and the future of app stores lies with new entrepreneurs.
