Let's start with reading. I think Chinese candidates are all good at reading, and we rely on this section to score... There is a method called "Wordless Reading" which is quite useful. The essence is DOTHEBEST, [guess]THEREST. Since IELTS is a paper-based test, you can make various marks. I strongly recommend looking at the questions first, then the article. Underline the locating words and key words in the questions, and then find them in the article according to the order of the questions. British examiners are very gentlemanly, and the questions are arranged in sequence. If the order is slightly messed up, that's the difficult part. You don't need to get a 9, getting three or four wrong is still acceptable. For specific question types, you can listen to Lianshan Qi's interpretation in "Wordless", which I personally find helpful. Emphasize one question type, TRUEFALSENOTGIVEN, you must write it fully, no abbreviations!

Listening:

Now for listening (although I didn't perform well this time), just listen more. I scored 8.5 last time by doing the listening parts of Cambridge IELTS books 3,4,5,6 three times. The first time, listen to what is required to be filled in; the second time, listen to other valuable information besides what is required; the third time, understand every sentence. Listening is just about practice, if you don't practice enough, your ears will become dull. This time I only practiced once. Another annoying thing about listening is capitalization when transferring answers. Sometimes it's weird, but they are very strict about it, so you need to find the patterns during practice.

Writing:

Then let's talk about writing, my biggest headache. This time was a historic breakthrough for me. I scored 6,6,6 before, and I didn't know how to review anymore. My experience is that you need your own template (be careful, too many people use Shen Xiaoyi's book, don't copy it directly, it can be disastrous~) Don't think of everything on the spot, there's really no time! Analyzing the topic is important, going off-topic is useless. You must answer every question in the topic. Prepare two to three opening sentences, either phenomenon-based or contrast-based. Then prepare transition sentences for paragraphs, usually write three paragraphs, prepare three to four sentences. Prepare two to three concluding sentences. Write in the [general-particular-general] format, I don't recommend one-sided arguments as they require too much evidence, it's hard to come up with so many points in the exam. Each paragraph should have a sub-point and an example to illustrate it, concise and to the point. Sentence structure should combine short and long sentences, rich in variety, use more special sentence structures, don't always use there is/there are, sb has sth, use various clauses is a good way to avoid monotony. For example, you can use cause adverbial clause (since, as instead of because) or move the adverbial clause and adverb to the front, these are cute highlights.
