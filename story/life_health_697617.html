Rhinitis is not just a simple cold.

As the weather gradually cools down, some people will experience symptoms like sneezing and nasal congestion at this time, which they might mistake for a common cold. In fact, frequent colds may actually be due to rhinitis.

Experts from the Nanning ENT Department of the Guangxi Corps Hospital introduced that to judge whether it's a cold or rhinitis, there are clear distinctions between allergic rhinitis and colds. Colds are usually caused by viruses or bacteria invading the body after being chilled, with specific symptoms including nasal congestion, runny nose, fever, cough, muscle pain, and general fatigue in severe cases. Colds are self-limiting diseases; with adequate rest and water intake, they can heal within 7 to 10 days.

Allergic rhinitis, on the other hand, is triggered by exposure to allergens such as dust mites, pollen, and mold. Patients may experience itchy noses, sneezing, especially when exposed to allergens, cold air, or even cigarette smoke. Some patients with allergic rhinitis may sneeze dozens of times consecutively, followed by a runny nose with watery discharge. Some may also experience nasal congestion and headaches but without systemic symptoms.

If patients exhibit prolonged symptoms such as runny nose, nasal congestion, headache, sore throat, excessive phlegm, occasional fever, or overall discomfort that lasts long and doesn't heal despite repeated treatments or recurs frequently, they should consider whether they have sinusitis. Many cases of sinusitis are due to prolonged colds. If the nasal discharge is thick and yellow-green or accompanied by headaches, forehead pain, or orbital pain, nine out of ten patients can be diagnosed with sinusitis. Sinusitis can lead to memory decline, mental lethargy, and damage to the optic and auditory nerves, making timely treatment crucial.

Experts remind that patients prone to rhinitis should strengthen the protection of their nasal cavities by doing the following:

Keep the nasal cavity clean: rinse the nasal cavity with dilute salt water 6-8 times daily to reduce nasal mucosal congestion, swelling, and allergic reactions.

Maintain indoor air humidity: use an air filter and avoid letting the nose become too dry.

Massage to unblock nasal passages: rub your palms together until slightly warm, then cover the nose for 1-2 minutes, pinch the nose, and rub the nostrils for 1-2 minutes each. Do this once in the morning and evening.
