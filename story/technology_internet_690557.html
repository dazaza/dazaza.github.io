A Brief Discussion on the Legal Regulation of the Inflammation of Hongming Trademark

by cvxzty on 2012-03-05 10:50:10

[Keywords] Dilution, Infringement, Unfair Competition, Legal Regulation

[Abstract] Trademark dilution is both an infringement and an act of unfair competition. Therefore, foreign countries and international legislation have adopted different legislative systems to regulate trademark dilution behavior. Based on the experience of foreign and international legislation, China has improved its trademark dilution laws but still has certain shortcomings that need to be addressed in future legislation.

The term "dilution" (Dilution) originally means "dilute". When applied to trademarks, some scholars define it as the unauthorized use of a well-known trademark on dissimilar goods without the permission of the owner of the well-known trademark. This definition does not reveal the essence of dilution, which is to weaken or reduce the distinctiveness and significance of a well-known trademark for its products or services, leading to a weakening of the special appeal of the well-known trademark to consumers.

I. Nature of Dilution

(A) Trademark Dilution is an Infringement Act. Traditional theory defines trademark infringement as using a trademark identical or similar to a registered trademark on the same or similar goods without the permission of the trademark registrant. The act of using a trademark identical or similar to a registered trademark on non-similar goods is not specified. The theory of trademark dilution breaks this restriction and requires expanded protection for well-known trademarks. In fact, trademark dilution acts already meet the conditions for infringement liability: (1) Illegality. Dilution acts confuse consumers about the provider of goods or services and are considered illegal acts. Foreign case law all recognizes this point. For example, the French court ruled in 1967 that if a well-known trademark like "Calor" is used on non-identical goods that could be considered similar and likely to cause confusion among consumers, it should be protected. In the United States, courts do not allow the use of trademarks such as "Kodak" on bicycles or lighters, or "JohnyWalker" on cigarettes; (2) Damage Fact. The damages caused by dilution acts mainly include three aspects: First, degrading a well-known trademark in a certain way. For example, using the "Shuanghui" sausage trademark for a toilet cleaner. Second, dimming a well-known trademark in a certain way. There was a case in Japan where the famous camera trademark "Sanyo" was later used on cosmetics, causing disputes. The Tokyo District Court pointed out in its judgment: "Using marks identical or similar to the 'Sanyo' trademark on cosmetics will dilute the influence of the trademark and weaken its association with cameras... reducing the customer attraction of the trademark on cameras, diminishing its advertising effect, and decreasing the intangible property value of the trademark." Third, indirectly misinterpreting the trademark as a common name for the product. For example, some well-known trademarks lose their distinctiveness due to dilution and become generic names for products. Aspirin, thermos bottles, nylon, Vaseline, these historically famous trademarks have now become generic names for products; (3) Causal relationship between damage fact and result, i.e., the dilution of well-known trademarks is caused by dilution acts; (4) Trademark dilution acts do not consider the subjective fault of the actor. Mr. Chengsi Zheng emphasized that fault is a condition for the actor to bear compensation responsibility, but not a condition for recognizing infringement. Therefore, as long as the dilution actor enters another's rights range, it constitutes infringement.

(B) Trademark Dilution is an Unfair Competition Act. Dilution of trademarks is a typical "free ride" act. The actor uses the reputation of well-known trademarks to promote themselves, leveraging the competitive advantage of well-known trademarks to compete with other operators who have the same or similar business, harming the interests of some competitors and obtaining improper competitive advantages, constituting unfair competition acts. At the same time, dilution acts confuse consumers about the goods or services provided by the actor and the owner of the well-known trademark, thus purchasing their goods or accepting their services, disrupting market order, and being detrimental to the healthy development of the market economy. Mr. Chengsi Zheng once gave an example of how dilution acts are unfair competition. For example, in the competition between Lekai and Kodak, if Kodak wants to harm Lekai, it could find a domestic publishing company or bribe a domestic writer to write in books, especially unified textbooks, that "Lekai is an alias for film." Then, when others buy film in stores, they might say they're buying Lekai, or even Kodak-branded Lekai, gradually diluting the Lekai trademark. This method is a relatively "advanced" form of unfair competition.
