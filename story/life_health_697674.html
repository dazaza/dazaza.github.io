Introduction to the best otorhinolaryngology expert outpatient hospital in Nanning: There is a membranous structure called the eardrum deep in the external auditory canal, which separates the outer ear from the middle ear. When sound waves from the outside world are transmitted to the eardrum, it vibrates along with the movement of the ossicles in the middle ear. The mechanical vibrations of the sound waves are then converted into vibrations of the inner ear lymphatic fluid, exciting the inner ear hair cells and converting the sound wave signals into nerve impulses that are transmitted to the brain. It can be seen that the vibration of the eardrum plays a very important role in the formation of hearing in the cerebral cortex. Under normal physiological conditions, the eardrum can only vibrate with sound waves when the air pressure on both sides of the eardrum is equal. The key structure that maintains equal pressure on both sides of the eardrum is commonly known as the balance tube. This is a long and curved pipe located between the middle ear and nasopharynx, known medically as the Eustachian tube. In adults, the Eustachian tube is about 35mm long, with the narrowest part being only 1mm wide. Under normal circumstances, the Eustachian tube is in a closed state, but when opening the mouth, swallowing, yawning, or singing, the surrounding muscles help open the pharyngeal end of the Eustachian tube. One of the functions of the Eustachian tube is to maintain the balance of pressure inside and outside the eardrum. Since the opening of the Eustachian tube in the nasopharynx is directly opposite the posterior part of the lower nasal turbinate of the nasal cavity, and its surface mucosa is connected to the nasal mucosa, when rhinitis occurs, the nasal mucosa swells, and the amount of nasal secretion increases, not only affecting nasal breathing, but the disease may also spread to the Eustachian tube. Swelling at the opening of the Eustachian tube in the nasopharynx may cause narrowing or even closure of the Eustachian tube, making it impossible to open even when opening the mouth, swallowing, yawning, or singing. Rhinitis causes partial or complete obstruction of the opening of the Eustachian tube on the nasopharyngeal side, leading to functional disorders and imbalance of pressure on both sides of the eardrum, affecting the conduction of sound waves by the tympanic membrane in the middle ear.

How do you know if the Eustachian tube is normal? Let's conduct an experiment: If you pinch your nostrils and hold your breath, after forcefully exhaling and then swallowing, you can hear a "bang" sound in both ears. This is due to the gas from the nasal cavity and pharynx entering the middle ear through the Eustachian tube and vibrating the eardrum. This proves that the nose and ear are interconnected and functioning normally; otherwise, there may be abnormalities.

Early treatment of rhinitis is essential

The dysfunction of the Eustachian tube is not limited to affecting the effective vibration of the eardrum. When persistent functional disorders occur, the pressure inside the eardrum becomes lower than the external pressure, resulting in negative pressure. At this point, the serum from the capillaries in the middle ear cavity seeps out into the middle ear cavity, forming middle ear effusion, which is known as secretory otitis media. If left untreated in time, fibrosis and other pathological changes may occur, making the initially exuded liquid in the middle ear increasingly viscous and eventually wrapping around the ossicles in the middle ear. In medicine, this is referred to as tympanosclerosis, similar to installing a tight ring around the ossicles, preventing them from vibrating effectively, leading to gradual hearing loss. In severe cases, surgery under a microscope is required to "release" the restricted ossicular chain and restore its function. If bacterial infections such as suppurative otitis media occur on the basis of middle ear exudation, it will worsen the condition, possibly leading to perforation of the eardrum and necrosis of the ossicles and other serious lesions. Of course, diseases affecting the function of the Eustachian tube are not limited to rhinitis but include other various diseases such as nasopharyngeal tumors, which need to be treated differently. Therefore, although rhinitis seems like a minor issue, it actually involves significant complexities. If you encounter a situation where a cold affects nasal breathing and hearing, it is recommended to visit the ENT department of a regular hospital for treatment under medical guidance. Depending on the condition, doctors may consider treatments such as tympanic massage, Eustachian tube inflation, and nasal mucosal constriction. Some patients may require tympanic tube placement or even tympanoplasty. In cases of bacterial infection, appropriate antibiotic therapy should be administered.
