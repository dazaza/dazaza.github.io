From Nokia to iPhone: Touchscreen Technology Changes History

by geekzhang on 2013-05-17 09:36:10

In 2006, the movie "The Touch" made the word 'touch' resonate deeply in people's hearts. However, even more astonishing than the movie was the iPhone introduced by Apple the following year. It revolutionized multi-touch technology and allowed fingertips to dance seamlessly across the screen. Subsequently, other manufacturers joined in with great enthusiasm, transforming small screens into larger ones, and then super-sized screens. Thus, touch control changed history and became a beloved feature of the era.

Nokia's single-point touch

More than a decade ago, touch technology had already entered our lives. However, at that time, it could not be called complete touch technology because it required external tools such as styluses or only supported single-point touch. The dream of fingertips dancing lightly seemed far away.

Single-point touch refers to being able to identify and support only one finger's touch or click at a time. If two or more points are touched simultaneously, it cannot respond correctly. Many computers in hospital and library lobbies use this type of touch technology, as do touch-screen phones, MP3 players, and digital cameras, which mostly adopt this single-point touch technology. They generally use resistive screens that require light pressure when operated. When mentioning single-point touch, we can't help but think of Nokia from the past.

Nokia once dominated the mobile phone industry. Although Symbian met its end, the fresh experience brought by single-point touch delighted many people. Do you remember the Nokia 5800? This was Nokia's most classic single-point touch phone in recent years, with the accompanying stylus becoming an important symbol of its existence. Due to rapid updates and replacements, single-point touch could no longer remain a lifelong companion for phones and was eventually eliminated by the times. Along with it went Nokia, unable to keep up with the pace of innovation.

The multi-touch loved by iPhones

What truly allows fingertips to dance is the even more magical multi-touch, surpassing single-point touch. Multi-touch is a technology realized through human-computer interaction techniques and hardware devices, enabling human-computer interaction operations without traditional input devices like mice and keyboards. Multi-touch technology can create a touch screen (on screens, desktops, walls, etc.) or a touchpad, allowing multiple points on the screen to interact with the computer simultaneously. It usually employs capacitive screens with superior sensitivity.

The industry generally believes that the breakout year for multi-touch was 2007. That year, Steve Jobs launched the iPhone, which everyone fell in love with. The iPhone took human-computer interaction experience to its peak, and the release of the iPhone 4 truly achieved perfect synchronization between fingertips and the screen. Your fingers can slide freely across the screen and perform multi-finger operations on the phone screen simultaneously. This is most evident in games developed later, such as Fruit Ninja. As Apple phones gained popularity, so did the Android system. Currently, almost all smartphones and tablets have achieved multi-touch functionality, leading to the rise of mobile internet and making smart terminals a powerful industry.

Win8's early growth and large-screen touch awaiting verification

The application of touch technology in mobile terminals has given PC and large-screen practitioners hope for the future. They thought that true fingertip dancing should involve achieving touch operation on both large and small screens. Thus, Win8 appeared with some anticipation. After Win8's launch last year, a batch of notebook manufacturers began releasing their Win8 products. These notebooks were endowed with the identity of reformers of the times, not only achieving full-screen touch but also enabling 360-degree flipping or separation of the body.

Some say this is a revolution in the touch-screen era, while others believe that Win8 merely represents an attempt that may not succeed. The latter market voice seems stronger. Recently, there have been reports that Microsoft investors are very dissatisfied with Win8's performance and even hope the CEO steps down sooner. It is understood that the touch operation of this system is not convenient. Many buyers, although accepting Win8 laptops, ultimately choose the traditional mode of Win8 instead of the seemingly innovative touch mode. The industry generally believes that after more than half a year on the market, these Win8 laptops have not occupied the main market, and Microsoft's innovation capabilities are weakening. It seems that large-screen touch for PCs is still immature, and touch technology for even larger screens like TVs requires further exploration.
