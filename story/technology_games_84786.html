by hexiaoyao on 2008-08-14 23:27:45

On August 13th, NetEase became the only choice for Blizzard's Battle.net, but perhaps NetEase was not Blizzard's first choice.

Perfect's Hesitation

Blizzard began large-scale negotiations for the Chinese agency rights of Battle.net in the first half of 2007, and the first "hot candidate" was Perfect World Company.

Blizzard initially valued Perfect World's development capabilities. Compared to Chinese companies like Shanda and The9 that relied on agency operations, Perfect World is a completely self-developed Chinese gaming company that has successfully developed three 3D online games consecutively, which Blizzard, who also values technology, highly recognized.

Secondly, Blizzard saw the opportunity of Perfect World's impending IPO. If Blizzard and Perfect were to successfully cooperate before the IPO, it would enhance Perfect's brand value, making it a company with both R&D and agency operations, thereby increasing its IPO value and giving Blizzard additional benefits.

The negotiations between Perfect and Blizzard ultimately stalled over price issues. Afterward, Perfect was busy with its NASDAQ listing and thus faded out of Blizzard's sight.

Some industry insiders believe that strategically, Perfect's abandonment of cooperation with Blizzard was quite regrettable, and it also showed Perfect's lack of experience in negotiating with Western major companies. Even if the price could not be agreed upon, there should have been moderate contact and significant attention maintained.

Ding Lei's Skill

After Perfect voluntarily withdrew, Tencent and NetEase became the top contenders.

At the start of the competition, the industry seemed to favor Tencent because Blizzard's technology complements Tencent's user base. However, what ultimately decided the outcome was the mindset of the two companies.

After experiencing issues with three products: Tang Dynasty, Dahua 3, and Tianxia 2, NetEase urgently needed external support to prop up its stock price. Therefore, Ding Lei no longer had the arrogance of "mocking EA" from years ago and changed his stance from "no agency, no export." On the other hand, Tencent's self-development was gradually entering a golden period with income records being broken frequently, so they were less enthusiastic about products like Battle.net that are difficult to profit from and come with harsh cooperation terms.

By the end of 2007, this competition had already produced a winner, and NetEase eventually signed a MOU with Blizzard. In the following months, both parties engaged in a tug-of-war over business details and government relations.

Currently, there are many speculations within the industry regarding the price at which NetEase acquired Battle.net. The latest version suggests that NetEase must achieve $20 million in sales in the first year, and the revenue-sharing ratio between the two parties is roughly 50-50. However, this claim has not been confirmed by either NetEase or Blizzard.

There are rumors that Ding Lei employed numerous tactics during the negotiations with Blizzard, such as deliberately showcasing his technical background as a programmer during meetings with Blizzard executives to gain favor. Moreover, Ding Lei adopted a strategy of waiting until the last moment in the negotiation details. For example, he first agreed to the conditions and signed an intention agreement, then negotiated specific issues such as piracy with Blizzard, forcing Blizzard to make concessions.

The Value of Battle.net

Industry insiders generally believe that profitability and piracy issues will determine the value of Battle.net in China.

Battle.net is a platform that provides online multiplayer services for Blizzard's single-player game users, and it belongs to free services. Before Battle.net officially entered China, there were already some platforms in the country that mimicked Battle.net. They had successfully devised ways to charge fees, such as monthly subscription fees for VIP users, SMS reminder fees, advertising fees, and revenue sharing through joint operations with other online game companies.

According to industry insiders' estimates, the largest similar platform in China, Hao Fang, has over ten million registered users, with approximately 600,000 to 700,000 active users, but its annual income is only around 10 million RMB. This is far from the profit level of an MMO game with a similar number of players. Thus, it's understandable why companies like Perfect and Tencent hesitated initially.

Selling legitimate games is an important way for Battle.net to make profits, but according to relevant estimates, although Blizzard's games like StarCraft and Warcraft III have at least 500,000 to 600,000 active users in China, almost all of them are pirated users. Even the upcoming StarCraft II may see pirated versions appear within a week of its release. Therefore, how to prevent piracy will be a crucial link in Battle.net's profitability.

Additionally, how to deal with "pirate Battle.nets" will also be a thorny issue. As long as those free third-party platforms exist, Battle.net's ability to charge freely will be limited, and piracy will be hard to control. Regarding the existing third-party platforms in China, even Blizzard's own attitude is unclear. In March 2008, I interviewed Blizzard's Executive Vice President Frank Pearce and the then Greater China General Manager Michael Fong about this issue. On one hand, Blizzard considered these platforms as "private servers," but on the other hand, Blizzard did not clearly indicate how to resolve this issue.

However, some industry insiders are not particularly impressed with the inherent value of Battle.net itself. They believe that NetEase acquiring Battle.net might just be the beginning of a larger strategic purpose, and it's possible that NetEase has its eyes on other Blizzard products.
