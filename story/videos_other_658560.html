by beetgk2958 on 2012-02-04 10:22:32

Group buying has been prevalent for a long time, especially in recent years the home furnishing industry has also taken large strides to join the group-buying ranks. Relevant surveys show that more than 80% of people have participated or want to participate in group buying when renovating their homes. However, while group buying drives a surge in sales performance, related complaints are also increasing day by day. Various issues such as counterfeit goods, additional charges, non-standard invoices, and difficult after-sales repairs have gradually come to light, becoming an unsightly scar on furniture and building materials group buying.

The editor learned from the city's Home Association that a large part of the complaints caused by group buying have fallen into a state of wrangling. This is mainly because consumers are unable to provide valid proof, and merchants firmly deny any fault, making it difficult to determine responsibility. According to the person in charge: "A Ms. Li complained to us that the toilet she bought through group buying did not match the goods finally delivered by the merchant. But when she made the purchase, the merchant informed her that no invoice or warranty card would be issued for group-buying products. Now without any evidence, it is impossible to verify the product number and batch, and the merchant is strongly arguing its case. All we can do now is mediate actively." He advises consumers that regardless of whether the product was purchased through group buying, consumers have the right to request that the merchant issue an invoice and warranty card. It must clearly note the material, origin, and grade of the product. Consumers should pay extra attention when inspecting the goods and keep the invoice and receipts for future returns or rights protection.

In fact, this kind of trick often appears in group-buying activities. Many merchants to increase product sales will offer ultra-low prices to attract consumers. After consumers rush to buy the goods, they often overlook the product's after-sales service. Staff at the city Consumer Association told reporters: "This behavior by merchants to a certain extent infringes on the consumer's right to know. In such cases, consumers have the right to demand that the merchant refund the full amount and file a complaint with us to protect other consumers' rights."

Refusal to issue tickets makes it hard to protect rights

"I remember that day it was raining, but there were still many people participating in the group buying. I squeezed back and forth and finally managed to order a brand floor that I had coveted for a long time. The discount price below 50% off let me boast to my colleagues for a long time," Mr. Ma recalled. What Mr. Ma didn't expect was that a week later he was told that due to the large number of group buys, the product was out of stock. If he wanted to pick up the goods quickly, he needed to pay a "rush fee." Reluctantly paying the rush fee, he was then required to pay installation fees much higher than the market rate. On the day the goods were delivered, Mr. Ma paid a large sum for unloading and carrying the goods upstairs. By the time everything was settled, Mr. Ma realized that he had saved a little money on buying the floor, but lost big on the additional fees.

It is understood that there are many "generic brands" in the furniture and building materials industry. A large portion of them do not have independent factories or stores. They often rely on so-called professional group-buying websites. These merchants do not need to provide relevant qualifications; they just need to pay a certain advertising fee to the website to start gathering group-buying members. For this, the staff of the Lin Product Quality Supervision Station advised consumers to first try to avoid online group-buying activities where physical items cannot be seen. Second, choose reputable group-buying websites and well-known furniture and building materials brands.

Low selling price, high additional fees

Mr. Zhao, with his years of online shopping experience, could be considered a network expert. However, this time the furniture bought through online group buying came as a great shock. "During the New Year, I saw a set of furniture on a professional group-buying website. Although it wasn't a famous brand, I particularly liked the shape of the wardrobe. The product workmanship was also relatively fine, and most importantly, the product had a significant discount based on the direct sales price. But within less than three months, the furniture had already appeared with varying degrees of cracking and deformation. After the manufacturer repaired it twice and still couldn't restore it to its original condition, they refused to accept returns or exchanges. When I inquired about the group-buying website, no one replied."
