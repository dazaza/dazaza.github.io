One, Water Dispenser Incident

When my daughter was nearing one year old, she became curious about everything, especially the water dispenser switch. My concerns were high as she could easily get hurt by it. While her grandma constantly watched and stopped her from touching it, I decided to take a different approach. The water dispenser switch wasn't that dangerous; once you let go, the water stops. Instead of always worrying, I thought it would be more useful if she got burned once and learned her lesson. Soon after, she did get burned and remembered that it was dangerous. For a long time, she didn't touch it again. Incidentally, I told her why we stopped her because this thing is dangerous, so remember, there's a reason behind stopping you from doing something. Even though one-year-olds might not understand everything, they can comprehend more than we think. Girls may understand earlier than boys.

Two, Socket Incident

When my daughter was almost two years old, she became interested in the wall sockets. So, I brought her cassette player and told her that there was electricity in the wall sockets, which the cassette player needed. When plugging in, you need to hold the plastic part at the back, only then is it safe. If you touch the front metal parts or plug in with wet hands, it could be fatal. My daughter had a certain respect for death, even if she didn't fully understand what would happen, but it was definitely something bad and scary. Before she turned two, she learned how to plug in the socket. From that day on, we often asked her to help us plug in various sockets. She would drag her cassette player around the house, listening in one room and then dragging it to another. She lived safely until now without ever getting electrocuted. I even once told her that if you see a metal wire and want to know if it's electrified, you can use the back of your hand to test it. If it's electrified, it will shock you, it might hurt a little and feel numb, but remember, never use the palm of your hand to grab it. Using the back of your hand will make you bounce off, no danger, but using your palm will make you grip it tighter, which could be fatal.

Three, Dressing and Getting Her Bottom Smacked

At the age of two, my daughter started dressing herself after taking baths. But after the novelty wore off, she found ways to be lazy. One day after her bath, she was halfway dressed and said to me, "Mom, can you dress me?" I happily replied, "Sure, but there's a condition. For every piece of clothing I help you with, you let me smack your bottom." She considered it and agreed. So, I helped her put on two socks in exchange for two smacks on her bottom. I still remember how sore my hand felt afterward. Afterward, her face looked very awkward, while I was indulgently praising how good her bottom felt when smacked, making it addictive... The next day, when dressing, I cheerfully volunteered to help her and reiterated how much I enjoyed smacking her bottom, hoping she'd give me another chance to enjoy it. Of course, until now, I haven't been able to find another opportunity to enjoy it.

Four, Going Home Alone

My daughter was two and a half years old and had been attending nursery school for some time. She deeply understood the rule of stopping at red lights and going at green lights.

Once, while shopping with her in a busy area, I told her that I had to go to my workplace and asked her if she knew the way home and whether she could go home alone. Of course, she thought it was no problem, so I followed her from afar to see how she would go home. My daughter seriously waited for the traffic lights at each intersection and even waited until the sixth green light before crossing. Later, I found out that she not only looked at the green light but also checked if any cars might be running the red light, confirming 100% safety before crossing.

In fact, when children take responsibility for themselves, they are far more responsible than adults think.

Five, Bugs Are Very Adorable Creatures

I am very afraid of those squishy meat worms, even though I know they're actually harmless, but I'm just scared.

Children don't know fear, and my selfish intention was to teach my daughter not to be afraid so she could protect me from bugs in the future. So, one day with a worm-infested peach, I told her that worm-infested peaches are sweeter, and those worms are very adorable, fat and white, and they crawl around. My daughter asked if the worms would bite her, and I replied that these worms are vegetarians, and since she eats meat, she's too old and tough for them to bite.

My daughter believed it completely, holding the worm in her hand and watching it crawl back and forth, from her palm to the back of her hand, left hand to right hand, and vice versa. During meals, she placed the worm on the table and watched it eat, bringing it back if it crawled away to continue observing. I, I, I screamed silently, unable to show fear, because if I showed even a hint of fear, my daughter would have an irrational fear of harmless worms forever. I can't recall how I managed to finish my meal that day.

She began bringing all sorts of green caterpillars home to raise, and eventually, my nerves became exceptionally strong. I no longer feared these fat and soft caterpillars and even discovered that some green caterpillars had round heads and looked quite cartoonish. We hoped they would turn into butterflies, but in reality, all the green caterpillars eventually turned into moths of various sizes.
