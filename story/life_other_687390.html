Humbly embracing happiness

by huiming on 2012-03-02 15:31:07

Happiness in Humility

Happiness doesn't require wielding power over the court, being fabulously wealthy, indulging in endless revelry, or making a grand splash. A person, despite living very "humbly," can still feel very happy.

In the neighborhood where I live, there is a young couple who sell pickled foods. Every morning, the couple pushes a handcart out of their home. The cart is filled with glass jars, and inside these jars are all sorts of delicious pickled foods: pickled radish, pickled green beans, pickled cucumber, pickled papaya, pickled garlic, pickled chili peppers, pickled cabbage, and more. By evening, when the couple pushes the handcart back, it's clear that many of the jars have been emptied of their contents. Whether leaving or returning, the couple always wears smiles on their faces, and I have never seen them look sad.

In a corner around the bend outside the neighborhood gate, there is an elderly couple who recycle waste paper and other discarded items. I see them every day as I go to and from work. Sometimes, they are busy tying up bundles of waste paper with ropes and sorting the recyclables. Other times, they scratch each other’s backs, or the old man lies on a broken recliner, eyes closed, basking in the sun, while the old woman sits on a small stool beside him, incessantly chatting. Their expressions are tranquil, perhaps because they have experienced too many storms in life, and their hearts are now calm and untroubled.

These two couples are as ordinary as can be. Their status might even be described as somewhat "humble," yet in their eyes, life seems to be something full of flavor. Each day is so plain, and every plain day is worth living through well.

For some reason, whenever I see them, I can't help but think of a short story titled "Breakfast" by American writer John Steinbeck. The plot of the story is simple: a passerby, rushing along a cold winter's dawn, encounters a cotton-picking family who invites him to warm himself by the fire and share breakfast with them. After eating, the passerby continues on his way. If you only consider the plot, this is truly a bland, tasteless story, and one might even question whether it qualifies as a novel. However, Steinbeck uses a great deal of ink to describe the process of eating breakfast and the feelings of both the traveler and the family, giving the story a sense of "atmosphere." The story reveals that this family is actually quite poor, having just finished picking cotton for 12 days. They are grateful to be able to eat bacon and black bread and drink bacon grease and hot coffee. A complete stranger, walking alone in the biting cold wind, is warmly invited to share what they consider a "feast." No wonder, when the traveler smells the aroma of frying bacon and baking bread, he exclaims from the heart, "This is the most pleasant and warm smell in the world." At the end of the story, Steinbeck writes, "That's all there is to it. It's obvious why it's enjoyable, but it possesses an incomparable beauty, and every time I recall it, a warm current surges through my heart." I believe that the "incomparable beauty" Steinbeck refers to is probably the beauty of the ordinary. Although it is ordinary, it radiates the warmth of human kindness, shines with the brilliance of humanity, and expresses gratitude for life. Everyone has only one chance at life, and no matter how ordinary life may be, each day is worth cherishing and enjoying. Perhaps, this is the simple and endearing happiness of "ordinary people."

Happiness doesn't require wielding power over the court, being fabulously wealthy, indulging in endless revelry, or making a grand splash. A person, despite living very "humbly," can still feel very happy. Of course, this state of mind—being happily humble—is not something everyone possesses, nor is it something everyone can fully understand. "Alas! Without such people, who would I align myself with?"
