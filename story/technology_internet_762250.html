"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 14:29:57

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been severely affected by the departure of senior executives, suffered another blow. On July 17th, it was confirmed by an insider from Yougou that Zhang Xiaojun, COO of Yougou, will be leaving and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou, Zhang Xiaojun's departure is particularly significant, indicating that Yougou is indeed facing a serious crisis.

Let’s first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, the parent company of Yougou:

Belle cautiously ventured into e-commerce several years ago, developing steadily through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed via Taoshow.com). Led by veteran group member Xie Yunli, a not-so-lavish e-commerce team began by selling products under Belle's own brands. They then introduced exclusive online models and subsequently launched new online brands. This development path was considered a successful case of traditional brand companies entering the e-commerce sector before 2011, surpassing Li-Ning in its solid approach.

However, with the rapid rise of e-commerce platforms like Letao and Haolemai after securing over $100 million in funding in 2010, Belle Group felt the pressure. Concerned about being overly reliant on these channels and recognizing their own significant advantages in the e-commerce field, they decided to act. These advantages included:

1. Numerous brands: In addition to Belle's own major women's shoe brands, the agency-managed international sports brands such as Adidas and Nike were experiencing astonishing growth in online sales.

2. Easier category expansion: For Belle Group, known for its women's shoes and agent-managed famous sports brands, achieving absolute market advantage and status through multi-brand and multi-category strategies became a primary focus. Expanding offline stores proved too slow, while their ongoing e-commerce business could easily expand brands but struggled to quickly add categories.

3. Financial advantage: During cooperation with e-commerce enterprises, Belle realized that e-commerce is a capital-intensive business. With financial strength, Belle could develop independently without relying on external capital, potentially outlasting many vertical e-commerce companies dependent on capital.

4. Talent advantage: Through years of e-commerce practice, Belle had cultivated a highly professional and execution-focused e-commerce team capable of scaling up or even exceeding expectations.

5. Supply chain advantage: This thrilled Belle internally and attracted top executives from other e-commerce platforms. For e-commerce, supply chain management—covering product development, production, warehousing, delivery, and after-sales service—is crucial, often determining survival beyond just capital. In a "business-heavy, internet-light" traditional enterprise, this is seen as a competitive edge over pure e-commerce companies.

With these advantages in mind, Belle e-commerce decided to go all-in. First, they acquired the double-barrelled domain name yougou.com,

abandoning the slightly copycat “topshoes.cn” Taoshow website in favor of redirections. All distribution sites were cut off, although direct-operated third-party stores on Taobao and Tmall were retained but with reduced investment and attention. They recruited high-profile executives from JD.com and Vancl as CMO and COO respectively, paired with a veteran group member as CEO, forming a golden triangle. Their aim was to create a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing a multi-brand strategy while expanding into clothing and other categories, realizing the dream of an impossible-to-achieve department store empire offline!

All of this sounded promising, but every dream must face the harsh realities of implementation. The reality was:

1. Higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Based in Shenzhen, Belle couldn’t attract more top technical talents, and outsourcing technical services were scarce there. Technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and reliable data. Independent B2C involves dynamic technology, unlike the relatively static ERP systems previously used. Continuous changes remain a hurdle for many traditional enterprises.

2. Increased management difficulty with category expansion: Someone once said that excelling at women's shoes doesn't guarantee success with men's shoes, something Belle likely experienced when expanding into men's footwear. Expanding into women's wear and accessories within a year would undoubtedly present backend challenges. Traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous planning may find this rapid approach difficult, especially when trying to replicate the special period successes of Vancl or the internal understanding of JD.com. Despite having Vancl's COO and JD.com's CMO, whether Belle can execute with "exaggerated costs" remains questionable.

3. Organizational structure: Many people fail to understand the differences between e-commerce and traditional enterprises. Simply put, aspects like department setup (organizational structure), approval processes, finance and budgeting, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, and procedural systems form the so-called "mechanism" of a company. The conflict arises as e-commerce tries to adapt its "system" to the traditional side, while the traditional team struggles to fully grasp the e-commerce "system," leading to inefficiency and instability in the execution team. Ultimately, the chairman or board might say: "E-commerce and traditional businesses each have their strengths and weaknesses; the future lies in integration, so you must integrate in these areas..." If this "integration without harmony" occurs, it leads to so-called management innovation, resulting in a "CEO rotation system" appearing even in a company like Belle!

Initially, it seemed that Yougou, Belle's e-commerce platform, was far ahead. The senior executives were expected to enjoy the glory of a spin-off IPO, becoming a model of success for many traditional enterprises venturing into e-commerce. Instead, they are now grappling with these critical issues.

After all, whether they are long-time veterans who grew with Belle or e-commerce professionals recruited from outside, it is clear that their departures aren't due to lack of ability. When VPs from JD.com return to continue their impressive performance, or VPs from Vancl dare to start their own ventures, the question remains: Are these the three hard injuries mentioned above? Or are these not just Belle's hard injuries, but also those of many traditional enterprises attempting to enter the e-commerce arena?
