[Omega Watches Constellation] 106 Secrets to Networking for Success

by frttptk02903 on 2012-03-08 16:15:03

On the path to success, networking is more important than knowledge. Developing interpersonal relationships should be your top priority. The book "Never Eat Alone" introduces the rules of networking for the 21st century. It includes many practical techniques that allow you to thrive through interpersonal relationships. To preserve the essence, here are 106 tips summarized from the book. Practicing these skills in real life will help you become a successful networking master.

1. Help others succeed. The essence of networking is continuously helping others succeed in various forms. Share your knowledge and resources, time and energy, friends and connections, sympathy and care, thus continuously providing value to others while improving your own value.

2. Strive to give more than you receive. People will contact you because you provide them with value. So focus more on others than yourself.

3. Don't hold back. Don't think friendships are limited. This is an investment that grows over time.

4. The key to success is generosity. In networking, what prevails is not convenience but generosity.

5. Understand your life goals. The intersection of your abilities and desires is your inner passion. What makes you happiest? What makes you feel time fly by?

6. Clearly know what you want. The clearer you know what you want, the easier you'll find ways to achieve it.

7. Get into the habit of setting goals. The simplest way to stand out is to set goals. Turn dreams into life goals, then break them down into smaller goals.

8. Set plans for developing interpersonal relationships. Building a network takes time, and your plan should include three parts:

1. Your 3-year goal and progress every 3 months.

2. List people who can help you achieve each goal.

3. How to connect with the people listed in point 2.

Once you set your goals, post them where you can see them often.

9. Build your personal "advisory board." Find knowledgeable individuals willing to responsibly help you. They are your "think tank."

10. Build a good network before you need it. Keep in touch as early as possible before you realize you need someone. Treat these people as friends, not potential clients.

11. Maintain good contact with those you know. At first, focus on the people in your current network.

12. Be brave. Courage is very magical. Two people with similar talents may develop differently simply due to their level of confidence.

13. Be willing to ask for help. Being willing to ask can create opportunities. You should be just as willing to ask others for help as you are to help others. Remember, prepare for the worst case scenario where others might say "no."

14. Consider the harvest when facing fear. Talking naturally to strangers can be daunting because you might fail. It's risky, but to gain, you must take risks. Doing nothing leads only to mediocrity.

15. Respect others. Respect everyone, regardless of status.

16. Be transparent. An open heart is a beneficial and popular attitude.

17. Make friends. Skilled networkers aren’t “building networks,” they’re “making friends.” Extensive sowing is a natural result, not a carefully designed goal.

18. Prepare thoroughly. Who do you want to meet? How do you meet them? What impression do they have of you after meeting you? These factors cannot be ignored.

19. Understand the people you interact with. If you sufficiently understand the people you interact with, you can deeply engage in their field and professionally converse with them. You can easily earn praise. Find a rich and deep common ground, and afterward, you can easily communicate and leave a lasting impression.

20. Understand others' interests. Love the things they love, and you'll quickly become part of their lives.

21. Never eat alone. Eating is a very easy way to exchange ideas. Eating with others is an effective method of networking.

22. Manage your information effectively. Effective information management is crucial. If you are organized, focused, and persistent, no one will leave your network.

23. Create lists. Classify according to your objectives (such as potential customers, potential employers) and organize your lists. Not only list relevant organizations, but also list influential people within those organizations.

24. Recognize authorities in your professional field. To achieve your goals, you need to know who is the best in your field. List current authorities.

25. List people you already know. List people you already know: relatives, university classmates, past classmates, previous teachers, former colleagues...

26. Organize information flexibly. Your method of managing lists should be flexible. By location, by industry, by closeness...

27. Use others' contact lists to supplement your own. Other people's lists are your resources.

28. Establish a "people I hope to meet" list. Your list classification can include "people I aspire to meet," high-level individuals you hope to meet in the future.

29. Contact complete strangers (Cold Call). When you need to call strangers, you will inevitably be somewhat afraid. Just push through. Only think about succeeding. Meeting new people is a challenge and an opportunity.

30. Persist. If you contact others and they don't respond, continue contacting them. Take the initiative. Even if it seems aggressive.

31. Contact people with indirect connections (Warm Call). Use the following four rules:

1. Express reliability: mention related individuals or organizations.

2. Propose value: what can you do for them?

3. Indicate urgency and convenience: most of the time, the sole purpose of a cold call is to schedule a meeting.

4. Prepare a negotiation plan: start high and leave room for negotiation.

32. View gatekeepers as allies, not enemies. Gatekeepers should be respected. Don't irritate them.

33. Never disappear. When building a social network, remember: first, never disappear. Disappearing is worse than failing.

34. Strive to remain visible and active. Fill your social, meeting, and event schedules. You must remain visible and active in your initial circle of friends and relationships.

35. Combine multiple tasks. To successfully connect with others, you need to make efforts, but this doesn't mean spending a lot of time. You can arrange events together to save time, such as inviting all the people you want to meet at once.

36. Find enjoyment. Networking is enjoyable and not a waste of time.

37. Share your passion. Sharing interests is the foundation of any relationship. When you're genuinely interested in something, it's contagious.

38. Emphasize the quality of time. Friendships are built on the quality, not the quantity, of time spent together.

39. Follow-up determines success or failure. After meeting someone you want to build a relationship with, let them remember you. Follow-up is key.

40. Follow up immediately. Within 12 to 24 hours after meeting them, follow up. Email is a quick way.

41. Don't forget to follow up again. After a month, send another email. Stay in touch.

42. Spend time communicating with people at meetings. Meetings are often misunderstood as places to seek opinions. Wrong! The only advantage of meetings is that they provide an opportunity to meet like-minded people.

43. Be the organizer of the meeting. Don't just be a participant; be the organizer. Prepare information in advance: plan who to meet, how to meet, and where to meet.

44. Speak publicly. Speaking is one of the simplest and most effective ways to make others remember you.

45. Follow up as much as possible. Don't always stick with your best friend at every meeting. Follow up with as many others as possible.

46. Stay in touch with networking experts. Some people know far more people than we do. These people are the core of various networks. If you can befriend these people, your connection with thousands of others will only require going through one person.

47. Know as many acquaintances as possible. Usually, the most important people in our network are those acquaintances. Why? Because our closest friends and we know roughly the same things. Therefore, the more people you know, the more powerful you become.

48. Meet people from different fields. We not only know thousands of people, but also thousands spread across different fields.

49. Exchange networks with others. The most effective way to expand your circle of friends is to connect your circle with others' circles.

50. Establish your organizing committee. Politicians have "organizing committees": gathering people working in various fields. If you want to more broadly engage with the world, you need to find a central figure to act as your "organizing member."

51. Improve your "language fluency." "Language fluency:" the ability to confidently communicate with anyone in any situation. This is a common characteristic of many successful people. The ability to get along well with others is crucial for personal advancement.

52. Be yourself. To leave a lasting impression and stand out, being unique is key. To ensure uniqueness, be yourself. Being yourself has charm. Your uniqueness is your strength.

53. "Obey." The power of obedience is often overlooked. Obedience is highly effective in casual conversations.

54. Speak sincerely. Once you realize the best way to break the silence is to speak sincerely, initiating conversation won't seem so terrifying anymore.

55. Learn to use non-verbal communication. Others can subconsciously decide whether they like you within 10 seconds of meeting you. Such judgments are based on non-verbal communication.

56. Prepare topics to discuss. Have topics ready to discuss. Pay attention to current events and cultivate some interests and hobbies.

57. Learn to listen. First seek to understand others, then to be understood.

58. Always remember others' names. There's nothing more pleasant to hear than one's own name.

59. Be sincere. Without a doubt, to be seen as special, you need to make others feel special in your eyes.

60. Try to discover others' motivations. You can find someone's motivation during your first interaction. Their motivation generally falls into three categories: making money, finding love, changing the world.

61. Help others fulfill their deepest desires. Actively helping others solve their critical problems can strengthen relationships significantly.

62. Make yourself indispensable to others. Being indispensable is very important. To be indispensable, you need to constantly convey your information, social connections, and goodwill to as many people as possible.

63. Think about how you can help everyone around you succeed. When someone tells you they've encountered a problem, think about solutions. Solutions come from your experience, knowledge, and your friends and assistants.

64. Offer help proactively. Don't wait for others to ask; offer help proactively.

65. Spread knowledge. Even when you have little financial or networking resources, gaining benefits through networking is still feasible. This involves spreading knowledge within your network.

66. Show interest in others' successes. Showing interest in others' successes can make you more successful within two months. Alternatively, spend two years making others interested in your success. (Dale Carnegie)

67. Regular small contacts. 80% of relationships are maintained through small contacts. Continuously contact, contact, contact... never stop. Continuously add fuel to your network to ensure it does not wither.

68. Repeat, repeat again. A very key way to stay in someone's mind is repetition.

69. Establish a scoring system. Establishing a scoring system can make maintaining relationships easier. For example:

1. "Level 1" - contact at least once a month.

2. "Level 2" - make at least a phone call or send an email every quarter.

3. "Level 3" - contact at least once a year.

70. Incorporate small contacts into your schedule. You need to incorporate small contacts into your work scope.

71. Use your personal name whenever possible.

72. Don't forget birthdays. Birthdays should be your best opportunity for contact.

73. Eat with "main forces." We establish relationships with older, wiser, and more experienced people. These are the "main forces." Eating with them helps recharge.

74. Be interesting. Every person you meet will ask themselves a question similar to: "Is it worth spending an hour having lunch with this person?"

75. Be content-rich. Content is the professionalism of knowledge. It’s what makes you stand out. Develop your unique perspective.

76. Learn and share your content without reservation. To be different from others, relentlessly learn, share, and distribute your content.

77. Obtain others' content. Sometimes you can easily turn others' ideas into your own content. Then share and utilize these ideas first.

78. Provide your own content. At other times, you need to generate content yourself. You need to understand various information and uniquely connect them.

79. Capture attention. Once you have resonance, it captures attention. What you need to provide is simple: lively stories.

80. Become an expert. The most common way to become a content creator is to be an expert. Do what experts do: teach others your professional knowledge, write for them, and speak to them.

81. Teach what you want to learn. Teaching others is the best way for you to learn and understand.

82. Use stories to express your content. Powerful content expressed through stories helps achieve goals. When telling stories, use your emotions to convince skeptics.

83. Build a personal brand. Strong brands are highly competitive, but they are always based on the value provided rather than the description style. A good personal brand can do three things: make others believe it's trustworthy, extraordinary, and reliable.

84. Make yourself unique. Your brand expresses what you can offer, why you are special, and tells others why they should stay connected with you.

85. Continuously add value. In becoming a brand, you need to focus on what you do and continuously add value. This means you need to constantly exceed expectations.

86. Establish a personal brand logo. When others hear or read your name, what do you want them to think of? Your brand logo should include all the words people use when mentioning you.

87. Package your brand. To look valuable, you need to appear polished and professional. So why not build a personal website?

88. Promote your brand. If you don't promote yourself, no one else will. Your success depends on how others recognize your work, which relies on the quality of your work.

89. Stay in touch with the media. Start keeping in touch with the media now, not when you have a story to tell them.

90. Promote message content, not the author. What you promote to the world should be your sense of mission, not your ego.

91. Co-author works. If your writing skills are lacking, you can co-author with others. Through co-authorship, people you don't usually contact can exponentially increase your network.

92. Write articles. Writing articles can greatly promote your career development and make you quickly noticed.

93. Keep writing to become a good writer. To become a good writer: "Write, write again. When you finish writing, keep writing."

94. Associate with celebrities. Are you only associating with unknown individuals? You need to shift your focus to some important figures, which can make a big difference in your life and the lives of those around you.

95. Build trust. Trust is the key to getting close to these authorities. Let others trust that you have no hidden motives when contacting them. Let others trust that you treat them as ordinary people, not stars.

96. Join associations. Now there are various associations. If you want to be shoulder-to-shoulder with influential people, you need to become a participant first.

97. Establish your own club. Sometimes you want to join some valuable clubs but can't for various reasons. Why not start your own club? Develop your own promotion plan, establish a new organization, and invite people you want to meet to join your organization.

98. Stay humble. Arrogance is a disease; it makes you forget true friends and the importance of friendship. As you progress, stay humble. Help others progress with you, even surpass you.

99. Reflect on your past. Regularly reflect on your past and maintain contact with those who were important to you from a young age.

100. Find several mentors. Find mentors who are talented, experienced, and willing to invest time in helping you improve. Don't decide your career direction solely based on salary and reputation.

101. Learn from everyone. Among three people walking, there must be my teacher. You have many people around you to learn from.

102. Establish a good mentor-mentee relationship. Successful mentor-mentee relationships require results and enthusiasm. Results mean you can show your mentor the impact they have on you. Enthusiasm means your mentor will invest in your improvement.

103. Find several mentees. As you strive to move forward, be sure to turn around and help others. You will learn a lot from your students.

104. Stick to principles. Networking should move forward, not backward. Interacting with others must not violate your values.

105. Don't try to balance your career and personal life. You should provide the same value to people in different relationships. Instead of dividing life into work and personal life.

106. Build intimate friendships. How many people can walk into your home, open the fridge, and find something to eat? Having intimate friends will make you happy.
