Microsoft Loses $18 Billion in Value After Nokia Deal

by anonymous on 2013-09-08 19:42:45

Since Microsoft announced the acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business for $7.2 billion, Microsoft's market value has dropped by $18 billion, offsetting the increase in stock prices since Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer announced his retirement.

Securities company Atlantic Equities LLP stated that Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business further confirms Ballmer's strategy: Microsoft will transition to consumer electronics business. Therefore, the next CEO of Microsoft will have little room to push Microsoft in other directions.

Microsoft is trying to achieve growth in a market dominated by Apple and Google. In reality, the returns from the device business are far less than those from the enterprise software business. Nokia's CEO Stephen Elop will return to Microsoft in this deal. Investment company Sanford Bernstein said that Elop will be one of the strongest contenders for the next CEO of Microsoft, and Microsoft will develop its smartphone business in the long term. However, some investors believe that Microsoft's strategy is wrong.

Tim Schwartz, manager of Schwartz Investment Advisory which holds Microsoft shares, said: "I was not surprised by this deal. The market feels that this will be the continuation of Microsoft's old management and old mindset."

The Next CEO is Restricted

Ballmer became Microsoft's CEO in 2000. After he announced on August 23 that he would retire within 12 months, Microsoft's stock price surged by 7.3%. Some investors hope that his successor will be an outsider who can take bold actions to reverse the trend of Microsoft's declining market value, such as splitting the Xbox and other consumer businesses and focusing on enterprise software and services.

However, news of Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business made investors speculate that Elop, a former Microsoft executive, will become the next CEO of Microsoft. He will continue to implement Ballmer's strategy, conducting both enterprise and consumer businesses. Since the announcement of the deal until this Wednesday, Microsoft's stock price fell by 6.6%, and its market value dropped to $260 billion.

Chris Hickey, analyst at Atlantic Equities in London, said: "A considerable number of investors hope that Microsoft will weaken its consumer business and focus on more profitable and predictable enterprise business. However, this acquisition clearly indicates that the possibility of Microsoft doing so is very small, and the next CEO will be constrained."

Key Market

Microsoft stated that through the acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business, the company will generate more revenue through Windows Phone phones and develop better products faster in some key markets. Microsoft believes that this will bring long-term revenue and profit opportunities to shareholders.

Brad Smith, Microsoft's General Counsel, said: "Mobile phones are the most personalized products people currently use. The success of the mobile phone business is important for the success of tablets and PCs. We need to act faster."

Rick Sherlund, analyst at Nomura Holdings in New York, said that the timing of Microsoft's announcement of this acquisition makes it difficult for active investor ValueAct to take measures to oppose. Last week, ValueAct reached an agreement with Microsoft to gain a seat on Microsoft's board next year and meet regularly with part of Microsoft's board and management. ValueAct will no longer seek or participate in proxy fights.

A source said that ValueAct hopes Microsoft focuses on enterprise software and internet-based cloud computing services instead of consumer technology. ValueAct's spokesperson has not yet commented on this transaction of Microsoft's. Sherlund said: "ValueAct has reached a cease-fire agreement. It is unclear how ValueAct will respond to shareholder dissatisfaction with this deal."

Mark Moerdler, analyst at Bernstein in New York, said that shareholder dissatisfaction is not due to the deal itself but the significance behind it. "This may not be the end of Microsoft's investment in the consumer market. And Wall Street may think that the consumer market is not the best way for Microsoft to utilize its resources."

More Acquisitions

Jason Maynard, analyst at Wells Fargo, said that if Microsoft wants to execute Ballmer's strategy of fully integrating services and devices, then Microsoft needs to obtain more applications and services. This is similar to what Apple did before.

Sources said that BlackBerry's advantages in the enterprise market still attract Microsoft. Since the announcement of Microsoft's and Nokia's deal, BlackBerry's stock price has risen by 6.2%. Data shows that the price of Microsoft's acquisition of Nokia's mobile phone business is equivalent to 0.42 times the business's revenue over the past 12 months. Based on this valuation multiple, BlackBerry's enterprise value is $4.8 billion. Currently, BlackBerry's market value is $2.8 billion.

However, Chris Hickey of Atlantic Equities believes that if Microsoft acquires BlackBerry, then the problems of Microsoft's consumer business will again become the focus. The difficulties faced by this business in competing with Apple and Google are likely to continue.

Nandan Amladi, analyst at Deutsche Bank in New York, said that compared to Microsoft's previous cooperation with Nokia, this deal does not bring much value to Microsoft. He said: "The two companies already had a cooperative relationship, so people wonder why Microsoft acquired an asset with declining revenue and challenged profit margins."

Todd Lowenstein, fund manager at HighMark Capital Management, said that Microsoft paid a high price for Nokia's assets, "exceeding the market's most optimistic estimate of the value of this asset." He pointed out: "This deal somewhat binds the next CEO. It feels like Microsoft lacks capital discipline, paying too high a price in the deal and trying to pursue development in areas where it does not have core competencies."
