The Love of Stones

In the second year of college, Miss "Corn" fell in love with "Soybean." He was Corn's teacher, an oil painting instructor who liked to paint fruits and women. He had a flat-top haircut, a small mustache, sometimes wore a fisherman's hat, rarely smiled, and even seemed a bit aloof. But all of this, to Corn, was a kind of charm. Corn, who considered herself rather cold and proud, finally knocked on his door one moonlit night: "Teacher, I'm so cold..."

Without saying a word, Soybean took her inside and gently closed the door behind them. Their age difference was 10 years, which wasn't a problem. The real issue was that at the end of the 1980s, "teacher-student romance" was not allowed. Therefore, their relationship could only be kept secret, late at night, somewhat like the meetings between a female ghost and a scholar in the stories from "Strange Tales from a Chinese Studio." This was exciting for Corn; she enjoyed this kind of sneaky romance. Choosing what kind of boyfriend is actually choosing a certain way of life, ultimately reflecting one's personal taste. Corn was always self-assured. In her view, though she wasn't the class beauty, she was undoubtedly the most unique flower. So, in class or in the studio, she didn't miss any opportunity to have an electric connection with Soybean through their eyes. Indeed, her figure and lively, enthusiastic personality were unmatched. Soybean was an introverted man; he liked this fiery girl. He usually gave special attention to this student, and the students' eyes were sharp; everyone knew he liked Corn. But what made Corn grind her teeth in hatred was that this cold-blooded man never directly confessed or gave her even a hint of unusual attention. At most, he would stay a few minutes longer behind Corn, intently watching her paint. Every time this happened, Corn would feel a faint fragrance around her body, and the teacher's breathing behind her would become increasingly coarse. This was an enticing yet startling signal. After receiving this signal multiple times, Corn finally made the bold move to knock on his door under the moonlight that night. Thinking back to that night, Corn still feels fear now, realizing how brave she could be, much like Liu Hulan, the female hero. Only, she faced the killer called "love," a male teacher named "Soybean."

Their midnight love was splendid. Soybean gradually relaxed, and the wildness he had hidden in his heart finally erupted. And Corn's boundless tenderness also blossomed under his influence, misty and ethereal like clouds and fog.

At this moment, a fierce knocking on the door shattered their dream. Leaders from the art department and school security officers, each holding a flashlight, stood solemnly at Soybean's door, like a monument. The next day, Corn was publicly criticized, and Teacher Soybean was required to write a self-examination statement and make deep reflections, otherwise, he might be expelled.

Corn was extremely afraid because her father had been beaten to death during a previous "movement." At the same time, she felt wronged. She and Soybean had never done anything too out of line. They believed that "the final defense" should be completed on their wedding night. Their love was only in the "prelude" part. They were still innocently browsing through the first page of love. They painted each other's nudes and used each other as models, but they both truly adhered to the storm within their hearts. However, the relevant leaders did not believe it. A young man and woman locked in the same room at midnight—if nothing "happened," even ghosts wouldn't believe it.

This was a "miscarriage of justice," concerning a pure midnight love. At this point, Corn decided to drop out. Getting into university at that time was very difficult, but Corn wanted to protect this love and Soybean's career. She applied to withdraw from school, meaning she was no longer a college student, more precisely, she was no longer Soybean's student. Thus, their relationship was no longer a "teacher-student romance," and they could come out from underground, openly loving each other. By changing roles, they could still happily share different breaths with each other. Unexpectedly, the relevant authorities readily agreed to Corn's request. Soybean opposed Corn making an "unnecessary sacrifice," but he couldn't stop her. Finally, they were together again. Corn rented a bungalow outside and took on some tutoring jobs. Life must go on. She continued to pretend to be a college student in that city because she couldn't tell her mother the bad news about being expelled.

This incident concluded satisfactorily. But soon after, their love faced a crisis. Public love, for Soybean, lost its fighting power. He no longer held Corn while talking about his extensive knowledge. Instead, every time Corn came to see him with excitement, he would light a cigarette and smoke silently on the side, almost speechless. Each time, Corn had to take the initiative to ask him questions, and he would lazily answer. Initially, Corn thought he was disheartened due to the impact of this "incident," but half a month later, Corn keenly sensed an ominous atmosphere. He no longer loved her. He even stopped kissing her. Even when he opened his mouth, it was cold. Unable to bear it, that night, Corn shook his shoulders with both hands and stared into his eyes: "Tell me, don't lie. Have you lost feelings for me?" Soybean nodded. Corn slowly let go of him, tears streaming down her face. One year and six months of love sprinting ended like this, like a dream, the first half a beautiful dream, the second half a nightmare. Waking up, she realized she had nothing left.

Corn walked until dawn in the cold wind of midnight, feeling pain, then guilt. Why did she make so many sacrifices? But in the end, the person she loved turned into a thief so quickly, a thief of emotions who stole her heart and then discarded it casually.

The best medicine for healing such wounds could only be time.

Corn understood that she couldn't fall apart; she had to stand up. So, she worked hard, filling every day tightly with various jobs: tutoring, advertising design, drawing portraits for passersby, painting cultural shirts, and even knitting sweaters... Once, while selling cultural shirts, a female college student brought a stone and asked Corn to help add a few strokes with a brush, turning it into a little gift. Since her boyfriend was born under the tiger zodiac sign, she hoped Corn could make the stone look "tigerish."

This incident greatly moved Corn. Yes, why not open a gift shop specifically targeting couples in love? Each gift would be different, with the raw material basically being small stones. Then, using her painting skills, adding lines and colors to the stones, wouldn't that make each one a vivid and unique love token? Putting them in gift boxes, each stone could really sing!

Beautiful stones singing songs of love. Saying it, Corn immediately started doing it. After a month of preparation, Corn's "The Love of Stones" boutique officially opened. Business was good, and her mood improved as well. Though the scale was small, she put a lot of effort into it. Corn used this beautiful work to heal the old wounds in her heart, earning money while beautifying her own life and utilizing her artistic talents. That was great.

Corn is 35 years old now and still single. She has created tens of thousands of tokens of love, given tens of thousands of cold stones warmth and spirit, and sent blessings to tens of thousands of people. Each stone she personally picked from the riverbed in the wild, natural, unpolluted, each with its own uniqueness. If she added a heart to these silent stones, they became precious. Looking at them was like reading countless love stories, and she couldn't help but smile inwardly.

Frequent smiling inwardly is Corn's current life. In her store, there is a signature shelf, composed of the characters "female" and "son" forming the character "good." She said, if men and women are always "good" together, wouldn't that be wonderful!

If fate allows, she doesn't exclude the possibility of love returning, including from the painter named Soybean. Even at 100 years old, Corn would not reject love, provided her teeth haven't fallen out because she likes to use her teeth to test whether the ring her boyfriend gives her is the purest and truest gold.
