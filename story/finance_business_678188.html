Emotional Diary of a 12-Year-Old Girl

by lp49x7 on 2012-02-27 12:18:50

As I'm about to graduate from elementary school, I've realized that I'm already 12 years old. Looking at the picture frame, seeing that little girl with round cheeks and an innocent face, I suddenly realize how adorable she was back then. But the girl in the mirror now has worries, her small heart slowly swelling. How I wish I could hold onto those carefree times! Thus, this book came to be, recording every little memory of my childhood. Regardless of its bittersweet experiences, they are all invaluable treasures to me. So, I have frozen my memories in a small windowpane, capturing that naive little girl who often daydreams about growing up overnight. I hope to keep my childhood in my heart, preserving a little girl's dream of purple and blue.

To my parents, teachers, classmates, and those who love me and whom I love, I dedicate this book, inviting them to look out the window with me and reminisce about our childhood journey together.

Chapter One: The Little Rabbit That Produces Chocolate Beans - My Preschool Story

Before attending elementary school, as an uninformed little girl, I often had some天真and amusing thoughts. Now recalling them, they seem quite interesting. I remember it was during my mid-kindergarten year when one of the children brought a little white rabbit. As a child, animals were always fascinating to me! So, I went to see the little rabbit every day. Not long after, I discovered a shocking secret - the rabbit produces "chocolate beans"! Black little beans rolled out from under the rabbit's tail. I wanted so badly to pick one up and taste it. But every time I reached out to do so, the auntie would come with a shovel and take the "chocolate beans" away. I thought to myself: She's so stingy! The rabbit gave her so many "chocolate beans," but she wouldn't let me try even one. Humph! So stingy!

Although I never got to taste the "chocolate beans," I quickly forgot about it and happily skipped home. Back then, there was a boy my age who often came to play at my house. He was a very honest boy, and in the courtyard, the boys of our age often bullied us because I was the only girl, and he was honest. Therefore, we mostly played house inside. We arranged the dolls, discussed how many baby rabbits and baby bears to raise, whether to drive a big crane or a truck, whether to use plates as pots or cups as pots, and what to name the rabbit - "Little Flower" or "Sweetie." In short, we were completely self-satisfied.

We grew older day by day, gradually drifting apart. After starting elementary school, he never came again. I knew we had grown up. As time passed, I don't know what things have been forgotten. Until one day, hearing the lyrics from "Dong Feng Po" - "I still remember that year, you and I were both very young, walking through the ancient path outside the fence." Suddenly, I felt like this song was written specifically for me, and I couldn't help but think of that little boy. Alas, those songs scattered in the wind, do they remember a little rabbit that produced "chocolate beans"?

Chapter Two: My Countryside Life - Entering Elementary School in Grade One

In Grade One, my mother went to pursue her postgraduate studies and didn't have time to take care of me. Thus, I, who had been pampered in the city, moved to the countryside with my father, transforming from a little princess into a wild child running around the mountains and fields. I started my school life at a Hope Primary School.

In that small town, we set up a temporary home. A flat with a small yard, surrounded by many neighbors, which was all new and fresh to me. It was here that I began my countryside life. Quickly, I found a new playmate - "Xiao Xiao" sister, who was in Grade Six. Although there was a significant age difference, we got along well. From then on, every evening, two small figures could be seen running one after another in the vast wilderness. Everything in the countryside was so fresh to me, and I was filled with curiosity about everything. Under Xiao Xiao's guidance, I soon became familiar with this beautiful small town and began my countryside adventure.

Every day after school, I would rush out of the house as soon as I put down my schoolbag. Often, the two small figures would bump into each other. Then, while rubbing our noses, we would make strange signals at each other, complaining that the other had hurt our noses. During that period, we climbed over walls to steal others' figs, falling and bruising our arms without daring to tell the adults, being swollen for a long time. Yet, when we ate the figs, we were satisfied. We slid down from a several-meter-high soil slope, too scared to open our eyes but insisting it was more fun than a slide, eventually sliding a few more times and realizing we weren't afraid anymore! However, our pants turned into mud buckets, and we returned home proudly despite being scolded. We excitedly carried a hedgehog we found in the small forest back home, not even noticing our hands were pricked. Later, when we couldn't find the hedgehog, the whole family mobilized to search for it, finally discovering the mop moving - the hedgehog had crawled inside and couldn't get out. Such events made me enjoy myself immensely, just like the song "Rice Fragrance" sings: so-called happiness is chasing dragonflies barefoot in the fields until tired, stealing fruits and getting stung by bees until scared. But not everything goes as planned; at least, my initial school life wasn't like that.

Just entering school, I knew nothing. My mind was blank, lost and unsure of what to do. Strange language, strange place, strange faces, strange textbooks - everything was terrifying to my young heart. I was deeply afraid; everything happened too suddenly, without any preparation, and I didn't know what to do. At school, the other children spoke in local dialects, which I couldn't understand, and naturally, I dared not communicate. Thus, I had no friends, standing alone, unable to do anything without my mother by my side.

During my first school exam, facing the stern atmosphere and a paper full of small tadpoles (characters), looking at the students next to me who were a year older and had attended preschool, I was stunned, staring blankly at their diligent backs. Halfway through the time, I decided to look at the paper, discovering that I actually knew some numbers and letters. So, I wrote a few numbers and capital letters, planning to put the rest in my bag to ask my mother later. Somehow, I ended up taking the test paper home and forgetting to show it to my mother, probably because I was busy catching dragonflies. It wasn't until my mother found it that I remembered, and she then handed it to my teacher. Later, I learned I scored fifty points, the worst score I ever got in primary school, but to me at that time, it was just a number, nothing more.

Thus began my muddled learning and listening. But I must say, I had a talent for being confused and somehow became a good student, became a class officer, and passed through the first grade with its innocent, worry-free, confused, colorful times.

Chapter Two: Entering the Life at Weicheng Experimental Primary School

In the second year of my mother pursuing her postgraduate studies, she returned to write her thesis and could take care of me. So, I reluctantly said goodbye to my little hedgehog, my wild jujube tree, my small yard, my big sister, and everything else with the earthy smell. Returning to the tight and restrictive city, I drew a conclusion to my unrestricted, free-spirited country life. But I believe I will return because the fragrance of the earth is so free and passionate, leading me forward, step by step...

Returning to Weifang, the oppressive atmosphere and fast-paced life left me breathless. Problems also multiplied, and one of them was my English. In Weifang, students start learning English from Grade One, but after a year in the countryside, I didn't even know the basics. So, during the summer vacation, I frantically caught up with my English studies, completing a year's curriculum within a few weeks. Strangely, I didn't feel tired, perhaps because Grade One English was simple, which made me quite relieved. Within these few weeks, I also quickly transformed from a rural to an urban lifestyle, reverting to the obedient and sensible little princess, re-integrating into this fast-paced life.

Subsequently, I entered a new school life but soon realized that my rustic charm compared to my classmates was laughable and uncouth. I was no longer that carefree little girl who only ran wildly in the fields. My competitive spirit told me: catch up with them, and do better than them! Thus, for a few days, I seemed like a different person. The handwriting I produced shocked even myself, and I worked hard to learn correct Mandarin, leaving my mother speechless with surprise. I didn't know what force drove me, but I changed beyond recognition within a few days. But I still tried to change. I had to admit I was somewhat inferior, always feeling I wasn't as good as others. A few days later, encouraged by the gentle and kind eyes of our homeroom teacher Ms. Zheng, I overcame my inferiority and fear of the unfamiliar teacher, raising my hand and answering questions. What I answered has been forgotten, but I remember the approval and encouragement in the teacher's eyes. For other students, it might not mean much, but they didn't know that from that moment on, the world lost a self-conscious girl and gained a confident, literature-loving girl. In my heart, I shouted loudly: I can do as well as them! And so, carrying confidence, I integrated into this group and found my own friendship.

A few days later, however, I faced another challenge. The first math test of the new semester, although I was no longer confused about exams, I still hadn't adapted to the new environment and methods. Watching the other students answer quickly and the teacher urging continuously, I panicked, fearing I couldn't keep up. The more anxious I became, the slower I got, and I eventually cried. After class, the other students went to do exercises, while I stayed in the classroom to finish the test. When the exercises were done, I finally submitted my test. Later, my mother visited the school once, but I didn't know what the teacher said to her. It wasn't until more than a year later that I found out. The teacher suspected I had intellectual issues! My mother said I just needed time to adapt and would soon improve, convincing the teacher that I wasn't mentally disabled. To protect my self-esteem, my mother didn't tell me. By the time I found out, it was probably just a memory. But I know if I had known at the time, how it would have affected me. It was my mother's love that preserved the basic dignity of a young heart. I love my mother. Now I face growing up, but the drops of maternal love in my growth have already converged into a river, flowing into my memories, flowing into my heart... So, let's keep some memories in our minds; write some words in the past, praising my most ordinary yet greatest mother.

Chapter Four: Beautiful Friendship - The Most Beautiful Memories of Third Grade

In third grade, I found the purest friendship of my life. Every noon after school, the roads were filled with the cheerful laughter of two little girls, and the air was filled with the sweet aroma of friendship.

By third grade, I had undergone a transformation, becoming an enviable top student with decent grades. But I was always alone, beginning to yearn for friendship. This is when Wei Xiangjie appeared. Back then, she was the most popular girl in our class, outgoing and lively, always surrounded by a group of girls, and I was no exception. But soon, I realized how much we had in common. Thus, the whole world disappeared, and we became two conjoined twins. Playing together, eating together, sleeping together, studying together, discussing whose hairpin was prettier, sharing an ice cream cone. In short, it seemed like the world only revolved around the two of us. Gradually, I discovered that we had become inseparable, absorbing each other's few strengths or weaknesses. Although on the surface, we still seemed different, our stubborn personalities and competitive hearts had formed a tacit understanding. So much so that later, when we met, we knew exactly what to say. After school, you would see two girls meeting and shouting in unison: "I'm hungry, you treat me!" Then laughing loudly, making others look at us like aliens. Ultimately, the result was always us patting our empty pockets and gazing longingly at various snacks, then listening to the symphony of hunger from our stomachs and marveling: "Listen, my stomach is making regular sounds!" Occasionally, we'd find a small treasure trove in our pants, wishing to split a coin in half to spend, licking our lips contentedly after eating the snacks, watching the last streak of red in the sky, and feeling inexplicably that this was the happiest thing.

This was the deepest memory of my third-grade year. Other stories are told by my third-grade essay notebook. Firstly, I kept a cat in third grade, becoming an undeniably devoted cat lover, always feeling a connection with cats. Below is an article I wrote at the time about our Mimi:

Mimi (My Observation Diary)

We have a kitten named Mimi, with long whiskers and big eyes, incredibly pretty!

Through a few days of observation, I discovered that Mimi loves cleanliness. Whenever Mimi wakes up, she licks herself all over, washing her face. Her bathroom is a small box filled with sand. Mimi takes care of her business there, and after finishing, she buries her waste with sand to prevent odors from escaping. Look how clean my little cat is!

I also found out that Mimi loves chicken liver. Whenever I give her chicken liver, she wolfs it down, losing her delicate demeanor, wanting to eat it all at once.

Isn't my Mimi cute?

Besides this diary entry, my third-grade essay book also includes an award-winning article titled "My Smoking and Drinking Dad." Here's what I wrote back then:

"My Smoking and Drinking Dad"

I have a dad who smokes heavily, with big eyes, stylish, a high nose bridge, thick eyebrows, wearing glasses - very handsome! But he loves smoking.

One morning, as soon as Dad woke up, he started looking for his lighter. His face was pale, his hair all sticking up. I knew Dad was uncomfortable, so I helped him look for the lighter. Dad even searched my room, and in the end, Mom found it for him. I asked, "Dad, why are you searching my room?" Dad laughed and said, "I'm not, you don't even have a lighter." Isn't he a smoker?

At the same time, Dad is also a drinker, averaging a drink every day. Dad's health isn't great to begin with, and alcohol exacerbates the damage. Both Mom and I worry about Dad, and whenever we talk about him, Dad just says, "It's all for work."

I hope Dad quits smoking and drinking, becoming a healthy dad.

Third grade quietly slipped away before I realized it. Looking out the window, spring grasses greened again, and I sighed at the swift passage of time. But what's the use? I can only hide my laughter deep in my heart. Listen, it's still so bright and intoxicating.

Chapter Five: Growth and Happiness in Fourth Grade

In fourth grade, I finally understood what sorrow, troubles, and worries are. Often, I rested my chin and sighed deeply at the window, but am I truly worried or just pretending? Even I don't know. Maybe there's a bit of "forcing oneself to express sorrow in new poems." Anyway, my understanding of "worries" is still vague, but I vaguely feel a light silk coolly wrapping around my heart. Forget it, most of the time, I'm still happy.

Wei Xiangjie transferred schools, and I consoled myself with the thought that we could still stay in touch, but who knows when people far apart will meet again? So, I gently closed my heart door, keeping this longing inside, perhaps never to be opened by anyone again. Meanwhile, my obsession with books reached a near-maniacal level. Since I no longer had a companion in real life, I sought solace in books. Thus, I read obsessively, the content covering a wide range, reading one book after another. I didn't know what benefits reading these books brought, but I just wanted to finish them, and after finishing one, I wanted to finish another. Unconsciously, my writing improved with extensive reading, thanks to my homeroom teacher Ms. Zheng, who carefully taught us how to write essays, tirelessly revised our work, inspired our creativity in various ways, sometimes even taking us out for extracurricular experiences. She guided me to develop good reading habits in the first two years of primary school, leading to my later obsession with reading. In my primary school life, I thrived under Ms. Zheng's care. She consistently instructed, cared for, and nurtured me. Below is an article I wrote at the time, showing a significant improvement from third grade, clearly demonstrating Ms. Zheng's dedication. I love my Ms. Zheng.

Grandma's House

"Grandma!" With a shout, I returned to the familiar courtyard of this year's most popular clothing combination. The emerald leaves of the melon trellis waved at me. The large pot beside the bellows exuded fragrant rice, and the guard dog Ah Huang barked excitedly, as if welcoming me. Grandma appeared, her kind face radiating even more joy. I threw myself into Grandma's arms, where she asked me countless questions.

After a while, Grandma asked me to tend the fire. So, I sat in front of the stove, fiddling seriously. I kept adding firewood, but smoke kept billowing out, making me cry. Grandpa pumped water, the water pump following his moving hands with an "iyah, iyah" sound, while water flowed into the tank with a "splash, splash." Grandma and Mom chopped vegetables with a "dong dong dong" sound, laughing and chatting happily. Finally, I boiled the water and played in the courtyard, chasing a group of fluffy little chickens, amused by their frantic running and eventual hiding under the mother hen's wings. At that moment, Uncle Grandpa arrived with a herd of lambs. I touched the lambs, which meekly allowed me to pet them, cuter than the chicks.

With Grandma's call, "Let's eat!" our family gathered in the yard, eating under the vast sky, accompanied by the red clouds on the horizon and the evening breeze, chatting and laughing freely, feeling extremely comfortable.

This was my life in fourth grade, plain and unremarkable but not empty, lacking novelty but not devoid of progress. These peaceful days taught me how to live, walking along the way, resting and moving forward...

Chapter Six: The Price of Growth - A Sad Farewell

In fifth grade, I stood at the intersection of maturity and innocence, often pondering questions that seemed unfathomable in my childhood. Among them, life and time were frequent visitors. Alone, I often thought: Facing the myriad of beings, how insignificant I am, and how powerless compared to the universe. Faced with these questions, I often humbled myself to the dust. But I also often thought: As a member of all living beings, how fortunate I am, always better off than countless other creatures. Involuntarily, I took pride in it. And towards time, I was more bewildered, from Qian Hetan's "People suffer from tomorrow's burdens, spring turns to autumn and old age approaches," to Li Bai's "Do you not see the sorrowful white hair in the bright mirror, turning from black silk in the morning to snow in the evening," to Zhu Ziqing's "Haste" today. Generations of literati lamented the fleeting nature of time, yet none escaped time's mercilessness, now all gone. What am I pondering here? Wasting time, that's all. Sometimes, I feel I am the master of time. I must act, not letting it slip away, filling my life with meaning.

Fifth grade was like this. On the road to growth, I explored forward, too busy to catch my breath as growth's speed carried me far ahead, only making my life fuller and fuller.

Postscript: Deep Gratitude and Longing

With infinite nostalgia for my teachers, alma mater, and classmates, I entered sixth grade. Starting this little book, years ago, I heard Ms. Zheng say that graduating seniors used to compile articles into a little book as a keepsake for her. In her heart, it was the best gift. At that time, I resolved to write a little book for my Ms. Zheng, but it seemed so far away then. But in the blink of an eye, I'm graduating. Thus, with infinite gratitude and longing for Ms. Zheng, I began writing these words with utmost reluctance. Each word I write brings me closer to graduation, so every word is filled with my gratitude and nostalgia. When I finished my final exams and heard Ms. Zheng say we would soon part ways, I tried hard to hold back my tears. Looking at these familiar surroundings - the classroom, classmates, teachers - they were so dear. Thinking of parting, my heart ached. So, I recorded every little memory of my childhood, walking through it all. At this moment of departure, I pour out my heart to Ms. Zheng, who is like a mother to me, expressing my most humble gratitude.

I love you, Ms. Zheng.
