Caribbean Journey (Part I) (With Photos)

By Shi Hanbing

Introduction

Uncle Guangyuan and Auntie have repeatedly invited me to visit the Caribbean nations, but I've always been too busy to go. However, the stories they told me about the Caribbean deeply fascinated me. Combined with the fact that my nerves have been tightly strung for over a decade, leaving me mentally exhausted, it was a perfect opportunity to take advantage of the May Day holiday to relax.

After more than 9 hours of flight from Charles de Gaulle Airport in France, we arrived at Saint Martin Island. To travel from France to Caribbean countries, one must pass through Saint Martin Island. The island, which is only 88 square kilometers in size, is divided between two countries: the Netherlands (southern part of Saint Martin) and France (northern part of Saint Martin). The Dutch and French regions each follow different systems, with a population of approximately 70,000 people.

Among the hundreds of passengers, there were only two Chinese individuals: Mr. Wang from Shandong and myself. At Charles de Gaulle Airport, Mr. Wang was surprised that I, as a first-time traveler to the Caribbean, had been allowed to leave the country. He said that even if all the procedures are legal, mainland Chinese citizens are usually prohibited from traveling to Caribbean countries without any explanation given. Moreover, Saint Martin has a strong bias against Chinese people, often deporting them without question, especially groups of Chinese travelers. It’s difficult to leave China, and it’s even harder to enter the Caribbean, which might explain why there are so few Chinese tourists here.

Discussing the root cause, Mr. Wang became very emotional. Besides the famous incident 20 years ago, there are probably two reasons: one is the group smuggling incidents involving Chinese nationals, and the other is the frequent reports of toxic food incidents in China, which have been widely reported by CNN and other foreign media. This has led many people in this region to view Chinese people as monsters, fearing those who would add poison to food. Their logic is: since these people are so ruthless towards themselves, they can do anything to others.

I cannot confirm whether what Mr. Wang said is true. However, on Saint Martin Island, we indeed encountered the situation he described: when immigration officers saw our Chinese passports, they immediately became cautious, confiscating the passports and making Mr. Wang and I wait aside rather than directly allowing us entry like they do for citizens of other countries. Then, a big fat woman came over and took us to a nearby simple office (Mr. Wang said that this is how all their offices are due to strict taxpayer supervision and government frugality). The fat woman processed our boarding passes while ensuring neither of us left her sight. Despite Mr. Wang having worked in Antigua for over six years and me possessing an invitation letter and visa from the Antiguan government and a visa from Dominica, we were still treated with suspicion.

It was a long period of time. Most of the immigration officers on Saint Martin Island are women, and they are all incredibly fat, with large waists and enormous bottoms (forgive me for repeating the same words used in elementary school essays; I can't think of better terms). They all wear tight clothing, looking bulging and ready to explode. Each time the big fat woman holding our passports walked past me with her bouncing movements, I worried intensely. I told Wang, "Thank goodness the clothes are sturdy; otherwise, that pile of meat could have crushed us to death! Would our company consider it a work injury?" Wang ignored my jokes, his expression blank. He said, "If we had dignity back home, who would willingly endure such humiliation?"

He told me many stories of Chinese people being humiliated, which made me feel extremely sad, and the waiting time only added to the sense of shame in my heart. Overseas Chinese originally came out under such difficult circumstances!

But at the time, I wasn't overly worried. My brother and sister-in-law have not only a good reputation in the Caribbean region but also close relationships with various governments. They were coming to pick me up, which would put an end to this torment. Sure enough, soon after, I saw my brother and sister-in-law. They had specially come from New York the day before to meet me, and I felt both sorrow and joy, immensely happy. Everything afterward was arranged by them; otherwise, someone like me, who could get lost just wandering around the streets domestically, would never dare to travel so far alone. I didn't tell my parents before leaving the country because I was afraid they would worry about me getting lost. After returning, I would inform them so they wouldn't be constantly anxious for me.

My brother and sister-in-law told me many interesting stories about the Caribbean islands. One of the criteria for judging a woman's beauty here (and also the most important one) is the size of her buttocks—the bigger the better, and the more sexy and beautiful she is considered. My siblings mentioned a woman on Saint Martin Island whose buttocks were extraordinarily large. Whenever she walked by, all the men held their breath, as if struck dumb by her super-sexy beauty. Only after she passed did the men exchange glances and smile contentedly. Everyone in the country knew about this beautiful buttock, and seeing it was considered a great honor. That reminded me of the big-bottomed woman who processed our boarding passes earlier—she was actually a stunning beauty. When that enormous bottom swayed gracefully in front of us multiple times, we failed to appreciate its beauty. Perhaps many black friends were envying us: "Look, these two guys are really lucky!"

The people here are extremely confident, especially the women. All men compliment every woman they see, calling them sweet names like Sweetheart or Darling, regardless of age or familiarity. From children to centenarians, everyone is addressed this way. As a result, no woman in the entire Caribbean feels unattractive, and they are all full of confidence. Weight loss pills would definitely not sell a single pill here. Women's confidence comes from within and shows on their faces. This, of course, is a later realization. It seems that improving comprehensive national strength includes men praising women as an important aspect.

Traveling in Antigua (Antigua)

Similar to Saint Martin Island, Antigua is extremely strict and harsh regarding Chinese entry.

Antigua is a limestone island. In 1493, during Columbus's second voyage to America, he reached Antigua and named it after the Santa Maria la Antigua church in Seville, Spain. In 1632, the British occupied the island, bringing African slaves to grow tobacco and sugarcane. In 1967, it became an associated state of the UK and established an internal self-governing government, though Britain retained control over foreign affairs and defense. On November 1, 1981, Antigua declared independence, becoming a constitutional monarchy and a member of the Commonwealth. The total population is less than 100,000.

This island nation is most famous for its white sand beaches, which, along with those in Bermuda and Barbados, are considered among the best in the world.

When I arrived in Antigua by plane from Saint Martin Island, it was already dark (the time difference here is 12 hours compared to Beijing time; 7 PM here is 7 AM in Beijing).

With my brother and sister-in-law present, everything went smoothly. Uncle Guangyuan drove the car to the airport exit, and we rushed to our accommodation. On the way, we passed Allen Stanford's bank in Antigua, which left a deep impression. Allen Stanford was once the biggest boss on the island, akin to a monarch. In 2006, at the request of the Antiguan government, Prince Edward, Earl of Wessex, granted Stanford a knighthood. Now, "The swindler has gone away on the yellow crane, leaving behind only the Yellow Crane Tower," Allen Stanford's assets have been acquired by others.

After arriving at the hotel, my brother and sister-in-law arranged everything for me, and I fell asleep as soon as I lay down. Waking up late at night (due to jet lag), I pulled the curtains and saw a water-like expanse outside, resembling a dream. After reading for three hours, I went back to sleep.

The next morning, I got up and went to eat breakfast at the hotel. A few black service staff were chatting leisurely, each looking extremely happy. Their smiles deeply infected me. I gave my room number and ordered food without needing to sign anything; they trusted me absolutely.

In Antigua, everyone I met greeted me warmly. At the hotel, a black man even shook hands with me like an old friend. Thinking I had met him before, I searched my memory for who he was, but then he kindly asked me, "What's your name?" This reminded me of what my sister-in-law had said: people here greet everyone.

I don't usually eat Western cuisine, and some of my actions were so stiff that I couldn't bear to watch myself. Regardless of being left-handed or right-handed, everyone uses a knife and fork, with clear divisions of labor. I use my right hand for both. After using the knife, I put it down and then use the fork. Sometimes, unable to bear watching anymore, someone stepped in to help. A plump woman came over and demonstrated for me, warming my heart. Truly, she was a wonderful server! According to my brother and sister-in-law, in Antigua, if you accidentally get lost, locals with cars will drive you to your destination. If your car breaks down on the road, they will ask if you need help, showing such warmth that it feels like the whole country is filled with Lei Feng. Moreover, vehicles always let pedestrians go first, reasoning that cars are already faster than walking, so it's only fair to let pedestrians go first. Without the cultivation of advanced concepts like the Five Advocates, Four Beauties, Three Loves, Three Represents, Eight Honors, and Eight Disgraces, the high level of civic quality here is surprising.

Then, my journey officially began.

Figure 1: The natural scenery of Antigua is exceptionally beautiful, especially the white sandy beaches. Standing on the hotel balcony, one can see a vast expanse of blue. The blue sky and sea blend seamlessly. Antigua has no chemical plants, only pollution-free enterprises like beverage processing. Of course, the locals lack the extensive chemical knowledge we possess. From elementary school to university, education is entirely free. Later, parents suggested that if tuition is waived, they would be happier if the government also paid for school uniforms, so the government, to please the people, covered the cost of uniforms as well. Regarding healthcare, patients only pay a $5 EC (Eastern Caribbean Dollar) registration fee, and the government covers all other expenses. A Chinese person fell ill here, and the doctor refused to discharge them until their indicators returned to normal, demonstrating a responsible attitude that moved people. (In terms of welfare, other Caribbean countries are roughly similar.)

Figure 2: The crystal-clear seawater shines brightly even on the beach. According to Antiguan law, all natural resources are equally open to every Antiguan, including beggars, who can freely access any beach. No one dares to enclose an area for profit or arbitrarily raise prices.

Figure 3: Due to the financial crisis, the number of tourists has sharply decreased, leaving only Uncle Guangyuan, Auntie, and me on the beach.

Figure 4: During our outings, we frequently encountered flocks of sheep grazing freely. No one worries about losing them, and no one steals them. When Auntie took me shopping, my passport and cash were in my laptop bag inside the car. Upon returning, we found the car window open, but the bag was still there. People here are very relaxed and do not work as hard as we do. Their social security is relatively well-established, and they prioritize enjoying life. Society is quite transparent.

Figure 5: Another flock of sheep.

Figure 6: This is a sales point built by the Antiguan government for vendors. This management approach ensures livelihoods while maintaining orderliness. Compared to our armed urban management model, it might be considered advanced.

Figure 7: People here have a weak sense of earning money, and shops rarely open fully. They believe that enjoying life is central, and earning money is for enjoyment. Therefore, they are unwilling to exhaust themselves for money. This phenomenon was unprecedented for me—whether it's foolishness or a higher realm, I am unsure.

Figure 8: A local market in Antigua where people also trade on the roadside, appearing disorganized. Antiguans are very honest and sincere, trusting what you say almost without doubt. However, their mental arithmetic skills are poor, and they greatly admire those who can calculate as accurately as a calculator. For example, when selling fish, they do not categorize them. Whether expensive fish like barracuda or grouper, or other types, all are sold at the same price because they fear complex calculations. Sometimes, they simply sell fish by the pile.

Figure 9: A solitary speaker in front of the market passionately proclaiming, "Your desire is your right, your right is your opportunity, your opportunity is your destiny..." His impromptu speech filled with philosophy amazed us. Even a ragged person might be a thinker. My siblings told me that in Antigua, even beggars face the prime minister without inferiority. They often directly seek the prime minister to resolve issues, speaking frankly if unresolved, showing no courtesy.

Figure 10: A beautiful woman encountered on the street. I've learned to judge beauty according to their standards. This woman should be considered top-tier in my eyes. She moved quickly, and it took me half a block to catch a photo. Amazing how someone so overweight can be so healthy!

Figure 11: A church. Most Antiguans are Christians or Catholics, and this is a famous local church.

Figure 12: Jessica, the general manager hired by my brother and sister-in-law in Antigua. Her appearance resembles Condoleezza Rice, with quick thinking, clear logic, dedication to her job, and is considered a gifted local managerial talent.

Figure 13: A casino. In Antigua, casinos are legal, with government-appointed supervisors checking equipment and overseeing gambling processes to ensure fair play. Some unemployed Antiguans often frequent casinos. If they spot high-ranking officials (like the prime minister) gambling, they can make a fortune. They chase after these officials demanding money, threatening to insult them if denied. Consequently, high-ranking officials rarely appear here. On the other hand, due to low civil servant salaries, some with large families often work double shifts, working part-time at casinos at night. One evening, my siblings took me to the casino, and we played blackjack. I was purely guessing, sometimes taking cards that caused everyone to lose together, much to the distress of a professional gambler who kept losing money. However, when facing me, he smiled instantly, knowing I truly didn't understand. Later, my sister-in-law taught me, and together we won $200. Then, a fat tax official took over dealing, quickly reclaiming our winnings—truly worthy of being a tax collector! We eventually stopped due to exhaustion, having earned $85. Here, the casino rules are excellent. It is important to note that these casinos are purely for entertainment, completely different from the extravagant gambling indulged in by corrupt officials from China.

Figure 18: My sister-in-law picked me a pair of chubby baby sandals, which I hadn't worn since I was very young. Suddenly, I felt like I had returned to childhood.

Figure 19: Returning to the hotel, we found two sheep leisurely eating grass. Even at such a nice hotel, they know how to choose the best spots. But no one disturbed them, letting nature take its course. Neither the sheep nor the humans feel discriminated against, and no one claims the sheep intruded on their property to chase them away, let alone humans. I don’t know why, but I felt very moved!

Figure 24: Sunbathing, I realized I had gained weight recently. Of course, another possibility is that my eyes have become smaller, otherwise, why does everything I see seem round and chubby? Checking in the mirror at night confirmed my fears: my eyes might indeed be getting smaller. Oh my god!
