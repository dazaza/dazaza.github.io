Big Data Is the Only Way to Compete With Google

Google's position in search is unparalleled. However, Google's products and services have long since expanded beyond just search.

Google's product lineup includes advertising (AdWords), communication and sharing (Drive and Hangouts), development resources (OpenSocial), social networking (Google+), maps (Google Maps), streaming media (Google Play), analytics tools (Analytics), operating systems (Android and Chrome OS), desktop and mobile applications (Gmail), and hardware (Galaxy Nexus).

Google's search business continues to grow, and in many of the areas it has expanded into, there seem to be few obvious competitors. Microsoft and Apple are the two most direct competitors, but Amazon and Salesforce also warrant Google's attention. Amazon, as an e-commerce giant, reduces Google's ad revenue by diverting online shopping traffic away from Google. Salesforce combines social network analysis with sales data, redirecting companies' promotional budgets toward social networks.

Amazon

Amazon's dominance in e-commerce is threatening Google's traditional interests. Just as Google is synonymous with search, Amazon is the go-to name for e-commerce. Around 30% to 40% of Google's ad revenue comes from e-commerce, but as Amazon increasingly becomes a one-stop online supermarket, how many users will skip Google and head straight to Amazon?

Amazon’s Prime membership (with a $79 annual fee) exceeds 10 million members, and in the U.S. alone, monthly shopping visits reach 103 million, leading to a significant increase in direct website traffic. My wife used to like searching before shopping, but now she always shops directly on Amazon because she’s a Prime member and gets free shipping. Moreover, Amazon's numerous third-party sellers offer diverse shopping options, often at the lowest prices available online.

Beyond just diverting traffic from Google, the real way to beat Google is through big data. Amazon extracts personal information, shopping patterns, and individual preferences from customer transaction data. Does Google have this kind of data? Perhaps, but probably not as rich or comprehensive as Amazon's.

Salesforce

Salesforce has long been the leader in CRM, B2B, and SaaS. However, mergers and acquisitions over the past few years have transformed Salesforce from a single customer relationship management system into a comprehensive marketing platform.

Two years ago, Salesforce acquired Radian6 (a social intelligence analysis company), last year they acquired Buddy Media (a social media management platform). Last month, Salesforce completed another major acquisition for $2.5 billion—ExactTarget, a cloud-based marketing software company. Salesforce hasn’t stopped there; they recently acquired enterprise insights startup EdgeSpring.

The core purpose of all these acquisitions is data.

More specifically, it’s about connecting end-of-sale data with social media marketing data. Salesforce analyzes social networks to help salespeople obtain actionable insights that lead to successful transactions. With newly acquired technologies and enhanced social network analysis capabilities, Salesforce's services not only provide possible leads but can also directly convert social marketing efforts into revenue—this will encourage companies to spend more on social marketing. Who are the real winners here?

Of course, social networking companies—not just Facebook, but also Pinterest and others—will gain more revenue. Salesforce, which facilitates and promotes this shift, is also a winner.

At the same time, the technology and services provided by Salesforce are direct competitors to Google Analytics. Currently, Google Analytics doesn't match the comprehensiveness of Radian6 in social analysis. As social media marketing becomes increasingly important, companies are willing to pay for effectiveness analyses of their social media marketing campaigns.

They leverage the value of data in their respective areas of expertise. Their commonality lies in their greed for data; big data is their only opportunity to compete with Google. Regardless of whether they aim to achieve their goals through transactions, acquisitions, or new technologies, fundamentally, this isn't a competition against Google—it's a competition for data.

There's no doubt that Google is an excellent company. But without the support of big data, no matter how many products, companies, or technologies they acquire, it would be meaningless. Data is the foundation upon which Google stands, and it's also the reason Google can provide high-quality services.

Big data will be the force that reshapes industry landscapes.
