House price increase in many cities in the first two months, local governments are dilemma about the house price target

by m0c7i0f7r5 on 2012-03-07 20:43:33

⊙ Reporter Zhu Nan, Editor Heng Daoqing. According to the relevant provisions of the "New National Eight Articles," local governments must announce their housing price control targets for 2011 before the end of the first quarter. After investigating several second-tier cities that have announced their housing price control targets and first-tier cities that have yet to do so, the reporter found that compared to last year's annual average, the average housing prices in many cities from January to February have already significantly increased. To achieve the regulation target of controlling the full-year housing price increase to around 10%, local governments still face significant pressure.

Among the cities that have announced their regulation targets, the reporter selected five cities: Lanzhou, Kunming, Shenyang, Foshan, and Xi'an, comparing the increases in the average housing prices from January to February this year with last year’s annual averages. It was found that in three out of the five cities, the average prices from January to February this year had already significantly risen compared to last year’s annual average; some increases even exceeded the full-year regulation target of 10%.

According to the monitoring data from the China Index Academy, the annual average new commodity housing price in Shenyang last year was 5063 yuan/square meter, while the average price from January to February this year reached 5599 yuan/square meter, a significant increase of 10.6% compared to last year’s annual average. In Foshan, the annual average new commodity housing price last year was 7457 yuan/square meter, while the average price from January to February this year was 7938 yuan/square meter, an increase of 6.5% compared to last year’s average. The upward trend in housing prices in Xi'an has been even more pronounced, with the average price from January to February this year reaching 6856 yuan/square meter, while last year’s annual average was only 6065 yuan/square meter, indicating a substantial increase of 13% since the beginning of this year. The full-year housing price control targets for these three cities are 12%, 12%, and 15%, respectively.

In the five sampled cities, only Lanzhou and Kunming showed a downward trend in the average prices since the beginning of this year. The data shows that the annual average new commodity housing price in Lanzhou last year was 5518 yuan/square meter, while the average price from January to February this year was 5110 yuan/square meter, a decline of about 7% compared to last year. In Kunming, the average new commodity housing price last year was 6390 yuan/square meter, while the average price from January to February this year was 6338 yuan/square meter, a slight decrease of 0.8% compared to last year.

For first-tier cities, the upward trend in housing prices from January to February is even more evident, which may also be the reason why some first-tier cities are still hesitant to announce their housing price regulation targets. Based on the data, the annual average housing prices in Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Shenzhen last year were 20063 yuan/square meter, 21728 yuan/square meter, 12484 yuan/square meter, and 20106 yuan/square meter, respectively. However, the average prices from January to February this year were 22680 yuan/square meter, 22425 yuan/square meter, 13787 yuan/square meter, and 20378 yuan/square meter, showing an upward trend compared to last year’s annual averages, with increases of 13%, 3%, 10%, and 1%, respectively.

"Due to the great pressure of rising housing prices, there will be regulation targets," said Wang Juelin, deputy director of the Policy Research Center of the Ministry of Housing and Urban-Rural Development, to media reporters. Once local governments set the targets, they must achieve them.

Industry insiders told the reporter that January and February are still considered the market off-season, yet housing prices in various places continue to rise, indicating that local governments face significant pressure in achieving their full-year housing price control targets.
