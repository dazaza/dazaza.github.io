How long does it take for vision to recover with femtosecond laser?

by shww on 2012-03-09 16:13:16

Among the population receiving surgery for myopia [http://www.pr021.com/jinshi/], there are many college students and white-collar workers. The effect of femtosecond laser treatment is evident, as it only takes a few short minutes to say goodbye to a life with myopia. However, how long it takes for vision to recover after femtosecond laser surgery should be a more concerning issue for patients with myopia. Due to academic and work reasons, they are very concerned about how long it will take to recover from the surgery to treat myopia using the femtosecond laser and how long it will take to return to normal eye use after the surgery.

The femtosecond laser [http://www.pr021.com/rwzt/lasik.html] is a new type of equipment used to treat myopia. Not only is the surgery time short, but the patient also experiences good comfort throughout the entire procedure. When creating the corneal flap with the femtosecond laser device, the process is quick, the light spots are small, and the energy is low, resulting in the smallest possible bubbles being produced during the corneal flap cutting. There is no need to wait during laser treatment, which not only reduces the exposure time of the corneal flap in the air but also greatly reduces complications that may arise after laser treatment for myopia.

Patients with myopia mainly worry about safety issues, after all, surgery is being performed on the eyeball. A large proportion of patients have such concerns. Up to now, the development of excimer laser surgery has become a relatively mature technology, and most patients can usually resume normal daily activities with their eyes the day after the surgery.

Doctors at Shanghai PR Eye Hospital [http://www.pr021.com/] remind that if the patient is over 40 years old, the recovery period will be relatively longer. For patients with mild to moderate myopia, vision usually returns to normal within two days after the surgery. Close-range work should still be minimized, and each working session should not last too long; rest for about 5-15 minutes. At this point, driving during the day is possible, but excessive eye strain should still be avoided. Nighttime driving is not recommended during this period. Generally, one month after the surgery, the patient's vision has stabilized. Eye medication has stopped, and nighttime driving can be resumed.
