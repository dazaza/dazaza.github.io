"The Inevitable 'Death of Yougou': Belle's E-commerce Has Many Fatal Flaws"

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:19:03

Recently, Yougou.com, which has been suffering from a severe loss of senior executives, received another blow. On July 17th, it was confirmed by an insider at Yougou.com that its COO, Zhang Xiaojun, will be leaving and is currently processing his resignation. As one of the founding members of Yougou.com, Zhang Xiaojun's departure carries significant implications, indicating that Yougou.com is indeed facing serious crises.

Let’s first review the e-commerce journey of Belle International, to which Yougou.com belongs:

A few years ago, Belle cautiously tested the waters in e-commerce, steadily developing through three channels: Taobao distribution, Tmall, and B2C distribution (B2C distribution was completed via Taoxiu.com). Led by group veteran Xie Yunli, a relatively modest e-commerce team began by selling products under Belle's own brands, later introducing exclusive online models and eventually launching new internet-exclusive brands. This development path was considered a more solid success story for traditional brand enterprises entering e-commerce than Li-Ning before 2011.

As companies like Letao.com and Haolemai gained over $100 million in financing and rapidly developed in 2010, Belle Group, which supplied these two platforms, became restless. On one hand, there was concern about being constrained by distribution channels again; on the other hand, after evaluation, they realized their immense potential for growth in the e-commerce sector. These advantages included:

1. A multitude of brands: In addition to Belle's own several women's shoe brands, the agency-managed international sports brands such as Adidas and Nike experienced astonishing growth in online sales;

2. Easier category expansion: For Belle Group, which specializes in women's shoes and agency-managed well-known sports brands, how to achieve absolute market dominance and status through multiple brands and categories has been a question much pondered upon. Expanding offline stores for brand and category expansion was obviously too slow, while the ongoing e-commerce business could easily expand brands but not quickly enough to include categories;

3. Financial advantage: During cooperation with e-commerce enterprises, Belle discovered that e-commerce is a capital-intensive business. However, Belle Group's financial strength allowed it to develop without relying on capital, thus enabling it to outlast many vertical e-commerce companies that depend heavily on capital, achieving the "survivor king" status;

4. Talent advantage: After years of e-commerce practice, Belle had cultivated a highly professional and efficient e-commerce team, capable of handling large-scale developments or even more;

5. Supply chain advantage: This excited Belle internally and was the core reason for attracting top executives from brand e-commerce or platform e-commerce. After all, supply chain management always fascinates in e-commerce, as it seems to be the part deciding life or death aside from funding. The supply chain includes product research and development, production, warehousing management, delivery and after-sales service - a series of "business" contents. In traditional enterprises that prioritize "business" over "e-commerce," this is considered the most advantageous competitive edge over e-commerce enterprises.

With these advantages, Belle's e-commerce decided to go all out. First, it purchased the double-pinyin domain name yougou.com,

discarding the slightly copycat “topshoes.cn” Taixiu website and redirecting it, stopping supplies to all distribution websites. Although it retained direct third-party online shops like Taobao and Tmall Mall, the investment and attention decreased. It hired JD.com VP and Vancl VP at high salaries to serve as CMO and COO respectively, dispatching a veteran group official to serve as CEO, forming a golden triangle. They aimed to build a full-category vertical B2C mall, implementing a multi-brand strategy on one hand, and expanding into clothing and other categories on the other, realizing the dream of a department store empire impossible to achieve offline!

Everything sounded perfect, but any dream must face the harsh reality of the backbone. The actual situation was:

1. Higher technical requirements for independent B2C: Being headquartered in Shenzhen, Belle couldn't recruit more excellent technical talents, and technical outsourcing service companies were scarce in Shenzhen. However, technology determines user experience, backend management efficiency, and more reliable data. Independent B2C involves dynamic technology, different from the relatively static technologies like ERP, and continuous changes remain a hurdle that many traditional enterprises cannot overcome;

2. Increased management difficulty with category expansion: Someone once said that doing women's shoes well doesn’t necessarily mean doing men's shoes well, something Belle probably deeply felt when expanding into men's shoes. Expanding into women's wear and accessories within a year would undoubtedly bring challenges to the backend. For traditional brand enterprises accustomed to meticulous calculations, this rapid approach worked for Vancl during its special period, and could be understood within the systems of Vancl and JD.com. However, whether Belle could understand and execute with "exaggerated costs" despite having Vancl's COO and JD.com's CMO remains a big question mark;

3. System: Many people find it hard to cross between e-commerce and traditional enterprises. To put it plainly, departments (organizational structure), approval processes, finance and budget, reporting methods, corporate culture, communication styles, execution methods, risk control, process systems, etc., form what is called the "mechanism" of an enterprise. The result of this conflict is that e-commerce tries to convince and accept the "system" of e-commerce, while the traditional team attempts to understand but can never fully grasp the "system" of e-commerce, leading to inefficiency and instability in the execution team. Ultimately, the chairman or board might say: "E-commerce and traditional enterprises each have their strengths and weaknesses, and the future is certainly fusion, so in these aspects, you must also fuse..." If this so-called "fusion without harmony" appears, it leads to so-called management innovation, and thus, the "CEO rotation system" actually appeared in a company like Belle!

It was originally thought that Yougou.com of Belle e-commerce had taken the lead, and the senior executives were waiting for the spin-off IPO to enjoy success and wealth, becoming a glorious case for countless traditional enterprises in the e-commerce era. Instead, they are now troubled by these inherent issues.

After all, whether it's the veterans who grew up with Belle or the e-commerce professionals recruited, it can be seen that they didn't leave because of incompetence. When the VP from JD.com returns to JD.com, he can still perform excellently; when the VP from Vancl has the courage to start a business. So, are these three points the inherent problems? Or are these not just Belle's inherent problems but also those of many traditional enterprises attempting to enter e-commerce?
