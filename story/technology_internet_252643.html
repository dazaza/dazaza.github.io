Eating too much licorice can lead to intellectual disability in children!

by alexsgxu on 2009-10-27 17:19:50

Eating too much liquorice can lead to developmental issues and behavioural problems in children, according to a study published in the American Journal of Epidemiology.

The research followed 321 pregnant women and found that consuming excessive amounts of liquorice, which contains a compound called Glycyrrhizin, can cause stress hormones to transfer from the mother to the fetus. This can result in premature birth, developmental issues, behavioural disorders and other growth problems in children.

Pregnant women should therefore be mindful of their diet and pay particular attention to foods marketed as heat-clearing and detoxifying, checking for ingredients like liquorice. Below is an introduction to liquorice, excerpted from various sources:

Liquorice refers to the roots and rhizomes of the perennial herb Glycyrrhiza glabra from the legume family, mainly produced in northwestern China. As early as over two thousand years ago, the Shen Nong Herbal Classic recorded: "Liquorice treats cold and hot pathogenic qi of the five zang and six fu organs, strengthens tendons and bones, grows muscles, doubles energy, heals wounds, and detoxifies...". Traditional Chinese medicine believes that liquorice has the effects of tonifying the middle energizer and benefiting qi, clearing heat and detoxifying, expelling phlegm and stopping coughs, alleviating urgent pain, and moderating the properties of other herbs.

Modern pharmacological studies suggest that liquorice mainly contains glycyrrhizin, glycyrrhizic acid, sterols, β-sitosterol, sugars, organic acids, etc. Therefore, liquorice has detoxification effects, anti-diuretic effects, inhibitory effects on histamine-induced gastric acid secretion, and anti-inflammatory and anti-allergic reaction effects. New uses of liquorice include the following aspects:

One, Detoxification: It has excellent detoxification effects against various types of poisoning and is known as the universal antidote. The main effective detoxification components are the adsorption action of glycyrrhizin and the corticosteroid-like action of glycyrrhetinic acid; glycyrrhizin hydrolysis produces glucuronic acid conjugates for detoxification.

Two, Used for endocrine and nervous system diseases: Can be used to treat chronic adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease), postpartum pituitary insufficiency (Sheehan's syndrome), diabetes insipidus, etc. Its effective components are related to the corticosteroid-like action and anti-diuretic action of liquorice. In addition, liquorice can also treat trigeminal neuralgia, congenital myotonia, calf muscle cramps, etc., which are related to the antispasmodic and inhibitory peripheral nerve excitability actions of liquorice.

Three, Respiratory system diseases: Liquorice can be widely used to treat pharyngitis, laryngitis, tracheitis, bronchitis, asthma, cough, hemoptysis, etc. These are related to the cough-suppressing and antispasmodic effects of liquorice.

Four, Digestive system diseases: Liquorice has anti-gastric ulcer and anti-gastritis effects. This is related to liquorice's ability to relieve smooth muscle spasms, inhibit gastric acid and pepsin secretion.

Liquorice can also be used to treat rheumatoid arthritis and allergic purpura, thrombocytopenic purpura, etc.

However, long-term use of liquorice can cause hypertension, edema, and reduced potassium levels in the blood. Therefore, patients with hypertension and edema should use it cautiously.
