Bohai Sea Marine Ecology: Where to Go

by wujizhui on 2011-11-21 05:10:50

Economic Observer reporter, Zhong Ang: "This year, the shrimps have died by half. If it continues like this, aquaculture will be impossible to continue." Facing the vast sea, Zhang Xinmin frowned and revealed a melancholy look in his eyes. Beneath his feet lay countless shrimp fry, scorched by the sun, emitting waves of stench, with flies buzzing around, refusing to disperse.

Behind Zhang Xinmin was the place where he and his ancestors had lived for generations - Jianguo Village in Dagang, Tianjin Binhai New Area. In Zhang Xinmin's memory, twenty years ago, this sea was tranquil and azure, and the villagers, old and young, made their living from fishing. But now, this small fishing village has completely changed its appearance.

What was once a "treasure pot" and "cradle of fisheries" is now sparse in fish and shrimp, with many species going extinct one after another. Large areas of tidal flats and wetlands have been occupied, replaced by factories and chimneys standing everywhere. The fragile Bohai Sea ecosystem can no longer bear the "heavy" load.

The Bohai Sea is known as the "cradle of fisheries" and is the source of marine fishery resources for the entire northern region. But now the Bohai Sea fishery has disappeared, and even officials from the State Oceanic Administration openly commented that "the function of the Bohai Sea as a fishing ground has basically been lost."

In Zhang Xinmin's memory, the sea at the entrance of the village was calm and blue, and every time they returned from fishing, the elders always came back fully loaded and overjoyed. But later, the fishery in the sea gradually disappeared, forcing the villagers to abandon fishing and turn to aquaculture by enclosing pools near the coast.

In 2006, the Tianjin Binhai New Area was approved as a national comprehensive supporting reform pilot zone, becoming the third pole driving China's regional economic growth after the Shenzhen Special Economic Zone and Shanghai Pudong New Area. The area where Zhang Xinmin lived, Jianguo Village, began to undergo tremendous changes.

Following the direction Zhang Xinmin pointed towards the southeast, it was the Tianjin Nanport Industrial Zone in its early stages of construction. What used to be a quiet bay and picturesque tidal flat has completely transformed into a different scene – countless large trucks roaring past, raising dust trails stretching hundreds of meters; pile drivers shaking the ground with their roar; red banners plastered everywhere proclaiming "Building a World-Class Heavy Chemical Base," reminding everyone that a petrochemical new city would soon rise here.

Covering an area of 160 square kilometers, about 40 square kilometers were shrimp ponds and salt fields, while the remaining 120 square kilometers needed land reclamation through filling the sea. This resulted in a billion-yuan investment scale every three days in the Nanport Industrial Zone. In the future, all petrochemical industries in Tianjin and surrounding areas would gather here.

Facing the vast sea to the east, Zhang Xinmin wore a bewildered expression as the pollution of this sea, on which he relied for survival, worsened year by year, and his harvest deteriorated year by year. According to the "Tianjin Marine Environmental Quality Bulletin," none of the three monitoring sessions conducted in 2010 along the coastal waters of Tianjin met the Class I seawater quality standard (suitable for protecting marine life and ensuring safe human use). The area of lightly, moderately, and heavily polluted seas consistently exceeded 80%, reaching 100% in the spring.

Each time he saw large amounts of dead shrimp fry being scooped up and rotting away, Zhang Xinmin felt both heartbroken and powerless – because other aquaculture farmers nearby faced the same predicament. However, surrounded by numerous factories, the aquaculture farmers could not determine the cause of the mass shrimp deaths, making compensation claims nearly impossible.

However, regarding the increasing pollution, Old Zhang harbored complex emotions alongside his pain. His two children, one worked in a nearby chemical plant, the other did concrete business. He admitted that without the large-scale development, his children would also have been subjected to the hardships of aquaculture under the sun and wind, with their bodies smelling of fish. Without these outside investors, Dagang’s urban construction wouldn’t have undergone such a dramatic transformation in just a few years.

Unsustainable Burden

Zhang Xinmin's plight is not an isolated case along the Bohai Sea coast. On the other side of the Bohai Sea, Feng Tianfu, a breeder from Erdao Village in Laobian District, Yingkou City, Liaoning Province, also shared the same sentiment.

As the only intersection area of the Bohai Economic Circle, Liaoning Coastal Economic Belt, and Shenyang Economic Zone, Yingkou launched its coastal industrial development comprehensively in 2009, and large tracts of coastal land were requisitioned. A survey report from the Yingkou Municipal Committee of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference showed that among Yingkou's nearly 100 kilometers of coastline, less than 20 kilometers remained as natural coastline. Before 2006, Yingkou had 60,000 hectares (900,000 mu) of wetlands. According to the latest statistics, the marsh wetland area in Yingkou has decreased to between 45,000-65,000 mu.

On one hand, the rapid increase in industrial land requisition forced a large number of fishermen to relocate and abandon aquaculture; on the other hand, the worsening seawater pollution made it increasingly difficult for the remaining breeders to sustain their livelihoods.

Unfortunately, Feng Tianfu and nearby breeders are helpless since “the losses suffered by the breeders may not necessarily be caused by pollution from Yingkou.” Li Wei, director of the Marine Management Division of the Ocean and Fisheries Bureau of Yingkou City, pointed out that most of the current marine pollution comes from terrestrial sources, and the most serious impact on the Liaodong Bay ecology is still from the Daliao River.

The Daliao River belongs to the Liao River system, and upstream, it receives industrial wastewater and domestic sewage from cities such as Shenyang, Fushun, Benxi, Liaoyang, Anshan, and Panjin. Yingkou is located at the estuary downstream.

Li Guoying, director of the Environmental Monitoring Center Station of the Yingkou Environmental Protection Bureau, pointed out that the pollution of the Liaodong Bay is not only related to coastal areas but also involves cities through which the rivers flowing into the sea pass longitudinally. Before entering Yingkou, the water quality of the Daliao River was already classified as sub-class V, and comprehensive management requires overall planning by Liaoning Province.

However, coordinating a major river and multiple cities is not an easy task. Taking Yingkou as an example, in 2008 and 2010, the provincial environmental protection department and the national environmental protection department respectively ordered the Yingkou Paper Mill to suspend operations for rectification and set deadlines for pollution control. However, the Yingkou Paper Mill continued to operate against regulations, discharging untreated wastewater directly into the Daliao River. A local official said that the Yingkou Paper Mill is a state-owned enterprise in the locality, involving the livelihoods of hundreds of people and social stability, so the government naturally hopes that the factory can continue to operate.

As China's only inland sea, the Bohai Sea covers an area of only 77,000 square kilometers with an average depth of less than 20 meters, and its water body can only be exchanged once every 60 years (some experts say about 200 years). Rich in fishery, salt, oil, and shoreline resources, before the 1990s, the Bohai Sea mainly served functions such as fishing, salt production, and shipping. Since then, its rich resources have ushered it into an era of large-scale industrial development.

With the rise of the Bohai Economic Circle, the water quality of the Bohai Sea has plummeted drastically. From 2006 to 2010, the GDP of the Bohai Economic Circle soared from 5.5 trillion yuan to 8.7 trillion yuan, an increase of 58%; the polluted sea area rose from 11,890 square kilometers (about 15%) to 16,990 square kilometers (about 22%). According to the "National Environmental Quality Report," "the water quality of the nearshore areas of the Bohai Sea in 2010 was poor, rated as moderately polluted."

The Jinzhou Bay once experienced a phenomenon where heavy metal zinc in the seabed mud exceeded standards by more than 2000 times, lead exceeded standards by more than 300 times, and there was no biological life within a 7-square-kilometer beach area near the discharge outlet, known as a "dead beach."

The Bohai Sea, known as the "cradle of fisheries," is the source of marine fishery resources for the entire northern region. However, the Bohai Sea fishery has now disappeared, and even officials from the State Oceanic Administration have publicly commented that "the function of the Bohai Sea as a fishing ground has basically been lost."

Mystery of Exceedance

According to the "2010 China Environmental Quality Report," the water quality of the nearshore areas of the Bohai Sea is poor, rated as moderately polluted. The main pollutants are inorganic nitrogen, active phosphate salts, petroleum, lead, and cadmium.

Among these pollutants, inorganic nitrogen and active phosphate salts account for a larger proportion, with the exceedance rate of inorganic nitrogen reaching as high as 95%. However, both belong to nutrients that can be decomposed and converted through the self-purification ability of seawater, mainly originating from the discharge of agricultural sewage and urban domestic sewage; while lead and cadmium are heavy metals, along with petroleum, resulting from industrial pollution. Although they account for a smaller proportion, the ocean itself cannot decompose them, causing greater harm to the environment and lasting effects for decades or even centuries.

80% of the pollution in the Bohai Sea originates from land and is mostly discharged into the sea via rivers or pipelines. For a long time, China's economic development has been dominated by land-based thinking, and the governance of the marine environment has been neglected and ignored. Often, the more economically developed the region, the more severe the situation of exceeding pollution discharge standards. In 2010, monitoring results from the Tianjin Ocean Bureau on 14 various types of discharge outlets showed that all monitored discharge outlets in 2010 exceeded emission standards.

However, what few people know is that the complete exceedance of discharge standards at marine outlets does not necessarily mean that enterprises along the coast are discharging sewage beyond standards. On the contrary, environmental protection bureaus in various places have all indicated that enterprises' sewage discharge has reached the emission standards.

Zhang Jingguo, Director of the Marine Environment Department of the Tianjin Ocean Bureau, pointed out that the reasons for pollution in China's marine environment, including the Bohai Sea, not only lie in the involvement of multiple departments, making coordination extremely difficult, but also in the fact that emission standards are formulated by multiple authorities, leading to inconsistent standards.

After enterprises discharge sewage into rivers, which then flow into the sea, it involves three departments – the Environmental Protection Bureau supervises enterprise sewage discharge, the river administration department manages the rivers, and the Ocean Bureau oversees the sea; there are three standards – the "Comprehensive Sewage Discharge Standard", the "Surface Water Environmental Quality Standard", and the "Seawater Quality Standard".

Take chemical oxygen demand (COD), one of the main pollutants discharged, as an example. The "Comprehensive Sewage Discharge Standard" requires that the lowest level of enterprise sewage discharge should be within 120 mg/L; the lowest fifth-level standard for surface water requires that it must be controlled within 40 mg/L; the lowest requirement for seawater quality is even lower.

Inconsistent standards mean that even if enterprises, as the source of pollution, meet discharge standards, the discharge outlets at the end of the process will still significantly exceed standards. The Ocean Bureau, responsible for monitoring seawater quality, is managed vertically by the industry, while the Environmental Protection Bureau, responsible for monitoring enterprise sewage discharge, is subordinate to the local government and needs to consider local interests. Clearly, the current multi-departmental, multi-standard management system cannot effectively curb the deterioration of the marine ecosystem.

"Although foreign countries have unified discharge standards, we also need to consider the economic development status of a country," said an official from the Yingkou Environmental Protection Bureau. Liaoning Province has the strictest requirements for sewage discharge nationwide, with enterprises required to reduce chemical oxygen demand (COD) to below 50mg/L. To meet discharge standards, enterprises need to spend billions of yuan purchasing environmental protection equipment. If discharge standards are further increased, many enterprises may go bankrupt due to their inability to bear the huge costs.

Cost of Unregulated Competition

Multi-departmental management and inconsistent pollution control standards allow excessive sewage to be discharged into the ocean; unregulated competition and the proliferation of heavy industrial projects make the Bohai Sea ecosystem face the threat of excessive total pollutant discharge.

An insider from the Tianjin Ocean Bureau pointed out that in the past, pollution control standards were low, but the total amount of emissions was also small. Now, although enterprises' sewage discharge standards are constrained by national standards, the absolute quantity has greatly increased. Once the total amount of sewage discharge exceeds the self-purification capacity of seawater, the quality of seawater will deteriorate rapidly.

According to relevant statistical data, from the end of the last century to 2009, the total GDP of the Bohai Rim region increased from 1.3 trillion yuan to 7 trillion yuan; the total amount of pollutants discharged annually from the land into the Bohai Sea was more than 600,000 tons, and in 2009, this figure soared to 940,000 tons.

Unregulated competition in the Bohai Rim has increased pollutant emissions. Zheng Gui bin, deputy director of the Shandong Academy of Social Sciences and marine expert, pointed out that the fishery administration, transportation, water conservancy, and environmental protection departments involved in the ocean have communication mechanisms, and the provinces and municipalities also have platforms for joint meetings. However, due to the lack of an overall plan for the Bohai Rim and inadequate control of industrial layouts, coastal areas inevitably compete to launch large-scale heavy industrial projects for their own benefits.

Professor Zhou Yixing from the Urban and Regional Planning Department of Peking University also believes that the Bohai Rim has yet to form a single economic zone but rather consists of three secondary economic zones, each with its own economic characteristics and industrial layout. The Bohai Rim remains in a tripartite division pattern.

Looking at the Bohai Economic Circle, each "lord" has its own development plan. Tianjin is building the Binhai New Area, Hebei is constructing the Caofeidian Development Zone, the Shandong Peninsula Blue Economy Zone and the Yellow River Delta High-efficiency Ecological Economic Zone have entered the national strategic level, and Liaoning has planned the "five points and one line" coastal strategy.

Amidst the enthusiastic construction fever along the Bohai Rim coast, heavy chemical industries, with the highest output value, the longest industrial chain, and the most job creation, have become the focus of competition among the three provinces and one municipality of the Bohai Rim. For example, petrochemicals have become Tianjin's largest industry; although the "Yellow Triangle" of Shandong is a high-efficiency ecological economic zone, its core cities of Dongying, Binzhou, and Weifang are vigorously launching petrochemical projects, with refining capacities approaching 100 million tons; the northeastern coastal area around Dalian is also densely populated with chemical plants.

Taking the South Port Industrial Zone of the Tianjin Binhai New Area as an example, this "world-important heavy chemical base" will have a total investment of 800 billion yuan by 2023, providing 200,000 employment opportunities, and these 200,000 people can create a trillion yuan worth of output. Tianjin's GDP in 2010 was one trillion yuan, and a single South Port Industrial Zone can recreate a Tianjin. It is therefore understandable why all regions are eager to attract chemical projects. The integrated project of producing one million tons of ethylene, with an investment of 20.1 billion yuan, was fiercely contested between Tianjin and Dalian, eventually landing in the then-Dagang District of Tianjin.

Heavy chemical industries contribute the most to GDP but also cause the most pollution to marine ecosystems. The main pollutants in the Bohai Sea are inorganic nitrogen, active phosphate salts, petroleum, lead, and cadmium, with the latter three pollutants directly related to the petrochemical industry. From pollution cases, whether it is the Tazman Sea incident in Tianjin, the Sinopec pollution accident in Dalian, or the ConocoPhillips pollution accident in the Penglai 19-3 oilfield, they are all tragedies caused by the petrochemical industry. After the Sinopec oil spill accident in Dalian, the compensation for the local ecological environment was to build another refinery there.

Moreover, large-scale heavy industrial projects built along the coast inevitably occupy and destroy large areas of wetlands and tidal flats.

In 2008 alone, the Bohai coastal areas of the three provinces and one municipality have already planned or are currently constructing 28 reclamation projects, covering an area of 807 square kilometers. Someone calculated that at this speed, it would take only 90 years for the Bohai Sea to be filled up. Wetlands and tidal flats have natural purification functions and are called the "kidneys of the earth." Major polluting elements such as nitrogen and phosphorus in agricultural and urban sewage settle, filter, and decompose here. If wetlands and tidal flats disappear in large quantities due to land reclamation, sewage will be discharged directly into the sea, losing its buffering function.

What worries Zhang Jingguo, Director of the Marine Environment Department of the Tianjin Ocean Bureau, the most is that every heavy industrial project launched not only leads to a surge in total sewage discharge but also poses a significant risk to the marine ecosystem. If a project experiences a leakage accident similar to the Sinopec incident in Dalian or the ConocoPhillips incident, the Bohai Sea ecosystem may suffer severe damage, and the achievements of pollution control by environmental protection institutions over several or even dozens of years may be wasted.
