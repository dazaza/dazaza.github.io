Valentine's Day

Hashimoto chats about Japan (Season 2): Valentine's Day in Japan by Takanori Hashimoto

Author's note: Valentine's Day has taken on new meaning in Japan. Despite various opinions from all sides and the occasional financial burden, as long as the function of facilitating communication between men and women remains, Valentine's Day will continue.

It’s almost that time of year again – Valentine’s Day, and the girls around me are already preparing chocolates. Most will buy over 20 boxes to give away. In Japan, St. Valentine's Day is generally a day when women express their feelings for men by giving them sweet chocolates. It is said that some merchants took advantage of the shyness of young girls, encouraging them to give chocolates not only to their loved ones but also to those around them, turning the Western holiday into a tool for Japanese department stores to promote chocolate sales. Under such promotion, women began buying the most desirable gifts for the men they like. Each country has its own cultural background and soil. Valentine's Day in Japan isn't like it is in China, where boys must give something to girls. Instead, it serves as a bridge to increase contact between men and women. The reason is that two people who were originally unfamiliar or unknown can first exchange small gifts and then reciprocate, increasing their understanding until they can hold hands. This might be a good choice. Recently, due to Japan's economic downturn, men feel a heavy burden to reciprocate after receiving chocolates on Valentine's Day, making it a factor of social instability in certain aspects.

Valentine's Day in Japan is a chocolate festival, a day for men and women. Although Valentine's Day is not originally from Japan, its popularity there dates back only to the last 40 years. Based on my observations, the true Valentine's Day should be Christmas, when many couples progress from dating to engagement, and finally to commitment. However, the origin of Japan's Valentine's Day was initiated by Japanese department stores to promote unsold chocolates. Therefore, I once told Japanese students that the current trend of celebrating Valentine's Day in China is similar to Christmas in Japan. Last Christmas, a small protest with about 20 participants occurred in the bustling Shibuya district of Tokyo, guarded by approximately 10 policemen. The protest was against Christmas and Valentine's Day. The organizers were part of Japan's "herbivore men" group, who have difficulty finding partners, so they protested against using holidays to find partners and opposed sexual relations during these holidays. In fact, this protest revealed a small secret about Japan's Valentine's Day: the probability of sexual relations between men and women increases significantly during various Western holidays. Japanese women love the romantic atmosphere of festivals like Christmas and Valentine's Day. In a romantic setting, under warm candlelight, when a man confesses with seductive words, nine out of ten women fall for it. The business at love hotels around Mount Rokko in Osaka is especially good on Christmas or Valentine's Day evenings. Some men even hire event planners to design all the activities for Christmas day to make them novel and touch the heart.

In Japan, there are also people opposing Valentine's Day, namely social workers. According to data, the frequency of sexual encounters among young Japanese people rises sharply around Valentine's Day or Christmas. Besides those who plan carefully, many relationships occur hastily, resulting in an increase in unintended pregnancies and AIDS cases in Japan. A social worker told me: About 73% of Japanese women do not want to get pregnant, yet most of them don't even know how to prevent pregnancy. Despite Japan's developed sex culture, many high school students and young women don't know how to use condoms, so the post-Valentine's Day and Christmas period becomes a peak time for pregnancies.

Finally, a public opinion survey in Japan can explain a lot: Among women aged 10-30 across Japan (69%) believe that the significance of Valentine's Day lies in expressing gratitude to the recipient; secondly, it enhances communication between both parties (49%); and thirdly, Valentine's Day is an interesting activity that attracts attention (32%). However, as age increases, among Japanese women over 30, 78% advocate abolishing Valentine's Day activities, probably because older women no longer need to meet and interact with the opposite sex through Valentine's Day. Regardless, Japan's Valentine's Day has lasted over 40 years amidst various opinions. As long as the function of promoting communication between men and women remains, Valentine's Day will continue.
