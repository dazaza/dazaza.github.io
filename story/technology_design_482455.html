Caribbean Journey (Part 1) (with photos)

By Shi Hanbing

Introduction

Brother Guangyuan and his sister-in-law had repeatedly invited me to visit the Caribbean countries, but I was always too busy to make it. However, the stories they told me about the Caribbean Sea deeply attracted me. Additionally, after being on edge for over a decade, I was mentally exhausted, so it was a good opportunity to take a break during the May Day holiday.

After flying for more than 9 hours from Charles de Gaulle Airport in France, we arrived at St. Martin Island. To travel from France to the Caribbean countries, one must go through St. Martin Island. The island, which is only 88 square kilometers, is divided between two countries: the Netherlands (southern part of St. Martin Island) and France (northern part of St. Martin Island). The Dutch and French regions each follow different systems, with a population of approximately 70,000 people.

Among the hundreds of passengers, there were only two Chinese individuals: Mr. Wang from Shandong and myself. At Charles de Gaulle Airport, Mr. Wang was surprised that I, as a first-time traveler to the Caribbean Sea, was allowed to leave the country. He said that even if all the procedures were legal, mainland Chinese citizens are generally prohibited from traveling to Caribbean countries without any explanation given. Moreover, St. Martin has a strong bias against Chinese people. When they see Chinese people, especially groups of Chinese, they often deport them without asking questions. It's difficult to leave the country, and even harder to enter the Caribbean Sea, which might explain why there are so few Chinese people seen along the way.

Discussing the root cause, Mr. Wang was very saddened. Aside from the famous incident 20 years ago, there are probably two reasons: one is the past group smuggling incidents involving Chinese people, and the other is the frequent toxic food incidents in China, which have been reported by foreign media such as CNN. As a result, many people in this region view Chinese people as monsters. They fear those who poison their food, reasoning that since these people are so ruthless to themselves, they would certainly do anything to others.

I couldn't confirm whether what Mr. Wang said was true. However, on St. Martin Island, we indeed encountered the situation he described: border inspection personnel immediately became cautious upon seeing a Chinese passport, taking away our passports and making Mr. Wang and me wait aside instead of directly allowing us entry like citizens from other countries. Then, a big fat woman came over and took us to a nearby shabby office (Mr. Wang said that the working conditions here are all like this because taxpayers are strictly monitored and the government is frugal). The fat woman processed our boarding passes while keeping us within her sight. Despite Mr. Wang having worked in Antigua in the Caribbean Sea for over 6 years, and I possessing an invitation letter and visa from the Antiguan government sent by my brother and sister-in-law, as well as a visa from Dominica.

It was a long period of time. Most of the border inspection personnel on St. Martin Island are women, and they are all exceptionally fat, with large waists and enormous hips (apologies for repeating phrases used in elementary school essays, but I truly couldn't think of better words). They all wear tight clothing, looking full and ready to burst at any moment. Each time the big fat woman holding our passports walked past me with her heavy steps, I worried anxiously. I joked to Wang, "Thank goodness the clothes are sturdy, otherwise, that pile of meat bouncing out could kill us both! Would our company consider this a work-related injury?" Wang ignored my jest and looked lost. He said, "If we had dignity back home, who would willingly endure such humiliation." He told me many stories of Chinese people being humiliated, leaving me feeling extremely sorrowful, and the waiting time made me feel an indescribable sense of shame. Overseas Chinese originally emerged under such difficult circumstances!

However, at that moment, I wasn't overly concerned. My brother and sister-in-law not only have a good reputation in the Caribbean region but also maintain close relationships with various governments. They coming to pick me up would end this torment. Sure enough, shortly after, I saw my brother and sister-in-law. They had specially traveled from New York the day before just to meet me, and I felt a mix of sadness and joy, extremely happy. Everything afterward was arranged by them. Otherwise, someone like me who gets lost even when going shopping domestically wouldn't dare to come alone. I didn't tell my parents before leaving the country because I didn't want them to worry about me getting lost. After returning home, I would inform them, so they wouldn't be anxious for me.

My brother and sister-in-law told me many interesting stories about the various Caribbean countries. One of the standards for judging whether a woman is beautiful here (and also the most important standard) is the size of her hips; the bigger a woman's hips, the more sexy and beautiful she is. They said that on St. Martin Island, there is a woman with exceptionally large hips. Whenever she walks by, all the men hold their breath as if stunned by her super sexy beauty. Only after her big hips pass do the men look at each other and smile contentedly. Everyone in the country knows this beautiful pair of hips, and catching a glimpse of them is considered a great honor. This reminded me of the big-hipped woman who processed our boarding passes earlier. She turned out to be a stunning beauty. Every time her incredibly large hips swayed gracefully in front of us, we failed to appreciate it. Perhaps many black friends were envying us: "Look, these two guys have great fortune!"

The people here are extremely confident, especially the women. All men compliment every woman they see, calling them sweet names like "sweetheart" or "darling," regardless of age or familiarity. From children to centenarians, everyone is addressed this way. Consequently, no woman in the entire Caribbean Sea feels unattractive, all filled with confidence. Weight loss pills sold here would definitely not sell a single pill. Women's confidence is genuine and evident on their faces. Of course, this is another story. It seems that the improvement of comprehensive national strength and men's compliments to women are also crucial aspects.

Traveling in Antigua

Similar to St. Martin Island, Antigua is extremely strict and harsh regarding Chinese entry.

Antigua is a limestone island. In 1493, during Columbus's second voyage to America, he reached Antigua Island and named it after the Antigua Cathedral in Seville, Spain. In 1632, the British occupied the island, bringing African slaves to cultivate tobacco and sugarcane. In 1967, it became an associated state of Britain and established an internal self-governing government, but foreign affairs and defense were still controlled by Britain. On November 1, 1981, it declared independence, becoming a constitutional monarchy and a member of the Commonwealth. The total population is less than 100,000.

This island nation is renowned for its white sandy beaches, ranking among the best beaches in the world alongside Bermuda and Barbados.

When I arrived in Antigua by plane from St. Martin Island, it was already dark (the local time differs from Beijing time by 12 hours; 7 PM here is 7 AM in Beijing).

With my brother and sister-in-law present, everything went smoothly. Brother Guangyuan drove the car to the airport exit, and we hurried to our accommodation. Along the way, we passed by the bank of the big fraudster Allen Stanford in Antigua, feeling quite astonished. Allen Stanford was once the biggest boss on the island, akin to a monarch. In 2006, at the request of the Antiguan government, Prince Edward, Earl of Wessex, granted Stanford a knighthood. Now, "the swindler has gone, leaving behind only the empty tower," and Allen Stanford's assets have been acquired by others.

After arriving at the hotel, my brother and sister-in-law helped arrange everything for me. I lay down and fell asleep immediately. Waking up in the middle of the night (due to jet lag), I pulled the curtains and saw a watery expanse outside, resembling a dream. After reading for three hours, I went back to sleep.

The next morning, I got up and went to the hotel for breakfast. A few black service staff were chatting leisurely, each appearing very cheerful. Their smiles infected me deeply. I gave my room number and ordered meals without needing to sign, as they trusted me completely.

In Antigua, every person I met greeted me warmly. At the hotel, a black man even shook hands with me as if we were old friends. Thinking I had met him before, I tried hard to recall who he was, but then he kindly asked me, "What's your name?" I remembered what my sister-in-law had said: people here greet everyone they meet.

I usually don't eat much Western cuisine, and some of my actions were stiff enough to make me cringe. Regardless of being left-handed or right-handed, everyone uses a knife and fork, each with a specific role. I use my right hand exclusively, putting down the knife to use the fork, or sometimes, unable to bear it anymore, I intervene like a knight in shining armor. A chubby woman came over to demonstrate for me, warming my heart. Truly, she is an excellent public servant! According to my brother and sister-in-law, in Antigua, if you accidentally get lost, locals with cars will drive you to your destination. If your car breaks down on the road, they will voluntarily ask if you need help, making you feel like this country is full of Lei Fengs everywhere. Moreover, vehicles always let pedestrians go first, reasoning that cars are faster anyway, so they should yield to walkers. Surprisingly, civic qualities reach such high levels without the refinement of advanced concepts like the five stresses, four beauties, three loves, three representatives, eight honors, and eight disgraces.

Then, my journey officially began.

Figure 1: The natural scenery in Antigua is exceptionally beautiful, especially the white sandy beaches. Standing on the balcony of the hotel, one can see a vast expanse of blue. The blue sky blends seamlessly with the blue sea. Antigua has no chemical plants, only non-polluting enterprises like beverage processing. Of course, the people here lack the extensive chemical knowledge we possess. From elementary school to university, education is entirely free. Later, parents suggested that tuition fees should be waived, and if the government also covered the cost of school uniforms, they would be even happier. Thus, to please the public, the government also assumed the cost of uniforms; healthcare is provided free of charge except for a $5 EC (Eastern Caribbean Dollar) registration fee borne by patients. A Chinese person here fell ill, and despite not fully recovering, the doctor insisted on not discharging them until recovery, demonstrating a responsible attitude to the end. (In terms of welfare, other Caribbean countries are roughly similar.)

Figure 2: The crystal-clear seawater sparkles even on the beach. According to Antigua's laws, all natural resources are equally open to every Antiguan citizen. Even beggars can freely access any beach. No one dares to enclose an area for profit or repeatedly raise prices.

Figure 3: Due to the financial crisis, the number of tourists has sharply decreased, leaving only Brother Guangyuan, my sister-in-law, and me on the beach.

Figure 4: When going out to play, we often encounter flocks of sheep along the way, which are free-range animals belonging to some families. There is no concern about losing them, as no one steals them. When my sister-in-law took me to the store to buy things, my passport and cash were in my laptop bag inside the car. When we returned, we found the car window was open, but the items were still there. People here live very leisurely, unlike us who work hard. Their social security is relatively sound, and the public prioritizes enjoying life. Society is fairly transparent.

Figure 5: Another flock of sheep encountered.

Figure 6: This is a sales point built by the Antiguan government for vendors. This management method ensures people's livelihood while maintaining tidiness. Compared to our armed urban management model, it is unclear whether this approach is more advanced.

Figure 7: People here have a weak awareness of making money, and few shops are fully open. They believe that enjoying life is central, and earning money is also for enjoyment. Therefore, as long as they are happy, they are unwilling to expend energy for profit. This phenomenon is unprecedented for me—am I stupid or am I at a higher level?

Figure 8: An Antiguan market where people also trade on the roadside, but it appears chaotic. Antiguans are very honest and sincere, trusting what you say almost without suspicion. However, their mental arithmetic skills are poor, and they highly admire those who can calculate as accurately as calculators. For example, selling fish here does not involve categorization. Regardless of whether the fish is expensive elsewhere, such as barracuda or grouper, they are all priced the same because they fear complicated calculations. Sometimes, fish are simply sold in piles.

Figure 9: A lone speaker in front of the market entrance. As we passed, he was passionately speaking: "Your desire is your right, your right is your opportunity, your opportunity is your destiny..." His impromptu speech was full of philosophy without reading from a script. A ragged person might be a thinker. My brother and sister-in-law told me that in Antigua, even beggars face the prime minister without inferiority. They often directly seek help from the prime minister, and if their problems aren't solved, they will speak frankly and rudely.

Figure 10: A beauty encountered on the street. I've learned to evaluate beauty through their eyes. This beauty should be considered heavyweight by my standards. She moved swiftly, and I managed to capture this photo after chasing her halfway. Amazing how healthy obesity can be!

Figure 11: A church. Most Antiguans practice Christianity or Catholicism, and this is a famous local church.

Figure 12: Jessica, the general manager hired by my brother and sister-in-law in Antigua. Her appearance resembles Condoleezza Rice, with quick thinking, clear logic, dedication to her job, and is considered talented in local management.

Figure 13: A casino. In Antigua, casinos are legal, and the government dispatches supervisors to oversee the tools and monitor the gambling process to ensure fair gaming. Some unemployed people in Antigua also frequent here. If they discover government officials (such as the prime minister) gambling, they become wealthy. They chase the official demanding money, and if refused, they insult them. Consequently, no high-ranking officials easily show up here. On the other hand, due to low civil servant salaries, some civil servants with many children often work two jobs, moonlighting at casinos at night. That evening, my brother and sister-in-law took me to the casino, and we played blackjack. I purely stumbled upon wins sometimes, leading to collective losses, causing a professional gambler immense grief as he continuously lost money. But when facing me, he smiled immediately because he knew I really didn't understand. Later, my sister-in-law taught me, and together we earned $200. Then, a fat tax bureau employee took over dealing, quickly reclaiming our winnings. Truly deserving of being a tax collector! We stopped because we were too tired, ending with a profit of $85. Here, the casino rules are excellent. It is worth emphasizing that the casinos here are all for entertainment and are completely different from the extravagant gambling of corrupt officials from China.

Figure 14: Inside the casino. Casinos worldwide prohibit photography, but I quickly snapped a picture.

Figure 18: My sister-in-law picked me a pair of fat-headed doll sandals. I hadn't worn shoes like this since I was very young, instantly reminding me of childhood.

Figure 19: Returning to the hotel, we found two sheep leisurely grazing on the grass. Such a nice hotel, they surely know how to choose a spot :) But no one disturbed them, everything returns to nature. Neither the sheep nor the people are discriminated against, and no one claims that the sheep have intruded on their territory and drives them away, let alone humans. I don't know why, but I'm deeply moved!

Figure 23: That unidentified floating object is me, drifting into the sea while sleeping. Mastering this skill is crucial. If learned, one could drift all the way back to the great motherland, saving on airfare, of course, assuming no crocodiles or sharks have any improper intentions toward me.

Figure 24: Sunbathing, realizing I've gained weight recently. Of course, another possibility is that my eyes have become smaller, otherwise, why does everything I see appear round and plump? Checking in the mirror at night confirmed my fears: my eyes may have really become smaller. My goodness!
