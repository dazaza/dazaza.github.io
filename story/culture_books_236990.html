"The Secret of the River" Su Tong's source of inspiration for writing

by jqrxass on 2009-09-12 15:10:49

As one of the most distinctive writers in contemporary China, Su Tong is adept at intertwining the pure, sensitive perspective of a child with the world of women filled with desire, loneliness, and distortion in his novels. Where does this strange literary concept come from? What are the writing secrets of Su Tong, who is known as "the male writer who understands women best"?

In his new book "The Secrets of the River" recently published by the Writer's Publishing House, Su Tong recalls his humid, transparent, and slightly unsettling childhood life through autobiographical essays. In describing the customs, lives, and characters of the South, he reveals all his "writing secrets". At the same time, he gratefully recounts the details that have had a turning point impact on his reading and writing career.

"The Secrets of the River" Related Content

Su Tong has always been praised as "the male writer who understands women best and excels in writing about them." His deep sympathy and sadness for the women he depicts have been greatly affirmed by female readers. However, few people know that Su Tong's understanding, sympathy, and sadness towards women and their fates mostly come from the memories and reflections of his childhood observations and feelings. He said: "I believe that whether it be love or hatred, the heaviest baggage in a writer's life may just be their childhood memories. Regardless of whether these memories are dark or bright, we must carry them and cherish them. Besides this, there is no other way to deal with them."

First Volume: Fragments of Memory

In fact, the soul of the river is always much richer and deeper than you can describe, like the same river my mother described, which was also the river visible from our back window.

What an amazing story: One winter, the river froze over, and my mother was eager to get to the factory on the other side of the river. She was in a hurry, so she rashly treated the frozen river as a bridge. My mother said that after taking a few steps on the ice, she regretted it. The ice layer was very fragile and thin, and she heard the dangerous sound of broken ice under her feet. She became hesitant, but going back was even more dangerous. So, my mother prayed to the river while walking towards the other side. Guess what? She crossed the river smoothly! For me, this is a tale from Arabian Nights, and I don't believe this story. I asked my mother how she prayed to the river, and she laughed and said, how could I pray? I asked the river to let me pass, let me pass, and the river let me pass!

Second Volume: Literary Career

Pleasant reading experiences occur several times a year.

The deepest impression I have is from reading J.D. Salinger's "The Catcher in the Rye." At that time, I was studying at Beijing Normal University, and a good friend recommended and lent me the book. I finished it in just one day. I remember when I finished the last page, the classroom was already empty, and the janitor passed through the corridor, turning off the lights one by one. I walked out of the classroom, feeling a melancholic darkness inside. I imagined the journey of the American boy in the city, and I imagined having a little sister like "Old Phoebe," with whom I could joke around and confide my troubles.

For a period of time, Salinger was the writer I was most obsessed with. I read all of his works that I could find. I couldn't explain why I was so fond of him, maybe it was the youth enlightenment and free-flowing language sense that deeply moved me.

Third Volume: Life Legends

The marital life between Sister Hong and Hong San was not harmonious. They argued at midnight, and the content of their arguments seemed somewhat obscene, so they lowered their voices. After arguing for a while without solving the problem, the rude Hong San started hitting her. That brute Hong San, used to handling steel ingots, how heavy were his blows? Sister Hong screamed from being hit. The neighbors listened with their ears perked up, expecting her to cry and make a fuss, but the result was disappointing. Sister Hong valued her dignity; she only cried and didn't say any foul words.

The next day, although Sister Hong remained silent, everyone could see the hatred in her eyes and heart. She didn't cook dinner for Hong San, but instead, she cooked more snails for herself, harboring deep hatred. With a "hiss," she sucked them in angrily, and with a "pop," she spat out the snail shells angrily. Suddenly, she threw down the toothpick and stopped eating. The neighboring women nearby were puzzled, then they heard her say, why eat snails? Eat something good, eat shrimp, eat crabs, eat ducks, eat until he goes bankrupt, only then will I feel relieved!
