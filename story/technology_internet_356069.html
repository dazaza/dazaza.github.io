[Parenting] Don't Let Early Education Turn into Psychological Abuse 3

by kaning05 on 2010-05-26 18:32:22

Recently, the pediatric department of a hospital received a little boy aged about two years old named Yangyang. Yangyang was originally lively and active, loving to play and laugh with an extroverted character, and he would never feel timid when seeing strangers. However, in the past half month, Yangyang's temper gradually became more and more irritable, crying and making a fuss without reason. His appetite also got worse and worse, and he even threw up the food that his mother fed him. During the day, Yangyang always looked listless, and at night, he often woke up in the middle of the night, had nightmares, and cried.

The doctor conducted multiple examinations for Yangyang, but the results showed no physical discomfort. The doctor carefully asked Yangyang's mother about the child's recent living habits and understood the cause of Yangyang's illness: It turned out that two months ago, the ambitious mother started early education for Yangyang, making him watch English conversation DVDs for one and a half hours every day, then playing intelligence development games for about an hour. This made Yangyang, who was naturally active, very uncomfortable. However, the mother, eager for her son's success, insisted on letting Yangyang complete his learning tasks every day to achieve the goal of cultivating her son into a "genius"...

Dr. Yan Xiaoqin, the chief physician of the Child Health Care Department of the Second Affiliated Hospital of Zhengzhou University, deduced that the reason why Yangyang had poor appetite and mental fatigue might be due to his mother's early education. Because for young children, too much coercion and sudden changes in living habits can make children feel lost, and excessive restriction of children's activity freedom, such as implementing excessive early education against the nature of children, has actually formed a kind of psychological abuse unintentionally.

Following the doctor's advice, the mother no longer forced Yangyang to do anything he didn't want to do, but accompanied him to visit parks and play with toys, letting him do whatever he wanted. After half a month, all of Yangyang's previous symptoms had completely disappeared, and he regained his lively and extroverted personality.

In recent years, early education has gradually become popular. Many preschool children start their "student life" at the age of two or three, practicing writing, learning English words, reciting ancient poems, etc., under their parents' requirements. If early education is not properly implemented, it may cause psychological abuse to young children, and severe cases may even lead to tragedies. Therefore, Dr. Yan Xiaoqin does not advocate early education for young children. Even if early education is implemented, it must be adapted to the intellectual development level of the child. It is best to learn while playing and playing while learning. For example, when taking the child to visit a park, knowledge can be imparted to the child at any time, through vision, hearing, and touch to increase the child's knowledge. Such "early education" is far more scientific than simply making the child memorize books and write.
