Stop children from being hurt any more -- unsafe milk and powdered milk...

by zzf000zxye0 on 2011-07-01 16:47:22

The news about Saint Yuan baby formula and infant precocious puberty has spread far and wide. The Ministry of Health has intervened in the investigation, the Ministry of Agriculture has provided methods, and experts have made statements. However, there will be no conclusion to this matter. According to reports (http://finance.sina.com.cn/roll/20100812/03008466324.shtml), the investigation entrusted by the Ministry of Health to Hubei Provincial Department of Health first put pressure on children and their parents. They did not test the formula first but instead focused on physical examinations for three children, even conducting some checks that I, as a parent, find hard to accept (I kept thinking that if I were the parent, I would never let my child suffer like this). For example, they drew twice the normal amount of blood from the children (I wonder how much problematic formula they would need to feed them?). Moreover, CT scans were used to determine bone age for infants, knowing well that the radiation dose of a CT scan is very high. Some experts even believe that one CT scan is equivalent to 200 X-ray exposures (http://www.zjyl120.com/ac_article/2008/11/2008111312250095460.html). To determine bone age, I think ordinary X-rays should suffice. Also, these experts even suggested that the children take sedatives for ultrasound examinations (fortunately rejected by the parents). The report also stated that the children were frightened by being forced to undergo examinations in different clinics. What's more, at around 6:30 PM, when the Ministry of Health needed results that evening, the female infant and her parents were still unable to leave. Therefore, regardless of the conclusions drawn by the Hubei expert group, I believe that this inspection did not respect the rights of the children and their parents, indicating issues with medical ethics and conduct, and it should be condemned. At the same time, I regret that the parents allowed their children to endure such torment and feel sympathy for the children who suffered undue fright and harm. I believe that the medical experts of the Hubei Provincial Department of Health expert group should apologize to the three infants and their families. Relevant departments should investigate whether they informed the parents of the pros and cons before conducting harmful tests like CT scans, whether they informed them of their right to refuse the tests, and whether they signed informed consent forms.

Regarding the identical consultation results announced to the three families: "Consultation data shows no problems, it is simple breast development, not necessarily precocious puberty." Many pediatric experts remained unusually silent upon learning of the above consultation results.

Additionally, I suggest that consumers and parents carefully understand the statements made by experts from the Ministry of Agriculture. Some insiders and experts believe that hormones are drugs, and milk, as food, should contain no hormones at all. Journalists noticed that in the newly formulated "National Food Safety Standard Milk Powder (GB19644m2010)," there was no mention of estrogen detection projects. Spokesperson for the Ministry of Health, Deng Haihua, clearly stated that estrogen should not be detected in milk powder. "What is meant by not detecting estrogen refers to not detecting artificially added synthetic estrogen substances," explained researcher Wang Shuhuai from the Chinese Veterinary Drug Supervision Institute. These two words are important because the expressions used by the Ministry of Health and the Ministry of Agriculture already differ significantly. That is to say, what the Ministry of Health says does not count; unless it can be proven that the estrogen in the milk powder was artificially added and must be synthetic estrogen (not natural or environmental estrogen), the Ministry of Agriculture considers it unadded, and even if the estrogen in the milk powder could lead to infant precocious puberty, it is not considered to contain estrogen.

Thirdly, many experts do not believe that the estrogen in milk would cause any harm, believing that if it were harmful, it should affect a large number of people, not just three. But I am not so optimistic because there is a clear relationship between estrogen in milk and the occurrence of breast cancer and prostate cancer. Moreover, the damage caused by estrogen, especially environmental estrogens like BPA and NP4, does not strictly follow the dose-effect curve but rather exhibits a certain randomness at low doses. This can be understood as genetic differences or differences in liver and kidney function metabolism, especially based on the immature development of infants. Therefore, blindly consuming estrogen definitely does not benefit health.

However, the above-mentioned points are not significant for most people concerned about their own health or their children's growth. I believe that we should now remain calm. First, we should accept reality: our children have developed precocious puberty or breast development. We should not blindly check them, because even if diagnosed with breast development or precocious puberty, it holds no significance without the expert group's examination. As a parent, I would rather not receive a single cent of compensation than subject my child to such "abusive" examinations (if indeed reported by journalists). Furthermore, if it's merely an issue of breast development, I think stopping the continued intake of estrogen and other drugs would generally allow recovery, without needing blind testing or exposing the child to radiological examinations, which would be counterproductive.

Therefore, everyone should not panic. I believe that currently, in the absence of safety guarantees, the best approach is to avoid milk powder and milk altogether, which is what I do. But this is unrealistic, because children need milk, and some mothers cannot breastfeed due to illness or medication reasons. Artificial hormone-induced lactation produces breast milk similar to estrogen-laden milk powder. So, what should we do? Here, I quote the official food safety expert of our country, Academician Chen Junshi, from http://www.hicq001.com/article/article_4155.html, saying, "Since the overall situation of food safety in our country is good, but there are indeed so many problems that cannot be fundamentally solved in a short period of time? Are consumers helpless and passive? Academician Chen Junshi said, 'For example, if vegetable pesticide residues exceed standards by 5%, no one wants to eat those vegetables, what should we do?' He taught three self-protection measures for food safety. First: Diversify your diet. Diversifying your diet is the first guideline in the dietary guidelines for Chinese residents, ensuring balanced nutrition, which is equally effective against food safety issues. Academician Chen Junshi gave an example: 'Some say that chives easily retain pesticides, so why eat them every day? If you eat them once a week, the risk of pesticide residue greatly diminishes.' In other words, qualified products make up the majority, while unqualified varieties are few. By changing varieties often, potential risks can be reduced and resolved. It benefits both nutrition and food safety."

Although I don't think the overall market for milk and milk powder in our country is good, this statement holds true. However, our parents and milk drinkers indeed make mistakes. That is, always focusing on one brand. From Sanlu's melamine to Saint Yuan's estrogen, it's always been like this. Of course, this helps us trace the causes, but it's not beneficial for children. Because the metabolic pathways and target organs of various toxic substances are different, for instance, melamine is metabolized through the kidneys, while estrogen is mainly metabolized through the liver. If you alternate drinking these, you may not continuously damage one organ, and symptoms might not appear. Long-term use of one brand often stems from parental laziness and economic considerations, such as milk subscription discounts or group purchases, etc., but this is detrimental to health. Therefore, we should heed Academician Chen's advice and diversify milk powder consumption to avoid getting stuck on one tree.

So, I would like to end with one final sentence: Parents, quickly change your baby's milk powder or milk, no matter which brand you're currently using, and frequently switch brands. Mixing different types can also be considered, to prevent further tragedies involving harmful milk powder.

Dr. Yue Dan Wang
