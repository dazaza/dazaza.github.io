An old man spent 21 years building a house in a cave, claiming that it is free for visitors to visit (Picture)

by ztt0288868 on 2010-04-07 14:18:15

This "hall" in the cave is really spacious. There are also three "guest rooms" built inside the cave. The old man has planted many flowers in the stone crevices.

Liang Shusen, a resident of Dington Village, Tanluo Town, Xixiangtang District, Nanning City, is considered an eccentric person. He abandoned the comfort of his big house and chose to live alone in a mountain cave, leading a reclusive life. For 22 years, he has been called the "Cave Man" by his fellow villagers. Liang Shusen has spent all his lifetime savings on "renovating" the cave, saying that he wants to make the cave more beautiful and offer free tours to visitors.

There is a suite in the cave

At around 4 PM on March 31st, under the guidance of an informed person, I arrived at Dington Village. As I entered the village entrance, two impressive buildings caught my eye. In front of the building stood a sign that read "Wholesale Pesticides, Fertilizers." A middle-aged man came out and greeted me with a smile. He was Liang Wei'er, the eldest son of "Cave Man" Liang Shusen.

When talking about his father's "cave life," Liang Wei'er expressed a mix of amusement and helplessness. He told the reporter that his family had plenty of empty rooms in their houses, but his father was particularly fond of living in the cave, which left them as children feeling quite helpless.

After a brief greeting, Liang Wei'er led the reporter to a cave about 3 kilometers away. At the cave entrance, I saw blooming bougainvilleas intertwined with lush green trees, creating a vivid and picturesque scene. Pushing open the wooden door at the cave entrance, I saw a stove for making fire, several pots, and a generator. Liang Shusen was sitting on the sofa waiting. Liang Shusen told the reporter that this was his kitchen, and the generator was used for pumping water.

I noticed that on the right side of the cave, there was a cement staircase. Climbing up the stairs led to a spacious and cozy living room. On the clean cement floor were wooden sofas, coffee tables, dining tables, and other furniture. In one corner of the living room hung a bunch of golden bananas. The layout of the living room was fresh and unique, filled with warmth. Being there made you forget entirely that you were in a cave.

Walking through the living room further inside, there was another spacious hall. On the left side of the hall was a wooden bed, with four corners tied with thick sticks and placed in the stone holes of the cave. A mosquito net hung over the bed. On the right side of the hall were three cement rooms with wooden doors. Opening the wooden doors revealed that each room had a wooden bed with a mosquito net. One of the rooms even had a desk. Liang Shusen said that due to the frequent visits from villagers and outsiders who came to admire the cave, he specially built these three rooms himself a few years ago using cement bricks. "My cave actually has a '3-bedroom, 2-living room' suite," Liang Shusen laughed.

The cave rooms have a hidden charm

"This is 'Maitreya Buddha,' that's 'Guanyin,' and there's the monkey carrying its baby..." Liang Shusen introduced as we walked through the cave. Walking through the cave, I was often attracted by some interesting things: the natural "walls" were covered with various shapes of stalactites. After being carefully decorated and modified by Liang Shusen, these stalactites became lifelike and vivid, evoking admiration.

Even more astonishingly, this cave has a second level. Under Liang Shusen's guidance, we moved from the "hall" to the second level of the cave. "This is the elephant drinking water, that's the fairy descending, and over there is the crane spreading its wings..." Liang Shusen pointed with the light beam from his flashlight and introduced the cave sights like treasures. Following the light from the flashlight, I saw the differently shaped stalactites shimmering faintly in the light, truly resembling fairies gracefully descending and cranes flying high. It was unimaginable how breathtakingly beautiful this cave could be.

Exiting the second level, I arrived at a large "platform." This platform was approximately 20 square meters and was planted with neem trees, kapok trees, cacti, daffodils, and more, forming a lush greenery. A gentle breeze passed by, and the red daffodils swayed gracefully, moving left and right with the wind, full of poetic charm. Continuing upwards along the large "platform," I saw another small "platform." Standing on this small "platform," the green fields and winding roads of the distant villages were clearly visible, giving a sense of "looking down upon all mountains."

It took 21 years to create the cave rooms

"If I were ten years younger, I would definitely connect the entire cave, turning it into a large villa." During the interview, Liang Shusen regretfully mentioned several times that he spent seven or eight years meticulously crafting this cave, using up all his lifetime savings to achieve its current state. However, it still hasn't reached his ideal result. He wanted to fully connect the entire cave, but doing so would require at least another 50,000 to 60,000 yuan, a sum too large for him to manage.

Liang Shusen carved a "chronicle" in the cave, recording every action he took to modify the cave. From the "chronicle," I learned that in 1989, Liang Shusen started building a lime kiln near this cave for convenience while watching the kiln. He began sleeping in the cave and modifying it. In 1996, due to poor business conditions, Liang Shusen had to close his lime kiln, but he had grown accustomed to living in the cave and didn't want to return home. Coincidentally, he discovered two springs in the cave. Since the village often faced water shortages in March and April each year, he decided to dig a well inside the cave. Without hesitation, in 1998, Liang Shusen borrowed a drill machine from a nearby quarry and bought seven boxes of explosives to start digging the well. After six months, the well was completed. He then purchased pipes and water pumps. Whenever the village encountered droughts, he would use the water pump to provide water to the villagers, charging only a minimal "water fee" for diesel and equipment maintenance.

With water available in the cave and a suitable temperature, Liang Shusen was even more reluctant to leave. Thus, he decided to settle in the cave permanently. After settling in, Liang Shusen began contemplating ways to renovate the cave. Each renovation was fraught with difficulties and dangers. To build a "hall" inside the cave, Liang Shusen erected wooden scaffolding on steep cliffs and gradually poured cement onto it. To construct the second level inside the cave, over the course of 21 years, Liang Shusen used primitive methods with chisels, painstakingly carving out paths and constructing bit by bit.

Seeing his father independently complete such a high-difficulty "project," Liang Wei'er couldn't help but marvel. He said that because he was busy with his business, he rarely had time to visit the cave. Unexpectedly, when he came this time, he found it transformed into something akin to a villa.
