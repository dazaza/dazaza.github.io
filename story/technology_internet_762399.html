How Startups Can Compete With Big Companies for Talent

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 15:17:13

A Former Google Exec On How Startups Can Compete With Google To Score Great Talent

Editor's note: The author of this article is Douglas Merrill, founder and CEO of ZestFinance. He was formerly the CIO and Vice President of Engineering at Google. ZestFinance is a financial service technology company located in Los Angeles that leverages big data to help make better credit decisions, providing alternatives for people without bank accounts.

For tech startups, finding good talent is undoubtedly a very headache-inducing issue. Especially when competing with large companies like Facebook and Twitter for talent.

First, let’s face reality: there is a misconception that good software engineers won't leave the tech hub to work in remote areas. For example, in the U.S., many people believe that good software engineers won’t leave the San Francisco Bay Area. If your company isn't nearby, you can't compete with other companies for talent.

In fact, it doesn't matter where your tech company is located. Finding the right talent is the most important thing. Under the limited budget of your company, you cannot compete with larger companies on salary, which will be your weakness.

The recruitment process involves various trade-offs, including whether to use internal recruiters or seek help from headhunting companies. Based on my experience — comparing the results of using these two recruitment methods in both small and large companies — the best employees are often found by internal recruiters. Here are some experiences I have gained along the way:

Use Your Personal Network

Whether you are a company of two or three people, there is really only one way to expand your core team: use your personal network. Pay more attention to friends, family, former colleagues, and even LinkedIn and Facebook contacts around you. This will save you valuable time and money, and I have also found that members obtained through this method usually stay longer and integrate faster with the team.

The challenge with this approach lies in how to find smart people who are different from you. I know this sounds counterintuitive and difficult because some people who are not entirely like you may also be people you don't particularly like. However, you hope that the members of your team dare to question others' views and dare to voice dissenting opinions. Otherwise, team discussions will lose their meaning. You must find a balance within this great but diverse team.

Once you've exhausted your personal resources, what should you do next? Your team has expanded to a certain extent. At this stage, I suggest finding someone internally as early as possible to specialize in recruitment matters.

Internal Recruitment vs. External Agency Recruitment

From my own young company, finding someone to help with recruitment is more cost-effective than seeking external headhunting companies. Let's look at some relevant data:

Typically, recruitment agency fees are about 20%-30% of a new employee's salary, making the cost of recruiting an employee approximately $20,000 to $30,000 per person.

Employees responsible for internal recruitment typically earn salaries ranging from $75,000 to $150,000, but they can help you recruit dozens of engineers annually.

Although early-stage startups tend to avoid fixed costs and prefer to maintain flexibility, in this case, fixed costs are much cheaper than third-party recruitment agencies. For companies planning to hire more than 20 engineers in the coming year, hiring an employee dedicated to recruitment can save over $500,000. This is already a significant amount for many startups.

Don't Limit Your Recruiters

When you're looking for someone to handle internal recruitment, it's best to find someone from your personal network who has worked with you before and can be trusted. I found a colleague who had worked with me at Google and participated in internal recruitment. I had personally witnessed how he built our team, and we felt comfortable working together.

Once you've found someone to handle internal recruitment, provide them with some essential tools — from applicant tracking systems to LinkedIn's paid recruiter subscription.

Then, let them take full charge. Don't forget, you hired them because you trust them and recognize their capabilities, so try not to limit them; let them shine.

Company Culture is Key

Third-party recruitment agencies don't work in your office, so they have no way of knowing your company culture. It's hard to understand from the outside exactly what kind of talent you need or who fits your company culture.

In contrast, internal recruiters can focus on more than just resumes. They can also better assess whether candidates fit the company culture.

Recruitment Agencies Benefit More Than You

Ultimately, internal recruiters can attract more top-tier talent. But that 1% of the best candidates constantly receive messages from headhunters — receiving anywhere from 15 to 120 emails per week. Most of the time, these individuals do not agree to switch jobs.

Why? Because they believe that if the company didn't pay the agency fee, they should receive higher salaries.

Their thinking is correct: typically, internal recruiters will be your capital in competing with larger companies. They will help you save on recruitment agency fees, allowing you to offer more attractive packages under a limited budget. This will help you compete with large companies for talent, trust me.
