A Nine-day Tour of Playing while Rushing (Part One)

by nnphotowyl on 2011-08-11 22:22:49

After enduring a dull workplace for a year, I finally looked forward to my annual leave. Taking advantage of my son's summer vacation, I and my husband (LG) decided to take our annual leave together. We joined another family, whose members are LG’s colleagues, for a trip full of sightseeing, eating, and having fun.

July 10th

We set off early Saturday morning towards Mount Heng, taking Guangshen-North Second Ring-Jingzhu Expressway. However, before reaching Luyuan, all cars were inexplicably forced off the highway. The narrow national road, with some sections under repair, caused us to stop and go repeatedly, wasting much time. Finally, at around 5 pm, we arrived at a small county near Mount Heng. Worried that hotels at the foot of Mount Heng would be more expensive, we almost stayed in a decent-looking "Holiday Hotel" (180 yuan/double room). But after insistence by the female group members, we continued for half an hour more to the foot of Mount Heng.

At the foot of Mount Heng lies the famous Nanyue Temple, which has a newly developed clean and tidy commercial street, somewhat resembling Yangshuo's West Street. Due to its newness, it still lacks popularity. Inside, we found the Wu Hua Inn (188 yuan/luxury double room). The room was spacious, equipped with an automatic mahjong table, a large LCD TV, and two comfortable single beds. The hardware facilities here were better than those in the five-star Huatian Hotel we stayed at later. Of course, the fewer staff meant less service — but if you don't like being disturbed, this place is perfect.

After checking in and dropping off our luggage, it was already dark. We drove back along the same route to find a local farmhouse restaurant for our first meal in Hunan! Almost every dish, even the greens, was spicy... Starting from this meal, we couldn't go without Wanglaoji and soda throughout our journey...

July 11th

I woke up feeling refreshed... But... Why could these two father-son figures so leisurely lounge watching TV and playing PSP, while I had to do all the tidying up? I started to feel a little overwhelmed...

For breakfast, we had rice noodles across from the hotel for five yuan per bowl. Actually, locals had advised us to park at the hotel and then wait for free electric carts at the archway on the street to take us either to the foot of Mount Heng or to the Nanyue Temple (which isn’t on the mountain, by the way). But LG's colleague drove straight to the parking lot at the foot of the mountain, where the car baked under the sun for half a day (20 yuan parking fee).

The entrance tickets to Mount Heng, the shuttle bus, and the cable car are sold separately. At the main gate, if you can gather ten people or more, you can buy group tickets through ticket scalpers, saving about 20 yuan per person. Children under 1.3 meters are free (it wasn’t strictly checked; our son, who is just under 1.4 meters, managed to get in too). After entering the gate, you need to purchase separate tickets for the shuttle bus and the cable car. Usually, local incense pilgrims climb the mountain wearing straw shoes... Hats off to them...

Even with the scenic area shuttle bus and the cable car, it took over half an hour to reach the top. The cable car holds forty to fifty people, and if you manage to get a seat or stand at the front, it's quite thrilling going up or down... Our son loved it! The cable car passes over a long and deep valley, and for a considerable distance, there are no supports under the cables. The children thought it was fun, but the adults were tense the whole time...

After arriving at the South Heaven Gate via the cable car, there was still a long distance to the unnamed highest temple. Here again, hats off to the incense pilgrims... As soon as we stepped out of the cable car, the cool mountain breeze hit us, refreshing yet making us shiver slightly. It was truly a different world between the top and bottom of the mountain.

Climbing the mountain was physically demanding. Although the current trail is far better than it used to be, for someone like me who hasn't been active for a long time, it was absolutely grueling. Sweating profusely, once I stopped, the mountain wind would dry and chill me to the bone... Watching the incense pilgrims walking briskly past me, I wondered if their straw shoes were enchanted... Maybe I should shamelessly ask for a pair?

After a very long time, we finally reached the summit. Compared to our trip to Mount Huang, this one didn't even come close, it was too easy. But for me personally, it was extremely painful... At the summit temple, I neither burned incense nor made wishes. LG said that if you make a wish, you have to fulfill it, so I didn't dare make any wishes at temples outside my hometown. But donations were definitely necessary. I only briefly observed the temple's culture without delving deeply. Though monks guard the temple, I felt it was more of a Taoist shrine rather than a Buddhist temple. Why all the monks were there remains a mystery. The wind at the temple entrance was fierce, and the incense smoke was very thick. Ashes swirling into the sky could be seen frequently, and I dared not walk too close to the edge, fearing the wind might really blow someone off the cliff...

Along the way, I noticed that there were indeed many people coming to burn incense here. Local incense pilgrimage groups wear very ethnic clothing. Even those returning from working away to burn incense wear hand-woven straw shoes over their pretty shoes and carry special belly pouches identifying them as incense pilgrimage groups from certain places, very distinctive. Out of respect, I didn't dare photograph this unique scene. Another feature is that people here really just come to burn incense. They carry big and numerous sticks of incense up the mountain to worship and burn, unlike in Guangdong where people bring fruit and incense oil as offerings. Most of the donors are from other places, truly deserving the name "incense pilgrimage group."

With many people on the summit, we took photos as souvenirs and then headed downhill, preparing for our next destination: Shaoshan.

The descent was nothing like the difficulty experienced when climbing down from Mount Huang. Whether by mountain path or road, the air was fresh and free of the scorching sun. There was none of the uncertainty of how much longer we had to endure before reaching the end. My mood brightened along with the clear skies. Occasionally, local mountain residents would lay out a few of their homemade mountain products (small dried bamboo shoots, sweet potato chips, wild vegetable chips, etc.) on the ground for sale (not fixed roadside stalls). When asking prices, the dried bamboo shoots cost five to ten yuan per bundle, and the sweet potato chips were three large pieces for five yuan. I was about to make a big purchase when my companions warned me not to buy on the mountain, saying it was cheaper down below. To satisfy my craving, I bought three sweet potato chips, munching them as I walked. They were delicious! After reaching the bottom, I regretted not trusting my own judgment and buying more since everything down there was more expensive—bamboo shoots from thirty to fifty yuan per bundle, sweet potato chips twenty to thirty yuan... I was furious!! The remaining sweet potato chips weren't eaten but carried back to Shenzhen as gifts for my colleagues—a light gift with heavy sentiment...

After descending, we rushed to Shaoshan. The highway signs were unclear, and since we were all tired and sleeping, no one helped navigate. The driver missed the interchange on the highway and drove straight to Changsha Ring Expressway... Then we turned back toward Shaoshan, wasting much time and fuel, but we finally arrived before dark (the further west we went, the later it got dark, nearly eight o'clock and still bright). Upon entering Shaoshan territory, motorcyclists carrying Hunan girls approached cars with out-of-town plates, offering themselves as free guides and helping lead to hotels (you can choose freely until satisfied, prices generally 80-200 yuan). A guide costs only thirty yuan a day, fully inclusive. However, the biggest trap here is that although your guide seems excellent, she will excuse herself on the first stop of the tour the next day and be replaced by someone usually inexperienced, too timid to speak... You'll have to figure out how to deal with this issue yourselves...

After several meals, it was still spicy. Actually, I can handle spiciness well, but unfortunately, during this trip, my stomach issues flared up, causing excruciating pain with cold sweats. I missed out on two meals, which was truly a major regret of this trip!

Dinner was handled at a farmhouse next to the inn, where we also gossiped with locals about various matters concerning dragon veins... Hehe~
