What kind of food does white wine pair well with?

by uefbp54187 on 2012-02-28 14:35:09

Generally speaking, white wine is suitable for lighter foods, such as seafood, chicken, and salad that haven't been prepared with heavy sauces. This is because if the food's flavor is too strong, it will overshadow the delicate aroma and taste of the white wine. However, finding the best pairing still requires readers to experiment themselves. Due to the brilliance of Chinese culinary culture, various cooking methods, and individual preferences, attention should be paid to the sense of smell and taste during dinner. The perfect match between wine and food might seem elusive. Whether sweet, salty, sour or spicy, the laughter of friends and family represents the most wonderful life. In fact, besides pairing with food, white wine also tastes great on its own.

One point that confuses many wine enthusiasts is how to pair wine with food. Traditional rules say white wine goes with seafood, red wine with red meat. Of course, any rule may seem clichéd, but generally, white wine pairs better with seafood without a doubt. However, there is a more practical basic principle: instead of considering the color of the wine, consider its body and texture. Simply put, white wine usually has a certain acidity that pairs well with acidic foods like lemons and vinegar, making creamy sauces more palatable. Therefore, Burgundy white wine pairs well with fish like turbot. Lighter seafood suits wines like Soave, Pinot Grigio, or Muscadet from the Loire Valley, while oilier seafood like sardines needs a fuller-bodied Chardonnay with oak influence. Chardonnay also pairs well with smoked salmon, but you can try Macon or Chablis from the northernmost part of Burgundy. Soft red wines from Beaujolais, Chinon in the Loire Valley, or fruity Pinot Noirs often pair well with thicker fish meats like swordfish or tuna steaks.

I like to pair red wine with Spanish paella, regardless of whether it contains seafood; I also enjoy Fino Sherry with mackerel and fine Champagne with oysters. But why must white wine only go with seafood? Can't it also go with meat or vegetables? What about the classic dish at dinner - the cheese board?

Now let's consider the taste of wine. A tannin-rich dry red often leaves a slightly bitter aftertaste that dries out the palate, which can be balanced by chewy meat. I usually like Bordeaux or Châteauneuf-du-Pape reds with beef, and if not, Chilean Syrah or Spanish Rioja with lamb. Poultry or game pairs wonderfully with Burgundy reds and Pinot Noir, and top Cru Beaujolais works well too.

In summer, I tend to use rosé with vegetable dishes, especially dry Provence rosés. In winter, a soft, mature, dense red wine with low tannins is the best choice, such as California Merlot or Argentine Malbec.

Pairing wine with cheese can present various unexpected issues because some cheeses have special flavors that clash with the tannins in red wine. Mature Gouda, Cheddar, and Mimolette pair well with Bordeaux reds. Although it might sound clichéd, Port wine and Stilton are indeed a perfect match. In many cases, I enjoy drinking Sauternes with Roquefort blue cheese, leaving a lasting impression.

If you're unsure what cheese to pair with wine, I strongly recommend Comté. It's a versatile cheese whose nutty caramel flavor pairs well with white wines like Sancerre from the Loire Valley, light Beaujolais reds, vintage Champagne, or even rich reds from the Languedoc-Roussillon region in France. In fact, this cheese pairs well with all kinds of wine, breaking all the rules of wine pairing.
