Hold your laughter, the news about celebrities' customized mobile phones is pretty reliable.

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 19:56:51

Following Cui Jian, Han Geng has become another artist to launch his own custom smartphone. The news sparked discussions from multiple angles, such as price, configuration, and customized content. Most people still hold conservative opinions about celebrity-customized phones, and some even mock celebrities for entering a field they don't understand. However, what I want to say today is: don't mock too quickly, because the concept of celebrity-customized phones actually makes sense.

Not understanding C2B means not understanding the future

Has the traditional B2C era ended? Not necessarily, but the C2B market is on the rise, a trend that few would oppose. In essence, celebrity-customized phones are also a form of C2B model. It collects the relatively widespread needs of a group (the fans of a certain star), such as wanting to know about the star's performance information, work-life dynamics, or wanting to see their past works. Then it customizes corresponding built-in applications for the phone, and the appearance can also reflect the star's temperament, personal avatar? Signature? Or perhaps a QR code? (Personally, I think QR codes have better prospects).

All of this is the result of technological progress, which in turn changes people's consumption concepts. Nowadays, more and more people are buying personalized products, which in turn drives the development of the C2B market. Although 3D printing technology still needs improvement, there is no denying that this technology will greatly change traditional production models and become an important tool for personalized production.

This also brings many opportunities to enterprises. The first phone Motorola launched after joining Google — moto x — provides customizable phone shells. Recently, Motorola's C2B efforts have advanced further. There are media reports that the company is advancing a project named "Ara". Simply put, it's a platform that allows users to freely choose phone modules, then let Motorola produce them. Modules include processor, battery, and even storage chips. Before that, domestic Qingcheng had similar projects, and last month, Baidu invested in Hundred Percent Company, which is a mobile phone manufacturer that started with the C2B model.

Some might ask, as early as last year, including TCL, Skyworth, and other home appliance companies jointly launched some C2B products with Tmall and Taobao, but the results were less than satisfactory. Why does the C2B of celebrity-customized phones have a future? This leads me to my second point — why only "small worlds" can achieve this business model?

Small worlds are the prerequisite for the success of C2B

Thoreau once said: The telegraph made relevant things irrelevant. If Thoreau were alive today, he might add: The Internet reconnected irrelevant things. The Internet not only completely changed the time and space restrictions for people to obtain information (similar to the function of the telegraph), but also created a more community-friendly platform, technically facilitating human community living. In other words, the Internet established new connections between seemingly unrelated things, and rightly so, it connects all things. In my view, this is the greatest contribution of the Internet to human society.

The Internet communities formed by various connections are not much different from real-world social groups. People gather due to shared interests. This interest could be a product, like a group of people who buy iPhones forming a community, or it could be liking the same star. Regardless of how the community forms, they all share one common characteristic — they are all "small worlds."

The concept of "small worlds" was not created by the Internet, but the Internet gave the concept of "small worlds" new extensions. In a "small world," people share a common hobby and interact to form a common daily routine. And in terms of member numbers, the members of a "small world" remain stable within a certain period. These characteristics are crucial to the C2B business model.

In the C2B industrial chain, the hardest part is how to quickly find the demands of C-end users while ensuring a certain degree of stability among C-end users. In the "small world" of celebrity fans, these two problems are very easy to solve. Taking Han Geng as an example, whether it’s the performances of Super Junior in earlier years or his solo performances afterwards, they have accumulated a large number of fans both domestically and internationally. And the Internet provides these fans with a natural communication and interaction platform, forming a not-so-small "small world."

On the other hand, despite you may think most people in this group will exit this "small world" after reaching a certain age, have you considered the constantly emerging "post-" generations?

"Small worlds" reinforce the concept of communities, making human connections tighter; while C2B is a big trend in manufacturing, making the relationship between humans and machines (products) friendlier. From this perspective, looking at celebrity-customized phones, doesn't it seem a bit reliable? But we should also see that behind each "small world," there may also be many walls erected. Whether it's Guo Jingming's movies or Han Geng's phones, they are business models built within "small worlds," and it's hard for them to break through their own walls.
