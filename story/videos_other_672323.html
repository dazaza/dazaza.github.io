People familiar with studying abroad in Quebec, Canada, all know that independent application for graduate studies is very difficult, regarding the costs of studying abroad for associate-to-bachelor programs in Canada. So where is the difficulty? First, the requirement for major alignment is very high. Graduate majors in Quebec, Canada, require very specific alignment. For example, if you want to pursue a master’s degree in engineering, your bachelor’s degree must be in an engineering-related major; if you want to study economics, your university must have been in mathematics, economics, or related fields. In terms of academic performance, most majors require a university average score of B+, equivalent to 83-85 points on China's percentage scale. Moreover, schools will particularly focus on the grades from the last two years of university because there are more professional courses during this period. Regarding language requirements, generally, TCF scores need to reach 430 points. Additionally, the range of graduate majors offered in Quebec, Canada, is relatively limited, especially in business-related fields. Many students who studied business in China wish to continue their business studies in Quebec, Canada, or many science students in China want to switch to business. For instance, they may want to study marketing, logistics, e-commerce, etc. In fact, in Quebec, Canada, many of these business-related subjects at the graduate level fall under the directions within the MBA program and are not independently listed as separate majors. It is worth noting that there are also flexible moments in applying for graduate studies in Quebec, Canada. When students apply for graduate programs in Quebec, Canada, they usually browse the entrance requirements for each major on the school's website. However, the actual situation might differ from what is stated on the website. Moreover, at least two years of industry work experience is required, concerning the costs of studying abroad for associate-to-bachelor programs in Canada. However, we once successfully obtained an offer for a senior student without any work experience. This student was the only one in his graduating class without work experience. This student had excellent academic performance, and when preparing the supporting documents, we supplemented with outstanding internship and daily life examples to make up for the student's weakness in this area. This case shows that universities in Quebec, Canada, do not rigidly adhere to rules when admitting students; they consider the overall situation and quality of the student.
