The birthday banquet prizes all have symbolic meanings.

"To make children understand giving and sharing."

(Wang ?)

Q: We hope such an event will convey the message of love. Parents often teach their children to understand giving and sharing, to be responsible to society, and to learn how to help those in need. But nowadays, I find that many parents have gotten into the habit of giving the best things to their children, and the children feel that this is something they deserve, to the point that they don't even respect the elderly. I think this is a problem in education.

A: Why did you decide to host such a lavish birthday banquet?

A: For many people, such prizes might be very expensive, but we focus not on the value, but on the symbolism. For example, the grand prize was three cars, symbolizing progress. Life’s journey will encounter many obstacles, but when faced with obstacles, it doesn't mean the car stops moving forward or that one should give up. Instead, one must refuel, repair, dig deep, and move forward relentlessly; our first and second prizes were all jade, and why did we set two levels of awards? This also has a symbolic meaning. Because jade isn't just any ordinary stone; it undergoes countless trials and tribulations, but the final outcome isn't always the same. This symbolizes that life also experiences difficulties, sometimes despite efforts, one may not reach what they desire, and one must accept it with a calm heart; the third prize was laptops, the fourth prize was Parker gold pens, both symbolizing the importance of studying; the fifth prize was Yixing purple clay, we do not advocate children drinking coffee or carbonated drinks, instead we expect them to drink tea, because tea is fragrant and symbolizes tranquility; the sixth prize was MP4 players, telling us that life contains beautiful songs, and we should love life.

A: We turned it into a parent-child activity themed around charity as a birthday gift for the child. On one hand, the child couldn't attend school due to health reasons, but she really wanted to go to school and longed to see her classmates; on the other hand, we usually keep a low profile and didn't want too much publicity.

The hosts of the birthday banquet were Ai'ai, a second-grade student from a primary school in Beijing's Haidian District, and 37 classmates attended, each accompanied by a parent. Teacher Lu said that Ai'ai hadn't been able to come to school for a while due to health reasons, and the classmates missed her very much. Knowing it was her birthday, many prepared small gifts. "There were magic shows, performances by little singing stars..." Today, the classmates still talk excitedly about Ai'ai's birthday banquet.

This lavishly produced birthday banquet has also sparked speculation and discussion among many people. A teacher told reporters that Ai'ai's family was indeed wealthy, and the birthday banquet was extravagant! Another parent said that Ai'ai's family runs a business, is affluent, and values the education of children's compassion, showing that the parents are far-sighted. Ai'ai's mother said during the interview yesterday that this was a very private gathering, with all costs borne by the family. "We turned it into a parent-child activity themed around charity and gave it as a birthday gift to the child. We don't want the child to 'lie' on wealth, and since childhood, we've educated her to understand contribution. She once donated 398,000 yuan of her lucky money to build a kindergarten in Sichuan, and has visited Sichuan five times to show love."

This event conveys messages of love.

To hold such a luxurious and special birthday banquet for a child, what exactly were Ai'ai's parents thinking? This afternoon, the reporter had a conversation with Ai'ai's mother.

Sociology doctoral professor Xu Xiang from Nanjing University believes that now there are more and more "rich second generations," and how to educate children growing up in "sugar water" is a big issue. Ai'ai's parents' educational philosophy is commendable. Giving children material wealth isn't crucial; what matters is teaching her from a young age to understand love and contribution. Of course, holding such a luxurious birthday banquet under the guise of charity is debatable, as it might cause envy among classmates. Moreover, showing love doesn't necessarily require such a grand occasion. However, Professor Xu also feels that we should be tolerant of this matter. Society's thoughts are diversified, and wealth is stratified, making it difficult to measure an event by a single standard. Spending within one's means and focusing on social effects should be the starting point for doing things for children.

Q: For ordinary families, this birthday banquet might seem very luxurious and even invite skepticism. How do you consider this?

"The rich second generation" education is no longer a fresh topic for both their children and the education sector. Professor Bu Lin from Beijing Industrial University's sociology department believes that the most important thing for their education is to focus on "human" education. Therefore, one should start with self-discipline and gradually guide them to establish a positive attitude in everyday educational carriers, eliminating the notion of being superior to others, and start by learning to be "a person" who can coexist equally with others and society.

Children aren't clueless about money.

Bu Lin said that educating "rich second generations" is not an easy task. In the West, the wealthy never hesitate on material consumption for their children. While providing superior material living conditions, they emphasize shaping the children's maternal consciousness, independent spirit, and social responsibility at every turn. This is worth China's reference. Not all "rich second generations" have the flaws of flaunting wealth, recklessness, and impulsiveness. Jiangsu is a major hub for private economies, and many enterprises have reached the stage of cultivating successors. Bu Lin stated that only by allowing "rich second generations" to establish a proactive and healthy civic consciousness from a young age, understanding love and responsibility, will they have the potential to integrate into society and master the ability to "create wealth" in the future. Yang Yumei

A: It's said that Ai'ai actually has a good concept of money. The cash gifts at the birthday amounted to over 200,000 yuan, and the little girl quickly planned how to use this money to help others. This money will be used to purchase medicines, nutritional supplements, books, shoes, etc., to be sent to villages near Mianyang and Yingxiu in Sichuan Province. Additionally, we originally ordered 10,000 sets of quilts and cotton-padded clothes, but due to transportation issues, only 2,500 sets of quilts and 7,200 sets of cotton-padded clothes were delivered to Sichuan by company staff afterward. The child does these things out of nature, like the fifth day after the Sichuan earthquake when Ai'ai went to Sichuan with me. The child fully understands what she is doing, so she carefully decided to donate her accumulated lucky money to the disaster area, building a kindergarten on the Yingxiu dam. Now this kindergarten has started enrollment, and the child is particularly happy. She told me, "I am a small molecule, and I want to play the role of a molecule." I was deeply moved after hearing this.

Q: Is it because the child's health is not good that you spoil her especially?

Dialogue with the "Big Birthday Star" Ai'ai's mother

Our reporter Wang ?

Q: At the age of three and a half, Ai'ai was hospitalized due to persistent high fever, but unexpectedly, she was diagnosed with leukemia. Our family only has this one child, and this news was like a bolt from the blue for us. I could hardly believe it at the time. But Ai'ai was strong and gave us strength. During the bone marrow puncture, little Ai'ai curled up in my arms, sweating profusely from pain, but she refused to cry. She told the doctor that if she cried, her mom would cry. This child was very understanding and hasn't cried loudly since she got sick. Sometimes, when the pain became unbearable, she would quietly cry in the blanket. After a long and painful treatment, Ai'ai's illness is basically under control now, but due to medication, there are still some side effects remaining. When the weather gets cold, her cerebral blood vessels tend to spasm, so she cannot go to school for now. But Ai'ai hasn't been idle; she often goes to nursing homes, orphanages, etc., to volunteer. After the Sichuan earthquake, Ai'ai has already been to Sichuan five times. Every time she goes, she helps with work tirelessly, and most importantly, she finds happiness in giving. We hope that the child can receive equal and friendly education. So we abandoned the original idea of sending the child to an international school and chose the current ordinary but well-atmosphered school.

"The rich second generation" faces the most obvious dilemma in family dilemmas. In many wealthy families, parents are extremely busy and spend little time communicating and interacting with their children. More importantly, there is a societal dilemma: "rich second generations" have pocket money that they can't spend, and their clothing, food, housing, and transportation are supplied materially, making it hard for them to find true friends among classmates.

Our reporter reports that the grand prize was three cars, and there were also a series of other prizes such as jade ornaments, laptops, etc... On the evening of November 28th in Beijing's Xiaojie Village, the birthday banquet of a real 10-year-old elementary school student shocked the guests and the 37 classmates of the "big birthday star." However, what shocked the guests wasn't just the prizes, but the decision made by the "little birthday star" at the banquet: all 200,000 yuan in cash gifts from the evening would be donated to the Sichuan disaster area, along with tens of thousands of sets of quilts and cotton-padded clothes purchased by her family for nearly 500,000 yuan.
