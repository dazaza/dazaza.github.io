Google's cards, "post-skeuomorphic design" for information presentation

by geekzhang on 2013-05-17 08:56:54

Even though there was no new Android version and no eye-catching parachute show, this year's Google I/O still gave us a sense of fulfillment. It was a solid technical performance, indicating the direction in which Google is moving forward. We saw that Google is improving its services from all aspects. This is akin to a craftsman meticulously refining their work, occasionally eliciting admiration for their technical prowess. During this developer conference, Google not only showcased advanced intelligence in handling data (smart optimization of Google+ images, evolution of search), but also demonstrated progress in design.Observant audiences may have noticed that an element in the interface design of Google products has become increasingly prominent: cards. This has become an important medium for Google to display information.We cannot be certain about the origin of this trend. Boldly guessing, it might be related to Matias Duarte, the head of Android user experience. When he was at Palm working on webOS design, he began exploring card interfaces, with multitasking cards leaving a deep impression. After joining the Android team, he started introducing some elements of webOS, naturally bringing along the concept of cards. In Google's most important product, Google Now, cards became the medium for presenting information, followed by Google+ mobile, PlayBook, which extensively used cards. The latest version of the Google Play Store uses cards to present information, and Glass's interface is based on cards. At the Google I/O conference, we saw that the newly revamped Google Maps will also introduce cards.The source of the rise of cards may not be important. Their omnipresence proves the agreement of designers across Google’s various products. Co.Design interviewed several Google designers to discuss the significance of cards in Google product design.We didn't invent a new way to organize information, said Matias Duarte, head of Android user experience. In fact, we use one of the oldest graphic and information design methods: business cards, phone cards, greeting cards, playing cards. They all have the same presentation style because they all reflect the same design problems.These issues existed before touch-screen devices were invented and are rooted in mobility. Therefore, when Google's designers wanted to find an interface for Google Now that could carry short, diverse streams of information, they realized they needed a tangible container.The limitation of cards actually made other designs for Google Now easier, Duarte recalled. It forced us to focus. It forced us to think about questions like, you can't put two or three pictures on one card; that would just look messy.Co.Design believes that cards are an excellent tool. They can attract attention without clicking links; they can combat information overload; they give a comfortable feeling on any screen. Cards provide a tactile sensation, sitting between minimalist design and skeuomorphic design, almost like a post-skeuomorphic design. However, in conversations with Google designers, Co.Design learned that cards don't just solve information presentation problems but can also be used to address a series of complex UX issues.For example, for the maps team, cards solved a core interaction problem: how to present a large amount of information on maps.We didn't want to cover the entire map, so we created a panel that could expand and contract, said Jonah Jones, UX designer for Google Maps. The problem was, when it expanded, it couldn't really give the feeling of being able to contract. And after it contracted, it couldn't really give the feeling of being able to expand. They found that cards could solve this problem. Cards can naturally stack on top of each other. By stacking cards and revealing a small amount of information from the cards behind, users can conveniently handle these pieces of information.Due to the prominent position of cards in Google's design, Co.Design believes that it has become the company's new voice for expressing data, a universal remedy facing multiple platforms.As Google delves deeper into our lives, reading our emails with their algorithms, determining our locations, predicting our preferences, cards will be a buffer between companies that know too much and users who understand too little.Pictures come from Co.Design
