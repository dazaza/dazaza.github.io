Feelings on Watching "Hachi: A Dog's Tale"

by chongwuyin5r on 2012-03-07 06:14:24

I've known the story of Hachiko, the loyal dog, for a long time. Many friends have told me that they were deeply moved after watching it, and some even couldn't help but shed tears. I spent nearly two hours carefully watching the story of Hachi, about Hachi and the professor, and the professor's family. Finally, the professor's grandson said that Hachi let him know what loyalty was, and that it was a hero in his heart forever. I didn't shed tears, but I empathized with the child.

We, as people in the pet circle, frequently interact with pets and their owners. We see countless pet owners carefully nurturing their pets, crying and being heartbroken when their pets are sick. I saw how the professor carefully nurtured Hachi, treating it like a family member. Its life was without pain, only filled with longing for its owner and endless waiting. In the end, it left this world in a dream about its owner, deeply telling us pet owners that if we truly want to be responsible for our pets at home, we should try to understand them, read them, and know what they need and what they shouldn't have.

I really want to tell my friends that dogs communicate with us in many languages, perhaps through barking or body language. Why don't we take the time to understand and analyze thoroughly, reading our most loyal and kind friend? When it barks at you, it might not be an attack, just excitement, or it wants to play with you, or maybe it has noticed something unusual. On the day the professor passed away, Hachi sensed it. It didn't want the professor to leave. At the station, Hachi pleaded with the professor to stay and play with it, holding a toy ball it had never played with before. That sense of foreknowledge can only be understood by those who have experienced it. When I saw the professor fall in the classroom, I was completely stunned. I didn't know how to express my feelings at the moment. I hope the professor knew then that Hachi could have stayed, even if it meant missing that day, the professor could have spent a little more time with Hachi. This isn't sympathy for Hachi, but for that inexplicable feeling of moving emotion. They could be happy together, loyal to each other. How much longer could that scene last? Unfortunately, there are no ifs.

Thinking of Hachi, I remember last summer, when a golden retriever owner screamed in anguish when her pet left. Recalling how she begged the doctor to save it, the expression of helplessness on her face, her heart was broken. Her boyfriend told her that the doctor had done everything possible, but she said, "Impossible, impossible, it can still be saved. Doctor, please, save it." I cried beside her, my tears betraying my emotions. I truly saw that love. Without saying goodbye to them, I left. I didn't want to see anymore, nor did I know how to comfort them. I sincerely wished they could have another beloved pet, and hoped that a cute little one would enter their lives, following them and surely being very happy.

In 2005, when I first entered the circle, I followed my master and left the workplace. At the time, I had a dog named Jingang. When I said goodbye to it, I held a bag of ham sausage and told it my troubles. It ate while looking at me. To this day, I still think it didn't understand my farewell, but I knew it was trying hard to understand what I said. I sat next to it in the kennel and cried because I was leaving my brother and couldn't take it with me. After doing everything I could, I entrusted Jingang to a colleague who liked it very much, hoping it would live well. I also hope it is still living healthily now.

In the industry, I am a professional dog dealer. Of course, I am also a breeder and any role in this industry. Actually, when selling dogs, I hope every puppy that leaves my hands gets the best care from its new owner, and the most appropriate care. Because reasonable breeding is the greatest protection they receive during their most vulnerable stage. So, dear friends, I hope you don't spoil these children we take away. They will get rich man's disease. If you encounter any problems, don't rush to handle them. Consulting first is very important. I also hope that before preparing to keep a pet, you learn about how to raise and manage it. When you take our little ones away, you should be responsible for them. This is a responsibility to life. They may start as your children, then become your friends, and eventually your family. Always treat them as friends, not pets. We possess all the joys and sorrows they do. Carefully reading them is a process. When you understand them, and they understand you, I believe you will become the happiest person in the world.

Finally, I wish all parents to get along well with your children, friends, and family. If you have any questions, you can consult us. Please share your happiness with me. They are all Hachiko, all Lassie, all Fangya and Princess. As long as you give, they will repay you for a lifetime...
