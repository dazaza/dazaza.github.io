Shenzhen Mobile Phone Vendors: The Knockoff Phones Are Dead

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 16:20:39

A company owner who has been in the business of selling knock-off phones in Shenzhen for 15 years called me late last week, his voice filled with anxiety, disappointment, and unease.

This is an old friend of mine whom I've known for many years. Whenever I travel to Shenzhen, we always meet up at a bar in Nanshan District for a small gathering.

My old friend chose to leave the Shenzhen knock-off phone industry in February this year and transitioned into local carrier channel sales. After facing several setbacks, he returned to Shenzhen this month. However, compared to the beginning of the year, the knock-off phone industry in Shenzhen is on the brink of "collapse," leaving this old friend extremely disappointed.

Over the weekend, I flew to Shenzhen and spent two days examining the Huaqiangbei market, engaging in deep conversations late into the night with several seasoned mobile phone industry professionals. I was shocked to discover that the market shakeout had already begun, and many knock-off phone manufacturers were becoming the first batch of "losers" in this changing landscape.

Collapse

1. July is the peak of the hot season in the south, but the once largest global mobile phone transit market, Huaqiangbei, has entered its coldest period. Mingtong, Longsheng, Gaokede, Tongtiandi, and Pangyuan, the five major digital cities, have now been reduced to just one—Mingtong. The scale of merchants in Mingtong has also shrunk to 1/5 of its peak, reducing from the original 1-5 floors to less than one floor of bottom-level shops.

2. Old Nie, who has been selling phones in Mingtong Digital City for eight years, put up a "transfer" sign on his store this month. Counters that were once "worth their weight in gold" are now mostly ignored. A shopfront of 6-7 square meters originally required a monthly rent of around 80,000-100,000 yuan, plus an additional entrance fee of 150,000-200,000 yuan. Now, the rent has dropped to just 3,000 yuan per month.

3. The most bustling market in Huaqiangbei now is undoubtedly the "Yuanwang Digital City." Yuanwang mainly sells Apple accessories and gray-market products such as Apple, Samsung, and HTC, having no relation whatsoever to knock-off phones.

4. According to managers of the Shenzhen Huaqiangbei market, starting from February this year, about 200-500 knock-off phone manufacturers "ran away" each month. Compared to the "peak period" of around 6,000 knock-off phone manufacturers in 2006-2007, there are now only about 800 left, most of which have transitioned to small and medium-sized self-branded operations.

5. According to relevant personnel from Shenzhen's LePhone Mobile, domestic cities below the third tier were once the primary market for knock-off phones, but they have now been occupied by small and medium-sized brand phones, strong domestic brands like Gionee, and operator contract phones, effectively pushing knock-off phones out of the market.

Ten Reasons as Described by Phone Sellers:

Almost all Shenzhen mobile phone practitioners believe that the knock-off phone industry is beyond salvation. I have also interviewed some industry "bigwigs" regarding this issue. He Shiyu, Executive Vice President of ZTE responsible for mobile phone business, stated: "The shakeout has already begun, and many mobile phone brands will disappear next year." Yu Chengdong, Chairman of Huawei Terminal, previously said: "Before 2015, it will be a period of intense industry shakeout."

Summarizing the statements of more than ten Shenzhen mobile phone manufacturers and practitioners, the collapse of the knock-off phone industry can be attributed to the following ten reasons:

1. Severe overstocking. Starting from February this year, a large number of Shenzhen knock-off phone companies began receiving returns from distributors and were refused further orders due to drastically declining sales. Excessive inventory accumulation led many business owners to choose to "run away," causing a chain reaction of bankruptcies among sales channels, phone companies, solution integrators, and supply chains.

2. Broken capital chains. The entry threshold for knock-off phones is extremely low, with a complete Shenzhen industrial chain allowing a finished product to be launched within three months with an investment of just 600,000 yuan. Throughout the process, only MTK downstream solution integrators, as well as camera and display components, require advance payment; everything else can be done on credit, with payments made after the phones are sold and funds recovered.

However, under the circumstances where many knock-off or small and medium-sized brand manufacturers "run away," the battery, mold-making, packaging, and other industrial chains now require prepayment or full payment upfront, leading to broken capital chains for knock-off phone manufacturers, making them unable to continue.

3. Disorderly competition among knock-off manufacturers. Google's open Android system, MediaTek's bundled solutions, and downstream solution integrators greatly lowered the threshold for smartphones, also resulting in numerous companies entering the industry.

The head of Shenzhen MiGe Mobile Phone Company stated that the lack of brands and channels forced new entrants to engage in price wars. No profit, even losses, product similarity, poor quality, and lack of after-sales service accelerated the collective death of knock-off phone manufacturers.

4. Domestic brand phones lowering prices. Price wars are not just tools used by knock-off phones; even domestic brand phone manufacturers like ZTE and K-Touch have started hardware price wars. For example, ZTE partnered with internet companies to launch a quad-core phone priced at 699 yuan, while K-Touch collaborated with Guangdong Mobile to launch a dual-core 5-inch smartphone priced at 199 yuan.

A boss of a Shenzhen mobile phone manufacturer lamented: "If I were to make this phone myself, the cost would be 260 yuan. The market has become too bizarre!"

5. Public channels turning against knock-offs. The domestic county-level and below markets were once fertile ground for knock-off phones, but with the expansion of branded phone manufacturers like Gionee, TCL and Hisense using home appliance channels to "bundle" phone sales, and the downward reach of operator channels, knock-off phones are being rapidly expelled from the market.

Wang Jiyang, president of TCL Communication China Division, revealed: "TCL has over 30,000 color TV sales outlets nationwide, and this year the mobile phone business will gradually enter the home appliance channels for sales." In contrast, China Unicom has fewer than 10,000 of its own business halls and cooperative sales stores nationwide.

6. Operator subsidies cutting profits. Operator subsidies for airtime fees have led to the rapid popularization of thousand-yuan 3G smartphones domestically. However, operators have strict requirements for phone brands, quality, supply, and built-in features, preventing knock-off phones from entering the operator channels that account for more than half of domestic sales. In the face of airtime subsidies, the cost-performance advantage of knock-off phones is completely lost.

Old Ding has been selling knock-off phones in Shenzhen for six years. Last December, he invested over 3 million yuan to switch to producing self-branded smartphones. An acquaintance claimed they could help him enter the procurement of a provincial operator in Yunnan. After spending over 400,000 yuan, nothing came of it. After drinking a glass of beer, Old Ding indicated he would switch to selling gray-market Samsung and Apple phones.

7. E-commerce channel counterattack. Why did traditional public channels previously focus on selling knock-off phones? Because the rebate was high, but the rapid development of e-commerce sales changed everything. Under the extensive coverage of mobile phone manufacturers + e-commerce websites + express delivery, young rural consumers can now purchase phones by comparing configurations and prices, reversing the high-rebate model of traditional channels through e-commerce.

Some newly transformed small brand phone bosses view "opening a store on Taobao and hiring 1-2 people" as a transformation into e-commerce channels. Not understanding the internet, not knowing e-commerce sales, and not knowing how to invest have become the "pain points" of transformation for many small and medium-sized self-branded mobile phone manufacturers.

8. Joint crackdown by industrial and commercial regulators. According to data from the Shenzhen Market Supervision Administration, during the special rectification period of the past two years, 8,200 "knock-off" mobile phone operators in Qiangbei alone exited the market, with some former "knock-off" mobile phone electronic markets transforming into legitimate electronics and mobile phone accessory markets.

9. Increased demands on mobile phones due to mobile applications. In the 2G feature phone era, making calls and sending text messages were the two most commonly used functions. However, in the 3G smartphone era, users spend more time on games, Weibo, WeChat, videos, and internet surfing than on voice calls and text messaging. Traditional knock-off phones, with their low hardware configurations and lack of software debugging, can no longer meet user needs.

10. Shrinking overseas markets. Overseas markets once accounted for more than 70% of knock-off phone shipments, but starting last year, international phone brands intensified their crackdowns on patents and intellectual property rights. For instance, the largest knock-off phone manufacturer, G'Five, was "encircled" by Nokia in the Indian market and "returned" to the domestic market last year. However, function phones have largely exited the domestic market, and the competition for smartphones is incredibly fierce. How to complete the transformation before running out of cash is a severe problem they face.

After nearly a decade of development, knock-off phones once prided themselves on "micro-innovation" and indeed promoted the popularization of domestic mobile phone users, even forcing domestic brands like BIRD and Amoi to the brink of extinction. However, in the 3G smartphone era, knock-off phones were ultimately ended by the combined efforts of operators, public channels, e-commerce platforms, domestic brand phones, and users.

In Shenzhen, there are still nearly a thousand former knock-off phone manufacturers transitioning into small and medium-sized brand phone enterprises, representing a dream and persistence. Only how to build a brand, increase R&D, utilize e-commerce platforms to expand channels, gain operator recognition, and regain user trust remain serious considerations for every guardian.
