In memory of the heroes on the Internet

by molei on 2008-07-17 16:12:14

I want to ask what the Internet is, how it makes a profit, what kind of structure its profit model has, and what its future development direction is? How much profit margin is left for us to make? Does our failure to make a profit so far mean we're on the wrong path or that the model is incorrect? Then, how do we find the right way? Is it a leadership issue, an employee capability issue, a funding issue, or simply an inaccurate understanding of the network and our own positioning?

The Internet is moving towards maturity. It is a platform and node for information. The formation of a certain pattern will stabilize a large number of related industries, making it difficult for external and new enterprises to break this pattern. Once, we used innovation to demonstrate survival space on the network, but in long-term stable and incremental development, innovation is just a transitional phase.

Looking back at yesterday, we can find a large number of dominant and territorial figures like Charles Zhang, Robin Li, and Jack Ma. Since then, more people have followed and repeated their footsteps, not realizing that even one day in the network world can lead to vastly different results. Consequently, many more people have perished in the network. The network hasn't changed; it's still an information platform and tool. We can still live without it, just as easily as living without TV or newspapers. Alas for those who perished in the network after them.

From someone who builds websites to the executive president of a network information group, from a staff member to a star brand being established, more effects are generated. Perhaps, this brand effect can completely detach from its original entity to develop independently, far more conveniently than those who started later. It's not hard to see that the relationship between successfully building a website and becoming a star brand is both successful and critical. Of course, it includes a basket of factors such as effort, opportunity, money, and connections. We just want to set aside these factors and look at the result.

Too many people see the results of the network while ignoring the factors. Today's Baidu, Alibaba, and Tianya provide reference objects for blindly dreaming network aspirants, and this phenomenon completely breaks the conventional network operation model, marketing model, and development model. We have data proving that the capital structure of this network outside the capital market is built on piles of corpses. They march with great courage, bravely walking toward the abyss below the cliff...

There is nothing that can be referenced, referencing is imitation; there is nothing truly innovative, innovation is following; there is no miracle, miracles are suicide. In this virtual space of the network, the survival space we see is much smaller than in reality, yet because it's virtual, this space is amplified countless times on the network, forming a bait that draws many people into this black hole. Few come out or conquer the black hole, and the fewer there are, the more tempting this bait becomes. History repeats itself over and over again.

This is a market with no threshold, a market with the lowest cost, a market with almost no rules, where entering and exiting is extremely simple. But within it lies astonishing differences. Have you succeeded in the internet market? Ask the successful ones, they will talk endlessly, and asking more people can make them even more successful. Ask the failures, they don't even know what success is, they only ask the successful ones, making the successful ones even more successful. What kind of rule is this? Is it the story of a general and a dead soldier?

The network, how much of the virtual is not virtual, requires more network entrepreneurs to sacrifice themselves to highlight the value of a generation of network heroes. Such a high price, why aren't those planning to enter or already in the network careful and cautious? Or do you simply not know the difficulties and treachery of this path? Is it this virtuality that relaxes your vigilance or do you inherently hold unrealistic fantasies?

There is no need for too much theoretical discussion. The network is real, as real as newspapers and television media. Real enough that behind every website is a large number of hardworking employees, real enough that every ID, IP represents a real person. What's different is its rules differ from reality. The total number of Chinese websites has reached 788,400. Compared to this data, the number of websites increased by about 55,000 in six months. Despite the rapid increase in the number of websites now, very few are profitable, achieving a thousandth would be good.

In today's or tomorrow's China, when websites reach millions, writing this article merely expresses my deep love for the network and for those who work in the network field. Regardless of whether you've lost money, time, love, life, or passion in the network, you should all say it was worth it because you were once willing.

The network itself hasn't changed, what has changed are the people involved in it. I'll end with one last sentence: All things in the world are constantly changing, but there is an unchanging truth supporting these changes. May all website executives find this unchanging truth.
