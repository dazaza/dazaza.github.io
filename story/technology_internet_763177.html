The path of big data is not without thorns, but the opportunities within it outweigh everything else.

by anonymous on 2013-07-29 16:31:29

Big Data Business Analytics, Storage, Integration, Challenges and Opportunities

As life becomes increasingly rich, big data is also becoming harder to handle. At the same time, due to the increase in data volume and the diversity of data types, technical personnel face numerous challenges and obstacles during analysis. This article will discuss why data has become more complex and difficult to manage, what challenges and barriers we face when analyzing, integrating, and storing this data, as well as the opportunities that big data may bring for the future.

Big data is indeed large and complex

How big is big data?

Take a simple example: attending a child's birthday party. Before you leave, you send out a tweet, generating data. On the way, you stop to refuel your car, and the payment process generates data. When you buy a birthday card at the supermarket, scanning your shopping card and checking out generates data. During the birthday party, taking photos and recording videos, and then uploading them to Facebook, Flickr, and YouTube generates data. Messages sent during the party also generate data. Throughout the entire process, your phone generates data by continuously sending GPS location updates, and your car generates data by tracking fuel consumption. Clearly, our daily activities generate a vast amount of data.

According to IBM, we create approximately 2.5 quintillion (1,000,000^3) bytes of data every day. In the past two years, we have generated 90% of all existing data, with data volumes increasing exponentially. As companies' data capture capabilities improve, multimedia becomes more popular, social media conversations increase, and people use the internet for more purposes, the volume of data grows at an incredible rate.

How complex is big data?

Big data is complex because of its diversity, including both structured and unstructured data. Its complexity also lies in the speed of delivery and usage, such as "real-time." Additionally, the complexity stems from the sheer volume of data. Previously, home storage was measured in MBs and GBs, but now we talk about TBs, and enterprises have already entered the PB unit.

The Big Data Market

Big data increases the demand for information management businesses. Companies like Software AG, Oracle Corporation, IBM, Microsoft, SAP, EMC, and HP have spent $15 billion on software companies specializing in data management and analysis. In 2010, the value of this industry exceeded $100 billion and grew at an annual rate of 10%—twice as fast as the overall software business.

In developed economies, big data-intensive technologies are being used more widely. Globally, there are 4.6 billion mobile terminals generating data, and between 1 and 2 billion people accessing the internet. Between 1990 and 2005, over 1 billion people entered the middle class, leading to further growth in information. In 1986, the world's telecommunications network had an effective information exchange capacity of 281 PB, 471 PB in 1993, 2.2 EB in 2000, 65 EB in 2007, and it is estimated that the total communication volume in 2013 will be 667 EB.

Big Data Analytics

Big data requires special techniques to process large volumes of data within tolerable time frames. Big data analytics practitioners generally prefer not to share storage, opting instead for Direct Attached Storage (DAS), which combines high-speed SSDs with high-capacity SATA drives within parallel internal processing nodes. Meanwhile, current shared storage architectures SAN and NAS have been labeled as slow, complex, and expensive, failing to meet the performance, commercial server, and low-cost standards required by today's big data technology.

Real-time and near real-time information delivery has become a defining feature of big data analytics, with minimizing latency being one of the primary challenges in big data technology. Data is preferably stored in memory rather than on mechanical hard drives connected via FC SANs. In big data scenarios, SAN mode imposes much higher requirements on analytical applications compared to other types of storage.

Of course, shared storage does have its own advantages in big data analytics scenarios, but since 2011, it has not been adopted by most big data practitioners.

Challenges and Barriers in Big Data

Given its complexity, big data processing faces a series of challenges:

1. How do we understand and utilize non-structured data like text or video?

2. How do we capture the most important parts of data as it is generated and deliver it in real-time to the right people?

3. Given the current data volume and computational power, how do we store, analyze, and understand this data?

4. Lack of talent

A major issue discussed currently is the lack of big data talent. Fortunately, many educational institutions have introduced relevant academic courses to address this. We also see positive trends where companies collaborate with universities to combat this talent shortage, which is the most effective way to cultivate talent.

5. Other inherent challenges include privacy, access security, and deployment

According to the latest report "Mind the Digital Marketing Gap" by EIU (Economist Intelligence Unit) and Lyris (a digital marketing software provider), 37% of marketing executives find it very challenging to convert big data insights into decision-making, while 45% believe they lack the capability to effectively analyze big data.

24% of marketers say they are using big data technology to uncover insights and formulate market strategies, though most of them only occasionally use data for feasibility analysis and personalized customer communication.

Other barriers include lack of funding (43% of respondents), excessive emphasis on digital tools and social media, increased channels, and human resource shortages (around 33% of respondents).

Opportunities in Big Data

Despite the many challenges in the application of big data technology, the opportunities far outweigh these challenges. Big data has become an absolute weapon for innovation, competition, and productivity enhancement. We can use big data to answer previously unsolvable questions. We can use big data to gain insight and knowledge, identify trends, improve productivity, gain competitive advantages, and create more value for the global economy.
