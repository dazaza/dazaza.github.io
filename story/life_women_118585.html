Facial Wrinkle Removal Surgery

(Picture 1)

As time passes and age increases, wrinkles of varying severity will appear on the face, showing signs of aging. This is something that most people do not wish to see. It can increase feelings of inferiority, weaken our self-confidence, and add pressure to work. How can we reduce these wrinkles and delay the aging of the skin? The early or late appearance of wrinkles varies from person to person and is related to skin care, living conditions, climate, and other factors. Generally speaking, around the age of 20, shallow small wrinkles may appear on the forehead; around 30, the forehead wrinkles deepen and increase, crow's feet appear at the outer corners of the eyes, and wrinkles of varying degrees appear on the upper and lower eyelids. At 40, eye bags appear, nasolabial folds deepen, and fine wrinkles appear at the corners of the mouth. At 50, the eye bags deepen and lower eyelid wrinkles appear, and wrinkles also appear on the upper and lower lips. By 60, skin elasticity decreases, and facial wrinkles deepen.

Forehead Suspension Surgery:

This is a suspension surgery for solving forehead wrinkles and sagging. During the surgery, the skin in the middle of the forehead is cut along the designed line to reach the aponeurosis, and a special flat separator is used to separate this layer forward until the area above the eyebrows and the root of the nose. Then, a specially designed small pointed hook knife is used to cut the frontalis muscle, corrugator supercilii muscle, and depressor supercilii muscle, while laser coagulation is used to stop bleeding. A sharp separation is performed under the SMAS fascia forward until the root of the nose to solve the problem of glabellar lines (the "川" character lines).

Temporal Suspension Surgery:

This is performed discreetly within the hairline on both sides of the temples to address radiating crow's feet at the outer corners of the eyes, drooping eyelids, drooping eyebrows, and sagging cheeks. It improves nasolabial folds and solves the problem of drooping corners of the mouth, achieving multiple benefits. This surgery is the most common type of suspension surgery, accounting for more than 90% of fascial suspension surgeries. It causes little pain, does not result in facial swelling after the surgery, does not affect normal work and study, and provides good facial lifting effects. Post-surgery, individuals appear about 6 to 20 years younger. Clinical observations over 10 years indicate that the results of the surgery can last for 6 to 10 years.

Forehead and Temporal Suspension Surgery:

This is generally aimed at those who need large-scale lifting of the forehead in addition to facial sagging. It is suitable for middle-aged individuals aged 35 to 45. After the surgery, individuals look about 10 years younger overall.

Full Face Suspension Surgery:

Also known as "big facelift," this involves making circular incisions along the hairline above the ears on both sides of the head. The epidermis, muscles, and fascia are not interrupted, and a wide strip of excess tissue is removed. It is suitable for individuals aged 40 to 65, providing obvious lifting effects. It eliminates the signs of aging and sagging caused by facial wrinkles and resolves the "excess fat" phenomenon. The operation usually takes between 4 to 5 hours. Due to the large area of dissection, an assistant should be present, and high-frequency electrocoagulation equipment should be used for hemostasis. This procedure is a comprehensive restoration project for facial youth.

Lower Face Suspension Surgery:

Primarily used to address aging and sagging in the lower half of the face. It mainly addresses issues such as drooping corners of the mouth, sagging and excess fat under the jaw, and "fatty lumps" below the cheeks. Since the operation uses a circumaural method, the first step is subcutaneous dissection in front of the ear, exceeding the anterior edge of the parotid gland. A fascial suspension instrument is used for blunt upward dissection of about 5mm. According to traditional anatomical concepts, the facial nerve runs directly beneath the superficial musculo-aponeurotic system (SMAS) beyond the anterior edge of the parotid gland. If the SMAS is dissected beyond the anterior edge of the parotid gland, it is extremely easy to damage the facial nerve. Therefore, the SMAS in the mid-face should not be extensively and thoroughly freed but should primarily involve O-shaped incision suspension and dissection along the hairline behind the ear. Deep vascular branches must penetrate the SMAS layer to supply blood to the surface skin and subcutaneous tissues. Despite having its own blood supply network, the blood supply to the flaps and SMAS flaps will significantly decrease after extensive dissection. Therefore, attention should be paid to the tension of the flaps and SMAS flaps during suturing to avoid blood circulation obstacles, which could lead to flap necrosis and delayed wound healing.
