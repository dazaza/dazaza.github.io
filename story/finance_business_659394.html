For keloid scars, single therapy often results in poor efficacy and a high recurrence rate. Relevant experts conducted a study on 60 patients with keloids who underwent surgical excision followed by either radionuclide patch treatment or local injection of glucocorticoids as combined treatments. The efficacy was compared with another group of 30 patients treated solely with local injections of glucocorticoids. The results showed that combined treatments were more satisfactory than single therapies. The 90 patients with keloids were randomly divided into three groups: A, B, and C.

1. Surgical Excision: For patients in Groups A and B, their keloid lesions were removed under local anesthesia. Smaller lesion areas were vertically incised along the keloid boundary, removing the scar tissue while separating the subcutaneous tissue to ensure no obvious tension after suture closure. Larger lesions required excision and autologous full-thickness skin graft transplantation, ensuring the grafts were sutured without tension and slightly larger than the secondary wound to prevent tension after suturing. All cases had abdominal donor sites and were appropriately pressure-fixed postoperatively.

2. 90Sr-90Y Patch Treatment: For patients in Group A whose wounds were directly sutured, 90Sr-90Y radiotherapy skin patch treatment began 3 to 4 days post-operation. For those receiving skin grafts, treatment commenced once the grafts were fully viable. The active surface of the 90Sr-90Y patch treatment device was placed directly on the suture site with small doses administered multiple times to prevent scar formation at the donor site.

3. Local Glucocorticoid Injection Therapy: For patients in Group B, intraoperative and postoperative glucocorticoid injection treatments were given. Triamcinolone acetonide injection was injected along the edges of the incision, targeting the dermis just below the epidermis. For patients in Group C, high-pressure injectors were used to inject triamcinolone acetonide into the damaged area until the surface turned white.

4. Efficacy Evaluation: Using a three-level classification method, the outcomes were assessed as follows:

Cured: Pain and itching disappeared, the scar completely softened, flattened, and felt soft upon touch, with no hard lumps or cord-like marks. No recurrence within 12 months post-treatment.

Markedly Effective: Pain and itching disappeared or significantly reduced, with 60%–70% of the scar softened or flattened, or the keloid severity decreased from severe to moderate or mild. No recurrence within 12 months post-treatment.

Effective: Recurrence occurred but did not exceed the original lesion range. Pain and itching persisted but improved.

Ineffective: Symptoms alleviated minimally or remained unchanged, with no changes in scar texture or size or only slight changes. Recurrence occurred within 12 months after clinical cure.

Keloids are a type of skin fibrosis disease characterized by excessive production and deposition of collagen fibers and other extracellular matrix components. Patients experience localized excessive proliferation of fibrous tissue, spreading beyond the wound, leading to pain and itching. Treatment is challenging due to the lack of a single effective method and the difficulty in controlling the high recurrence rate. Borok et al. reported a recurrence rate of 50%–80% with sole surgical treatment, which is why combination therapies involving surgery alongside radiation or drug treatments are now preferred.

The mechanism of 90Sr-90Y patch treatment involves β-rays released during the decay of radioactive isotopes acting on collagen-producing fibroblasts in scar tissue. Fibroblasts exposed to ionizing radiation undergo degeneration, reducing collagen synthesis and deposition, achieving therapeutic effects. Local injection of glucocorticoids inhibits globulin and collagenase inhibitors, increasing collagen degradation, reducing collagen synthesis and fibroblast proliferation, mitigating inflammatory responses, decreasing elastin synthesis in fibroblasts, inhibiting fibroblast proliferation and apoptosis, and reducing the synthesis of Types I, II, and III collagen mRNA in normal skin and scar fibroblasts, thereby preventing scar hyperplasia.

Combining surgery with radiation therapy or glucocorticoid injection therapy removes the proliferative keloid lesion fundamentally, relieves contracture at the scar site, and repairs local shape changes or activity impairments caused by excessive keloid growth. This greatly improves external deformities and enhances the aesthetic value of keloid treatment. Additionally, these two mechanisms significantly suppress recurrence rates and markedly improve clinical efficacy.
