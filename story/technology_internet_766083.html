Can you imagine how cheaply Nokia was sold?

by anonymous on 2013-09-04 15:01:50

It was originally thought that the focus of September would be a carnival of new products from mobile phone manufacturers, but the technology world always brings unexpected "surprises" to onlookers - the news that Microsoft is about to acquire Nokia's devices and services department quickly swept across all tech media outlets.

This acquisition is destined to be recorded in history, with its novel-like plot and bizarre price lingering for a long time. The $7.17 billion acquisition price is considered very low, and a comparison makes it clear.

Google acquired Motorola Mobility for $12.5 billion

On August 15, 2011, Google announced the acquisition of Motorola Mobility at $40 per share, totaling $12.5 billion, representing a premium of 63% over Motorola Mobility's closing price on the last trading day.

In the second quarter of 2011, Motorola Mobility's net revenue was $3.3 billion, with an operating loss of $23 million, and had 19,000 employees. That quarter saw a total shipment of 11 million mobile devices, including 4.4 million smartphones and 0.44 million tablets.

In the second quarter of 2013, Nokia’s Devices & Services division reported net revenues of $3.56 billion, with an operating loss of $43.46 million. After the transaction is completed, 32,000 employees will transfer from Nokia to Microsoft. During this quarter, total mobile device sales amounted to 65.4 million units, including 53.7 million feature phones, 7.4 million WP phones, and 4.3 million Asha phones.

Microsoft acquired Skype for $8.5 billion

On May 10, 2011, Microsoft announced it would spend $8.5 billion to acquire Skype. Although Skype claimed to be the "largest internet telephone operator in the world with nearly 660 million users," the company had never made a net profit since its founding in 2003. In 2010, Skype's revenue was only $860 million, with a net loss of $7 million, and there were no signs indicating that Skype's financial situation would improve.

Notably, Skype had only around 500 employees when it was acquired by Microsoft.

Microsoft acquired aQuantive for $6 billion

In May 2007, Microsoft announced the acquisition of all shares of online advertising company aQuantive for $6 billion. The acquisition price of $66.5 per share was 85% higher than aQuantive's closing price on its last trading day.

In 2006, aQuantive's sales were $442.2 million, with a net profit of $54 million. By the time of the acquisition, aQuantive had approximately 2,600 employees.

Xiaomi Valuation at $10 billion

Xiaomi was founded in April 2010, and just over three years later, its valuation reached $10 billion. In the first half of 2013, the company achieved revenues of $2.15 billion, selling 7.03 million phones. Lei Jun once stated that the company's full-year revenue for 2013 would reach $4.57 billion, with a potential phone sales volume of 15 million units.

Although MIUI is one of the key reasons many people are optimistic about Xiaomi, the monthly revenue generated around MIUI is only 20 million yuan, less than 1% of the company's average monthly total revenue in the first half of the year. Therefore, hardware remains Xiaomi's core source of income.

It is understood that Xiaomi currently has around 3,500 employees.

Summary

A company that has been mass-producing phones for nearly 20 years, a company whose current phone sales still reach 65.4 million units, a company with a deep cultural heritage, rich technical accumulation, and countless loyal fans, finally sold its long-prided devices and services department for an almost unimaginable $7.17 billion.

A plot like a novel.
