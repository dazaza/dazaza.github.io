Abstract: In the late 19th century, photography reached its peak in an environment where classical painting art was flourishing. Photography influenced realistic painting but also found new paths with Impressionism; it also gave rise to ancient art.

Keywords: photography, Impressionism, the end of art; mechanical reproduction in photography's nearly 170-year history has had a noticeable impact on our lives. From the beginning, it changed the way people review themselves, and almost all areas underwent revolutionary changes due to photography. In human history, photography made a firm mark, separating the world of photography into distinct generations. Coincidentally, as photography matured in the late 19th century, history kept echoing Hegel's prophecy: the end of art. So, what is the connection between the emergence of photography, art, and the rise of modernism at the end of the year? First, in the 19th century, European painting experienced unprecedented prosperity with the birth of photography, from classicism to romanticism, from realism to impressionism, especially in the last decade of the 19th century, when the seeds of modern art began to sprout in various schools. Throughout history, Western painting has followed the unique interpretive methods of Greek philosopher Aristotle. Impressionist painters interpreted the world in their own unique way of expression. In the latter half of the 17th century, British scientist Isaac Newton concluded through his prism that light is the root of color. Taking this as a basis, impressionist painters sought to express the rich and subtle color changes of light by directly observing nature and sensitively capturing their own feelings. There is still controversy over who exactly invented photography because many were working on it at the same time, but it can at least be attributed to two individuals: Frenchmen Louis Daguerre and Nicéphore Niépce. Daguerre was born in 1787 in the town of Cormeilles-en-Parisis in northern France. As a young artist, he designed three dioramas at around fifteen years old that had special lighting effects displaying panoramic views. Engaged in this work, he equipped himself with the worldview necessary for automatically reproducing images without brushes or paints – that is, becoming interested in the camera. Daguerre worked on previous efforts to make cameras functional but failed. He met Niépce in 1827, who had been striving to invent a camera and had achieved some success. Two years later, they became collaborators. Niépce passed away in 1833, but Daguerre continued his efforts. In 1837, he successfully invented a practical photographic method called daguerreotype (silver plate photography), which was officially announced on August 19, 1839, marking the symbolic birth of photography. Since the advent of photography, it has influenced painting. Indeed, photography has had the greatest impact on the concept of painting. Before the invention of photography, Western painting pursued the sole reality of true representation. The advantages of classical painting were challenged by photography, allowing artists to create better works using machines rather than relying on human hands, thus changing perspectives and freeing painting from realism.
