Dianping: The Bone Giants Can't Chew, Living in the Zone of Discomfort

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:06:33

Tao Zhang, CEO of Dianping.com

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Jing Yang, photographed by Nan Xiao

Dianping.com's CEO Tao Zhang has postponed the company’s IPO once again.

In 2011, when Dianping received a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, its valuation reached $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an appropriate time for an IPO within the next one to two years, but it didn’t. Instead, in 2012, it carried out another $60 million Series D funding round.

With Dianping’s scale and development momentum, going public wouldn't be difficult. However, Zhang feels that an IPO now would be too costly. He breaks down the large internet (PC + mobile) into four elements: people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point presents an opportunity for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is where the fourth giant could arise. Since 2003, Dianping has been working hard to accumulate data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where phones bridge "location" and "services." Following Zhang’s logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping to imagine as the fourth giant.

However, outsiders don’t see it this way. In recent months, with frequent acquisitions by BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; whichever family name from BAT it adopts would help them achieve great things sooner. On the other hand, Dianping’s size is far smaller compared to BAT, and it's still premature to declare it a future giant. Moreover, whether it’s Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in Gaode and Meituan, they all directly target Dianping’s territory — local life services primarily focused on dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure.

Before proving the following two points, a Dianping IPO would be equivalent to selling at a discount. First, Zhang needs some time to prove that what Dianping is doing now, BAT cannot replicate. Second, Dianping is about to enter the final battle of group buying. If it secures the top position, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping’s “decisive year.” For all the speculation and rumors, Zhang pays them no heed. He is clear: “The internet has indeed entered a great era of integration, but Dianping will not be acquired.”

Zhang categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type possesses platform attributes but is better suited to develop within a giant's ecosystem, such as maps and email. These products can easily attract large numbers of users but struggle to find independent business models. Others, like browsers, may make money, but the revenue generated through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. “Giants are willing to invest in a mail product because it helps with branding and user retention, but I've never heard of any standalone email company succeeding.” The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type includes companies facing financial difficulties who sell themselves to survive.

Now Meituan has been brought under Alibaba's wing. Theoretically, Dianping should quickly seek a “backer” to avoid being defeated by a rival gaining capital and rapidly expanding. Zhang, however, says, “The giants offer us limited help. We do need users, but execution is even more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution capability and there's also the risk of being marginalized by a larger company.” Furthermore, he wants Dianping to maintain the most precise "distance" from the giants. If Dianping gets too close to one, it would be akin to "taking sides," making it an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically speaking, acquiring Dianping becomes a dead end. Zhang counters, “Why would we take their money? We’re not short of funds; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to hold a small stake and provide us with many resources, I might consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they only held a small stake?”

Weapon One: Dirty and Tiring Work

In the mobile internet era, opportunities for companies solely focused on pure internet products are decreasing. New opportunities come from those that combine with traditional industries. Zhang believes that life services mainly revolve around dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure. Other niche markets like ride-hailing can form good business models, but it's unlikely for any of these to grow into giants.

For dining, drinking, entertainment, and leisure, user demands are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is at the core of life services, which is exactly where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or brand, later entrants find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship battle, and Dianping plans to face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market. As long as there are two strong competitors, the industry's gross profit margin will be pulled down, and stable profitability will be hard to achieve.

Another foreseeable future lies in reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline closed loop, but this must be based on Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, building a closed loop will be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began trying out reservations. Zhang said, “Both users and merchants currently give positive feedback, but if we were to separately focus on reservations, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem.”

Of course, life services present some heavier opportunities. For instance, food delivery is a rigid demand for users, and merchants can increase sales. In the past one or two years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet Society" have flourished. However, the core of food delivery is distribution, meaning delivery within a very short period of time, along with issues like soup spilling. The logistics requirements are higher than even fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang said, “If executed well, food delivery still holds potential, but it's harder dirty work, and Dianping is already doing enough dirty and tiring work.”

If judged by age, Dianping is already an “old” company. Over the past decade, it has diligently accumulated merchant information and user reviews, which Zhang refers to as “dirty and tiring work.” This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but past hardships have become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real rivals in the life services sector.

Still, he must remain vigilant against BAT's “clutches.” Baidu’s involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, allowing users to search for nearby dining and other life services using the map. Tencent, considered to have secured a ticket to the mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest physical goods trading platform, and it makes sense to expand its offerings to include services. Moreover, it has included Meituan and Gaode in its strategic map.

“Whether it’s Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there are no signs of explosive growth in terms of reality and data,” Zhang said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to dine, drink, entertain, or relax.

Moreover, in the vast field of life services, competition among giants will inevitably overlap, making it impossible for anyone to completely monopolize the area. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on platforms like Weibo and WeChat. Zhang feels that this actually makes Dianping safer. “In niches like car rentals and business travel, the massive user base directed by the giants could easily overwhelm existing companies.”

As for Alibaba, which has invested in Gaode, invading Dianping’s territory won't be easy either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can always be categorized no matter how peculiar. A diner’s experience depends on factors like the day’s environment, waitstaff, and chefs. Managing merchants can't rely on systems and tools like Alibaba does; it requires a "ground force" managed by humans.

“All three giants want to go down this path, and there is room for imagination, but this isn’t a highway; it’s a rugged mountain road.” Zhang believes that first, providing life services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, something BAT lacks the genes and ability to manage offline teams; second, they lack the motivation. Life services require dedicated effort and aren’t something a small team can accomplish. Moreover, earning money is slow. “A company finds it hard to excel in two important areas simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate its advantages in its original field.”

Weapon Two: Small Boats Are Easier to Turn

In 2011, when Dianping raised $100 million in financing, Zhang told the outside world that Dianping was “Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare.” Unfortunately, the world changes fast, and before Dianping went public, his comparison objects had all fallen from grace.

He wasn’t anxious about it either. “They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams, now they want to become America’s Dianping.”

The current path is something Zhang has gradually figured out himself. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying lit up his eyes. Initially, he also viewed group buying as a marketing platform, similar to the American model, with one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized that group buying had the characteristics of a trading platform. Thus, he saw the future of Dianping as a life services trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used to transition Dianping from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this transformation, Zhang might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth seat among the giants today. But while talking about transformation sounds simple, it was equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process filled with extreme pain.

First, compared to simply selling ads, a trading platform has lower gross margins and requires stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, a trading platform demands higher cross-departmental communication capabilities.

Previously, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and the sales team were completely separated. Zhang even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments — even for important clients, sales couldn’t alter information on the platform, which once led to some mutual hostility between content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication to achieve good marketing plans, which require both planning and editorial cooperation. He said, “Maintaining information quality remains important, but there’s no need to reinforce animosity. We need to dismantle the firewall.”

Though editors can logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, the inertia is even greater. Zhang specifically held several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. Months later, an editor manager casually mentioned to a sales manager, “I used to think you were my enemy, but now I realize you're my colleague.”

The sales department also needed transformation. To promote conversion, Zhang cleverly gave the offline team dual roles. First, for merchants, salespeople should become marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants are the company's buyers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety of goods must also be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Of course, dual roles will inevitably lead to a series of complex problems. For frontline personnel, this can be quite challenging, requiring them to balance the scales themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual role" is very similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to the front lines allows the company to react faster, but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffered more after the transformation.

Every year, Zhang breaks down Dianping's company goals into various departments, then executives further break them down layer by layer, but breaking down isn't just simple addition and subtraction. Co-founder Li Jing is responsible for the sales team. Sometimes, if a merchant's needs aren't met, it's a sales issue, but it affects sales, so Li Jing broke down a new position, setting up communication specialists who daily collect information from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change doesn't mean liking change. No one likes change; everyone feels uncomfortable when faced with it. All employees must go through discomfort zones, including myself."

The numbers reflect the weight of Dianping's execution team. Out of the current 3000+ employees, over 2000 work offline. The ground forces are responsible for directly communicating with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and combined marketing solutions.

For this core team that resists BAT's clutches, Dianping has a unique management evaluation system.

They aren’t called “salespeople”; from the first day, their business cards read “Marketing Planning Consultant.” Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the phase of watching news and chatting on QQ, at least three to five years behind Taobao merchants in terms of internet understanding. Marketing planning consultants need to tell merchants what online users' needs are and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, “Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing advisors wouldn’t suggest a fish maw or shark fin group-buying deal; perhaps another dish instead, because online users differ from offline users.”

An excellent marketing advisor must be a versatile player. They need to explain clearly to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the essential differences between discounts and group buying; among different discounts, which one better fits their positioning. If dealing with a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing advisor also needs to know whether it’s the off-season or peak season, which products have rising costs during certain seasons, and propose a group-buying menu that both merchants and users like.

This sets a high standard for marketing advisors, who must not only be internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, “I must admit, not every advisor can be a double expert, so we set star ratings for advisors.” Like hotels, marketing advisors range from one-star to five-star, with the best ones being Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren’t elite tech men from prestigious schools, making it difficult for them to understand internet products. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing advisors, containing knowledge points and related cases, accessible to both new and experienced employees. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will help answer.

A large number of knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees combining practical experiences. Dianping has established a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, with results directly linked to promotions.

Compared to internet knowledge, industry knowledge is harder to acquire. Some experiences are summarized by traditional merchants after decades of industry practice. A Super Star marketing advisor told Li Jing that the hairdressing industry should avoid offering shampoo-cut-blowout group-buying packages, because men have little motivation to buy, and women prefer blowouts without shampoos. Li Jing was astonished, “We men would never have thought of that.”

Therefore, Dianping’s marketing advisors are not only divided by cities but also by industries such as catering, hairdressing, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is even subdivided into hotpot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing advisor responsible for one or two cuisines. “This way, marketing advisors can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, making merchants less resistant when selling products,” he said.

Each marketing group must conduct its own case-sharing sessions: what challenges a particular type of merchant faces, what marketing needs they have, what solutions the company provides, and what effects they achieve. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition — Entrepreneur Winner. In the first three months of joining, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing experiences. If they are responsible for hot pot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees defend their presentations on-site, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, rewarding them accordingly.

Strategically speaking, regardless of Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, their tasks are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O, whenever someone understands the industry somewhat, their words sound alike. “The era where storytelling and creating concepts could lead to success has passed. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy,” Zhang said.
