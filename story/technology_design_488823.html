"The author of 'This Is a Farewell Song'"

"This Is a Farewell Song" Author: Chen Zhiyao

1)

The essence of an event is never what it appears to be.

In September 1996, I had just turned 16, knew nothing, and hadn't experienced anything, not even the method for growing asparagus ferns. I was carefree, at peace with myself, and my heart was as hard as an ice cube. Things I liked came easily, so my passion for anything was fleeting. I attended a decent middle school with average grades. I wasn't obedient, but I also didn't cause trouble.

The only issue was that my family was a bit unusual. My parents were once French teachers at the same university. My father went to France in the early 80s, shortly after I was born, to pursue a degree in either linguistics or comparative literature. Then, like a relay race, he returned home while my mother went abroad. The difference was, under unforeseen circumstances, after receiving her degree, she got an excellent opportunity to stay in Europe as a diplomat. Naturally, several years later, she divorced my father and married an American working in Paris. But don't worry, this didn't make me eccentric or self-motivated. They parted amicably, both behaving like mature, civilized individuals. After the divorce, my father continued to wear beige overcoats and act flirtatiously among groups of 20-something female students. My mother visited me every year, bringing fashionable clothes and exotic gifts, making me the envy of my friends.

So, up until then, apart from my homeroom teacher occasionally speculating about my mental state due to family reasons, my life was all normal. In fact, compared to my friends, I had more freedom and pocket money. I watched movies, bought records, and read all kinds of books. For me, life was filled with fleeting passions and boredom. Until I fell in love with someone, a boy my age. That unfamiliar, vivid, slightly bitter yet sweet taste lingered for the next ten years.

That boy was Zhou Junyan.

That year, we both entered the first year of high school. In junior high, I already knew about him; we were in the same grade but different classes. He was the best student among the boys, not nerdy, had obtained a national second-level athlete qualification in swimming, and was universally handsome. His father was a district government leader. He was everyone's favorite: the math teacher admired his effortless ability to solve an algebra problem in three ways; the female classmates loved watching him serve gracefully in shorts and short sleeves on the baseline; the parents dreamed of cloning such a son; and the principal adored his dad. I, who often daydreamed in class and avoided group activities whenever possible, was no exception—I liked him too.

On the first day back to school in September, I walked into the classroom and saw him cleaning with a group of classmates. I had grown a lot during the summer, reaching 172 cm, but he was still half a head taller than me. He wore the school uniform, a white shirt and navy blue khaki pants, holding a cloth in his hand. His hair at the forehead was slightly damp with sweat, yet he still looked neat and clean. He looked up, saw me, couldn’t recall my name, but smiled briefly as a greeting. I was captivated by this small expression that lasted less than a second.

The high school rule was that boys sat with boys and girls with girls. After the seating arrangement, I found out I was sitting directly behind him, which made me exceptionally happy. My desk partner was Han Xiaogeng, with long hair tied in a ponytail that, if let down, would reach her waist. Thinking about how her soft, shiny long hair hung right in front of him made me feel disheartened because of my own extremely short haircut.

When I got home, I found one of my ID photos and used a black ink pen to draw long hair hanging from my ears to my chest, but it looked awkward, so I tore it up.

During dinner, I hugged my father’s neck and asked him, “Do you prefer girls with short or long hair?”

“Long.”

“Should I grow my hair long?”

“You look better with short hair; your face shape suits it.”

“Nonsense, does that mean I can never be attractive?”

I gave him a glare and resolved to grow my hair long. However, transitioning from very short hair to long hair isn’t easy—it goes through a long phase where the hair is neither here nor there, looking quite unappealing. Eventually, after only two months, I couldn’t bear sitting in front of Zhou Junyan with such a messy and embarrassing hairstyle, so I cut my hair short again.

In junior high, I loved reading novels or simply daydreaming during class. If called upon to answer a question, I wouldn't hesitate to say, "Teacher, I didn't hear the question clearly, could you repeat it?" Then the classmates would laugh, and the teacher would faint. However, in the first year of high school, I behaved especially well in class. Even when it was cold, I would get up without being called, leave early, listen attentively in class, and diligently do my homework, improving my grades significantly. My father thought I had matured, but in reality, I just didn't want to embarrass myself in front of Zhou Junyan.

By the end of autumn, the first-year students performed a play called "Thunderstorm." Zhou Junyan played Zhou Ping, and Han Xiaogeng played Fan Yi. I didn't even get to play a maid. Though I usually lacked interest in such group activities, I was genuinely disappointed this time. Moreover, the Chinese teacher directing the play acted very professionally, imitating Ang Lee (that year, "Sense and Sensibility" won the Oscar), encouraging natural interaction between the main characters by arranging many outings or sports activities beyond rehearsals. To stay true to the original work, some details in the play greatly challenged the acting limits of high school students.

Rumors began circulating among the students that Zhou Junyan and Han Xiaogeng were dating. No one really knew how they were dating, but everyone was willing to believe it. Han Xiaogeng was widely recognized as a beauty, with a good temperament and popular among peers. On her birthday, she invited 50-60 classmates to sing karaoke and eat cake, a grand gesture at the time. Furthermore, Han Xiaogeng's father was the president of a massive corporation, having recently purchased the city's first private yacht, proudly docked at the suburban pier and reported in newspapers. If any girl could win Zhou Junyan's heart and gain universal approval, it could only be Han Xiaogeng.

Possibly the entire school believed they were dating except for me. Han Xiaogeng might have had feelings for Zhou Junyan, but he didn’t show her any particular attention, remaining consistently polite. What bothered me was that Han Xiaogeng was universally acknowledged as beautiful, and I, too, was said to be the most beautiful girl in our school. However, this claim came from a tomboy from another school, rumored to be a genuine lesbian who menstruated only once every three months.

Not long after, I joined the school volleyball team, naturally because Zhou Junyan was also participating. By April, as the weather warmed up, we almost stayed late every day after school to practice for an inter-school competition. Once, while performing a blocking move, I was bumped by a teammate, and my hand hit the net pole, creating a cut and causing my hand to swell considerably. I didn’t cry or shout; the coach looked at it and said, “This girl is tough.” Then he shouted from the field: “Zhou Junyan, accompany Cheng Wenjin to the medical room to treat her hand.”

The male team training had already ended, and Zhou Junyan was packing up equipment. He agreed immediately, ran over, looked at my hand, and said, “It’s serious; let’s go quickly.” Seeing that I was wearing short sleeves and shorts for playing volleyball, he added, “Where are your clothes? It’s pretty cold outside.” Before I could respond, he had already run to a pile of bags and clothes at the side of the field and fetched my sportswear for me. I clumsily dressed, and he helped pull the sleeves to avoid touching my wound. I felt many people were watching, and my face flushed hot.
