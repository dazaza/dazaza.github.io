In BIPAP Vision ventilator, clinical application and care _

by ncpimqudxsx on 2012-02-15 13:48:08

In the clinical application and care of the BIPAP Vision ventilator, a calm response is needed to alleviate the patient's psychological worries and enhance their confidence. Before preparation: (1) psychological care; (2) test the machine; (3) select the appropriate nasal mask. Care includes: (1) Life Care. The use of non-invasive ventilation limits the patient's daily activities, so increase inspections by ward nurses, establish an effective means of communication, and correctly interpret the patient’s expressions through eye contact, facial expressions, and gestures. (2) Diet Care. Non-invasive ventilation requires higher nutritional needs due to varying degrees of coughing and expectoration, leading to more loss of body protein and water. Based on the patient’s nutritional status and dietary preferences, arrange a reasonable diet. High-calorie, high-protein, vitamin-rich, and easily digestible food should be given. (3) Respiratory Management. Keeping the airway open is essential for mechanical ventilation. Although the BIPAP Vision ventilator uses the patient’s own respiratory ventilation without an artificial airway, when secretions increase causing shortness of breath, it can still affect ventilatory function. An appropriate position such as semi-supine or sitting should be given to patients, ensuring the head, neck, and shoulder are at the same level with the head slightly tilted back. This facilitates smooth airflow and prevents gas exchange inefficiency. Guide patients in deep breathing and effective coughing, assist with back tapping and hydration to facilitate sputum expulsion. For difficult phlegm discharge, oxygen inhalation can be started or the humidifier adjusted to increase gas temperature, alleviating throat discomfort and diluting respiratory tract secretions, thus facilitating sputum discharge and reducing lung infection incidence. (4) Monitoring between II and the suction machine. When using non-invasive ventilation with BIPAP VI, distilled water must be used in the humidifier. Pour 200ml of distilled water into the humidifier before each use to moisten the gas entering the airway, preventing respiratory drying. Maintain a temperature of 32~34°C, adding distilled water according to season, indoor temperature, and humidity to regulate the humidifier temperature. Closely observe the normal operation of the ventilator and its indicators, paying attention to any alarms. (5) Observation condition. Closely observe the frequency of spontaneous breathing, rhythm, ventilator synchronization, adequacy of ventilation, and the quietness of patients actively synchronized with the breathing machine. Complications include: (1) Abdominal distension, the most common complication in non-invasive ventilator patients. Nurses should adhere to the principle of prevention as soon as possible, performing abdominal massages or using Glauber’s salt hot compresses to stimulate bowel movements and reduce bloating. Patients with obvious abdominal distension may undergo decompression or be given drugs to promote motility. (2) Compression injury is a common complication. Non-invasive ventilator patients should relax every 4 hours during continuous use, massaging local skin every 15~30 minutes. Apply homemade cod liver oil ointment on both sides of the nose to reduce friction injuries. Ventilator disinfection involves maintaining a certain temperature and humidity conducive to bacterial growth and reproduction, requiring the ventilator filter to be disinfected every two days. Wipe the surface of the machine with 75% alcohol daily; wipe the nasal mask with 75% alcohol and then with saline-soaked gauze daily; soak the threaded tube in 2% glutaraldehyde weekly, rinse with running water, and dry for backup. After weaning, in addition to the above steps, internal cleaning, dust collector maintenance, debugging, and maintenance should also be performed. Place the ventilator in a clean, dry fixed location for timely maintenance if necessary. Conclusion: The BIPAP Vision non-invasive ventilator treatment for mild respiratory failure, COPD, and OSAHS patients is easy and safe, effectively correcting hypoxemia and hypercapnia, increasing ventilation while avoiding invasive treatments like endotracheal intubation and tracheostomy, reducing complications and mortality, making it more acceptable to patients and their families. This requires nurses to have exquisite techniques, a cool head, and good professionalism to better serve patients.
