According to experts from the Yangzhou Armed Police Hospital's Anorectal Center, as the saying goes, "nine out of ten people suffer from hemorrhoids," and many friends have experienced hemorrhoids or their recurrence. Surveys show that due to changes in lifestyle and eating habits, the incidence of hemorrhoid diseases is gradually increasing. Anorectal experts inform everyone that in fact, 90% of patients with hemorrhoids have poor eating habits.

Hemorrhoids are actually formed by the dilation of subcutaneous blood vessels around the anus, partly related to genetic factors. However, poor eating habits are the biggest culprit. Nine out of ten patients have very unhealthy eating habits, preferring fried, stir-fried, and "heaty" foods while rejecting high-fiber vegetables and fruits. Teachers, office administrative staff, servers, and other professions that involve prolonged sitting or standing are high-risk groups for hemorrhoids.

It is worth noting that having bad eating habits does not always mean consuming things one shouldn't, such as spicy foods or alcohol. Although these foods indeed cause congestion in the anal and rectal vessels, and chili peppers can directly irritate the anus leading to venous congestion and inflammation of the anal sinuses. However, some seemingly minor dietary habits can also be triggers for hemorrhoids, such as eating only refined grains and high-protein foods while consuming fewer vegetables and fruits, which easily cause and worsen constipation, thereby triggering hemorrhoids.

Tip: Prevention of Hemorrhoids Starts with Diet

To prevent hemorrhoids, experts from the Yangzhou Armed Police Hospital's Anorectal Center suggest drinking more water, light tea, and consuming foods like radishes, tomatoes, pears, lilies, and silver ear fungus to replenish bodily fluids. The diet should also appropriately include coarse grains, beans, vegetables, and fruits rich in fiber, increasing intestinal motility, and preventing constipation. Additionally, do not suppress the urge to defecate, avoid reading or looking at newspapers while on the toilet, and refrain from squatting for long periods or excessive straining. Prolonged squatting and straining can cause anal congestion, leading to pathological hypertrophy, prolapse, and bleeding of the anal cushions.

Experts from the Yangzhou Armed Police Hospital's Anorectal Center state that using food to regulate and prevent constipation is the most scientific method. Moreover, high-fiber vegetables like spinach, celery, bamboo shoots, pig and sheep intestines, black sesame seeds, and honey can be consumed more often, while spicy foods should be minimized.
