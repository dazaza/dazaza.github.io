"Shut down all social internet cafes? Run by the government?" This statement by CPPCC member Yan Qi came across as quite abrupt - Nanjing SEO Bar.

by maomaochaoren on 2010-03-02 11:03:57

Yan Qi, a member of the Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference (CPPCC), should be famous now. Her remarks this time can be described as stunning. She actually proposed the idea of "closing all social Internet cafes and having the government run public Internet cafes." This suggestion has caused a great stir and strong reactions from all sectors.

With the imminent convening of the National Two Sessions, Yan Qi, a national CPPCC member and chairman of Tao Ran Ju Catering Group, made a startling statement: close all social Internet cafes and have the government run public Internet cafes.

Regarding this proposal, Yan Qi will submit a related proposal during this year's National Two Sessions. "The various social problems derived from Internet cafes have become a chronic social issue, and for chronic issues, drastic measures should be taken."

A Letter

Mother: Child skips class to play games

Just before the convening of the National Two Sessions, Yan Qi received a letter from a mother. This mother wrote in her letter: her child had always performed well academically in primary school, but after entering junior high school, she became addicted to online games.

"The child plays games at home, skips classes to play games. In order to go online and play games, the child would rather starve than give up buying gaming equipment online."

This mother's letter deeply touched Yan Qi's heart: "I am also a mother, and I can understand this mother's feelings."

"Regarding various social Internet cafes, regions across the country, including Chongqing, have implemented various measures, such as promoting online cards to prevent minors from going online; implementing timed disconnection measures to prevent Internet cafes from operating all night."

Yan Qi said that currently, these measures seem to have little effect.

A Research

Yan Qi: Many elementary school students playing in Internet cafes

After receiving this mother's letter, Yan Qi conducted an on-site survey at some social Internet cafes.

Yan Qi said that near a primary school in the main urban area, there were two Internet cafes. When she entered, she found that many elementary school students were online there, and the rule requiring holding a card to go online was merely a dead letter.

Upon entering the Internet cafe, Yan Qi carefully observed and found that most of the students online were playing online games; some were chatting online; others were browsing various inappropriate websites.

Yan Qi said, according to the "2007 China Youth Internet Addiction Data Report," 9.7% of youth internet users are identified as having internet addiction, where more than 20% of those with strained teacher-student relationships, family relationships, and peer relationships were affected. According to surveys of incarcerated juvenile offenders and juvenile labor re-education personnel, 54.1% of juvenile crimes and offenses were related to the internet, among which 35% of internet-related criminal offenses stemmed from the lack of funds for internet use, leading to criminal motives, while 28.3% were due to the influence of pornographic and violent information on the internet, causing impulsive criminal behavior.

Yan Qi said, this shows that many social problems arise because of Internet cafes. For profit, Internet cafes neglect the social responsibilities they should bear.

A Radical Solution

Proposal: Ban all social Internet cafes

How to treat the chronic problem of Internet cafes?

Yan Qi will submit a proposal during this year's National Two Sessions, prescribing a radical solution: suggest banning all social Internet cafes.

Yan Qi said that although the operation of various social Internet cafes brings in some tax revenue and solves employment issues for some people, compared to the negative social impact it causes, these "benefits" are insignificant.

Therefore, Yan Qi said that the government must take strong measures and forcibly close down social Internet cafes.

After closing social Internet cafes, there are still families and groups in society who cannot afford computers. Due to their normal work and life needs, they still require access to the internet. Yan Qi suggested that the government fund a batch of public Internet cafes.

"Publicly-run Internet cafes do not aim for profit, so they are easier to supervise," Yan Qi said. Public Internet cafes absolutely prohibit minors from entering; install bad information filters in public Internet cafes to prevent browsing of pornographic and violent websites; operate within specified hours.

Close Social Internet Cafes

Losses Covered by Government

Reporter: Social Internet cafes solve employment problems for a large part of the population. How will the employment of this group be addressed if social Internet cafes are closed?

Yan Qi: While closing social Internet cafes, the government must introduce a series of support measures, including employment measures. For example, the government can fund skill training for employees already working in Internet cafes to enhance their employability; create more public positions to provide them with job opportunities.

Reporter: Who will bear the losses from their operation of Internet cafes?

Yan Qi: The government will cover the costs. For the stability of society and the healthy growth of young people, the government should and is capable of covering these costs.

At the same time as closing social Internet cafes, the government will open a batch of public Internet cafes. These public Internet cafes can utilize some venues and equipment from social Internet cafes, compensating for the losses of Internet cafe operators while saving time and some funds for opening public Internet cafes.

Netizens: Cannot Implement a "One-Size-Fits-All" Approach

Regarding this proposal, some teachers and parents interviewed yesterday expressed support: government-run public Internet cafes will surely have managers who strictly enforce rules to prevent minors from accessing the Internet.

Yesterday, the reporter listened to some netizens' opinions online regarding this proposal, and most netizens supported it. However, some netizens pointed out that banning social Internet cafes requires a process and cannot be done with a "one-size-fits-all" approach.

Netizen "My Love" said that if a one-size-fits-all approach is used, and all social Internet cafes are simultaneously shut down while government public Internet cafes are not yet sufficient, it will inevitably fail to meet the needs of some Internet users. Therefore, depending on the different situations in various regions, a timetable should be arranged with a buffer period.

Netizens List the "Four Sins" of Internet Cafes

Yesterday, a netizen detailed the "four sins" of Internet cafes in a blog. First, accepting minors is a common occurrence. Second, operating within 200 meters of schools. Third, licensed Internet cafes operate beyond permitted hours. Fourth, unlicensed Internet cafes or "black Internet cafes" exist.
