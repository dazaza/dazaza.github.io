What does the term "black shadow in rotary kiln" refer to?

by hx515317 on 2012-02-28 13:26:48

The so-called "black shadow" is the dark shadow of the raw material observed through a blue glass mirror at the kiln head, below the flame. The raw material quickly heats up from around 1000°C to about 1280°C in the exothermic reaction zone. Lime at around 1280°C emits intense radiation light, which compared to materials at lower temperatures at the start of this zone, creates a noticeable difference in brightness. This low-temperature material appears darker and, after the light refracts towards the fire observation hole, it forms the commonly seen black shadow.

Uniformly fine clinker particles in a rotary kiln indicate appropriate mix proportions, normal calcination, complete material reactions, and solid, smooth kiln lining. Stable or unstable heat engineering within the kiln can lead to different colors in clinker particle formation. If the out-of-kiln clinker particles are uniformly fine, gray-green in color, with a dense fracture surface when broken open that has small pores and shiny crystals without severe melting traces or yellow core, it indicates normal calcination, reasonable parameters, proper mix proportions, and good clinker quality. If the clinker in the kiln is large like melons, small like fists, with very few small particles, and the out-of-kiln clinker is gray-white and smooth with a strong tactile sensation, it means the fire temperature was too high. If there are fewer particles and more powder, with loose texture of the clinker particles, it indicates the fire temperature was too low. Another situation is when the material is burnt sticky and scattered, with few clinker particles and much 0.5mm fine clinker sand. After leaving the kiln, it appears deep ink green, lightweight, and with many pores on the fracture surface. Fireworkers call this "flying sand material," which can seriously affect the long-term safe operation of the kiln. There is another type where the particle formation is normal, but after breaking open, the core is brownish-red. Fireworkers call this "yellow core material," which results from incomplete combustion of coal powder reducing high-valence iron to low-valence iron. In summary, the quality of clinker particle formation marks the calcination condition and should not be overlooked. Controlling uniform and fine clinker particle formation during rotary kiln operation, ensuring normal calcination, is something fireworkers should always focus on. During rotary kiln operation, controlling the position of the black shadow plays an important role in ensuring clinker quality and stabilizing the kiln's thermal engineering system.
