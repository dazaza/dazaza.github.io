"After 'Breaking the Waves' won the highest honor 'Palme d'Or' at Cannes in 1996, in 2000 Lars von Trier took 'Dancer in the Dark' and won two awards at Cannes again for best film and best actress.

Since winning the award, 'Dancer in the Dark' has received widespread acclaim, and its DVD has become a must-have item for many DVD collectors. Generally speaking, when mentioning Lars von Trier, those more familiar with film history will likely bring up the 'Dogma Manifesto', or even the 'Dogma Oath' led by Lars von Trier in 1995. Of course, there are also those who sneer at it; Cui Weiping believed that the use of the 'Dogma Manifesto' in 'Dancer in the Dark' was pretentious. Cui Weiping thought that due to the use of handheld cameras, the film featured many shaky shots, making ordinary audiences feel restless, dizzy, and disoriented. The movement of the camera created quite a few unnecessary shots, and the direct movement of the lens did not just allocate time between the two people talking but also excessively allocated it to the blurred 'transitions'. This 'caused the depth of events to be canceled out due to an inability to focus, and appeared hasty, flat, and rigid due to excessive haste.'
