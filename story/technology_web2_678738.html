(2009-11-09 18:13:49) Miscellaneous talk Classification: Science Fiction Listening to Xia Jia's lecture is a kind of enjoyment, and this theme may be the most attractive one in the series of science fiction grand lectures (thanks to the Forty-Two Working Group). Xia Jia told us that women, apart from being virtuous wives and good mothers and other "things" (she always said these two words and then corrected herself), can also be five types of people: female warriors, female slaves, female monsters, goddesses, and female scientists. These are the five most common female images in science fiction movies (it seems that they are not common in real life in China, except for the increasing enthusiasm for launching female warrior formations during military parades. I wonder which category Chen Lin, who was given a funeral on the 6th and 7th, belongs to. The madams captured by male heroes in the Henan anti-pornography videos probably don't even qualify as female slaves). But the key question is whether we can transcend these five types of people - this is also what a college student asked later. In short, it's about the issue of new women. Only science fiction can answer this question. No wonder so many people love science fiction, mainly men. This question cannot be clarified at the Great Hall of the People (I am increasingly suspicious about why there are so many red pillars placed in Tiananmen Square). Xia Jia said: What is science fiction? Science fiction is the myth centered around "Western white males." The main body of humanity is Western white males, wielding technological weapons, transforming wastelands, and also transforming flawed women, www.17xinyu.com. This theme is repeatedly expressed in science fiction movies. (Then, what is Chinese science fiction? What is the root cause of the underdevelopment of Chinese science fiction? Xia Jia seems to have already given the answer, making one feel inferior. I am increasingly fond of the female characters depicted by Liu Cixin and Wang Jinkang... Later, Xiao Ji extended the microphone to the noses of the male science fiction writers present, asking them how they viewed women, and everyone answered shyly.)

Xia Jia gave a lecture titled "Female Images in Science Fiction Movies" at the Tsinghua University station of the Science Fiction Grand Lecture on November 8th. To me, she herself looks very much like a female warrior. Walking through the Tsinghua University campus, standing beside those towering buildings filled with masculine features, watching groups of students pass by with high spirits, I felt the strength of our country's scientific and technological power, my heart surging with excitement, thinking, "Western white males, come on!" Below is content excerpted from my listening notes:

1. Female Warrior: A unique "thing" in science fiction movies, wearing shiny breastplates, holding laser guns, constantly fighting. Thus, women are also violent. Muscular, agile, fulfilling the narrative function of men, becoming male comrades-in-arms, or even protagonists. For example, Ripley, Trinity. These images only appeared after the improvement of women's status in recent decades, satisfying the audience's expectations and creators' desires. In "Alien," the alien resembles a penis. The weak Earth humans are the raped females. All the males on the spaceship were killed by rape. The only surviving female, Ripley, takes a defensive stance, overcoming the super-masculine force with strong aggression. She is a woman who does not want to give birth, resisting reproductive desire.

2. Female Slave: The Three Laws of Robotics are a set of tyrannical terms. But why do so many science fiction novels use them? Because they are human-centered, an I/Other structure. Knowing what the Other is allows one to know what the Self is. Seeking the self within the structure. Establishing a master-slave relationship (the relationship between men and women) in imagination. Helen Olay is a household robot, remodeled into a man's ideal wife. This is a man's fantasy; if robots could be made more beautiful than real women, perform better in bed, and never rebel, wouldn't that be great? This is all men's daydreams. Another type of female slave is dressed minimally, chased by monsters. In front of King Kong and Godzilla, they can do nothing but run. During the viewing process, male audiences gain satisfaction, imagining themselves as King Kong and Godzilla.

3. Female Monster: Monsters are fierce! In 1818, Mary Shelley created "Frankenstein." In 1935, the movie "Bride of Frankenstein" was filmed. A bride was created for Frankenstein's monster. The result was a zombie-like image. Completely a monster. This is a story about how much men fear women. The T-X in "Terminator" has greater significance as a female monster, with terrifying appearances, chilling, expressionless faces, displaying men's fears of women in their hearts. White Bone Spirit isn't scary; the White Bone Spirit turning into a beauty to kill people is scary.

4. Goddess: Demonstrates the relationship between science fiction and mythology, religion. On one hand, movies depict rationality and science, while on the other hand, they describe divinity. Scientific and divine elements reach reconciliation. Divinity is often represented by women. In the 2006 movie "Children of Men," all humanity lost its ability to reproduce, facing extinction and a hopeless future, a black woman appears, pregnant, protected by a resistance organization, like Mother Mary, the new hope for humanity. There is a profound intrinsic connection between goddesses and female monsters, both stemming from men's mysterious understanding of women. In "Metropolis," scientists also created a robot like Mother Mary. She is a saving force, destroying large machines. But when workers realized she was just a robot, they tied her to the stake and burned her alive.

5. Female Scientist: Do women have intelligence? There are few female scientists in movies. Susan Calvin in "I, Robot" is a classic image. But she remained a virgin all her life. Intelligence for women is a luxury and also a dangerous product. Intelligent women are non-self-kind. Women are for viewing. Scientists don't need to be women. In "Contact," the female scientist is the first protagonist. This is the most reliable science fiction movie. It satisfies the yearning for science fiction fundamentalism. The film reflects humanity's extraterrestrial complex. This is the attachment to a civilization that has lost its father. After discovering there is no god, people feel lonely. God is not in heaven, satellites have gone up, nor is he on the moon, but somewhere in the universe. The extraterrestrial civilization that the female protagonist encounters is not like ET. She saw a beach, a painting she drew in her childhood, and her father walked towards her. The extraterrestrial civilization approached her with transcendental technology, and the female protagonist shed tears, finding a spiritual pillar-like existence. It is very similar to a pilgrimage process.

Xia Jia talked about "King Kong." In the 1933 version, Kong is an evil figure, metaphorically representing American blacks; in the 2002 version, Kong becomes more humane, a man worth women's lifelong dependence. Women, symbols of civilization, enlighten the gorilla, making him willing to brave fire and water for her. The female slave of '33 became the goddess of '02. "The best man is the one who stands on top of the Empire State Building and helps the woman fight planes," Xia Jia said.

Love science fiction! Science fiction is too interesting!
