Dianping: A Bone the Giants Can't Chew, Living in the Discomfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 16:37:03

CEO of Dianping, Zhang Tao

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping, has once again postponed the company's IPO timeline.

In 2011, Dianping received a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, valuing the company at $1 billion. According to convention, it should have chosen an appropriate time to go public within the next one to two years, but it did not. Instead, in 2012, it carried out another $60 million Series D funding round.

Given Dianping's scale and development trend, going public would not be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that doing so now would be too costly. He breaks down the large internet (PC + mobile) into four elements - people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point offers the opportunity for a giant to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is the seat for the fourth giant. Since 2003, Dianping has been working hard to accumulate data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where smartphones bridge "location" and "services." Following Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping to fill the role of the fourth giant.

However, the outside world does not think this way. In recent months, with frequent acquisitions by BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent), Dianping has become a hypothetical acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition candidate; whether bearing the surname of any of the BAT trio, it could help them achieve great success early on. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it is premature to declare it as a future giant. Moreover, whether it’s Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, all directly target Dianping's territory - local life services focused on dining, drinking, entertainment, etc.

Before proving the following two things, Dianping's IPO would equal a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs some time to prove that what Dianping is currently doing cannot be done by BAT. Second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying. If it secures the top position, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." Regarding speculation and rumors, Zhang Tao pays no heed, stating clearly: "The internet era is indeed entering a phase of great integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes companies that might be acquired into three types. The first type possesses platform attributes but is more suitable to develop within a larger ecosystem, such as maps and emails. These products can easily attract a large number of users but struggle to find independent business models. There are also browsers, which can generate revenue, but the money earned through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to major companies. "Big companies are willing to spend money maintaining an email product because it helps with branding and user engagement, but I've never heard of a standalone email company succeeding." The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, where acquisitions expand market share. The third type includes companies facing financial difficulties who sell themselves for survival.

Now, Meituan has already joined Alibaba's ranks. Theoretically, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid being defeated due to competitors obtaining capital and expanding rapidly. Zhang Tao, however, says, "The giants offer us limited help. We do indeed need users, but execution is more important. Mergers and acquisitions would reduce our execution capability and there's even the risk of being marginalized by a large company." Not only that, he aims to maintain the most precise "distance" between Dianping and the giants. Getting too close to any one of them would be equivalent to "taking sides," making Dianping an enemy to the others.

Thus, logically speaking, acquiring Dianping ends up being a dead knot. Zhang Tao counters, "Why would we take their money? We don't lack funds; what we need are resources. If a giant were willing to hold a small stake while giving us many resources, I would consider it. But would they? Would a giant provide resources if they held only a small percentage?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Tiring Work

In the mobile internet era, opportunities for purely internet-based companies are becoming fewer. New opportunities arise from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that life services mainly revolve around dining, drinking, and entertainment. Other niche markets, such as ride-hailing, can form good business models, but it's hard for them to grow into giants.

For dining, drinking, and entertainment, user needs are simple: information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of life services, which is exactly where Dianping started. In terms of content, member accumulation, and brand recognition, newcomers find it hard to surpass Dianping.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Group buying has now entered the championship battle stage, and Dianping plans to face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market. As long as there are two strong players coexisting, the industry's gross profit margin will be reduced, preventing stable profitability.

Another foreseeable future is reservations. Reservations can create a true online-to-offline loop, but they must be based on Dianping having absolute advantages in both information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop would be difficult. In 2012, Dianping began testing reservations, and Zhang Tao said, "Currently, both users and merchants give positive feedback, but if we only focus on reservations, it would be hard to solve the chicken-or-egg problem."

Of course, life services present some heavier opportunities. For instance, food delivery, which is a rigid demand for users and helps merchants increase sales. In the past couple of years, companies like "To Home Gourmet" have gained significant traction. However, the core of food delivery is distribution—delivering within a short period while handling issues like soup spills—and logistics requirements are even higher than for fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If food delivery is executed well, there is still an opportunity, but this is harder dirty and tiring work, and what Dianping is already doing is enough of both."

If judged by age, Dianping is already an "old" company. Over the past decade, it has diligently accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, which Zhang Tao calls "dirty and tiring work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but the pain of the past has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the life services sector.

Nevertheless, he must remain vigilant against BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. With over 100 million users, Baidu Maps allows users to search for nearby dining and other life services. Tencent, deemed to have secured its ticket to mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and extending its offerings to services is a natural progression, especially since it has incorporated Meituan and AutoNavi into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there hasn't been a noticeable explosive growth in reality or data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial—it comes down to which product users turn to first when they want to eat, drink, or entertain themselves.

Besides, in the vast field of life services, competition among giants is bound to overlap, and no one can completely monopolize the sector. Just like information—while Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, information can also be obtained from Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that this makes Dianping safer instead. "In segmented areas like car rental and business travel, the massive user base directed by giants could easily flood and overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in AutoNavi, invading Dianping's territory isn't that simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can always be categorized regardless of how peculiar they are. A user's experience of a meal depends on various factors like the day's environment, waitstaff, and chefs. Managing merchants can't be done using systems and tools like Alibaba's; it requires establishing a 'ground force' managed by people.

"All three giants want to walk this path, and there is room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing life services necessitates ground teams deployed at various levels, and BAT lacks the genes and capabilities to manage offline teams. Second, they lack the willingness; life services require dedicated effort, not just sending a small team, and generating profits is slow. "A company finds it hard to excel at two critical tasks simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate its advantages in its original domain."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Turn Easily

In 2011, when Dianping secured $100 million in financing, Zhang Tao told the media that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, the world changes fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogies had fallen from grace.

He wasn’t overly worried about this. "They didn’t become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they want to be America's Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang Tao has painstakingly mapped out step by step. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was too outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, like the Americans, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform—one deal per day—but as orders increased, he realized that group buying possessed transactional attributes. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a life services trading platform, and the two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used to transition Dianping from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this transformation, Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth seat among the giants. But this transformation, though seemingly simple, was akin to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process filled with immense pain.

Firstly, compared to solely selling ads, the gross margin of a trading platform decreases, demanding stricter cost control and efficiency. Secondly, a trading platform requires stronger cross-department communication skills.

In the past, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content team and sales team were entirely separate. Zhang Tao even deliberately established a "firewall" between departments—no matter how important the client, information on the platform couldn’t be altered for sales purposes, which once led to mutual hostility between the content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication to craft effective marketing strategies. Besides planning, editorial support is also necessary. He said, "Maintaining the quality of information remains crucial, but there's no need to reinforce enmity anymore; we need to dismantle the firewall."

Though editors may logically accept this, individual behaviors and values have inertia, and collectively, the entire company’s inertia is even greater. To address this, Zhang Tao specifically convened several meetings to encourage more communication between departments. After a few months, an editor responsible for content casually told a sales manager, "I used to see you as an enemy, but now I see you as a colleague."

The sales department also needed transformation. To promote conversion rates, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team a dual identity. First, from the perspective of merchants, salespeople should transform into marketing consultants—not just selling products but helping merchants devise marketing strategies. Second, from the company's perspective, marketing consultants are the company's buyers. Dianping is like a supermarket—not only must the quality of the products be good, but the variety must also be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products accordingly. Of course, this dual identity inevitably leads to a series of complex issues. For frontline personnel, this is quite challenging—they must balance the scales themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual identity" resembles Alibaba's division into 25 business units. By decentralizing decision-making to the frontlines, the company gains faster response speeds but increases management difficulty. Similarly, compared to frontline employees, the management team suffers more after Dianping's transformation.

Each year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, which are then further broken down by executives layer by layer. However, this breakdown is not a simple arithmetic operation. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping and head of the sales team, sometimes finds that unmet merchant demands stem from sales issues but affect overall performance. Hence, Li Jing created a new position—communication specialists—who daily aggregate information from marketing consultants and submit it to other departments while tracking feedback. He often tells his team, "Being good at embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change, and everyone feels uncomfortable when encountering change. All employees must undergo discomfort zones, including me."

Numbers reflect the weight of Dianping's execution team. Out of more than 3,000 employees, over 2,000 work offline. The ground forces are responsible for direct communication with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group-buying packages, and combined marketing strategies.

Dianping has a unique management evaluation system for this core team that resists BAT's influence.

They are not called "salespeople;" from day one, their business cards read "marketing strategy consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the mindset of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging at least three to five years behind Taobao merchants in their understanding of the internet. Marketing strategy consultants must tell merchants what online users need and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing consultants wouldn’t recommend a group-buying package for abalone or shark fins, possibly suggesting other dishes, because online users and offline users have different demands."

An outstanding marketing consultant must be a versatile player. They need to explain to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the fundamental differences between discounts and group-buying packages and determine which discount better fits their positioning. If dealing with a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing consultant must know whether it's the off-season or peak season, which products' costs rise during certain seasons, and which decrease, proposing a group-buying menu that both merchants and users prefer.

This places high demands on marketing consultants, requiring them to be not only internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, it's impossible for every consultant to be a double expert, so we rank consultants by stars." Similar to hotels, marketing consultants range from one-star to five-stars, with the best ones called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping has established a knowledge base for marketing consultants, containing knowledge points and related cases that both new and experienced employees can browse. If encountering a new issue, employees can ask questions within the knowledge base, and thousands of consulting advisors nationwide will assist in answering.

A large amount of knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experiences. Dianping has set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, where employees who help others the most each quarter receive rewards. Every quarter, Dianping also conducts skill assessments for employees, with results directly linked to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to obtain compared to internet knowledge. Some experiences are accumulated by traditional merchants after decades of practice in their respective fields. A Super Star marketing consultant told Li Jing that hair salons should avoid offering shampoo-cut-blow-dry group-buying packages because men lack motivation to buy them, and women mostly blow dry their hair without needing all three services together. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing consultants are not only divided by cities but also by industries such as dining, hair care, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, dining is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing consultant responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing consultants can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, ensuring merchants won't reject the sale of products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what challenges a particular type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, what solutions the company's products offer, and how effective they are. Outstanding cases are reported upwards, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Winning Entrepreneurs." In the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants share their experiences. If responsible for a hot pot restaurant, after three months, they must submit a business plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees defend their presentations on-site, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, rewarding them appropriately.

Strategically speaking, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, their objectives are quite similar. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with basic industry knowledge, and their spoken ideas sound alike. "The era where storytelling and concept creation could lead to success is over. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao stated.
