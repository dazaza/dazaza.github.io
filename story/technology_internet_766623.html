Facebook's Sandberg: Praising Ambition, A Mixed Reception

by anonymous on 2013-09-24 21:00:17

Sheryl Sandberg, the Chief Operating Officer of Facebook, has been very busy, and her recent visit to China was no exception.

Although Facebook has stated that Sandberg's visit to China is to promote her best-selling book "Lean In," her first stop in China was a visit to Cai Mingzhao, the Director of the State Council Information Office. Despite Facebook not yet being available in China, discussions between Sandberg and Cai focused on the important role of the Facebook platform in helping Chinese enterprises expand into overseas markets and potential areas of cooperation. Industry insiders speculate that Facebook may be attempting to enter the Chinese market.

After attending two exchange activities in the afternoon and evening, Sandberg appeared the next day at the 2013 Summer Davos Forum.

Of course, during this trip to China, female leadership remained the topic Sandberg discussed most frequently.

Praising Ambition: The High-Level Female Executive

On the evening of September 10th, dressed in a blue dress, Sheryl Sandberg appeared in Beijing for an event hosted by CITIC Publishing House, Xin Rui New Media, and the Cheung Kong Graduate School of Business titled "Sheryl Sandberg's China Tour: CKGSB Grand Lecture Hall and CITIC Xin Rui New Media Association Launch Ceremony." She engaged in discussions with China's new generation of internet dreamers about female leadership and the future prospects of internet innovation.

Smiling constantly and in excellent spirits, Sandberg liked to walk around while giving speeches, using many gestures and directly engaging in dialogue with the audience.

"People like to use the term 'very dominant' when describing women, implying that women cannot lead. So I want to say that if you are very dominant and you are a woman, please raise your hand." Sandberg encouraged the female audience members to raise their hands and informed them that this mindset exists worldwide.

Sandberg paused for a few seconds before emphasizing, "It's time to change these prejudices."

Despite the uncertain prospects of whether ordinary women around the world will respond to the top-down revolution initiated by this Harvard-educated billionaire worth hundreds of millions of dollars, Sandberg's confidence during her speech that night was evident. "We need to understand these prejudices and refuse them to change them," she said.

However, this sharp yet gentle speech perfectly aligned with the tastes of Sandberg's admirers in the audience. Most of the listeners were female students from the Cheung Kong Graduate School of Business, who interrupted Sandberg's speech with gentle applause as the backbone of their enterprises.

Sheryl Sandberg's resume is undoubtedly impressive.

Ranked fifth on the Forbes list of the world's most powerful women, she has been hailed by American media as "the most influential woman in Silicon Valley." While working in government, she served as the Chief of Staff to Lawrence Summers, then Deputy Secretary of the U.S. Treasury; in business, she helped two of America's greatest internet companies, Google and Facebook, achieve profitability.

However, despite her numerous accolades as the number two at Facebook, Sandberg has not had a smooth ride in her career. In fact, as a woman, even Sandberg cannot avoid the existence of workplace biases.

Talking about her work experience at Facebook, she admitted that many people have opinions about their team, thinking that female bosses are too domineering. One male employee once complained that working under a woman often meant dealing with someone who was overly ambitious.

But Sandberg does not see this as something to fear or feel uneasy about. Smiling proudly, she said, "If someone tells you that you're too ambitious, you should thank them for their compliment. If someone tells a little girl that she has leadership potential, you should congratulate that little girl because she indeed has the makings of a leader."

In fact, Sandberg's current work performance is easily interpreted as "ambitious."

After Facebook's successful IPO, Sandberg was not referred to by the media as "the woman behind a successful man," but rather as the equally indispensable "First Lady of Facebook."

Moreover, within Facebook, there is an image of "the woman managing external affairs and the man handling internal ones." As the founder, Mark Zuckerberg's expertise mainly lies in technology, whereas Sandberg is a genius in marketing and management. It is precisely under her leadership that Facebook's user numbers surged dramatically, and operating profits increased significantly, bringing immediate results to this social networking site.

However, Sandberg has good reason to be assertive. According to public data, in 2008, Facebook's advertising revenue was less than $3 billion, but by 2011, this figure had exceeded $30 billion. Revenue increased ninefold in three years, and Sandberg created a sales miracle. Without Sandberg, Facebook's growth would definitely not have been so rapid. For this reason, Silicon Valley and internally within Facebook even privately refer to Sandberg as the "Queen Behind the Throne" of Facebook.

No wonder Zuckerberg once remarked, "Facebook without Sheryl Sandberg is incomplete."

Not Being "The Woman Behind a Successful Man"

In fact, besides the inherent gender biases in the workplace, many female executives must also face role positioning in marriage and family. It seems that everywhere in the world, the ideal of a very happy marriage is a successful man with a virtuous wife and caring mother behind him.

However, the ambitious Sandberg in the workplace would not willingly become the virtuous wife and caring mother behind a successful man in her marriage.

Public information shows that Sandberg's husband, Dave Goldberg, is the CEO of the tech company SurveyMonkey. As a wife, Sandberg is busy managing Facebook and plans to invest more time in her new ventures.

However, due to her success in the workplace, many people worry about her husband's status when they talk to Sandberg. They carefully inquire about her husband, assuming he must be unhappy because she is too successful, overshadowing his achievements.

Regarding this point, Sandberg chose to express her dissatisfaction with a series of rhetorical questions: "Is it inevitable that a man who marries a very successful professional woman will be unhappy? Why must the husband in a marital relationship always be the one playing the role of professional success?"

In her view, over time, more men, perhaps even 50% of them, will believe that it is entirely possible for their wives to earn as much as or even more than themselves.

However, her persistence in marriage is somewhat related to her personal character. At her wedding, her brother and sister told her husband, "We're not Sheryl Sandberg's younger siblings; we're her first and second employees," jokingly complaining that Sandberg has always liked to give orders and organize and control things.

But as a mother of two children, even the strong-willed Sandberg sometimes lowers her guard voluntarily, and the reason she wrote "Lean In" is because of her daughter.

When her daughter was four years old, Sandberg chose to tell her the story of U.S. presidents on Presidents' Day. At that time, her daughter asked a question: why are all these presidents boys?

This simple question from her daughter touched Sandberg deeply, "I think if my daughter sees such a world, it's not a beautiful world, and this message isn't a good one. So I hope the world can change and provide a better environment for the growth of our children."

"Mixed Blessings" Feminist

Today, Sandberg has achieved great success in the business world and has become a billionaire in terms of wealth.

American Democratic Senator Claire McCaskill once said: "The young Sandberg will achieve equally extraordinary accomplishments in politics. But currently, she is making her own outstanding contributions to women's rights in her personal life." Clearly, this is the second battlefield of the "Queen Behind the Throne" of Facebook.

Sandberg hopes that women can unite and participate in self-awareness-raising activities. In her book, Sandberg calls on women to recognize the current situation, where women are judged more strictly than men and receive lower wages. She also advocates against the practice of many companies delaying promotions for female employees nearing childbirth, equal sharing of household chores among husbands and wives, and writing short-term and long-term career plans for women.

To this end, Sandberg also established a foundation called Leanin.org. Around the world, various women can join this network, which provides them with support.

According to members of the project initiation committee and publicity team, Sandberg's project "launch partners" include companies like American Express, Google, Sony, Johnson & Johnson, and several media companies. The majority of these companies are required to authorize their logos for use by the Lean In project and distribute promotional materials to their employees. In return, these companies will receive official recognition for supporting the Lean In project.

However, these series of activities have also brought some criticism to Sandberg.

Some voices accuse Sandberg of placing too much responsibility on already struggling professional women while failing to make sufficient demands on governments and employers, such as providing better childcare environments, more flexible work arrangements, and other tangible improvements.

"Sandberg's mistake is similar to that of many successful women in the past: blaming other women's lack of success on their insufficient efforts. All males resisting the feminist movement in the future will cite Sandberg's words, claiming that women must be more ambitious," said Avivah Wittenberg-Cox, a consultant specializing in helping businesses improve gender balance.

In a recent popular online post titled "16 Tragedies of Working at Facebook," when discussing Facebook founder Mark Zuckerberg and COO Sheryl Sandberg, an employee mentioned that they spent a lot of time on activities unrelated to the company, such as Sandberg's new book "Lean In."
