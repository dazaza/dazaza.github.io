Nine out of ten kindergarten teachers in Qingdao are not formal public employees, with a monthly salary of only hundreds of yuan and no housing provident fund

by sgba1jm1nf on 2012-03-01 15:37:50

According to statistics from the Qingdao Education Bureau, by 2010, among more than 2000 kindergartens in the city, there were about 2000 public kindergarten teachers, accounting for only 10% of the total number of kindergarten teachers.

Because of the lack of a public identity, these kindergarten teachers have long been outside the system, and their treatment and status cannot compare with those of public teachers.

Improving the quality of preschool education and changing the current situation of severe talent loss, breaking the [same work but different pay] has become imminent.

Recently, the Liuting Subdistrict Office of Chengyang District made a deposit of housing provident fund for 230 non-public identity kindergarten teachers within its jurisdiction, which was praised as an innovative move. However, an embarrassing situation occurred during the deposit: because in the category of housing provident fund identity, non-public identity kindergarten teachers could not be classified under [public institution] like public teachers, and were eventually categorized under [urban contract workers].

[If we say they are teachers, but they have not enjoyed the treatment of teachers; if we say they are not teachers, but they engage in the same profession as teachers every day, even working longer hours, more tiring, and with greater responsibility.] A heartfelt statement by Li Baomei, director of the Chengyang District Childcare Office, evokes sympathy.

Not an isolated case

Public vs non-public kindergarten teacher ratio 1:9

Li Jie is the principal of the Love Kindergarten in the Liuting Subdistrict Office of Chengyang District. She will reach retirement age in two years. But after more than 25 years in early childhood education, she still does not have a public identity, and it is precisely this identity that causes her monthly income at retirement to be only around 600 yuan despite the subdistrict office having paid and supplemented her old-age and other social insurances.

Among nearly 20,000 kindergarten teachers in Qingdao, Li Jie is not an isolated case, but rather represents the majority. Data provided by the Education Bureau shows that by 2010, among more than 2000 kindergartens in the city, there were approximately 2000 public kindergarten teachers, accounting for only 10% of the total number of kindergarten teachers. This phenomenon is more prominent in the three districts and five cities, for example, among 1600 kindergarten teachers in Chengyang District, only 73 are public kindergarten teachers.

The seemingly unremarkable identity of kindergarten teachers brings about how large a gap in terms of salary and benefits? According to the results of the journalist's investigation, it can be described as [a huge disparity] without exaggeration—

First, let's talk about salaries. Although some kindergartens try to eliminate the wage gap between public and non-public teachers within the kindergarten, quite a number of kindergartens have the phenomenon of unequal pay for equal work due to differences in teacher identities, especially the wage gap between urban and rural kindergarten teachers is larger. It is understood that the average monthly salary for public kindergarten teachers in Qingdao is around 2600 yuan, while the average monthly salary for those without public identity is less than 1000 yuan, and some economically underdeveloped areas' rural kindergarten teachers earn only a few hundred yuan per month.

Next, let's discuss social insurance and provident fund. Currently, the payment of social insurance for non-public kindergarten teachers has been basically completed, but due to their low wages, the contribution ratio is much lower compared to public teachers who do the same work. As for the payment of housing provident funds, it currently belongs to a gray area, and the Liuting Subdistrict Office being the first in the city to make deposits of housing provident funds for non-public identity kindergarten teachers in its jurisdiction indicates that currently, out of the 17,000 non-public identity kindergarten teachers in the city, none have housing provident funds and thus cannot enjoy the preferential interest rates brought by provident funds for home loans.

More worrying is the treatment of non-public identity kindergarten teachers after retirement. Due to identity differences, public kindergarten teachers receive over 3000 yuan per month after retirement, while non-public kindergarten teachers can only hope for over a thousand yuan in monthly retirement pensions. Especially for non-public kindergarten teachers around 40 years old, the issue of treatment is more severe, because of their age, the time spent paying social insurance is short, making their post-retirement income not optimistic.

Not accidental

Long-standing debts in preschool education

What are the reasons that keep people like Li Jie outside the educational system for a long time? In our interviews, we learned that there are deeper historical roots, roughly three reasons:

Firstly, kindergartens have never implemented the corresponding national educational staffing standards. In 2001, when the central and provincial governments set new standards for the staffing of primary and secondary school teachers, the group of kindergarten teachers was not involved. Because the cost of hiring a public teacher is much higher than that of a non-public teacher, relevant functional departments strictly control the number of public kindergarten teachers, causing the situation where, for nearly ten years, kindergarten staffing has only decreased and not increased. Some original public kindergarten teacher staffing gaps have become increasingly larger, leading to the temporary hiring of teachers and self-solving of teacher salaries and benefits; newly established kindergartens, especially township center kindergartens and village-run kindergartens, are basically in a state without staffing. According to incomplete statistics, the proportion of public teachers with staffing among existing full-time kindergarten teachers in China is relatively small, and currently, public kindergarten teachers in rural kindergartens in Qingdao occupy the staffing quotas of primary school teachers.

Secondly, kindergartens have long lacked investment, especially in rural kindergartens where development is difficult. Currently, most kindergartens in Qingdao rely on childcare fees for operating expenses. At the same time, various kindergartens, due to insufficient funds, are unable to fundamentally improve the conditions of the kindergarten and are even less able to effectively raise teacher salaries. Rural kindergartens are mostly collectively run, with town (subdistrict) finances responsible for overall coordination. If the local collective economy is tight, naturally there will be no extra financial resources for developing preschool education. Since the economic capacity of parents in rural kindergartens is limited, raising the fee standard would lead to a vicious cycle of reduced enrollment rates. In other words, limited fiscal input and low childcare fees make the development of rural kindergartens very difficult, and the problem of kindergarten teacher salaries also cannot be guaranteed.

Thirdly, preschool education is currently the only educational system without an independent professional title promotion series. The professional title advancement of kindergarten teachers follows the primary school series. A large number of private kindergarten teachers and non-staff teachers in public kindergartens have no path for professional title promotion. Nowadays, young teachers highly value opportunities for professional development, and the lack of development space leads some young teachers to leave angrily. Thus, hiring teaching staff in the form of contract workers in kindergartens has become an unfortunate norm.

Simultaneous progress

Hardware, faculty, and benefits improved together

The current situation and predicament of non-public identity kindergarten teachers weaken their work enthusiasm and professional passion, leading to an increasingly serious phenomenon of talent loss. A most practical issue is that to improve the quality of kindergarten teachers and attract more outstanding talents, it is necessary to ensure that this profession has a high social status and economic income.

Sun Yujie, Director of the Early Childhood Education Department of Qingdao University's Normal College, believes that the unclear definition of non-public kindergarten teacher identity has led to problems such as unequal pay for equal work and significant disparities in treatment among the current preschool education teacher force, which is the root cause of the current talent loss in preschool education. [Improving the quality of preschool education requires policy support and increased funding, but the faculty is even more critical, and it should start with improving the treatment of all kindergarten teachers.] Sun Yujie said.

Fortunately, preschool education in Qingdao is receiving increasing attention. In May last year, the municipal government issued the "Opinions on Accelerating the Reform and Development of Preschool Education," clearly requiring the improvement of the treatment and status of non-public identity kindergarten teachers. [Before 2012, the salary of non-public rural kindergarten teachers should reach more than 1.8 times the per capita net income of farmers in the previous year locally, and the wages, insurance, and other benefits of non-public kindergarten teachers in rural town (subdistrict) center kindergartens and village (community) collectively run kindergartens should be included in the district (city), town (subdistrict) fiscal budget at a proportion of no less than 70%, coordinated and distributed by the district (city) government.]

In 2012, [increasing the investment in preschool education] has been listed as one of Qingdao's key tasks, namely [establishing a per capita fiscal subsidy system for public and inclusive private kindergartens, providing subsidies for newly built and renovated public kindergartens in the five cities.]

Recently, our province took the lead nationwide in issuing the "Staffing Standards for Public Kindergartens", clearly stipulating that the ratio of staff to children in public kindergartens is 1:6-8 for full-time ordinary kindergartens and 1:5-6 for boarding kindergartens. It also requires that the finance department strictly allocate the relevant expenses of kindergartens according to the personnel staffing approved by the institutional compilation department. Currently, relevant departments such as the Education Bureau and the Institutional Compilation Office are conducting special research, indicating that the group of public identity kindergarten teachers in Qingdao will further expand.

Experts suggest that although the identity-based management of kindergarten teachers is prevalent nationwide, to build a high-quality, dynamic team of kindergarten teachers, it is necessary to explore policies and measures that can truly achieve industry management of kindergarten teachers, hoping that Qingdao can take the lead in breaking through in this field.
