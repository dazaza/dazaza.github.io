Synopsis:

A classic in the genre of business warfare films, this movie is also a mainstream piece that unapologetically defends human greed during an era dominated by the pursuit of money. Set against the backdrop of Wall Street, the global financial center, the film depicts the unscrupulous stock market tycoon Gordon Gekko, who manipulates stock prices behind the scenes using any means necessary due to his insatiable greed. However, he ultimately meets his downfall at the hands of a young broker with a conscience. Oliver Stone, the son of a stockbroker, uses his signature dramatic style to expose the insider trading secrets of the stock market while questioning the moral issues of modern people selling their souls under the allure of money through Gekko's actions. Michael Douglas delivers a compelling performance, earning him the Best Actor Oscar. Martin Sheen and Charlie Sheen also deliver outstanding performances as father and son.

This film, directed by Oliver Stone after his anti-war themed "Platoon," focuses on the perspective of a rookie white-collar stock trader to criticize and expose the greed and sins within the capitalist economic system. The timing of the film was clearly chosen strategically, appearing when Wall Street was particularly vulnerable to attack following the U.S. financial market's bond scandal.

The film points out that most small and medium investors are easily deceived. Market killers like Gordon swoop down like birds of prey, mercilessly taking entire companies right under the noses of shareholders. Their actions are morally corrupt and illegal, but they often justify their behavior with excuses such as "No one will get hurt," "Everyone does it," "There's something in it for everyone," or "Who would know besides us?"

The movie follows a traditional plot structure: a hungry young man yearns to approach his predecessor for success, only to be seduced and deceived, eventually attempting to turn the tables. The actual details may not matter much. Few characters on the big screen in the '80s were as ruthless and convincing as Gordon. If the film has any flaw, it might be the portrayal of Charlie Sheen's character, who doesn't seem greedy or ruthless enough to convincingly have the courage to bring down Gordon.

Oliver Stone's greatest success with this film lies in his widely acknowledged ability to portray the cutthroat nature of the financial markets in a complex yet believable manner, making it highly meaningful. Anyone can be drawn in because the details of stock manipulation and insider information leaks are depicted realistically. Most of the time, we know what's coming next, and throughout the film, we understand why everything happens.

Although Gekko's illegal actions oppose most of Wall Street, his broader values will be endorsed — legal scams that bring money will be pursued. Big financier Donald Trump, who could be nominated as a general candidate, once stated in his autobiography: simply put, money doesn't attract him much anymore; his motivation is more about participating in the challenge and becoming a winner. His honesty is commendable. However, the key caution lies in the dual nature of this value system — focusing on money and winning without mentioning any creation of goods and services, manufacturing products, inventing devices, or building factories related to real wealth creation.

The true purpose of this film isn't actually to criticize the crimes of Wall Street. Oliver Stone attacks the value system that places the pursuit of profit and wealth above everything else. His film portrays the brutal atmosphere of the financial world, where morality is disregarded in favor of money above all else. The resulting laws should merely be part of the punishment.
