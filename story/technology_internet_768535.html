Touchscreen Office is of great significance to Microsoft, pricing strategy needs to change

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 19:15:30

　　Major Test

　　In the eyes of analysts, the soon-to-be-released touch-screen version of the Office suite is a major test for Microsoft.

　　"There is indeed no product in the mobile field that can match Office in the desktop field," Reticle Research analyst Ross Rubin said in an interview last week, "just retaining most functions poses a huge challenge."

　　Microsoft has previously stated that it will launch a touch-screen version of the Office app, including Word, Excel, PowerPoint, and Outlook, but has not disclosed the specific appearance of these apps or a timeline. However, there are reports that these applications will be launched in the first half of 2014.

　　The most detailed statement from Qi Lu, head of Microsoft's Applications and Services Group, was: "We are developing touch-screen versions of the core applications in the Office suite, including Outlook, Word, Excel, and PowerPoint. We will introduce these applications to Windows devices and other devices at the appropriate time."

　　Outgoing Microsoft CEO Steve Ballmer promised that the touch-screen version of Office would first land on the Windows 8.1 platform before gradually expanding to other platforms like the iPad.

　　The exact timing may still be a secret, but as Rubin said, the challenges faced by Microsoft are clearly significant.

　　Key Decisions

　　To develop a touch-screen version of Office that consumers are willing to accept, the key lies in making trade-offs in functionality: is it to develop a substitute for the powerful desktop Office or just target casual use and casual users?

　　IDC analyst Al Hilwa also agrees with Rubin's statement: "Introducing the high-end features of Office into touch-screen applications poses a great challenge. I think the focus should still be on simple editing functions."

　　Mobile applications usually have fewer functions than desktop applications. Microsoft can choose between two options: either remove most functions or design an entirely new user interface that hides most functions on the initial interface but still allows users to access the tools they need through some method.

　　Office's Ribbon interface might help hide some complex content in the touch-screen application. "However, compared to the changes required for the mobile version of Office, the Ribbon used in Office 2007 can only be considered child's play," Rubin warned.

　　Analysts do not doubt Microsoft's capabilities, but they want to know the company's specific plan and the actual reaction of users. Since Microsoft is crucial to the entire business sector and will have a significant impact on its profits, this is truly a case of one move affecting the whole situation.

　　Hilwa believes that even without significant price pressure from mobile applications, the streamlined version of Office cannot be priced the same as the fully-featured desktop version. Paid applications seem to be declining, being replaced by the "free-to-premium" (freemium) model, which offers free app downloads but still generates revenue through in-app purchases.

　　Currently, Office already supports non-Windows platforms, and the Office 365 subscription plan charges consumers $100 per year and businesses $150 to $264 per person per year. The purpose of this strategy is to enhance the appeal of Office 365 by supporting mobile devices.

　　But Rubin believes that the Office 365 model won't work, and Microsoft must sell Office in a different way.

　　He said, "I think Microsoft will eventually have to release native Office applications, possibly offering free or ultra-low-cost applications and charging for additional content." Rubin believes that the price of the application should not exceed $60, less than half the price of the entry-level Office 2013 Home and Student Edition, and slightly higher than the six-month subscription fee for the Office 365 Home Premium Edition. However, he thinks that even this price might be too high.

　　Other analysts hold different views. J. Gold Associates analyst Jack Gold said, "Microsoft will invest in those who are willing to pay for Office features and guide everyone towards the online version."

　　Future Strategy

　　Microsoft currently provides free web-based versions of Word, Excel, and PowerPoint, serving as the foundation for the lowest-priced package of Office 365, which also provides hosted email and other internet services.

　　The touch-screen version of Office faces competition from Apple and Google, both of which have released free Office alternatives - iWork and Quickoffice. Although these software programs cannot yet compete with the current desktop version of Office, they could pose a threat to Microsoft by competing against the feature-limited touch-screen version of Office.

　　This will have a significant impact on Microsoft, as Office contributes billions of dollars in revenue each quarter - $6 billion in the most recent quarter, accounting for 32% - and with the decline of PCs and the rise of tablets, the touch-screen version of Office will become increasingly important for Microsoft's future.

　　Rubin believes that Microsoft can emulate Google's latest strategy for QuickOffice, directly embedding Quickoffice into the latest 'KitKat' system. "Without a touch-screen version of Office, it will undoubtedly reduce the appeal of Windows tablets," he said.

　　"But Microsoft still has great growth potential," Rubin continued, "if they can make the touch-screen Office on Windows a powerful productivity suite, they can gain a huge advantage. It all depends on Microsoft's execution."
