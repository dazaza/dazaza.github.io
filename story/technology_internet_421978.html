To start with, Yesmywine would like to introduce: China's red wine culture.

Yesmywine has found that when it comes to China's red wine culture, people often think of the famous line "grape wine in a jade goblet under the moonlight." According to historical records, China began cultivating grapes and producing red wine during the Han Dynasty (206 BC). At that time, the country had already learned grape cultivation and winemaking techniques from neighboring countries. The earliest recorded mention of red wine was by Sima Qian in his "Records of the Grand Historian," along with other historical documents detailing the planting, operation, leasing, and trading of vineyards and red wine in the Turpan region during the 4th-8th centuries AD.

However, the technology introduced during the Han Dynasty did not gain widespread application or dissemination, so red wine remained scarce in Central Plains regions, only appearing as tribute from remote areas. Similar to other luxury goods at the time, red wine was also a symbol of status, and only the nobility or wealthy families could taste red wine from the Western Regions.

The rise of red wine culture in China occurred during the Wei-Jin and subsequent Southern and Northern Dynasties periods. As production and consumption of red wine recovered and developed, literature and poetry from the time show that red wine became a common drink at banquets attended by princes, ministers, and social elites, marking the beginning of red wine culture.

In the Tang Dynasty, many well-known poets wrote poems about red wine, indicating its high popularity. In Chang'an, Hu merchants even opened shops selling red wine from the Western Regions.

In modern times, red wine has clearly lost its aristocratic mark, with bottles ranging from tens to hundreds of yuan readily entering the lives of ordinary people.

Yesmywine discovered that the origins of red wine date back to 6000 BC in ancient Persia, approximately in the Fertile Crescent (modern-day Iraq) region between the Nile River and the Persian Gulf. This vast agricultural area saw the initial cultivation of grapes and the brewing of red wine. This is where the grapevines used for winemaking first flourished.

As city-states emerged from primitive agricultural tribes, ambitious ancient Phoenician (modern-day Syrian) people spread grapevines and winemaking knowledge throughout the Mediterranean, reaching the entire European continent.

The development of the wine industry has always been closely related to the social context of the time, with rulers' policies directly affecting the growth of red wine culture. An open and developed economy, along with institutional systems, allowed the planting and brewing of wine in various regions to reach unprecedented levels of mutual cooperation and development.

For example, as the ancient Roman Empire conquered the European continent, it also promoted grape cultivation and red wine brewing. Around the 1st century AD, the conquest of Gaul (modern-day France) marked the origin of French red wine, with the initial grape plantations located in the southern Rhone Valley of France, spreading to the Bordeaux region by the 2nd century.

From 768 to 814 AD, Emperor Charlemagne ruled the Western Roman Empire and envisioned vineyards stretching from southern France to northern Germany, once owning the famous Corton-Charlemagne Grand Cru vineyard in Burgundy. Queen Elizabeth I’s maritime trade brought red wine from many European wine-producing countries to Britain. Thus, red wine reached more corners of the world and became known to more and more people.

Initially, red wine was a symbol of status, a fine drink accessible only to the upper echelons of society. Gradually, with the widespread cultivation and advancement in winemaking techniques, red wine gained a more common touch, no longer being the exclusive enjoyment of the nobility.

There is an interesting legend about the origin of red wine involving a grape-loving king and a neglected concubine. The king stored grapes but forgot about them, while the neglected concubine, attempting suicide, mistakenly drank fermented grape juice thinking it was poison. Not only did she survive, but she became even more beautiful. Naturally, this led to a happy reunion where the concubine regained favor, and thus red wine was born, gaining appreciation from people.

Whether the legend is true or not, red wine has been associated with beauty, status, and taste since its inception.
