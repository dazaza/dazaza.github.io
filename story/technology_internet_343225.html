Methods to correct bad eating habits in babies

by ys78ujhy on 2010-04-10 20:33:25

Not long ago, the results of a "Survey on Shanghai Children's Eating Behavior" jointly conducted by the Child Health Group of the Shanghai Pediatrics Association, the Shanghai Nutrition Society, the School of Public Health of Shanghai Jiaotong University, and Abbott Company found that picky eating and other unhealthy eating habits are troubling many families in Shanghai. 68.2% of parents expressed concern about this issue.

Scene 1: Despite grandparents or caregivers trailing behind with a bowl of food, or parents resorting to coercion or inducement, the baby continues to play on its own or frowns and spits out the only piece of vegetable leaf in its mouth.

Scene 2: During meals, the captivating images on the television screen firmly capture the child's attention, or a pile of cute toys keeps the baby busy playing. These rich visual and manual "activities" make the simple task of eating abnormally "arduous."

Scene 3: Worried that the baby is not eating enough and will go hungry, the mother supplements the baby with large amounts of snacks. The excessive intake of sweets leaves the baby with no appetite for meals.

Professor Jin Xingming from the Shanghai Children's Medical Center of Jiaotong University analyzed: The above phenomena are common scenes in many households. This "Survey on Shanghai Children's Eating Behavior" was conducted through face-to-face inquiries by community pediatric health doctors. The research group randomly surveyed 1,244 children aged 1-6 years old who were permanent residents of the city. The results showed: Among Shanghai children aged 1-6, 39.7% have the habit of being picky eaters, and this problem becomes increasingly prominent with age, with nearly half of toddlers aged 5-6 being picky eaters.

In response to some unhealthy eating behaviors in children, over 80% of parents use the method of "inducement" to solve issues of picky and selective eating, such as watching TV while eating, telling stories during meals, using toys or sweets as rewards, etc. Meanwhile, 12.1% of parents even resort to forcing their children to eat.

In fact, these measures are all wrong. The more they are used, the more likely it is for children to form a conditioned reflex: that eating requires watching TV, and after eating, there will be rewards. Over time, not only will these methods fail to fundamentally resolve existing unhealthy eating behaviors in young children, but they may also exacerbate picky and selective eating. Therefore, it is important to interrupt these incorrect and unhealthy associations. Of course, to ensure balanced nutrition for children, supplements like Nutren Junior can be added.

Western families typically follow the 3W principle when feeding their children: what to eat, when to eat, and where to eat, which are decided by the parents. However, how much to eat is left to the child's discretion, making the child willing to participate. In contrast, the Chinese approach often involves decisions made solely by the parents, leaving the child no choice but to comply. Therefore, correcting unhealthy eating behaviors in children, helping them develop good eating habits, and enhancing the scientific knowledge of feeding infants are very important.

Reminder:

Provide an appropriate amount of food

Parents should provide food suitable for their baby's age, neither too much nor too little. When feeding, pay attention to signals the baby sends. If the baby turns his head away when the spoon is placed near his mouth, it indicates he may already be full. Typically, babies start using this signal at a very young age. For instance, a baby around 7 months old might need about three large spoonfuls of food during lunch. If you give him more, he will turn his head away and refuse to eat.

Reminder:

Gradually add new foods

When introducing new foods to your baby, do not rush the process; instead, add them gradually. You might change one new vegetable every five days in your baby's diet. Even if the baby dislikes it and refuses to eat it, continue for 2-3 days. If he still dislikes the vegetable, change the menu, but reintroduce it in another menu next week.

Reminder:

Each meal should be on time

The main meal times each day should be spaced 3-4 hours apart, with fixed eating times. Each meal should last no less than 25 minutes. Regular meal times help the baby form good eating habits.

Reminder:

Let the baby focus on eating

Do not let the baby become distracted while eating, such as watching TV or playing with toys. If you want the baby to feel the joy of eating, you can give him a spoon, and gradually he will immerse himself in the happiness of eating on his own. Secondly, when feeding the baby, do not emphasize speed; fast feeding might lead to binge eating.

Reminder:

Parents should set an example

When feeding the baby, parents should not do anything unrelated and should look into the baby's eyes. If the parents lose focus or mechanically place food from the spoon into the baby's mouth, the baby will feel that eating is not enjoyable. Therefore, parents should set an example of focused eating.
