Half of all mobile phone water damage incidents occur in the toilet.

by lisa998ads on 2012-02-25 12:17:45

Mobile phones get wet - half of them happen in the toilet

Are you used to scrolling through Weibo on your iPhone while sitting on the toilet in the morning? Friends with this habit should take note. According to a survey result published by GoodMobilePhones.co.uk, about half of the water-damaged mobile phones in the UK were caused by falling into the toilet.

The website surveyed 1,937 British mobile phone users aged 18 and above. The results showed that 31% of the respondents had their phones damaged by water or other liquids.

Among the ways phones got water-damaged, toilets ranked first at 47%; followed by drinks spilling onto the phone or the phone falling into a drink, accounting for approximately 21%; 12% were damaged while taking a bath or shower; 7% fell into the kitchen sink; and 3% were damaged by being left in clothes pockets that went through the washing machine. In such accidents, the gender ratio of victims was roughly 3:1, with 73% male and 27% female.

As a result, this has somewhat boosted the sales volume of mobile phones. After all, few people would retrieve their phones from the toilet bowl to continue using it. Even if someone does, it depends on the quality of the phone. If you want to retrieve your phone, here's what you can do: First, act quickly and retrieve the phone immediately! Then, remove the battery as soon as possible – remember: quickly! This determines whether your phone survives or not! Remember: after retrieving the phone from the water, do not turn it on immediately. Doing so could very likely render your phone useless.

First, disassemble the phone case and observe the extent of water ingress. If there is no water or only a small amount has seeped into the phone, use a hairdryer to blow dry the disassembled phone until it is completely dry. Then, leave it for a few hours before reinstalling the battery and turning it on. It should generally return to normal function. If a large amount of water has entered, it should be sent to after-sales service for professional repair immediately. Please note: water ingress may cause data loss or changes in the charging IC and charging control circuit, affecting the charging process and potentially damaging the phone. Therefore, within six months after handling, avoid direct charging and recommend using universal chargers to charge the battery.

However, at the end of this article, the author would like to remind everyone to pay attention to keeping your phone secure, whether in the restroom or when going out. This is not only to prevent losing the phone but also for environmental considerations, aiming to reduce the frequency of phone replacements.
