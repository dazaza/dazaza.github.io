Normative Answering in Mock Exams: Pay Attention to Four Key Points for College Entrance Exam Preparation

by longersing on 2009-05-04 17:32:00

Every year, in the national college entrance exam and high school entrance exam, there are always some candidates who lose scores due to carelessness in answering questions, thus missing out on their dream universities or key high schools.

Currently, as students enter the final stage of mock exams in preparation for these major tests, Director Qu of the Office of Nanjing Normal University Affiliated Secondary School reminds candidates that they should take full advantage of the mock exam opportunities to practice standardized test-taking, addressing potential issues such as poor time management and excessive corrections on the answer sheets during the actual exam.

Situation 1: Blindly Answering Questions

Fill in Your Name First and Read the Instructions Carefully

As soon as the invigilator hands out the exam papers, some candidates start answering the questions without filling in their names. As a result, they do not fill in their names until it is time to submit the paper, meaning that no matter how well the answers are written, they will not receive any points because the paper is unsigned.

Some candidates also rush into answering questions without carefully reading the instructions. For example, mistaking a multiple-choice question for a single-choice one, or misinterpreting the requirement to use the language from the text as using their own words, leading to wasted effort with no results. When it comes to essay questions, it is important to carefully consider every word and fully understand the intent of the question to avoid making significant mistakes.

Response: Pay attention to adjusting your psychological state during the exam. After entering the exam room, don't panic; make sure to read the instructions carefully before starting to answer the questions. When doing multiple-choice questions, candidates often have the habit of selecting the "correct" option. However, some questions may require choosing the "incorrect" option, so it's crucial to read the instructions thoroughly before answering.

Situation 2: Poor Time Management

Skip Difficult Questions and Avoid Lingering

Some candidates meticulously answer questions from the beginning to the end. If they encounter a difficult fill-in-the-blank question, they spend a lot of time trying various methods to solve it, using up precious minutes, which leads to insufficient time for questions they could otherwise easily answer.

Response: The time in the exam room is very tight, so it’s important to manage it wisely. If you come across a question you can't solve, skip it and move on to the ones you can handle first, then return to the difficult ones later. Candidates should ideally wear a watch to keep track of time and avoid lingering too long on any single question.

Situation 3: Messy Corrections on the Paper

Choose the Right Writing Instrument and Write Cleanly After Drafting

Each year, some candidates write excellent essays that would otherwise be considered fine works, but they fail to achieve high scores due to poor handwriting and numerous corrections. Some incorrect characters are simply scribbled out, leaving the entire paper messy and untidy.

Many students fear poor sentence structure and repeatedly revise their sentences on the exam paper, adding notes and frames, resulting in a disorganized and unclean appearance, which is a big no-no and often leads to low scores.

Response: Choose the right writing instrument—preferably smooth, fine-tipped, and dark enough to leave a clear mark. Ink that is too light lacks vigor and makes grading difficult, while ink that is too dark can dirty the paper and affect its appearance. Candidates can use blue or black ink pens, ballpoint pens, or fountain pens, ensuring the font size is neither too large nor too small. If a mistake is made, lightly draw a diagonal line through the error instead of heavily marking it out, preserving the overall cleanliness of the paper.

Candidates should avoid using correction fluid or tape. When unsure about the flow of thoughts, especially in Chinese and English compositions, it is best to draft ideas on scratch paper first before neatly transcribing them onto the exam sheet.

Situation 4: Not Reviewing After Completion

Develop the Habit of Reviewing and Avoid Random Examples in Essays

Some candidates do not develop the habit of reviewing their work, being careless when answering questions. Even if they finish early, they do nothing and neglect to check their answers. As a result, obvious errors go unnoticed, and even questions they know how to answer are left blank, costing them tens of points.

Response: Reviewing is an essential step that must not be skipped. After finishing, carefully check for any omissions, typographical errors, missing titles, and areas where improvements can still be made.

Additionally, candidates should be cautious when writing argumentative essays. For instance, a candidate aiming to express the importance of cherishing life might inappropriately criticize figures like Qu Yuan and Leslie Cheung for not valuing their lives, which would be an ill-chosen example.
