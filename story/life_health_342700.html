Why Don't Indians Drink Alcohol or Smoke?

By Xu Shuguang, Chairman of GreenTree Hotels and 519.com Wine Direct Sales Network

The culture of alcohol in China has a long history. It's not an exaggeration to say we are the homeland and kingdom of alcohol. There are many countries around the world with drinking habits, but none can compare with China because China has imbued alcohol with truly "diverse and colorful" content, mostly for "social" purposes. As the saying goes, "No banquet is complete without alcohol." Some people may naturally enjoy heavy drinking and smoking, others might be forced by circumstances beyond their control, and still others seek solace through alcohol and tobacco to alleviate their sorrows. Without a doubt, our alcohol culture carries various "purposes" and permeates all aspects of society. And yet, in India, one could say this is not absolutely non-existent, but it is very uncommon. This raises the question: Why don't Indians drink alcohol or smoke? Regarding the dangers of smoking and excessive drinking, I won't elaborate further here, but suffice it to say, they pose significant threats to human health. Once, I read a report that said an Indian textbook had a picture of Jesus holding a cigarette in one hand and a glass of alcohol in the other. This quickly sparked strong dissatisfaction and condemnation from the public, especially devotees, who severely punished those responsible because, in their view, alcohol and tobacco were not only a desecration of Jesus but also a direct challenge to India's long-standing good habit of not smoking and drinking less.

India, as one of the birthplaces of world religions, has been profoundly influenced by religion, which plays an important role in the country. One of the commandments of some religions explicitly requires followers not to smoke or drink alcohol. Religion thus forms a good constraint on people's beliefs. Secondly, at the legal level, India prohibits smoking and drinking in public places. Although India's alcohol market is small, the Indian government continues to strengthen regulations, such as life imprisonment or even the death penalty for fake alcohol that causes consumer deaths, along with hefty fines. Legal stipulations not only purify the environment of public spaces but also create a good environment for the masses. Thirdly, television is prohibited from airing any advertisements directly or indirectly promoting tobacco and alcohol. In this way, tobacco and alcohol lose their "voice" on television and other media. Fourthly, in India, not every store is allowed to sell alcohol, and even stores authorized to sell alcohol have time restrictions on when they can operate. All these measures aim to create a good environment, controlling tobacco and alcohol from religion to law, to television advertising and sales channels, thereby influencing people's perceptions. Over time, the awareness in people's minds that "smoking is harmful to health" increases. The more cultured and prestigious individuals are, the more law-abiding and religious they become, avoiding involvement with tobacco and alcohol. Gradually, a good habit of not smoking and drinking less forms in society. Clearly, a good environment determines people's habits. Recently, I heard doctors urging Obama to quit smoking. Since Obama became president, he has repeatedly vowed to quit smoking, but so far, it remains just a promise. Clearly, once a bad habit is formed, changing it is extremely difficult, but not impossible.

In ancient times, Chinese scholars learned architecture, philosophy, and literature from India. Nowadays, India's advanced IT industry and financial systems, including the attitude of Indians towards tobacco and alcohol mentioned in this article, are areas where modern Chinese should learn. On the issue of tobacco and alcohol, our GreenTree Hotels has made considerable efforts. To provide guests with a more comfortable and healthy living environment, we have introduced smoke-free floors and smoke-free lobbies... For guests engaging in uncivilized behavior such as drunken disturbances, we refuse service because it affects others' comfort, safety, and health experiences in the hotel and tarnishes the image of GreenTree Hotels. They say you're bound by circumstances, but if this "circumstance" is India's non-smoking, non-drinking "world," how could you be bound? Even someone naturally inclined to heavy drinking would likely quit under India's environment. As for those addicted to alcohol and tobacco, that's a different matter. Because, if we liken tobacco and alcohol to slow suicide, one who insists on choosing this form of slow suicide cannot be stopped no matter how hard others try; at most, the date of suicide can be postponed. Environmental protection was a major focus at the Expo, and as one of the three designated hotel suppliers chosen by the Expo Organizing Committee, we at GreenTree are ready for the Expo because we have always been on the path of environmental protection and health, standing prepared.

If you can choose not to drink, please don't; if you can choose not to smoke, please don't. Health is more important than anything else. Perhaps it is only when we lie on our sickbeds one day that we will fully understand this truth.
