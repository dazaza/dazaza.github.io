Andy Rubin on Android

by ybcz0519 on 2010-10-13 15:47:49

PCMag recently interviewed Android chief Andy about some hot issues on Android, including: too many versions, the attitudes of OEM manufacturers and carriers, the new version 3.0, and whether WP7 will pose a threat to Android. Some of these issues are also generally of concern to domestic industry insiders - such as whether the numerous versions of Android will cause fragmentation, and how OEM manufacturers can form differentiated competition by adopting a unified Android system. Below is a summary by Wukong, with some additional comments.

Q: Currently, there are many versions of Android, with some still running on 1.6. How do you ensure a consistent experience for developers and consumers?

A: I believe that OEM manufacturers have quickly understood what sells and what doesn't. I am very satisfied with the pace of innovation on the Android platform. If we release versions like 2.3 or 3.0, it's a different level because these new features and innovations are something OEM manufacturers are very willing to adopt. Manufacturers who don't want to adopt new features will face consumer criticism and abandonment. We also see that consumers highly recognize each version update. I think manufacturers will adjust their market strategies according to the new versions. Manufacturers using old versions will be at a consumer disadvantage.

Commentary: Google sees consumers as the force influencing the market, and the market will adjust for those who don't keep up.

Q: Will you slow down the release of major Android versions?

A: When we released version 1.0 at Christmas, that version was like 0.8 to me. We added some features later to meet industrial needs. The previous rapid version updates were to catch up with the industrial development cycle. Now, I feel we've caught up. So new versions won't be those "catch-up" releases anymore; they will focus on innovation.

Commentary: As he said, Android is catching up with Apple, and getting faster and closer.

Q: People generally think that the free Android platform means low barriers for carriers.

A: If I release a system, claim it's open, and force all phones to look the same with limited functions and features, this platform won't win. Because OEM manufacturers and carriers have a lot of value to add to the phone, they need a tool that allows them to add differentiated features. Each phone should not look like any other phone. The essence of this issue is to find out consumer needs and tailor products accordingly.

Commentary: He responded to the question of how manufacturers can differentiate their products using the Android platform.

The best part of Android: Andy mentioned the security architecture, notification manager, and hybrid web-native applications. The last point is the most exciting - you can package web applications into native programs. Even if technicians update six times a day, users won't notice, providing a smooth and stable experience.

Android 3.0 Gingerbread:

Improving communication efficiency in social media.

Currently underperforming game sector - "We are innovating everyday games. If you look at Xbox, PS, Nintendo, they are all solid and experience-maximized gaming platforms, but mobile platforms are battery-driven. Mobile games are more about killing time rather than dedicated gaming moments"; Also mentioned mixed games based on HTML5 local applications.

About WP7:

Andy dismissively said, "I saw their screenshots. The only reason Microsoft launched WP7 might be political reasons."
