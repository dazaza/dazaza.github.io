A single Home button supports an iPhone 5s.

by anonymous on 2013-09-11 14:36:44

　　A single Home button supports the entire iPhone 5s.

　　Cupertino, Apple, after a year of preparation for the event that everyone eagerly awaited. Despite the entire presentation being laid bare, Apple still had the ability to elicit occasional screams from the audience.

　　It was Siri two years ago, and this year it's undoubtedly Touch ID.

　　When the entire smartphone industry is completely caught in the whirlpool of hardware competition, Apple decisively broke through and anchored its ship at a calm harbor. This harbor is the issue of smartphone security.

　　This is an era where "a single highlight can carry an entire event." The glory of Jobs, along with the surprise and excitement of the debut of the original iPhone and the iPhone 4, has long disappeared into the skies above Yerba Buena Center.

　　This is the inevitable result of technological development. What we should not be fixated on is whether what carries the 5s is one point or two points, but rather whether that glowing Home button with starlight adds just two lines of bold promotional text to the iPhone 5s page, or truly ignites the enthusiasm of the entire industry regarding smartphone security, benefiting consumers.

　　The so-called "cheap" version of the iPhone is not "cheap."

　　What has been loudly proclaimed in the media context for over a year as the so-called "cheap version of the iPhone" finally made its appearance, even though its price is higher than what its "name suggests".

　　Compared to the consistently high prices of iPhones, $549 (the domestic price is too unique and lacks reference value) is indeed cheaper, but when you think Apple's pricing has defied your common sense, please check the prices of older models when each new iPhone is launched.

　　From the iMac's $1299 to the Mac mini's $599, from the MacBook Pro's $1499 to the MacBook Air's $999, from the iPad's $499 to the iPad mini's $329, from the iPod Classic's $249 to the iPod Shuffle's $49. Apple has never been the stingy iron rooster in our fixed perceptions; entry-level products are quite common.

　　Low price does not equal cheapness, whether it's the Mac mini, iPod Shuffle, or iPad mini. Apple's lower prices are more often the result of supply chain optimization.

　　Plastic?

　　The first generation iPod Shuffle was plastic, and so was the MacBook. Were they cheap? Cheap are the loose, smelly polycarbonate back covers of Samsung Galaxy S, even though its price is on par with the iPhone.

　　History repeats itself, and this time it's the iPhone. But some people just refuse to believe that the iPhone in their hands will eventually become as accessible as the iPod.

　　The elephant is dancing.

　　In the Jobs era, polishing a product for two or three years was not uncommon. Even entering the explosive mobile internet era of 2010, 12 months was basically the shortest cycle for Apple product iterations. However, last October, the iPad 4 unexpectedly launched just six months after the iPad 3.

　　Despite Chinese consumers showing the same eagerness, by the time the iPhones on shelves across the ocean had already accumulated some scratches, the "eggs" in front of the Sanlitun Apple Store were just beginning to fly. But this time, Shenzhen grey market dealers don't have to rack their brains thinking about how to smuggle the iPhone 5s back to China in the first instance.

　　As new members like the iPad mini and iPhone 5c join the Apple family, and product launches begin to move beyond U.S. borders, the once leisurely elephant has clearly started running.

　　Behind this is the acceleration of decision-making within Apple, but also a reflection of the intensifying competition in the smartphone market.

　　If the elephant can jump so high, what about the audience and other small animals?

The iPhone event will be held on September 10th.
