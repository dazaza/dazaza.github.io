It is right that Hong Kong's capital market does not give way to Alibaba

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:22:31

On October 11, the negotiations for Alibaba, in which Jack Ma holds a 7.4% stake, to list in Hong Kong hit a snag. The main reason was that the partnership system, which allows the current management team to retain control of the company after listing, does not comply with Hong Kong's listing regulations. Before discussing whether Hong Kong should change its rules to keep this multi-billion-dollar listing opportunity in Hong Kong, we need to first understand different methods of control and their pros and cons.

From a governance perspective, listed companies mainly have two issues to address: 1) conflicts between non-controlling managers and shareholders; 2) conflicts of interest between controlling major shareholders and minority shareholders. The first issue is more familiar to people. This time, we will mainly discuss the second issue, which is also related to Alibaba's listing. Many major shareholders of listed companies actually own only a small portion of the shares, but because they have control, they can use various means, such as giving high compensation to the CEO or appointing trusted confidants as executives, to appropriate most of the wealth of minority shareholders.

Dual-class structures perform less effectively

If Jack Ma and other members of the management team only hold about 10% of Alibaba's shares, what methods do shareholders have to control the entire company? One method is to establish a system where these 10% of people nominate most of the board members, and then the board appoints the CEO and other senior executives. However, Hong Kong's current listing rules do not allow such a scheme. The U.S. allows dual-class structures (Dual Class Structure), also known as super-voting rights plans in mainland China. Baidu used this method to go public, and Google in the U.S. is another example. Baidu issued A shares when it went public in the U.S., where each share has one vote in voting. However, the founders' shares are B class, with each share having 10 votes. This system allows major shareholders to control the company by holding approximately 10% of the shares.

The general argument supporting dual-class structures is that companies can be free from market influence and better pursue long-term interests. However, economist Paul Gompers, in his study of the performance of U.S. dual-class structure companies from 1994 to 2002, found that these companies generally performed worse than ordinary structured stocks. Recently, some people believe that Alibaba's dual-class structure in Hong Kong is an innovative approach, which is completely misunderstanding the history of Hong Kong's capital market. The most famous example is Swire's AB share system. Back then, British capital wanted to divest due to concerns over Hong Kong's return to China, but did not want to lose control. Therefore, they issued shares with different par values but the same voting rights, both cashing out and retaining control. For instance, five B shares of Swire equal one A share in value, so the dividends and benefits enjoyed are only 1/5, and the value is roughly 1/5, but the voting rights are the same. In this way, the original British capital could retain control by holding onto the cheaper B shares.

Major shareholder rights can also be reduced

How is this practice that harms minority shareholders balanced in the U.S.? The U.S. has powerful legal teams, class action laws, financial supervision and disclosure systems, and is primarily dominated by institutional investors, enabling them to more effectively balance major shareholders. This might explain why Alibaba didn't want to list in the U.S. Google once wanted to issue new shares without voting rights, but had to compromise after losing a class action lawsuit initiated by shareholders in June this year.

Not only may minority shareholders be harmed, but even powerful major shareholders can have their interests stripped. Yahoo originally owned 43% of Alibaba's equity, but in 2010, Jack Ma transferred the main asset Alipay out without Yahoo’s and another major investor Softbank’s consent, eventually even leading to a lawsuit by Yahoo shareholders against poor management. After nearly a year of negotiation, an agreement was reached for Alipay IPO returns ranging from a minimum of $2 billion to a maximum of $6 billion. Alipay is Alibaba's main asset, and for a company potentially valued at hundreds of billions of dollars, this was clearly an undervalued price. If Yahoo, which owns 43% of the equity and occupies one seat out of four on the board, couldn't prevent the asset transfer, how can minority shareholders lacking regulatory capacity ensure they won't be exploited? Thus, a good listing system is an important measure to protect investors and ensure the proper functioning of the market.

Different tricks come at a cost

Dual-class structures are not the only way to raise capital while not giving up control. There are many ways to raise capital, including bank loans and issuing bonds. Creditors don’t interfere with the details of company operations, and the shares of existing shareholders won’t be diluted. Of course, creditors have different clauses to protect themselves, such as prohibiting asset transfers, requiring regular interest payments, otherwise taking over the company, not distributing dividends before paying creditors’ interest and installment repayments, maintaining operational efficiency and profit margins at certain levels, etc. These are all the costs of equity not being diluted.

Issuing preferred shares (Preferred Shares) is another method of raising capital without reducing control. Preferred shares generally have fixed dividends, and the company must pay the dividends on preferred shares before paying dividends on common shares. If the company lacks cash and cannot pay the dividends on preferred shares, these dividends will accumulate. The company must repay all accumulated dividends before it can distribute dividends to common shares. These are systems to protect preferred shares without voting rights. Generally, the dividend rate of preferred shares is higher than that of common shares, but the price is lower, so financing through preferred shares is not a cheap option. That is to say, major shareholders must pay the appropriate cost to continue controlling the company.

Another method to control with a small stake is through multi-layered holding companies. For example, A owns 51% of B; B owns 51% of C; C owns 51% of D. This holding method requires A to actually own only 13.3% of D to control D's management rights. Many family businesses in Hong Kong control their operations through this method, and the Cheung Kong Holdings conglomerate is a famous example. However, this method is not easy. Using the example of A, B, C, and D, each company needs to be listed, meaning finding minority shareholders to buy the remaining 49%. Moreover, generally speaking, the stock prices of these complexly controlled companies are not good. The reason is clear: if there's no way to remove a very bad management team, it makes sense that the company's value is discounted.

Negotiation process principles must be upheld

In summary, maintaining control comes at a cost, basically: 1) relying on oneself, using one's own capital, private company; 2) borrowing, but with a high risk of bankruptcy, requiring good fiscal discipline to avoid difficulties; 3) preferred shares, but with high fundraising costs; 4) complex multi-layered holdings, but difficult to operate and low stock prices; 5) listing in the U.S., using dual-class structures, but possibly facing class-action lawsuits and strict disclosure and regulatory requirements.

Negotiations require knowing your bargaining chips and what you stand to lose if they fail. Compromising on the rules for Alibaba and driving away minority shareholders would damage the investment environment, which is not a good thing. Corporate governance in Asia has always been relatively weak, and without better regulations, there will be a problem of bad money driving out good in the long term. Hong Kong can compete with other Chinese stock markets, an important factor being the trust gained through its good regulations. More importantly, listing elsewhere, like in the U.S., is not necessarily beneficial for Chinese companies, as meeting American listing regulations is extremely difficult.

Alibaba not listing in Hong Kong would also incur significant losses. Personally, I believe negotiations should continue with a positive attitude, but important principles should not be compromised.

(Author of this article: Zekai Wang, Professor of Applied Finance at the Chinese University of Hong Kong, Director of the Master's Program, former Managing Director of Barclays, over ten years of investment banking experience, PhD in Economics from Oxford University.)
