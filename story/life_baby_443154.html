For children, toys are not just commodities. Anything that captures their interest can become a toy or material for making one, even if it is worth nothing, it will still become a cherished plaything.

A child's way of thinking is completely different from an adult's, so the concept by which children measure the value of things is also very different from adults'. In a child's little mind, the value of a toy has nothing to do with its monetary worth but everything to do with personal preference. For a child, as long as something attracts their attention, or the process of making it into a toy stimulates their interest in playing, then that thing is a very good toy. Regardless of how worthless this thing may seem to parents, for the child, it is a priceless "treasure".

1. Give children the freedom to choose their toys, even if what they pick up is what you consider "trash".

No matter what a child brings back from outside, as long as it does not pose a health hazard, parents should respect the child's choice. In the process of choosing these toys, the child's imagination and creativity are unknowingly exercised and developed. Help the child wash those small stones and sticks, then create something together with the child using these items, even if it's just simply lining up the stones in a long row. A parent's tolerance nurtures both the child's wisdom and happiness, and from any perspective, the right for a child to collect such 'trash' is highly significant.

2. Respect the child's feelings towards their toys.

No matter how old or simple a toy may be, if a child remains interested in it for a long time and never tires of playing with it, then that toy naturally has its unique qualities. To the child, it is no longer just a toy but may have already established some form of emotional connection. Besides the need for good food and clothing, children also yearn for respect, independence, and the freedom to create. Respecting a child means giving them back their freedom and independence, allowing them to make autonomous choices and explore freely.

3. Understand the true essence of playing with toys.

The most important thing that toys bring to children is health and happiness, and all of this cannot be measured by money. If a child is deeply attached to a paper airplane and shows no interest in the newly bought remote control airplane, what fault lies in the child's right to play with the paper airplane?

Give children the right to choose their toys, respect their right to choose toys, and do not judge their choices based on the value system of the adult world. Even if they choose what you see as "trash", it will become an "angel" in their eyes, becoming a "treasure" in their eyes.
