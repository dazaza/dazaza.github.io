Are you really sure you want to buy Google Glass? Check this out first.

by anonymous on 2013-11-17 19:46:10

Having always been particularly interested in mobile devices and wearable technology, I recently had the good fortune to be invited by a friend to join the Google Glass Explorer Program. Despite the pain of spending over $1,600 on Google Glass, I have some personal thoughts after using it and would like to ask whether, after reading this, you still feel an urgent desire to try out this new wearable device?

Google Glass 2.0

Firstly, let's talk about comfort when wearing it. Like many computer enthusiasts, I am part of the nearsighted crowd and rarely wear contact lenses due to laziness. This presents a natural difficulty for me in using Google Glass since the current version does not support any prescription glasses or lenses.

Although Google has promised to provide solutions soon, those of us who are eager to try it, such as myself, will need to wear contact lenses. The strain from wearing contacts combined with unconsciously looking at the screen in the upper-right corner of the glasses causes much more eye fatigue than usual. Aside from these two points, the rest is relatively comfortable; possibly because wearing glasses is already a regular part of my life, so there isn’t any additional discomfort.

Next, let me briefly introduce the differences between Google Glass 2.0 and 1.0. The biggest change in Google Glass 2.0 is the addition of a "dorky-looking" mono earbud. Actually, this change came from feedback Google received regarding the use of version 1.0. One issue was that the volume on Google Glass could not be adjusted, making it almost impossible to hear what your Glass was saying in noisy environments. Google’s straightforward solution, which made Google Glass look slightly less cool, was to add a headphone.

However, upon further thought, there really isn't a better solution. Google didn’t want you to miss hearing the sounds from Glass, nor did they want everyone around you to hear what you're doing. A headphone is the most direct and balanced solution. Apart from the added headphone, there are practically no other differences between 2.0 and 1.0.

Practical Google Glass

Now, I’d like to share my personal experiences and feelings from using Google Glass over the past few days. What I like most about Google Glass is that it allows me to be completely free from my phone while driving. Due to the traffic situation in Silicon Valley, I spend anywhere from half an hour to an hour each day stuck in traffic. During this time, handling texts and emails becomes nearly impossible. In this regard, Google Glass is indeed more convenient and intuitive compared to most voice assistants on smartphones.

Another feature I enjoy is the Google Maps function. As someone who frequently gets lost, Google Maps gives me peace of mind at all times. However, sometimes when using Google Maps on my phone, I worry about delays in voice prompts, and it's difficult to quickly change lanes on congested highways to head towards an exit. Often, I have to take out my phone to check how far the next intersection is. After several uses of the map function on Glass, I felt very pleased because I don’t have to wait for voice prompts or pick up my phone to see how far the next intersection is and whether I should turn left or right.

Ordinary Google Glass

Next, I'll discuss some features that haven't impressed me much, such as the well-known photo-taking and video-recording functions. For me, these features are rather irrelevant; it's hard for me to find a moment where I can't pull out my camera or phone but must take photos or videos. Perhaps for people with children, these two features might be more useful. Of course, having these two functions is still nice because Glass can directly upload your videos or photos to social media platforms like Facebook or Google+, and the source will be marked as "from Glass," making it a great tool for showing off to your friends. Other than that, I mostly use these features to capture my pet dog doing “silly things.”

The remaining features are almost dispensable for me—news notifications, Google+ Hangout video calls, instant search, translation, etc. These functions aren’t needed all the time, and when truly necessary, I usually have a computer or phone handy. Personally, I believe that Google Glass still has significant room for expansion. There aren’t many apps available yet, and the content is also quite lacking. So far, there hasn't been an app on Google Glass that has impressed everyone.

Google Glass's battery life also doesn’t last as long as Google claims—a full day of continuous use per charge. Additionally, when using voice commands to control Glass, you need to maintain a normal speaking volume, which can feel awkward in quiet offices or among crowds.

In summary, I personally believe that the current Google Glass is not yet fully capable of leading the wearable device market. Similar to the Samsung Galaxy Gear before it, there are plenty of exciting features, but practical applications in daily life are limited. Google Glass gives me a similar impression—some surprising features cannot mask its current limitations. However, based on our understanding of Google, they wouldn’t release a product they’re proud of without continually improving it. New functionalities for Google Glass are surely being actively explored.

Finally, I still hope that one day Google Glass can automatically recognize people, objects, and events, allowing users to interact more effectively and simply with Glass. More and more app developers will make Glass even more practical and interesting.
