Synopsis:

The entire film is narrated from the first-person perspective of the unnamed female protagonist. It tells the story of a young woman who marries into a wealthy family and discovers that the shadow of her husband De Winter's deceased wife, Rebecca, looms over the entire Manderley estate. The mysterious housekeeper, Mrs. Danvers, also appears like a ghost around her. In an attempt to please her husband De Winter, the protagonist deliberately wears the same dress as in Rebecca's portrait at a grand ball, inadvertently uncovering a mysterious murder case from years ago.

Behind-the-Scenes Production:

This film was produced by Selznick International Pictures in 1940. This "Rebecca" is both a representative masterpiece of Hitchcock's early films and a milestone that brought him a brighter future. The atmosphere of the film is eerie and enchanting, making it a highly skilled psychological drama. In the movie, Hitchcock brilliantly portrays the dark and oppressive image of Manderley Manor, with its desolate castle-like ruins, a dog in the spacious hall, and the expressionless, ghostly presence of the housekeeper, all becoming timeless memories in the history of world cinema. The novel was adapted twice for television in 1980 and 1997. Based on the best-selling suspense novel "Rebecca" by the renowned British writer Daphne du Maurier, the film won the 1940 Academy Awards for Best Picture and Best Cinematography (Black and White). It solidified Hitchcock's status as a master in Hollywood. His films such as "Dial M for Murder," "Psycho," and "The 39 Steps" have become unforgettable classics in cinema history. Both lead actors, Laurence Olivier and Joan Fontaine, were nominated for the Academy Awards for Best Actor and Best Actress because of this film.

Joan Fontaine, under Hitchcock's direction, delivered a sensitive, subtle, and natural performance, propelling her to stardom. She later won the Academy Award for Best Actress, the New York Film Critics Award for Best Actress, and the National Board of Review Award for Best Performance for her role in "Suspicion," becoming the youngest Oscar-winning actress at the time and the only one to achieve such honors through a Hitchcock film.

The male lead was portrayed by Laurence Olivier, a renowned British stage actor, film star, screenwriter, director, and producer. Known for his successful portrayals of Shakespearean characters, he was called "the greatest contributor to Shakespeare's works in modern times." He was a multiple-time Oscar-nominated star, receiving 11 nominations and winning once. In recognition of his special contributions to world cinema and theater, he received a special Oscar in 1944 and was knighted as the first actor knight in Britain in 1947. In 1970, he was ennobled as a baron, and in 1978, the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences awarded him a special honor for his lifelong contribution to the art of film. In 1981, Queen Elizabeth II personally awarded him the Order of Merit, making him the only noble movie star in British history. Laurence Olivier successfully portrayed the character of De Winter tormented by the memory of his deceased wife Rebecca in this film, delivering a deeply moving performance.

Trivia:

- Director Alfred Hitchcock makes a cameo appearance, walking past a phone booth after George Sanders makes a crucial call.

- Hitchcock had wanted to make this movie for many years but lacked the funds to acquire the adaptation rights to the novel.

- This is Hitchcock's second movie based on Daphne Du Maurier's novels; the first was "Jamaica Inn" (1933).

- To maintain the dark atmosphere of the original novel, Hitchcock insisted on filming the movie in black and white.

- Due to the enormous success of "Rebecca" in Spain, the blouse worn by Joan Fontaine in the film was named "Rebecca." The term "Rebecca" is still used in Spain today to refer to this type of clothing.
