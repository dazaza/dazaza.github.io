Dianping.com: A Bone Too Big for the Giants to Chew, Living in the Discomfort Zone

by anonymous on 2013-07-24 17:16:23

CEO of Dianping.com, Zhang Tao

Written by Yuan Yin, edited by Yang Jing, photographed by Xiao Nan

Zhang Tao, CEO of Dianping.com, has postponed the company's IPO once again.

In 2011, Dianping received a $100 million Series C funding round from Sequoia Capital and others, valuing the company at $1 billion. According to convention, it should have gone public within one to two years, but it didn't. Instead, in 2012, it raised another $60 million in Series D funding.

Given Dianping's scale and development momentum, going public would not be difficult. However, Zhang Tao feels that listing now would be too costly. He deconstructs the big internet (PC + mobile) into four elements - people, information, goods, and services. Each connection point offers an opportunity for giants to emerge. Baidu connects people with information, Tencent connects people with people, and Alibaba connects people with goods. The space between people and services is where the fourth giant could reside. Since 2003, Dianping has been diligently accumulating data, finally waiting for the explosion of mobile internet, where phones connect "location" and "services." Following Zhang Tao's logic, no company seems more fitting than Dianping for the role of the fourth giant.

However, outsiders don't see it this way. In recent months, BAT (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent) have frequently acquired companies, with Dianping being speculated as a potential acquisition target. On one hand, it is indeed an excellent acquisition target; regardless of which BAT it aligns with, it could help them achieve great success early on. On the other hand, Dianping's size pales in comparison to BAT, and it's too early to declare it a future giant. Moreover, whether it's Tencent WeChat's public platform, Baidu Maps, or Alibaba after investing in AutoNavi and Meituan, they all target Dianping's territory - local life services centered around dining, drinking, entertainment, etc.

Before proving the following two points, Dianping's IPO would equal a "discounted sale." First, Zhang Tao needs time to prove that what Dianping is doing, BAT cannot replicate. Second, Dianping is about to engage in the ultimate battle of group buying, and if it secures the top spot, its valuation could increase several times over.

2013 is Dianping's "decisive year." For speculation and rumors, Zhang Tao pays no heed. He is clear: "The internet has indeed entered a great era of integration, but Dianping will not be acquired."

Zhang Tao categorizes potentially acquirable companies into three types. The first type has platform attributes but is better suited to develop within the ecosystem of a giant, such as maps and email. These products can easily attract large numbers of users but struggle to find independent business models. Some, like browsers, can make money, but the revenue earned through navigation services is far less than their strategic value to large companies. "Giants are willing to invest in email products because they help with brand and user stickiness, but there haven't been any successful standalone email companies," he said. The second type involves industry consolidation, like in the video industry, using acquisitions to expand market share. The third type consists of companies facing financial difficulties, selling themselves to survive.

Now Meituan has been brought under Alibaba's wing. In theory, Dianping should quickly seek a "backer" to avoid defeat due to rapid expansion funded by competitors. Zhang Tao, however, says, "The help from giants is limited. We do need users, but execution is even more important. Mergers and acquisitions reduce execution efficiency and could lead to marginalization by large companies." Not only that, he aims to keep Dianping at the most precise "distance" from the giants. If it gets too close to any one of them, it would be equivalent to "taking sides," making it an enemy of the others.

Thus, acquiring Dianping logically leads to a dead end. Zhang Tao counters, "Why should we take their money? We're not short of funds; what we need are resources. If a giant is willing to take a small stake and provide us with many resources, I might consider it. But would they? Would a giant give you resources if they hold only a small stake?"

Weapon One: Dirty and Tiring Work

In mobile internet, opportunities for companies doing purely internet products are becoming fewer. New opportunities come from those combining with traditional industries. Zhang Tao believes that lifestyle services mainly revolve around dining, drinking, and entertainment. Other niche markets, such as ride-hailing, can form decent business models, but it's hard for them to grow into giants.

For dining, drinking, and entertainment, user needs are simple - information and discounts. Among these, dining is the core of lifestyle services, which is precisely where Dianping started. Whether in content, member accumulation, or branding, newcomers find it hard to surpass.

The most representative product form of discounts is group buying. Now group buying has entered the championship battle, and Dianping plans to face off against Meituan in 2013. This is a winner-takes-all market; as long as there are two strong players, the industry's gross profit margin will be lowered, making stable profitability impossible.

Another foreseeable future is reservations. Reservations can form a true online-to-offline closed loop, but they must be built on the foundation of Dianping having absolute advantages in information and discounts. Otherwise, creating a closed loop is difficult. In 2012, Dianping began trying out reservations. Zhang Tao said, "Currently, both user and merchant feedback is good, but doing reservations alone makes it hard to solve the chicken-and-egg problem."

Of course, lifestyle services present some heavier opportunities. For example, food delivery is a rigid demand for users and helps merchants increase sales. In the past few years, food delivery companies like "To Home Gourmet" have thrived. However, the core of food delivery is delivery - getting meals delivered within a short time frame while solving issues like soup spilling. The logistics requirements are higher than for fresh produce e-commerce. Zhang Tao said, "If food delivery is executed well, there is still opportunity, but this is harder dirty and tiring work, and Dianping is already doing enough dirty and tiring work."

If judged by age, Dianping is an "old" company. For the past decade, it has diligently accumulated merchant profiles and user reviews, which Zhang Tao refers to as "dirty and tiring work." This primitive growth model once limited its speed, but the pain of the past has become Dianping's core barrier today. It can be said that currently, apart from Meituan, Dianping has no real competitors in the lifestyle services sector.

However, he still needs to be wary of BAT's "clutches." Baidu's involvement is the most direct. Baidu Maps already has over 100 million users, and users can use the map to find nearby dining and other lifestyle services. Tencent, considered to have secured its ticket to mobile internet, launched membership cards on WeChat. Alibaba is already the largest goods trading platform, and expanding its offerings from physical goods to services is natural, especially since it has incorporated Meituan and AutoNavi into its strategic map.

"Whether it's Baidu Maps or Tencent WeChat, there are no signs of explosive growth in terms of reality and data," Zhang Tao said. Brand positioning is crucial; the key is which product users turn to first when they want to dine, drink, or entertain.

Moreover, in the vast field of lifestyle services, competition among giants is bound to overlap, and no one can completely monopolize the field. Just like information, Baidu became a giant by aggregating information, but information can also be obtained on Weibo and WeChat. Zhang Tao feels that this actually makes Dianping safer. "In niche fields like car rental and business travel, the massive user base of giants could flood and overwhelm existing companies."

As for Alibaba, which has invested in AutoNavi, wanting to invade Dianping's territory isn't that simple either. All services are non-standardized, unlike goods, which can always be categorized no matter how peculiar. A user's experience of a meal depends on factors like the day's environment, waitstaff, and chef. Managing merchants cannot be done using systems and tools like Alibaba does; a 'ground force' managed by humans is required.

"All three giants want to take this path, and there is room for imagination, but this is not a highway; it's a rugged mountain road," Zhang Tao believes. First, providing lifestyle services requires ground teams deployed at various levels, which BAT lacks the genes and capability to manage. Second, they lack the willingness; lifestyle services require dedicated effort, not just assigning a small team, and profits come slowly. "It's hard for a company to excel at two important things simultaneously, let alone needing to consolidate its advantage in its original field."

Weapon Two: Small Boats Are Easier to Steer

In 2011, when Dianping received $100 million in funding, Zhang Tao told the world that Dianping was "Groupon + Yelp + Foursquare." Unfortunately, times change fast, and before Dianping went public, his analogies fell from grace.

He wasn't anxious about it either. "They failed to become big companies because they lacked offline teams; now they want to be America's Dianping."

The current path is one Zhang Tao has painstakingly explored step by step. When he felt that selling keyword ads and coupons was outdated, the emergence of group buying caught his eye. Initially, like Americans, he viewed group buying as a marketing platform, one deal per day. But as orders increased, he realized group buying had the attributes of a trading platform. Thus, he envisioned Dianping's future as a lifestyle services trading platform. The two rounds of financing in 2011 and 2012 were precisely used for Dianping's transition from an information platform to a trading platform.

Without this "genetic modification," Zhang Tao might not have had the confidence to compete for the fourth seat among the giants. But genetic modification sounds simple, yet it's equivalent to Dianping's second entrepreneurship, a process fraught with pain.

First, compared to selling ads individually, trading platforms lower gross margins and require stricter cost control and efficiency. Second, trading platforms demand higher cross-departmental communication capabilities.

In the past, to maintain Dianping's impartiality and independence as an information platform, the content and sales teams were entirely separated. Zhang Tao even deliberately erected a "firewall" between departments - even for important clients, information on the platform couldn't be altered for sales purposes, leading to some mutual hostility between content and sales teams. Now, as a trading platform, the editorial and sales teams need deep communication. Creating a good marketing plan requires not only planning but also editorial cooperation. He said, "Maintaining information quality is still very important, but there's no need to reinforce animosity; the firewall needs to be dismantled."

Though editors may logically accept this, individual behavior and values have inertia, and when combined across the entire company, the inertia becomes greater. To address this, Zhang Tao specifically held several meetings to encourage more inter-departmental communication. After a few months, an editorial manager casually mentioned to a sales manager, "I used to think you were an enemy, but now I realize you're a colleague."

The sales department also needed genetic modification. To promote conversion, Zhang Tao cleverly gave the offline team dual roles. First, for merchants, salespeople need to transform into marketing consultants, not just selling products but helping merchants formulate marketing strategies. Second, for the company, marketing consultants act as the company's procurement officers. Dianping is like a supermarket; not only must the product quality be good, but the variety must also be rich. Marketing consultants should aim to help users purchase group-buying products. Of course, dual roles inevitably create a series of complex problems. Frontline personnel find this headache-inducing, as they must weigh the balance themselves.

Substantially, Dianping's "dual role" is quite similar to Alibaba's division into 25 business units. Decentralizing decision-making to frontline employees allows the company to respond faster, but it increases management difficulty. Likewise, compared to frontline employees, Dianping's management team suffered more during the transformation.

Every year, Zhang Tao breaks down Dianping's corporate goals into various departments, then senior executives further break them down layer by layer. However, breaking down goals is not simple arithmetic. Li Jing, co-founder of Dianping, who oversees the sales team, sometimes finds that unresolved merchant demands are due to sales issues, but these affect sales performance. Therefore, Li Jing broke down a new position, setting up communication specialists to collect information daily from marketing consultants, submit it to other departments, and track feedback. He often tells his team, "Being adept at embracing change doesn't mean liking change; no one likes change, and everyone feels uncomfortable when faced with it. All employees must go through uncomfortable phases, including me."

Numbers reveal the weight of Dianping's execution team. Out of over 3,000 employees, more than 2,000 work offline. The ground force is responsible for directly communicating with merchants, selling discount coupons, keyword promotions, group buying, and combined marketing solutions.

Dianping has a unique management and evaluation system for this core team resisting BAT's influence.

They aren't called "sales"; from the first day, their business cards read "Marketing Planning Consultant." Many traditional merchants are still stuck in the phase of watching news and chatting on QQ, lagging behind Taobao merchants by at least three to five years in understanding the internet. Marketing planning consultants need to tell merchants what online users need and how to conduct internet marketing. Li Jing said, "Even for a high-end restaurant, marketing advisors wouldn't suggest offering abalone or shark fin group-buying deals, possibly suggesting other dishes instead, because online users differ from offline users."

An outstanding marketing advisor must be a versatile player. They need to explain clearly to restaurant owners and hair salon owners the fundamental differences between discounts and group buying and determine which discount suits their positioning best. If dealing with a hot pot restaurant owner, the marketing advisor should know whether it's the off-season or peak season, what product costs rise in this season, what costs decrease, and propose a group-buying menu that both merchants and users like.

This places high demands on marketing advisors, requiring them to be not only internet marketing experts but also industry experts. Li Jing said, "I must admit, not every advisor can be a double expert, so we set star ratings for advisors." Similar to hotels, marketing advisors range from one-star to five-star, with the best ones called Super Stars.

Unlike BAT employees, Dianping's frontline employees aren't tech-savvy graduates from prestigious universities, making it challenging for them to understand internet products. Dianping established a knowledge base for marketing advisors, containing knowledge points and relevant cases accessible to both new and experienced employees. If encountering new problems, employees can ask questions in the knowledge base, and thousands of consultants nationwide will assist in answering.

A large number of knowledge points in the knowledge base are summarized by employees based on practical experience. Dianping set up a reward mechanism for the knowledge base, rewarding employees who help others the most each quarter. Every quarter, Dianping conducts skill assessments for employees, with results directly linked to promotions.

Industry knowledge is harder to acquire than internet knowledge. Some experiences are distilled by traditional merchants after years of trial and error in the industry. A Super Star marketing advisor told Li Jing that the beauty industry should avoid offering group-buying packages for washing, cutting, and blow-drying because men lack motivation to buy them, and women prefer blow-drying without washing and cutting. Li Jing was astonished, "We men would never think of that."

Therefore, Dianping's marketing advisors are divided not only by city but also by industry, such as catering, beauty salons, and leisure. In cities like Beijing and Shanghai, catering is further subdivided into hot pot, Western cuisine, and buffets, with one marketing advisor responsible for one or two cuisines. "This way, marketing advisors can deeply understand the industry and even share industry knowledge with merchants, making merchants less resistant when selling products," he said.

Each marketing group holds its own case-sharing sessions: what difficulties a certain type of merchant encounters, what marketing needs they have, what product combinations the company provides, and the outcomes. Outstanding cases are reported upward, and Dianping officially shares one case each week.

For new employees, Dianping has a special competition called "Winning Entrepreneurs." Within the first three months of employment, new employees must actively communicate with each potential client, not necessarily selling products but listening to merchants sharing their experiences. If responsible for hot pot restaurants, after three months, they must submit a commercial plan PPT titled "If I Were a Hot Pot Restaurant Owner." Like graduation, employees present their defense, and industry supervisors evaluate the winners, with the company providing certain rewards.

Strategically speaking, whether it's Dianping, Meituan, or BAT, they all have similar tasks ahead. Nowadays, concepts like closed loops and O2O are familiar to anyone with basic industry knowledge, and the words spoken sound alike. "The era where storytelling and concept creation could secure success is over. Now, the key to the success or failure of a business model lies in management and execution, not strategy," Zhang Tao said.
