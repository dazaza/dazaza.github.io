We have to go home with our wallets, big or small.

by qtqa875gf7 on 2010-04-20 14:09:53

Suddenly, I had an urge to read Sloan's "My Years with General Motors," but I couldn't find it anywhere. After careful reflection, perhaps it was lost during one of my many relocations. Regardless, it is gone. In a rush, I went to the bookstore on Fuzhou Road and bought a new copy. Looking at it now feels so unfamiliar; I no longer have the same feeling I once did when reading it before. Those disturbances seem so distant now. On Subway Line 2, there were still bustling crowds, white-collar workers busy with their own affairs, migrant workers carrying large bags rushing to the train station. Another year is about to end, and everyone must tightly hold onto their wallets, big or small, to return home. Watching the hurrying crowd, I lost any desire to continue reading this book. What should I do? After some anxious deliberation, I could only stand quietly, hoping to see the platform sooner.

After getting off, I received a text message from Ale Collins. He said, "Things definitely won't just end like this. Just wait and see. We can bet, and the loser has to treat the winner to a night at MGM, capped at $200." Collins is an independent automotive industry observer I met in Detroit. We got acquainted while waiting for a taxi downstairs from the General Motors building. Later, we gradually started chatting, and he shared his views on Congress and the White House's bailout of the Big Three American automakers. He said that no matter how the federal government helps the Big Three, the U.S. auto industry will undergo significant changes, and even the rescue policy itself will come with various restrictions. When I saw reports indicating that results would be out this week, he refuted me and was willing to make a bet by playing a game at the MGM hotel casino. Of course, it would be a simple game of slot machines, each costing 20 cents, with a total cap not exceeding $200. I am completely inexperienced in these matters, having only played once during an accidental trip to Detroit, where I made a small profit. As a friend who traveled with me put it, I hit a stroke of dogshit luck. Knowing I had such an experience, Collins hoped to play this game with me again. Or maybe he didn't want our conversations to always revolve around cars, leaving no other choice but this simple game as a viable option.

This is merely one way of observing the automotive industry. The number of issues between Detroit and Washington that need debating is as vast as the media's interpretation of this event. Until the final result is announced, things have roughly progressed through these stages: First, in October 2008, when the Big Three released their previous month's sales figures, they all showed varying degrees of losses. At this time, due to the intensification of the financial tsunami, the U.S. financial market experienced what was called a "historic crisis." The Big Three felt they also needed federal assistance, further stating the duration their company cash flows could sustain operations. Then, by early November, Detroit began direct contact with Washington regarding the aid, which was opposed by the Bush administration. Detroit let out cries of woe. When the CEOs of the Big Three arrived in Washington on company jets, the situation turned into the CEOs flying private jets to seek help. After the verbal sparring, Washington wanted Detroit to provide reasons worthy of their generous support. Recently, things have advanced quickly but occasionally stagnated.

However, I always feel that Washington won't let Detroit go bankrupt. Just read Nikkei News' interpretation of this crisis; they believe the federal government won't let the American auto industry collapse. This made me very happy. But after seeing various news reports, I always wonder what's next. Since I'm currently working on a special feature at the newspaper about the 30th anniversary of reform and opening-up, I don't have time for a thorough analysis of the situation. I can only look at various materials and observe the development of events. Therefore, I also want to read Sloan's works.

Then I thought, the General Motors and American automobile described in Sloan's writing are already a distant past, at least the GM of the 1960s. How much connection does today's GM have with the 1960s? I haven't fully figured it out yet. So, when looking at today's GM, I chose to watch from afar.

Who can guarantee that Detroit's current actions aren't part of a negotiation game with Washington?

And then, looking at China's current auto market, similar situations are happening with GM. For instance, the fuel tax. After the announced plan was released, opinions varied among different parties. However, after CCTV's explanation came out, things might become clearer. The current fuel tax standard in China did not align with domestic standards but instead chose the higher European standard. Of course, this can be said. Then, take a look at the arguments supporting the fuel tax. They basically express support for this reform, using language anyone could guess with their eyes closed—phrases reminiscent of Cultural Revolution slogans. Chinese people are already very familiar with such rhetoric, which has even become part of their character and lives. In short, "change is better than no change." Who knows what they're really talking about? The opposing side makes a bit more sense, saying this reform benefits certain groups but doesn't address the public's expenses. But who knows how the public spends their money? Regardless, one thing remains unchanged: the oil groups will definitely not sit idly by in this reform; otherwise, the fuel tax would have been implemented long ago.

So, who can guarantee that the fuel tax isn't a game of lobbying among various interest groups and the government?

If possible, Detroit could claim that "national conditions" have caused their difficulties, especially prominent in negotiations with the United Auto Workers union, making them bear more pressure and higher costs in America compared to Japanese and European manufacturers. This is determined by national conditions. After criticizing Washington politicians enough, Detroit surely hopes those politicians understand their pain, but the reasons given by those politicians say, how can taxpayers' money be spent recklessly? In short, everyone follows established rules. Where does the money come from, and where does it need to go? There needs to be a clear definition.

Of course, China's expertise in using "national conditions" determines that the process of introducing the fuel tax is a difficult one, with data showing it took about 20 years. How long exactly? Taxpayers would never know. With "national conditions" as the backdrop, the new fuel tax rule must consider the interests of the oil groups. In the media, many experts and scholars have raised questions. But these doubts won't affect the progress of the matter. That is to say, they can speak up, but they can't change anything.

The fuel tax rule is essentially a game of lobbying among interest groups.
