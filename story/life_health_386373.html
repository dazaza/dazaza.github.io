The black patches on the teeth may be decay, be careful.

by 454kq on 2011-03-22 10:39:45

Dental caries is a progressive lesion of hard tooth tissue caused by the combined effects of various factors in the oral cavity. It manifests as demineralization of inorganic substances and decomposition of organic matter, evolving from color changes to substantial damage as the disease progresses. Dental caries is a bacterial disease and can therefore lead to pulpitis and periapical periodontitis, and may even cause inflammation of the alveolar bone and jawbone. Secondary infections from dental caries can form focal points that may cause or exacerbate arthritis, endocarditis, chronic nephritis, and various eye diseases, among other systemic conditions. Prevention through health care should be the primary focus for dental caries.

In clinical terms, based on the extent of tooth destruction, dental caries can be divided into shallow caries, moderate caries, and deep caries: ①Shallow caries: The erosion is limited to the enamel, initially showing brown or black spots or patches on the enamel, followed by surface damage, usually without any noticeable symptoms; ②Moderate caries: The erosion has reached the dentin, forming a shallow cavity in the dentin. The tooth feels sensitive when exposed to cold water, cold air, or sweet and sour foods; ③Deep caries: The erosion has reached the deep layer of the dentin, approaching or affecting the pulp. The tooth feels pain when exposed to cold, heat, sweetness, or acidity, especially sensitive to heat. At this point, root canal treatment should be considered.

Dental caries is a multifactorial disease, and its causes mainly include three aspects: bacteria, diet, teeth, and saliva. These three factors are interrelated, and the absence of any one factor cannot lead to dental caries. Scholars currently believe that saliva, as the external environment of the teeth, is an important factor influencing the development of caries.

1. Bacteria

During the occurrence and development of dental caries, bacteria play a dominant role. Therefore, internationally recognized types of cariogenic bacteria are numerous, with the most important being certain strains of Streptococcus mutans and Lactobacillus. These bacteria mix with mucins in saliva and food debris, firmly adhering to the tooth surface and fissures. This adhesive substance is called dental plaque. The large number of bacteria in the plaque produce acid, causing demineralization and dissolution of the enamel surface beneath the plaque. Clinical surveys have proven that children with more plaque in their mouths also tend to have more cavities.

2. Diet

In the process of cavity formation, diet serves as an important substrate for bacteria. Food contains large amounts of carbohydrates and sugars, which not only supply the bacteria in plaque with energy for living and activity but also, through bacterial metabolism, cause sugar fermentation to produce organic acids. These acids remain on the tooth surface and fissures for long periods, leading to enamel demineralization and destruction. Subsequently, some bacteria dissolve proteins to form cavities. Many types of sugar can cause cavities, with sucrose being the most significant.

During the development of teeth, nutrition determines the biochemical structure of the dental tissues. Teeth with good calcification have higher resistance to cavities. If there are insufficient mineral salts, main vitamins, and trace elements such as calcium, phosphorus, vitamin B1, D, and fluoride in the diet, the teeth's resistance to cavities will be low, creating conditions for the onset of cavities. Baby teeth begin to develop, grow, and calcify during the fetal stage. Although maternal nutrition during lactation does not have a decisive impact on the development of fetal baby teeth, enhancing maternal nutrition still benefits the calcification of baby teeth. Unless the mother suffers from severe metabolic disorders or genetic diseases, baby teeth generally are not severely affected.

3. Teeth

The shape, structure, and position of teeth have a clear relationship with the onset of cavities. The pits and fissures on the biting surfaces of teeth are defects left over from the developmental process. Deep fissures easily retain cells and food debris and are difficult to clean, making them prone to causing cavities. Teeth with insufficient mineralization, particularly those with inadequate calcification, have low density in the enamel and dentin, resulting in low resistance to cavities and a high likelihood of developing cavities. Although fluoride content in the mineralized structure of teeth is minimal, it plays a crucial role in enhancing the teeth's resistance to cavities. Teeth containing an appropriate amount of fluoride are less likely to develop cavities. The structure and degree of calcification of deciduous teeth and young permanent teeth are not yet fully mature, making them susceptible to cariogenic factors and resulting in a high incidence of cavities.

Saliva serves as the external environment for teeth, playing roles such as buffering, washing, anti-decay, or bacteriostasis. Saliva that is abundant and thin can wash the tooth surface, reducing the accumulation of bacteria and food debris. Saliva that is scarce and thick tends to linger, promoting the formation and attachment of plaque on the tooth surface. The nature and composition of saliva affect its buffering capacity and also influence the living conditions of bacteria.

Understanding the characteristics of cavities allows for targeted prevention and treatment from several aspects:

1. Reducing or eliminating pathogenic irritants: The most practical and effective method is brushing and rinsing. It is recommended to rinse after each meal and brush twice daily, morning and night. For infants, parents can use soft towels or flannel to clean the teeth. Children aged three and above can try brushing their own teeth.

2. Reducing or controlling the sugar content in the diet: Parents should educate children to develop habits of eating fewer snacks and sweets, especially before bedtime. Attention should be paid to the proper combination of three meals for children, cultivating the habit of consuming more vegetables, fruits, and foods rich in calcium, phosphorus, and vitamins. Additionally, breastfeeding should be emphasized for infants.

3. Enhancing the teeth's resistance to cavities: This is primarily achieved through fluoridation methods to increase the fluoride content in teeth, especially altering the structure of the enamel surface to enhance the teeth's resistance to cavities. Currently, the fluoridation methods considered to be more effective include fluoridated tap water, school drinking water fluoridation, fluoride application on teeth, fluoride toothpaste brushing, and fluoride solution rinsing. Any fluoridation method for preventing cavities can only be used in areas where the water source lacks fluoride or has low fluoride levels, and should not be used in high-fluoride areas. Additionally, children with dental fluorosis should not use fluoridation methods.
