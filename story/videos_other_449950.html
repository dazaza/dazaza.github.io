Man miraculously survived after falling from the 16th floor and was discharged from the hospital after recovery

by zzf000zxyn7 on 2011-06-22 10:36:58

Xinhua News Agency, Nanjing, February 8th (reported by Zhu Xudong) - Normally, falling from the 16th floor to the ground would result in fatal injuries due to the immense impact force. However, Xu from Nanjing survived a fall from the 16th floor and was successfully rescued by the Jiangsu Provincial Hospital of Traditional Chinese Medicine. The patient has now recovered and been discharged, creating a remarkable medical miracle.

According to the patient, Mr. Xu, more than 20 days ago one morning, for various reasons, he "drifted" down from the 16th floor. Just before hitting the ground, his back struck the windshield of a car, and he finally landed on the cement. Staff at the Jiangsu Provincial Hospital of Traditional Chinese Medicine stated that when the patient was brought to the hospital by the emergency services, his blood pressure could not be measured, he had no breathing, his heartbeat was extremely weak, both pupils were dilated, and his thigh was broken into three pieces. Due to the tremendous impact force, shards of glass were embedded deeply into his back, and he showed almost no signs of life.

The hospital quickly opened a green channel for emergency rescue, with multiple departments including the emergency internal and external medicine, anesthesiology, and ICU working together to save the patient. First, the anesthesiologists quickly intubated the patient to establish an airway, maintaining his breathing using a mechanical ventilator. Meanwhile, the nursing staff rapidly established multiple intravenous channels to administer fluids, raise blood pressure, and perform cardiopulmonary resuscitation. Within three minutes, the patient's airway was clear, within ten minutes, his blood pressure could be detected, and after half an hour, the patient's blood pressure began to slowly rise, his heartbeat stabilized. Further blood transfusions and fluid replacement gradually normalized his blood pressure. At this point, the patient was still unconscious. Based on clinical observations, doctors suspected possible damage to the brain and other organs, requiring immediate further examination. After CT scans and other tests, it was found that the patient had severe cranial injuries, subarachnoid hemorrhage, brainstem swelling, bilateral hemothorax, lungs compressed into a "bean pancake" shape, multiple rib fractures in the chest, and both legs broken into several segments. For a patient with such serious injuries to vital organs like the heart, lungs, and brain, even though the blood pressure improved, failure of any one organ could still be fatal.

To save the patient, the medical staff quickly transferred him to the intensive care unit. In the following two weeks, due to the severe cranial injuries, the medical team administered intracranial dehydration treatment; as the entire chest cavity was filled with bloody fluid and the lung lost its respiratory function, the doctors performed closed thoracic drainage; simultaneously, the orthopedic surgeons fixed the fractures. Through the full efforts of the medical staff, the patient eventually crossed the threshold between life and death.

Ma Zhaoqun, director of the Emergency Surgery Department at the hospital, told reporters that theoretically, surviving a fall from a 16-story building is extremely unlikely, and there are currently no statistical data available on such cases. This man truly represents a medical miracle. The successful rescue of this individual can be attributed to several factors: first, his back hit the ground and was cushioned by the car windshield, reducing the degree of injury to non-fatal levels instantly; second, he was promptly taken to the hospital; third, the hospital's emergency green channel was smooth, enabling collaboration among multiple departments where medical personnel raced against time to implement comprehensive and accurate diagnostic rescues, ultimately buying precious time for the patient's survival.
