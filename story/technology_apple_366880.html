Desert Jobs: What did NeXT teach Jobs?

by diglog on 2010-10-04 05:04:35

**[Original Source: The New York Times, Author: Randall Stross, Original Link]**

The legend of Steve Jobs has become so well-known that it is now part of the mythology of this country: the prodigal son who returned to Apple in 1997 and, through a series of right decisions made at the right time, transformed it into the world's most valuable company.

However, if Jobs had not gone through that grueling business odyssey, the Steve Jobs of the mid-1980s might not have been able to bring Apple to its current level of success.

(Pictured: Steve Jobs in 1999, after returning to Apple. What would Apple look like today if he had never left?)

Larry Ellison, president of Oracle Corporation, summarized this period when reflecting on those years. In August of this year, after Mark Hurd was forced to resign as CEO of HP, Ellison wrote in an email to The New York Times that the HP board had made "the worst personnel decision in Silicon Valley since the morons on Apple's board fired Steve Jobs many years ago."

In fact, the co-founder of Apple (Steve Jobs) was not fired but had his operational duties removed during a corporate restructuring in May 1985. He remained chairman of the company. At that time, Apple was already showing signs of weakness: the sales of the Macintosh, launched a year earlier, were declining and far below expectations; there was excess inventory; and the company seemed poised for its first-ever loss. In September 1985, Jobs resigned from Apple and founded a new computer company he called Next (the next step).

Assume that in 1985, Jobs had not left Apple. Assume that Jobs convinced the Apple board, ousted his rival John Sculley, and took over as both CEO and chairman of the company. Would Apple, under Jobs' uninterrupted leadership, have reached the same heights it has today? Would it have happened just 12 years earlier?

It’s hard to imagine how the story would have unfolded under these circumstances. However, the Steve Jobs who returned to Apple in 1997 was clearly a more capable leader — more precisely, because he had experienced a significant fall. He spent 12 painful, tumultuous years without finding a profitable path forward for his new venture.

"I firmly believe that if it weren't for the 'wilderness experience' at NeXT, the Steve Jobs who returned to Apple wouldn’t have been as successful as he became," said Tim Bajarin, president of the technology consulting firm Creative Strategies.

Jobs founded NeXT — spelled with a capital "N" and "T" — with the intention of targeting the higher education market by developing high-performance computers for college students and faculty, which he described as "personal mainframes."

University advisors had suggested to Jobs that the price of the computer should be kept under $2,000 (at the time, Macs sold to colleges at a discount for only $1,000). However, when the NeXT machine was finally unveiled in 1988, it cost $6,500. Additionally, its performance wasn't strong enough, and the accompanying printer cost another $2,000.
