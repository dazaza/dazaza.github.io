When the pursuit of higher product quality had become a thriving force for reform in trade and manufacturing, Philip Crosby wrote the best-selling book "Quality is Free." In the 1980s, about 40% of the Fortune Global 500 companies consulted Crosby's firm on issues related to quality management. His prominence in the field of management consulting was partly due to his ability to express quality management concepts in an accessible manner. This ability was undoubtedly the result of over 40 years of management experience.

**Biography**

Philip Crosby was born in West Virginia in 1926. He graduated from Western Reserve University and served in the Korean War. In 1952, Crosby began his career with quality management work on a production line. While serving as Quality Manager at Martin Marietta, he introduced the concept of "Zero Defects." Later, he worked as Vice President and Quality Manager at International Telephone and Telegraph Corporation (ITT) for 14 years.

In 1979, the publication of "Quality is Free" highlighted Crosby's personal interests, which led him to leave ITT, establish PCA, and begin teaching principles and practices of quality management to businesses. By 1985, his company's assets had exceeded $30 million. In 1991, after retiring from PCA, he founded Career IV, a company that provided consulting and advice to help current and future corporate executives develop. Crosby passed away in August 2001.

**Key Ideas**

Crosby emphasized that quality is neither elusive nor immeasurable. It is strategically necessary, quantifiable, and its bottom line can be improved. For Crosby, traditional quality control methods that define an "acceptable" level of quality are evidence of failure, not guarantees of success. The goal of quality control should be meeting all requirements on time, every time, from the first attempt. Therefore, the focus of quality control should be on prevention rather than inspection and rework.

Crosby's approach to quality management is clear-cut. He viewed abstract concepts like good, bad, high, or low quality as meaningless. The true meaning of quality, according to Crosby, is "conformance to requirements." Conformance means products should meet the specifications set by the company based on customer needs. He also believed that the primary responsibility for defects lies with managers, not workers, as standards are initially set by management.

Non-conforming products are those that managers fail to specify clearly or control effectively. The cost of non-conformance refers to problems arising early in production that are not corrected later, leading to wasted costs. "Zero Defects" does not mean people never make mistakes but that businesses should not accept errors as inevitable or allow standards to be lowered. Instead, work should be seen as a series of activities or processes with clear requirements that ultimately produce acceptable results.

According to Crosby's estimates, if a company allows defects (which must be reworked), it spends approximately 20%-35% of its total revenue on its quality system.

**The 14 Steps of Quality Management**

1. **Establish a Quality Management Committee:** Improvement in quality must be recognized by management and designed into a plan aimed at preventing defects. Quality improvement is equated with profit increase. The strategy can be expressed as: "Everyone should work appropriately according to rules, and when we and the consumers feel a need for change, these rules will be revised by management."

2. **Form a Quality Improvement Team:** Representatives from various departments within the company should form this team and have sufficient authority to represent their respective departments.

3. **Conduct Quality Audits:** The state of quality should be measured throughout the company. This means establishing and recording audit findings at each stage of production to identify where improvements can be made.

4. **Evaluate Quality Costs:** While quality costs do not need to be calculated with absolute precision, identifying areas for action can improve profitability.

5. **Increase Quality Awareness:** Employees should understand the high costs associated with defects. This can be achieved through training, communication, and highlighting the benefits of quality improvement.

6. **Encourage Error Correction:** Discussing problems not only finds solutions but inspires improvements in other areas.

7. **Create a Special Committee for the Zero Defects Program:** The program aims to instill the idea that everyone should do their job correctly from the start.

8. **Train Managers:** Managers should receive formal training on the "14 Steps" before implementing them.

9. **Designate a Zero Defects Day:** This day serves as a reminder of the new attitude towards quality.

10. **Set Goals:** All managers should require their subordinates to set specific, appropriate, and achievable goals, typically including 30-day, 60-day, and 90-day objectives.

11. **Eliminate Causes of Errors:** Employees are encouraged to describe any obstacles to smooth work completion on a one-page form. Issues should be addressed within 24 hours.

12. **Recognition:** Acknowledging employees who achieve goals or perform exceptionally well is important, though recognition should not be limited to financial rewards.

13. **Hold Quality Seminars:** Experts and team leaders should regularly discuss quality improvement and product upgrades.

14. **Repeat the Zero Defects Program:** After a typical 12-18 month cycle, repeating and adjusting steps reinforces quality awareness within the company.

**Later Research**

In his 1984 book "Quality Without Tears," Crosby developed the concept of "inoculation serum" for quality management, consisting of:

- Integrity among CEO, all managers, and employees.

- Systems for verifying product compliance, educating employees and suppliers, enabling systematic production improvement and defect prevention.

- Communication systems to recognize problems, broadcast progress, and acknowledge achievements.

- Business operations organized such that processes, products, and systems are validated before execution and continuously tested during implementation.

- Clear policies prioritizing quality in the organization.

In his 1988 book "The Eternally Successful Organization," Crosby expanded on improving quality, emphasizing five key factors for business success:

- Doing things right the first time.

- Predictable change used to maximize benefits.

- Stable, beneficial growth.

- Providing new products and services when new needs arise.

- Making it enjoyable for everyone to work at the company.

**Evaluation of Philip Crosby**

From Crosby's writings, his ideas are characterized by four main features:

- Quality means conformance to requirements, not perfection.

- Prevention, not appraisal, is key to achieving quality.

- Zero Defects is a quality standard, not an acceptable level of defects.

- Quality measurement is based on the cost incurred due to different standards of products.

Terms like "Zero Defects," "Doing it Right the First Time," and "Conformance to Requirements" reflect Crosby's significant contribution to management thought. These terms are now both specialized quality management terminology and widely used in general management discussions.

Although Crosby's name is not always mentioned alongside Deming, one of the most famous quality management thinkers, it is almost certain that the next most renowned quality management thinker remembered will be Crosby. His practical and accessible writings on quality management have become and will continue to be the "bible" for many companies, clarifying much of the confusion around complex quality management jargon. His timing was appropriate for quality management, and his works have spread quality management ideas to a broader audience.
