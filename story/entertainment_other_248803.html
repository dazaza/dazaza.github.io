The highest bridge in the world

by cqflw on 2009-10-17 21:35:48

This photo taken on May 28, 2004 shows the Millau Viaduct located in southwestern France. On that day, the world's tallest bridge - the Millau Viaduct - completed its final alignment. The bridge deck is 270 meters above the ground, and the highest point of the cable stays is 343 meters above the ground, which is 23 meters taller than the Eiffel Tower.

Xinhua News Agency, Paris, December 14, 2004 (reporter: Yang Jun) - The Millau Viaduct located in the Tarn River Valley in southern France was officially completed on December 14. It is currently the world's tallest bridge, with a vertical distance between the deck and the ground at its lowest point reaching 270 meters. French President Jacques Chirac attended the completion and ribbon-cutting ceremony of the bridge on the morning of December 14.

The Millau Viaduct is named after the city of Millau in southwestern France and is a long cable-stayed bridge. Despite having a total length of 2.46 kilometers, it is supported by only seven piers, among which the second and third piers are respectively 245 meters and 220 meters tall, making them the world's tallest two piers. If the bridge tower used to support the cable stays above the pier is included, the tallest pier reaches 343 meters, surpassing the famous Eiffel Tower in Paris, France by 23 meters.

The total weight of the bridge is 290,000 tons, and the steel structure deck alone weighs 36,000 tons. How does such a massive structure standing high between mountains and rivers ensure its stability? How can the bridge avoid trembling or swaying like a string under wind force, or even collapsing? The chief engineer of the bridge construction project, Gernay, introduced to the media that the construction of this bridge utilized the most advanced technology available today. The chief designer from the UK, Norman Foster, designed the bridge deck into a triangular shape to effectively reduce wind resistance. Besides computer simulation tests, outdoor wind simulation experiments began as early as 1997. Experts from the French Meteorological Bureau even built an artificial valley near Toulouse, then poured water into this "valley," mixing many small particles in the water. The experts simulated various complex wind directions that might appear in the Tarn River Valley through changes in the water particles, thus continuously modifying the proportions of various architectural structures of the bridge. Ultimately, the design of the bridge allows it to withstand winds with speeds up to 250 km/h.

Moreover, during construction, whenever the pier rose every 4 meters, experts would use the Global Positioning System to correct any possible deviations. This resulted in the building's vertical error being no more than 5 millimeters after three years of construction, thereby ensuring the accuracy of the construction very well.

It is expected that the future traffic capacity of the bridge will reach an average of 15,000 vehicles per day, and up to 25,000 vehicles per day during peak tourist seasons.
