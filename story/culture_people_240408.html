Our Chinese nation has many traditional festivals, and each festival has a beautiful legend. The following article is about the beautiful legend of the Mid-Autumn Festival: the love version of the custom of eating moon cakes on the Mid-Autumn Festival - Chang'e and Houyi. The customs and ways of celebrating these festivals reflect China's long history, culture, and traditions.

The 15th day of the eighth lunar month is a traditional Chinese folk festival - the Mid-Autumn Festival. An important activity during the Mid-Autumn Festival is moon gazing. On this night, the bright moon is in the sky, and the autumn wind brings coolness. After working hard for a whole year, people see their crops ready to be harvested and feel full of joy. Families gather together to admire the moon, eat moon cakes, and celebrate the Mid-Autumn Festival.

Regarding the origin of the Mid-Autumn Festival, experts believe that it evolved from ancient ceremonies to honor the First Farmers. After a bountiful harvest, people would first offer sacrifices to the First Farmers before harvesting their crops. Later, many legends about the Mid-Autumn Festival appeared, with the most widely spread being the story of "Chang'e Flying to the Moon." In ancient times, people regarded the bright moon as a symbol of auspiciousness and reunion. Therefore, the Mid-Autumn Festival is also called the Reunion Festival. Since the 15th day of the eighth lunar month falls right in the middle of autumn, it is named the Mid-Autumn Festival.

The Legend of Chang'e Flying to the Moon

A long, long time ago, there were ten suns in the sky. They scorched the land until it smoked, dried up the crops, and made life unbearable for the common people. At that time, there was a man named Yi. He had incredible strength and could draw a ten-thousand-pound divine bow to shoot down giant snakes and fierce beasts. Out of sympathy for the suffering people, he used all his might to draw his bow and arrows, shooting down nine suns in one breath. The last sun, terrified, admitted its mistake, and Yi let it stay, ordering it to rise early and set late, doing more good deeds for the people.

After that, Yi's name spread across the land. He became a hero admired by everyone. Later, Yi married a girl named Chang'e, and they lived happily, loving each other dearly.

Chang'e was beautiful, diligent, and kind-hearted. She often shared the wild animals that Yi hunted with the villagers. One day, while Yi was hunting in the mountains, he met a Taoist priest. The priest said to Yi, "You have done great deeds for the people. I will give you a packet of divine medicine. If you take half of it, you will live forever; if you take it all, you will ascend to heaven and become immortal." Yi took the divine medicine home and entrusted it to Chang'e for safekeeping. He planned to find a suitable time to share the divine medicine with Chang'e so that they could both live forever and never be separated.

After Yi became famous for shooting down the suns, many people came to learn martial arts from him. Among his disciples was a man named Feng Meng, who learned archery from him. Feng Meng appeared respectful to Yi on the surface, but he had a wicked heart. Knowing that Yi's home contained divine medicine, he secretly plotted evil intentions. This year, on the 15th day of the eighth lunar month, Yi went hunting with his disciples.

As evening approached, Feng Meng sneaked back alone, broke into Chang'e's house, and forced her to hand over the divine medicine. Chang'e shouted loudly, but Yi was still out hunting and couldn't hear her. To prevent the divine medicine from falling into the hands of the despicable Feng Meng, Chang'e opened the packet and swallowed all the medicine. Suddenly, Chang'e felt her body become as light as a swallow, and she involuntarily flew out of the window, floating up into the sky. Reluctant to leave her family and home, Chang'e flew to the moon, which was closest to the earth, and stayed in the Palace of Eternal Cold.

When Yi returned home and heard about what happened, he rushed out of the door to chase after her, but it was too late! All he could see was Chang'e's faint shadow on the round moon. The villagers said that Chang'e would return and encouraged Yi to wait patiently. The next year, on the 15th day of the eighth lunar month, Yi missed Chang'e and took out the fruits she loved, placing them in the courtyard, and made round moon cakes to express his wish for reunion, hoping that Chang'e would come back. Year after year passed, and Chang'e still didn't return. Gradually, the tradition of celebrating festivals developed, and every year on the 15th day of the eighth lunar month, people prepared moon cakes, fruits, and other foods.
