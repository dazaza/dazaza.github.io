Some college students over-rely on computers and become "Mr. Wrong Character"

by wszdw142 on 2008-04-14 00:20:36

Every year between March and April is the peak period for current college graduates to seek employment, there has always been a saying of "golden three silver four". However, some college students are not solid in basic skills, with pages full of misspelled words, becoming truly worthy "white character masters", bringing themselves no small obstacles in job hunting. Xiaoxie is a senior student from a certain university; he told reporters about his job-hunting experience.

Xiaoxie said that on March 28th, he received a notice to go attend an interview at a Fortune 500 company. The interviewer took out a form and had him fill out his resume. When he finished filling it out and handed it over to the interviewer, the person looked at it for a while and couldn't help but laugh. It turned out that in the mere hundred plus characters of the resume, he wrote "current graduate" as "should graduate"; in his major, he wrote the left side of "tong" in "statistics" as the left side of "you" in "young"; when describing himself, he wrote "outgoing personality" as "personality open wheat"; and the use of "de" was completely mixed up. When the other party pointed out these errors one by one, Xiaoxie immediately felt too ashamed to face anyone, explaining: "Normally at school when completing assignments and papers, I basically rely on typing on the computer, feeling a bit awkward when picking up the pen after such a long time without writing."

Although the interviewers from the company didn't outright reject Xiaoxie, they still expressed dissatisfaction towards him in their words. After the interview ended, the interviewer told him: "In today's world where so many people are seeking jobs, the first impression made on employers is very crucial. Although misspelled words cannot reflect a person's true abilities, the old saying goes 'a person's handwriting reflects their character', which can more or less reflect a person's cultivation and inner quality." When Xiaoxie recounted this incident to the reporter, he was still filled with regret, unintentionally letting misspelled words become a stumbling block in his job hunt. Besides misspelled words, his handwriting was often criticized as looking like a primary school student's, "like crab tracks."

Xiaoyang, who just found a job, also shares similar feelings. She told the reporter that now many graduates are enthusiastic about taking civil service exams. A few of her classmates encountered a problem when writing essays, wanting to use a few idioms in their articles to embellish their literary style, but for some reason, although some commonly used idioms were on the tip of their tongues, they couldn't remember how to write them, like "鞠躬尽瘁" (devote oneself wholeheartedly) and "锲而不舍" (persevere tirelessly). After the exam, everyone talked about this matter, all blaming themselves for being too reliant on computers normally, leaving them at a loss when asked to write by hand.

After investigating multiple university graduates, the reporter found that most students have gotten used to "computerized" learning: looking up materials, writing papers, chatting online almost all abandon writing by hand. Moreover, many teachers require either USB drives or printed copies for homework submissions, none of which require handwriting. One student admitted that once away from the computer, his brain feels blank, saying that if he were to take the college entrance exam now, his essay score would definitely not reach what it was before.

An interviewer from a foreign company sighed and said that in the recruitment fairs they participated in, only about one-third of the resumes received met the requirements. Since they don't worry about not finding satisfactory employees, when they see resumes full of misspelled words, they often feel nauseated and throw the resume aside immediately. But at the same time, they are concerned about the current generation of university students becoming "white character masters."

The reporter learned that currently due to fierce competition in the job market, most graduates' resumes are beautifully printed manuscripts. Once they are required to write by hand, their true colors are revealed. Therefore, here we remind all graduates, before job hunting, first strengthen your basic skills, don't let your first career fall because of writing.
