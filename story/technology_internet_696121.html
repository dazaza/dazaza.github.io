The Development Path and Strategic Choices of Chinese Industry

by cxxzsty on 2012-03-07 20:48:32

[Abstract] As a developing country, during a certain period of economic development, China's industrialization process will inevitably manifest as the transfer of industries from developed industrialized countries. Therefore, the development of most industries in China will go through three stages: infancy, maturity, and robustness. In order to expand towards the third stage of industrial development, corporate strategies can roughly be divided into three choices: brand-oriented strategy, capacity-oriented strategy, and opportunistic strategy. The key to strategic choice may not lie in which strategy is more correct or preferable, but rather who has the motivation to implement a certain strategy. Corporate strategy selection does not merely shift based on interest concerns but is jointly determined by pure rational factors and supra-rational factors. In a certain sense, pure rationality is secular and undifferentiated rationality, while supra-rationality is faith-based and differentiated rationality. Therefore, corporate strategy selection is not merely the result of pure rational logical deduction, but also an [ideal] pursuit and [mission] drive based on certain values. National industrial development strategy selection also shares some similarities, always based on a certain rational foundation. This rationality does not merely use pure rational judgment as a criterion but necessarily includes strong supra-rational factors, including ethnic, ethical, and various humanistic value standards. Therefore, a feasible industrial development strategy must be based on a scientific development outlook, whose value not only conforms to objective laws but also meets certain socially agreeable goals.

[Keywords] Industrial Development; Corporate Strategy; National Strategy; Competitiveness

Entering the 21st century, China's economic development exhibits a series of very significant changes, especially with the development of various industries seemingly presenting increasingly clear trend trajectories. From the perspective of output, China has become a major industrial production country. If calculated by purchasing power parity rather than currency exchange rates, China has almost entered the ranks of medium-developed countries. However, all these observational conclusions are based on one assumption: that China's industrialization trajectory is consistent with that of other countries, and the history of industrialization experienced by China is the same as what other developed countries have gone through at corresponding stages of industrialization. The problem is that such an assumption may differ greatly from reality. From the entire historical process of human industrialization, China's industrialization is a very unique process, where phenomena occurring and about to occur are unprecedented in other countries, and unique problems faced have not been encountered by other countries. During this process, both the strategic choices of Chinese enterprises and national industrial development strategies will face many significant theoretical and practical issues. Deeply understanding the significance of these issues and fundamentally establishing the right concepts and principles for recognizing and handling relevant strategic issues are of great importance for China's future path of industrialization. This article mainly discusses several issues directly related to industrial development, leaving broader issues for further research and discussion in the future.

I. General Trends and Stage Characteristics of China's Industrial Development

As a developing country, within a certain period of economic development, the industrialization process of China will inevitably manifest as the transfer of industries from developed industrialized countries. That is, accepting the gradual transfer of global industries constitutes the main content of China's industrialization process over a relatively long period. Therefore, the development of most industries in China will go through three stages.

The first stage is the industry's [infancy], characterized generally by products being [low-quality low-price] (while monopolistic industries may exhibit high prices with low quality). During this period, the production technology of the industry is at a very low level, product grades are low, and added value is also low (while monopolistic industries may show very high monopoly profits). The characteristic of enterprise competition during this period is imitative competition, where products are mostly copies of outdated foreign varieties and often suffer from defects such as [cutting corners] and [making do]. Thus, industries at this stage of development, even if they are export products of relatively better quality, can only be placed in the lowest-tier stores abroad. However, due to the typically thin market during this period, all competing producers are quite weak, so the competitive pressure on production enterprises is not very large, the development space is relatively broad, and life seems relatively easy.
