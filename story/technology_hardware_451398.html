In the minds of global consumers, Marlboro (Malboro) is undoubtedly one of the most famous and charming international brands. In terms of sales, globally, on average, as many as 1 million Marlboro cigarettes are consumed every minute! Whether you smoke or not, Marlboro's global image and charm leave a deep impression on you, making it unforgettable.

Hardly anyone would have thought that the globally popular Marlboro cigarette started as a small shop in 1854, officially registered as the Malboro brand in the United States in 1908, and only established Philip Morris & Company in 1919, which almost went bankrupt in the 1940s.

In the early days of Marlboro’s business, it was positioned as a "lady's cigarette," with the vast majority of its consumers being women. Its advertising slogan was: "As mild as May weather." However, despite the fact that the number of smokers in the U.S. increased year by year during this period, Marlboro’s sales remained stagnant. Women complained that the white cigarette tips would stain their red lipstick, looking very unattractive. So, Morris Company changed the tip to red. But none of these measures could save the fate of Marlboro's lady cigarettes. The Morris Company finally ceased production of Marlboro cigarettes in the early 1940s.

After World War II, the number of American smokers continued to increase. Marlboro reintroduced the newly developed filtered cigarettes back into the female cigarette market and launched three series: one plain version, one with white and red filters, and another advertised as "matching your lips and fingertips." At that time, the annual U.S. cigarette consumption reached 382 billion cigarettes, with each consumer smoking an average of 2,262 cigarettes per year. However, Marlboro’s sales were still poor, with few smokers choosing Marlboro, and even fewer people knowing about the brand.

The Mastermind Surgeon: Leo Burnett

In a state of helplessness, in 1954, Morris Company turned to the then very famous marketing strategist Leo Burnett, entrusting him with the task of figuring out how to get more women to buy and consume Marlboro cigarettes.

Dialectical Thinking on Limited Tasks

As the executor of this marketing task, Leo Burnett faced such resource conditions: the already determined Marlboro cigarette product, packaging, etc. He also faced the task of making more women familiar with, like, and ultimately purchase Marlboro cigarettes. Leo Burnett had to find ways to accomplish this task within such resource constraints. How to solve this problem?

If Leo Burnett, I am saying "if," had strictly adhered to the task proposed by Morris Company and the given resources, following the line of thinking to expand the share of the female cigarette market, then the globally popular Marlboro would never have appeared in this economic world. Fortunately, Leo Burnett did not let himself be confined by the tasks and resources, but instead conducted dialectical thinking on the subject provided by Morris Company.

Bold Reforms Transform Marlboro

After conducting a profound analysis and careful consideration of the cigarette market, Leo Burnett completely broke free from the tasks and resources restricted by Morris Company, performing a completely new "sex change surgery" on Marlboro. He boldly proposed to Morris Company: reposition Marlboro cigarettes as a manly cigarette, changing the light flavor to a heavier taste, increasing the fragrance content, and boldly reforming Marlboro's image: adopting the then pioneering flat-opening box cover technology for packaging and using red, symbolizing strength, as the main color of the outer box. A major shift in advertising was: Marlboro cigarette advertisements no longer targeted women as the primary audience. The advertisements repeatedly emphasized the manly aura of Marlboro cigarettes, using the image of a rugged, bold, heroic American cowboy to attract all consumers who admire and pursue such an aura.

This remains one of the most successful and significant marketing strategies ever. Due to Leo Burnett's bold strategy that broke through the limitations of resources and tasks, the fate of Morris Company was completely transformed. In the second year after Marlboro's brand, marketing, and advertising strategies were changed according to Leo Burnett's strategic ideas (1955), Marlboro cigarettes leaped to the 10th rank among U.S. cigarette brands in terms of sales, and subsequently kept advancing. Today, Marlboro has become the world's second-largest brand after Coca-Cola, with a brand value of as high as $500 billion.

Breaking Through Limited Tasks in Marketing Thought

Leo Burnett deserves to be called a great master strategist. His greatness lies not only in the myth of Marlboro he created but also in his practical demonstration of the philosophy of marketing strategy: strategy lies in the dialectical thinking of resources and tasks. This philosophy reveals the essence of marketing strategy.

Marketing Strategists Need to Break Linear Planning

When Leo Burnett initially took over Marlboro, Morris Company gave him limited tasks and resources. As a common idea, strategizing could only proceed under such limitations. What makes Leo Burnett most valuable is that he conducted repeated revisions and dialectical thinking within the tasks and resources provided by Morris Company, eventually breaking entirely free from the tasks and resources set by Morris Company, ultimately achieving Marlboro's success worldwide.

For marketing strategists, the situation they face is exactly the same as what Leo Burnett faced. Almost every marketing strategist faces a task, which manifests as specific or long or short multi-objective goals. They also face a "resource situation." Their job is to find ways to achieve the task from the "resource situation." However, if there is an obvious path from resources to tasks, so-called strategy is merely a process of linear planning solutions.
