Foxconn's 'leap to death' scandal: how far has it gone?

by kqpziifd on 2010-05-31 00:54:38

"If you don't work hard, then just go and work at Foxconn." A friend recounted his boss's scolding of them with a laugh. From the Foxconn jumpings, it has evolved into the "jumping gate" scandal. What is wrong with Foxconn? Or is there something wrong with the post-80s and post-90s generations? Foxconn has made us aware of what "outsourcing" means. Most people who have newly learned the term "outsourcing" have been informed by Foxconn's "jumping gate". The "jumping gate" has become Foxconn's new image. What is wrong with Foxconn? Does Foxconn have the ability to solve these problems?

On January 23, 2010, a 19-year-old employee named Ma Xiangqian died in the dormitory of Foxconn's South China Training Center. After relentless pursuit by the family and continuous media exposure, the second autopsy concluded "death by falling from height," and the police decided not to file a case.

At 9:30 PM on March 11, 2010, a male worker surnamed Li in his twenties fell to his death from the fifth floor of the C2 dormitory building in the living area of Foxconn's Longhua base. The reason was suspected to be due to stolen overtime pay during the Spring Festival, leading to temporary despair and suicide. Foxconn has yet to comment on the cause of the employees' suicides.

At 8:00 AM on March 17, 2010, a woman surnamed Tian at Foxconn's Longhua Park jumped from the dormitory building and was injured. She stated that the reason for jumping was "living is too tiring."

At 3:00 AM on March 29, 2010, a 23-year-old male employee from Hunan University was found dead in the corridor of the first floor of Building J1 in Foxconn's Longhua Park. He was later identified by the police as having "died from a fall before death."

At around 3:00 PM on April 6, 2010, a female employee surnamed Rao, who was under 19 years old, fell from the seventh floor of Dormitory Building C8 at Foxconn's Guanlan factory. She is still being treated in the hospital. This employee had only been in the factory for 28 days and was a frontline operator. Her fall was initially blocked by tree branches, saving her life temporarily. The preliminary suspicion is that the cause was related to emotional disputes.

At around 5:30 PM on April 7, 2010, an 18-year-old female employee surnamed Ning from Yunnan province fell to her death from an off-campus dormitory building at Foxconn's Guanlan factory. The factory has already reported the incident to the police and notified the family. On the same day, a male employee living in Zhanget Village in Guanlan was found unconscious by his parents who lived with him.

At 4:30 AM on May 6, 2010, another tragedy occurred at Foxconn. A male employee who was only 24 years old and had worked there for less than a year jumped from the balcony of Room 6 on the sixth floor of the reception building at Foxconn's Longhua headquarters, ending his young life.

At around 7:00 PM on May 11, 2010, in a residential building in Shuidou Fuhao New Village in Longhua Street, Bao'an District, Shenzhen, a woman renting a room on the eighth floor suddenly climbed to the roof and jumped without saying a word. She remained motionless on the ground and was confirmed dead on the scene by the 120 emergency medical personnel.

On the evening of May 14, 2010, near the North Gate of Foxconn's Longhua factory in Shenzhen, an employee surnamed Liang fell to his death. He was from Anhui Province and was 21 years old. This was the ninth employee to fall this year, resulting in 7 deaths and 2 injuries. A bloody dagger was found at the scene, and the deceased had four stab wounds. The police have intervened in the investigation.

At 4:50 AM on May 21, 2010, Foxconn experienced its "tenth jump" of the year, with the deceased being a 21-year-old male employee.

In the early hours of May 25, 2010, an employee at Foxconn Technology Group's Guanlan Park in South China Training Center fell to his death. This was the eleventh employee falling incident at the company this year, resulting in 9 deaths and 2 serious injuries.

Based on common sense, no one would wish to end their life unless they are utterly desperate and have no other choice. As employees of a renowned company, even under immense work pressure, these individuals could have chosen to resign and leave. Why did they choose death rather than resignation to "leave" Foxconn? In this regard, the issues reflected by the "serial jumping gate incidents" are not limited to within Foxconn itself, and we cannot place all the blame on Foxconn. So, what is the real nightmare behind the "serial jumping gate"?

While analyzing Foxconn's management problems, we should also focus on other social issues revealed by this situation, including questioning whether the government has failed in its responsibilities. For instance, has the government adequately supervised enterprises? Have labor inspection departments regularly visited factories to understand the living and working conditions of workers? Have they frequently organized cultural and sports activities, legal publicity, and psychological counseling for migrant workers? Has the government specifically established assistance institutions for migrant workers? Have they conducted life education and values education for migrant workers?

The reason for this perspective is that after the "nine consecutive jumps" garnered significant public attention, the local government decided to investigate the employee jumping incidents. Media reports have disclosed that in recent years, there have been employee suicides almost every year in Shenzhen. If Foxconn's indifference to employee lives is a form of cruelty, then what is the government's long-term "silence"?

Foxconn's "serial jumping gate" is not a private issue but a major public concern. We cannot allow more young lives to fade away. Relevant authorities should urgently initiate investigations into Foxconn's "serial jumping gate" incidents. If we still cannot ensure that employees can work with dignity, it will bring shame to "Made in China."
