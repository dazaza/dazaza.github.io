Ansoff Model and Turbulence Management

by okzhuce on 2007-06-29 15:33:04

In order to give himself ample time and energy to summarize strategic management experiences and conduct further in-depth research, Igor Ansoff left Lockheed. In 1963, at the age of 45, Ansoff entered the Graduate School of Industrial Administration at Carnegie Mellon University, beginning his professional research and teaching in strategic management. His experience at Lockheed convinced him that there were practical methods for making decisions within a commercial enterprise. This became the driving force behind his writing of "Corporate Strategy," a classic work in strategic management that made him famous.

Ansoff believed that the difference between strategic management and traditional management lay in the fact that strategic management is dynamically and continuously completing the process from decision-making to realization, facing the future. Ansoff defined business strategy as the strategic decision-making that companies make regarding their current and future business activities in response to external environments. Therefore, Ansoff believed that the core of corporate strategy should be: understanding your current position, defining your goals, and clearly identifying the actions necessary to achieve these goals. He limited business strategy to the scope of products and markets, believing that the content of business strategy consists of four elements: product-market range, growth direction, competitive advantage, and synergy. He categorized corporate decisions into three types: strategic (regarding products and markets), administrative (regarding structure and resource allocation), and daily operational (regarding budgeting, supervision, and control). Ansoff believed that corporate survival is composed of environment, strategy, and organization, and only when these three are coordinated and mutually adaptive can the effectiveness of corporate benefits be effectively improved. Based on these theories, he designed the Ansoff Model, whose core is determining effective corporate strategies through analysis of businesses and markets.
