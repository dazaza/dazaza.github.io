It is only an invitation to offer.

by loveyan5 on 2012-02-10 09:48:53

A bank is like a clever and intricate machine. Through it, scattered small amounts of money are aggregated into large sums, forming a powerful monetary force. Therefore, for banks, the source of funds always holds the most crucial significance. To achieve its profitability, a bank must acquire large amounts of low-cost funds, and in China, these funds mainly come from deposits. In Western countries, deposits generally account for 70%-80% of a bank's total liabilities.

In what appears to be a simple deposit business, many legal issues arise between the bank and its customers.

**I. Is the identity of bank customers important?**

There is an example where an organization deposited 30,000 yuan in cash under the name of Zhao Jia. This act of using public funds as private savings and setting up a slush fund is illegal in China. Once discovered by the bank, it can impose penalties. In developed countries, a bank customer refers to any natural person, legal entity, or association without legal personality who has opened an account with the bank and conducts transactions with third parties through the bank. Regardless of whether the customer is a natural person, legal entity, or group, their relationship with the bank is one of equal contractual parties, with no special legal distinctions. This reflects the principle of freedom of contract. The reason why the phenomenon of public funds being privately saved occurs in China is mainly due to the existence of a cash management system. Different deposit regulations apply to state-owned enterprises, groups, collectively owned enterprises, residents, and individual operators, with residents enjoying the greatest freedom in depositing and withdrawing money. In other words, because of different identities of customers, the rights and obligations granted by law also differ. These rules, which have strong administrative characteristics, are products of the original planned system and do not conform to the principle of freedom of contract required by the market economy. In practice, the different treatment caused by different identities leads units to pursue the freedom enjoyed by individuals in an illegal manner, breaking through the cash management requirements for units. However, as long as the law still distinguishes the identity of depositors, such behavior by units will still be considered illegal under the law. Therefore, banks still bear the responsibility of supervising units to comply with the cash management system.

**II. Legal nature of the contractual relationship between banks and customers based on deposits, and different definitions lead to different consequences**

When a customer enters the bank's business hall and hands over a sum of money to a bank teller, and after the teller counts it clearly, issues a passbook or deposit certificate to the customer, this seemingly simple act lasting one or two minutes establishes a contract. Behind this simplicity lies an important legal issue: What is the legal nature of the contractual relationship between the bank and the customer? In fact, different legal natures will bring about different legal consequences, some of which may even make it impossible for banks to operate or change their operating nature. When we define the legal nature of the contractual relationship, we also define the rights and obligations of both parties.

The relationship formed when a customer deposits money in a bank is a contractual relationship between a creditor and a debtor. When a customer deposits money in the bank, they essentially obtain the right to repayment. Of course, one person's claim is another person's debt. The money deposited in the account is the customer's claim and also the bank's liability to the customer. In British court rulings, it has long been stated that once a depositor deposits money in the bank, that money no longer belongs to the depositor; instead, it becomes the property of the bank. A debtor-creditor relationship is established between the two parties. The bank has the right to use the deposited money according to its own intentions. The bank only needs to pay the equivalent amount (sometimes including a certain amount of interest) to the depositor upon request. This indicates that once the money is deposited in the bank, the customer no longer owns the ownership of this money but only enjoys the claim. The difference between ownership and claim can be explained by an example: If customer Li Jia deposits 10,000 yuan in the bank and still owns this 10,000 yuan because ownership is a real right, i.e., a universal right, Li Jia can exercise the right of disposition in ownership and instruct the bank to lend it to a specific person Wang Yi. This is a trust loan in trust business. At this time, the relationship between Li Jia and the bank is a trust relationship rather than a debtor-creditor relationship.

**III. What legal elements are required for banks and customers to conclude a deposit contract?**

According to the law, these elements generally include three aspects: the parties have capacity to act; the intention is genuine; and legality.

**(1) Capacity to Act**

At the age of 14, Hua took 10,000 yuan from home to the bank to deposit. If the bank accepts this deposit, his parents can claim that the contract is invalid because Hua lacks the capacity to act. Thus, determining whether the customer has the capacity to act is very important. Capacity to act refers to the qualification of the parties to enjoy rights and bear obligations through their own actions. This capacity mainly manifests in the legal definition of the age of majority in various countries. According to the "General Principles of Civil Law" of China, capacity to act is divided into three types: those under 10 years old have no civil capacity; those between 10 and 18 years old have limited capacity to act; and those who are 18 years old or older have full capacity to act. Additionally, those between 16 and 18 years old who primarily rely on their own labor income are considered to have full capacity to act. Furthermore, capacity to act also manifests in the mental condition of the parties. The "General Principles of Civil Law" stipulates that mentally ill persons who cannot identify their own actions are considered to have no capacity to act, while those who cannot fully identify their own actions are considered to have limited capacity to act. The legal distinction between no capacity to act, limited capacity to act, and full capacity to act lies in the different legal consequences of these acts. All civil acts (such as deposits) by persons with full capacity to act are naturally valid; persons with limited capacity to act can only engage in civil activities appropriate to their age, intelligence, and mental health status, and other civil activities require the agency of their legal representatives (such as parents); all civil activities of persons with no capacity to act must be conducted by their legal representatives. It should be noted that the restrictions on the capacity to act of the parties by law are generally not due to discrimination but for protective purposes. Therefore, deposit contracts established for smaller amounts can be considered valid. For example, if Party A opens a fixed-term deposit account (i.e., concludes a deposit contract) with the bank, whether this deposit contract is valid depends on whether Party A has the capacity to act. If Party A was under 16 years old at the time of concluding the contract, the contract would be invalid unless the deposit amount was not too large. Therefore, if a teenager who appears to be under 18 years old comes to the bank with 100,000 yuan to deposit, the bank has the right to inquire whether he has obtained the consent of his legal representative. In the above example, the legal representative of the teenager (such as his parents) can claim that the contract is invalid because Party A lacks the capacity to act, thereby protecting their interests.

**(2) Genuine Intention**

Deposit contracts must be established on the basis of mutual genuine intention. If the parties are coerced or deceived, or if there is a significant misunderstanding about the content of the contract at the time of conclusion, or if the contract is grossly unfair, it will affect the legal validity of the contract. Moreover, the degree to which the intention is not genuine will also affect the validity of the contract. For example, in some countries, contracts entered into under coercion are considered invalid because there is no genuine intention of the parties in such cases; whereas contracts entered into under deception are considered "revocable contracts" because, in such cases, the genuine intention of the parties exists nominally, meaning that the parties believed their intention was genuine at the time of entering into the contract. The difference between the two results is that invalid contracts are invalid from the outset, while revocable contracts are valid until revoked by the parties, after which they are also considered invalid from the outset. It should be noted that, according to Chinese law, contracts entered into under deception are also considered invalid.

In the aforementioned other situations, contracts are generally "revocable contracts." According to Chinese law, in these cases, the parties have the right to request the court or arbitration body to amend or revoke the deposit contract. However, in such cases, the parties must file for amendment or revocation within one year from the establishment of the deposit contract; otherwise, the court will not protect them.

**(3) Not violating the law or public interest**

When both parties have the capacity to act and there are no factors affecting the validity of the contract, the validity of the contract also depends on its legality. Legality refers to whether it violates the law or public interest. If it does not meet the requirement of legality, the contract is invalid. For example, relevant regulations in China prohibit the private saving of public funds. If a unit deposits public funds in the bank under a personal name, the resulting deposit behavior constitutes an invalid contract.

**IV. Different determinations of the time of formation of deposit contracts can lead to different legal consequences**

Let's look at a case that occurred in New Zealand. An employee of the defendant (a supermarket) prepared to deposit a sum of cash and several checks into the bank. After entering the bank, he took out the cash and checks from his money bag and placed them on the counter of the bank cashier. The cashier took out part of the cash to start counting, placing the counted cash at the other end of the counter. Just as a small portion of the cash had been counted clearly, several robbers suddenly broke into the bank and took away the uncountried cash. The plaintiff then filed a lawsuit with the court, demanding that the bank compensate for the loss. The Supreme Court of New Zealand believed that "before the bank verifies the deposit and signs receipt, the depositor's funds should not be regarded as having been deposited in the bank or becoming the property of the bank." Therefore, the uncounted portion of the funds still belonged to the depositor, so the bank did not bear the responsibility of compensation.

This case actually contains a legal issue regarding the time of formation of the contractual relationship. "The time of formation of the contract" has important significance in law. It signifies the generation of legal binding force and the occurrence of rights and obligations. Neither party may arbitrarily change or terminate the contract unless prescribed by law or with the consent of the other party. Once the contractual relationship between the bank and the customer is determined, any breach of contract by either party, including negligent acts, will result in legal consequences. The negligent or breaching party must assume legal responsibility. As for which legal obligations the bank and the customer should bear, it must be determined according to the agreement reached by both parties and the nature of the transaction.

So, when exactly does the contractual relationship between the bank and the customer begin? Some scholars argue that the contractual relationship between the bank and the customer begins when both parties start negotiating deposit or collection arrangements. For example, when a customer named Zhang San takes money to the bank and hands it over to the bank clerk, the contractual relationship has already begun. Of course, the negotiations referred to here must be ones that can directly lead to an agreement between the two parties. Because this helps to urge both parties to better perform their respective obligations. During the negotiations, the bank and the customer may learn much financial and commercial intelligence and secrets about the latter. If the negotiations do not lead to the signing of a deposit contract, the bank considers the contract unformed and therefore does not bear the obligation of confidentiality. At this point, the customer may face the risk of trade secret leakage, and their interests may be harmed.

According to the provisions of China's "Economic Contract Law" regarding the time of formation of the contract, the economic contract is formed when the parties reach an agreement on the main terms of the economic contract through negotiation. Chinese law lacks provisions on pre-contractual negligence, but from the cases listed in this issue's "Mock Trial," in China's judicial practice, there is also a tendency towards the view that the contractual relationship begins when both parties start negotiating, meaning that the bank may need to compensate for losses suffered by the customer during the contract formation process. Of course, this case was ruled based on tort liability, and for detailed analysis, please refer to the "Mock Trial" column.

In normal circumstances, individuals or companies open deposit accounts in banks without necessarily depositing a sum of cash into the bank. In modern society, depositors can deposit checks or transferable checks or other claims as cash into the bank. In such cases, the contractual relationship between the bank and the customer should start from the time the depositor opens an account, not from the time the bank converts the check or other claim into cash.

Here, it is necessary to mention the issues of offer and acceptance in the contract. Did the bank first issue an offer to the customer? This legal issue is very practical because if the bank's posted interest rate announcements and other documents are considered offers to conclude a contract, the bank, as the issuer of the offer, is legally bound. That is, once the customer accepts the bank's conditions such as interest rates and prepares to deposit a sum of cash into the bank, the bank must accept this commitment, otherwise it will be considered a breach of contract and bear corresponding legal responsibilities. In fact, the bank has the freedom to choose its customers, so the announcements posted by the bank do not constitute an offer to the customer, but merely an invitation to offer, and the act of the customer handing over money to the bank is the offer, while the bank's act of accepting the deposit is the acceptance.

**V. Termination of the contractual relationship between the bank and the customer**

Once the contractual relationship between the bank and the customer is established, it can normally last for a long time unless otherwise agreed by both parties. Without special agreements, events leading to the termination of the relationship between the bank and the customer include: the bankruptcy of the relevant bank; the death or bankruptcy of the customer; the active request by the bank or the customer to terminate the relationship; changes in relevant laws or the passage of new laws can also cause the existing relationship between the two parties to terminate, etc.

Legal issues related to the termination of the contractual relationship between the bank and the customer involve the determination of the termination time and the rights and obligations that both parties should enjoy and bear. When the relationship between the bank and the customer terminates, the customer has the right to withdraw the funds deposited in the bank.

Moreover, the bank's initiative to terminate the relationship with the customer is more complex than the customer's initiative to terminate the relationship with the bank. For example, if the bank does not give the customer reasonable notice before terminating the customer's account, causing the customer to believe that the account is still valid and use it, the resulting losses, such as interest losses, should be compensated by the bank.

**VI. Characteristics of Deposit Contracts**

One characteristic of the contractual relationship between the bank and the customer is that much of the content is not explicitly written in the contract. In fact, when we go to the bank to deposit money, there is never a written, visible contract. The contractual relationship between the bank and the customer is often written in legal provisions or bank regulations. When the customer deposits money, it is equivalent to accepting the conditions stipulated in these laws or regulations, thereby establishing the contract. However, even if some contents of the rights and obligations relationship between the bank and the customer are not specified in the laws, regulations, and bank regulations, they should still be followed by the parties. For example, in some countries, even if the law, bank regulations, or contract do not specify the confidentiality of the customer's account and transactions by the bank, the bank must still keep the customer's secrets.

Such a contract is called a standard contract, which differs from a general contract in that the bank, as one of the contracting parties, possesses far greater strength than the customer. The customer cannot negotiate the terms of the deposit contract when depositing money. He can only choose to accept the standard deposit contract set by the bank or not deposit money in this bank. This standard contract violates the principle of freedom of contract, but it conforms to the economic life of modern society, so it is also recognized by the law. However, in order to protect the interests of weaker contracting parties, the law stipulates that the interpretation of standard contracts should be beneficial to the non-drafting party. In China, banks generally specify the types of deposits and the conditions for various deposits. To protect customers, these terms will be reviewed by the central bank. Whether a contract interpretation favorable to the customer is made has not yet been tested by litigation. But theoretically, it should be so.

Compared to general contractual relationships, another characteristic of deposit contractual relationships is that, as the debtor, the bank does not actively seek out creditors to repay debts but should allow creditors to exercise their rights by coming to the bank, i.e., withdrawing deposits. This is determined by the characteristics of the banking industry. For example, Party A cannot claim that the bank failed to actively repay him after his deposit matured and thus demand that the bank bear the responsibility for breach of contract.
