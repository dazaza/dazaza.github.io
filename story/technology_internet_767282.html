The task application Any.do, which inspired the iOS 7 design, was actually developed by Israeli intelligence officers?

by anonymous on 2013-10-11 19:14:34

Taskmasters: how Israeli intelligence officers helped inspire the look of iOS 7

In October 2009, the annual creative project exhibition GeekCon brought vibrant energy to the Wingate Institute sports venue in Israel.

The event organizer Eden Shochat is an Israeli investor and also the founder of the face recognition technology company Face.com (acquired by Facebook last year). As Shochat strolled around the venue, he marveled at these "cool but impractical" creative projects: a suitcase that follows its owner using heat sensors, a computer that uses the user's jumping silhouette as a login password. However, what truly caught Shochat's eye was Alice, an intelligent inflatable doll capable of conversing with users, developed by then 25-year-old hacker Omer Perchik.

Shochat and Perchik hit it off immediately. Shochat commented: "I really liked his way of thinking and appreciated his technical skills. Even the most mediocre projects become star-studded teams under his leadership." After the exhibition ended, Shochat and Perchik kept in touch. Months later, Perchik mentioned his ideas for his next project to Shochat.

However, this project was vastly different from the seductive Alice. Perchik planned to start with a minimal task application aimed at developing a series of office applications for mobile devices. At the time, a test version of the app he developed had already garnered tens of thousands of users on Android, but he intended to leverage this development experience to create an entirely new app, which would later become Any.do, the task management app that skyrocketed in popularity, attracted millions of users, and inspired the design of iOS 7.

Perchik told Shochat at the time that he needed some funding to support the development of this project, as he first needed to hire a group of Israeli defense intelligence officers to create a perfect and highly disruptive task application.

Omer Perchik on the left during his military service

Hiring Intelligence Officers

Unit 8200 is Israel's intelligence monitoring agency, consisting of thousands of intelligence officers whose main task is to monitor internet and broadcast media information through communication intelligence interception and code decryption, eliminating national security threats. In the eyes of Israelis, Unit 8200 intelligence personnel are godlike figures.

Schonat stated: "These intelligence personnel have the ability to find needles in haystacks. During their training, they were instilled with the concept of 'I can do anything.' When they turned 18, they once told their superiors that they needed a supercomputer. Twenty minutes later, they received one. As adults, they process exabyte-level data in data centers and participate in critical projects."

While in university, Perchik spent every weekend playing with Aviv, a friend who had served in Unit 8200. They usually started by having a few drinks at Aviv's house before heading out to nightclubs. But this meeting between Perchik and Aviv was different from previous ones.

Perchik asked Aviv to list everyone he knew from Unit 8200 and learn about them individually. After Aviv listed 20 intelligence officers he considered the smartest, Perchik took this list and embarked on a recruitment journey.

Data Processing and Analysis

After completing his military service, Perchik entered university to study business and law courses. Simultaneously, he was working on a personal project involving web design and search engine optimization for small businesses. However, he quickly lost interest in this work. Once, while helping a friend with a visa application, he couldn't find a satisfactory task application throughout the entire process, which frustrated him. What he wanted was a fast, concise, and automatic task application, so he decided to create such an application himself.

Two years ago, Perchik used Aviv's network to build his team. Currently, about half of Perchik's 13-member team comes from Unit 8200. Perchik envisioned a series of mobile applications that could understand user intentions and recommend relevant resources. For example, if a user records a product on Any.do, Any.do can help the user purchase it; if the user notes an event on the Any.do calendar, Any.do can help the user book a taxi. This is a free life assistant application but generates revenue by providing related services.

To develop such a product, Perchik needed to hire excellent language analysis experts. During his military service, Perchik developed a human resource management application that was commended for its outstanding text analysis capabilities and automatic grouping algorithms. Any.do engineer Yoni Lindenfeld said: "The most important thing I learned while serving in Unit 8200 is to believe in my own omnipotence. I can't reveal too much information about the projects, but I want to say that we indeed have many talented individuals."

Inspiring the Design of iOS 7

After hiring intelligence officers, Perchik began assembling a UI team. Perchik planned to launch the product on Android first because he believed it would be easier for Any.do to stand out on this platform. Currently, most task applications on the market use a simulated pen-and-paper approach, whereas Any.do's design abandoned skeuomorphism. Its interface is more similar to the WP system than iOS. Jonathan Saragossi, the earliest interface design lead for Any.do, stated: "Task applications look like office supplies rather than life assistant applications. Our initial idea was to make Any.do more lifestyle-oriented."

Any.do Moment is a significant feature of Any.do, reminding users daily: "Spend some time planning your tasks today." The development team indicated that this function ensures user loyalty. Most of Any.do's tasks can be completed on a single screen, with voice input as a key design focus. Compared to touch operations, the app emphasizes gesture control. The iOS version of Any.do was officially released in June last year. To date, Any.do's total downloads across iOS and Android have exceeded 7 million.

According to informed sources, Apple's flat design for iOS 7 was inspired by Any.do, music application Rdio, word game application Letterpress, task management application Clear, among others (Apple has not yet commented on this).

iOS 7 features thinner fonts, fewer embellishments, and a cleaner overall interface. The leather-textured calendar interface and Las Vegas casino-style game center interface are gone, replaced by a white background, text-based labels, and simple geometric patterns. Apple stated: "The new design eliminates elements that do not add value to the system, allowing users to focus more on the content of the system." — This is precisely the design philosophy Any.do has adhered to from the beginning.

This July, Perchik and his team released Any.do Cal, a calendar application embodying Any.do's consistent simplicity and smoothness. Currently, only the iOS version has been released, with the Android version scheduled for release in a few weeks. Moving forward, Perchik and his team will continue developing email and note-taking applications. Any.do's motto is "good day every day," and all their products are designed with this goal in mind. Perchik remarked: "If no one uses it, even the most advanced technology is useless. We need to create products that users can use daily."

Why People Hate Task Lists

The Any.do team stated that once the user base reaches a certain scale, the profit model doesn't need to worry. This week, Any.do announced partnerships with Uber and others. Users of Any.do Cal can call for rides via Uber, purchase gifts through Amazon and Gifts.com, and Any.do will charge a fee for these transactions.

In 2011, Any.do received $1 million in investment from Shochat and several angel investors; in May this year, the company raised another $3.5 million. Moreover, Yahoo co-founder Jerry Yang, YouTube founder Steve Chen, former Twitter growth vice president Othman Laraki, and Facebook Gift Services manager Lee Linden recently became strategic investment partners of Any.do. Perchik moved from Israel to San Francisco this year, continuing to seek financing while exploring new investment opportunities.

Meanwhile, according to market research firm Onavo Insights, currently, none of the third-party task applications on iOS have an active user base exceeding 1%. Alex Schiff, the founder of lightweight note-taking application Fetchnotes, pointed out that there are two main reasons why users dislike task applications: one is the inherent aversion to task lists — when your tasks pile up to a certain extent, you're likely to give up altogether, ignoring the list until eventually uninstalling such applications; the second reason is the strong substitutability of task applications — current users mostly enjoy trying new things, so when new task applications are released, users are very willing to try a new one.

With the support of a geek team obsessed with "compulsion to complete tasks," Any.do's concept of creating a series of products can win more users. Any.do has only been around for a short time but has already influenced an operating system. However, when discussing the issue of user loyalty for task applications, Saragossi stated: "This is exactly what makes Any.do interesting — we still face the problem of user loyalty. In fact, this is a big issue, and no one has solved it yet. We've taken a small step, and in the future, we will definitely solve it."

Meanwhile, the Any.do team continues to analyze data and the connection between user needs and various businesses. Lindenfeld stated: "We've already invested a lot of money in this technology, and we have grand plans for the future."

It seems that the team, once responsible for monitoring secret intelligence, is about to show the world their works. Let's wait and see.
