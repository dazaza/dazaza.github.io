1. Registration line: 3 mm is the standard. The registration line on both sides should be emphasized, and the bite side can even be omitted.

2. Bite: For general printing equipment, the minimum bite can be adjusted to the range of 6-11 mm. During teaching, 8 mm is usually used as the standard.

These two items do not need to be made by us, but the paper size must be considered when calculating the combined plate area.

For example: for paper with dimensions of 880*590, its printable area should subtract the positions of the registration line and the bite. Theoretically, the printable area is: 590-8-3-3 * 880-3-3 = 576*874

In actual operation, the same principle applies, but the paper size depends on the specific situation because factories always try to use as much paper as possible.

Some factories can achieve 889*597 for the half sheet of large format paper measuring 889*1194.

3. Bleed: Bleed should be set in actual production, generally at 3-5 mm. For ease of calculation and operation, usually each side has a bleed of 2 mm, making the total bleed 4 mm. Of course, the outer side of the boundary page still only has 2 mm.

4. Combined plate rules: In addition to considering the positioning of the registration line and the bite, as well as the setting of the bleed, the rule of combined plate printing is to save as much paper as possible.

Common knowledge of combined plate printing process:

1. How does combined plate printing make the ink color of printed products more uniform?

Avoid having long narrow blank windows in the middle of solid or dark tone areas along the direction of rotation of the cylinder as much as possible, and try to arrange the long narrow graphics on the plate along the front and back directions. Because the ink transmission capability of the printing press is limited, after printing, the ink color at the top and bottom ends of the long narrow window along the direction of rotation of the printing cylinder will become darker, seriously affecting the appearance of the printed product. If this window is not within the shape of the finished product, it can be considered to fill it with dots or solids similar to the surrounding area, or rotate the entire pattern by 90 degrees if possible.

Similarly, when cross-combining plates, there are often long narrow blank parts at the junctions. These blank parts should also be filled with dots or solids similar to the surrounding area as much as possible.

2. How does combining plates help reduce production costs?

Cross-combined plate: Place the blank or inner-sticking part of the product at the bite (1cm~1.5cm), use single knife connection longitudinally, and add color blocks of different color plates on the sticking edge, which can serve as a density control strip on one hand, and take away excess ink from the ink roller on the other hand to ensure ink-water balance;

Change the registration lines from crosses to T-shapes placed close to the finished product; or place the registration lines on the finished product that will be stuck inside or folded inside; or place the registration lines in positions that will be die-cut;

Comprehensively consider the number of combinations, specifications of the printing press, printing quantity, and paper price. Because increasing the number of combinations can reduce the printing quantity, but will increase the cost of making film, increase the difficulty of adjusting ink color and registration before printing, and increase the loss of trial printing paper.

For multiple varieties with small quantities, it can be considered to combine them on the same plate, but attention should be paid to avoid mixing. Color blocks can be added on the sticking edge or shaking cover to separate them.

3. How does combining plates help improve the accuracy of offset printing?

For products with high registration accuracy requirements, their main parts should be arranged as close to the bite as possible during plate making, because under the action of pressing force, the width of the paper and the length of the two corners at the trailing edge will expand slightly, the greater the pressure, the more expansion, resulting in trapezoidal shapes, causing misregistration.

Use smaller size paper to print products with high precision and fewer prints, because the larger the printed sheet, the greater the deformation caused by changes in moisture content and printing pressure.

Thicker paper should be printed with the fiber length direction with less elongation rate aligned along the axis of the cylinder, because after the paper fibers are fully moistened, the diameter increase is much greater than the length increase. (Feed paper horizontally)

The positioning of paper on the offset press is determined by the front gauge and the side gauge. The front gauge controls the up and down position of the paper, and the side gauge controls the left and right position of the paper. In theory, the corner where the bite edge intersects with the side gauge should be the most accurate position for registration. In actual production, in order to avoid serious misregistration on the opposite side of the side gauge, the part of the product with the highest registration requirement should be placed in the middle of the left and right directions of the paper.

4. How does combining plates help with die-cutting processing?

When combining plates, try to place the side of the cardboard edge that will be glued or in an inconspicuous part at the bite edge, because during flat-bed automatic die-cutting, it is necessary to knock several small holes on the knife near the bite edge to smoothly pull out the finished product from the machine. The knocked-out small holes make the edges uneven, affecting the appearance.

Between the two combinations, try to use the post-processing method of one cut if possible. If it cannot be done, generally at least a 4mm wide knife placement should be left, and each product should have at least 2mm of graphic bleed.

Automatic packaging lines generally require the direction of the paper grain to be perpendicular to the longest crease line on the cardboard. If it is also considered to improve the crease line during die-cutting, the layout method where the longest crease line is parallel to the direction of the paper grain can also be chosen.

Principles and Common Knowledge of Prepress Process for Printing Plates

In the printing process flow, exposing plates is an important link that connects the previous and subsequent processes. Some problems that occur in the previous processes can be corrected during plate exposure, and some special requirements for the subsequent printing process can also be adjusted and resolved during the plate exposure process. Therefore, the quality of plate exposure directly affects the quality of the final printed product. In actual production, several issues may arise during the plate exposure process, mainly including the following points:

1. Differences between proofing plates and press plates

In fact, for proofing and printing, two different sets of plates should be exposed. For proofing, due to the structural characteristics of the proofing machine itself, it generally belongs to the platform type, with lower proofing pressure and slower speed compared to printing. At the same time, the dot gain caused by proofing is smaller, so during plate exposure, the dots between 2% and 3% should be retained, and the 98% dot should not be overexposed.

For printing, due to higher printing pressure, faster speed, softer printing ink (lower viscosity), dot gain easily occurs, and it can reach up to 30% - 40%. Therefore, the effect of using the same plate for proofing and printing is different, and this contradiction has been unresolved for a long time.

Therefore, when exposing printing plates, it is required to retain dots between 4% and 5%, and the 96% dot should not be overexposed, leaving room for dot gain during printing, making it easier for the printed product to approach the proof.

2. Dot loss during plate exposure

If dot loss occurs, first check the dot reproduction level of the film itself, such as whether the 50% dot on the film is square, and whether the 4% - 5% dots are intact. In short, check the quality of the phototypesetting film, and then judge whether the dots were lost during the plate exposure process based on it. Dot loss due to improper plate exposure operations frequently occurs, which is directly related to exposure time, development time, temperature of the developer, concentration, etc. Over-exposure and excessively concentrated developer will cause the dots that should be retained on the plate to be lost or the strokes of the text to become thinner.

Strictly speaking, plate exposure workers should control exposure and development through test strips and gray scales, and test their own test data.

3. Blurred plate surface

To investigate the reason for blurred plate exposure, it might first be related to the exposure film. If the output film is overexposed, it will lead to blurred dots on the exposure film, and thus the exposed plate will naturally be blurred. From the perspective of plate exposure operations, it could be due to foreign objects or dirt on the glass of the exposure machine or the film; or inappropriate cushioning of the exposure machine; or insufficient vacuum extraction, causing insufficient adhesion between the film and the plate material; improper splicing of the exposure film; too strong light before exposure (when punching holes) or between exposure and development; and excessive temperature, concentration, or development time of the developer, leading to excessive development, which is an important cause of blurred plate surface that cannot be ignored.

At this point, cleaning agents should be used to clean the glass or film; adjust the cushioning to be flat; tape should be spliced 3mm outside the image part, and confirm whether the film tightly adheres to the plate material; operations should be conducted under safe lighting, such as anti-fading fluorescent lamps, yellow lamps, or fluorescent lamps equipped with safety lampshades; for inappropriate development issues, they should be executed according to standards.

4. Uneven development

Uneven or poor development is often caused by reduced activity of the developer, which is mainly reflected in several aspects: untimely replenishment of the developer; too short development time; too low developer temperature; backflow of rinse liquid, etc. At this point, besides standardizing the development conditions, the developer should be replenished quantitatively and promptly.

Additionally, dirt or foreign objects on the plate material can hinder development, requiring staff to regularly inspect and clean the developer machine and plate exposure machine.

5. Dirty plate surface

Different situations and causes can lead to dirty plate surfaces. A dirty plate surface could be due to insufficient exposure, which includes two possibilities: one is that the transparency of the film is poor or the film is expired with high fog density; the second is that the exposure light source has been used for too long and shows signs of aging. If encountering the first situation, the only solution is to remake the film; if it's an issue with the exposure light source, the exposure time should be appropriately adjusted using a gray scale ruler, or the exposure light source should be directly replaced. Poor development could also cause a dirty plate surface, in which case the specific cause of poor development should be identified and promptly adjusted to standard conditions.

During production, sometimes spot-like dirt appears. Analyzing the reasons, there are quite a few [culprits]. Such as ① dirt spots on the glass of the plate exposure machine, dust or dirt spots on the film; ② after splicing the exposure film, the tape leaves tape marks on the plate surface; ③ during development, poor development caused by water bubbles adhering (bubbles caused by clogged spray holes of the developer); ④ during plate exposure operations, fingerprints are left on the plate surface due to unclean hands; ⑤ the protective gel solution is too thin; ⑥ the protective gel is not dried before heavy stacking storage, causing the plates to stick together, and peeling off the protective gel layer when using; ⑦ after plate making, the plates are stored under high temperature and humidity conditions.

Targeted solutions should be applied based on different causes. Among them, dirt caused by tape adhesive and tape marks is the most common, and prevention should be prioritized. When splicing plates, transparent tapes that are thin and clean should be used as much as possible. If the tape adhesive sticks to the plate, it should be gently wiped off before development. During plate exposure, sufficient vacuum extraction should be performed to minimize the air layer around the spliced tape and the original film, reducing the possibility of light interference phenomena to the lowest extent. If there are still tape marks on the plate after exposure, manual dirt removal using a dirt remover or secondary exposure dirt removal should be performed.

For dirt removal in other aspects, dirt removers, plate repair knives, pumice sticks, scattered film exposure dirt removal, or dirt cover plate dirt removal can be used.

6. Poor ink acceptance on the printing plate

Excluding the problem of low photosensitive agent content in the plate material causing non-ink acceptance, the reasons should be found in the relationship between the ink acceptance of the printing plate and the exposure time, and the concentration of the developer. Too long exposure time or too high concentration of the developer can easily cause poor ink acceptance on the printing plate. Because the photosensitive layer of PS plates is not absolutely soluble or insoluble, but lies between the two, if not controlled properly, it will cause the photosensitive layer of the printing plate to dissolve what should dissolve and also dissolve what shouldn't dissolve, increasing the deinking rate and causing poor ink acceptance.

Additionally, if ① the protective gel does not meet the requirements, the gel solution is too concentrated, and the manual coating is uneven; ② the drying temperature of the printing plate is too high or too low, causing poor ink absorption or poor film-forming performance; ③ the printing plate contacts strong light after coating; ④ the printing plate is stored in a high temperature and high humidity environment after plate making, it will cause a decrease in ink acceptance.

At this point, the designated protective gel for the plate material should be used, the gel solution should be appropriately diluted, and the drying temperature should be reasonably determined, generally around 60°C; after coating, light exposure should be prevented, and the plate-made printing plate should be stored in a qualified environment. If poor ink acceptance on the printing plate is discovered during printing, it should be checked whether there is too much gel component in the dampening solution, or whether it was not rinsed with water after using the cleaning agent.

7. Reduced durability of the printing plate

The main reasons for the reduction in the durability of the printing plate lie in the plate exposure operation and printing. In terms of plate exposure: ① light leakage occurs before exposure or before development; ② the density of the film is not high enough; ③ due to high temperature and concentration of the developer, long development time or excessive supplementation causes excessive development; ④ strong acids, strong bases, organic solvents, dirt removers, etc., adhere to the printing plate; ⑤ excessive wiping of the plate material with a plate cleaning agent, etc., all these affect the durability of the printing plate.

In terms of printing, if the printing pressure is too high or there is too much paper powder, as well as the use of strong plate cleaning agents and cylinder cleaning agents in UV printing, it inevitably leads to a decrease in durability. It should be particularly noted that to prevent light leakage, yellow safety lights should be used for ordinary PS plates, and harmful light should be fully shielded.

8. Blue background on the exposed printing plate

In production, no matter whether it is due to problems in the initial production of the plate material or whether the matching conditions of exposure and development time are inappropriate, the exposed plate has a blue background. The "secret" behind the blue background lies in: ① during the production of the plate material, the sand grains are too coarse, and the current of electrolytic oxidation does not match; ② during plate exposure, the exposure is insufficient or the concentration of the developer is not enough, or the two conditions are not properly matched.

The phenomenon of blue background troubles many operators. Whether more development or less development doesn't work. More development would shrink or lose the dots that need to be retained; less development would affect the entire plate surface. Under appropriate exposure and development conditions, if there is still a blue background after development, dirt needs to be repaired, but repairing dirt leaves watermarks on the plate surface. After removing the watermarks and printing on the machine, the surroundings of the watermarks will get dirty again. If the dirt around is repaired with dirt remover, another circle of dirt will appear, and if it spreads to pictures, texts, or screens, the plate cannot be used anymore.

To solve this problem, a thin layer of glue should be applied before dirt removal, and high-quality printed products cannot use this plate.

The above eight points are common issues in the plate exposure process, briefly introduced here. The various problems involved and their solutions still need to be analyzed specifically in actual production, and further arguments are needed.
