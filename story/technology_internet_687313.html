In October 2008, China's foreign exchange reserves experienced negative growth for the first time since the currency reform. Subsequently, due to the outbreak of the international financial crisis, the reduction of overseas speculative capital led to a not high growth rate of foreign exchange reserves. However, half a year later, with signs of economic recovery, the situation of substantial growth in foreign exchange reserves reappeared. By the end of June 2009, China's foreign exchange reserve balance reached $2,131.6 billion, breaking through $2 trillion for the first time. By March 2011, China's foreign exchange reserve balance was $3,044.7 billion, breaking through $3 trillion for the first time.

However, some changes occurred in September this year. According to data released by the People's Bank of China, the foreign exchange reserve balance at the end of September was $3,201.7 billion, a net decrease of $60.8 billion compared to the previous month, marking the first decline since May 2010.

The reason lies in the sharp strengthening of the dollar against major currencies in that month, which caused a book exchange loss for China's foreign exchange reserves, leading to a decline in the reserve balance. However, Guan Tao, director of the Balance of Payments Department of the State Administration of Foreign Exchange, wrote in an article that the increase in purchase and payment of foreign exchange and the improvement in the imbalance of foreign exchange supply and demand were also important reasons for the negative growth of foreign exchange reserves. According to data from the State Administration of Foreign Exchange, bank customer purchases of foreign exchange increased by about $10 billion compared to the previous month in September, while customer settlements decreased by about $2 billion, reducing the surplus of settlements and sales to $26 billion in that month, a decrease of 31% compared to the previous month.
