In the initial market, when a product became popular, most companies thought that it had already lost its competitiveness. They hastily increased advertising spending, improved promotional efforts, upgraded or eliminated products, and the most commonly used method was to reduce prices. Price reduction indeed can promote sales, but at the same time, it also loses profits and may damage the brand image. Moreover, in today's market with intense homogenized competition, if you reduce your price, competitors will follow suit, sometimes even with a larger price drop. In this case, the so-called "ultimate weapon of the market" - price - will lose its effectiveness. What other weapons do you have? Simply reducing prices has become elementary and ineffective in today's market environment. So, is there a way to promote sales without reducing prices?

In Western management theory, there is a "transfer theory" which could help us break out of the deadlock to some extent. However, the transfer theory does not focus on practical market perspectives; it only deduces from theoretical basics, lacking specific operational methods. Furthermore, since the era when the theory was proposed is far removed from today, the market situation has undergone tremendous changes. Therefore, in today's market, the transfer theory is merely an operational concept with little practical value. Based on years of market experience, I have summarized a set of customer transfer operation methods suitable for China's market realities, defined as transfer marketing. This method can help enterprises maintain profit margins while allowing consumers to obtain satisfactory product values, achieving a win-win transaction outcome.

The balance between product value and purchase cost

Product value refers to the overall benefits customers can obtain from a product, including physical attributes and emotional feelings. However, although a product may contain benefits that consumers want, they may not necessarily buy it because of another important influencing factor: the purchase cost. Before consumers decide to buy and place the product on the real scale, they first weigh it on the invisible scale in their hearts. The two ends of the scale are the purchase cost and the product value. When the side of the purchase cost on the scale becomes heavier, it is difficult to complete the transaction; when the scale tilts towards the product value, the transaction can be smoothly completed. Therefore, only when the product value and purchase cost reach a balance in the consumer's mind or have a higher product value recognition, will the consumer possibly make a purchase.

At this point, we can draw a consumer psychology balance chart. On one side of the scale, list the various values consumers can obtain by purchasing the product, and on the other side, list the various costs customers must bear to purchase the product. At this time, as long as we increase the weight on the side of product value on the scale and simultaneously reduce the various costs for customers, the product will sell easily. However, business common sense tells us that increasing product value and reducing consumer purchase costs will both increase the company's operating costs. Then, how can we enhance product value and reduce consumer purchase costs without increasing operating costs or as little as possible?

Reducing customer purchase costs

According to different industries, the formation of consumer purchase costs varies, roughly divided into four types: time cost, physical effort cost, risk cost, and choice cost. We can lower or eliminate these costs through the following methods to promote consumers' willingness to purchase.

1. Time Cost

In today's fast-paced society, time cost has become an important value reference factor in the consumer consumption process. For example, we often complain about the time wasted waiting for buses, checking out while shopping, or deliveries arriving late, but rarely complain about the price of goods. This shows that reducing customers' time costs is very necessary.
