Website's Arbitrary Order Cancellation Questioned: Lawyer Says Continuation of Performance Can Be Demanded

by tuoqing1188 on 2011-08-04 09:26:35

Business Cancellation of Transactions without Informing Consumers and Shifting Responsibility: Lawyer Says Original Order Can Be Enforced

"Overbearing" websites have repeatedly canceled user orders arbitrarily. In response to consumer inquiries, these sites evade responsibility citing website agreements, sparking strong consumer dissatisfaction.

Relevant lawyers indicate that due to the difficulty in providing evidence for online transactions and the high cost of defending rights, consumers are often at a disadvantage. Consumers should first attempt to resolve issues through negotiation with merchants; if the merchant does not cooperate, consumers have the right to file complaints with the industrial and commercial administrative departments or even initiate litigation in courts with jurisdiction.

By reporter Xue Song

Recently, it has been learned that some e-commerce websites have frequently experienced arbitrary cancellations of user orders. Some shopping websites or merchants evade responsibility citing website agreements, causing strong consumer dissatisfaction.

Cancellation of Orders without Any Penalty

On the evening of July 28th, collector Miss He found a product on China Collection Hotline, spotting a set of "Dream of the Red Chamber" picture books priced at 6 yuan in the "Picture Book Exchange Home" store. Since Miss He only needed this one book to complete her collection, she immediately placed an order. The next evening, she discovered that the order had been confirmed as completed, but just as she was about to pay, she noticed that the order had been canceled by the seller, within less than 24 hours of placing the order.

The seller explained that the actual price of the book was 60 yuan, and it was only marked as 6 yuan to save on upload fees. Miss He then complained to the website administrator.

According to the website's records, after receiving the complaint, the administrator promptly reminded the seller: according to relevant website rules, the displayed price is the sole legal price. After a customer selects a product, the store owner must unconditionally sell it to the customer at the listed price (except for reasonable shipping costs), and the store owner cannot demand a higher price during order negotiations.

However, the seller insisted that the book had already been sold, making further transactions impossible, and mocked Miss He. Later, although the seller claimed they were willing to compensate her with 18 yuan, Miss He rejected it. She demanded that the website penalize the seller, but after three days, the administrator had still not handled the complaint, and the offending merchant had received no punishment.

Miss He told reporters that first, the merchant did not sell the item at the listed price but instead raised the price arbitrarily. Second, the order was canceled before the expiration time (within three days) without any consultation with the buyer. As the managing party, the website has an obligation to impose penalties. When reporters subsequently called the website's customer service hotline, the other party said they needed more information and suggested users complain again via email.

Dangdang Cancels 1 Yuan Shoe Orders

On July 28th, Dangdang.com offered authentic Adidas shoes for 1 yuan. Many consumers who successfully paid and placed orders were later informed that their orders were canceled without notification. Dangdang's proposed solution was not accepted by the consumers, leading them to form a "1 Yuan Order" rights protection group to collectively seek a reasonable solution from Dangdang.

Ms. Liu described her shopping experience on a forum: she saw blue Adidas Originals sneakers being sold on Dangdang for 1 yuan (originally priced at 680 yuan). Thinking it was a promotional offer, she bought 16 pairs at once, paying a total of 36 yuan including shipping fees via Alipay. After a successful order placement, the webpage showed "preparing goods."

Unexpectedly, the next day when she checked online, the order had already been canceled. That day, Ms. Liu received a text message from Dangdang saying: "The product you purchased is sold by Dangdang's partner store, Name Shoes Warehouse Sports Leisure Specialty Store. The merchant replied that there is no such product available, due to a system error. Your order has been canceled and cannot be restored." However, some customers received items in subsequent days, such as slippers and two pairs of socks. These were all rejected by buyers.

Dangdang explained that the 1-yuan shoe offer was due to a system error, and the compensation plan was to give each person a 200-yuan gift voucher. Buyers expressed that they could not accept it and would continue to protect their rights.

Legal Opinion:

Consumers Can Demand Merchants Continue with Original Orders

Li Xiaoning, Deputy Director of Guangdong Shenglun Law Firm, stated that orders generated from online shopping are also contracts. Price errors are due to the merchant's own negligence, and the fault does not lie with the consumer, so consumers can request the merchant to continue fulfilling the original order. However, if the price difference is too large, according to the Contract Law, "mutual consent is a condition for the establishment of a contract," etc., contracts established due to significant misunderstandings are revocable, and the seller can also apply to revoke the contract. But significant misunderstandings are often hard to define.

She believes that some contracts cannot be revoked by merchants. Many websites now offer tempting low-price banners, like 1-yuan flash sales and limited-time grabs. Consumers find it difficult to distinguish between genuine and fake offers, and many conditions are set, making it hard for consumers to protect their rights.

She thinks that the biggest challenge consumers face in protecting their rights is obtaining evidence, because shopping webpages can be modified or deleted at any time. Consumers can only provide evidence by capturing webpages or through other consumers, but these evidences often lack sufficient validity. Strictly speaking, consumers should go to notarization, but the costs incurred by consumers are also relatively high, often resulting in abandonment due to lack of returns.

Some merchants often refuse to compensate or indemnify consumers citing website agreements, but these "overbearing" terms may not necessarily be valid. For example, Dangdang's transaction terms stipulate that Dangdang has the right to unilaterally withdraw if obvious errors or stock shortages are discovered on its website.

According to relevant regulations, online commodity operators and online service operators shall not make unfair or unreasonable provisions for consumers through electronic format contract clauses, or reduce or exempt the obligations and responsibilities of operators or exclude or limit the main rights of consumers.
