"Crash" is a film that primarily focuses on human relationships (or humanity), using multiple simultaneously occurring independent stories interwoven as a narrative technique. It is based on the connections between seemingly unrelated characters in each independent story (each character can be said to represent a class/race, such as whites, blacks, Mexicans/Central Americans, Asians/Chinese, Middle Easterners, etc.) to reflect profound issues like racial problems. The title "Crash" (although it has the meaning of a car crash, I have reservations about directly translating the title as 'car crash'), is because in today's society, everyone only cares about their own affairs, with no time to care about others, let alone another class/race. We spend much of our busy days inside the small iron box of a car (a more capitalist critique). The only opportunity we have to interact with others is when there is a traffic accident. The ending is probably also to echo this theme.

Discrimination exists everywhere, especially reflected in the little people and disadvantaged groups, but the key is whether you discriminate against yourself. Some discrimination is superficial, like the old policeman's discrimination against black people, or the black person's discrimination against Asians, but even more deadly is the unconscious self-discrimination from within, like the new policeman at the end who opposes discrimination both on the surface and subjectively, but deep down has already accepted the discrimination against black people. "Discrimination" may be too big of a term; using "prejudice" might make "Crash" more general. Everyone has prejudices. Every group has stereotypes, and these stereotypes form "prejudices" in everyone's eyes. Stereotypes have a statistical basis, and prejudices are justified, although we should assess each individual separately. This film reflects the nature of humans, not just in America, but in any country, not just in terms of skin color, but between different social groups. Skin color is just used to make it more understandable, and easier to distinguish than attire. Think about the examples around us; we are all yellow-skinned, yet there are still various "prejudices." I know a Chinese-American child, less than three years old, attending kindergarten in the U.S., who hasn't been told what discrimination means, but he thinks black children are dirty.

The cultural differences are too great: the issue of racial discrimination and survival environment are things we do not understand. So at least in understanding the motivations of the characters in the movie, there will definitely be an impact. You won’t truly understand why the old man shot; you won’t understand why the director had to stop his car; you won’t understand... many things. Watching this movie hurt me twice—two gunshots. Beyond that, I know nothing.

The collision of hearts, actually, many films don't need to be fully understood. Being moved and touched is enough, even if it's just for a moment.

The biggest problem with this film might be that the story is too perfectly crafted, too polished, thus lacking sufficient power! There are too many coincidences and accidents, making it too much like a soap opera. These artificial arrangements are too exposed, weakening the film's potential for intellectual elevation. This problem exists in many American screenwriters and first-line directors, not just Paul Haggis. Because Hollywood’s rules are set, requiring directors to cater to audiences, too many directors habitually think about their works from the audience's perspective, and end up over-correcting and compromising too much. Paul Haggis's problems will re-emerge in his future films. As an outstanding screenwriter and quite competent director, I hope that while being close to the Oscars, he can also do some slightly unconventional things, especially when adapting "Flags of Our Fathers," without overly catering to Hollywood producers and a small group of critics.

This film is another story where no one is happy. Director Paul Haggis allows the audience to feel the anxiety of the characters while hoping they can see the underlying paths of interpersonal conflicts. The director is not furious, nor does he have any visual ambitions. He consciously handles the actions of the protagonists in parallel, reflecting ordinary people's accidental life reversals through unflashy cinematic lenses. Conflicts unfold step by step, contradictions accumulate point by point, focusing more on emotional and moral conflicts between people rather than just the issues of communication and racial prejudice. Although life often plays out tragic fates, the film still strives to convey to the audience a sense of struggling effort when trying to see the light.

"Crash" - Outstanding Reviews

One: Who Do We Think We Are?

On GO Forum, someone said Crash is one of the best movies this year. Abus said Crash is so boring, a huge bore. XM said, if Sin City is the extreme of formalism, then Crash is the extreme of substance. Crash is indeed a hodgepodge. What shocks us in this movie is not the car crashes, but the collisions of race, culture, dignity, personality, and family. Each collision hits my heart, again and again, leaving me speechless.

In the movie "Crash," every character is like a pearl, not the kind that shines brilliantly and flawlessly, but pearls with slight imperfections, not entirely perfect. As the plot develops, coincidence and conflict link one pearl after another into a string, shining brightly and captivating the soul. Although each pearl has a slight flaw, that flaw adds a touch of authenticity to its brilliance.

Authenticity - what kind of life is authentic? In this society, pressure is immense, and everyone has unspeakable suffering. This suffering is suppressed and distorted, reflecting as irritability, radicalism, and rebellion. Is human nature inherently evil or inherently good? This proposition has no answer. Anyone has a good side and an evil side, depending on the angle of observation. In the movie Crash, it's even more evident that there is no absolutely good person or absolutely bad person. Everyone, as mentioned earlier, has different issues, different sins, but each sin has its cause, evoking disgust along with a bit of pity and sympathy in some corner of your heart. This is the charm of the movie, authentically reflecting human nature, the duality of human nature, and the struggle of human nature. The inner struggle and contradiction is another kind of "crash," which may be the most harmful.

A violent police officer can be a filial son with a heart of steel. His violence is the release of frustration over his father's suffering. He is angry about his father's situation and grieves over his father's outcome. But as a junior police officer, he lacks the power to change this outcome. His strength is too weak to help his suffering father. He hates everything, believing his father contributed much to black people, but received no return, turning him into a radical, a racist. In the early part of the movie, this police officer is very disliked, but after learning about his story with his father, I felt compassion for him. He is not devoid of emotions; he just needs an outlet. The next day, he saves the woman he insulted the night before at the car accident scene, almost risking his life to save her. Besides his professional duty and inherent goodness, a significant part of it is to atone for his behavior the previous night. Human contradiction lies in the struggle between good and evil. After the release of maliciousness, guilt and emptiness may follow. Such people exist in life, doing harm to others in impulsiveness, depression, and anger. On the surface, they seem powerful, but after the event, self-reproach, guilt, and regret cannot be expressed, cannot be remedied, leaving only regret.

Insults are often self-inflicted. For example, the teenager who complains about racial discrimination every day, the Chilean old man whose house was robbed, the TV director who would rather be humiliated than lose face. In the teenager's eyes, everything in the world indicates discrimination against colored people. He believes the whole world wears colored glasses, but in fact, those colored glasses are in his heart. Seeing nails in others' eyes but beams in one's own. While complaining, the teenager forgets that he is doing everything that causes others to complain. The Persian old man is the same, suspecting everything around him, angrily, disbelieving others, eventually harming himself. The TV director is even worse, sacrificing his wife's dignity for his face in front of friends, extremely cowardly. His original intention was to preserve his face, but the result was losing his dignity, and that too, in the eyes of his beloved woman. A man's dignity is very sensitive, especially in front of the beloved woman. His wife's accusation is a great blow to him, but I have no sympathy for him. I believe he brought this humiliation upon himself. His reaction when encountering the police the next day confuses me. Suddenly becoming aggressive, for what reason? Is it a sudden awakening, or the collapse of long-term suppression, or perhaps to save the young man in the car?

"Crash" is difficult to analyze, just feeling that people are unpredictable, anything can happen.

The film devotes a large portion to describing racial and cultural conflicts. White people want to appear equal and respectful towards people of color, but secretly discriminate against blacks. Black people are vigilant against white discrimination, while discriminating against Asians, and Asians also discriminate against other people of color. The two car crash arguments at the beginning and end are very interesting. The first car crash involves the wife of an Asian arms dealer and a South American female detective. Both curse each other's ethnicity and discriminate against each other's race. The final car crash features a clinic receptionist confronting an insurance adjuster, one black and one Asian, both using each other's race as an object of attack. This phenomenon is both amusing and tragic. Such mutual insults reflect their lack of confidence, as they all strive to please mainstream society. Only whites are the dominant mainstream, speaking hypocritical words, purposefully dispensing their equality like charity, but when it comes to interests, their true colors immediately show. Just like the hypocritical judge, shouting equality while engaging in dirty dealings.

The chief says, people are too distant from each other. We all crave collisions; only collisions allow us to feel real contact. The normally glamorous judge's wife embraces the maid saying, "You're my best friend," revealing her bitterness. Communication between people does not improve with the increasing number and convenience of communication methods. Instead, communication becomes more difficult and complex. Communication between husband and wife, mother and son, colleagues, and different ethnicities can significantly impact one's life. People know communication is important, but they don't know how to handle this important matter well. People turn simple matters into complicated ones. A complex society makes it impossible for people to simply face life. People cannot act according to moral standards but must act according to interest standards. When the chief tells the judge something idealistic, when the young police officer pushes the boy out of the car, they unwillingly trample on their conscience, and I hear their sighs in their hearts. But, what else can be done? If it were us, what could we do? A few days ago, I saw someone discussing, if you were the TV director whose wife was humiliated by the police, what would you do at the scene? No man responded, no man gave a direct answer to this question. I think the answer lies in this avoidance, but this attitude is neither blameworthy nor reproachable.

"You'll figure it out after a few years. You think you know who you are!" The old policeman said to the new policeman. Who do we think we are?

Two: The Shining Light of Humanity

Car crash, the friction of human nature. It's a movie composed of multiple small stories. Within two hours, the director skillfully intertwines the stories.

The Spanish locksmith has a five-year-old daughter, cute and innocent. Due to past trauma, she fears gunshots and bullets, sleeping under the bed. Dad tells her a story: when he was very young, he met a fairy who gave him an invisible cloak that made him impervious to everything. The girl listens innocently to the story. Dad solemnly tells her: now he is giving it to his daughter. Taking off the invisible cloak, he hands it to his daughter. From now on, the daughter will no longer fear any bullets. A beautiful story to encourage the daughter to overcome the shadow of bullets.

- One of the shining moments, the greatness of paternal love. Fairy tales are kind.

An Arab grocery store owner. The lock on his door is broken, so he asks the locksmith to come fix it, replacing it with a new lock, but it doesn't work because the door itself is broken. They argue. The next day, the grocery store gets stolen, and there is no insurance company that can compensate. The boss is furious. Then he blames everything on the locksmith. So he brings a gun to the locksmith's house. As he holds the gun arguing with the locksmith, suddenly he raises the gun. And the little daughter rushes out: "Dad has no invisible cloak." She jumps into her dad's arms, blocking the bullet for him. Then follows the heart-wrenching cries of the father, the girl barely managing to smile: "Dad, I can protect you." Fortunately, the daughter did not die. And the gun-shooting boss is also stunned, clearly hitting, why didn't it work?

- Second shining moment, the daughter's love. The power of love is greater than everything, including stopping bullets.

Sitting alone in the grocery store, unaware of his daughter Dolly coming back. The boss tells his daughter: "I met a fairy, she's an angel." Yes, indeed a kind little angel. Actually, he doesn't know, his daughter is his angel. When buying the gun, she bought a box of blank ammunition.

"Crash" - Third shining moment, the daughter's love, understanding the father, deeply loving the father.

A black director and his wife encounter trouble on their way home from a white police officer. The white police officer takes the opportunity to insult the wife. As a husband, facing such a situation, he can do nothing but endure. However, the wife mocks him for being incapable of protecting her. They develop a conflict. - Fourth shining moment, sometimes enduring requires bearing a lot.

The wife encounters a car accident and is trapped inside the car. The white police officer goes to save her. The wife refuses to let the white police officer touch her due to the previous incident, still harboring fear. The white police officer finally yells at the woman out of frustration. When people pull the police officer out of the car, he disregards his own safety and returns to the car to pull the woman out. The first thing the woman does after being pulled out is to hug the white police officer and cry.

Three: The Thin Line Between Good and Evil

Paul Haggis, the director and screenwriter of "Crash," deserves admiration. He accurately captures the subtle psychological activities of the characters, turning them into a strong driving force for this multi-threaded story. In Los Angeles, this multicultural metropolis, people's emotions are both fragile and cold. They constantly endure unknown fears, yet recklessly vent their anger. The prosecutor's wife Jane becomes suspicious of all people of color after her car was snatched by two black youths; Police Ryan vents his anger on a black couple due to his father not receiving timely treatment; the Arab shopkeeper who was insulted when buying a gun vents his anger on the locksmith Daniel... Fear and anger spread like an epidemic in the city, making everyone live in unease, accumulating negative pressure until even a minor quarrel can escalate into a fatal accident.

Originally, seeing people's fates veering towards an irretrievable tragedy due to insignificant errors is already heartbreaking enough. But more distressing is that behind these seemingly pure accidents lies a mastermind controlling everything, one that no one can easily defy—the inherent flaws of human nature: prejudice, fear, and anger. This isn't merely present in the United States, this multi-cultural nation, but is also common in our daily lives, such as judging people by their appearance, bullying the weak, transferring anger to others, venting dissatisfaction, etc. Usually, we don't see the consequences of these behaviors, thinking they're trivial and quickly forgetting them. However, the director shows us their subsequent effects, how they can trigger massive storms like the butterfly effect.

The first half of the film focuses on the accumulation of details and the brewing of emotions. I thought it would lead to a major tragedy, leaving viewers with a morally shocking message. However, when I saw the clever endings the director arranged for different characters, my heart was once again strongly moved. Officer Ryan deals with a car accident and discovers that the victim is Christine, the black woman he once molested. Christine refuses Ryan's rescue due to fear, but Ryan still risks his life to save her. At that moment, both their hearts are probably greatly impacted by the crash. Should Christine hate or thank Ryan? Is Ryan good or evil? No one can say for sure. The only thing certain is that Ryan is lucky to have an opportunity to make amends and cherishes it. In contrast, Tommy, the young police officer who always appears positive and passionate, ends up mistakenly killing a black youth due to suspicion and personally covers up the evidence... The 180-degree reversal of character images leaves viewers who thought they could discern right from wrong dumbfounded, forcing them to overturn their preconceptions and reconsider the complexity of human nature.

In the first half, the director shows us the chain reactions of arbitrarily venting negative emotions. In the second half, he lets us fully recognize the great amplitude and elasticity of human nature, leading to vastly different outcomes. In fact, everyone is a combination of angels and demons. Changes in circumstances and situations can lead to cycles of good and evil. Sometimes, those who commit evil may have reasons worthy of sympathy, but this cannot justify their wrongdoing, because people in society may seem isolated but are actually interconnected. Even a small amount of malice can grow like a snowball, not only harming innocent people but ultimately causing retribution on oneself. The difference between good and evil is just a thought, yet it can lead to completely different outcomes. Perhaps we cannot solve deep-rooted ethnic issues or class differences, but at least, we can calm down for a few seconds before our fear or anger overflows, trying to rationally make the right choice.
