I gave him the eye. By now, evening had started to fall a bit, and as a result, visibility wasn't so great, but there was still enough light for me to see him clearly. What I saw convinced me that I'd feel a lot more at ease if there were a sturdy rustic bench between us. Accordingly, I rose, mimicking the style of a rocketing pheasant, and proceeded to position myself on the other side of the object in question. My swift agility didn't go unnoticed. He seemed somewhat taken aback. He stopped, and for about the time it takes for a bead of perspiration to trickle from the top of the brow to the tip of the nose, he stood silently gazing at me.

"So!" he said eventually, and it came as a complete surprise to me that people actually say "So!" I had always thought it was just something you read in books. Like "Quotha!" or "Odds bodikins!" or even "Eh, ba goom!" Nonetheless, there it was. Quaint or not, bizarre or not, he had said "So!" and it was up to me to deal with the situation accordingly.

It would have taken a duller man than Bertram Wooster to fail to notice that the dear old chap was rather worked up. Whether his eyes were literally shooting forth flames, I couldn’t tell you, but there appeared to be a distinct incandescence. For the rest, his fists were clenched, his ears quivering, and the muscles of his jaw rotating rhythmically, as if he were making an early supper off something. His hair was full of twigs, and there was a beetle hanging to the side of his head which would have fascinated Gussie Fink-Nottle. To this, however, I paid little attention. There is a time for studying beetles and a time for not studying beetles.

"So!" he said again. Now, those who know Bertram Wooster best will tell you that he is always at his shrewdest and most level-headed in moments of peril. Who was it who, when gripped by the arm of the law on boat-race night not so many years ago and hauled off to Vine Street police station, assumed in a flash the identity of Eustace H. Plimsoll, of The Laburnums, Alleyn Road, West Dulwich, thus saving the grand old name of Wooster from being dragged in the mire and avoiding wide publicity of the wrong sort? Who was it... But I need not belabor the point. My record speaks for itself. Three times caught, but never once sentenced under the correct label. Ask anyone at the Drones about this.

So now, in a situation threatening to become increasingly dicey, I did not lose my head. I maintained the old sang-froid. Smiling a genial and affectionate smile, and hoping that it wasn’t too dark for it to register, I spoke with jolly cordiality: "Why, hallo, Tuppy. You here?"

He replied that yes, he was here.

"Been here long?"

"I have."

"Fine. I wanted to see you."

"Well, here I am. Come out from behind that bench."

"No, thanks, old man. I like leaning on it. It seems to rest the spine."

"In about two seconds," said Tuppy, "I’m going to kick your spine up through the top of your head."

I raised my eyebrows. Not much use, of course, in that light, but it seemed to help the general composition. "Is this Hildebrand Glossop speaking?" I said.

He replied that it was, adding that if I wanted to make sure I might move a few feet over in his direction. He also called me an opprobrious name. I raised my eyebrows again.
