Guzang Festival: Its Origins and Development

---------------------------------Xijiang Thousand Household Miao Village

       No one knows exactly when the Guzang Festival began, only that it has been passed down from generation to generation. Many of the elders and leaders of the festival recount legends they heard from their forefathers, saying that the Miao people's Guzang Festival originated from the ancient times when the Miao ancestor Jiang Yang offered sacrifices to his mother "Mei Bang Mei Liu" (Butterfly Mother). According to legend, after Jiang Yang and his wife had children, every year was blessed with favorable weather and bountiful harvests. However, there were two consecutive years of severe drought where no crops grew. To find out the reason, Jiang Yang consulted a shaman. The shaman said it was because Jiang Yang's eldest son had not properly managed the water buffalo, allowing it to step on Butterfly Mother’s grave, angering her. She demanded that Jiang Yang sacrifice a water buffalo to honor her during the New Year. Reluctantly, Jiang Yang followed the order. In the following year, the weather was once again favorable, and there was another good harvest. From then on, the Miao ancestors began holding annual ancestral worship ceremonies.

       A few years later, Jiang Yang and his descendants, inspired by the ancient practice of using hollow logs as coffins for burials, combined this with the need for frequent migration and rituals. Since Butterfly Mother was born from the heart of a maple tree, they hollowed out the core of the maple wood to make cylindrical drums symbolizing her soul's dwelling place. They covered one or both ends with animal skin, tightly secured with bamboo nails. People called these drums "Ancestral Drums," representing the resting place of ancestral spirits. These sacred drums could not be struck casually, except during the Ancestral Worship Festival when the spirits were awakened to enjoy offerings and celebrate with their descendants. Afterward, the drums would be returned to specially designated caves for safekeeping. Because they were often left outdoors, they easily deteriorated, necessitating replacement every few years. Due to the ceremonial processes involved in awakening the drum, replacing it, offering sacrifices, and hiding it again, the festival came to be known as the Guzang Festival. As it was celebrated by each drum society unit, it was also called the Drum Society Festival. Additionally, due to its focus on honoring ancestors, it is sometimes referred to as the Ancestral Worship Festival.

       Li Tinggui points out in his article "A Brief Discussion on Miao Social Organizations" that the basic social unit of ancient Miao society was the drum society. The earliest Miao ancestors conducted ancestral worship activities focused solely on female ancestors, and the maple wood drum symbolized the resting place of the female ancestor Butterfly Mother. Wei Qiguang mentioned in his article "On the Origin of the Miao Drum Society and Yilang" that this indicates the Guzang Festival (Drum Society Festival) originated during the matrilineal clan period of the Miao ancestors, at which time the drum society was an exogamous group.

       Later, the Miao ancestors created another type of wooden drum — the progeny drum — specifically for the purpose of sacrificing to Jiang Yang and his wife, hoping for the prosperity and safety of the clan. This drum could also be used for entertainment, such as dancing during New Year celebrations or the Guzang Festival, providing joy for both ancestors and themselves. After the ceremonies and festivities, the drum could be stored safely in the house, requiring replacement only after more than a hundred years unless destroyed by fire or war. People realized through experience that to hold a successful Guzang Festival, it needed to be organized by democratically elected drum society leaders and their assistants.

       Tang Chunfang wrote in "The Significance and Origins of the Miao Drum Sacrifice Festival" that the Miao drum sacrifice custom originated in the early maternal power system, with the wooden drum evolving from a coffin. One of the authors of this article, Li Guozhang, discussed this in "Miao Wooden Drums," stating that the drum concealment customs were already very prevalent in areas like Mount Tai, Mount Lu, and the Yimeng Mountains in Shandong during prehistoric times. However, due to limitations in productivity and calendar civilization, the early maternal power system's drum sacrifice customs lacked fixed rules, taking various forms and being performed arbitrarily. It was only after the lunar calendar was established that a relatively standardized Guzang Festival was formed. The Guzang Festival began with Jiang Yang's sacrifice to Butterfly Mother, occurring in the second month when butterflies start to gather nectar. Even today, some Miao villages in Leishan still celebrate the Guzang Festival in the second month.

       Perhaps because the annual ancestral worship and celebration of harvest became too cumbersome with the many rituals involved — awakening the drum, raising the drum, establishing the drum (rotating the drum), sacrificing to the drum, sending off the drum, and concealing the drum — the Guzang Festival gradually evolved into a small annual ceremony (Miao New Year) and a larger one held every few years (3, 5, 7, 13, 25 years; currently 13 nominal years in Leishan), a tradition that continues to this day.

       Later, Chi You, the cultural ancestor of the Miao people, engaged in a decisive battle with the Yellow Emperor near Zhuolu in Hebei. Chi You was defeated and killed around the middle of the tenth lunar month. His remains were secretly retrieved by a dragon-totem Miao clan and buried in Puyang, Henan. Subsequently, the Miao people began celebrating the New Year and offering sacrifices to Butterfly Mother, Jiang Yang, Chi You, and their clan ancestors in the tenth month. Most Miao villages in Leishan still adhere to celebrating the Miao New Year and the Guzang Festival in the tenth lunar month.

       After the defeat of the Jiu Li tribal alliance led by Chi You, large numbers of Miao ancestors were forced to flee and migrate. The clan leaders and village elders convened a meeting and decided that after splitting into different clans, each clan would independently host the festival according to a cyclical schedule based on the Earthly Branches (the Chinese zodiac), rather than gathering at the original settlement to celebrate. Some drums were changed from maple wood (black drum) to nanmu or camphor wood (white drum). White represents snow, symbolizing coldness, sorrow, and grief, corresponding to mourning rites. This was because Chi You was captured and intentionally humiliated by having shackles made from the maple trees regarded as sacred by the Miao ancestors. Therefore, the Miao ancestors avoided using maple wood for drums, opting instead for nanmu or camphor wood. This explains why later legends claim that the Miao drum societies have black drums and white drums, with the white drum evolving from the black drum.

       Due to the numerous long-distance migrations of the Miao ancestors, the clans continuously divided their properties and settled in different places. Consequently, the use of wooden drums varied among different lineages and clans. Some still maintained both the ancestral drum and the progeny drum, storing them separately in nearby caves and residences. Others combined the two, keeping them in drum towers, drum halls, or the homes of respected village elders. Still others used the drums both for ancestral worship and as musical instruments for dance accompaniment, as seen in the current Wuliou village in Leishan.

       Around the time copper drums appeared during the Shang and Zhou dynasties, the Miao people also began incorporating them into the Guzang Festival. By modern times, some Miao villages even replaced wooden drums with copper ones, storing them in private homes. Besides serving for ancestral worship, the copper drums also functioned to celebrate harvests, unite the clan, and provide entertainment, enriching the content and atmosphere of the Guzang Festival.

       Nowadays, some Miao villages still follow the traditional sequence of the drum-raising year, drum-rotating year, and drum-sending (concealing) year for the Guzang Festival, but most have simplified the three-year process into a single event held in the third year. These varying processes, evolving from simple to complex and back to simple across different historical periods, can all be observed in the Guzang Festival celebrations of the Miao villages in Leishan County today.
