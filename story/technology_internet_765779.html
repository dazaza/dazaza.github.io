Details of Samsung's smartwatch are revealed: its functions may rely on its own smartphones

by anonymous on 2013-08-20 16:00:13

Samsung may release a smartwatch product named "Galaxy Gear" on September 4th. Some insiders have revealed some software and hardware information about this product to the media.

Hardware

People familiar with Samsung's plans told the media that among the designs disclosed to developers, there are many different variations of this product, but most of them come with square screens and all use high-quality OLED displays capable of showing full-spectrum colors. It is reported that the diagonal size of this device is 2.5 inches (including the casing it reaches 3 inches), equipped with a dual-core processor, and has a very long battery life.

Earlier reports claimed that the product would use Samsung's own Exynos 4212 dual-core processor with a clock speed of 1.5GHz, including the Mali-400 MP4 graphics processing unit, with a resolution of 320x320.

In addition, insiders also revealed that this watch band features a camera, and there is a mini-speaker on the buckle. The product includes an NFC (Near Field Communication) module for synchronization and authentication. This watch supports Bluetooth 4.0 Low Energy standard (Bluetooth 4.0 LE), which can be used to connect to smartphones.

This watch also comes with an accelerometer. When users lift their wrist to view content or time, the device automatically turns on. This is a good solution for waking up the watch and applications, as well as managing battery power. The screen supports common touch, swipe, and point-and-click operations, but may not support text input. For a device of this size, such settings make sense. Galaxy Gear will be equipped with enough sensors to rival wearable devices like Nike's "Power Band." The company has already launched the S-Health health tracking application in the Galaxy S4.

Software

The Galaxy Gear watches sent to developers are based on Android 4.1 system (some are on 4.2 system). The watch can synchronize tightly with phones and tablets. For example, if someone swipes over a notification on the watch and stops at an email title, they can pick up their smartphone to see the corresponding email content. At launch, Samsung will integrate Twitter and Facebook.

This watch can connect to a Samsung watch management app inside the phone. This connection will be achieved using Samsung’s proprietary accessory protocol with low-energy Bluetooth serving as the network transmission method. Clearly, these apps on the watch run in a client-server manner.

Interestingly, insiders revealed that these apps designed to take advantage of the hardware do not come from Google Play, the official Google app store, but from Samsung’s own app store. It seems that Samsung is gradually courting developers to release apps through its own platform rather than overly relying on Google.

In summary, if you want to buy a Samsung Galaxy Gear smartwatch, you will need a Samsung Galaxy phone or tablet. This might be the company going too far in mimicking Apple recently, trying to create a 'walled garden'; it is unlikely that Android fans will be receptive.

Significance

Notification functions are essential in the post-PC era and are becoming increasingly frequent. Notifications are becoming a way for users to interact with hardware and services, so mobile hardware manufacturers no longer just see phones as phones, but as personal servers, centers for handling other small devices carried by users. This will become a new way to experience information, potentially reshaping people's understanding of user experience and design.

At the beginning of this month, Phil Libin, CEO of note-taking app Evernote, told the media, "The transition from smartphones to wearable devices has greater implications for product development. Wearable products won't become mainstream in the next 6 to 12 months, but they could in the next two to three years, and their adoption rate will exceed people's expectations."

If Samsung had its way, things would develop even faster. Clearly, Samsung executives know about Apple's efforts on iWatch. This time, Samsung leads Apple by launching the smartwatch product first. If this device succeeds, Samsung's efforts won’t have been wasted.

However, if Galaxy Gear encounters problems like disconnection, difficult-to-use interfaces, or other issues previously seen on smartwatches, Samsung might just be paving the way for Apple, because Apple wouldn't launch a product until it is perfect.

Other Competition

There are now many companies trying to catch the smartwatch trend, and many of these products use the Android system. Sony has launched three modified Live View wearable products as a second screen for Android phones. In 2011, Motorola released MotoACTV, a motion tracker that can also connect via Bluetooth to Android devices to display notifications, call numbers, texts, and social network messages. An important difference is that Motorola's product can function independently without a phone as a GPS locator, wireless MP3 player, etc.

Besides, there are many lesser-known small vendors. MetaWatch products provide reminder functions and use low-energy reflective displays. Pebble raised more than $10 million through the crowdfunding site Kickstarter, and its products are now sold at Best Buy.

None of these products have convinced consumers that "smartwatches are a necessity." Part of the reason is the definition of a smartwatch: do we need a simple second screen for mobile devices, or a more independent device? It seems that Samsung chose the former, and given the current situation where smartphones are replaced every year, this is a safe choice.
