In basic vocabulary collocations, there are mainly two types of relationships: verb relationships and modifier relationships. Verb relationships refer to the subject-predicate or verb-object relationships formed by verbs and nouns. For example, in Chinese expressions like "dragon soars and tiger leaps," "chickens fly and dogs jump," and "people fall off and horses tumble," swapping the nouns inside would result in ridicule. Modifier relationships refer to adjectives modifying nouns or adverbs modifying verbs. For instance, in phrases like "fox borrows tiger's might," "pigs are mean and dogs are dangerous," and "graceful cranes wear hats," swapping the objects being modified also leads to nonsensical results. In English, such collocation relationships are presented in another form of text, which often leaves many candidates at a loss.

Fixed collocations are a must-test type in the cloze test for postgraduate entrance examinations. It requires candidates to memorize necessary common English idiomatic expressions. This type of question has lower flexibility and is relatively targeted in preparation. However, candidates should avoid falling into a misconception, which is spending too much time and energy on memorizing fixed expressions. In fact, if time permits, memorizing fixed collocations word-for-word is indeed an effective means. But if time is limited, the aforementioned method is obviously not feasible, as it may disrupt your entire preparation plan due to occupying too much time. Here, teachers remind candidates to note that our goal for the cloze test in postgraduate entrance examinations is only one: scoring! In other words, choosing the correct options. Remember: no one forces you to remember idiomatic expressions perfectly, which is unnecessary and unneeded. You just need to have a relatively deep impression of some commonly tested fixed collocations. For example, in much the ____ way as, followed by four options A) familiar B) similar C) same D) identical. For this question, if you have a deeper impression of the fixed expression in much the same way as during regular times, you will find that option C) has higher recognizability than the other options, or simply put, it looks more appealing. Why does it look appealing? Because you see it frequently, even though you may not be able to write it down perfectly. So for such question types, you don't need to force memorization and certainly don't need to distinguish the differences between various options.

The connection between sentences actually examines the candidate's understanding of sentence meanings and their ability to infer the relationships between sentences. Many candidates often confuse if only and only if, despite them almost never being selected. Many candidates also confuse even if and even though, which actually have no difference; more candidates confuse as if and even if, causing much trouble during exams. Therefore, clarifying the four common types of relationships makes solving sentence connections in the cloze test in postgraduate entrance examinations effortless. These four types of relationships are: addition, such as furthermore, moreover, in addition, etc.; reversal, such as although, whereas, even if, on the contrary, etc.; cause-effect, such as because, so, consequently, as a result, etc.; time, such as while, since, before, etc.
